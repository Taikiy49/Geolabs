Soils Investigation Report
Proposed Swimming Pool Retaining Wall
552 Pepeekeo Place
This report presents the results of our preliminary site
inspection and hand excavated test pit examination of the
proposed swimming pool and retaining wall site at the above
property The general location of the site is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate l
The proposed swimming pool and retaining wall site is located
on the rear portion of the property on a relatively steep cut
slope It is our understanding that the tentative plan is to
situate the proposed swimming pool on a new terrace partly cut
into the slope and partly filled above the existing house floor
grade The swimming pool generally will measure 20 by 30 feet
with an 8 foot deep diving portion
W Page 2
It is our understanding that retaining walls of about 20 feet
high may be required on the deeper side of the pool to retain
the upslope side of the cut It is anticipated that the
retaining walls on the downslope or fill side will be lower
perhaps about 8 feet The actual heights of the retaining
walls are yet to be determined

SITE CONDITIONS
According to a grading plan provided to you by Kacor Realty
most of the house lot in question was previously cut below the
original ground surface for subdivision work The cut slope
indicated on the grading plan was H V Our field
measurements indicated that the existing slope is somewhat
flatter probably at 2 1 A concrete lined drainage ditch
exists at the top of the slope
GEOLABS HAWAII
Based on the results of the shallow test pits excavated we
believe that the upper Slope material generally consists of
stiff dark gray silty clay with angular to subangular basalt
boulders and smaller rock fragments The stiff dark gray silty
clay is generally expansive and is locally called adobe soil
A sample from Test Pit 3 was tested in our laboratory and it
exhibited an expansion of 10 1 upon saturation A layer of
stiff orange brown silt was encountered at about 3 foot depth
at the lower part of the Slope in Test Pits 2 and 4 This
orange brown silt is believed to be a decomposed ash deposit
and it is only slightly expansive This ashy layer is believed
to be relatively thin and underlain by another layer of
Clay boulder deposit
Groundwater was not encountered in our test pits nor was it
observed on the slope at the time of our field exploration
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our preliminary findings we believe that the site is
marginally suitable for the proposed swimming pool construction
due to the existence of expansive adobe clay boulder materials
on a relatively steep slope
GEOLABS HAWAII
Potential Slope Instability
One of the major concerns is possible existence of seepage or
old ground slip surfaces under the Slope If such a condition
exists there would be a higher risk of Slope instability and
the situation may need extensive study to identify the potential
risk We would like to emphasize that such a condition cannot
be readily recognized by borings or shallow test pits There
fore at the onset of the site work immediately after access is
provided for a backhoe to move on the Slope at least two test
pits should be excavated to allow partial examination of the
slope conditions by the soils engineer to see if seepage or
Signs of pre existing groundslip could be detected If these
conditions are detected we will then recommend the project be
suspended all excavations backfilled and slope restored to its
original condition
Expansive Adobe Clay Problems
When wetted the expansive clay induces high lateral pressures
on retaining walls and its swelling characteristics could also
lift up future lightly loaded structures that are placed on it
When the ground is covered with a slab or swimming pool moisture
generally tends to accummulate under the slabs This in turn
will give the soil an opportunity to swell

Assuming that the higher than normal risk of potential slope
instability and seepage has been recognized and accepted by your
client the following points should also be considered
1 Massive and strong retaining walls are required to retain the
high cut on the upslope side of the swimming pool
2 Overexcavation is required behind the retaining walls below
the swimming pool and the on grade decks to minimize the
swelling effect of the expansive adobe soils The backfill
should consist of non expansive imported select borrow fill

modifications may be necessary if the actual conditions are
substantially different from those described

Retaining Walls
It is anticipated that the height of retaining walls may range
up to 20 feet above their footings along the upslope side of the
pool Otherwise most of the remaining walls may range up to
about 8 feet in height
We recommend that design active lateral earth pressures of 60
p s f per foot of depth be used for the design of the upslope
retaining wall These active earth pressures may be reduced in
part by backfill in front of the wall under the pool deck
This counter pressure may be assumed to be 40 p s f per foot
of depth below the deck surface The adobe clay and boulder
soils within at least 18 inches behind the upslope retaining
wall should be replaced by permeable crushed rock such as
Crusher Run or No 3B Fine material However the top 2
feet of the retaining wall backfill should consist of relatively
impervious non expansive soil such as reddish brown silt with
less than 3 percent expansion to minimize surface water infil
tration All backfill behind the upslope retaining wall should
be compacted to 85 of the maximum dry density determined by
ASTM D 1557 78 test method No compaction is required on the
No 3B Fine gravel used behind the wall
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 7
To resist horizontal forces on the retaining wall a passive
earth pressure of 250 p s f per foot of depth below the top of
the swimming pool slab may be used A friction factor of 0 4
may also be used at the base of the retaining wall footings
The maximum allowable vertical bearing pressure should not
exceed 4 000 p s f The bottom of footing should be at least
12 inches below the bottom of the swimming pool slab and should
rest on at least 6 inches of compacted granular fill
A high cut will be required for the upslope retaining walls We
believe that the cut may be made as steep as possible However
the upper 5 feet of cut may need to be trimmed back at
H V slope Such a trimming may undermine the existing lined
ditch on the top of the Slope It must be taken into considera
tion that an interrupted ditch may cause severe runoff and
erosion problems during a heavy rain Therefore the integrity
of the ditch must be maintained Methods to maintain the ditch
over the cut should be evaluated before proceeding with the
construction
A properly constructed subdrain system should be installed
behind the retaining wall to prevent build up of hydrostatic
pressure The subdrain should consist of a vertical rock
curtain consisting of permeable crushed rock materials such
as the No 3B Fine gravel material recommended for backfill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1226 00 Page 8
This rock curtain should drain into a perforated lateral
collector pipe at the back bottom of the retaining wall The
perforations should not be larger than one fourth of an inch in
diameter The pipe should be sloped to discharge into an open
area The outlet of the subdrain pipe should be protected and
free of obstructions
For the design of lower walls on the downslope side of the
swimming pool the active lateral earth pressures may be reduced
to 40 p s f per foot of depth provided that granular material
is used for backfill between the pool and the lower retaining
wall Where such condition cannot be met the active earth
pressures should be increased to 60 p s f per foot of depth as
mentioned earlier The footing should be embedded a minimum
of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Weepholes or
subdrains behind the downslope retaining wall should also be
provided to prevent the build up of hydrostatic pressure
Swimming Pool
One of the most important considerations for the swimming pool
construction is the reduction of the swelling potential of the
subsoils after construction Normally a minimum of 3 feet of
the expansive soil below and adjacent to the swimming pool
should be removed and then replaced with select borrow fill We
believe that the following alternate construction procedures may
also be used This would involve lime saturation and could
reduce the amount of overexcavation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1226 00 Page 9
The expansive clay should be overexcavated to at least 24 inches
below the bottom of the swimming pool slab Hydrated lime
should be placed over the overexcavation surface and the
subsoils should be moistened for at least 2 days to induce some
amount of swelling of the subsoil We believe that five 5
20 ib bags of hydrated lime would be sufficient to cover the
overexcavated pool area
The overexcavation should then be backfilled with non expansive
granular material such as the crusher run or crusher waste
material All backfill materials under the pool deck and wall
footings should be placed in 8 inch maximum horizontal lifts
compacted to 90 of the ASTM D 1557 78 maximum density The
subgrade material should be kept moist throughout the construc
tion to prevent drying of the underlying on site clay soil
After the backfill is Placed the area should be kept wet
continuously for a period of at least 3 days before concrete
is poured Presumably the drainline work backfilling and
other activities before concreting may take more than 3 days
to complete Therefore there should be adequate time to
pre moist the soil before concrete placement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1226 00 Page 10
It is recommended that a subdrain system be provided to drain
off the possible free water that may accummulate below the
swimming pool This drainage should be free draining by gravity
and should not be tied to the pool drain itself nor should it be
connected to any storm drain lines The drainline should be
located at the bottom of granular fill and should have its own
discharge points Thus it may be necessary to raise the pool
level so that the bottom of excavation will be higher to provide
adequate gradient for the subdrain line
Design Review
Plans for retaining wall and swimming pool construction should
be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written
comments prior to construction to see that the intent of our
recommendations is included in the project plans and
specifications
CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the Slope excavation for retaining wall
construction be observed by the soils engineer during construc
tion This is to further examine the Slope conditions and to
see if the subsurface conditions are consistent with the
findings of our subsurface investigation In addition the
compaction of backfill should be monitored to see if adequate
compaction has been obtained The recommendations given in this
report are contingent upon the above inspections

INVESTIGATION LIMITATIONS
Our services consist of professional opinions and recommen
dations made in accordance with generally accepted soil and
foundation engineering principles and practices This war
ranty is in lieu of all other warranties either expressed or
implied
The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are
based on our site reconnaissance soil information from test pit
data and laboratory tests obtained from this soil investigation
There is an inherent risk of instability associated with any
hillside development Although this risk can be reduced
somewhat by means of proper drainage and site work greater than
normal risk will remain for this project
Unanticipated soil conditions are commonly encountered and
cannot be fully determined by reconnaissance and soil borings or
test pits These unforeseen conditions such as pockets of
organic soils trash fill or weak soils May occur in localized
areas Therefore additional expenditures may be needed during
construction to attain a properly constructed project Some
contingency fund is thus recommended to accommodate these
possible costs
