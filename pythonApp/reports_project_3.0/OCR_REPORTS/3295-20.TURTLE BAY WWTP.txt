INTRODUCTION
This report presents the findings of our injection well performance evaluation for
the Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant in Kahuku on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The findings and recommendations presented herein are based on site observations
made during a site visit the performance of short term injection testing on the wells
and personal communication with operators and managers of the treatment plant
facility The approximate locations of the treatment plant facility and the injection well
field are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated
and the Hawaii State Department of Health Underground Injection
Control UIC Injection Well Status Report Guidelines Version 1 dated June 1992
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for the
disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Turtle Bay Resort
development The majority of the effluent treated at the plant is used for irrigation of
the resort golf course within the Turtle Bay complex Therefore the wells are used for
the injection of treated effluent only on a stand by basis during periods of time when
the effluent cannot be utilized for irrigation purposes generally due to wet weather
conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 20 Page 2
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit issued for the
facility UIC Permit No UO 1532 it is required that periodic monitoring of the wells be
made by a professional engineer or geologist familiar with injection well operations
Geolabs Hawaii has prepared this injection well evaluation report to provide the owner
the operator and the regulatory agency with information regarding the performance of
the injection wells to aid in the prevention of adverse public health effects from an
injection well failure This evaluation is intended to assist in ascertaining that the wells
remain operational

DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of
Golf Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and
the moat around Punahoolapa Marsh The topography of the well field area is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 20 Page 5
generally level with some topographic mounds and depressions associated with the
adjacent golf course terrain Based ona topographic map of the well field and vicinity
ground surface elevations range from about 5 to 6 feet Mean Sea Level At the time
of our site visit on June 26th the weather conditions were generally dry under sunny
skies No evidence of surface ponding of water was visible in the vicinity of the well
field
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults of approximately 4 by
4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The vaults are covered with hinged
steel plate doors and are approximately flush with the adjacent grass covered ground
surface
Inside of the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant representatives for the inspection and performance of the
injection tests Based on our recommendation provided in our Injection Well Inspection
Report dated two inch diameter inspection ports were installed to
eliminate the need for removal of the entire top plate during future inspection testing
However to allow complete access to the well interior for the performance of the short
term injection tests the top plate of each well was removed
At any time during the injection of effluent two of the wells are placed in use for
effluent disposal The three wells are placed into operation on a rotating basis so that
at any given time one well will not be in operation Flow to an individual well is
controlled by a valve located adjacent to each well head A main control valve diverts
the effluent flow from the treatment plant to either the well field or the lagoon which
serves as storage of water for use as golf course irrigation

Injection System
The injection system is regulated by valves which can be used to isolate the
wells so that any one or combination of the wells may be used for injection At the time
of our site visit the normal system operation was set up such that Wells A and B
were currently on stand by for use as disposal wells for the injection of effluent Well
C was isolated from the system as an emergency back up to the system
The system is equipped with a flow totalizing meter This meter measures all
flow to the well field rather than to individual wells Flows to individual wells may be
estimated by records of which valves are open during injection periods
At the time of our site visit the totalizing meter had an initial cumulative reading
of about gallons The original reading on the meter when the well field was
placed in service was about 400 000 gallons Parker 1992
Daily Flow to Well Field
Based on a review of the operational log of the injection well system for the
preceding six months daily flows ranged from zero to a maximum flow of about
436 000 gallons per day The month of January was the most active period of injection
well usage due to the higher incidence of rainfall and reduced irrigation needs for the
golf course
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 20 Page 11
For the preceding six months flow to the well field generally followed a pattern
of several days of injection followed by several days of zero flow to the well field We
believe this pattern of injection is beneficial for maintaining good well capacity as it
provides the well with a chance to recover and keep biological growth in balance
From the flow records it appears as though injection quantities experienced a
significant reduction beginning in about May in response to a drier weather pattern as
the summer months approach We anticipate lower than normal flows to the well field
will occur throughout the remainder of the summer months
Short Term Injection Tests
The short term injection tests were performed by diverting treated effluent from
the treatment plant into each well separately for a period of about 9 minutes Each
well was isolated from the others for the duration of each test by adjusting the valves
controlling the effluent flow to individual wells
During injection the head level response was measured and recorded
Readings from the totalizing meter were recorded prior to and after each short term
injection test to obtain the total quantity of injected effluent and to estimate the average
flow rate into each well
In general it was observed that the operation of the three wells were
satisfactory The average injection rates ranged from about 256 to 311 gallons per
minute gpm During injection at these rates the head level in each of the three wells
was observed to rise ranging in height of about 2 to 8 inches before the test was
terminated Wells A and C appeared to have the smaller capacity in comparison to
Well B Based on our observations of the injection testing Well B appears to be the
most permeable well of the three wells The results of the short term injection testing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 20 Page 12
indicate the wells are operating satisfactorily with about the same level of disposal
capacity noted during the preceding well inspection of December 1994 Appendix B

Recommendations
During the visual inspection of Well A a steady stream of clear water was
observed entering the concrete vault through a perforation in the lower portion of the
vault sidewall Due to the close proximity of the adjacent lagoon and a probable
elevated water table located in the immediate vicinity of the well head during injection
the water entering the vault is likely groundwater However efforts should be
undertaken to affirm that no failure in the well casing exists In addition the perforation
should be sealed to eliminate the accumulation of water in the well vault
During the visual inspection of Wells A and C the visible portion of the well
casings were observed to have a higher accumulation of encrusting material than Well
B Well B had been scraped and treated within the past year to slow the corrosion
on the visible portion of the well casing above the static water level At this time it may
be necessary to scrape the encrusting material and apply a corrosion inhibitor to the
surface of the well casing to preserve the upper casing integrity of Wells A and C
In general the wells appear to be operating normally with no observed indication
of significantly reduced well capacity or imminent failure To maintain the injection
capacity of the wells we recommend that the operation and maintenance program be
continued There is a tendency for injection wells to clog over time as a result of
suspended solids in the effluent and biological action in the wells In general to reduce
the level of biological activity the use of the wells should be rotated so that the wells
are allowed to recover between extended periods of injection
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 20 Page 13
To partially restore capacity lost by the introduction of suspended solids and gas
accumulation due to biological activity we recommend that the wells be redeveloped
on a periodic basis It is our opinion that pumping or airlift pumping could be effective
for redevelopment of the wells Other methods of redevelopment such as jetting or
scrubbing the interior of the well may be too abrasive and may damage the well screen
The method of redevelopment should be carefully evaluated by a professional
experienced with the available well development methods and the construction of
injection wells In addition a means to effectively contain and dispose of the effluent
generated during a pumping or airlifting effort should be accounted for prior to well
cleaning
Redevelopment should be scheduled and conducted based in part on field
observations recorded by Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant personnel of the
system operation and head levels within the wells during injection In addition the
results of our bi annual short term injection testing of the wells may be used to aid in
determining whether the injection wells could be experiencing a reduced capacity
between inspection visits Generally redevelopment should be conducted if observed
head levels indicate an average increase of about 10 to 15 percent over conditions
established after a redevelopment event
