REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch
The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these
two volcanoes The project site is located on the lower western slopes of the older West
Maui Volcano
The older West Maui Volcano generally is comprised of three lava flows The lower
unit was formed during the Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch and is identified as the
Wailuku Volcanic Series The lavas of the Wailuku Volcanic Series generally consist of
thinly bedded a a and pahoehoe lavas Overlying this unit are lavas of the Honolua
Volcanic Series late Pleistocene Epoch that consist of alkalic lavas and stiff trachyte
Volcanic inactivity occurred during the Middle Pleistocene Epoch resulting in
weathering of the lavas carving of canyons and deposition of materials During the late
Pleistocene Epoch renewal of volcanic activity occurred near the southern coast with lava
flows of the Lahaina Volcanic Series The lavas of the Lahaina Volcanic Series generally
consist of olivine rich basalt and nepheline basanite

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area and the field exploration cavities and or voids
commonly exist in the basalt rock formation To reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend
implementing a cavity probing and grouting program at the foundation locations for the
bridge structures during construction
We recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every 100 square feet of foundation
area or at 10 feet on center along the abutment strip footings The probe holes should be
at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to depths of at least 15 feet below the
bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during
the probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in
delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures We recommend utilizing a low strength sand cement grout mixture
with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches for the grouting operations As an alternative to
sand cement grout Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM also may be used for the
grouting operations
We recommend a Geolabs representative observe the footing over excavations
prior to placement of the select granular fill materials to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths In addition we recommend that the
foundation probing and grouting be observed by our representative

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide strip of geocomposite

Anchored Wall System
In general the anchored wall system consists of a concrete wall stem and footing
with permanent ground anchors drilled into the underlying basalt rock formation The
advantage of this retaining wall system is that it reduces the required footing width
reduces the amount of excavation required for wall construction and shortens the duration
of wall construction compared to conventional cantilever and drilled shaft retaining walls
In general the permanent ground anchor system is constructed by drilling a
borehole installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the
borehole The basic components of a grouted permanent ground anchor consist of the
anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar In
general the unbonded length of the ground anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or
reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with the grout allowing the tendon or
reinforcing bar to elongate elastically transferring the resisting force from the structure to

tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the

In general the permanent ground anchors would derive support principally from the

Construction Considerations
The performance of the permanent ground anchors depends upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of the potential
variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project site we

Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions in the area it should be noted that
drilling of the ground anchor holes may encounter clinker gravel and basalt rock
Therefore special drilling tools for drilling into and through the gravel and basalt
rock will be required Temporary casing of the drilled holes for the permanent
ground anchors may be required if caving in conditions occur during drilling of the

Grouting of the tieback anchors has a major impact on the load carrying capacities

after drilling to reduce the potential for significant caving in of the sides of the drilled
holes

for fill materials Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Where scarification of the subgrade is not possible especially in the basalt
formation we recommend proof rolling the subgrades with a large vibratory drum
roller minimum 20 tons static weight for a minimum of eight passes to help detect
and collapse near surface cavities and or voids The vibratory drum roller should be
operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute Yielding areas loose areas or
cavities disclosed during the clearing and proof rolling operations should be
over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials The depth of
over excavation should extend until dense underlying materials are exposed and
should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs representative
Fills and Backfills
In general we anticipate that the excavations likely will encounter fill clinker
colluvium residual soils and basalt rock with the majority of the excavation
encountering the underlying basalt rock formation The excavated basalt rock
materials may be used as a source of general fill and backfill materials provided
that the material contains rock fragments no greater than 6 inches and contains
between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Select granular fill
materials will be used to a depth of 2 feet from finished subgrade Select granular
fill materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than
3 inches in largest dimension have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more with a
maximum swell value of 1 percent or less and contains between 10 to 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The fill material should also be free of
vegetation and deleterious materials
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should consist of
crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Roads Bridges and Public Works Construction dated 2006
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at

least 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement of the fills within
3 feet of the finished grades should be increased to at least 95 percent relative
compaction
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Observations and soil density tests should be performed by Geolabs
during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of
compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction
Conventional compaction testing generally is not practical in fills composed of rock
fragments and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes by a specific compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the
number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose basalt rock formation In general we believe
that cut slopes into the basalt rock formation may be cut at an inclination of one
horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Slope
benches are recommended at maximum 30 foot vertical height intervals Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V such as at the gullies should be keyed and
benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations
Fill slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should be constructed by overfilling and
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face

Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should

Underground Utilities
The project will involve installation of new drain and water lines In general we
recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the pipes and manhole structures for uniform
support The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum
width of one fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or 12 inches whichever is greater
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes up to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or select granular fill
materials provided the backfill materials are fairly well graded with no particles greater than
6 inches in largest dimension The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch horizontal loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below the
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should
be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
