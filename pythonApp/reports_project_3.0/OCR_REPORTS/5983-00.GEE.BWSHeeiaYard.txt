In accordance with our proposal dated May 31 2007 we have performed a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the proposed storm water management plan
improvements located at Board of Water Supply Heeia yard in Kaneohe on the Island of

laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for
design input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at the Board of Water Supply Heeia yard along
Kamehameha Highway next to King Intermediate School in Kaneohe on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The planned improvements include a new concrete pad at the vehicle
wash area and new underground utility lines to divert all discharge to the sanitary sewer
system We anticipate that the existing asphalt concrete pavement will be partly
removed and replaced by Portland cement concrete pavement and new asphalt
concrete pavement A trench drain is planned along the entire length of the south edge
of the concrete pad Two utility lines are planned at the site to divert water into the
existing sewer manhole and drain inlet

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger

Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits
and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively
high energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered the
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes consisting of
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered the natural condition
of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a
valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and
deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older
alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial soil
deposits or by the soil like highly weathered basaltic lava flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban
development has since altered the natural formation of the area to its present form

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with

samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
In general our field exploration encountered 2 inches of asphalt concrete underlain
by stiff to hard alluvial deposits consisting of silty clay and clayey silt extending to the
maximum depths explored of approximately 15 to 16 feet below the existing ground
surface
We did not encounter groundwater in our borings at the time of our field exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
seasonal precipitation surface runoff and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
2 inches of asphalt concrete over stiff to hard alluvial deposits We recommend that the
concrete wash pad consist of 6 inches of Portland cement concrete over 6 inches of
compacted aggregate subbase course bearing on the stiff alluvial materials New
asphalt concrete pavement section should consist of 2 inches of asphalt concrete over
6 inches of aggregate base course We recommend giving special attention to the
transition between pavement sections Detailed discussions and our geotechnical
engineering design recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Vehicle Wash Pad
We understand the vehicle wash pad will be utilized by trucks in the maintenance
yard Based on the information provided we understand the vehicles that will utilize the
wash pad will consist of trucks weighing about 33 000 pounds Total number of vehicles
and frequency of the trucks using the wash pad were not available at the time this letter
report was prepared We assumed five trucks per day in our analysis Our laboratory test
results show that silty clay and clayey silt encountered near the ground surface exhibit
slightly to moderately expansive potential when subject to moisture fluctuation A design
CBR value of 8 was assumed in our design We anticipate that the vehicle wash pad will
bear on the in situ silts and clays We recommend using the following pavement section
for preliminary design purposes
Rigid Pavement Vehicle Wash Pad
6 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
The aggregate subbase course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The recommended pavement section assumes good drainage will be
provided for areas adjacent to the pavements
We understand that a trench drain will be constructed on the north edge of the
wash pad to collect the water from truck washing We recommend sloping the wash pad
and maintaining drainage gradients to carry surface water off the pad Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
We recommend compacting the subgrade soils to a minimum 95 percent relative
compaction The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Asphalt Concrete Pavement
We understand that a section of the existing asphalt concrete pavement will be
removed This section will be replaced by concrete wash pad and new asphalt concrete
pavement around the concrete wash pad We envision that the vehicle loading for the
proposed area will be same as that of the existing pavement
Based on our field observation the existing asphalt concrete pavement appears to
be in relatively good condition We assume that the vehicle loading will not change
significantly in the future Therefore we recommend that the new asphalt concrete
pavement section consist of 2 inches of asphalt concrete over 6 inches of aggregate base
course
We recommend compacting the subgrade soils to a minimum 95 percent relative
compaction The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during or
after construction
Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and
grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious materials
broken asphalt concrete and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
properly off site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination
of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated where
necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate subbase course
layer for rigid pavement or bottom of base course for flexible pavement The pavement
subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
AASHTO Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Soft and or yielding areas encountered at the bottom of the excavation should be
over excavated to expose stiff and or dense materials The resulting excavation may be

Backfilling of Excavations
The excavations will need to be properly backfilled The excavated on site
sandy silty gravel may be used as backfill above the cushion layer up to the finish
grades The backfill materials should have a maximum particle size of 3 inches The
soil backfill above the No 3B Fine gravel should be placed in thin lifts normally
8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM D 1557 The top 3 feet below the finished subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
If the excavation is too small for a compactor the excavation should be backfilled
with lean concrete or Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM
Utility Trenches
We anticipate that the utilities for the project will primarily consist of drain lines
connecting to the existing sewer manhole and drain inlet In general good construction
practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new
utilities The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench
excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the
excavations should comply with the applicable federal state and local safety
requirements The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and
installation
Based on the results of our field exploration the silty and clayey soils encountered
near ground surface are generally stiff to hard It appears that conventional excavation
methods generally may be utilized for utility line excavation
In general we recommend providing a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation under the pipes for support
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils with a maximum
particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in

Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage are especially critical
considering the slightly to moderately expansive nature of the surface soils Therefore the
pavement areas should be sloped to shed water away from the pavement structures and
to reduce the potential for ponding around the structure Excessive landscape watering
near the pavement structures should be avoided Planters next to pavement should also
be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive
water infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
pavement recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength Drainage should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
pavements
