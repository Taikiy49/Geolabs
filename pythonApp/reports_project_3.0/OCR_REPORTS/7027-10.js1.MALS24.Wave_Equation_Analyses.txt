In general accordance with the project contract documents, we performed wave 
equation analyses to evaluate whether the concrete piles may be driven to the specified 
pile tip elevations with the proposed equipment for the above project. The wave equation 
analyses were conducted based on the information provided by your office, the subsurface 
conditions contained in the referenced report, and project plans and specifications. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed “Design-Build” project involves the design and construction of a 
Composite Shop and a Marine Aviation Logistics Squadron (MALS) Ground Support 
Equipment (GSE) Holding Shed within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii at Kaneohe Bay on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
We understand that both the new Composite Shop and GSE Holding Shed will be 
supported on 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete piles, designed for an 
allowable compressive capacity of 75 tons with various tip elevations of -26 to -61 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) (Composite Shop) or Elevation -26 to -56 feet MSL (GSE Holding 
Shed) or to a termination refusal criterion.  
To examine pile drivability for proposed hammers, a wave equation analysis is 
required for each pile location where different subsurface conditions exist. Due to the

Hammer and Driving System 
We understand that a Delmag D30-23 single acting diesel hammer and/or Junttan 
HHK-7A hydraulic impact hammer is proposed for the pile driving operations. Based on the 
pile driving equipment data forms provided, the Delmag D30-23 hammer has a ram weight of 
6,600 pounds and a maximum stroke of 10.02 feet for a maximum rated energy of 
66,100 foot-pounds at the maximum pump setting. The Junttan HHK-7A hammer has a ram 
weight of 15,400 pounds for a maximum rated energy of 61,600 foot-pounds at a maximum 
stroke of 4 feet. 
A pile cushion consisting of at least 6 inches of plywood cushion block is proposed 
for the pile driving operations. The plywood cushion blocks generally have cross section 
areas of approximately 342 square inches for the 16.5-inch octagonal concrete piles. 
Schematic details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and Driving Equipment 
Data Forms in the Appendix (Pages 1 and 2).

Driving Stresses 
Graphs showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during 
driving are provided on Plates 2.1 through 2.4 for the four analyzed cases. Please note 
that the graphs were prepared based on kips per square inch (ksi), which is equivalent to 
1,000 psi. 
Based on our analyses, the compression stresses developed during pile driving did 
not exceed the allowable compression stresses of 4,200 psi for the full range of driving 
resistance for the Delmag D30-23 diesel hammer and Junttan HHK-7A hydraulic impact 
hammer, except when using the Junttan HHK-7A hydraulic impact hammer with a stroke 
height of 4 feet in Soil Profile No. 2. 
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving estimated for 16.5-inch 
octagonal concrete piles are less than the allowable tensile stress of 1,120 psi for the full 
range of driving resistance for the Delmag D30-23 diesel hammer and Junttan HHK-7A 
hydraulic hammer, except when using the Junttan HHK-7A hydraulic impact hammer with 
stroke heights between 3 and 4 feet in Soil Profile No. 1.

refined after the test pile program.

The pile driving operation may be terminated when the pile driving resistance is 
greater than 48 blows per foot and the pile driving penetration exceeds the 
specified minimum tip elevation.  
Junttan HHK-7A (2-Foot Stroke) 
The pile driving operation may be terminated when the pile driving resistance is 
greater than 38 blows per foot and the pile driving penetration exceeds the 
specified minimum tip elevation.  
Care should be carried out during pile driving, such as when pile driving resistance 
is low or within predrilled depth, to reduce the potential for pile damage and high pile 
tensile stress. The pump setting of the diesel hammer or stroke of hydraulic hammer 
should be carefully controlled to reduce the potential for pile damage and/or 
over-stressing.

                   ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS                      
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by         
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer.  The program is based on          
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving         
system, pile and soil under the hammer action.  Under certain conditions,        
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.          
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents        
normal situations.  In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the        
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order.  All of the input      
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time      
when the analysis is performed.  However, input data and therefore results       
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.                           
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.     
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or         
dynamic testing and measurements.  Estimates of bending or other local           
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the       
user.                                                                            
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,       
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity         
assessment of a driven pile.  Soil setup occurring after pile installation       
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those         
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation.  This    
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers.  The GRLWEAP user   
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow    
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil           
resistance and blow count during early restriking.                               
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values.  They MUST be reduced by    
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.      
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the           
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties      
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.

                   ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS                      
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by         
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer.  The program is based on          
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving         
system, pile and soil under the hammer action.  Under certain conditions,        
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.          
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents        
normal situations.  In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the        
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order.  All of the input      
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time      
when the analysis is performed.  However, input data and therefore results       
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.                           
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.     
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or         
dynamic testing and measurements.  Estimates of bending or other local           
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the       
user.                                                                            
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,       
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity         
assessment of a driven pile.  Soil setup occurring after pile installation       
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those         
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation.  This    
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers.  The GRLWEAP user   
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow    
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil           
resistance and blow count during early restriking.                               
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values.  They MUST be reduced by    
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.      
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the           
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties      
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.

                   ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS                      
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by         
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer.  The program is based on          
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving         
system, pile and soil under the hammer action.  Under certain conditions,        
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.          
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents        
normal situations.  In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the        
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order.  All of the input      
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time      
when the analysis is performed.  However, input data and therefore results       
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.                           
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.     
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or         
dynamic testing and measurements.  Estimates of bending or other local           
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the       
user.                                                                            
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,       
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity         
assessment of a driven pile.  Soil setup occurring after pile installation       
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those         
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation.  This    
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers.  The GRLWEAP user   
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow    
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil           
resistance and blow count during early restriking.                               
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values.  They MUST be reduced by    
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.      
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the           
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties      
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.

                   ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS                      
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by         
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer.  The program is based on          
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving         
system, pile and soil under the hammer action.  Under certain conditions,        
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.          
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents        
normal situations.  In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the        
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order.  All of the input      
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time      
when the analysis is performed.  However, input data and therefore results       
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.                           
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.     
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or         
dynamic testing and measurements.  Estimates of bending or other local           
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the       
user.                                                                            
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,       
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity         
assessment of a driven pile.  Soil setup occurring after pile installation       
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those         
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation.  This    
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers.  The GRLWEAP user   
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow    
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil           
resistance and blow count during early restriking.                               
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values.  They MUST be reduced by    
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.      
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the           
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties      
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.
