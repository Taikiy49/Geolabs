SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff clayey soils extending 
to the maximum depths explored. The project site is generally covered by the existing 
pavement supported on thin man-made surface fills underlain by residual and saprolitic 
soils. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend using 
shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings bearing on 
recompacted in-situ soils or on engineered fill materials to support the new McDonald’s 
building. 
The near-surface soils at the site generally consist of silty clay and clayey silt soils 
of moderate to high plasticity. Therefore, the near-surface soils are susceptible to shrinking 
and swelling when subjected to changes in soil moisture content. In order to reduce the 
potential for future distress to the slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of 
the clayey soils due to changes in moisture content, we recommend providing a minimum 
of 12 inches of non-expansive, select granular fill material below the building slab and 
sidewalk areas. The select fill should extend beyond the footprint of the new slab-on-grade 
and sidewalk areas by a minimum of 24 inches. 
The near-surface soils at the site (and in the general area) are susceptible to 
heaving and/or loss of strength when saturated. Therefore, special attention should be 
given to the drainage design at the site. Proper surface drainage and reduction of water 
infiltration into the subsurface are integral components to the recommendations provided 
in this report.  Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils 
and new fill materials are critical elements of earthwork, Geolabs should perform 
observations and soil density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining 
the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the McDonald’s Mililani Mauka project in the Mililani area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, review of 
in-house archival information, and engineering analyses.  These recommendations are 
intended only for the design of site grading, building foundations, slabs-on-grade, 
retaining structures, and pavements. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below the Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lava flows from the Koolau Volcano 
ponded against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late 
Pleistocene Epoch.  The dips of the lava beds are near horizontal (between 3 and 
5 degrees from horizontal).  The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in-situ 
weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that 
grade to saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is mainly composed of silty material 
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  Spheroidally weathered 
boulders are typical in the Schofield Plateau.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock 
formation with depth.  The project site is on the Schofield Plateau and is underlain by fill, 
residual soils, and saprolite.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff clayey soils 
extending to the maximum depths explored.  In general, the thin surface man-made fills 
were underlain by residual and saprolitic soils. We did not encounter groundwater in the 
borings at the time of our exploration.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered 
at the project site, we recommend using shallow foundations consisting of spread 
and/or continuous footings bearing on recompacted in-situ soils or on engineered fill 
materials to support the new building. 
The near-surface soils at the site generally consist of silty clay soils of moderate 
to high plasticity. Therefore, the near-surface soils are somewhat susceptible to 
shrinking and swelling when subjected to changes in soil moisture content. To reduce 
the potential for future distress to the slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and 
swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in moisture content, we recommend 
providing a minimum of 12 inches of non-expansive, select granular fill material below 
the building slab and sidewalk areas.  The select fill should extend beyond the footprint 
of the new slab-on-grade and sidewalk areas by a minimum of two (2) feet. 
The near-surface soils at the site (and in the general area) are susceptible to 
heaving and/or loss of strength when saturated. Therefore, special attention should be 
given to the design of drainage at the site. Proper surface drainage and reduction of 
water infiltration into the subsurface are integral components to the recommendations 
provided herein.  Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey 
subgrade soils and new fill materials are critical elements of earthwork, Geolabs should 
perform observations and soil density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in 
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each 
fill lift.

3.1 
Building Foundations 
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, 
we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support the new 
McDonald’s building. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square 
foot (psf) may be used to design shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted 
in-situ soils or engineered fills.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the 
lowest adjacent finished grades.  For footings bearing on the in-situ soils or engineered 
fills, the bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction prior to placing reinforcing steel or concrete. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope.   
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from the 
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the 
recompacted in-situ clayey soils or engineered fills may be on the order of less than

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We envision that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the new structure.  
Based on our field exploration and our laboratory test results, the project site is 
generally underlain by near-surface silty clays with low to moderate expansion potential 
when subjected to moisture-fluctuations. To reduce the potential for future distress to 
the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the silty clays 
due to changes in the moisture content, we recommend capping the on-site clayey soils 
with a minimum of 12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill (capping fill) material 
below the slab cushion. 
Prior to placement of the capping fill, we recommend scarifying the subgrade 
soils to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioning to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction 
(ASTM D 1557).  It should be noted that proper moisture-conditioning and preparation 
of the clayey subgrade soils is critical to the performance of the foundation and slab 
system.

The underlying subgrade soils should be kept moist until the final placement of 
slab concrete.  Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, 
we recommend preparing the soils again as recommended.  Saturation and subsequent 
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may 
require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. 
A cushion fill consisting of a minimum 4-inch thick layer of No. 3 Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) should be placed below the slabs.  The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a 
capillary moisture break.  To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through 
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is 
recommended on top of the No. 3 Fine gravel cushion fill layer.  Flexible floor coverings, 
such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered since they can better mask minor 
slab cracking.   
We recommend supporting exterior concrete walkways on at least 6 inches of 
select borrow subbase or non-expansive, select granular fill material.  The select borrow 
subbase or non-expansive, select granular fill material should be compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Control joints should be provided at 
intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints provided at right-angle 
intersections. 
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at 
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Areas adjacent to the slab 
should be backfilled tightly against the slab with the on-site soil and graded to divert 
water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs.

3.3.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material 
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered 
fill.  The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site or 
used in landscaping areas, if appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas receiving fill and finished subgrades in cut areas 
should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement should be increased 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in areas subject to vehicular traffic. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after preparation of the subgrade, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks and then 
recompacting.  Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to 
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas 
and replacement with engineered fill.

3.3.2 Fills and Backfills 
The on-site soils generated from the cut areas may be re-used as a source of 
general fill material.  Imported general fill materials should consist of soil materials 
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or less with sufficient fines (between 10 
and 80 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve) to prevent the occurrence of 
voids in the compacted mass.  In addition, imported general fill materials should 
have a CBR value of 12 or greater and a swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
Non-expansive select granular material should consist of crushed basalt or coral.  
The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The material should have a laboratory CBR 
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less.  Select 
borrow and aggregate base course should meet the requirement as specified in 
Sections 30 and 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, 
Department of Public Works, City and County of Honolulu, September 1986. 
Geolabs should test imported fill material for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use. 
3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
In general, fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The non-expansive select granular fill, base course, and select borrow subbase, 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.3.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe 
that cut slopes (if any) will expose stiff soils.  In general, cut slopes may be 
designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
If permanent fill slopes are constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill 
materials), these may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be 
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a 
well-compacted slope face.  In addition, slope planting should be provided as soon 
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

side of the structure, a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 
33 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the 
structure, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be used in design.  For retaining 
structures that are restrained, a rectangular distribution equal to 50 percent of the 
vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the structures may be 
used for design.  Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the 
surcharge effects of point loads and line loads. 
In general, a subdrain is recommended behind the retaining wall structure to 
collect and discharge excess water that may infiltrate behind the wall structure. A 
typical subdrain would consist of a perforated pipe (with perforations down) 
enclosed by at least 12 inches of permeable drainage material, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation).  The perforated pipe should 
be directed to discharge into a proper drainage facility.  The permeable drainage 
material should be wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent.  Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill 
should consist of relatively impervious material (compacted on-site soils) to 
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining wall. 
3.4.3 Backfill Materials 
We recommend that the backfill material for the retaining walls consist of imported 
select granular materials.  The material should be well graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension.  The material should 
also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The 
material should have a CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent 
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
Backfill materials should be placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness and be compacted to at least 90 percent of the maximum dry

3.5 
Site Seismic Classification 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain predominantly by 
saprolite, consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays.  Based on this information, the project 
site may be classified under an SD soil profile, “stiff soil” based on the International 
Building Code, 2006. 
3.6 
Liquefaction Potential 
Liquefaction occurs when a build-up of excess pore water pressure is induced on 
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength.  The most 
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes.  This 
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil, and if not 
confined, can result in significant deformations.  Loose, uniformly graded, fine grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction.  Generally, 
liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the 
ground surface. The greater overburden pressure can be sufficient to prevent 
liquefaction in deeper deposits. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by stiff to very stiff 
cohesive soils.  Additionally, the natural groundwater table is greater than 50 feet below 
the site.  Therefore, we believe that there is no potential for liquefaction at this project 
site.

foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength.  The foundation excavations

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, to depths of about 11.5 to 21.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on Plate A.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D 2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.  Results 
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear Tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the strength parameters such as angle of friction and cohesion. 
Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-2 and B-3 
Five one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on either undisturbed or remolded 
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures.  These 
tests were run on air-dried samples.  Swell test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
One California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement 
subgrade support.  CBR test results are presented on Plate B-5.
