In accordance with your request, we performed a geotechnical engineering 
exploration in support of the design of the proposed Humuhumu Restaurant Renovation

This letter report presents our geotechnical recommendations for design of the proposed

The geotechnical recommendations presented herein are based on our site visit

limitations noted at the end of this letter report.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located within the Grand Wailea Resort in Wailea on the Island 
of Maui, Hawaii. The Humuhumu Restaurant Renovation project consists of renovating 
the existing bar area and replacing it with an aquarium built within a new bar. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on structural loading provided by WSP, an allowable bearing pressure of 
1,500 to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) was used for design of the new aquarium bar 
supports. The project’s structural drawings reference previous recommendations 
submitted by our office for the overall Grand Wailea Resort Improvements project. 
However, our previous exploration did not include the Humuhumu Restaurant project 
area, and our geotechnical recommendations were not intended for use at the

We explored the subsurface conditions by performing DCP testing at six selected 
locations, designated as DCP-1 through DCP-6, to provide data and information for our

evaluation and analyses. The approximate DCP test locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The DCP test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ resistance to penetration. 
The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground by repeatedly striking it with 
a 17.6-lb (8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 22.6 inches (575 mm). The penetration 
of the cone is measured after each blow and is recorded to provide a continuous measure 
of shear resistance. Test results can be correlated to Standard Penetration Test (SPT) 
blow counts, California Bearing Ratio (CBR), in-situ density, resilient modulus, and 
bearing capacity. 
At the time of our site visit, the existing subgrade at the proposed aquarium bar 
area had been excavated, exposing soil consisting of tan sand. The DCP tests extended 
to depths of about 3 to 7.4 feet below the existing ground surface at the test locations. All 
DCP tests were terminated once refusal conditions were encountered. Field logs of the 
DCP tests and the correlated SPT blow counts are included on Plates 3.1 through 8.3 of 
this letter report for reference. Based on the correlated blow counts and our field 
observations, the subsurface conditions within the Humuhumu bar area consisted of very 
loose to loose materials extending to depths of approximately 1.5 to 2.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The very loose to loose materials generally graded to medium 
dense to very dense materials extending to the maximum depth tested of approximately 
7.4 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Two bulk samples, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained near the 
ground surface to evaluate the existing fill soils to determine acceptability for reuse below 
the new slab-on-grade. Laboratory testing consisting of sieve analysis, maximum dry 
density, and laboratory CBR was performed on each bulk sample. Laboratory test results 
are presented on Plates 9 though 13.  
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the current plans, we understand that the Humuhumu Restaurant 
Renovation project consists of renovating the existing bar area and replacing it with an 
aquarium built within a new bar. Confirmation that the subsurface conditions below the 
proposed bar footings are adequate to achieve the design bearing pressure is desired. In 
addition, we understand it is desired to evaluate the existing subgrade materials for re-use 
under the new slab-on-grade. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the project 
are presented in the following sections. 
Bearing Capacity Evaluation 
Based on available plans and our field observations, the existing Humuhumu bar 
was constructed on a circular concrete retaining wall, approximately 18 to 20 feet in 
diameter, extending approximately 5 feet above the existing pond elevation. The interior 
of the retaining wall was backfilled with fill materials up to the bottom of the existing floor 
slab elevation. Based on our observations, the top 1 to 2 feet of backfill material consisted

of tan, poorly-graded sand. The fill material likely extends at least 5 feet below the existing 
top of slab elevation. An existing concrete footing (measuring approximately 2.5 feet by 
2.5 feet) supports the roof structure at the center of the retaining wall.  
Based on the field DCP testing and laboratory test results, we believe that an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
design of the new slab-on-grade foundations bearing on the recompacted fill materials. 
This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-
third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. The fill 
materials should be compacted in accordance with the Site Grading section of this letter 
report.  
Lateral loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by frictional resistance 
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive 
earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. An 
allowable coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the medium 
dense to dense sandy soils. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated 
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). 
The passive earth pressure value assumes that the soils around footings are 
well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance 
in the upper 12 inches of the soils should be neglected.

Based on the proposed finished grades, relatively minor site grading will be 
required to achieve design grades. The following grading items are addressed in the 
succeeding subsections:

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Debris, deleterious materials, existing structures, 
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-
site. 
Based on the results of the DCP testing, we recommend removing all existing 
backfill to the bottom of the existing roof structure footing elevation. Care should

be taken while excavating to prevent over excavation and undermining of the 
existing roof footing. Once the excavation is complete, a Geolabs representative 
should evaluate the exposed subgrade to determine if additional excavation is 
necessary.  
The excavated areas to be filled should be scarified to a depth of about 6 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90% relative compaction. Scarification should not be performed within 
2 feet of the existing footing to prevent disturbance of the existing compacted soil 
below the footing. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. Based on our laboratory test results, 
we believe the exposed subgrade should be compacted with a vibratory plate 
compactor.  
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and 
poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil 
conditions should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs representative. 
Fills and Backfills 
Based on the laboratory test results, we envision the excavated granular on-site 
soils may be reused as a source of fill materials provided that the materials are 
free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and clay lumps and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported fill materials should consist 
of non-expansive select granular materials, such as crushed coral or basalt. The 
material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension. The material also should contain between 10% and 
30% particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have a 
laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1% or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Geolabs should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 6 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 90% relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the 
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density 
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry

density. Compaction should be accomplished by using vibratory plate compactors, 
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
The placement and compaction of the fill materials are critical elements of the 
earthwork. Therefore, Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests 
during the backfilling operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the required 
degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift. Where 
compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be applied 
with adjustment in the moisture content as necessary, to obtain the specified 
compaction.  
Underground Utility Lines 
New underground utility lines, such as water, drain lines, and electrical lines, may 
be required for the project. We anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be 
excavated in the recompacted onsite soils consisting of poorly graded sand. 
In general, the existing onsite sand may be used as backfill below and around the 
proposed utilities. Alternatively, a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform 
bearing support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
12 inches above the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the 
potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and 
improper placement of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of onsite soils or imported 
non-expansive select granular materials less than 3 inches in maximum particle size. The 
backfill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 
6-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to at least 90% relative compaction.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon limited 
information obtained from the DCP testing in the field. Variations of subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the DCP test locations may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided 
herein. 
The field DCP test locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by having been obtained field measurements off known features. Elevations 
noted on the DCP test field logs were estimated in the field based on information provided 
by Nordic PCL Construction, Inc. The physical locations of the DCP test locations should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
