Based on our field exploration program conducted for the project the project site
appears to be underlain by highly variable subsurface conditions across the footprint of the
future store building Weak and compressible soil deposits were encountered in the upper
16 feet of the subsurface in Boring No 3 and between about 16 and 53 feet of the
subsurface in Boring No 7 However these soft and or loose compressible soil deposits
were generally absent in the upper subsurface within Boring and 9 drilled across
the future store building footprint
Based on our field exploration and the structural loads column loads of about 24 to
80 kips and wall loads up to about 5 kips per linear foot provided by the project structural
engineer we believe the relatively thin upper fill crust in localized areas and the
underlying compressible soil deposits would not provide adequate foundation support for
the proposed building without appreciable settlements under the building loads
static conditions In order to reduce the settlements under static loading conditions we
recommend over excavating a portion of the compressible deposits at the footing locations
and replacing with structural fill Generally the depth of over excavation would be on the
order of about 2 to 3 feet below the bottom of footing elevations as discussed further in this
report For the footing supported on the 2 to 3 feet of structural fill we estimate the total
footing settlements under the anticipated design loads static conditions to be on the order
of about 1 5 inches Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on the
structural fill under static loading conditions should be on the order of about 0 75 to 1 inch
Our analyses also indicated that the project site could be subjected to appreciable
ground settlement on the order of 1 to 5 inches in the event of liquefaction during a
moderate earthquake M6 with a peak ground acceleration of 0 18g Therefore the
owner should be aware that the shallow foundation support system recommended herein
may experience appreciable settlement and the building may sustain some damage that
would disrupt the operation of the store If the estimated foundation settlement during a
moderate seismic event is not acceptable consideration should be given to the use of a
deep foundation system for support of the proposed Target Store building

SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct
while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the
Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulated reef
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits The project site is situated
adjacent to Kawainui Marsh where the subsurface soil deposits are dominated by
lagoonal deposits and coralline soil deposits Land development and reclamation
projects within the last century have brought the project area to its present form

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage

Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by
earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to
vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur
rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress
internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During
liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and
vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the site is underlain by beach sands coralline detritus materials and
lagoonal deposits at varying depths below the existing pavement surface The
medium dense sands and gravel and soft silty clays at the project site are generally
not susceptible to liquefaction However the beach deposits and coralline detritus
materials consisting of loose sands and gravel with little cohesion are potentially
liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6 with an associated peak ground
acceleration of 0 18g
Based on these conditions it appears that portions of the loose granular soil
deposits at the project site may have factors of safety of less than 1 0 against

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
eleven borings designated as Boring 14 16 and 17 The
borings extended to depths of about 11 5 to 102 5 feet below the existing pavement
surface In addition six shallow borings designated as SB 1 through SB 6 were drilled
to a depth of about 2 feet below the existing pavement surface for collection of bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate their pavement support characteristics and
potential use as fill materials for the project The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck or
trailer mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our engineer geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 17
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 2
Sixteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B 3
through B 6
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D 2850
were performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear
strengths of the in situ soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures
were used as the applied confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil
samples The test results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 7
through B 9
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plates B 10 through B 12
Two Consolidation tests with time rates ASTM D 2435 were performed on
samples of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered The test results are presented on
Plates B 13 and B 14
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils Results of the CBR tests were also used to evaluate the
quality of the materials tested for use as fills The test results are presented on
Plates B 15 through B 18

Seismic Piezocone Penetration Tests
Geolabs Inc conducted cone penetration testing using the seismic piezocone
penetration testing equipment at nine probe locations designated as 4 6
through 11 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D 5778 Standard
Test Method for Performing Electronic Friction Cone and Piezocone Penetration Testing
of Soils The probe extended to depths of about 32 5 to 121 2 feet below the existing
pavement surface using seismic piezocone penetration testing equipment SCPTU
before refusal conditions to our system were encountered
The seismic piezocone penetration test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone Serial Number DSG1063 manufactured by Vertek
with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1 55 square inches 10 square
centimeters connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 23 25 square inches
150 square centimeters into the ground at a rate of approximately 20 mm second
5 mm second using a Mobile B 80 truck mounted drill rig
The tip and side friction resistance and pore pressure induced immediately
behind the cone tip designated as the U2 parameter during penetration are measured
by strain gauges within the instrument and are recorded versus the depth of penetration
at about 2 inch 50 millimeter intervals When the material encountered was too hard for
further penetration by the SCPTU equipment which might cause potential equipment
damage penetration was halted and the cone was withdrawn
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone with an embedded inclinometer
to detect the arrival of a shear wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic
measurements are made when the SCPTU has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the
subsurface A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank in
opposite directions with a switched hammer at approximately 6 foot intervals The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
The field data collected were subsequently processed in the office using the
computer software program CPT Coneplot developed by Vertek Logs of the seismic
plots include cone tip resistance friction ratio sleeve friction over tip resistance pore
pressure local friction shear wave velocity soil behavior based on the Rf zone and
interpreted soil description Values of the tip resistance friction ratio and pore pressure
may be correlated to soil strength and classification The center of the friction sleeve is
approximately 4 inches 10 cm behind the cone tip To calculate the friction ratio the
average friction resistance and tip resistance are compared at the same depth This

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Tests
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was performed
using Seismic Piezocone Penetration Testing SCPTu equipment Shear wave velocity
testing using seismic piezocone penetration test equipment was performed at one
selected boring location designated as B 9 SW as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at approximately 6 foot
intervals extending to a depth of about 124 7 feet below the existing pavement surface
In order to conduct the shear wave velocity test in the boring the test boring generally
was advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring Logs
of the materials encountered in the boring are presented in the Logs of Borings in
Appendix A After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole the
bored hole was backfilled with 0 25 inch diameter coated bentonite pellets The
temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the
bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward When the bentonite
pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the borehole the pellets start to hydrate
slowly As the bentonite pellets hydrate they swell and soften The probe was then
pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a depth of about 124 7 feet below
the existing pavement surface using the seismic piezocone penetration testing
equipment SCPTU
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone into the bentonite backfilled borehole The cone has an
apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected surface area of 1 55 square inches
10 square centimeters equipped with an electronic depth sensor attached to the cone
rod The seismic cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a
shear wave generated and propagated from the ground surface Seismic measurements
are made when the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A
shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched
hammer The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was
measured at each discrete depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided
by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths provided the
average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
The shear wave velocities measured and the average shear wave velocity
calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the boring location are presented on
100 feet of the soil profile were calculated based on the average shear wave velocity
method described in Section of the International Building Code 2003 Edition
