INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have performed a preliminary
geologic reconnaissance of the above referenced site This report
presents our findings and recommendations
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our reconnaissance was to make observations of the
general surface soils and rock conditions at the project site in order to
obtain data to aid in evaluating the developability of the site from a
geotechnical engineering viewpoint
The reconnaissance consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1
A review of available soils and geologic maps of the site and its
vicinity
A review of available soils and geologic literature pertinent to
the site and its vicinity
A general geologic reconnaissance of the vicinity of the project
site including visual observations of nearby road cuts and
conversations with local residents
An on ground reconnaissance of the site performed by a Project
Geologist and a Staff Geologist from our firm

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the Ouli section of the District of
South Kohala on the island of Hawaii and encompasses approximately 485
acres The site is divided by the Queen Kaahumanu Highway into two areas
the upper mauka area and the lower makai area
To the east of the highway the mauka area is bordered by Waiulaula
Gulch to the north raw undeveloped land to the south and east and Queen
Kaahumanu Highway to the west
The western makai area is bordered to north by the golf course of the
existing Mauna Kea Beach Hotel to the south by Hapuna State Beach Park
to the west by the northern part of Hapuna Bay and to the east by Queen
Kaahumanu Highway
The terrain is generally rolling with some steep slopes locally
approaching a ratio of 1 1 H V The terrain typically reflects the
original surface of thick basaltic lava flows in the A a form The ground
surface elevations range from about sea level to approximately 600 feet
above sea level with drainage trending towards the west see Photo 1
The site is vegetated with a thin cover of dry grasses with kiawe
algaroba trees occurring in the lower areas near Hapuna Bay and in the
drainageways
Most of the ground surface is covered with basaltic cobbles and gravel
mantling an ashy soil There are localized exposures of basalt clinker
and rock formation Photo 2

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the site into a planned resort residential
and recreational development The resort portion of the development is
currently planned to be a hotel facility in the makai portion of the site
on a low bluff overlooking Hapuna Bay The remainder of the makai area is
planned as golf course Photos 4 5 and 6
It is proposed to develop the mauka area primarily as residential and
golf course with other recreational facilities and maintenance support
within the area
CLIMATIC CONDITIONS
The project site and its vicinity may be considered to be situated in
an arid climate with the average annual rainfall on the order of 5 to 10
inches per year State of Hawaii 1970 The average temperature varies
between 75 and 85 degrees Fahrenheit State of Hawaii 1970 The area is
frequently subjected to strong tradewinds forced through the wind gap
between the Kohala Mountains and Mauna Kea It is common for winds in the
vicinity of the site develop a typical land sea convection cell with
tradewind breezes in the morning which turn into onshore breezes in the
afternoon as the air over the land mass becomes heated and rises

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene
Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally
referred to as Pahala Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from
Pahala Ash found in other portions of the island of Hawaii in that the
in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a dry
friable state
The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has
resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the
site This desert pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel
which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away see foreground of
Photo 2
At and in the area of the project site the lava flows are generally
oceanite basalt with a high percentage of well developed olivine and
augite crystals The flows are in the A a form which is characterized by
a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain by a
dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less
continuous layer of clinker below the massive core The clinker surface
of the lavas at the site have been mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed
above
In parts of the main drainageways of the site the ash soil and
clinker have been stripped by erosion exposing the underlying dense rock
see center of Photo 2

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Visual examination of the project site and its environs including
nearby road cuts leads to the conjecture that the subsurface conditions
throughout the site are relatively uniform
Underlying the surficial desert pavement is a layer of yellowish brown
to reddish brown soil Pahala Ash This layer is predominately a dry
low plasticity silt which contains occasional gravel and cobbles The
silt has very little cohesion and once exposed is subject to wind and
water erosion The thickness of the silt layer generally appears to vary
from 2 to 5 feet Photo 7
Based on visual examination of road cuts and previous experience in
the area the ash soil usually grades into a layer of volcanic clinker
which generally ranges from 3 to 8 feet in thickness This clinker
material consists predominantly of cobble and gravel sized scoraceous
basalt fragments and represents the original surface of A a flows The
clinker also appears to contain many accretionary lava balls boulders up
to 5 feet in diameter Photo 8 Exposures of the clinker are weakly to
moderately cemented
Below the ash and clinker layers are the cores of the individual A a
flows These cores are generally composed of dense fractured to massive
basalt rock The flow cores appear to range in thickness from about 3
feet to as much as 20 feet
During our reconnaissance no flow of water was noted in any of the
drainageways or gulches at the project site nor were any signs of surface
seepage observed However the growth of kiawe trees and green grasses in
some of the drainage courses may be indicative of higher in situ soil
moisture contents and possible seepage Photos 6 and 9

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our reconnaissance of the area our preliminary findings and
recommendations are presented below
General
Our reconnaissance indicates the the site is suitable for the proposed
development from a geotechnical engineering viewpoint
The in situ volcanic formation observed at the project site is
generally competent and should provide adequate bearing for the proposed
development With the exception of the ash soil which could be
stockpiled for use as topsoil excavated volcanic materials should
generally provide a suitable source of fill material if properly handled
Site Grading
In general it is our opinion that more or less conventional grading
methods may be used for the site and roadway grading of the proposed
development It should be understood that customary grading practices on
the western side of the island of Hawaii are usually modified somewhat due
to the rocky nature of the material
Prior to commencement of grading surface vegetation and organic
matter should be cleared and grubbed The resulting spoils should be
disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping
Ash soils generally have relatively low strength and if the in situ
moisture contents are high enough they are thixotropic i e they lose
strength when remolded Therefore it is our recommendation that the ash
soil be removed from fill roadway and structural areas The ash soil may
be either stockpiled for use as topsoil or thoroughly blended into
rockfill material as discussed below

Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture
conditioning of fill material will be necessary It is also advisable to
institute a dust control program during grading operations
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should
be overexcavated to firm natural material and the resulting depression
backfilled with well compacted fill
The existing miscellaneous rubbish fill should be removed and wasted
off site
In general the excavated soil and rock materials less than 12 inches
in size may be suitable as fill material The excavated rock material
may not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material
therefore the surficial ash soil should be mixed into the rocky excavated
material to increase the percentage of fines and to create a better graded
fill material
Generally fills should be placed in level lifts and compacted to 90
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Conventional
compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to determine the
number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the
project On the basis of this testing program the number of passes may
then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
All fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than 5 1
horizontal to vertical should be keyed and benched into the underlying
consolidated clinker or basalt formation to lessen potential instability
of the new fill see Plate 4 The keys and benches should not be based
in the upper ash soil or in loose clinker

Excavation
The field reconnaissance indicates that the site is most likely
underlain by massive dense basalt at relatively shallow depths
It is anticipated that most of the ash and clinker materials may be
conventionally excavated Some hard excavation and or ripping may be
encountered in localized well cemented areas or when large accretionary
lava balls boulders are encountered
The underlying basalt formation will most likely require some hard
excavation such as heavy ripping hoerams or possibly blasting
Slopes
Provided the granular materials 12 inch minus are utilized for
recommended If fine grained soils are used we recommend flatter slopes
ratio of 2 1 H V
For cut slopes the following ratios are recommended

Any cuts or fills greater than 20 feet in height should have a bench
at the midpoint of the slope
