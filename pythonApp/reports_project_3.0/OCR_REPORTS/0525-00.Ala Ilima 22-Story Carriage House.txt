INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our foundation investi
gation for the proposed 22 story apartment building which is
located between Ala Ilima and Likini Streets in the Salt
Lake district of Oahu Hawaii
The scope of our work was to evaluate the subsurface condi
tions and develop recommendations for use in design of
foundations and their probable behavior

W
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is presently planned to construct a 22 story reinforced
concrete structure approximately 68 by 36 feet in planned
dimension A partial basement area will be located on the
lower Likini Street side Maximum anticipated column loads
will be on the order of 400 kips Bearing wall loads will
be on the order of 40 kips per lineal foot Parking will be
on grade on the upper and lower levels A cut on the order
approximately 6 to 8 feet will be required for the partial
basement area
SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The site which is approximately 120 by 200 feet in planned
dimensions is bounded on the south by Ala Ilima and on the
north by Likini Street The site is split level with an
approximate five foot difference in elevation The lower
level is on the Likini Street side and the upper level on the
Ala Ilima Street side
At the time of our field investigation a portion of the site
was used as a storage area for construction materials

Geology
Geologicly the site is underlain by bedded volcanic tuffs
from the Salt Lake crater These tuffs are members of the
Honolulu volcanic series Late in Oahu s geologic history
hot magma rising in fissures along the coast encountered sea
water The subsequent hydromagmatic explosions generated a
series of tuff cinder and breccia volcanic cones Salt Lake
crater is one of these cones The ash and rock fragments
were often welded together by heat to become fairly resistant
rock which is locally known as mudrock Much of the origi
nal pore space in the rock was blocked by welding palagoni
zation causing Salt Lake to act as a large reservoir Rain
water carrying sediment and dissolved minerals were deposited
in this basin Dissolved carbonates and salt were generally
deposited during periods of dessication and possibly by
calcareous algae Later a lakeside marsh apparently covered
the site Within the last eight years a man made fill con
sisting of silty sands and mudrock was placed over the lake
side deposits The thickness of this man made fill is
approximately 15 feet at the site

Subsurface
In order to investigate the subsurface condition at the site
three borings were drilled The locations of these borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 A more detailed
description of the field exploration ahd laboratory testing
is presented in the Appendix which also contains a log of
boring and the results of our laboratory tests
The site is immediately underlain by about 15 feet of fill
that is composed of silty sands and tuff fragments and cobbles
Some boulders are found throughout this fill The fill place
ment was performed approximately seven to eight years ago
We have obtained copies of reports for the density tests that
were taken at various elevations throughout the fill by
another firm The general condition of the fill appears to
be that it was fairly well compacted The placement of the
fill was not continuously supervised therefore may contain
local softer or less compacted areas Underlying the man
made fill is an approximate 10 foot layer of a dense silt
that is slightly cemented Underlying this formation is a
very hard and dense bedded tuff formation This material was
encountered to the depths explored

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
LT
Foundations
Our study indicates that the proposed high rise structure
may be supported on strip or spread footings bearing in the
compacted fill The fill overlying the site is dense and
well compacted and may contain some local uncompacted or loose
areas It is our opinion that shallow strips or spread
footings may be designed for total imposed dead plus live
load up to Footings should be placed a minimum of
3 feet below final adjacent grade Anticipated total settle
ment may range up to one half inch Because of this inherant
nature of fill the design should anticipate differential
settlements between footings on the order of one fourth inch
A one third increase in the recommended bearing value may be
used when considering short term wind or seismic loads

W
Lateral Pressures
Lateral loads may be resisted by the passive resistance of the
soils and their friction A coefficient of friction of 0 4
may be used between the supporting soils and footings The
passive resistance of the compacted granular fill at founda
tion level may be considered to be equal to an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 psf
Basement retaining walls on the lower level should be designed
to resist lateral active earth pressures Laterally restrained
walls that will be backfilled after being tied to the struc
ture should be designed to resist an equivalent fluid weight
of 55 pounds per cubic foot Yielding walls although back
filled prier to connection to the structure should be designed
to resist a fluid density of 35 pounds per cubic foot The
backfill immediately against the wall should be an open graded
compacted granular material An adequate drainage system
should be provided behind the wall to prevent buildup of
excessive pore pressures The excavation behind the retain
ing wall may be cut near vertical up to a maximum height of
10 feet This excavation phase should be inspected by the
Soil Engineer to verify that local planes of weakness or
excessively loose material do not exist

compacted granular fill similar to the surface soil existing

INVESTIGATION LIMITATIONS
The materials encountered on the project site and utilized in
our laboratory investigation are believed representative of
the total area However soil materials may vary in charac
teristics between boring locations Should other soil condi

tions be encountered during construction the soil engineer
issuing this report should be consulted and the appropriate
construction modification implemented if necessary Due to
the possible variations that may be encountered in the
existing fill we recommend that all foundation excavation
be inspected and approved by a soil engineer prior to place
ment of steel or concrete to assure that the subsurface
conditions encountered during construction are of comparable
competency as those encountered during this investigation
and upon which the recommendations presented in this report
are based If loose soil or debris materials are encountered
they may have to be replaced and compacted with competent
material Any overexcavation should be replaced with founda
tion concrete We also recommend that including preparation
of pavement subgrade and base and backfilling of utility
lines be inspected and tested by a soils engineer

APPENDIX
Field Exploration
The subsurface soil conditions at the site were investigated
by drilling three test borings utilizing truck mounted rotary
auger equipment The depths of the borings extended to 40
feet below the existing ground surface The boring locations
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 in the text of this
report
The drilling operations were continuously supervised by our
field geologist who identified and classified the materials
encountered in the borings and maintained a continuous boring
log The classifications were later verified by inspection
and testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in
accordance with the Unified Classification Soil System and
rock with a local geology geologic terminology Graphical
representations of the encountered materials are presented on
the borings logs Plates A 1A to A 1C Undisturbed soil
samples were bbtained by driving a 2 4 I D sampler with a
140 pound hammer free falling a distance of 30 inches
Disturbed samples were obtained by driving a standard pene
tration sampler The blow counts required to drive the sampler

Laboratory Testing
Attempts were made to determine the in place characteristics
of the man made fill materials However due to the high con
tent of rock fragments it was difficult to obtain adequate
undisturbed samples for strength testing Correlations of
strength with blow counts were made with data of similar
materials obtained from adjacent site investigation by
Geolabs Hawaii
To aid in the classification of the site materials for grain
size analysis were made of representative samples The
results of these tests are shown on Plates Bl Grain Size
Analysis In place moisture and density determination were
made on undisturbed samples where ever possible Only moisture
determinations were made on disturbed samples The results
of these test are presented on the enclosed boring logs
Atterberg Limits were made on the finer grain materials to aid
in classification and to determine other physical character
istics of the site materials The results of these tests are
presented on the log of borings at the appropriate sample
locations
