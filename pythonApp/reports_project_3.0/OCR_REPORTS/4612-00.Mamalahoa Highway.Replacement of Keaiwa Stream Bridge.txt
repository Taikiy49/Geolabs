INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the Mamalahoa Highway Emergency Replacement of Keaiwa Stream Bridge project
located along Route 11 in the District of Kau on the Island of Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be dormant and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both are still active The project site is situated on the southeast
flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
The volume of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano extending from the ocean floor is
estimated to be on the order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest
volcanic mountain on earth Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that
Mauna Loa may have been forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is actually
composed of two separate shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter having been
covered by Mauna Loa lavas left only a few localized surface expressions visible at present
The Mauna Loa shield has been built by volcanic eruptions along two principal rift zones that
extend southwestward and east northeastward from the summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consist of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Kau Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age and
the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash deposit
identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and interbedded
within the rocks of the Kau Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically occur as kipukas
isolated deposits with some significant accumulations ranging up to about 30 feet in
thickness
Pahala Ash is a low plasticity silt derived from the weathering of ash cinder and
Pele s Hair Pahala Ash generally has low shear strength and when the moisture content
is high enough it loses significant strength when it is remolded becomes thixotropic
Therefore the typical Pahala Ash may be potentially liquefiable during seismic events

The project site is located on land that has been mapped by others as covered with
Kau Basalt Holocene to Pleistocene age at the ground surface The map symbols k and
k3 that are shown on the Geologic Map Plate 3 identify the general location of the lava
flows The Kau Volcanic Series unit k3 essentially consists of lava flows composed of
tholeiitic basalt that were deposited approximately 750 to 1 500 years ago The Kau Volcanic
Series unit k also consists of tholeiitic basalt mixed with some voicanic ash and was
deposited in excess of 10 000 years ago making it the older geologic unit of the area Based

The project site is also located near some younger lava flows that were erupted from
Kilauea Volcano and are approximately 200 to 750 years in age This extensive lava flow
is located less than approximately 4 mile toward the southeast of the project site and is part
of the Puna Volcanic Series map unit p4
Based on a review of previous geotechnical data in the vicinity of the project site the
basalt rock underlying the project site generaily consists of vesicular pahoehoe type java
Pahoehoe basalt rock is generally the result of thinner more fluid lava flows The feeding
river lobes of a pahoehoe flow crust over to develop a continuous roof leaving the interior
lava stream to flow through a tunnel of its own making These remnant flow tunnels or voids
are known as lava tubes The lava tubes feed the advancing lava flow front and margins As
a result the lava tube may branch or converge generally in a downhill direction to maintain
the lava flow In the waning stages of an eruption the lava may drain from the main tube
system leaving open tunnels ranging from several feet to over 40 feet in diameter The open
tubes resemble tunnels with arched roofs and nearly flat floors The main lava tubes may
extend downslope for several miles occasionally reaching all the way to the coastline
Another type of void space that is typically encountered in pahoehoe rock is a pocket
blister These blisters are typically isolated closed void structures formed by trapped
GEOLABS INC
W O 4612 00 Page 7
volcanic gases within the flow interior Some blisters may extend a short distance downhill
before pinching out and terminating
The surface of pahoehoe type lava flows are generally smoother than their rough and
irregular counterpart the a a flow A a flows form from generally more viscous slower
moving lavas and are expressed by a solid rock core sandwiched between an upper and
lower less dense clinker layer The pahoehoe flow surface may be expressed by broad
dome shaped hiliocks created by the heaving and buckling of the flow crust as it approaches
obstructions
It should be noted that the Keaiwa Stream Bridge project site is located in the vicinity

The faults are approximately located toward the northwest and across the project site
Based on the available geologic map of the area the fault located toward the north and
northwest of the site appears to be located approximately 0 5 miles to the north northwest
at its closest approach The second fault appears to intersect Keaiwa Gulch at or near the
project site The second fault extends toward the northeast and southwest from the project

ground surface of the project site is not known at this time

Seismic Design Considerations

Transportation Highways Division the Keaiwa Stream Bridge Replacement project will need
to be designed based on a seismic acceleration coefficient of 0 80g Based on the
subsurface materials encountered in the borings drilled and the geologic setting of the area

scour Potential
Scour is the result of the erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying away
material from the bed and banks of the stream Total scour over a period of time generally
consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and
3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as the shape and
dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity of approach flow size and gradation
of streambed material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristic of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material Dso in conjunction
with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate the flow velocity of the streambed
materials in scour depth analysis Based on the materials encountered in the borings drilled
for the project we anticipate that moderately fractured basalt formation with relatively high
RQD values greater than 50 percent will be encountered at the streambed and foundation
GEOLABS INC
W O 4612 00 Page 12
level Based on the guidelines on scour potential by the Federal Highway Administration the
basalt formation at the site may be considered to be a non erodible material and would not
be subject to stream scour
Although the RQD value is less than 50 percent at the surface for Boring No 4 the
fractures in the rock are less severe than is indicated by the lower RQD value Based on our
review of the core logs it appears that the lower RQD value is due to the presence of a
12 inch thick clinker layer below the surface basalt formation
Bridge Foundations
Based on the design criteria provided the replacement bridge foundations will be
designed based on the Load and Resistance Factor Design LRFD method Based on the
generally competent subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread footings bearing on the medium hard to very hard
basalt rock formation be used for support of the planned replacement bridge structure
Based on LRFD methods an ultimate bearing capacity of up to 60 000 pounds per square
foot psf may be used to evaluate the extreme event limit state of the footings bearing on
the medium hard to very hard basalt formation To evaluate the strength limit state of the
bridge structure foundations pier abutment and wing wall structures a bearing value of up
to may be used
In general footings should be embedded a minimum depth of 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent grade or bear directly on the surface of the medium hard to very hard basalt
formation whichever is deeper Based on our field observations basalt rock formation
appears to be exposed at the ground surface of the project site except for the north
abutment location We anticipate that the bottom of footings at the north abutment and
wingwall structure will need to be located at about Elevation 199 to 201 feet MSL in order
to bear directly on the basalt rock surface It should be noted that the foundations for the
replacement bridge should be placed directly on or embedded in the medium hard to very
hard basalt rock formation In addition the bottom of footing should be located at least 5 feet
above the underlying ash deposit in order to develop the recommended bearing values

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities commonly exist in the
basalt rock formation at the project site In addition small voids were encountered in the
basalt rock formation encountered in the one of the borings drilled To reduce the potential
for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations piers
and abutments be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the pier and abutment
foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to
a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or
voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probeholes
GEOLABS INC
W O 4612 00 Page 15
should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the
cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the
grouting operations As an alternative to sand cement grout Controlled Low Strength
Material CLSM may also be used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be made available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete In addition it should be noted that a longer lag time between the
probing grouting operations and the foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
and or voids and to allow for additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take
and or changed conditions are observed

Since the project site is located in a seismically active area on the Island of Hawaii

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system should consist of permeable material such as AASHTO
M 43 No 67 gradation material placed near the bottom and along the length of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weep holes As an alternative the
drainage system may consist of about 1 cubic foot of permeable material such as
AASHTO M 43 No 67 gradation material wrapped with non woven filter fabric at
each of the weep hole locations The weep holes should be spaced not more than
8 feet apart
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of Type A Structure
Backfill Material conforming to Section 703 20 of the HSS Unless covered by
concrete slabs or pavements the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration
behind the walls In addition the backfill from the bottom of the wall to about the
elevation of the weep holes should consist of relatively impervious soil backfill such

Site Grading
The extent of grading required to construct the proposed bridge and approaches will
generally consist of cuts and fills of up to about 10 feet to construct the new abutment walls
and foundations In general grading work should conform to Section 200 of the HSS and
the site specific recommendations contained in this report
Site grading operations should be observed by qualified personnel or a representative
from Geolabs It is important that qualified personnel observe the site grading operations to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our exploration
In general areas within the designated grading limits should be cleared of vegetation
and deleterious materials The resulting grub spoil material should be disposed of properly
off site Soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm or dense ground and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with general fill materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches where possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density In areas exposing rock subgrades the rock
subgrades should be proof rolled with a 10 ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
nine borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 extending to depths of about 14 5 to
41 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using truck mounted and
portable drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
