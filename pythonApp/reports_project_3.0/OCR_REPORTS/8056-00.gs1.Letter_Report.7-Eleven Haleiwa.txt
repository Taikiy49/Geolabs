Project Considerations 
The project site is located at 66-810 Kamehameha Highway in Haleiwa on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii.  We understand that a new septic tank and leaching field system is planned 
for the existing 7-Eleven Haleiwa store. 
We understand that the new septic tank and leaching field will be located behind the 
existing store.  In order to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils and the disposal 
capacity of the proposed absorption bed site for wastewater disposal, a field percolation test 
was conducted at a selected depth and location in general accordance with the State of 
Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater 
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards. 
The project site behind the existing store generally slopes down towards the south 
with ground elevations ranging from about 25 to 32 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 3.5 and 16.2 feet below 
the existing ground surface.  We also conducted a percolation test in the shallow boring.  
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The borings generally encountered a relatively thin surface fill layer about 2 to 3.5 
feet thick underlain by residual soil, weathered basalt, and saprolite extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 16.2 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
surface fill soils generally consist of stiff to very stiff clayey silt and very dense boulders.  
The residual soil was composed of stiff silty clay and sandy silt.  The weathered basalt 
boulders were dense to very dense.  The saprolite consisted of hard clayey silt and very 
dense sandy gravel. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings in Appendix A, Plates A-1 and A-2.  Laboratory test results are presented on the 
Logs of Borings in Appendix B, Plates B-1 through B-3. 
Field Percolation Testing 
A field percolation test was performed in one of the borings in general accordance 
with the provisions of the State of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, 
Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards 
to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. 
The percolation test results are presented on the Site Evaluation/Percolation Test form, 
Plate 3. 
The percolation test was performed in the subsoil material by drilling a 4-inch 
diameter borehole to a depth of about 3.5 feet below the existing ground surface. During 
testing, the hole was carefully filled with clean water to a minimum depth of 12 inches 
above the bottom of the hole. The time intervals and water level drops were recorded to 
provide field data upon which calculation of the percolation test was based. 
Based on the percolation test results obtained from our field testing, it appears that 
the percolation rate of the shallow subsoils is about 20 minutes per inch. Therefore, it is 
our opinion that the infiltration characteristic of the subsurface soils is suitable for the use 
of a shallow depth absorption bed and seepage pit system. Based on the percolation rate, 
the effluent loading at this site should be about 1.1 gallons per day per square foot of 
leaching field trench bottom.

Septic Tank Excavation 
We anticipate that the bottom of the new septic tank will be about 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  Installation of the new septic tank will involve excavations into 
the stiff to very stiff and very dense fill and residual soils.  It is anticipated that the fill and 
residual soils may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as a large 
backhoe excavator.  However, excavations into the boulders may require the use of 
hoerams and/or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our experience in the project vicinity and the field and laboratory data from the 
borings drilled at the project site.  Contractors should be encouraged to examine the site 
conditions and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent 
interpretation. 
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance 
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in order 
to reduce the potential for ground movement.  In addition, the excavated soils should be 
stockpiled at least 10 feet away from underground utilities to reduce the potential for 
ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the underground utility lines. 
Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our field borings and in-situ testing. Variations of subsoil 
conditions between and beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from the features shown on the topographic survey map by Walter P. 
Thompson, Inc., dated February 12, 2020. Elevations of the field borings were 
interpolated from the spot elevations and contour lines shown on the same plan.  The 
physical locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered accurate only 
to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil types, and as such, may denote a gradual 
transition.  Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or in the text of this report.  These data have been reviewed 
and interpretations made in the formulation of this report.  However, it must be noted that 
fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, tides, temperatures, and other factors.
