SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The geologic character of the existing hillside at MP 19 of Hana Highway may be
classified as undulating basalt rock containing discontinuous joints with adverse orientation
as well as major differential erosion features and moderate difference in erosion rates The
basalt rock hillside is eroding and producing falling rocks ranging from small stones to
large boulders Due to a lack of open shoulder area along the roadway the falling rocks
have been reported to periodically encroach upon the travelway resulting in falling rock
hazards for personal injuries and traffic accidents
Based on the results of our field exploration engineering evaluation and input from
community and various agencies we believe that the rockfall mitigation program for the
hillside at about MP 19 should entail the installation of a rockfall impact barrier above the
toe area of the existing slope The rockfall impact barrier would serve to control falling
rocks from encroaching into the travelway
The rockfall impact barrier proposed for construction within the project site consists
of a specialized barrier fence structure which is designed to absorb the energy from
rockfall impact and retain the fallen material Based on our engineering analysis for the
project site the proposed rockfall impact barrier should consist of a 10 foot high barrier
rated at moderate impact energy absorption capacity with a rated capacity encompassing
about 74 foot tons The maximum spacing between the fence posts should be limited to
about 25 feet
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed discussions and
specific design recommendations

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene
Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain between these
two volcanoes The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding
on West Maui and is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and
East Maui Haleakala The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of
soil and rock masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials are transported
downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that large
volumes of water are transmitted for very short durations Because of this the transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
materials Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial soil deposits Depending on the local
base level and rate of transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms
of geologic time In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those
of the older partially consolidated deposits

2 3 Existing Rockfall Conditions
In general the intent for the installation of the rockfall protection system at the
site is to reduce the hazard of falling rocks from the existing steep rocky slopes located
adjacent to the highway Because the project addresses the potentially hazardous
condition of rockfall at the site a definition of rockfall is needed Where cliffs and steep
slopes composed of rock material occur rockfall is a common naturally occurring
process of erosion and mass wasting Rockfall is the detachment and transportation
downslope of individual rock fragments showers of rock fragments or large boulders of
rock material as a result of geologic weathering or disturbance to the slope The fall may
involve more than one rock but does not include large volumes of rock or landslides
Because of the proximity of the highway to the steep rocky mountain slopes
the rockfall source falling rock that bounces rolls or falls directly onto the highway is
a serious hazard with a significant potential to cause personal injury and or traffic
accidents Accidents involving personal injury or even death could occur as the result of
a direct impact from falling rock and collisions with other vehicles and objects and of
vehicles swerving to avoid fallen material in the roadway

indicated that seven substantial rockfall events with boulders up to 3 feet in diameter
have been documented for the portion of Hana Highway between to 19 52
during the period between July 1996 and August 1997 Based on our observation of the

3 1 Rockfall Mechanisms
Rockfall involves the detachment and fall of rock material from the slope face
Rockslide is another term that may be used to describe the detachment from a slope
and fall of a mass of rock material Rockfall is a more general term used to describe the
process of falling rock from a hillslope Rockfall may encompass the fall of a single
boulder or rock fragment or a shower of boulders or rock fragments Some of the
applicable mechanisms generating rockfall events at the site are described below
Loosening of rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of causes
including water pressure behind the rock differential weathering or erosion along
geologic contacts faults or shear zones weakening of rock due to temperature and
moisture cyclic fluctuations loosening by animals trees and wind ground vibration due
to earthquake seismic forces construction activity and vibration from loud sound
Waves
A frequent cause of rockfalls is by invasive tree roots prying on the existing
cracks in the rock slope surface Tree roots frequently develop and grow larger in the
discontinuities of rock materials The added force of wind action on tree branches may
create leveraged forces that are transferred to the roots lodged within the discontinuity
Over time the repetitive leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock and cause it to
fall
Inter layered rock such as lava flows with variable strength and rates of
weathering commonly exhibit accelerated erosion and rockfall The weaker layers
clinker tend to erode and undercut the more weathering resistant rock above This

The daily cyclic temperature change from alternating exposure to sunlight and

Large trees that are dead or weakened by disease can come loose and fall from

Groundwater seepage from a slope and high pore water pressure in a slope
reduces the shear strength of soil and rock materials leading to potential slope failure In
addition water pressure acting upon tension cracks in rock formation may lead to
rockfall Groundwater seepage and pore water pressure build up are related to the
regional and localized groundwater conditions Groundwater seepage and pore water
pressure build up are also related to precipitation therefore there may be seasonal
variation in groundwater levels The seasonal groundwater variation may compound the
effects of normal cyclic ground moisture fluctuations from precipitation events
Adverse geologic structures discontinuities may include the presence of
geologic contacts between differing earth materials geologic faults inclined bedding
surfaces fracture planes or other geologic conditions that may promote slope
instability Geologic faults are zones of potential weakness in earth material where
lateral movement across the fault trace may occur as a result of seismic force or
isostatic adjustment of the earths crust Bedding surfaces may be deemed adverse for
potential slope instability if the bedding plane dips downward and toward the observer
Adverse geologic structures have the potential to act as a focal point for the initiation of
rockfall and landslides as they are typically locations of low shear strength with the
potential for earth movement
Rockfall onto the Hana Highway poses a significant hazard to the public because
of the steep rock slopes next to the highway lack of adequate rockfall catchment area
along the road shoulder and limited site distance around the highway curves Three
main types of accidents involving rockfall may be defined First a vehicle may strike a
rockfall obstruction on the highway which could damage the vehicle and cause a loss of
vehicle control and injury or death to the occupants Due to the presence of the highway
curves and limited sight distance this is a significant hazard Second rockfall may also
directly impact a vehicle causing occupant injury or loss of control of the vehicle Third
a vehicle may swerve to avoid rockfall debris and strike another vehicle All types of
rockfall accidents have the potential to cause serious injury or death

Based on discussion with the highway maintenance department and a review of
the historical data of debris and rockfall events there are generally four types of soil and
rock slope failure that occur along the length of highway The following is a general
description of the types of rockfall and landslide activity experienced at the site in the
order of the frequency of occurrence
The most common and widespread type of rockfall experienced is the generally
random fall of smaller rocks and rock fragments in the range of baseball to football sized
materials These falls occur near daily and are typically swept to the road shoulder by
manual labor on regularly scheduled trips by highway maintenance personnel The
frequency of fall of these materials generally increases during wet weather conditions
though the falls are also experienced on a routine basis
The second type of slope failure experienced involves larger quantities of rock
material ranging on the order of about several cubic yards of material These rockfalls
appear to occur at more specific locations where the rock is generally weaker and more
fractured The location of this type of rockfall is generally more restricted to lengths of
hillslope that have recurring failure problems due to greater slope weathering and
incidence of open fractures in the rock It is estimated that rockfall of this type may
occur on intervals of several weeks apart and are initiated at greater frequency during
wet weather or extended very dry weather conditions
The third type of slope failure experienced involves large rock and soil landslides
from the steeper slopes This type of landslide generally involves quantities of material
on the order of truckloads of debris The debris may consist of a mix of soil rock and

3 3 Rockfall lmpact Barrier
The rockfall impact barrier proposed for construction within the project site
consists of a specialized fence structure which is designed to absorb the energy from
rockfall impact and retain the fallen material The rockfall impact barrier may consist of
ring nets spanning between support posts that are held in place by anchor plates and
lateral restraint cables The barrier is designed to absorb the energy of a rock impact
and dissipate the energy through the use of flexible nets which act as a braking
W O 4898 00 A GEOLABS INC Page 16
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
element The rocks are thereby caught in the barrier as opposed to bouncing or striking
with enough force to topple the retention system The barrier may be designed for
varying load impacts that are estimated based on the existing site conditions such as
boulder size slope inclination and composition computed rolling velocities and
possible boulder bounce heights
The barriers are commonly used along highways and other areas where
structures along lengthy slope segments must be protected from rockfall The barriers
are suitable for sites where rockfall sources are widely scattered and where it may be
generally impractical to locate and stabilize the individual hazardous blocks of rock
because of the quantity of hazards difficult slope access and other restrictions The
barriers would require some periodic maintenance to check the performance of the
system and remove accumulated rock material behind the barrier
The primary advantage of the rockfall impact barrier is that it is a comprehensive
rockfall protection system which should provide a high level of protection against most
of the potential rockfall hazards on the slopes In addition man induced disturbance to
the slope and natural vegetation would be limited to the immediate area surrounding the
rockfall impact barrier installation
The main components of the rockfall impact barrier include the support posts
wire ring nets anchorage ground plates wire retaining ropes braking rings and the
spiral rope anchors The steel components are typically hot dipped galvanized and the
wire ropes are DIN 2078 coated A special protective zinc aluminum coating may be
applied where environmental corrosion is of concern Under normal field conditions the
lifespan of the steel material components is estimated to range on the order of about
50 years Because of the higher corrosiveness of the environment in Hawaii primarily
due to the oceanic environment warm air temperature and higher humidity the
lifespan of the steel materials may be estimated on the order of about 20 years It is
recommended to implement periodic inspection occurring on the order of about 5 to
10 year intervals to inspect the condition of the rockfall impact barrier system for
possible signs of corrosion

coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and

observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in

a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material

74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

quality of coral rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
