SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills overlying basalt rock 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 15.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The surface fills generally consisted of medium stiff to stiff silty 
clays approximately 0.5 to 2 feet thick.  The basalt rock formation encountered is 
generally severely to moderately fractured, slightly to severely weathered, and medium 
hard to hard.  It should be noted a layer of colluvial boulders, on the order of about 2 to 
3 feet thick, was encountered between the surface fills and the underlying basalt rock 
formation in the borings drilled within Lot 120.  This colluvial boulder layer appeared to 
be absent in the borings drilled within Lot 121.  We did not encounter groundwater in the 
borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the relatively competent subsurface conditions encountered at the 
project site, it is our opinion that shallow foundations, consisting of (1) post-and-beam 
construction and (2) slab-on-grade construction, may be used to support the new 
structures planned.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for 
the design of shallow foundations bearing on the compacted structural fills or in-situ 
basalt rock formation as discussed herein. 
Because of the random deposition nature of the colluvial boulders, we believe 
that these bouldery materials would not be able to provide a competent bearing stratum 
for the foundation support of the new structures.  In addition, slabs-on-grade bearing 
directly on these bouldery materials could create sharp points with concentrated loading 
that may result in slab distress such as cracking. Therefore, we recommend 
overexcavating the bouldery materials underneath the building footprint (and extending 
2 feet beyond the building footprint or to the outside edge of exterior concrete 
walkway/patio slab, if applicable) and backfilling with compacted structural fill materials 
if slab-on-grade construction is utilized for the foundation support of the new structures. 
The thickened-edge footings may bear directly on the near-surface basalt rock 
formation (where bouldery materials are absent) or on the compacted structural fill 
materials (where removal of the bouldery materials is required as discussed above). If 
post-and-beam foundation construction is utilized, the bouldery materials may be left 
in-place with the exception of the footing locations, where the post footings should 
extend through the bouldery materials and bear directly on the underlying basalt rock 
formation. 
Cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in the basalt rock formation 
present at the project site. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support 
resulting from the collapse of cavities below foundations, consideration may be given to

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is 
the result of building on the southwest rift zone. The summit of the shield collapsed to 
form a caldera from which a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, 
extends south-southeast toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff 
indicate that this Manele Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. 
The project site is located within the Manele Graben. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located in the Manele District adjacent to the Kaphiha’a 
Preservation site on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii.  Manele Lots 120 and 121 are 
generally bounded by Mauna Lei Drive to the north, the Pacific Ocean to the south, and 
undeveloped lots to the east and west as shown on the Project Location Map (Plate 1) 
and Site Plan (Plate 2).  
Based on our field observations and the conceptual site plan provided, the 
project site generally slopes downward from Mauna Lei Drive at an average ground 
slope inclination of about five horizontal to one vertical (5H:1V). At the time of our field 
exploration, the existing ground surface was covered with relatively large basaltic 
cobbles and boulders, knee to waist-high land grass, and localized, tall haole koa trees.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling ten borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7 and LID-1 through LID-3, extending to depths 
ranging from about 5 to 15.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, 
two bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were 
obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the project site consisted of surface fills, 
colluvial boulders, and basalt formation extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 15.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The surface fills generally consisted 
of medium stiff to stiff silty clay approximately 0.5 to 2 feet thick. Underlying the surface 
fills, severely to moderately fractured, slightly to severely weathered, medium hard to 
hard basalt formation was encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 
15.5 feet below the existing ground surface. It should be noted a layer of colluvial 
boulders with silt and gravel, on the order of about 2 to 3 feet thick, was encountered 
between the surface fills and the underlying basalt formation in the borings drilled within 
Lot 120.  This colluvial boulder layer appeared to be absent in the borings drilled within 
Lot 121.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted groundwater levels are subject to change due 
to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for preliminary design of shallow foundations bearing on the in-situ basalt rock 
formation or compacted structural fill materials as described below.  This bearing value 
is for dead plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, 
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Where footings are located adjacent to other below-grade structures, utility 
trenches or easements, the footings should extend to a depth below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane projected upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or 
the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the bottom of footings to the face of the slope. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations (static condition) 
to be on the order of about 1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings 
supported on similar materials may be on the order of about 0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the 
bottom of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.45 
may be used for foundations bearing on the in-situ basalt rock formation or compacted 
structural fill materials. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using 
an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). The 
passive earth pressure values assume the concrete footings are placed against 
well-compacted materials or cast neat against the in-situ basalt rock formation. Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 and 36 inches of

3.1.1 Post-and-Beam Construction 
If it is desired to utilize post-and-beam construction for the proposed structures, we 
recommend using short cast-in-place drilled piers for foundation support.  The 
drilled piers should extend through the surface fill soils and colluvial boulders, if 
present. The drilled piers would derive support from a combination of skin friction 
between the pier shaft and surrounding soils and from end-bearing on the in-situ 
basalt rock formation. 
The drilled piers should be embedded a minimum depth of 3 feet below the lowest 
adjacent finish grades. Drilled piers should have a minimum diameter of 18 inches 
and a minimum center-to-center spacing of at least 2.5 times the pier diameter to 
avoid capacity reduction due to group action.  The bottom of the drilled holes 
should be cleaned of loose soils prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and 
concrete.  Installation of drilled pier foundations should be observed by a Geolabs 
representative to confirm the subsoil conditions and embedment depths.

3.5.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the 
boulders.  Placement should be by haul-trucks traversing previously placed lifts 
and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift.  Spreading of the rock fill

3.10 Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, 
and sulfate content, were performed on a bulk sample of the near-surface soils obtained 
during our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the 
project site. The test results are summarized and presented on Plate B-8 of Appendix B.  
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory testing, the subsurface soils at the 
project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of approximately 3,400 ohm-cm,

The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the 
relatively low value of pH, chloride content, and sulfate content tested on the in-situ fill 
materials, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may be considered 
“non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the 
concrete in contact with the ground.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
ten boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7 and LID-1 through LID-3, extending 
to depths between about 5 and 15.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Result of the pocket penetrometer test is summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling, mechanical, and handling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic representation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One One-Inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-2. 
Nine Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were 
performed on selected core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the 
basalt formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
One set of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected bulk soil 
sample obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-8.
