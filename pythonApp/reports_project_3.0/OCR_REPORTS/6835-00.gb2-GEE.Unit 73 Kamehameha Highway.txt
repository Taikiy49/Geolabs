SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The borings and corings drilled along Kamehameha Highway between Haiku Road 
and Kahekili Highway generally encountered about 2 to 16 inches of asphaltic concrete 
(AC).  The majority of the borings and corings drilled between the north side of the bridge 
structure and Kahekili Highway encountered Portland cement concrete (PCC) below the 
AC surface.  The PCC ranged in thickness from about 6 to 8 inches.  Base/subbase 
material or stiff clayey soils were encountered below the AC surface and/or PCC. The 
base/subbase material ranged from about 2 to 24 inches thick.  We believe that the PCC 
encountered below the AC was the old pavement surface.  
Our field observations indicated that the pavements along Kamehameha Highway 
between Haiku Road and Kahekili Highway exhibit medium to high severity pavement 
distress. Our field exploration results indicate that some areas may not contain 
base/subbase below the AC surface course. Where present, the existing base/subbase 
course layer underneath the AC surface course is generally thin and likely insufficient for 
clayey subgrade soils with relatively high moisture content.  In addition, our field 
exploration results indicate that the AC surface course may be underlain by PCC 
pavements for the highway north of the bridge structure.  Leaving the existing PCC 
pavement and placing new AC over the PCC may result in future reflection cracking. 
Due to the insufficient base/subbase material along the majority of the highway and 
the potential for future reflection cracking in areas where the old PCC pavement is present, 
we recommend removing the existing AC and PCC and replacing with a new pavement 
section consisting of 3.0 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 4.0 inches of asphalt 
concrete base underlain by 8.0 inches of aggregate base course. 
After removal of the existing asphaltic concrete surface, the PCC layer, and the 
existing soils to accommodate the new pavement structural section, the exposed subgrade 
soils should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a 
minimum of six passes to compact the exposed subgrade soils. The drum roller should be 
operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. It should be noted that the in-situ 
moisture of the soils are relatively high; therefore, we recommend proof-rolling the 
pavement subgrade without the use of vibration.  Use of vibration during proof-rolling will 
induce a pumping condition of the moist subgrade soils.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering analyses 
performed for the Rehabilitation of City Streets, Unit 73 Kamehameha Highway, Haiku 
Road to Kahekili Highway project in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our 
analyses for the roadway rehabilitation project. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Rehabilitation of City Streets project is part of an overall Pavement Survey 
Project covering various locations across the City and County of Honolulu. Generally, 
the Unit 73 project will involve rehabilitation of approximately 4 miles of Kamehameha 
Highway from Haiku Road to Kahekili Highway in the Kaneohe area on Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The roadway alignment of the pavement rehabilitation project is shown on the 
General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The existing Kamehameha Highway from Haiku Road to Kahekili Highway is 
generally a two-way street with a single travel lane in each direction.  A City Bus route 
was observed along this alignment.  Based on our field observations, it appears that the 
roadway has received multiple AC overlays in the past.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Koolau 
Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the continental glaciers advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present 
stand.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present level.  
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas 
and fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef 
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine 
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused 
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the 
sediments and calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and 
lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials.  In addition, renewed 
subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial 
soils under relatively high energy conditions. 
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii, chemical weathering has altered 
the basaltic flows to a soil-like condition.  Erosional processes, consisting of detachment 
of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward the axis 
of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium, have also altered the natural condition of 
the volcanic flows.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a

When alluvial/colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical

2.2 
Visual Pavement Condition Survey 
A pavement condition survey of the project area was performed on September 6, 
8, 15, and 16, 2013. During the field survey, our engineer walked on the sides of the 
street, recorded approximate pavement distress locations, and determined pavement 
distress types through visual classification. 
The visual classification was performed in general accordance with the 
definitions described in the “Distress Identification Manual for the Long-Term Pavement 
Performance Program” dated June 2003 prepared by the Federal Highway 
Administration (FHWA). Some of the pavement distress definitions are presented in the 
table below. 
ASPHALT PAVEMENT DISTRESSES  
Alligator Cracking 
(Fatigue) 
Alligator cracks are a series of interconnecting cracks caused by 
fatigue failure of the asphaltic concrete surface under repeated 
traffic loading. 
Block Cracking 
Block cracks are inter-connected cracks that divide the pavement 
into approximately rectangular pieces. 
Longitudinal 
Cracking 
Longitudinal cracks are cracks parallel to the pavement’s 
centerline or laydown direction. 
Transverse 
Cracking 
Transverse cracks are cracks extending across the pavement at 
approximately right angles to the pavement centerline or direction 
of laydown.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 20 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 20, at selected locations along the roadway 
alignment. The borings generally extended to depths of about 1.0 to 10.0 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. In addition, the boring exploration was supplemented by 
coring the existing pavement section to depths of about 0.7 to 7.0 feet below the 
existing pavement surface at 30 selected locations, identified as C-101 through C-130.  
The approximate boring and coring locations for this project are shown on the Site 
Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.8. 
In addition, Dynamic Cone Penetration testing was conducted in Boring Nos. 1 
through 12, 15 through 18, 20, C-1 through C-5 and C-127 through C-129 to estimate the 
in-situ CBR value of the subgrade soils.  This instrument is typically used to assess 
material properties of the pavement subgrade soils. The 17.6-pound DCP can be used 
to estimate the strength characteristics of fine and coarse-grained soils, granular 
construction materials and weak stabilized or modified materials. The 17.6-lb DCP 
cannot be used in highly stabilized or cemented materials or for granular materials 
containing a large percentage of aggregates greater than 2 inches.  The results of the 
DCP testing are shown on the DCP Records in Appendix C.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration from Haiku Road to Kahekili Highway generally encountered 
about 2 to 16 inches of AC.  The majority of the borings and corings drilled between the 
north side of the bridge structure and Kahekili Highway encountered PCC below the AC.  
The PCC ranged in thickness from about 6 to 8 inches.  A layer of base/subbase 
material or stiff clayey soils were encountered below the AC and/or PCC.  The 
base/subbase material ranged from about 2 to 24 inches thick.   
Our field observations indicated that the pavements along Kamehameha 
Highway between Haiku Road and Kahekili Highway exhibit medium to high severity 
pavement distress. Our field exploration results indicate that some areas may not 
contain base/subbase below the AC surface course. If present, the existing 
base/subbase course layer underneath the AC surface course is generally thin and 
likely insufficient for clayey subgrade soils with relatively high moisture content.  In 
addition, our field exploration results indicate that the AC surface course may be 
underlain by PCC pavements for the highway north of the bridge structure.  Leaving the 
existing PCC pavement and placing new AC over the PCC may result in existing cracks 
in the PCC pavement reflecting up to the new AC surface. 
Based on the information provided, we recommend removing the existing AC and 
PCC and replacing with a new pavement section consisting of asphaltic concrete,

After removal of the existing AC layer, the PCC layer, and the existing soils to 
accommodate the new pavement structural section, the exposed subgrade soils should 
be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of 
six passes to compact the exposed subgrade soils. The drum roller should be operated 
at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. It should be noted that the in-situ moisture of 
the soils are relatively high; therefore, we recommend proof-rolling the pavement 
subgrade without the use of vibration.  Use of vibration during proof-rolling will induce a 
pumping condition of the subgrade soils with high in-situ moisture. 
If broken up asphaltic or Portland cement concrete are exposed after the milling 
process, consideration should be given to complete removal of the broken up asphaltic 
or Portland cement concrete to expose the underlying soils before proof-rolling the 
subgrade.

If soft soils are exposed at the pavement subgrade or the soils are pumping

scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar

The aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as 
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with AASHTO T-180 (ASTM D1557). Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphalt concrete (AC) 
material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, and 
its amendments.

Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage

Field Exploration – Borings and Corings 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation by drilling 
and sampling twenty borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 20, extending to 
depths ranging from about 1.0 to 10.0 feet below the existing pavement surface. In 
addition, the boring information was supplemented by coring the existing pavement 
section at 30 locations along the roadway to depths of about 0.7 to 7.0 feet below the 
existing pavement surface.  The corings are designated as C-101 through C-130. The 
approximate boring and coring locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.8. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist and engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock 
Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings and 
Corings, Plates A-1 through A-30. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing was performed in the 17 boring locations 
(B-1 through B-12, B-15 through B-18, and B-20) and 8 coring locations (C-101 through 
C-105, C-127, C-128, and C-129) to obtain pavement support characteristics of the in-situ 
soils. The tests were performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone Penetration to 
depths of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the lower 
rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The upper rod 
holds a 17.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the 
cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone is just below the 
testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts 
and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the strength of the soil at the test 
location.  The number of blows for a minimum of 1-inch penetration is recorded.  The 
process is continued until the desired depth of testing is reached or the full length of the 
lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as millimeters 
per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the DCP Records, 
Plates C-1.1 through C-25.2.
