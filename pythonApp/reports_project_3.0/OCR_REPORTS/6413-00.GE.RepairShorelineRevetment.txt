PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at the United States Navy Support Facility NSF at Diego
Garcia an atoll of the Chagos Archipelago in the Indian Ocean Based on the
information provided we understand that shoreline erosion has been observed since
the 1970s and concern has been expressed regarding the rate of erosion potential
damage from storms and impacts to the infrastructure and operational readiness of the
facility Therefore it is desired to repair the existing revetment along the shoreline at
the back of Bachelor Officers Quarters BOQ 8 to BOQ 9 located on the eastern side of

ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITONS
As previously mentioned field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling
borings was not conducted for this project Instead a field exploration program using
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer DCP equipment was conducted by BCC to supplement
the subsurface information obtained through our literature research in order to obtain an
indication of the subsurface conditions anticipated at the shoreline protection site
Based on the information provided we understand that a total of fourteen 14
DCP tests designated as through 8 and 1A through 6A were conducted by
BCC at the locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The DCP tests were generally
conducted at 150 mm discrete depth intervals between depths of about 0 to 3 meters
below the existing ground surface at the test locations
Based on the field logs of the DCP tests it appears that the project site may be
underlain by near surface tan silty sands with traces of coralline gravel on the order of
about 0 3 to 1 meter thick overlying medium dense to dense tan white silty sandy
coralline gravel extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 3 meters
below the existing ground surface Field logs of the DCP tests provided by the Client
are included in Appendix A of this report for reference
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we believe that
the proposed new revetment may be constructed as planned Detailed discussions and
recommendations for some of the design and construction items are presented in the
following sections herein
Ground Settlement
Due to the heavy weight of the armor rocks the existing ground under the
proposed revetment could settle under the new loads if soft and or loose soils are
present in the shallow subsurface In order to evaluate the consistency of the
near surface soils we reviewed and processed the field data obtained from the DCP
tests conducted along the shoreline protection alignment
The procedures used in our data processing were generally derived from the
publication entitled ASTM Special Technical Publication 399 Dynamic Cone for

Shallow In Situ Penetration Testing prepared by George F Sowers and Charles S
Hedges In the publication the Cone Penetrometer Resistance in blows per increment
is determined by the number of blows to advance a 45 degree cone a distance of
45 millimeters utilizing a steel ring weight falling 500 millimeters The Cone
Penetrometer Resistance obtained is then correlated to the Standard N Resistance in
blows per 300 millimeters based on a chart developed by the authors which is
presented herein as Plate 4
In the DCP tests conducted by BCC the same equipment described above was
used to record the number of blows required to advance the cone in 150 mm depth
increments as shown in the field logs provided in Appendix A The field data was
processed by our office to determine the number of blows required to advance the cone
a distance of 45 millimeters instead of 150 millimeters using direct proportion
calculation The Cone Penetrometer Resistance obtained is then entered into the chart
presented on Plate 4 to determine the corresponding Standard N Resistance value
Based on the Standard N Resistance value obtained the apparent soil density is then

Based on our processing and evaluation of the DCP field data it appears that the
silty sand soils in the upper 1 meter generally exist in a loose to very loose condition
The underlying silty sandy coralline gravel appears to be of medium dense consistency
extending to the maximum explored of approximately 3 meters below the existing
ground surface
Based on our interpretation of the DCP test data presented above we believe
that the heavy armor rocks placed over the near surface loose silty sand soils could
result in potential ground settlement However due to the granular nature of the in situ
soils we believe that a significant portion of the ground settlement should occur as the
armor rocks are installed during construction In addition we believe that the long term
settlement of the revetment should be minimal due to the relatively thin layer of the
upper loose silty sand soils and the competent consistency of the underlying coralline
materials

Minimum Toe Trench Width
Based on the guidelines presented in Case Ill of Figure 2 4 Chapter 2 of the
Engineer Manual published by the US Army Corps of Engineers EM
Design of Coastal Revetments Seawalls and Bulkheads we believe
that a minimum toe trench width of 4 meters may be used in the design of the new
revetments planned for the project
Stability of Existing Soils
Based on the discussions presented in the Ground Settlement section above it
appears that the project site may be underlain by loose to very loose sandy soils near
the ground surface overlying medium dense coralline materials at greater depths We
envision that some unraveling or sloughing of the cut slopes in the near surface silty
sands may occur especially when subjected to wave action under prolonged period of
time Therefore we recommend constructing the revetment as soon as practical after
the existing revetment is removed
Porosity of Armor Stone Revetment
It should be noted that the porosity used in the armor stone revetment is
essentially an empirical number based on past experience with stone revetment design
and construction work It could not be calculated based on the gradation of the armor
rock and underlayer rock provided in the Request for Proposal RFP documents
In general the porosity of the armor rock and underlayer rock depends to a large
extent on the method of installation i e randomly placed versus tightly packed as well
as the shape of the stones etc Based on our literature research and discussions with
a local consulting firm experienced in revetment design we believe that a porosity
between about 30 to 40 percent may be used for both the armor rock and underlayer
rock
