1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the installation of 75 lineal feet of electrical duct line along 
the existing pathway stairs at the DAGS Mauna Kapu radio facility.  A portion of the 
installation will also include the replacement of a CRM retaining wall with a new 
concrete retaining wall. 
The site is at a remote location above the Makakilo area; and, is not accessible 
to drilling equipment for a conventional subsurface exploration.  Therefore, we 
conducted an exploration using field observations, Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) 
testing and shallow hand-excavated test pits to obtain information about the subsurface 
conditions at the site.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. 
The rocks of the Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into Lower, Middle and 
Upper Members.  The lavas of the Lower Member consist largely of thinly bedded 
pahoehoe flows whereas the Middle Member consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a`a 
flows with a higher occurrence of the a`a form.  The Upper Member consists primarily of 
massive a`a lava flows.  
The project site is located at the crest of the Waianae ridgeline above 
Puu Makakilo and adjacent to the head of Nanakuli Valley on the southern flank of 
Waianae.  In this area, the shallow subsurface conditions are dominated by residual 
soils derived from the in-situ weathering of basaltic rock. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located at the end of Palehua Road north of the Makakilo 
development on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project site is a portion of the Mauna 
Kapu repeater station, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field observations, the project site is presently a portion of the 
Mauna Kapu repeater station complex and consists of concrete steps and adjacent land 
ascending a relatively steep slope between two electrical equipment buildings.  There is 
a public hiking trail leading from the top of the steps.  The land immediately adjacent to 
the project site is heavily forested and slopes steeply to the east.  Based on the 
topographic map provided, most of the project site gently slopes down toward the 
southwest with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about +2,750 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL) at the northeastern end of the site to about +2,718 feet MSL at the 
southwestern portion of the site.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by advancing 
four Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) probes, designated as DCP Nos. 1, 1A, 
2 and 3, extending to depths ranging from about 3.5 to 7.8 feet below the existing 
ground surface.. The probes were supplemented by the excavation of three shallow 
hand dug test pits to depths of about 2.5 feet below the ground surface at the locations 
of DCP-1A, DCP-2 and DCP-3.  In general, our field exploration generally encountered 
thin surface fills about 1 foot in thickness overlying residual and saprolitic clayey silt 
soils. The approximate probe and test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to 
rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.  
However, the site is situated at an elevation such that groundwater is most likely not a 
concern for this project.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition, the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and soil profile type for 
seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so

small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be 
backfilled with compacted fills. The cleared and grubbed material should be 
properly disposed of off-site.  Contract documents should include additive and 
deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account 
for variations in over-excavation required during the site preparation work. 
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive 
fills should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 
85 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill, structural fills, and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about

in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 85 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.  We wish to emphasize that moisture 
conditioning of the fill materials is an integral part of the fill placement 
recommendations provided herein. Due to the limited space and restricted access 
at the project site, compaction should be accomplished by man-portable 
mechanical equipment, such as “jumping jacks”, plate compactors, or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment.

elements of earthwork, Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests

3.1.4 Excavation 
The subsoils at the project site appear to be residual clayey silts and are stiff in 
consistency. It is anticipated that the majority of the soils may be excavated using 
man-portable equipment or very small light excavation equipment.  However, 
residual soils may contain localized hard zones or remnant boulders. Therefore, 
some difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas during 
construction. It is possible that the excavation for a portion of the utility line trenches 
will require the use of breaking tools or chipping. The contractor must exercise care 
to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the surrounding soils, 
resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for improvements in the vicinity. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
visual observation of the site conditions and field data from the shallow test pits 
excavated. We recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions 
and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe that cut slopes may be cut at an inclination of two horizontal 
to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Fills 
placed on sloping ground with an inclination of 5H:1V or steeper should be keyed 
and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be 
accepted.  In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching

3.2 
Retaining Wall Foundations 
Based on our exploration results, review of the available soils information and our 
understanding of the current design concept, we recommend using shallow foundation 
systems, such as spread footing foundations, to support the retaining structures 
planned for the proposed site improvements.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
2,000 psf may be used for the design of the footings bearing on stiff in-situ residual soils 
or select fill placed and compacted in accordance with the recommendations presented 
herein.  The bearing value recommended above is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grade on relatively level ground.  For footings constructed near the 
tops of slopes or on sloping ground conditions, the footings should be embedded deep 
enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 8 feet measured from the 
outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope.  Foundations that parallel sloping 
ground should be stepped to provide level bearing surfaces. 
The bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to at least 
85 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface 
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be over-excavated to 
expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with the 
on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction, or the bottom 
of footing may be extended down to bear directly on the underlying competent 
materials.

Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench 
or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This requirement is necessary to avoid 
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to 
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate that total settlements of footings supported on the 
compacted structural fill or coral formation to be on the order of less than one inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system.  A coefficient 
of friction of 0.40 may be used for footings bearing on compacted structural fill.  
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid 
pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  This assumes that the 
soils around footings are generally undisturbed or well compacted.  Unless covered by 
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be 
neglected.   
A Geolabs representative should observe footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel and/or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing 
conditions and the required embedment depths.  Due to the expansive nature of the 
on-site soils below the foundations, observation of the foundation excavations and 
preparation operations should be designated a “Special Inspection” item in accordance 
with Section 1704 of the International Building Code (2006).

3.2.2 Wall Drainage 
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations down) at the base of the 
retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an 
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may 
be used in lieu of the drainage material.  The prefabricated drainage product should 
also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of granular fill or 
excavated on-site soils less than 3 inches in maximum dimension.  Unless covered 
by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively 
impervious materials to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the 
retaining structures.  
3.3 
Site Drainage 
The finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and 
to reduce the potential for ponding.  Excessive landscape watering near foundations 
and slabs should also be avoided.  Drainage swales should be provided as soon as 
possible and maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations.

recommendations provided herein.  If this review is not made, Geolabs cannot be 
responsible for misinterpretation of our recommendations.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by excavating three test pits, designated 
as Test Pit Nos. DCP – 1A through DCP - 3, inclusive, extending to depths ranging 
between 2.0 and 2.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Test pits were hand 
excavated using hand tools. The approximate test pit locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our engineer, who observed the excavation operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed by visual 
observation.  Text descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs 
of Test Pits, Plate A-1. 
The in-situ strength characteristics were explored using a Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer (DCP).  The DCP is a simple device for measuring the stiffness of 
unbound materials. The DCP works by driving a steel rod into base materials and soil 
with a preset amount of energy; the stiffness of materials at different depths can be 
measured by continuously monitoring the rate of penetration, yielding a stiffness profile. 
The results of the DCP tests performed at the site are presented on Plates A-2 through 
A-5.
