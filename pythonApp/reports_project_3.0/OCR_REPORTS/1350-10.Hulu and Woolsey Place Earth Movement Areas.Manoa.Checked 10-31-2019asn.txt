INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of cur geotechnical engineering
investigation for the remedial work related to the earth movements within
the City s right of way areas located in the vicinity of Hulu Place and
Woolsey Place in Manoa Valley Oahu Hawaii It incorporates cur initial
findings presented in our preliminary report dated March 14 1985
The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
There has been significant earth movements experienced in portions
of the above referenced residential development roadways within the last
several years These earth movements also occur above and below the
City s right of way affecting the stability of the adjacent residential
structures and improvements A soil engineering investigation was
GEOLABS HAWAII

performed by our firm previcusly for the City and County of Honolulu and
the result of our findings within the roadways was presented in our
report of The investigation disclosed that the major
cause of the soil movement in this part of Manca Valley is the poor
in place soil conditions consisting of very weak adobe clay which is
located cn relatively steep slopes and subject to saturation by rainfall
and seepage Several general methcds to restrain the ground movements
within the City s right of way were presented in our previous report
It is our understanding that the City County of Honolulu requires
a more detailed follow up design for possible methods to restrain the
earth movements on the roadway creep area which has been identified by
the City as the eastern portion of Woolsey Place and Hulu Place The
area to be stabilized was identified by the City and County and is shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
The purpose of this investigation was to perform additional field
exploration which was necessary for the development of appropriate soil
parameters for the structural design of the retaining structure Special
attention was directed to develop a soil profile along the alignment of
the proposed sections to be stabilized along Woolsey Place and Hulu
Place

along the toe of Waahila Ridge at Hulu Place and along the northeastern
portion of Woolsey Place
The general study area is on a sloping hillside with an average
Slope of approximately 3 to 5 horizontal to 1 vertical 33 to 20
slope The alignment of the proposed retaining structure is

SUBSURFACE CONDITION
Geology
The project site is located on the side of a ridge of the Koolau
Range along the eastern slopes of Manoa Valley This ridge is an
erosional remnant eroded from the leeward flank of the Koolau Range and
consists of steep rocky slopes of relatively unweathered dense basalt
The upper rocky slopes consist of massive basalt outcrops forming
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1350 10 Page 5
nearly vertical cliffs fractured in place basalt and dense boulders
some very large At the foot of the slopes where the site is located
extensive erosion of the upper valley sides has Geposited large
quantities of colluvium
The colluvial deposits are made up cf rocks that consist of angular
to subangular fragments of basalt rocks that are poorly sorted ranging
in size from 1 to 2 feet to less than 1 inch in a matrix of fine grained
partly weathered material The colluvial material represents the
shedding of erosional debris frem the steeper slopes by gravity soil
avalanche or by other means of mass transfer
Some of the colluvium has been partly consolidated cemented into
a fairly coherent rocky mass that may be called a gravel cobble breccia
The site was subsequently covered with a surface layer of young
alluvium consisting of gray clay adobe with cobbles and boulders

Soil Conditions
Our previous investigation indicated that the project site was
underlain by 9 to 14 feet of younger alluvium consisting of gray adobe
clay mixed with cobbles and boulders Underlying the alluvium was about
30 to 40 feet of colluvium consisting of stiff to hard clayey silt with
gravel and cobbles
However during this investigation our new Borings 6A and 7
encountered what appears to be a buried drainageway in which the
thickness of the upper adobe clay soil was found to be 25 and 15 feet
respectively Underneath the adobe clay was 25 to 35 feet of colluviun
The weathered bedrock layer was generally encountered at about 50 feet
below the ground surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATION
General
The design reconmendations presented in this report are essentially
for the remedial work which the City and County of Honolulu propesed to
carry out at the vicinity of Hulu Place and Woolsey Place The main
objective of the design is to protect the City s right of way and the
existing underlying utilities It should be noted that the areas cutside
the current project site may also have soil movement and also require
remedial work The following reconmendations may not be applicable to
other sections within the unstable area studied for this report
Our new Borings 6A and 7 encountered what appears to be a buried
drainageway in which the thickness of the upper adobe clay soil was
found to be 25 to 15 feet This thick deposit of adobe clay will have
a significant effect on the design and construction of the earth movement
retaining scheme It increases the lateral driving forces and
therefore requires deeper embeddment of the proposed retaining
structure
Because of the large thickness of the upper alluvium encountered
and the fact that very deep and extensive excavations would be required
it does not appear feasible to use a buttress fill to retain movement
within the roadway area studied Therefore it is our opinion that the

Retaining Structure
Buttress Fill
Construction of the buttress fill consists of excavating the low
strength clay soils down to the better colluvium soil and replacing
them with high strength compacted granular material This would
increase the shearing resistance of the soil against ground
movement However with the existence of the 15 to 25 foot thick
layer of low strength adobe clay as encountered during our recent
exploration some major constructional difficulties are
anticipated These include
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 9
To remove the thick layer of adobe clay deep excavation is
needed Adequate trench shoring would be required to prevent
caving in of the trench walls In order to maintain
sufficient safety during the trenching operation the width of
the trench should be increased with an increase of its depth
In sections where 15 feet or more of the clay is encountered
a major part of the roadway would be excavated resulting in a
blockage of the traffic flow
Braced shoring would probably be required However due to
the many existing utilities boulders and cobbles underground
the design and installation of the shoring may be difficult
In addition even with proper construction procedures distur
bances or damage to properties adjacent to the trenching
operations may be difficult tc predict and prevent There
fore close monitoring of the effect of this type of
construction would probably be required
The stability of the up hill slopes may be further endangered
by the buttress excavation especially if it is not carried
out in very small sections and the select backfills are not
placed in a timely manner

construction of the buttress fill more costly and therefore

Large Diameter Caisson Wall
i em Mi RS A
The construction of a caisson wall consists of Grilling a series of
large diameter holes along the proposed wall alignment These
holes are then reinforced and concreted as piers They are
designed to withstand external soil and groundwater pressures The
design may involve taking the lateral pressure either by cantilever
action or by the installation of post tensioned earth er rock
anchors
At Hulu Place and Woolsey Place rock is at a considerable distance
frem the proposed wall and the existing development and utilities
would severely restrict the installation of tie back anchors
therefore it appears that only a cantilevered wall can be
considered A schematic section of the caisson wall is shown on
Plate 6 Presented below are some considerations associated with
the design and construction of the cantilever type caisson wall
1 Design Earth Pressure The design earth pressure depends
largely on the soil profile and soil condition at the back and
in front of the wall The most critical consideration is the

soil at same sections and the fact that the soil in front of
the wall will not be excavated

Caisson Construction Depending on the dimensions of the
caisson and the quantity and size of the cobbles and boulders
underlying the site special equipment and technique may be
required
Boulders and cobbles will obstruct and slow down caisson
drilling and may make it very expensive Cobbles and smaller
boulders particularly when in a clay matrix can generally be
removed with caisson drilling equipment but drilling progress
is slowed somewhat Very large boulders up to two thirds the
shaft diameter may require removal by special tools like
boulder bits or buckets or in some cases require some manual
efforts using a jack hanmer
The construction of a caisson wall is often done in the
following sequence
a A series of caisson holes are drilled along the wall
alignment to the designated depth To prevent caving
in casing may be required Drilling fluid should not be

used as this may saturate and weaken underlying clay
layers In order not to disturb adjacent holes which may
be open or filled with concrete that has not yet set it
is often necessary to drill and concrete alternate holes
first then return and complete the series after the
concrete of the first caisson group has set up
When the concrete in the first series of caisson has set
up a second series of holes is drilled between the piers
of the first series such that each hole abuts the
hardened concrete of the two adjacent piers
These holes are cleaned to make sure the concrete of the
adjacent caisson is exposed then they are filled with
concrete completing the wall

to resist the bending moments the caisson holes may be overlapped

New Utility Line and Drainage
The old and damaged utility lines within the area will be replaced
Since water infiltration into the underlying clay soils would
increase the potential for slope movement we recommend that all utility
lines deep or shallow be underlain by a 15 inch gravel cushion layer
and a 6 inch perforated subdrain to intercept leakage A sketch of the
bedding of the utility trench and the subdrain is shown on Plate 10
For backfilling the utility trenches the compacted density of the
backfill frcem the pipe bed up to 12 inches above the pipe should be a
minimum of 85 of ASTM D 1557 78 The remaining portion should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 Under the roadways the top 12 inches of
the backfill from the finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1350 10 Page 16
It is difficult to try to intercept most of the water infiltration
at the surface and to detect broken utility lines immediately There
fore a trench drain extending down through the clay layer should be
installed within the road right of way adjacent to the caisson wall to
intercept and drain subsurface water A typical section of such a trench
drain is shown on Plate 11
The trench drain and the utility subdrain should be designed to
drain into a suitable outlet or catch basin in the lower stable areas of
the development The drain would have to be constructed in small
sections to permit through traffic and to minimize the potential for
instability due to excavation

Seismic Surveys
em Nn me a Re i me
In conjunction with the sampling operations geophysical
exploration techniques were used to obtain further subsurface data
A series of four 4 shallow seismic refraction surveys were
performed in selected areas of the project site These surveys were
performed using a BISON Model 1570 C Refraction Seismograph receiving
seismic signals generated by the impacts of a 10 pound sledge hammer
The signal enhancement feature of this seismograph provided a relatively
noise free signal as the result of repeated blows of the sledge hammer
The locations of the tests are shown on Location Plan Plate C l The
results of the surveys are presented on Plates C 2 through C 5
