SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The project site is generally underlain by beach deposits consisting of loose to 
medium dense sands extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 
11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the 
shallow drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. Due to the proximity of the 
project site to the Pacific Ocean, it should be noted that the groundwater levels are 
subject to change due to tidal fluctuation, storm surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We anticipate the new waterline will be installed using conventional open trench 
(cut-and-cover) method. We understand the invert depths of the new waterline will be 
around 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our drilled borings, trench 
excavations will likely encounter loose to medium dense beach sands underlying the 
project site. It is anticipated that most of the materials may be excavated with normal 
heavy excavation equipment.  It should be noted that a relatively thin layer of medium 
hard coral was encountered in Boring No. 3 near the ground surface. Therefore, 
excavations into the medium hard coral may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams 
to achieve the design invert elevations. 
For pipes bearing on medium dense beach sands, a minimum of 6 inches of 
bedding material, consisting of BWS S4C, should be provided below the pipe and up to 
6 inches above the top of pipes (or groundwater level), whichever is higher. Where 
loose sands are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer 
consisting of 24 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent), should be provided below the 
bedding layer for uniform support. 
In general, the on-site excavated sandy soils may be used as trench backfill 
materials, provided that they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and clay 
lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. The upper 24 
inches of trench backfill below the base course layer within the park access road should 
consist of select borrow material.  Backfill should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The upper 3 feet 
below the finished grade in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be compacted to 
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

performed for the Construction of Multi-Purpose Field project at Waimanalo Bay Beach 
Park in the Waimanalo area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting

These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of the 
new waterline installation using open trench method, corrosion potential, and pavement 
restoration only. In addition, general discussions on the excavation requirements are 
included in this report for information purposes. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau 
Volcano reached the end of its main shelf-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to about the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations. Because of this, 
transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream’s hydraulic load 
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When 
the erosional base levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits.

When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes 
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown of weathering of the 
material. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the 
coarse-grained portion of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary 
rock, or conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion continues in areas above the valley 
floors and upstream in headwaters. This continued erosion generates material, which is 
transported downslope, covering the older alluvial deposits. 
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, these newer sediments 
are generally transient in terms of geologic time. In addition, their consistency and 
density are generally less than those of the older, partially consolidated deposits.

3.1 
Open Trench (Cut-and-Cover) Method 
We envision the new waterline would likely be installed using conventional open 
trench (cut-and-cover) method. We understand the invert depths of the new waterline may 
be on the order of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
3.1.1 Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes 
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench, the size of the 
pipes, the unit weight of backfill material, and the friction resistance between the 
backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried 
utility pipes, we recommend that an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic 
foot (pcf) for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3 be used. Earth 
forces acting upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. 
Therefore, the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the 
buried pipes should also be considered for the portion of the pipes located in 
roadway areas. 
3.1.2 Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important 
design consideration. For pipes bearing on the medium dense sands, a minimum of 
6 inches of bedding material, consisting of BWS S4C, should be provided below the 
pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the 
pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of open-graded gravel, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) 
should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support.

The granular bedding layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater. 
Before the placement of bedding material, a Geolabs representative should 
observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials are exposed at 
the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should be provided below 
the bedding layer. 
3.1.3 Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from 
the bedding material to at least 6 inches above the top of the pipes (or 
groundwater level) should also consist of BWS S4C material to reduce the 
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is 
critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of 
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides 
of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage. 
The trench backfill from 6 inches above the top of the pipes (or groundwater 
level) to finished subgrade may consist of the excavated on-site sandy soils or 
imported fill materials meeting the requirements of Section 30 - Select Borrow for 
Subbase Course of the City and County of Honolulu Standard Specifications 
(September 1986).  Geolabs should test the imported fill materials for 
conformance with these recommendations. 
3.1.4 Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site sandy soils or imported select borrow 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in 
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum 
of 90 percent relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the finished grade in 
areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same

3.1.5 Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force 
acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the 
face of the thrust block.  
We anticipate that the planned inverts would be about 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Based on our field exploration, loose to medium dense sands 
were encountered within the depths explored.  
We recommend using lateral bearing pressures of 500 and 1,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf) for the design of thrust blocks bearing on the loose and medium 
dense sands encountered at the project site, respectively. 
3.1.6 Settlements 
Primary settlement of the new waterline is normally caused by the difference in 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted 
backfill material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause 
settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our 
engineering analyses, primary settlement on the order of less than 0.5 inches is 
anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could 
occur if improper trench support is used.

3.2 
Excavation 
Based on the invert elevations and the existing ground surface elevations provided, 
we anticipate excavation depths on the order of about 5 feet deep will be required for 
installation of the new waterline. 
3.2.1 Trench Excavation 
Based on our borings, the proposed waterline installation will generally involve 
excavation into the loose to medium dense beach sand deposits. In general, we 
anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed waterline alignment may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
As mentioned above, our field exploration encountered a relatively thin layer of 
medium hard coral at relatively shallow depths in Boring No. 3. Therefore, 
excavations through potential coral formation/ledge within the trench depths should 
be anticipated. Trench excavations into the coral formation/ledge may require 
chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials and to achieve the 
design invert elevations. 
The above discussion regarding the excavation of the subsurface materials is 
based on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site. We 
recommend encouraging contractors bidding on this project to examine the site 
conditions and soil/rock data to make their own interpretation. 
3.2.2 Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of about 5 feet deep below the existing ground 
surface will be required for the installation of the new waterline. Deeper excavations 
may be required where the water mains traverse existing underground utilities. 
Where deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Based on our field exploration results, the in-situ sandy soils may be classified as a 
“Type C” soils for excavation and shoring purpose in general accordance with the 
guidelines provided by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration.  
Therefore, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations should be

designed with a slope inclination of one and one-half horizontal to one vertical

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy 
and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be 
on-site at all times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to

inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the 
shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to 
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential 
for excessive ground movement.  It is important to install adequate shoring and to 
maintain it tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during 
construction. The properly braced shoring is essential to reduce the potential for 
appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation, which 
may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent structures or other 
improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, 
such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the 
subsurface conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously

In accordance with the City’s new storm water requirements, infiltration tests and 
test pits/borings are required to support the on-site disposal design. To provide 
information for the design of the detention/filtration system, we advanced four boreholes

casing in the boreholes. The infiltration tests were conducted through the bottom of the 
cased boreholes in general accordance with the procedures described in the Maryland 
Stormwater Management Manual. 
Each boring was pre-soaked with water prior to testing. Water was introduced 
into the boring to a depth of about 2 feet above the bottom of the boring; and, the drop 
of the water level in the boring was measured with respect to time for a falling head test 
consisting of four individual one hour trials per test, where feasible.

3.4 
Corrosion Potential 
Two sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), were performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site sandy soils have 
resistivity values of about 2,100 and 8,300 ohm-cm with pH values of about 7.7 and 8.5.  
Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered very corrosive to 
moderately corrosive (Corrosion Ratings of 2 to 4) based on the Board of Water Supply, 
City & County of Honolulu, Water System External Corrosion Control Standards, dated 
1991.  A professional corrosion engineer should be consulted to review the test results 
and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection, if deemed necessary.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 3 and 4 and I-1 through I-4, extending to depths of 
about 5 and 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
