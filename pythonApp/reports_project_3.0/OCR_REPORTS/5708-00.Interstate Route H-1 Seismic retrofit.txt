2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Waianae Volcano became
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lava banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded
lava formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau The project site is
located on the southern flank of the Waianae Volcano
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the
Waianae Volcanic Series These rocks generally are characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt rock with inter bedded clinker layers In situ chemical weathering
of the Waianae lava flows has formed a mantle of residual soils which grades to
saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is
typical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with
increasing depth

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not

SECTION 3 0 ASSESSMENT OF GEOLOGIC AND SEISMIC HAZARDS
As part of our scope of work we conducted an evaluation of potential geologic
and seismic hazards that may have an impact on the three bridge sites The potential
geologic and seismic hazards of concern include surface fault rupture ground shaking
ground failure tsunamis and seiches and flooding To evaluate the potential hazard
associated with surface fault rupture the proximity of the site with respect to nearby
active fault traces generally is considered appropriate However seismically induced
hazards such as ground failure and ground shaking also are dependent upon the local
geologic and soil conditions and the magnitude and duration of the earthquake Forms
of ground failure can be separated into landsliding liquefaction lurching lateral
spreading and differential compaction
3 1 Surface Fault Rupture
Based on our review of the geologic conditions there are no known significant
mapped fault zones located on the Island of Oahu A potential fault zone Diamond
Head Fault is located offshore to the southeast of Oahu about 17 miles from the project
site Due to the distance from the fault the potential for surface fault rupture at the
bridge sites is negligible
3 2 Ground Shaking
The Island of Oahu is located within a region of moderate seismicity associated
primarily with the volcanic activity occurring on near the Island of Hawaii A major
earthquake associated with volcanic activity and faulting is likely to result in moderate
ground shaking
3 3 Soil Liquefaction
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated granular soils undergo a
substantial loss of strength and deformation due to increased pore pressure resulting
from cyclic stress application induced by earthquakes Soils that are likely to liquefy
include saturated granular soils that are relatively clean and loose uniformly graded silt
and fine sand deposits

The three bridge sites are underlain by stiff silty clay and clay and basalt rock
formation The clayey soils are not considered liquefiable due to their cohesive
properties Therefore the potential for liquefaction at this site is negligible
3 4 Differential Compaction
Differential or dynamic compaction is a term that describes the process of
densification and settlement of relatively loose dry or partially saturated granular
deposits during earthquake ground shaking
The stiff clayey soils encountered at the bridge sites have a higher resistance to
volume change during earthquake shaking We believe that the stiff soils below the
bridge sites have a low potential for significant differential compaction and ground
settlement
3 5 Ground Lurching and Lateral Spreading
Lurching is a term used to describe a limited displacement of earth towards a
free face such as a creek bank slope or cliff as a result of earthquake ground shaking
The existing approach embankments for the bridge abutments may be considered a
free face feature The embankment soils at the bridge sites are stiff indicating that the
potential for lurching is low
Lateral spreading is a limited type of ground failure associated with liquefaction
Due to the negligible potential for liquefaction the potential for lateral spreading at the
bridge sites is non existent
3 6 Landsliding
Two of the bridge sites have relatively level topography and stiff ground
conditions At the Military Access Road Overpass site the bridge abutments are located
adjacent to the slopes of Waikele Stream The slope generally is comprised of stiff
residual soils and basalt rock formation Based on the stiff ground conditions we
believe the potential for landsliding is low

3 7 Tsunamis and Seiches
A tsunami is a seismic sea wave generated by rapid movement of the sea floor
during strong earthquakes These waves can cause considerable damage in low lying
coastal and bay shoreline areas Considering the ground elevation of the bridge sites at
approximately 84 to 172 feet Mean Sea Level a tsunami is not considered a potential
hazard
A seiche is a standing wave oscillation of the surface of an enclosed or
semi enclosed body of water that can be generated during an earthquake The closest
semi enclosed body of water is West Loch of Pearl Harbor which is located about
1 mile from one of the bridge sites Because of the high ground elevation at the bridge
sites seiches are not considered a potential hazard at the bridge sites
3 8 Seismically Induced Flooding
The bridge sites are not located within the flood path of any nearby reservoirs
No significant bodies of water are located nearby or upslope from the bridge sites
therefore the potential for seismically induced flooding is low
3 9 Flooding
Based on a review of Flood Insurance Rate Maps FIRM provided by the
National Flood Insurance Programs the risk of flooding at the bridge sites is low

Lateral Load Resistance of Shallow Foundations
For lateral load resistance of the shallow foundation four components of lateral
load resistance as shown on Plates 7 and 8 for the Makakilo Separation and

be noted that the lateral load resistance of shallow foundations is primarily from
friction and adhesion where provided between the base of the footings and the
supporting subgrade materials The two passive pressure resistance components
friction and cohesion should be secondary lateral load resistance mechanisms
The two components of passive pressure resistance should be reduced to account
for strain compatibility due to the depth of the footings Please note that the amount
of deflection needed to mobilize the full passive pressure is indicated on
Plates 7 and 8 For deflections less than the deflection needed to mobilize full
passive pressure a linearly interpolated reduced passive pressure may be used in
evaluating the lateral load resistance of the footing
