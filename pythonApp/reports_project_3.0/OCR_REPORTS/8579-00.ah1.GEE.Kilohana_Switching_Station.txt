SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered volcanic ash extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 71.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The volcanic ash 
generally consisted of soft clayey silt with pockets of stiffer soils with in-situ moisture 
contents typically ranging between 50 and 120 percent. It should be noted that volcanic 
ash has a high affinity for moisture when it is dry and is characteristically expansive when 
subjected to moisture fluctuations.  
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 23 feet below the existing 
ground surface at the time of our second exploration. Based on the review of the 
geological map in the vicinity of the project site, we believe that the groundwater level 
encountered in the borings may be attributed to seepage water through sandy seams 
within the soil profile. The groundwater levels will likely vary due to rainfall, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors at the project site. 
Based on the preliminary structural loads provided and soft to medium stiff 
subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using a deep foundation system such 
as driven pipe piles to support the transmission line poles. We recommend installing 
48-inch diameter steel pipe piles extending to at least 60 feet below the bottom of the pile 
cap. The driven pipe piles would derive vertical support from skin friction and end bearing 
between the pipe and the surrounding soils. A net allowable compressive load capacity of 
up to 150 kips per shaft may be used for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces. 
Based on the structural loads of the equipment supports provided and the 
subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend using the 
slabs-on-grade system with thickened-edge footing for the new equipment pads. We 
recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to 1,500 psf for the design of 
slabs-on-grade foundations bearing on the in-situ soil. This allowable bearing pressure is 
for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, footings should be embedded a 
minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grades. Due to the high in-situ moisture 
content anticipated near the ground surface, we recommend the bottom of the footing 
subgrade be over-excavated by 24 inches. The over-excavation should be backfilled with 
select granular fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

and required compaction is not able to establish, we recommend incorporating a layer of 
geogrid such as TX160 or similar to be placed in the middle of the select granular fills in

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Kilohana Switching Station project in the Lihue area on the Island of 
Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, site grading, and pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, we understand the project involves a planned 
switching station owned and operated by Kauai Island Utility Cooperative (KIUC) to be 
built in the Lihue area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The site is located within the 
central Kauai area and is about three acres in size. However, we envision that structural 
loads will be relatively light.

and overturning moments such as wind loading. Based on the preliminary loading 
provided on November 22, 2022, we understand the take-off poles will be subjected to 
substantial lateral load and overturning moment of about 35 kips and 2,500 ft-kips, 
respectively. In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, the 
transmission line poles would likely require to be supported by a deep foundation 
system consisting of a single driven steel pipe pile.

available at the time this report was prepared.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene 
Epoch (more than 2¼ million years before present). Following the cessation of this main 
shield building phase, there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic 
lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of 
alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon 
Volcanic Series. These lavas consist of four distinct formations:  Napali, Olokele, 
Haupu, and Makaweli. These formations are comprised of thin-bedded a`a and 
pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with 
the vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
During the Pliestocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Kauai remained 
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher 
and lower relative to the present sea level of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.

The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series are

clinker layers. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which 
grade to saprolite with depth. In general, saprolite is mainly composed of silty material 
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to a 
basaltic rock formation with depth. 
The project site is on the central-southeastern portion of the Island of Kauai.

3.1.1 Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of the steel pipe pile is a function of the stiffness of 
the surrounding soils, the stiffness of the steel pipe, allowable deflection at the 
top of the pipe pile, and the induced moment in the pipe pile. The lateral load 
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program 
solves deflection and bending moments along a deep foundation under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of 
nonlinear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then 
computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.  
It should be noted that a cyclic loading of 100 cycles was adopted as one of the

3.1.2 Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the steel pipe pile will result from elastic compression of the pile 
and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered at 
the site. The total settlement of the pipe pile is estimated to be less than 
0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the settlement will be elastic 
and should occur as the loads are applied. 
3.1.3 Pile Hammer Selection 
Based on our experience, we recommend using a pile driving hammer having 
rated energy of about 60,000 to 80,000 foot-pounds, such as a Delmag D30 
diesel hammer, a Junttan HHK 7A hydraulic impact hammer, or similar pile 
driving hammers, to install the piles. Wave equation analysis should be 
performed to demonstrate that the proposed pile driving hammer and systems 
are capable of driving the piles to the design tip elevations without overstressing 
or damaging the piles. 
The hammer should be equipped with an energy level control such that driving 
within the soft and/or loose soils may be controlled at lower energy to reduce 
tensile development. 
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into 
consideration the “matching” of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be 
driven. This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required 
energy to the top of the pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles. 
Geolabs should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the 
equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity and 
estimate/develop the pile driving criteria prior to the pile installation. The 
contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs prior 
to construction for evaluation. Geolabs should review the information and assess 
the drivability of piles with the use of the selected hammer.

3.1.4 Pile Driving Criteria 
The preliminary design of the steel pipe piles may be terminated when aggregate 
blow counts are at least 40 blows per foot for the last foot or five blows per inch 
for the last 6 inches. The preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and/or 
modified after the selection of the pile driving equipment and the performance of 
the wave equation analyses. 
In general, each pile should be driven continuously without interruption. A pile 
may be rejected, or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving 
resistance is interrupted for more than 4 hours, and the pile cannot be driven to 
the required depth and/or meet the pile driving criteria. During soft driving, such 
as when the pile tip is within loose and/or soft soils, the hammer stroke 
(or energy) should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do 
not develop within the pile. 
3.1.5 Pile Driving Construction Considerations 
The steel pipe pile should be driven to an embedment length of no less than 
60 feet below the bottom of the pile cap or reach the termination refusal criteria 
established by Geolabs. Pile termination criteria will be determined based on the 
pile driving conditions and the actual hammer used for the project. The 
acceptability of the piles reaching refusal above the design tip elevations will 
need to be assessed on an individual basis during pile driving. 
The load-bearing capacities of the pipe piles depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the pipe piles and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper 
construction techniques are important. The contractor should exercise care when 
predrilling and driving pipe piles into the ground. Proper contact between the

3.6 
Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the 
corrosion of metallic objects.

Two sets of corrosivity testing, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and Eurofins Sacramento on selected soil samples obtained 
from our field exploration. A summary of the corrosivity tests is presented in 
Appendix B, Plate B-18. A full report of the tests performed by Eurofins Sacramento is

Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory testing, the subsurface soils at the 
project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of approximately 12,000 and 
18,000 ohm-cm, corresponding to a corrosion rating of 5 (mildly corrosive) to buried 
metallic structures.  
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH value, chloride content, and sulfate content found in 
the soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the 
relatively high value of pH and relatively low contents of chloride content and sulfate 
tested on the in-situ soils, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may 
be considered “non-corrosive”, therefore, either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may 
be used for the concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult 
with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, 1A and 4A, extending to depths 
between about 5 and 71.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to 
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our logger classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Four Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-6. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, Unconsolidated Undrained 
Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil samples in 
accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure 
was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The test 
results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-7 through B-12. 
Two Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D2435) were performed on 
samples of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility 
characteristics of the material encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are 
presented on Plates B-13 and B-14. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-15 and B-16. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557B) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the relationship between the moisture 
content and the dry density of the near-surface soils as fill materials. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-17.

Field Infiltration Test 
A field infiltration test was conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The infiltration test was 
performed at a selected location, Boring No. 5, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. This 
test was performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the 
State of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, 
Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ 
procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration test was performed by drilling and sampling a borehole to a 
selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the 
test depth, a solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration 
only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. The falling head infiltration 
test was performed to determine the infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface 
materials. The test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water 
and taking periodic readings over a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is then 
calculated based on the result of the fourth and last trial for the test location. The test 
results are presented on Plate C-1.
