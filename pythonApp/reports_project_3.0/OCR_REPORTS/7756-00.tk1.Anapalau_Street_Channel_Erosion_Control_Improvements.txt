PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Anapalau Street drainage channel extends from Koko Head Park to 
Kuapa Pond in the Hawaii Kai area of East Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The existing drainage channel traverses through the residential subdivision and 
crosses (from east to west) Kaumakani Street, Hanamaulu Street, Lunalilo Home Road, 
and Anapalau Place before draining out to Kuapa Pond. The approximate limits of the 
drainage channel erosion control improvement project are shown on the Overall Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  The drainage channel is lined with a trapezoidal-shaped concrete structure, 
except for the majority of the section between Lunalilo Home Road and Kuapa Pond, 
which consist of a rectangular-shaped concrete structure.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that the slope above the 
concrete channel was initially constructed at a maximum inclination of about one horizontal 
to one vertical (1H:1V). Due to on-going soil erosion, the existing soil slopes above the 
concrete channel have been eroded to approximately 1.2H:1V or flatter. This on-going 
erosion process has also resulted in moderate undermining and exposing adjacent

is generally underlain by surface fills overlying alluvium and volcanic tuff rock at greater 
depths. Special attention was given to the consistency of the in-situ soils pertaining to the 
design of the drainage channel erosion control improvement project.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions of the sloping ground above the drainage 
channel by performing DCP testing at nine selected locations, designated as DCP-1 
through DCP-9, to provide data and information for our evaluation and analyses.  The 
approximate DCP test locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. 
The DCP test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ resistance to penetration. 
The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground by repeatedly striking it with 
a 17.6-lb (8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 22.6 inches (575 mm). The penetration 
of the cone is measured after each blow and is recorded to provide a continuous measure 
of shear resistance up to 10.2 feet below the existing ground surface. Test results can be 
correlated to Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow count, California Bearing Ratio (CBR), 
in-situ density, resilient modulus, and bearing capacity. 
The DCP tests were generally conducted at 1-inch (25.4-mm) discrete depth 
intervals extending to depths of about 31 inches (2.6 feet) and 120 inches (10 feet) below 
the existing ground surface at the test locations.  Field logs of the DCP tests and the 
correlated SPT blow counts are included in Appendix A of this report for reference. 
Based on our literature review, field observations, and DCP testing, it appears that 
the sloping grounds above the concrete drainage channel are likely underlain by medium 
dense, highly to completely weathered volcanic tuff and/or sandstone formation.

Concrete Retaining Structures 
The following geotechnical recommendations may be used to design the concrete 
retaining wall planned at the project site. 
Retaining Structure Foundations 
Based on our evaluation, it is our opinion that construction of new retaining 
structures between the existing drainage channel and adjacent residential 
properties may be considered as a long-term drainage channel erosion control 
measure. This alternative will require creating stepped terraced level ground 
between the existing drainage channel and the adjacent residential properties to 
minimize the on-going soil erosion. To avoid and/or minimize surcharging the 
existing drainage channel structure, the new retaining structures should be 
constructed with a minimum horizontal setback distance of 4 feet from the existing 
channel walls, as shown on Plate 4.1. 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, an allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used to 
design the retaining wall foundations bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils. This 
bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
For retaining wall footings bearing on the on-site materials, the bottom of the footing 
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to 
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of 
reinforcing steel or concrete.  Soft and/or loose materials that may be encountered 
at the footing subgrade level should be over-excavated to expose the underlying 
firm or dense materials.  The over-excavation should be backfilled with select 
granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or 
the footings may extend down to bear on the firm or dense materials. 
In addition, retaining structure foundations should be at least 18 inches wide and 
the bottom should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent 
finished grades. To avoid surcharging the concrete-lined drainage channel walls or 
footings, the front face of the retaining structure foundation should have a minimum

Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures.  A typical drainage system may consist of a sack of permeable material 
placed at each weephole location.  The sack of permeable material should consist 
of about 1 to 2 cubic feet of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) 
material wrapped with non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent).  The 
weepholes should consist of a minimum 4-inch diameter PVC pipe spaced no more 
than 6 feet apart. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weephole should 
consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for significant water 
infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the retaining wall 
backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure unless covered by 
concrete slabs at the surface. 
Protective Lining (Shotcrete or Grouted Rubble Paving) 
In lieu of installing retaining structures, consideration may be given to placing a 
protective lining along the soil slopes of above the concrete drainage channel to mitigate 
the on-going erosion process. This protective lining may consist of shotcrete and/or 
Grouted Rubble Paving (GRP) extending from the top of the concrete channel wall to the 
right-of-way property boundary. 
The shotcrete lining should be at least 4 inches thick placed at a slope inclination of 
1H:1V or flatter along the slope face and should contain synthetic structural fiber for 
reinforcement. The shotcrete should have a minimum compressive strength of 4,000 psi at 
28 days and a maximum water to cement ratio of 0.45.  To reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures, a drainage system consisting of 2-foot-wide strips of a prefabricated 
drainage composite product (geocomposite drains) should be placed on the face of the 
existing slopes. The prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top of the 
shotcrete lining to the toe of the slope with a center-to-center spacing of about 10 feet and 
should be hydraulically connected to a weephole near the toe of the slope. Shotcrete 
placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. 
The GRP lining should have a minimum thickness of 8 inches, consisting of rocks 
approximately 6-inch in nominal diameter with voids in-filled with concrete placed at a 
slope inclination of 1.5H:1V or flatter. The grouted rubble paving should be well-drained to

Site Preparation 
In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future 
improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or stiff material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated soft 
and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground should be over-excavated, where 
necessary, to the bottom of the retaining wall footing, shotcrete facing, or GRP lining. The 
over-excavated subgrade should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density. 
Fill materials required for the project should consist of non-expansive select 
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well-graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. In addition, 
the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Geolabs should monitor site grading operations to observe the site preparation to 
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to evaluate whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions on the sloping ground above the drainage 
channel by performing nine Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests, designated as DCP-1 
through DCP-9, extending to depths of about 2.6 to 10 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  The approximate locations where DCP tests were performed are shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The test results are shown on the Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer Data Sheet, Plates A-1.1 through A-9.4. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications.  The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter steel 
rod with a 60-degree conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a 
second steel rod. This rod is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be repeatedly 
raised and dropped from a height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods 
consists of an anvil to allow for quick connections between the rods and for efficient 
energy transfer from the falling weight to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and 
the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the 
rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and 
dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above was 
used to record the number of blows required to advance the cone in 1-inch (25.4-mm) 
depth increments, as shown in the field logs provided in Appendix A.
