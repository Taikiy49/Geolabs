SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
This report presents the results of our additional geotechnical engineering
exploration and consultation performed for the proposed Hilton Lagoon Improvements
at the Hilton Hawaiian Village in Waikiki on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations based
on our review and analyses of historical information the site conditions and the fieid
exploration conducted for this project The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 1 Project Considerations
It is proposed to make modifications to the existing Hilton Lagoon at the Hilton
Hawaiian Village to improve the water quality in the lagoon to conform to Hawaii State
water quality standards for recreational waters These modifications or improvements
include the installation of a new pump station new influent and effluent lines new
effluent intake inlets and partial filling of the lagoon to reduce the volume of water in the
lagoon The improvements are intended to reduce the residence time of water in the
lagoon and thereby increase circulation
Previous work at the site indicates that the existing depth of the lagoon is as
much as 10 feet below the water surface and that the bottom of the lagoon is underlain
by several feet of soft accumulated sediments It is understood that the partial filling of
the lagoon will involve the placement of up to about 5 feet of new sand material over
these soft sediments
1 2 Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration for the project was to
obtain an overview of the shallow subsurface conditions at the site for the formulation of
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design and construction of
the proposed water circulation system for the lagoon improvements The work was

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Koolau is the younger of the two and forms
the eastern two thirds of the island
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world The higher sea
level stands caused the accumulation of sediments in the heads of the old bays
accumulation of alluvial sediments and reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations
and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The
lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits
In the Waikiki area this series of glacial fluctuations created a low marshy area along
the coastline In many locations these low marshy areas are underlain at shallow
depths by coral reef formations and associated littoral deposits
Urbanization of the Waikiki area during the late 19 Century into the 20 Century
resulted in the placement of man made fills in the low and marshy areas The base of
the stratigraphic section in the project area is Koolau Basalts
2 2 Site Description
The project site is located on the seaward or makai side of the existing Hilton
Hawaiian Village in the Waikiki area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The existing lagoon
was excavated out of existing littoral and coral deposits to provide a recreational beach
environment The lagoon is roughly hourglass shaped and has a length of about
675 feet with a maximum width of about 420 feet and a minimum width of about
240 feet There is a small island in the eastern end of the lagoon The site and its
immediate surroundings are shown on the Site Pian Plate 2

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our exploration it is our opinion that the proposed pump station and
effluent intake inlet structures may be supported by mat foundation systems Special
bedding and construction procedures will be required for the new influent and effluent
lines
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
3 1 Foundations
Based on the available information it is anticipated that the wet well for the new
pump station will have an invert elevation of about 11 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The inverts of other structures such as well vaults and valve boxes may vary from 1 to
6 5 feet
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions for the pump station and the
other structures it is our recommendation that they be supported by mat foundation
systems Since it is anticipated that there will be loose or soft soil conditions at the
foundation subgrade levels we recommend the use of a stabilization layer under the
mat This stabilization layer should consist of a minimum of 24 inches of clean gravel
No 3B Fine contained and wrapped in a non woven geotextile such as Mirafi 180N
to stiffen the subgrade to support the mat
A net allowable bearing capacity of 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of the mat foundations bearing on the stabilization layer or on in situ
coralline deposits This value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 4 3 for transient loads such as those induced by wind or seismic forces
To provide a base for waterproofing under the structures we recommend the
placement of a minimum 4 inch thick mud slab under the foundation mats to provide a
level and stable base for both the waterproofing and the foundation mat

Due to the relatively deep depth of the pump station structure and the relatively
high groundwater level at the site the pump station will be subject to buoyant uplift
forces Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the dead weight of the structure
and the concrete mat provided that the structure and mat are mechanically connected
A high groundwater level of 3 MSL or the highest anticipated water level elevation in
the lagoon may be used to evaluate uplift forces on the pump station
The effluent intake structures would normally be submerged and subject to
buoyant forces only if dewatered for construction or maintenance
We estimate that settlements for mat foundations bearing on a properly prepared
stabilization layer will be less than one inch total Differential settlement between the
center and edges of the mat is estimated to be less than one half 72 inch
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs Inc during the construction of the stabilization layer and prior to placement of
reinforcement steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions

3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the
backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill immediately

For the portions of the waterlines that will be located within the limits of the
existing lagoon special design and construction will be required Our previous
exploration conducted within the lagoon indicated that the existing bottom of the lagoon
is underlain by 1 5 to 6 5 feet of soft sediments The current plan for the lagoon
improvements include the filling of the bottom of the lagoon with up to about 5 5 feet of
new sand This new fill will be placed over a geotextile to provide separation between
the new fill and the existing sediments It is anticipated that the existing sediments will
consolidate under the load of the new fill resulting in settlements on the order of about
1 to 4 feet Settlements of this magnitude have the potential to damage the pipes and
to damage their connections to the pump station or intake structures Therefore where
soft mud is encountered below the pipe the mud should be removed and replaced with
clean gravel wrapped in a non woven geotextile such as Mirafi 180N The waterlines
located outside of the limits of the soft mud but below water level may require a
stabilization layer under the recommended pipe bedding if loose conditions are
encountered This stabilization layer should consist of a minimum of 24 inches of clean
gravel No 3B Fine contained and wrapped in a non woven geotextile such as Mirafi
180N to stiffen the subgrade
It is also recommended that flexible and extendable connection fittings be
provided at all junctions and connections
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level of 1 foot above the pipes to
the finish subgrade should consist of select granular material The backfill material
should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of two borings to depths of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface
The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the field exploration operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materiais encountered are presented on Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designations D 3550 and D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler and a 2 inch
O D standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to
24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
