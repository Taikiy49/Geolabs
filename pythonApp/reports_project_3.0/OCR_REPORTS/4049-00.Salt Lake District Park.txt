INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Playcourt Lighting Improvements project within the existing
Salt Lake District Park in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau
The project site is located near the southeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and
on the northeastern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series As the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the
groundwater generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which
deposited low cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred
to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4049 00 Page 5
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated
over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase of volcanic activity began Then the
Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its
present level The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from
the older Aliamanu events
The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate from
depressional springs and rain water During periods of little rainfall the shallow ponded
water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the mineral salt content of the
ponded water increased Recently man has developed the crater floor by filling and
grading the area for residential and golf course purposes

Our field exploration indicates that the proposed light pole locations are generally
underlain by a surficial fill layer overlying volcanic tuff formation at relatively shallow
depths In consideration of the lateral loads and moments acting on the foundations of
the proposed light pole structures we recommend that drilled shaft foundations
embedded in the upper fill crust and or underlying volcanic tuff formation be used to
support the proposed lighting system Drilled shaft embedments on the order of about
5 and 7 feet below the design finish grades are recommended for the light pole
foundations along the northwest and southeast ends of the existing basketball courts
respectively

Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load capacities the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the
subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a
minimum diameter of 24 inches be used to support the proposed light pole structures
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site drilled shaft
embedment lengths of about 5 and 7 feet below the design finish grades may be used for
the light pole foundations along the northwest and southeast ends of the existing
basketball courts respectively
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 10 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind or seismic
forces The end bearing component of the drilled shafts should be discounted due to the
potential for caving in of the gravelly sand layer in the upper 10 feet that would preclude
obtaining a clean bottom during drilled shaft installation
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect and 2 passive resistance on
the vertical faces of shaft caps where applicable Lateral load resistance of the drilled
shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4049 00 Page 8
allowable deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the
information provided each drilled shaft will be subjected to a lateral load of 1 2 kips and
an overturning moment of about 31 kip feet at the ground surface Our lateral load
analyses indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be
less than inch
The anticipated settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than inch
We believe that a significant portion of the settlements is elastic and should occur as the
loads are applied
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the subject project because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations
Loose Subsoils Dense Rock Formation
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light pole locations are generally
underlain by dense gravelly sands and volcanic tuff formation extending to depths
of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface However loose sandy soils
may exist in localized areas and also within the design depths of the light pole
foundations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary
casing may be required during the foundation construction
Based on our field exploration hard volcanic tuff formation may be anticipated at
shallow depths along the northwest side of the basketball courts Therefore
coring into the dense volcanic tuff formation will be required Appropriate drilling
equipment and coring tools should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to
install the drilled shafts to the depths and dimensions recommended herein

Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole bases are used the
gap between the pole bases and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with
lean concrete A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to observe
the installation of drilled shafts during construction

one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations facing down
at the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain
may be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site granular
soils or select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs or
pavements the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of low expansion
impervious material to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the walls

Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand
or S4C is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as clean
sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the duct
lines to the top of the subgrade or finish grade may consist of the on site soils or
imported select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch
level loose lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Since volcanic tuff formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it appears that the trench work for utility installation will likely involve
excavation in the volcanic tuff formation The method and equipment to be used for
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations Generally the near surface weathered volcanic tuff formation may be
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large
bulldozers However excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of
hoerams or chipping Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping
which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation resulting in a potential loss of
bearing strength for footings in its vicinity

Drainage
The finish grades outside the improvements should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that the
discharge be diverted away from the pavement areas Excessive landscape watering

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths of about 15 1 and 15 4 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample The result of the pocket
penetrometer test is presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
