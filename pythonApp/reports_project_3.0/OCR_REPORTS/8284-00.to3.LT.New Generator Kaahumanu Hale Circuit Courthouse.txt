PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at the existing Kaahumanu Hale Circuit Courthouse at 
777 Punchbowl Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. Based on the information provided, a new generator as part of the 
Phase 3 of an Elevator Upgrade and Modernization project will be installed. The new 
generator will be located near Elevator #6 along Reed Lane, as shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2, and supported on a shallow foundation system. 
Due to congested existing underground utilities in the vicinity of existing building 
and new generator pad, our field exploration was focus on the interpretation of subsurface 
characteristics at one selected location, where is clear from the existing underground 
utilities and not interfere to the busy operational traffic.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to about 49 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by surface fill 
and weathered volcanic tuff over coralline reef formation. In general, the surface fill 
consisted of very loose to loose silty sand extending to about 8 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Below the surface fills, weathered volcanic tuff/cinder consisting of loose 
to medium dense sands were encountered extending to about 16 feet below the existing 
ground surface. We encountered medium hard to hard coralline reef formation, consisting 
of coral and weathered limestone with interbedded medium dense to very dense coralline 
detritus, extending to the maximum depth explored of about 49 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
We encountered groundwater in the drilled boring at about 6.3 feet below the 
existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  It should be noted that 
groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations, storm surge conditions, 
seasonal precipitation, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and other factors at the 
project site.

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the International Building Code (2018 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following sections provide discussions on the soil profile type for seismic design 
and liquefaction design consideration for the project. 
Our field exploration generally encountered very loose to loose surface fills 
approximately 8 feet thick overlying competent volcanic tuff/cinder and medium hard to 
hard coralline materials. In general, it appears that the loose material encountered at the 
site may have a factor of safety less than 1.0 with regard to potential soil liquefaction. 
Therefore, we anticipate that the project site could be subjected to ground settlements in 
the event of liquefaction on the order of approximately 2 inches during a moderate 
earthquake (M6+). However, we believe that lateral spreading associated with liquefaction 
would not be a significant design consideration based on the relatively flat topography of 
the project. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered and our engineering analyses, 
the project site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils. Therefore, the project site 
would need to be classified as Site Class “F” based on the ASCE 7-16 (Minimum Design 
Loads for Buildings and Other Structures) Table No. 20.3-1, referenced by the 
International Building Code, 2018 Edition, that may require a site-specific response 
analysis to determine the spectral accelerations due to the potential liquefiable soils. 
Based on the assumption that the new generator structure is relatively low in height, we 
assumed that the fundamental period of vibration of the structure will be less than 0.5 
seconds. The actual fundamental period of vibration of the proposed structures should 
be checked by the project structural engineer. In accordance with Chapter 20 in ASCE 
7-16, a site-specific response analysis will not be required. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the geologic 
setting of the area, and the anticipated fundamental period of vibration of the proposed 
structure, we believe the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis 
standpoint as a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock Profile”. Therefore, we believe the 
seismic design may be designed based on a Site Class C soil profile in accordance with 
International Building Code, 2018 Edition. Based on Site Class “C”, the following 
seismic design parameters were estimated and may be used for seismic analysis of the 
project structures.

Based on our analysis, it appears that the very loose granular soil deposits at the 
project site have factors of safety of less than 1.0 against liquefaction based on the design 
earthquake. Therefore, the loose surface fills are susceptible to soil liquefaction during the 
design earthquake event. The project site could be subjected to some seismically induced 
ground settlements (on the order of about 2 inches) in the event of liquefaction during a 
moderate earthquake (M6+) based on the intensity of the design earthquake ground 
motions. However, we believe that lateral spreading associated with liquefaction would not 
be a significant design consideration based on the relatively flat topography of the project. 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on information provided and the subsurface soil conditions encountered 
during our field exploration, we recommend supporting the new generator pad on a 
shallow foundation system. Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation 
design are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions and relatively light loads, we believe that the 
new generator pad may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread 
and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the 2-foot stabilization layer. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used to design 
shallow foundation. This allowable bearing pressure is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grades. The stabilization layer should consist of select granular fill

wrapped around with geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent. The stabilization 
layer should be at least 2 feet thick. The select granular fill should be placed in lift and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
Bottom of the stabilization layer should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 
percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, or easements should be 
embedded below an imaginary 45-degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge 
of the utility trench, or the foundations should be extended to a depth as deep as the 
inverts of the utility lines. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate total settlements of footing supported on the 2-foot stabilization layer to be on the 
order of about 1 inch or less. Differential settlements should be on the order of about 
0.5 inch or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.3 may be used for foundations bearing on the 2-foot stabilization layer over 
on-site materials. Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be 
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf) for foundations embedded in the recompacted on-site soils. These values 
assume the soils around the foundations are well-compacted. Unless covered by 
pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches below the 
finished grade should be neglected. 
Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to placing the 
reinforcing steel and/or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the 
required embedment depths. Observation of the foundation excavations should be 
designated a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of the 
International Building Code (2018).

Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting 
excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 8-inch 
loose lifts, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Utilities to be abandoned in-place 
under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete or 
Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) under low pressure. 
In general, the near surface excavated on-site soils, excluding other saturated 
materials below the water table, may be reused as a source of general fill and backfill 
material provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock 
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Select granular fills should be 
well graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 
20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. It should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 
200 sieve. Geolabs should observe and/or test imported fill materials for suitability prior to 
being transported to the site. 
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. In areas where the finished grades will be 
subjected to vehicular traffic, the compaction requirement of the subgrade should be 
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the site grading operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification 
process, and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those 
encountered in our field exploration. 
Underground Utilities 
We envision that new underground utilities and utility connections may be required 
for the project.  In general, good construction practices should be utilized in the installation 
and backfilling of the trenches. The contractor should determine the method and 
equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations.  In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable local safety 
requirements.  Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility of the 
contractor. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 
3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the pipes for support.  Free-draining 
granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), also should 
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to about 49 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
boring was drilled using a track-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers 
and coring tools. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM 
D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 and A-1.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual
