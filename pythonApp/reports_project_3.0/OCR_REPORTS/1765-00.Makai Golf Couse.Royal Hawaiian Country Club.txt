structures and a roadway bridge In this exploration our Scope has been
limited to general recommendations pertinent to the Clearing and site
grading of selected areas within the Proposed Makai Golf Course
Previously separate reports were issued for the private access road and
Mauka Golf Course
It is our understanding that since structures are not planned for
the project except for the Clubhouse area large settlements in fill
areas are tolerable and adjustments to cut slopes could be made in the
event that undesirable material is encountered or Sloughing occurs

1 Large cut and fill Slopes adjacent to existing streams where

2 Selected proposed major cut Slopes that exceed 20 feet in height
3 Natural drainageways within the Makai Golf Course where the
presence of springs or seepage zones could require the placement
of subdrains

STTE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the western flank of Olomana Peak in
Maunawili Valley Maunawili Stream forms the
western boundary of the site and Olomana Stream lies along the southern
boundary Kalanianaole Highway is located approximately 1 2 to 3 4 mile
north of the site
Topographically the site slopes down toward the stream channels and
is traversed by several drainageways trending east west Existing slopes
range from about five horizontal to 1 vertical 5 1 to three horizontal
to one vertical 3 1 along the ridge crests to about one horizontal to
one vertical 1 1 locally in the vicinity of the drainageways
Elevations at the site range from approximately 50 to 350 feet above
Mean Sea Level
Along the stream channels and drainageways the area is densely
vegetated with trees tall grass and shrubs including hao banyan palm
mango and guava trees The upper highland areas and ridge crests have
been used as a pasture and are generally covered in grass with patches of
locally dense vegetation
Large boulders were noted along stream channels and drainageways

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed of two 2 shield volcanoes Waianae
and Koolau built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the late
Pliocene and Pleistocene epochs Preliminary reconnaissance of the site
and its vicinity indicates that the area is underlain by transported and
residual soils derived from the weathering of Koolau lavas
Olomana peak is an erosional remnant within the Koolau caldera
complex the original summit crater area of the volcano Following
cessation of volcanic activity subsidence of the caldera resulted in the
accumulation of thick deposits of colluvium and alluvium in the caldera
floor Subsequently erosional processes carved a valley through the
colluvial and alluvial deposits thus exposing the underlying caldera
filling lavas as well as the original dikes or conduits which fed the lava
flows
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1765 00 Page 6
In general the dike rock is composed of dense finely crystalline
basalt and is therefore more resistant to erosion than the surrounding
basaltic lava flows Exposures of hard dike rock may be observed near the
top of Olomana peak However within the boundaries of the project site
in situ chemical weathering has altered most of these materials to
saprolite and residual soil
In more recent years alluvial deposits have accumulated in the
lowlying areas adjacent to the existing stream channels
Soil Conditions
Based on the preliminary field exploration the geologic soil types
in the project site may be generalized into the following three groups
Recent Alluvium Recent alluvial deposits were encountered mainly in
lower areas along existing streams This alluvium primarily consists
of medium stiff to very soft brown clayey silt with many boulders
Localized very soft organic soil conditions were encountered in
Probing 108 located adjacent to Maunawili Stream near the northern
end of the 1st fairway
Weathered Older Alluvium Colluviumm In general weathered older
alluvial and colluvial deposits were encountered in the upper
highland areas and ridge crests as well as adjacent to existing
stream courses The alluvium generally consists of medium stiff to
stiff clayey silt to silty clay with some cobbles and boulders
Colluvium includes talus material and previous landslide debris and
is locally intermixed with the older alluvium It generally consists
of unweathered to extremely weathered basalt cobbles and boulders in
a medium stiff to stiff clayey silt matrix

encountered soft or wet zones occurring within the weathered older
alluvial and colluvial deposits Therefore it is anticipated that

materials
Residual Soil In general residual soil to extremely weathered
basalt derived mainly from the in situ chemical weathering of

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our preliminary exploration indicates that from a
geotechnical engineering point of view the site is suitable for the
proposed golf course development However some areas may require special
design and construction procedures
The preliminary geologic reconnaissance and exploration generally
indicated that the proposed grading areas explored are underlain primarily
by medium stiff to stiff clayey silts of alluvial colluvial and residual
origin locally very soft to soft recent alluvial clayey silts were
encountered in lowlying areas adjacent to the stream channels
Based on our field exploration the most important aspect of the
proposed grading design and site improvements is the stability of proposed
cuts and fill embankments adjacent to existing stream channels which may
affect the present drainage pattern
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1765 00 Page 8
Based on the preliminary grading plans selected fill embankments
will be supported by retaining walls Special design and construction
procedures may be required where planned embankments bury existing
drainageways or will be located adjacent to existing stream channels
Slope Stability
Some major cut slopes ranging from about 10 to 30 feet high are
planned for the proposed golf course construction Based on our field
reconnaissance probing data and boring data from previous studies
performed in the vicinity of the site it is anticipated that most of the
proposed cut slopes will be excavated primarily in weathered older
alluvium colluvium and residual soils However borings performed
immediately south of the site encountered hard basaltic rock at depths of
33 to 40 feet Therefore relatively hard excavation may be necessary for
the deeper portions of the cuts
It is our opinion that cut slopes less than 40 feet high may be
designed with a slope ratio of up to one and one half horizontal to one
vertical 1 For cuts higher than 40 feet a slope ratio of two
horizontal to one vertical 2 1 or less is recommended see Plate 3
Slopes should have 8 foot wide benches every 20 feet of vertical slope
height If excess pore water pressure i e high groundwater levels or
seepage is encountered in the slope these slope ratios may need to be
re evaluated
Should wet or seepage zones be encountered in the cut slopes during
excavation the installation of trench drains is recommended to intercept
Seepage and minimize potential build up of excess pore water pressure
Soft soils and high water levels 0 1 foot below existing ground
were noted in a lowlying area adjacent to Maunawili Stream near the
northern end of the ist fairway A granular stabilization layer and a
multi phase fill placement program is recommended in this area as well as
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1765 00 Page 9
other areas where soft soils are encountered to minimize potential
excessive settlement and to stabilize the soft ground for short and
long term embankment stability
Portions of the golf course development will be constructed by the
placement of fill embankments on the existing hillsides as well as in
existing drainageways and valleys In order to reduce the destabilizing
influence of the build up of excess pore water pressure a trench drain
system should be included at the base of and in fill embankments that bury
existing drainageways and valleys or are immediately downslope of large
catchment areas see Plate 2 In areas where the toe of the fill
embankments are prone to scouring by flood water toe protection is
recommended to maintain the long term stability of the fill embankments
The difficult terrain often wet grounds and heavy vegetation at the
site limited the field exploration Therefore continuous monitoring of
the site clearing and grading operations by the geotechnical engineer is
essential in order to attempt to detect site conditions which could
require design modification
Detailed construction observations are of paramount importance in
verifying the cut slope design assumptions during site grading
Verification of ground conditions by additional borings may be necessary
and stabilization measures such as horizontal drains and or buttresses
may be required to increase the stability of the cut slopes

Ground Preparation
At the commencement of earthwork operations the site should be
cleared of vegetation debris and soft materials These deleterious
materials should be properly wasted off site Prior to fill
placement the existing ground should be scarified to a minimm of
six 6 inches and compacted to a minimm of 85 of the maximm dry
density as established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Conventional grubbing and clearing operations are anticipated for
most of the project area except in the lowlying soft area adjacent
to Maunawili Stream near the northern end of the 1st fairway Probing
108 location Soft swampy surface soils and a high groundwater
level were encountered in this area Therefore a working platforn
consisting of about 12 inches of granular material such as 3B Fine
gravel over a filter fabric should be established over the surface
of the prepared existing ground prior to filling The granular layer
will also serve as a drainage blanket to facilitate settlement due to
the weight of the new fill see Plate 4
Should other soft areas be encountered during construction we
suggest that the above recommended special ground preparation
procedures be followed
Fill Material
In general most of the on site soils are with proper moisture
conditioning suitable for use as fill
Boulders less than two 2 feet in diameter may be used in the fill
provided they are not allowed to nest together and the voids between
boulders are filled
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1765 00 Page 11
Large boulders in excess of 2 foot in diameter may be used to
construct riprap or buttresses or should be reduced to smaller sizes
If additional off site borrow is required the material should be
tested and approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to
transportation to the site The material should generally have low
expansion potential and should not contain large percentages of
boulders organic or deleterious material
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area the
on site subsoils have in place moisture contents ranging from about
40 to 60 which are well above the optimm moisture content
approximately 34 to 38 Therefore drying of the excavated
material may be required prior to its use as fill material
It is our opinion that the normal 90 compaction standard used in
other drier areas should be modified to 85 for this project
Laboratory shear strength tests on compacted samples obtained from
similar soils adjacent to the site indicate that the 85 compaction
can provide adequate soil strength for the proposed embankment fill
The fills should be placed in 10 inch maximum compacted lifts
Any local soft spots and soft material encountered within the
construction area should be removed and replaced with compacted
fill Fills placed where the existing ground is steeper than five
horizontal to one vertical 5 1 should be keyed and benched into
the existing firm ground to properly bond the new fill to the slope
For details see Plate 5 This detail is essential for the stability
of the fills particularly to reduce the potential of sliding along
the fill in situ ground contact The filling operation should start
at the lowest point and continue up in level compacted layers

Excavation
The field exploration indicates that the excavations will be
primarily in the medium stiff to stiff weathered older
alluvial colluvial clayey silt However previous exploration
performed for the Mauka Golf Course encountered hard basaltic rock at
depths of about 33 to 40 feet Therefore relatively hard excavation
may be necessary in the deeper portions of the cuts and may require
special excavating techniques such as hard ripping hoe ram
equipment and or blasting
Proper drainage is essential for satisfactory performance of the cuts
and embankment fills as excessive pore water pressure could weaken
the clayey silt soils
Trench drains are recommended for proposed cut Slopes where wet zones
are encountered during excavation Trench drains installed on the
slope benches would help to intercept seepage and lower the
piezometric level during heavy rains The drains would consist of
trenches excavated to depths of about 15 to 20 feet and then filled
with gravel and a perforated pipe at the bottom of trench daylighted
to drain at a suitable outlet For details refer to Plate 6
Subdrains arranged in a herringbone configuration are recommended for
fill embankments burying major valleys or located down slope of large
catchment areas The drains may consist of 3B Fine gravel and a
4 inch perforated pipe daylighted to drain at a suitable outlet see
Plate 2 for subdrain locations This would reduce the potential of
excessive pore water pressure build up along the base of the fill at
the in situ ground contact see Plate 7 for details of the
subdrains

Slopes
Two horizontal to one vertical 2 1 slope ratio or flatter is
recommended for cuts greater than 40 feet high and all fill slopes
A steeper slope ratio up to one and one half horizontal to one
vertical 1 may be used for cuts less than 40 feet high
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling past the design fill
slope and then cutting back
Surface water run off should be diverted away from the tops and toes
of slopes and slope planting utilized to reduce erosion Slope
benches 8 foot wide are recommended for slope heights in excess of
20 feet except for gently sloping three horizontal to one vertical
3 1 or flatter golf course embankments with adequate surface
drainage
Soft materials seepage zones and other adverse geologic conditions
may be encountered during site grading and may significantly affect
the stability of the proposed cut and fill slopes We recommend that
provisions should be made in the construction budget for the
installation of subsurface horizontal drains and or rock fill
buttress which may be required to increase the stability of the cut
and fill slopes refer to Plates 8 and 9 for details
For fill embankments to be constructed at or near existing
drainageways the toes of the fill slope may be susceptible to scour
or erosion by flood water during high river stages In these areas
adequate rock fill riprap should be used to protect the fill toes
from scouring by flood water

Settlements
ca ne me i ant
Except in lowlying soft ground areas adjacent to the existing stream
courses small settlements approximately 1 inch or less are
anticipated for most of the fill areas where medium stiff to stiff
subsoils are anticipated
Special procedures are required for the construction of fill
embankments in lowlying wet and soft areas adjacent to stream
courses Probing 108 indicates that locally very soft to soft
alluvium consisting mainly of organic clayey silts may be encountered
beneath the proposed fill embankment
Significant settlements may occur in this area and other areas where
fills are planned adjacent to the existing stream courses where soft
soils may be encountered
Since structures are not planned and large settlements of the fill
areas along the fairway are tolerable the main concern would be
bearing failure and the short and long term stability of the fill
embankment
We recommend that the placement of the required embankment fill
should be carried out in several increments of about five 5 feet
maximum thickness each with at least a one 1 week rest period in
between fill placement intervals This will allow the underlying
soft soils to gain strength incrementally prior to additional loading
and reduce the possibility of bearing or Slope failure

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
probes test pits and or observation wells Variations of conditions
between the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of
these variations may not become evident until construction If variations
then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The locations of the field data points were approximately determined
by scaling from the topographic map Elevations of the data points were
approximately determined from the topographic map The physical location
and elevation of the field data points should be considered accurate only
to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines showm on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field data
points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors

Placement and Compaction of Fill
A
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers which when
compacted will not exceed ten inches 10 in thickness Fach
layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain
uniformity of material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer
which has not been properly compacted Subsequent removal of
any unacceptable layer shall be done at the Contractor s
expense
Rock fragments or boulders less than eight inches 8 in
diameter may be utilized provided they are not concentrated in
any one area and that there is sufficient fine grained material
to fill the voids between the rocks
Rocks greater than eight inches 8 in diameter shall be
removed from the fill unless specifically approved for placement
in the fill by the Geotechnical Engineer
All fill shall be compacted to a minimum of 85 percent of the
maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
unless otherwise specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s
Report
The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or
pneumatic tire rollers suited to the soil type and capable of
producing the specified results in an expedient and efficient
manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1765 00 Plate B 8
Appendix B Continued
The compaction shall be accomplished in a systematic and uniform
manner over the entire fill area to produce a uniformly
compacted fill
The Contractor shall be required to obtain the above specified
levels of compaction out to the finish slope of fill slopes
This may be achieved by either overbuilding the slope and
cutting back to the compacted core or by direct compaction of
the slope face with suitable equipment
Fill placed against previously graded cut or fill slopes shall
be properly keyed through topsoil or loose slope material into
firm material and the transition stripped of loose material
prior to fill placement Fill shall be placed and compacted
from the low side up
Fill slopes should be planted or protected from erosion by
methods indicated on the plans
Fill material placed in an unsatisfactory condition and not in
accordance with these specifications shall not be approved and
shall be removed or reworked until the required density is
obtained
The Geotechnical Engineer shall be provided unrestricted access
to the fill to perform the compaction tests
No fill shall be placed during unfavorable weather conditions
When interruption of work is due to heavy rain the previous
fill surface shall be approved before resumption of earthmoving
operations
The Contractor shall maintain the surface of the fill so that it
will be free draining and will not pond surface water
