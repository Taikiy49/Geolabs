SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the proposed K 1 Road alignment the site is
generally underlain by shallow alluvial soils wind blown soil
deposits and stiff residual and saprolitic soils The dominant
soil type encountered along the K 1 Road alignment is the
residual and saprolitic soil type The residual and saprolitic soils
consist of silts and clays with fine sands The near surface soils
exist in a relatively dry state and are susceptible to erosion due
to wind and or surface water runoff In addition some basalt
cobbles and boulders may be encountered in the shallow
subsurface especially in the first 4 kilometers of the roadway
alignment starting from the western terminus
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be
re used as a source of general fill or controlled fill provided that
the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious materials
and the maximum particle size is less than 150 mm in
maximum dimension The excavated on site materials may
also be used as a source of materials for the soil cement
pavement structure provided that an adequate amount of
cement is added to the soil to produce a soil cement product
with a minimum unconfined compressive strength of at least
5 MPa However some potentially unsuitable soils for
soil cement pavements would be encountered along the
roadway alignment
The potentially unsuitable soils are currently defined as soils with
a CBR value of less than 12 These low CBR soils generally
appear to correlate with the alluvial soils and wind blown soil
deposits Where the potentially unsuitable soils are
encountered within 400 mm of the finished pavement grade we
recommend that the potentially unsuitable soils be

over excavated to a depth of about 400 mm and be replaced
with borrow soils with a CBR value of at least 12 Because of
the similarities in appearance of the soils on the island we
recommend that additional CBR tests be conducted on the

confirm that the materials meet the minimum CBR requirement
of 12
Based on our soil cement tests we believe that a cement

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of Maui and
about 27 kilometers to the southeast of Lana i as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 1
Kaho olawe is approximately 117 square kilometers in land area with a maximum elevation
of about 450 meters above sea level at the Lua Makika summit on the eastern portion of
the island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layered thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the rift zones and caldera at Lua Makika The primary eruptive rift zones of
the former volcano extend westerly from the summit and to a lesser degree to the north and
east from the summit Two cinder cone remnants can be seen located at Pu u Moiwi and
Pu u Kamama The area at Lua Kealialalo is believed to represent the weathered remains
of a collapsed crater at the crest of the broad lava shield Some of the more recent aged
rocks composing the upper caldera filling layers of the island have been dated at about
1 million years old
Intermittent stream flow has incised gullies and some deep gulches into the residual
soil and weathered rock surface of the island The climate is generally arid due to the
island s leeward location with respect to the Island of Maui Most rainfall occurs during large
Kona storm events and as light misting showers carried by the trade winds The
geomorphology of the island is most significantly affected by wind erosion which has created
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 D Page 7
a generally barren hardpan soil surface The lower slopes of the island show exposure of
bare rock with thin soil covering suggesting that some ocean wave erosion had occurred
during higher stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch It is believed that much of the
soil cover existing below about Elevation 240 meters Mean Sea Level MSL was
historically swept into the ocean by a large tsunami Similar large scale soil erosion and coral
fragment deposition have been found on the Islands of Maui and Lana i
GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The K 1 Road alignment traverses in a northeasterly direction across the central
upland portion of the Island of Kaho olawe from the southwest coastline at Honokanai a to
near the Lua Makika summit The proposed K 1 Road follows the general alignment of the
existing unimproved K 1 Road except in the vicinity of Pu u Moiwi where a relatively short
length of bypass road to the south is planned to avoid archaeological sites In general the
proposed road alignment rises in elevation progressing toward the northeasterly
direction The existing ground surface elevations at the project site range from about
12 6 meters MSL at the western road terminus to a maximum of about 448 meters MSL
near the eastern terminus at Lua Makika The existing ground surface slopes along the
proposed alignment range from nearly flat to about 10 percent grade It should be noted that
some localized and short length slope segments exceed 10 percent grade
In general the proposed K 1 Road traverses relatively gently undulating terrain that
skirts the headland areas of several major drainage gulches which trend down from the
central portion of the island to the southern coastline of Kaho olawe As a result the
proposed road alignment intersects only some relatively small scale drainage features and
broad topographic dips that feed the larger deeply incised gulches The proposed K 1 Road
alignment makes use of available less rugged topography by avoiding the steeper slopes
and following flatter topographic features Therefore the road alignment generally avoids
the more rugged terrain associated with the gulches and other well developed erosional
gullies
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 D Page 8
As previously noted the orientation of the proposed K 1 Road alignment generally
avoids existing rugged topography However the road alignment is parallel to and
intersects multiple small scale drainage features and headlands of some broad drainage
basins The more prominent small scale drainage features were generally observed on
the down slope side of the existing road alignment Based on our observations the
drainage features exhibit varying stages of gully evolution and show evidence that active
down cutting incision and uphill migration of the gully continues to occur over the passage
of time Therefore portions of the proposed K 1 Road that are located immediately
adjacent uphill and perpendicular to an evolving drainage feature may be susceptible to
future erosion caused by the drainage feature The photograph on Plate 3 1 shows the
formation of a typical road side erosional feature
In general the project site consists of gently sloping terrain vegetated with Kiawe and
some Ironwood trees and patches of dryland grass Much of the exposed ground surface
is barren exhibiting a hardpan soil surface with occasional patches of grass vegetation and
either individual Kiawe trees or thickets of Kiawe growth The exposed ground surface is
typically mantled with a thin scattering of loosely deposited friable gravel fragments believed
to represent mineral concretions and basalt rock fragments created by the weathering of
basalt rock materials
Based on our site reconnaissance the ground surface along the proposed
K 1 Road alignment is underlain by some variable soil conditions that will affect the site
grading and construction of the soil cement pavements The variability in soil conditions
is a result of the relatively long length of the road alignment traversing through regions of
different soil deposits created by contrasts in geologic origin and modes of deposition
Discussions of the soil variability observed along the proposed road alignment are provided
in the following sections of this report

Alluvial Soil Deposits over Shallow Rock
This observed ground condition generally consists of a relatively thin deposit of
alluvial soils with embedded cobbles and boulders overlying generally hard basalt rock
material at shallow depths The thickness of the alluvial soil cover may range from about
2 meters or less depending on location The underlying dense basalt rock
formation forms an irregular undulating surface which represents the top of the most
recent a a lava flow comprising the rock formation The alluvial soils generally contain a
relatively high percentage of loosely entrained cobble and boulder material within the
alluvial soil matrix The cobbles and boulders range from slightly weathered to highly
weathered and from dense to medium hard friable in character
The boulder and cobble content in the alluvial soils generally ranges from about
10 percent to greater than about 60 percent by volume depending on the location along
the alignment and whether the materials are located within the existing rough graded
roadway or along the sides and margins of the existing road The matrix of the alluvial
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 D Page 10
soils generally range from reddish brown to dark brown in color and are classified as silts
and clays with sands and gravel Based on appearance the soils may have variable
shrink swell characteristics ranging from low to moderate in shrink swell potential
The underlying basalt rock appears to be generally very dense and exists as a gray
colored vugular rock with generally widely spaced fracture patterns The rock tends to form
rounded rock outcrops with low relief at the exposed ground surface of the existing
roadway Based on the exposure of the rock outcrops we anticipate that the rock
formation may generate some massive large diameter boulders when excavated by
ripping of the ground surface The majority of the rock formation observed at the ground
surface is moderately weathered in appearance However some zones of highly
weathered rock may be encountered that would also generate some finer graded
materials such as smaller boulders and cobbles with sands and gravel
The occurrence of the rocky ground conditions will require some special
consideration for site preparation and grading because of the generally thin and rocky
alluvial soil deposits overlying the shallow dense rock formation in addition some of the
alluvial soils may exhibit moderate shrink swell potential and may be unsuitable for use in
the soil cement pavement mixture Overall suitable soil material for soil cement pavement
construction may be less available in areas underlain by rocky ground conditions
Wind Blown Soil Deposits
Soils and sediments were stripped by wind action from exposed ground surfaces
and transported to other locations for deposition forming the wind blown soil deposits As
a result these deposits generally consist of a mixture of fine grained silty and clayey soils
that are loosely deposited in stratified layers in areas where wind currents typically subside
such as on the leeward sides of hills and amongst thickets or groves of trees
The soils generally range from brownish to reddish in color and consist of clays or
silts with fine sands and some gravel The deposits appear loosely stratified in cross
section and generally contain few cobbles or boulders The surface deposits are typically
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 D Page 11
friable and crumble easily under slight force Some weak consolidation may occur as the
deposits become buried beneath successive layers of sediments Because of the origin
of these deposits the soils may show generally low California Bearing Ratio CBR values
and some variability in physical properties thus potentially affecting the suitability of the
materials for use in the soil cement pavement construction
Residual and Saprolitic Soils
This group of soil materials composes the majority of the near surface soils to be
encountered along the proposed K 1 Road alignment Because the soil types are
generally similar in physical properties the two soils types have been grouped together for
classification and identification purposes Some differences in appearance and
composition of grain sizes should be expected between the residual and saprolitic soil
types
Residual soils consist of completely weathered products of the in situ decomposition
of basalt rock with little visible remnant rock texture Residual soils typically overlie
saprolitic soils in a generalized soil profile extending below the ground surface The
residual soils generaily are tan brown or reddish brown in color and consist of stiff silts or
clays with some fine sands and gravel On the exposed ground surface the soils tend to
form a hardpan surface with a consolidated surficial crust
Saprolitic soils are extremely weathered products of the in situ decomposition of
basalt rock showing some remnant rock texture The saprolitic soils typically are mottled
multi colored tan brown red and gray and consist of silts or clays with sands and
embedded gravel The soils typically possess a blocky soil structure with some of the
original rock texture preserved The soil is friable and can be crushed by hand to clay silt
sand and friable gravel sizes
The residual and saprolitic soil types are closely interrelated with the residual soils
typically grading to saprolitic soils at relatively shallow depths below the ground level The
transition generally occurs within the upper 0 5 to 1 meter of the ground surface in most
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 D Page 12
places on the Island of Kaho olawe In many areas such as portions of the proposed
temporary landing strip Work Package 1 and portions of K 1 Road the residual soils have
been completely eroded away to expose the underlying saprolitic soils at the ground
surface In addition localized pockets of saprolitic soils were observed to exist within soils
mapped as residual soil deposits In some locations such as at Kleins Gulch deep gullies
on the order of about 2 to 3 meters in depth expose only the residual soil profile without
indication of the underlying saprolitic soil type The exposed residual soils in the erosional
gullies often contain thin stratified horizontal deposits of gravel on the order of about 20 to
30 millimeters mm in thickness

Cracking in Soil Cement
Cracking in soil cement products pavements is primarily the result of shrinkage
caused by drying Cracks are natural characteristics of soil cement and are evidence that
the cement mixture is producing a hardened material with significant strength properties The
shrinkage in the soil cement is due to a loss of moisture and will occur soon after
construction similar to a concrete pavement The shrinkage in the soil cement pavement
will be restrained by friction between the soil cement and the subgrade Therefore when the
tensile strength of the soil cement is exceeded cracks will occur It should be noted that the
load carrying capability and performance of the soil cement are generally not significantly
reduced by shrinkage cracking
Because cracking in the soil cement is primarily the result of drying and shrinkage the
least amount of cracking should occur in soil cements having the lowest moisture content
and highest density at the time of compaction it should be noted that the optimum moisture
contents of the soils at the project site are relatively high about 30 percent Consequently
the moisture contents of the soil cement pavements at the time of compaction will also be
relatively high Therefore considering the higher moisture contents of the soil cement
pavements required at the time of compaction more shrinkage cracking in the soil cement
pavements than usual should be anticipated
Based on consultation with our soil cement expert we anticipate that the amount of
transverse shrinkage cracking in the soil cement pavements constructed with the on site
fine grained soils may be on the order of about 4 to 6 meters on center based on well cured
soil cement Longitudinal shrinkage cracking of the soil cement pavements is anticipated to
be generally along the middle of the pavement The typical crack widths could be on the
order of about 1 to wide The above discussions regarding crack spacing and crack
widths are based on previous studies performed on soil cement by the Portland Cement
Association
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 D Page 26
In order to reduce the potential for significant shrinkage cracking of the soil cement
pavement constructed with the on site fine grained soils we recommend that special
attention be given to the curing of the soil cement pavements One of the most critical
aspects of proper curing of the soil cement pavement is early and effective retention of
moisture within the soil cement pavement The soil cement pavement should not be allowed
to dry out for the first 7 days after compaction This may be accomplished by keeping burlap
pieces continuously moist or by frequent applications of water from a water truck As an
alternative to water curing consideration may be given to sealing the soil cement pavement
surface with asphalt emulsion if the use of asphalt emulsions is permissible on the island
Use of Seawater
We understand that consideration is being given to the use of seawater for the
mixing of the soil cement and grading operations since a source of large volumes of fresh
water is not readily available at the site In general it is possible to use seawater in lieu
of fresh water to mix and produce a soil cement pavement Presence of chlorides in the
seawater will likely promote high early strengths of the soil cement which is generally not
detrimental to the performance of the soil cement pavement
Use of seawater for the soil cement mixing is not necessarily detrimental to the
soil cement pavement mixture especially since the soil cement pavements have no
metallic structures embedded in the pavements such as reinforcing bars However it
should be noted that using the seawater to mix the soil cement would make the
soil cement more corrosive to metallic structures embedded in the soil cement or even
reinforced concrete structures in contact with the soil cement Appropriate design of the
metallic and or reinforced concrete structures would be necessary in these conditions
We also believe that the grading operations may use seawater in lieu of fresh water
for moisture conditioning the soils for the earthworks Use of seawater for the earthworks
would be reasonable provided that the compacted soils will be covered by paving or other
improvements However it should be noted that the soils would have a higher salt content
which contributes to corrosion making buried metallic structures more susceptible to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 D Page 27
corrosion In addition the soils may also be more corrosive to buried and or on grade
reinforced concrete structures
It should also be noted that the use of seawater for the grading operations would
also make vegetative growth on the soils with higher salt content more difficult An
appropriate soil conditioning expert should be consulted on this issue of vegetation growth
on soils with high sait content due to seawater
If seawater is being considered for use in the grading operations and the soil cement
mixing operations some additional tests using seawater in the soil cement mixtures should

to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start
at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented herein In general
fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope

Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the soil cement pavements for the
roadway should be sloped to shed water away from the pavements to reduce the potential
for ponding Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
soil cement pavements since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength of the underlying subgrade soils The edges of the
soil cement pavements should be properly backfilled to reduce the potential for water
infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should
be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the soil cement pavements
