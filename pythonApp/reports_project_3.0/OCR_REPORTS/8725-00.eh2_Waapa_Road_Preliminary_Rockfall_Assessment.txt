SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our geological reconnaissance along the Waapa Road project site discovered 
three distinct zones of subsurface conditions along the exposed cliff face above Waapa 
Road. The three distinct zones of subsurface materials include the following: (1) the upper 
layer consisting of the upper soil horizon and saprolite; (2) a mid-section layer consisting 
of columnar joint patterned basalt; and (3) a bottom layer being a massive pahoehoe-type 
lava flow at the base of the slope. It should be noted that rockfalls and landslides are an 
ever-increasing concern on and along the hillsides on the Island of Kauai considering the 
subsurface conditions of the steep slopes observed. 
The cliff face along Waapa Road is approximately 60 to 70 feet tall throughout the 
entire length of the project site. Most of the existing slopes appear to stand at inclinations 
ranging between about 0.10H:1V and about 1H:1V (horizontal to vertical). Steeper slopes 
of near-vertical slope inclinations at the eastern end of the project site appear to be 
setback approximately 25 feet away from the edge of the Waapa Road pavement. 
Based on the subsurface conditions observed at the site and our engineering 
assessment, the predominant size of the rock fragments appear to be on the order of 
3 feet or less based on the predominantly columnar joint-patterned basalt formation 
observed at the project site. Therefore, we recommend installing draped wire mesh on 
the steep basaltic rock slopes at the site to control potential rockfall hazards. The existing 
draped wire mesh system (approximately 100 feet along the road) at the western end of 
the project site appears to be in workable condition with only some minor corrosion 
observed. For the remainder of the project area (another 600 feet along Waapa Road), 
we recommend installing a new draped wire mesh system throughout the remainder of 
the project site. 
Based on the above discussions on the conceptual rockfall mitigation systems, we 
are providing the probable estimated construction cost for the draped wire mesh system 
scope of work. For the scope of work contemplated, the rough order of magnitude of the 
probable estimated construction cost is about $1,000,000 including a 25% contingency 
factor in the estimate at this stage of the engineering study. A brief breakdown of the 
probable estimated construction cost is provided on the attached Plate 5. 
The text of this technical memorandum should be referred to for detailed 
discussion and specific recommendations for implementation.

This technical memorandum presents our slope evaluation and assessment for the 
Rockfall Assessment along Waapa Road project at Nawiliwili Harbor in the Lihue area on 
the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. This technical memorandum presents our findings from our 
site reconnaissance and presents our preliminary recommendations for rockfall mitigation 
to reduce the hazard associated with potential rockfall conditions at the site. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Rockfall Assessment along Waapa Road project is at the entrance to Pier 3 
of Nawiliwili Harbor on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Map 
(Plate 1). The Rockfall Assessment along Waapa Road project site spans approximately 
710 linear feet along Waapa Road between the entrance to Pier 3 and the Aloha 
Petroleum fuel tank site in the Nawiliwili Harbor area of Lihue on the Island of Kauai, 
Hawaii. Steep weathered basaltic rock slopes containing potential rockfall hazards make 
up the northern upslope side of Waapa Road between Sta. 0+00 and Sta. 7+10 as shown 
on the Site Plans (Plates 2.1 & 2.2). 
The project site is bounded on the upslope side by Niumalu Road and on the 
downslope side by Waapa Road. The shoulder area between Niumalu Road and the start 
of the exposed steep basaltic rock slope is slightly sloped and covered by thick vegetation. 
This portion of the project site has an existing draped wire mesh system that spans 
approximately 100 linear feet of slope face along the western project limits and is 
identified on Site Plan-1 (Plate 2.1). The existing draped wire mesh system covers a 
section of basaltic rock slope that previously was cut for the Realignment of Waapa Road 
project in 1998/1999.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basaltic shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch 
(more than 2.5 million years ago). Following the cessation of this main shield building 
phase, renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the 
post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial 
sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks generally are characterized by thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the 
vents are pyroclastic materials that usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series 
generally is characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin 
clinker layers. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils, which grade 
to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed of mainly silty 
material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades 
to basaltic rock formation (less weathered) with increased depth.

REGIONAL WEATHER PATTERNS 
The Climate Hazard Review Paper dated March 2, 2022 published by the Kauai 
Planning Department describes the following changes to our local precipitation patterns: 
“Precipitation patterns are being disturbed on Kaua‘i due to climate change. 
Hawai’i has seen an overall decline in rainfall over the past 30 years, with a 
particularly dry period ensuing from 2008 to present. Consecutive wet days 
and consecutive dry days are both increasing. The heavy rainfall and 
drought periods have intensified, increasing runoff, erosion, flooding, and 
water shortages. On Kaua‘I, there have been over 81 flood events between 
2005 and 2020, with especially severe flooding occurring in 2018 and 2020.” 
These changes all contribute individually to an overall decrease in clay soil slope 
stability and when coupled together only exacerbate the situation further. Overall 
decrease in rainfall causes the in-situ clay soils to have a lower moisture content, which 
causes soil shrinkage contributing to destabilization. When high-intensity rainfall events 
occur, which are becoming the norm for rainfall events in general, the clay soil does not 
absorb as much water as it would with a low intensity event due to the low permeability 
of the clay soils.  
This results in the soil only absorbing a small amount of moisture and creating a 
significant amount of storm water runoff. The subsequent storm water runoff leads to 
alternating drainage patterns causing more slope destabilization. Below is a graph

The cliff face above Waapa Road is approximately 60 to 70 feet tall throughout the 
entire length of the project site. The exposed face may be broken up into three distinct 
areas as shown in Photograph No. 2. These three distinct areas include the following: 
1. 
The upper layer consisting of the upper soil horizon and saprolite. The upper 
soil horizon was observed to be a reddish-brown silty clay with moderately 
to severely weathered basaltic cobbles. In certain areas, this feature has 
the appearance of highly weathered basalt or saprolite with a dark brown to 
gray surface rind. The thickness of this unit ranges from the upper 3 to 
10 feet depending on the setback from the cliff edge. 
2. 
The mid-section layer consisting of columnar joint patterned basalt. The 
columnar joint patterned basalt formation is within the mid-section of the cliff 
face and has an average thickness of approximately 18 feet. The outcrop 
appears as bluish gray on fresh faces with an outer surface rind ranging in 
color from gray to tan and brownish-red. The orientation of the jointed basalt 
varies widely with near-vertical faces on the western most edge of the site 
and transitioning into overhanging faces in the central and eastern portions 
of the site. Additionally, the degree of fracture is not consistent within the 
exposure and was observed to range from moderately fractured in the west 
and central portions of the site to highly fractured in the eastern portion of 
the site. The rock texture was verified by hand sampling to have a dense 
ground mass with minimal vesicles and visible mineral constituents 
(approximately 1 to 2 millimeters in width). 
3. 
The bottom layer being a massive pahoehoe-type lava flow at the base of 
the slope. The bottom exposure exhibits as a gray to brown massive basalt 
formation, which transitions into more defined pahoehoe. The inner rock 
has a dense aphanitic structure with bluish gray coloring and occasional 
vesicles. Sections that were observed to be massive did not exhibit flow 
banding or well-defined layers and were additionally observed to be 
moderately to highly fractured as shown in Photograph No. 6. Medium to 
large banyan trees are scattered throughout this lower section of the slope 
and contributing to the fracturing of massive basalt formation at the surface 
of the slope. Scree from both basaltic units were observed at the base of 
the slope with a noticeable increase observed in the areas of the massive 
flow being overhung by the jointed basalt.

CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used to 
estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating probable rockfall activity based on input 
parameters that are assigned on a site-specific basis. The input parameters for this 
project were assigned based on the observations and measurements collected in the 
field. The program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on 
hill slope topographic profiles and other specific input information, such as rock size, 
shape, and parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions. Information 
obtained from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity, bouncing 
height, kinetic energy, and roll-out distance. The output information is useful to assist in 
the site-specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall impact 
barriers or other rockfall containment systems.

Our CRSP analysis was performed using cylindrical shaped boulders ranging in 
size from 18 to 36 inches in dimension rolling from the approximate source areas 
identified during the field reconnaissance. For each simulation run, a total of 1,000 source 
rocks were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results. Numerical input 
coefficients were selected to approximate the typical condition of the existing ground 
surfaces. 
Our rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable interception and 
catchment of 90% of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the computer rockfall 
simulation analysis (CRSP). The 90% catchment criteria is a target that is commonly used 
in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified by a statistical and 
probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences such as rockfall activity. 
Because the analyses are based on statistics and probability, a 100% catchment criterion 
is impractical and impossible to achieve.

Conceptual Rockfall Mitigation Systems 
Based on our evaluation of the existing project site conditions with respect to 
potential natural hazards such as rockfall, we believe we will need to implement a rockfall 
protection system within the study area that best accommodate the geological and slope 
conditions. Some of these systems that we have reviewed and considered include the 
following: 
Draped Wire Mesh Systems 
Rockfall Impact Barrier Systems 
Draped Wire Mesh 
A draped wire mesh system is an active slope stabilization system where an 
alloyed, high-tensile steel wire mesh is draped over the slope face and held in 
place by applying a defined torque force over the grouted rock anchors at the top 
of the slope and secured with border cables along the wire mesh sides. 
The installation of a draped wire mesh covering the slope face serves to control 
the release of loose rock that may develop on the slope face in the years following 
the scaling work. In areas where relatively small rock blocks (about 3 feet in 
diameter or less) may dislodge from the existing cut slopes over time, we 
recommend a draped wire mesh system. The draped wire mesh system should 
help retain dislodged rocks and rock fragments on the slopes, but more 
importantly, should allow for the controlled release of fallen rock toward the bases 
of the slopes at the roadway level. 
The draped wire mesh essentially contain broken rock material against the slope 
face as the fallen rock materials move downward by gravity. In this way, the rock 
fragments are deposited along the wire mesh outlet located at the base of the 
slope. A narrow containment area along the road shoulder should be provided to 
collect the accumulation of debris and allow for periodic mechanized cleanup. 
It is not the primary intent to closely pin the wire mesh to the slope face as a 
retention system. The draped wire mesh should provide positive rockfall control for 
the useful life of the drapery material. Construction of the system should be 
performed by a specialty contractor experienced in the construction of draped wire 
mesh systems. At this time, we believe several local contractors in Hawaii have 
the expertise and the specialty equipment to construct the draped wire mesh 
system. A schematic of a draped wire mesh system is presented on Plate 3.

Rockfall Impact Barrier 
Rockfall impact barriers are used worldwide as an effective rockfall mitigation and 
protection system. Rockfall impact barriers are commonly constructed on hillsides 
where the protection of downslope areas from varied and widespread sources of 
falling rock is necessary. Rockfall impact barriers are considered to be a viable 
rockfall protection measure where a specific and limited source of potential rockfall 
cannot be readily identified and stabilized, or where the source area may 
encompass disturbance-sensitive land that makes other mitigation measures 
impractical to implement. 
Rockfall impact barriers are specially designed fences consisting of steel support 
beams linked by wire rope (cable) or steel wire ring nets, which are designed to 
flex and absorb rockfall impact energy. The nets and beams are supported by 
drilled and grouted wire rope anchors at specified intervals. The barrier height is 
typically constructed at 10 to 15 feet above ground level and black in coloration 
(especially in Hawaii to help blend in with the darker basaltic rock). Rockfall impact 
barriers require periodic inspection and possible future maintenance to remove 
accumulated boulder debris and replace worn components. Portions of the barrier 
may require repair or replacement following a very large impact where severe 
deformation of the system is experienced. Ultimately, the rockfall barrier will

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and preliminary recommendations submitted herein are based, in 
part, upon information obtained from visual site observations and computer simulation 
and statistical analysis of potential rockfall behavior only. Variation in the surface and 
subsurface conditions between our observations and analysis points may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field 
exploration or construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold 
cycles, and erosion conditions. Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting 
from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), 
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time. Therefore, 
the findings and preliminary recommendations contained herein may be used only within
