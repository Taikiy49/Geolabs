Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The caldera of the Koolau Volcano extends from Kaneohe to Waimanalo and
from the base of the Pali to Lanikai The project site is located within the Koolau caldera
area
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium

In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When
the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material that is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits The
deposits of low permeability soil horizons at the base of the Koolau Range have created
a confined aquifer condition At the Kaneohe area these confined aquifers are supplied
by groundwater from dikes within the Koolau Range Since water within a confined
aquifer is under pressure boreholes that penetrate these aquifers will encounter a
piezometric or artesian head of groundwater

Based on our field exploration we anticipate the drainage inlet structure will be
underlain by very soft to soft and highly compressible recent alluvial deposits To
reduce the potential for excessive settlements to the new inlet structure the use of a
deep foundation system is recommended During our field exploration high artesian
groundwater head was encountered Therefore the use of bored types of deep
foundation system such as micropiles and drilled shafts should not be considered

Helical Pile Foundation
Helical piles generally consist of a solid high strength steel shaft with one or more
circular steel plates welded to the shaft The piles are screwed into the underlying
soils to obtain the required pile capacity Some of the advantages of the helical pile
compared to pipe pile consist of the following
Use of smaller installation equipment with lower mobilization cost
Low noise and minimal vibrations
Can be removed and re installed
Based on the above the use of helical piles is preferred compared to steel pipe
piles to support the inlet structure walls and slab We understand that the inlet
structure needs to be designed for vertical load only We anticipate the helical pile
will be installed through the recent alluvium and into the underlying older alluvial
deposit The helical pile shaft should be galvanized per ASTM A153 for corrosion
considerations Since the helical piles will be installed underwater and not into fill
material we believe that the corrosion potential is low
Because of the thick deposit of very soft alluvium buckling of the pile needs to be
evaluated Based on our analysis a stronger pile section should be used within the
very soft alluvium and above the lead section of the pile
The lead section should consist of a minimum 1 75 inch solid square steel bar with
minimum yield strength of 90 kips per square inch ksi The parts used for the
steel bar assembly should be galvanized per ASTM A153 We recommend that
the lead section of the helical anchor have a minimum length of 7 feet with 8 inch
and 10 inch diameter helices Allowable compression capacity of 10 tons per pile
may be used for the 1 75 inch solid square bar embedded about 7 feet into the
older alluvium with a minimum installation torque of 5 800 foot pounds The helical
piles should have a minimum spacing of 6 feet

Construction Considerations
The performance of the helical piles depends upon the contractor s method of

Helical Pile Testing
In general we recommend conducting a minimum of one pre production
performance test on one sacrificial helical pile prior to installation of production piles
to confirm the contractor s installation methods and pile vertical resistance The
load test should consist of a vertical compression load test on the sacrificial pile In
general the vertical load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1143 as modified herein The vertical compression load test should
consist of subjecting the helical pile to at least 200 percent of the vertical design
load and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes
During the load test the helical anchor should sustain 100 percent of the vertical
design load with no more than 1 inch of total vertical movement of the pile head as
measured relative to the top of the pile prior to the start of the testing
The production helical piles should not be ordered until acceptance of the pile load
test Modification to the pile design and or modifications to the construction
procedure may be required for failed load test
We wish to emphasize that the pile load test on the helical pile is an integral part of
the design Therefore we recommend conducting the pile load test under the
observation of a Geolabs representative

Pipe Pile Foundation
The installation of a pipe pile foundation for the new inlet structure may be
considered for the project As discussed previously the pipe pile foundation
system has some disadvantages compared to the helical pile system Therefore
the helical pile system is preferred over the pipe pile system
Pipe piles driven through the recent alluvium and into the underlying older alluvial
deposit may be considered to support the inlet structure walls and slab The use of
8 inch Schedule 80 steel pipe piles with outside diameter of 8 625 inches is
recommended The pipe pile should be galvanized per ASTM A153 and have a
minimum yield strength of 45 kips per square inch ksi Allowable compression
capacity of 10 tons may be used for the 8 inch pipe pile embedded at least 7 feet
into the older alluvium
Pile load testing similar to the helical pile should be performed A minimum of
one pre production performance test on one sacrificial pipe pile should be
performed prior to installation of production piles to confirm the contractor s
installation methods and pile vertical resistance The load test should consist of a
vertical compression load test on the sacrificial pile

Site Grading
Since the project site is underlain by very soft compressible materials the
placement of new fills should be avoided to reduce the potential for settlement and
distress to adjacent structures
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation deleterious materials and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to reduce the potential for
contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted general fill The
excavated soft soils should be properly disposed off site
In general the over excavated subgrades and areas designated to receive fills
exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
Site grading operations should be observed by qualified technical personnel It is
important that a qualified representative be present to observe the site preparation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and

Imported material used as general fill should be non expansive granular material

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to maximum depths
ranging from about 40 5 to 79 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled using truck and track mounted drill rigs equipped with auger and coring
equipment The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1
and 2 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 The Rock Description System is based on the
publication Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in
Rock Masses by the International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977

Recovery REC may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses where appropriate Recovery is defined as the actual
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt
For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality

ASTM D 2937 were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1 Test results are also
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C136 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One Unconfined Compression test ASTM D2166 was performed on a selected
in situ cohesive soil sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soil
The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 3

ASTM D2850 were performed on selected samples to evaluate the undrained shear
strength of the soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure was used
as the applied confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed soil sample The test
results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 4 through B 10
Three One Dimensional Consolidation tests ASTM D2435 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the consolidation characteristics of the soils Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 11 through B 13
Five sets of and minimum resistivity tests
were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test
results are presented on Plate B 14
