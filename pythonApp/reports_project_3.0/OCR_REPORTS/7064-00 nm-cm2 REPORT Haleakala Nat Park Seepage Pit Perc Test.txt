SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The project involves converting the existing cesspool into a seepage pit for effluent 
disposal from new septic tank system installed for the base yard maintenance facility 
within Haleakala National Park. This project is being undertaken to comply with 
wastewater regulations that prohibit the use of large capacity cesspools to dispose of 
untreated sanitary wastes. Our services provided information on the disposal capability of 
the existing cesspool through short-term injection testing. 
The project involves a total of one cesspool that has been converted to a seepage 
pit for the base yard maintenance facility septic system in the park. Injection testing was 
conducted by using a water truck to place water into the seepage pit. As such, the test was 
a short-term injection test. However, the test exceeded the estimated daily usage (i.e., the 
amount of water placed into the cesspool during testing was more than the estimated 
average daily rate of wastewater generation for the facility being served by the cesspool). 
In addition, during the test, the septic system was in use. Therefore an additional water 
injection rate of roughly 2-3 gallons per minute was being added as the testing was 
performed. The sustained injection rate of about 113 gallons per minute maintained a 
constant water head level at a depth of 10.7 feet from the top of the existing pit. 
The text of this report should be referred to for discussion and specific performance 
data only. No design or engineering services were provided under this contract.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago. It was formed by 
the activity of two volcanoes, East Maui or Haleakala, and West Maui. The broad, gently 
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes, known as the Maui Isthmus, was formed 
when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui 
Volcano.  
The East Maui Volcano, better known as the Haleakala Mountain, makes up the 
eastern portion of the Island of Maui. Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant 
with the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790. 
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the 
mountain from three rift zones, probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene 
Epochs. 
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into 
three volcanic series. The oldest, Wailuku Volcanic Series, is comprised of basaltic lava 
flows and associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks, which built the major shield 
volcano. The Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin, discontinuous “frosting” of 
andesitic and trachytic flows, domes, and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua 
Volcanic Series. After a long period of inactivity, where erosion incised deep valleys into 
the volcanic flows, volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and 
cones of the Lahaina Volcanic Series. 
The project site is located near the summit of Haleakala. We anticipate that the 
project site is generally underlain by volcanic ash and sandy gravel cinder deposits 
overlying basalt rock formation at greater depths.

Subsurface Conditions 
The interior of the seepage pit generally consisted of existing concrete perforated 
rings. Therefore, logging of the side wall materials was not possible. 
Permeability of the basalt rock formations is generally high with fluids moving 
along the coarse gravel material in a`a clinker, through lava tubes and cavities and 
along joints and fractures in the rock formation. The cinder deposits also have relatively 
high rates of infiltration due to the coarse granular nature of the material. The 
permeability of ash deposits is generally relatively low because of the silty 
characteristics of the ash. 
Groundwater in the site region is anticipated to occur near sea level far below the 
site. However, higher level dike impounded groundwater may be present at portions of 
the site underlain by Haleakala various lava flows.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS 
The project scope involved converting the existing large capacity cesspool to a 
seepage pit for effluent disposal from a new septic tank system to be installed at the 
site. This project is being undertaken to comply with wastewater regulations that prohibit 
the use of large capacity cesspools to dispose of untreated sanitary wastes. The 
objective of our services was to provide information on the disposal capabilities of the 
existing cesspools through short-term injection testing. 
Injection testing was conducted by using a water truck to place water into the 
seepage pit. As such, the test was a short-term injection test. The average water 
injection rate during the constant head portion of the testing was about 113 gallons per 
minute. In general, the test exceeded the estimated daily usage (ie, the amount of water 
placed into the seepage pit during testing was more than the estimated average daily 
rate of wastewater generation for the facility being served by the cesspool). 
3.1 
Inspection and Injection Testing 
The seepage pit, at the time of our testing, had been located, cleaned and made 
accessible for testing. The pit was first visually inspected in an attempt to obtain a 
geological log of the formations penetrated by the individual seepage pit. Unfortunately, 
the side walls were not visible due to the presence of the concrete rings. The seepage 
pit was then sounded to determine the depth to bottom and the depth to fluid, if any, in 
the seepage pit.  
Following this inspection, testing was conducted by using a water truck to place 
approximately 4,000 gallons of clean fresh water into the cesspools and by recording 
the time required for the water to dissipate (i.e., a constant head/falling head infiltration 
test). Since the actual geometry of the wells were not available and the actual 
characteristics of the receiving aquifer were not known, the performance test data was 
not subjected to rigorous analyses to estimate hydraulic conductivities or maximum 
capacities. The test data was reviewed on a subjective basis to estimate the 
performance of the seepage pit.

Inspection of the seepage pit interior indicated that the well was filled with fluid to 
a depth of about 10.7 feet below the seepage pit cover as a result of the on line facility. 
The diameter of the seepage pit was measured at 96 inches and the overall depth from 
top of cover extended to 11.4 feet. The upper 1.5 feet consisted of concrete block build 
up from the concrete ring cover to the manhole cover. Prior to the site visit, the seepage 
pit was vacuumed out of sludge deposits and then flushed with clean fresh water. The 
amount of water required to maintain a constant water level for 24 hours was not 
available as the seepage pit disposed of the water as it was injected during the flushing 
process.  
This cesspool accepted all 4,000 gallons of water and the water level increased 
by approximately 0.8 feet to hold a constant head level at an average injection rate of 
about 113 gallons per minute. Upon completion of the constant head portion of the test, 
the water recovered back to the original depth of 10.7 feet in about 25 minutes.  
Based on the information provided to us, the design capacity for this wastewater 
system is 3,000 gallons per day. Unfortunately, the contractor providing the water could 
only provide a 4,000-gallon water truck with a limited pump rate. The injection rate 
averaged at 113 gallons per minute which did not provide a flow rate sufficient, for the 
subsurface conditions at the site, to meet the project specification of maintaining a 
4 feet minimum depth of water as it was injected into the pit. However, extrapolated 
calculation based on the data obtained from the percolation test indicates that a 
maximum injection rate of 610 gallons per minute could be maintained when the 
seepage pit is filled to the top. Based on a limited waste water generation period of 
12 hours per day, both the test rate of 113 gallons per minute and the extrapolated rate 
of 610 gallons per minute exceed the estimated rate of waste water generation. 
Based on our testing, it appears that this seepage pit cesspool has sufficient 
capacity for disposal of the effluent from the new septic system. It should be noted that 
our testing used only clear fresh water and does not factor in solids loading, clogging,

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and findings submitted in this report are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual observations, short-term injection tests and field 
injection tests. Variations of the subsurface conditions may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident. If variations then appear evident, it 
may be necessary to re-evaluate the performance of the seepage pit as required. 
Locations of the seepage pit and buildings indicated on the site plan of this report 
are based on the construction set plans transmitted by Pacific Tech Construction, Inc. 
on August 4, 2014. Elevations were interpolated based on the spot elevations and/or 
contour lines shown on same plans. The physical locations and elevations of the 
seepage pit should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods 
used.

Inc. and their client for specific application to this specific project for Haleakala National 
Park, in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and 
practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of performance evaluation 
of the seepage pit for this project. Therefore, this report may not contain sufficient data, 
or the proper information, to serve as the basis for preparation of construction cost 
estimates.  
This geotechnical engineering injection testing report was not intended to

be noted that the equipment, techniques, and personnel used to conduct a
