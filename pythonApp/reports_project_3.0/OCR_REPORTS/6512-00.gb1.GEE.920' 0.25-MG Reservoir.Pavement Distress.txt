This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Pavement Distress Evaluation at the Million Gallon MG
Reservoir within Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus in Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
Our geotechnical engineering exploration was conducted in an attempt to evaluate
the probable cause s and potential remedial measures pertaining to the cracks in the
access service road pavements at localized areas of the reservoir site The following
summarizes our field observations and findings during our site visit and field exploration
and presents our discussions and recommendations with respect to the observed
distresses of the pavements at the site

Site Description
The project site is at the existing MG reservoir site within the
Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus The reservoir site is located on the east
side of Girls Road The project location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The access and perimeter service roads for the reservoir consist of asphaltic
concrete pavements During our site reconnaissance we observed horizontal and
vertical displacements at the pavement surface The cracks were located between the
area near the entry gate and the south side of the reservoir tank Based on our
observations and the available grading plans the cracks in the pavements appear to be
located at the transition between cut and fill A small portion of the south side of the site
was in a fill embankment condition ranging from about 4 to 8 feet thick Based on our
site reconnaissance the slope inclination of the fill embankment appears to be about
We observed that the area to the south of the reservoir tank between the
crest of the embankment slope and the edge of the service perimeter road was sloped
to provide negative drainage
A cut slope is present on the north side of the reservoir We observed minimal
vegetation on the cut slope face In addition we observed some erosional features on
the cut slope face Significant amounts of soil debris which most likely originated from
the cut slope face were observed at the toe of the cut slope and along the edge of the
perimeter service road
We observed some cracking and minor depression along the access road
Based on our observations it appears that the depression and the crack could be
related to the backfill of the existing water line Based on the previous plans it appears
that the 8 inch diameter water line traverses near the centerline of the access road

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of
about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Boring No 1 was drilled in the cut area of the reservoir site In general very stiff
to hard clayey soils were encountered extending to the maximum depth explored of
about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Boring Nos 2 through 4 were drilled in the fill area where pavement cracks were
observed In general stiff clayey soils were encountered to depths of about 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The consistency of the clayey soils graded to very
stiff to hard extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the
existing ground surface
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
In general the site is at an elevation several hundreds of feet above the highest basal
groundwater table in the area However groundwater levels can vary significantly
depending on rainfall temperature surface runoff groundwater withdrawal and other
factors
Possible Cause of Pavement Cracks
Based on our site reconnaissance and field exploration at the project site we
believe that the cause for the cracks and movement in the pavement surface is due to the
following
Reduced strength of the pavement subgrade soils as a result of surface
water runoff ponding along the edge of the pavement
Improper placement of the water line backfill
Detailed discussions of the possible cause for the cracks in the pavement and our
recommendations for remedial measures are presented in the following sections
Reduced Strength of the Pavement Subgrade Soils
During our site reconnaissance we observed a significant amount of soil debris
on the perimeter service road near the toe of the cut slope north of the reservoir tank
We also observed erosion on the face of the cut slope In addition the previous grading
plans indicate a drainage swale near the toe of the cut slope Based on our
observations some soil debris and grass were observed within the drainage swale
preventing proper drainage

We also observed that the area south of the reservoir tank between the perimeter
service road and the crest of the fill embankment is sloped to provide negative drainage
preventing water from draining away from the pavement The previous grading plans
show the area was to be constructed with positive drainage A slope providing positive
drainage would allow surface water runoff to flow away from the pavement and down
the fill embankment slope face Since this area is sloped to provide negative drainage
and assuming the perimeter service road was constructed to provide positive drainage
there is a high potential for surface water runoff to pond at the transition area between
the positive and negative drainage area The conditions at the reservoir site are
illustrated on the Typical Condition at Reservoir Site Plate 3
Based on our observations we believe that surface water runoff originating from
the top of the cut slope flows down the cut slope and is not intercepted by the drainage
swale due to soil debris and overgrown grass in the drainage swale Since the surface
water is not intercepted by the drainage swale we believe the surface water runoff flows
onto the pavement and possibly to the south side of the reservoir tank where some of
the surface water runoff ponds in the transition area between the positive drainage of
the pavement and the negative drainage of the area south of the pavement Silt debris
was observed at the edge of the pavement indicating that water has previously ponded
in this area This transition area is at the edge of the pavement We believe that
ponding water can easily infiltrate the subgrade soils at the edge of the pavement
softening the soil under the pavement
The borings drilled in the fill area indicated that the consistency of the silty clay fill
within the top 5 feet is generally stiff Based on our experience the consistency of silty
clay fills assuming it was placed to at least 90 percent relative compaction should
generally be hard Based on the above information we believe that ponded water
infiltrates into the silty clay soils below the pavement reducing the strength of the silty
clay soils
We believe that the pavement cracks and movement appears to be in the area of
the cut and fill transition because of the softening of the pavement subgrade soils due to
saturation The boring drilled within the cut area where the potential for water ponding
is low indicated that the consistency of the residual soils is generally hard When the
pavement is subjected to vehicle loading cracks will tend to develop in the area where
there is a reduction in the soil strength
Water Line Backfill
We also observed some cracks and minor depression near the centerline of the
access road Based on our observations it appears that the cracks and minor
depression is possibly located within the trench limits of the existing 8 inch diameter
water line We believe that trench backfill may not have been placed and compacted
properly This results in cracks and minor depression when the improperly placed and
compacted backfill is subjected to vehicle loading

Potential Remedial Measures
It appears that the cracks and movement in the pavement are due to the differing
strength of the pavement subgrade Water ponding near the edge of the pavement on
the south side of the reservoir tank infiltrates into the subgrade and reduces the strength
of the silty clay soils Remedial measures should be directed towards addressing these
items
Erosion Control of Soils Exposed at the Cut Slope
Based on our observations it appears that surface water runoff originating from
the top of the cut slope has eroded the soils exposed at the cut slope face The eroded
soils have accumulated at the toe of the cut slope and edge of pavement The
accumulated soil debris has reduced the effectiveness of the drainage swale located
near the toe of the cut slope therefore surface water runoff bypasses the drainage
swale and flows toward the south side of the site
To reduce the potential for significant erosion of the soils exposed at the cut
slope face we recommend reducing the amount of surface water runoff that flows down
the slope by constructing a drainage swale along the crest of the cut slope The
drainage swale should divert surface water runoff to a nearby drainage inlet or to an
area away from the reservoir site In addition we recommend placing a permanent
Erosion Control Matting ECM on the cut slope face to reduce the potential for
significant erosion of the exposed soils and promote vegetation cover
Surface Water Runoff Control
The drainage swale along the toe of the cut slope should be cleaned of soil
debris to increase the effectiveness of the drainage swale to divert water away from the
pavement and into the drainage inlet located at the southeast corner of the reservoir
site In addition we recommend re grading the area between the edge of the perimeter
service road and the crest of the embankment fill slope located south of the reservoir to
provide positive drainage away from the pavement This will allow water to flow down
the embankment slope and reduce the potential for water ponding in the area The
embankment fill slope should be protected by appropriate slope planting or by
placement of an Erosion Control Matting ECM since the slope inclination is relatively
steep about
Pavements
In general we recommend removing the existing asphaltic concrete and base
course materials along the distressed areas of the access road and the southern portion
of the perimeter service road The subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction If the
underlying subgrade soils are soft the soft subgrade soils should be removed to expose

Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 21 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 We used a track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and
rotary coring tools to drill the borings
Our geologist observed the drilling operations on a near continuous basis and
classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in
the field These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
test results are provided on Plate B 1
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Two Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 7012 Method C were performed
on selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt
rock encountered Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plates B 4
and B 5
