INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the slope evaluation and stabilization project at the Keanu Tunnel West
Portal in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field reconnaissance and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended to assist in the review and selection of possible
remedial measures for erosion control and slope stabilization The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Keanu Tunnel West Portal project site is located within the Board of Water
Supply BWS property in the lower Palolo Valley area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The site is bounded by Sierra Drive to the east and Keanu Street to the south
The Keanu Tunnel West Portal site is a BWS facility and storage yard for water
supply construction materials and soil stockpile The property is situated in a topographic
low surrounded by steep slopes consisting of cinder and clinker deposits Residential
properties border the site on the western through northeastern boundary along the tops of
the steep unrestrained slopes These slopes have experienced continued moderate to
severe erosion posing a risk to some of the properties located at the top of the slope
We understand it is desired to evaluate various alternatives to stabilize the slopes
along the western through northeastern property boundary to reduce erosion and protect
the adjacent private residential properties The actual BWS property line varies in

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt lava from two separate
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middie to late Pliocene in age and the younger volcano Koolau is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity and deep
valley erosion following the eruption of the Waianae and Koolau Volcanic Series eruptive
activity returned to the southeast portion of Oahu as a series of lava flows followed by
cinder and ash eruptions These most recent volcanic eruptions are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series and have been dated as mid Pleistocene to Recent in age
From a regional perspective the project site is located on the southern flank of the
Koolau mountain range The site is situated on ground produced by a relatively small
volcanic vent known as the Mauumae cinder cone The Mauumae eruptions produced
cinder and spatter cone deposits associated with the Honolulu Volcanic Series The site
appears to have been developed as a quarry at one time hence the steep bordering
slopes and topographic low at the center of the site
The cinder and spatter deposits appear to be weakly to moderately cemented and
consist of layered poorly graded gravel and cobble size rock fragments deposited grain
to grain without appreciable matrix material The bedding appears to dip toward the west
suggesting the site may be located on the western flank of the source vent Most of the
deposits exposed on the slopes are friable and crumble relatively easily by hand or by
light force of a pick hammer

Slope Stability
The slopes currently being evaluated for this stabilization project exist along the
western through northeastern boundary of the site as shown by the dashed line on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on our observation the slopes range from about
15 feet to about 35 feet in vertical height The hillslope located along the northeastern
boundary of the site has been covered by the stockpile of soil and pavement rubble
forming a flattened embankment of uncontrolled fill that mantles the original slope
surface
The subject slopes exist as irregular outcroppings of generally poorly cemented
cinder spatter cone lava consisting of gravel and cobble size rock fragments Localized
portions of the slope stand near vertical or overhang and show signs of previous
detachment of soil rock as skin type failures and as block type failures Some open
fractures were observed that could represent possible fracture planes for future
block type slope failure Because of the generally dry and friable character of the
granular cinder deposits the slopes are also subject to slow erosion by ravelling and
sloughing of the exposed surfaces as a result of cyclic wetting and drying and flow of
surface runoff during heavy rainfall These erosional processes combine to create an
on going unstable condition of gradual slope retreat
In general the subject slopes range in inclination from about one horizontal to
one vertical 1H 1V to about 4H 1V A talus apron of loose silty sand and gravel
deposits are located along the base of the slopes The upper and lower segment of the
slopes are vegetated with scattered haole koa and a few large kiawe trees

Property Boundary
Based on the available survey plan the BWS property line generally exists along
the top of the slope on level ground and behind an existing chain link fence bordering
the property The chain link fence does not correspond with the actual property line
The width of level ground located between the top of the slope and extending toward
the surveyed property line generally ranges from about 25 feet to less than about 3 feet
of horizontal distance depending on location In localized areas the surveyed property
line extends to about mid slope fronting 05 126 and extends to about the
bottom of the slope fronting 05 80 The Site Plan Plate 2 shows the

the slope

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our evaluation of the subject slopes there are several slope stabilization
alternatives available for consideration to meet the project needs Because of the slope
geometry and property line impact considerations we believe that the ultimate solution
would be comprised of a combination of earthwork grading and construction of some type
of retaining wall structures For ease of discussion and reference in this report the
subject slope was divided into three segments as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 of this
report
A major consideration in the selection of a slope stabilization alternative is the
ultimate planned use or development of the site For instance if the site may be filled
later the expense of retaining wall construction may not be prudent Furthermore if the
site may be used for future building it would not be prudent to construct large fill
embankments that would consume valuable level ground area In this respect
consideration should be given to the anticipated future use of the site so that
improvements made would better fit the development scheme In anticipation of a
transfer of property ownership it may be feasible to leave the slope condition as is for the
developer to decide the best use of the site considering the existing condition of the
slopes
Temporary Alternatives
Based on our site reconnaissance and a review of applicable alternatives for slope
stabilization temporary remedial alternatives for this site may include the following The
alternatives should be considered as temporary fixes that will be subject to periodic
maintenance or alteration
e shotcrete slope facing
e slope screening with wire mesh
e stockpile of soil materials

The use of a protective coating such as shotcrete application may be considered
although there are some limitations that should also be considered Due to the badly
eroded slope condition with very steep or overhanging segments and the generally
poorly cemented nature of the granular cinder materials the use of a spray application
slope face coating would not be a viable long term alternative to preserve the slope face
The shotcrete method would be relatively inexpensive however it would not serve as
long term protection of the slope and property located at the top The slope would remain
susceptible to surface water run off and seepage from the porous subsurface cinder
formation composing the slope The seepage of groundwater behind the shotcrete facing
could create a build up of water pressure creating the potential for block type slope failure
of the weakly cemented cinder to occur
Screening of the slope may be considered however the undulating irregularity of
the existing slope face would preclude a tight and binding fit of screen to the slope face
without some slope grading to provide for an even surface In addition some engineering
and subsurface information would be necessary to evaluate the proper anchorage system
for the screen
The temporary stockpile of soil material along the slope may be considered the
least costly alternative that may not require an engineering study or grading permit We
believe that a stockpiling permit from the City and County of Honolulu would be required
The stockpiling of soil is anticipated to consist of the dumping of various loose soil
deposits derived from off site sources and placement against the existing slope face The
accumulation of stockpiled soils would lower the risk of slope failure and erosion of the
natural slope as the height of the soil is increased However the implementation of
erosion control measures for the stockpiled soils would be required
The loose stockpiling of soil should be considered a temporary un engineered fill
and contingent upon the particular restrictions of the stockpiling permit Because the
stockpiled fill exists as under compacted soil of varying quality the stability of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
4088 00 A Page 9
stockpile cannot be defined In addition when the stockpiled soils are eventually
removed and the native slope face is exposed the potential for slope stability problems
will exist In general as the stockpile height is increased the existing slope should be
stabilized with the equilibrium of restraining forces Once the stockpile soils are removed
the slope would be subject to the removal of the restraining forces and could become
increasingly prone to slope failure The potential liability issues involved with this
alternative should be carefully evaluated by qualified personnel
If only a temporary fix is desired it may be feasible to look further into these
remedial alternatives The design life of these temporary measures cannot be accurately
quantified since it is dependent on many factors such as rainfall localized cementation
slope angle etc In general the design life of shotcrete application may be expected on
the order of several years with annual inspection required to evaluate for wear and the
need for reapplication It may be desired to utilize shotcrete application in conjunction
with the placement of soil stockpile In this case the shotcrete may be applied to the
upper reaches of the exposed native slope face to reduce raveling of cinder material that
may not be covered by the soil stockpile
Long Term Alternatives
The most desirable alternative from a long term low maintenance standpoint
would be the construction of a retaining wall along the entire western through
northeastern property boundary Because the typical slope height ranges from 20 to
30 feet significant cost would be involved with the design and construction of such a wall
Various types of walls including concrete Mechanical Stabilized Earth MSE or cellular
blocks may be considered with a range in cost The construction of a wall at the property
line would serve to maximize the available space at the slope bottom while providing a
sound retaining structure for the properties located at the top of the slope
A less expensive alternative to a standard retaining wall would be to utilize stacked
gabion cells as a retaining structure The advantages to this alternative are the relatively
GEOLABS HAWAII
4088 00 A Page 10
lower cost ease of construction structural flexibility and ability to maintain vegetated
finish slopes as steep as H 1V The gabion cells essentially consist of self contained
cellular units constructed of woven wire mesh and filled with well graded stones or rock
The gabion cells can be stacked to the required height and secured together to form a
mechanically stabilized retaining wall A conceptual sketch of the gabion wall
construction is presented on Plate 5 of this report
Another viable alternative would involve constructing an engineered fill slope at
2H 1V against the existing slope to support and stabilize the cinder slope Engineering
design would be required in support of the necessary permits such as a Grading Permit
from the City and County of Honolulu Also the acquisition of a construction easement
would be required to construct the engineered fill slopes where encroachment on private
property is necessary Soils derived from off site construction such as BWS water main
breaks could be utilized provided the soils meet the specifications for the engineered fill
A major disadvantage to this alternative is that a significant loss of level ground extending
toward the center of the site would be experienced For example a 30 foot high slope
would consume a minimum of about 60 feet of ground extending from the top of the
existing slope Furthermore the earthwork may not conform with future development
planning for the site due to the loss of valuable building space A conceptual sketch of a
fill embankment is provided as Plate 4 of this report
A viable alternative for a portion of the slope along the western boundary involves
flattening the existing slope along Slope Segment No 1 by cutting back to meet the
surveyed property boundary The finish cut slope may be designed to stand at 1H 1V to
provide for a finish slope appearance The alternative would also serve to maximize the
open space at the bottom of the slope and negate the need for construction of a retaining
wall structure However this alternative could only be implemented along the slope
horizontal space permits excavation of the slope Based on our site observations the
Slope Segment No 1 may also be left as is since it appears that adjacent private property
GEOLABS HAWAII
4088 00 A Page 11
is not currently affected by slope erosion Should the slope remain as is effects should
be limited to the accumulation of loose soil at the base of the slope
Preferred Alternative
The most permanent solution for this site appears to be the construction of a
retaining wall along the western through northeastern property boundary as mentioned
previously However construction costs would be high therefore we present the
following preferred alternative
In light of the current slope geometry and property line constraints we believe an
appropriate slope stabilization alternative would consist of the combination of slope
flattening and construction of a gabion wall
The slope fronting 05 91 could be cut back to the surveyed property line
to achieve a 1H 1V finish slope consisting of the native cinder deposits This area is
referenced as Slope Segment No 1 on the Site Plan Plate 2 For the remainder of the
slopes on the western through northern boundary a gabion wall could be constructed to
retain the existing steep slope This area is referenced as Slope Segment No 2 on the
Site Plan Plate 2 A gabion wall could also be designed to retain the slope located along
the northern through northeastern property boundary provided the existing stockpile of
loose soil and pavement debris is removed to expose the original slope face This area is
referenced as Slope Segment No 3 on the Site Plan Plate 2
As a final consideration it may be desired to leave the existing stockpile soils and
debris in place at the northern portion of the site and delay or negate the construction of a
retaining structure along the area identified as Slope Segment No 3 In general the
existing condition of Slope Segment No 3 appears to be stable with only a slight risk of
erosion or slope failure hazard The placement of the soil and debris stockpile appears to
have aided in the stabilization of the slope segment by covering and flattening the original
hillslope However if the stockpile of soil and debris is intended for removal additional

subsequent remedial work A Stockpile Permit from the City and County of Honolulu
