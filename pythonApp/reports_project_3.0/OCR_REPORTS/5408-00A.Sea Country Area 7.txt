2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age
The rocks of the Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into Lower Middle and
Upper Members The lavas of the Lower Member consist largely of thinly bedded
pahoehoe flows whereas the Middle Member consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a a
flows with a higher occurrence of the a a form The Upper Member consists primarily of
massive a a lava flows
Erosion deeply dissected the Waianae Shield removing much of the western
slope of the dome resulting in broad amphitheater headed valleys During the
Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of
the calcareous reef materials
The project site is located at the foot of Puu o Hulu Kai and Puu o Hulu Uka that
are elevational prominences on an erosional remnant ridge from the western flank of the
Waianae Range Chemical and mechanical weathering of the volcanic rocks of the
ridge has resulted in the deposition of an apron of colluvium and alluvium along the
pediment of the ridge These deposits generally consist of remnant boulders and highly

SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 2 Site Description
The project site is located at the southern end of Pakeke Street in the Maili area
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is located along the northern pediment
of the ridge crested by Puu o Hulu Kai and Puu o Hulu Uka and is bordered by existing
residential developments as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field observations the project site is presently vacant land with a
light to heavy cover of dry land vegetation such as keawe haole koa lantana and
grasses Based on our field observations and the preliminary grading most of the
project site generally slopes down toward the north with existing ground surface
elevations ranging from about 20 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the northern corner to
about 50 feet MSL at the southern end of the project site
In the central western portion of the site there is a long deep excavated area
which is a borrow pit that was used to exploit the coral formation underlying the site for
fill material at other project sites This former borrow pit is over 1 000 feet in length
averages about 120 feet in width and has a depth of as much as 30 feet below the
existing adjacent ground surface Portions of the pit have been backfilled It is
understanding that this backfilling work was done under the observation of another firm
In the southeastern portion of the site there is an existing retention basin which
is believed to have been used for runoff and sediment control during previous grading
work at the site and in the neighboring residential development This basin is
approximately 300 feet in length and width and has a depth ranging from about 10 to
20 feet below the existing adjacent ground surface
Locally at various locations around the site there are mounds of soil debris and
other materials some with heights approaching 20 feet above the existing adjacent
ground surface It is believed that most of these piles are stockpiles from previous
grading work at the site however some of the piles may be the result of unauthorized
dumping at the site Observations at the site indicated the potential for other
unauthorized dumping practices

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 14 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 extending to depths
ranging from about 11 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a surface layer of silty clay
colluvial alluvial soil approximately 0 5 to 2 feet thick overlying coral formation
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Large cavities or voids were not encountered in the borings drilled
however cavities are commonly encountered in coral formations
Boring Nos 1 and 2 encountered 9 feet and 5 feet respectively of fill material
overlying colluvium consisting of expansive clay with boulders and cobbles
Boring Nos 9 and 10 encountered what appears to be man made fill consisting
of grayish brown expansive silty clay intermixed with corralline boulders cobbles and
gravel This suspected fill material extends to the maximum depth explored in this area
of 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface This material may be backfill for an older
borrow pit or an old drainageway
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to rainfall time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other
factors

Keying and Benching
Some fills will be placed over sloping ground in varying portions of the site
Therefore some keying and benching is recommended for the earthwork to be
performed at the site These keys and benches should be firmly seated into
existing ground or the coral formation underlying the site
At the toe of fills on ground where the existing slope is steeper than five horizontal
to one vertical 5H 1V a keyway should be cut down to the coral formation This
key should be a minimum of 2 feet in depth at the toe of the fill and should be a
minimum of 8 feet in width extending back under the toe of the fill The floor of the
key should be sloped back under the base of the fill at about 10H 1V to bond the fill
into the existing slope
As fill placement progresses up the existing slope benches should be cut into the
existing slope to bond the mass of the fill to the existing ground These benches
should be a minimum of 3 feet in height and approximately 10 feet in width A
schematic section illustrating the keying and benching recommendations is shown
on Plate 3
Based on the site conditions encountered we recommend that prior to the

for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below the
formations is very small Therefore it is our opinion that probing and grouting is not
required unless the owner requires a higher level of assurance against damage from
cavity collapse Additionally the coral subgrades will be proof rolled to detect and
collapse near surface cavities

3 5 Slabs On Grade
The field exploration and laboratory testing disclosed that portions of the project
site is generally underlain by near surface soils with moderate to high swelling potential
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the shrinking and swelling of
the subgrade soils due to changes in the moisture content we recommend that the
expansive soil be kept a minimum of 24 inches below the slab cushion layer and kept
within about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content prior to final placement of
concrete The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab
and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding
around the foundation
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed below
the slab The cushion fill consisting of No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB should provide more uniform support of the
slabs and should serve as a capillary break To reduce future moisture infiltration and
subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended
on top of the gravel or BTB cushion layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or
sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is
also recommended that interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to
accommodate small amount of possible ground movements
A minimum slab thickness of 4 5 inches may be used for preliminary design
purposes provided that rebar reinforcement is provided For house and garage slabs
we recommend that rebar reinforcement be utilized The thickened edges of slabs
adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways and street sidewalks are currently planned for this
project For these concrete slabs we recommend a minimum 4 inch slab thickness
The 4 inch gravel BTB cushion layer and the moisture barrier may also be omitted for
these slabs Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways or sidewalks and at right angle intersections

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One Sieve Analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
Seven One Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on samples of the native soils
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are
presented on Plate B 3
One set of Resisitivity test ASTM G 57 and pH test AASHTO T 289 was
performed on a selected sample to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soil The test
result is presented on Plate B 4
