PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves rebuilding the existing access road to the communication
system which will be owned by DAGS and shared with State and Federal agencies The
existing access road extends from the vicinity of the Maui Observational Station to the
Radio Tower Facility a distance of about 4 500 feet Currently the road is narrow with
asphalt pavement which is severely deteriorated Numerous longitudinal and transverse
cracks are present in the road surface Additionally the portion of the road that traverses
near the base of a cinder cone reportedly experienced rockfalls and cinder slides It is
desired to improve the road and provide protection against the rockfalls and cinder slides
The Maui Police RTR facility site is near the end of the access road We
understand that a new four legged self supporting solid steel rod tower will be constructed
at the station site Detailed discussion of the facility improvement is included in a separate
report
Based on our field observation and existing topography we anticipate the access
road improvements will include site preparation minor grading new pavements and
rockfall mitigation

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago It was formed by the
activity of two volcanoes East Maui or Haleakala and West Maui The broad gently
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes known as the Maui Isthmus was formed when
lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui Volcano
The East Maui Volcano better known as the Haleakala Mountain makes up the
eastern portion of the Island of Maui Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant with
the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the
mountain from three rift zones probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epochs
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into three volcanic
series The oldest Wailuku Volcanic Series is comprised of basaltic lava flows and
associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks which built the major shield volcano The
Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin discontinuous frosting of andesitic and
trachytic flows domes and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic Series
After a long period of inactivity where erosion incised deep valleys into the volcanic flows
volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and cones of the
Lahaina Volcanic Series
The proposed access road is near the summit of Mt Haleakala as indicated on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 We anticipate that the access road is underlain by volcanic
cinder and basalt rock formation

designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of approximately 10 to
11 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown

Some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with

Based on the borings drilled the subsurface conditions at the site generally
consisted of 1 5 to 2 inches of asphaltic concrete over volcanic cinder to the maximum
depth explored about 11 5 feet below the ground surface This layer of cinder generally
consists of sand with some silt and gravel The cinder is generally medium dense and
grades to loose at about 3 feet below the ground surface In addition cobbles were
encountered at approximately 7 feet below the ground surface in Boring No 5
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However the moisture conditions of the soil samples recovered were generally moist to
saturated Also seepage was observed at localized areas along the slope cut for the
access road It should be note that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
rainfall time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered cinder sand with silt and gravel to the
maximum depth explored of about 11 5 feet below the ground surface The cinder
generally grades from medium dense to loose with depth Detailed discussions and
recommendations for site preparation new road pavement design and rockfall mitigations
are presented in the following sections herein
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography we envision that the site grading work will
generally consist of minor cuts and fills At the on set of earthwork the area within the
contract grading limits should be cleared thoroughly Debris deleterious material and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed off site Soft and yielding
areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future
improvements should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting

excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed off site Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
The materials generated from cut areas may be reused as a source of general fill
material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension If required import fill should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed basalt or coral The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Geolabs should test imported fill material for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Prior to filling the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment Compaction by water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed for the on site soils
Pavement Design
The existing asphaltic pavement is severely deteriorated with numerous longitudinal
and transverse cracks It appears that the primary cause of deterioration may be the lack
of a base course layer under the asphaltic concrete surface layer We understand that
new pavements may be required for the access road that extends to the RTR facility In
general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed pavements for this project
would consist primarily of passenger vehicles with some light trucks Therefore we have
assumed generally light to medium traffic loading conditions for pavement design
purposes
We have assumed that the pavement subgrade soils will be similar to the cinder
materials encountered during our field exploration or structural fill materials in the fill areas

Rockfall and Cinder Slides
Due to the extreme weather conditions at the project site and the loose nature of
the surface cinder rockfall hazards and cinder slide hazards are present at the project site
To reduce the potential for these hazardous events along the slope we believe that it is
necessary to implement some forms of protection at the existing hillside of the access
road The potential hazards and mitigation measures can be separated into two distinct
areas as illustrated on the Typical Slope Section Plate 4 The lower hazard area consists
of the existing cut slope adjacent to the road which has experienced localized sloughing
and occasional boulder rockfall onto the road The upper hazard area includes the natural
slope above the access road and slope cut Rock outcrops were noted on the slope and
could be a source of future rockfalls Potential mitigation methods for the upper and lower
areas are discussed below

Based on our site reconnaissance and discussion with the project structural
engineers we believe that the rockfall mitigation program for the lower area may consist of
the following measures 1 cutting back the existing rock slope to provide a catchment
ditch along the toe area 2 installation of a rockfall impact barrier along the toe area of the
cut slope and 3 covering the cut slope face with colored shotcrete Possible methods for
the upper areas include covering the cut slope with draped wire mesh or a rockfall impact
barrier at the top of the road cut slope Detailed discussions and feasibility of these rockfall
mitigation measures are presented below
Cut Slope and Rockfall Catchment Ditch Lower Area
We believe that one of the construction alternatives that may be implemented for
rockfall hazard mitigation is to cut back the existing slope to no steeper than
one and one half horizontal to one vertical and also provide a rockfall
catchment ditch along the toe area of the cut slopes However due to limited
right of way for the proposed project we anticipate that significant cutting back of
the rock slope to provide a rockfall catchment ditch might not appear to be
feasible for most of the areas along the access road In addition some
maintenance will be anticipated to remove the rock accumulation inside the
ditches if implemented
Rockfall Impact Barrier Lower Area
In lieu of a rockfall catchment ditch rockfall impact barriers may be considered
along the toe of the hillside to prevent the falling rocks from bouncing off and or
rolling onto the access road and to restrain the sloughing materials As
mentioned above due to the limited space available for cut slope and catchment
ditch a rockfall impact barrier might be a more suitable approach for the project
access road It is anticipated that some periodic maintenance will be necessary
to remove the natural accumulation of soil and rock debris behind the barriers
However we believe that erection of a rockfall impact barrier may be
aesthetically undesirable to the users of the access road and RTR facilities
Slope Surface Shotcrete Lower Area
Since a rockfall catchment ditch or rockfall impact barrier might not be developed
or constructed due to physical limitations at the project road we believe that
covering the cut slopes with colored shotcrete is a possible solution to the
potential rockfall hazards at the site Shotcrete is a slope treatment that involves
retaining all the loose material at the slope face As mentioned above the
shotcrete could be colored to minimize the visual impact to the user of the access
road and RTR facilities
Draped Wire Mesh Upper Area
We believe that covering the cut slopes with a draped wire mesh is a technically
viable solution to the potential rockfall hazards at the site Draped wire mesh is a

slope treatment that involves the installation of wire mesh panels and supporting
cables on the slope face in an effort to control the fall of rock material down the
slope face toward the base of the slope We believe the draped wire mesh cover
should assist in the controlled release of loose rock that is shed from the slope
face during weathering and erosion The draped wire mesh should provide
positive rockfall control for the useful life of the wire mesh material It is
anticipated that some periodic maintenance will be necessary to remove the
natural accumulation of soil and rock debris at the bottom of the draped mesh
Rockfall Impact Barrier Upper Area
A rockfall impact barrier could be installed along the top of the slope cut to
restrain rolling boulders It is anticipated that some periodic maintenance will be
necessary to remove the natural accumulation of soil and rock debris behind the
barriers However we believe that erection of a rockfall impact barrier may be
aesthetically undesirable to the users of the access road and RTR facilities
Drainage
Pumping in pavement structures due to saturation of the base subbase and or
subgrade soils is one of the primary distress mechanisms In addition freezing and
thawing cycles shall be expected and will likely cause distress inside the pavement
structures Therefore special attention should be given to the surface drainage of the
pavements
The finished grades of the access road should be sloped to shed water away to
reduce the potential for ponding It is essential to slope the pavement surface and to
maintain the drainage gradients so that surface water may be carried off the pavement to
appropriate drainage outlet Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during or
after construction
