Short Term Slope Stability Assessment No 6

Dear Mr Abalos
This letter report presents the findings from our preliminary short term slope stability
assessment update conducted at the Poipu Bay Golf Course in Poipu on the Island of
Kauai Hawaii This report summarizes current findings based on our review of the ground
surface monitoring survey data and our recent reconnaissance of the project site
The intent of this Short Term Slope Stability Assessment No 6 is to provide the
owner with an updated review of the slope stability condition of a portion of sea cliff
adjacent to the 15th and 16th Holes of the golf course The preliminary findings presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted within and at the end of this letter report
Project Considerations
The project site is at the existing Poipu Bay Golf Course which is in the coastal
Poipu area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii A large multiple block type slope failure
occurred in March 2006 along a section of steep sea cliffs bordering the 15th and
16th Holes of the golf course The cliff failure occurred following an extended period of very
heavy rainfall in March 2006 The collapsed portion of the cliff is within the existing
shoreline conservation zone The collapse area involved the full height of the sea cliff and
extended approximately 300 feet long by about 25 feet wide

The 2006 cliff failure is adjacent to the existing golf course 15th Hole Green and
16th Hole Tees and resulted in the loss of a section of the public shoreline access trail
Following the cliff failure and at the owner s request we have conducted five previous
preliminary short term slope stability assessments in 2012 and 2014
in an effort to keep the owner informed of the possible changes in slope stability based on
limited visual site reconnaissance
We understand that the owner is working to develop a long term plan for safe use
of the area that may include realignment of portions of the golf course and the public foot
trail away from the affected area
In September of 2011 ten ground surface monitoring points were established along
the top of the slope for periodic survey monitoring of possible ground movement This
survey data including observations from our recent site reconnaissance comprise the
basis for this short term slope stability assessment report This report is valid for a limited
period of about one year
As part of the long term mitigation plan we recommend performing a detailed
geotechnical engineering exploration and slope stability analysis to develop a basis for
recommendations for the long term use of the golf course and shoreline access path
adjacent to the area of potential cliff instability

Site Reconnaissance
We conducted our site reconnaissance on to observe the existing
site conditions for visual signs of potential large scale shoreline slope instability The
purpose of our annual reconnaissance update was to review the existing ground surface
conditions along the top of the slope for visible signs of slope instability
The top of slope reconnaissance spanned between the 16th Hole Tees and the
15th Hole Fairway along a distance of about 1 400 linear feet The reconnaissance was
conducted by our geologist under generally dry fair weather conditions Overnight rainfall
was experienced and the ground surfaces were noted to be very moist at the time of our
visit
Existing Condition Observations
Based on our reconnaissance the 10 existing ground surface monitoring points
appear to remain in satisfactory condition with no overt visible sign of significant
disturbance or physical deterioration We observed that Monitoring Point No 2 was
partially covered by a thin veneer of soil however the survey marker is affixed to the
underlying coralline rock material Based on our observations it appears the monitoring
points are suitable for providing useful ground survey data The approximate location of
the 10 monitoring points is shown on the Site Plans 1 2 Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Based on our reconnaissance and review of the existing site conditions no new
area of potential slope instability was observed In addition overt visible signs of
progressive slope instability such as ground surface crack propagation and widening were
not observed at the project site
Based on our current observations we observed existing warning signage along
the foot trail extending easterly from the existing fenced area surrounding the 2006 cliff
avulsion area However warning signage is not present extending alongside the foot trail
in the westerly direction from the existing fenced avulsion area This area is readily
accessible to pedestrians and this section of cliff is identified as having a relatively high
risk for potential instability and sudden block collapse due to existing cliff undercuts of
various magnitude We observed many fishing rod holders placed in holes along the top of
the cliff in this region indicating that the public may be frequently using the top of cliff for
fishing

Existing Ground Crack
A ground surface crack within the coralline sandstone formation represents an area
of potential cliff instability partial block detachment The crack was identified
during our Short Term Assessment No 4 The area of potential cliff instability was
reviewed for visible signs of crack propagation The open ground surface crack is in
the vicinity of Monitoring Point No 3 as indicated on the Site Plan Plate 2 1
Monitoring Point No 3 is affixed to the potentially unstable block of coralline rock
seaward of the crack Survey data collected from Monitoring Point No 3 with the
passage of time should be useful in evaluating whether subtle and slow movement
such as block rotation or sliding may be occurring
The open surface crack was initially reported as 3 5 feet in length and trending
roughly parallel to the cliff face at a distance of about 10 to 12 feet inland from the
cliff face Based on our current observations the surface crack appears to extend
vertically through the coralline sandstone formation to a depth of about 12 to 15 feet
where it intersects and terminates at an existing prominent horizontal bedding
discontinuity rock fracture At the ground surface on top of the cliff the vertically
oriented jagged crack trends 3 5 feet in length roughly parallel to the cliff face at a
horizontal distance of about 10 to 12 feet from the top edge of the cliff Beyond the
measured 3 5 foot surface length exposure the crack is not visible at the ground
surface The surface exposure and trend of the crack becomes indistinct due to an
existing windrow of piled boulders covering the extrapolated crack alignment The
existing boulder windrow pile appears to discourage public foot access out onto the
potentially unstable block of rock The existing public shoreline foot path evidenced
by a worn traffic path in the turf is approximately 12 to 15 feet further inland
mauka from the existing crack alignment Thus it appears the existing shoreline
foot path is safe for use and sufficiently away from the area of potential cliff block
instability
Based on our observations the potentially unstable block of rock appears to remain
at least partially attached to the adjacent backslope and likely completely attached
to the backslope along the eastern perimeter of the block where surface cracking is
not observed It appears the block is bounded by the exposed southerly and
westerly cliff faces but remains at least partially attached to the adjacent slope
along the trend of the backslope fracture Cracking or fracturing is not visible at the
ground surface along the eastern side of the cliff block In addition the basal rock
terrace that the fractured block is founded on is rough and nearly flat with minimal
seaward slope

Ground Surface Monitoring
As mentioned previously overt visible signs of large scale ground movement
along the top of slope between the 16th Hole Tees and the 15th Hole Fairway were not
observed at the time of our reconnaissance Although we observed no visible indication
of imminent large scale slope instability the timing of possible large block type slope
failures cannot be predicted and the adequacy of relying on periodic visual observations
to assess the stability of the slope is limited
Based on our review of the available monitoring point survey data no significant
deflection of the ground surface markers were detected by the survey data collected
from Monitoring Point Nos 1 through 10
Due to the limitations associated with visual observations we recommend
continuing the annual site reconnaissance and ground survey monitoring program The
assessments should be conducted at least annually and at interim times following
significant natural hazards such as large earthquake tsunami impact or an extreme
storm rainfall event
The ground surface monitoring data provided by the surveyor for Monitoring
Point Nos 1 through 10 is presented in Appendix A

Public Access at Toe of Slope
Based on our observations the shoreline along the toe of the cliff alignments are
highly susceptible to the real danger of falling rock hazards Natural weathering and
erosion contribute to periodic toppling failure of existing loose rock blocks and boulders
comprising the top edge of the cliffs The public should be discouraged from accessing
the shoreline directly below the coastal cliffs
Long Term Setback Recommendations
We recommend adding to and maintaining the existing temporary safety fencing
and warning signage such that public access makai of the fencing is discouraged until a
permanent mitigation scheme is implemented It is our opinion the permanent mitigation
could consist of a setback and realignment of the affected portion of the golf course
further inland and away from the zone of potential coastal sea cliff instability
For preliminary planning purposes a safe use setback may be approximated by
extrapolating a hypothetical failure surface at about a one fourth horizontal to
one vertical inclination extending from the maximum undercut demarcation
to the top of slope elevation This assumes that a future large block type slope failure
would initiate at the assumed undercut depth and the new cliff face produced would
boundary assuming a large block type failure encompassing the existing undercut
depths is presented on Site Plans 1 2 Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The estimated new
top of slope boundary may be used for preliminary planning purposes provided some
additional buffer space beyond the extrapolated boundary is included to estimate the
preliminary setback boundary
A formal geotechnical engineering exploration is recommended to formulate
appropriate long term setbacks from the cliff face for portions of the golf course located
in close proximity to the steep potentially unstable cliffs
