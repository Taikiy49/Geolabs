INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Mauna Olu Non Potable Reservoir Infrastructure Repair project located
in the District of Makaha on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The site is located within the
Mauna Olu Estates Subdivision The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the grading
construction reservoir lining utility structures and utilities only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
GEOLABS INC
W O 4223 00 Page 5
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred
to as the Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
material which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series The Waianae
Volcanic Series is divided into lower middle and upper volcanic members The lower
member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that built the main mass of
the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock that accumulated and
gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a relatively thin capping
layer which covers the entire top of the shield volcano late in its history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of Oahu occurred which produced the large valleys of the
western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were gradually filled with
enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined result of
stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the island mass Over
time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and colluvial
deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation formed relatively dense
deposits However the current surficial deposits above these older deposits are typically
observed to be unconsolidated

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings near the Kaneaki Heiau along part of the new water alignment designated as
Boring Nos 1 and 2 and two borings near the reservoir designated as Boring Nos 3 and
4 to depths of about 15 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Near the Kaneaki Heiau along part of the new water line alignment our field
exploration generally encountered alluvial deposits consisting of stiff reddish brown clay
with cobbles from 4 5 to 5 5 feet below the existing grade underlain by loose to dense
boulders with grayish brown or orange brown basalt gravel to the final depths of the
borings at about 15 to 16 5 feet below the existing grade Groundwater was not
encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
At the Mauna Olu Non Potable Reservoir our field exploration generally
encountered dense to very dense grayish brown clayey basalt gravel from 6 3 to 7 feet
below the existing grade underlain by very stiff dark brown or reddish brown silty clay to
the final depths of the borings at about 15 4 to 16 3 feet below the existing grade
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
It should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may fluctuate
depending on seasonal precipitation and other factors Detailed descriptions of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 4 of Appendix A Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil

Utility Structure Design
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow footings may be used for support of the proposed overflow box and the
inlet channel structures An allowable bearing pressure of may be utilized for the
design of the overflow box and inlet channel structures bearing on the on site dense clayey
basalt gravel very stiff silty clay or properly compacted select granular fill material This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Footings should be
embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Footings should be
GEOLABS INC
W O 4223 00 Page 9
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 8 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footing to the slope face
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing bottom they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be deepened to the underlying firm materials
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pcf Resistance due to friction may be estimated using a coefficient of
friction of 0 35
The overflow box and inlet channel structures should be designed to resist an
equivalent fluid earth pressure of 55 pcf This value assumes that the structure is under
a restrained condition not able to rotate more than 0 5 percent of the wall height This
value does not account for surcharge or hydrostatic loads Surcharge stresses due to
areal surcharges live loads and point loads should be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the overflow box
and inlet channel structures a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 45
percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall may be
used in the design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the
surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
Backfill behind the overflow box and inlet channel structures should consist of
concrete or CLSM low strength concrete or flowable fill If it is desired to allow
possible future excavation with normal construction equipment then CLSM with a
compressive strength of 50 to 250 psi should be utilized
GEOLABS INC
W O 4223 00 Page 10
Due to the difficulty in achieving compaction on a sloping ground with a thin soil
cover over the utility lines we recommend that the 24 inch diameter overflow line located
between the overflow box and the reservoir be encased above and below pipe with either
concrete or CLSM low strength concrete or flowable fill This should also reduce the
potential of damaging the utility lines If it is desired to allow possible future excavation with
normal construction equipment then CLSM with a compressive strength of 50 to 250 psi
should be utilized Note that the weight of the pipe should be greater than the bouyant
force to prevent movement of the pipe during backfill placement
We recommend that overflow box overflow line and inlet channel excavations be
observed by a representative of Geolabs Inc prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be
used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill
immediately around utility pipes is not recommended

Reservoir Liner Subgrade and Anchor Trench Fill Repair
Prior to the installation of the new reservoir liner the upper 12 inches of existing
anchor trench material should be removed in 25 foot long sections The excavated
subgrade should then be moisture conditioned to at least optimum moisture and
compacted to 90 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 91 The remaining
excavated material should be moisture conditioned to at least optimum moisture placed in
the anchor trench and compacted to 90 percent relative compaction
Poor subgrade conditions beneath the existing reservoir liner will be determined by
the geotechnical engineer during a site reconnaissance of the exposed liner material after
the reservoir is drained Poor subgrade conditions will be based on visual observations
i e uneven subgrade and on yielding areas
Poor subgrade conditions beneath the existing reservoir liner as determined by the
geotechnical engineer after the reservoir is drained should be handled as follows
GEOLABS INC
W O 4223 00 Page 13
1 The liner at affected areas should be cut out and rolled back to expose the
subgrade material
2 Soft soils should be removed to firm material and replaced with select
granular fill moisture conditioned to at least optimum moisture
andcompacted to a minimum 90 _ percent relative compaction
ASTM D 1557 91
3 The new subgrade material should then be covered with a non woven
geotextile fabric AMOCO 4546 or equivalent and secured in place with
galvanized nails
4 The existing reservoir liner should then be replaced and resealed with proper
jointing techniques
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Inc It
is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operations to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the site preparation
process and to confirm that the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those

In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill
material provided the largest rock fragments in the material is less than 3 inches in
maximum dimension and that the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious
materials In addition the on site materials to be used as general fill should be relatively
well graded and should have a minimum of 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve
size
Select granular fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or
less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The on site clayey
GEOLABS INC

gravel materials are suitable for use as select granular fill material The on site clay and
silty clay materials are unsuitable for use as select granular fill
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by vibratory rollers multiple wheel
pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Because
moisture conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of earthwork
observations and soil density tests should be performed by Geolabs Inc during site
grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the
proper moisture content Where compaction is less than required additional compactive
effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the
specified compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths of about 15 to 16 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
auger equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 and D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive
are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three 3 one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures These
tests were run on air dried samples Results of these swell tests are presented on
Plate B 1
Two 2 Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Results of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 2
Two 2 sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification
The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 3
