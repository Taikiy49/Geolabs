Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff silty clay underlying
asphaltic concrete and base course materials extending to the maximum depth explored
of approximately 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater was not
encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration
Based on our field observation and subsurface exploration we believe that the
significant cracking on the existing playcourts tennis court and basketball courts was
caused by earth movement along the slopes on the edge of the playcourts and swelling of
the subsurface soils We recommend that the expansive materials be over excavated
3 feet and backfilled with properly compacted granular fill materials before the pavement

the playcourts perimeter to retain the fill materials underlying the playcourts and to reduce
moisture migration under the pavement

that the distress of the existing pavement was primarily caused by insufficient pavement
thickness at the road and parking lots and poor drainage Therefore new pavement
sections are recommended for the road and parking lots Considering the relatively wet
weather condition in the Waimanalo area we recommend that the pavement surface be
sloped and drainage gradients be maintained to carry the surface water off the pavement
to appropriate drainage structures

SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Repave Playcourts Road and Parking Lots project located within the
Waimanalo District Park in the District of Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design
of pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located within the existing Waimanalo District Park in the District
of Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided the
project involves repaving the existing playcourts road and parking lots The existing
asphaltic concrete pavement was observed to be deteriorated with aggregates exposed
on the majority of the paved surface areas Large potholes and depressions were also
observed in several paved areas Furthermore deep and wide cracks are present at
the eastern and northern ends of the basketball courts and southern and eastern ends
of the tennis courts

utilized to develop a soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of pavements for the proposed pavement
improvement project In order to accomplish this we conducted an exploration program
consisting of the following tasks and work efforts

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of
lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load

consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines
When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary
rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in areas above the valley
floors and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

Hihimanu Street in the District of Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project

of base course Very stiff silty clay clayey silt was encountered below the base course

Fills and Backfills
Fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed finished
subgrades should consist of select granular fill such as crushed coral or basalt
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension In addition the material should contain between 10
and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should meet the
material requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in
Sections 703 06 and 703 17 respectively of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 HSS Imported fill materials
should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The aggregate
subbase and base course materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

optimum moisture is an important requirement considering the moderately to highly
expansive nature of the on site clayey soils
Significant cracks up to 4 inches wide were observed along the north south and
east edges of the playcourts Based on the field observation the playcourts are
situated on fill materials which is sloping down to the adjacent areas Along the
northern edge of the tennis courts and basketball courts fill materials against the
concrete playcourt edges were eroded and the concrete headers appeared to be tilted
A wide gap between the concrete header and paved area was generated which created
a path for water to travel through the pavement section and get trapped under the base
course We believe that the underlying clayey material had expanded when the
moisture content changed because of water accumulation The expansion resulted in
stress within the soil body and caused earth movement towards the down slope

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As mentioned previously the on site clayey soils exhibited moderate to high
expansive potential when subjected to moisture fluctuation In order to reduce future
distress resulting from the soil expansion we recommend that the subgrade material be
over excavated a minimum of 3 feet and backfilled with select granular fill The fill
materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
We also recommend that a deeper 18 inch cutoff wall be constructed along the
north south and east edges of the playcourts to help confine the pavement section
The cutoff wall should be a minimum of 6 inches thick
3 3 Road and Parking Lots
Severe pavement distresses including weathering and raveling depression and
cracking were observed along the service road and within two parking lots Additionally
water ponding was observed in areas of the access road and along the edges of the
northern parking lot As mentioned previously wet soils were encountered in two of our
borings which were drilled near existing utility manholes We believe that the wet soil
conditions may be the result of relatively wet weather conditions and possibly utility line
leakage Based on the pavement distress types and our field exploration we believe
that insufficient pavement thickness and poor drainage condition may be the primary
causes of the pavement distresses
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing the near surface soils at
the parking lot appear to have a CBR value of about 2 5 with an expansion value of
about 6 1 percent In general we anticipate the vehicle loading for the parking lot of this
project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles light pick up trucks and handivans
Based on the above assumptions we believe that the existing pavement section is
insufficient Therefore we recommend that the following flexible pavement section be
used

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of
about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring
tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples of the upper clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Six one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface
soils The results of the test are summarized on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 3
