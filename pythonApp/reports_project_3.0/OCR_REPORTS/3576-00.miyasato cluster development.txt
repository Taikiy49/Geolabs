INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Miyasato Cluster Development project located in the Aiea
area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation site grading utility
trench and pavement only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised
deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are then transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3576 00 Page 6
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and density
are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

The proposed Miyasato Cluster project is located near the intersection of
Honomanu Street and Moanalua Road in Aiea on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project
site is bounded by an existing residence to the east and by existing fences to the south
north and west as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project site was vacant and heavily vegetated with grasses weeds and trees

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings to depths of about 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered stiff fills consisting of clayey silts and
silty clays with sands gravels and boulders to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Below the fills highly weathered rock consisting of dense sandy gravels
and very stiff clayey silts and silty clays were encountered extending to the maximum
depth drilled of about 21 5 feet below the ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to seasonal rainfall time of year tidal fluctuations groundwater withdrawal and or
injection and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on
the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8 of Appendix A
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the site generally
consist of a surficial fill layer of about 5 feet in thickness overlying highly weathered rock
The near surface fill materials generally consist of clayey silts and silty clays with
abundant gravels cobbles and boulders Based on the results of our field exploration
we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of slab on grade with thickened
edge footings may be used to support the proposed structures However due to the
variable nature of the near surface fill soils encountered we recommend that a minimum
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3576 00 Page 8
of 24 inches compacted engineered fill be provided below the bottom of the slab on grade
foundation for adequate foundation support
Laboratory tests performed on the near surface silty and clayey soils indicated that
the materials generally have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to
moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential effects of the moderately to highly
expansive soils we recommend that concrete slab on grade foundation be supported on
24 inches of non expansive capping fill
Alternatively a foundation system consisting of drilled piers with raised wood floor
may be used in lieu of the slab on grade foundation for support of the proposed
structures Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundation site
grading pavement and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the
following sections of this report
Slab On Grade Foundations
We understand that the proposed new residences will be one to two story
structures constructed near the existing grades Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered in our exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of
slab on grade with thickened edge footings bearing on the compacted engineered fill may
be used to support the proposed structures
Laboratory tests performed on the near surface silty and clayey soils at the
building locations indicated that the materials generally have a moderate to high
shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential of
structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on site moderately to highly expansive
soils we recommend that slabs on grade foundation be supported on a minimum of
24 inches of non expansive select fill material The non expansive select fill should
extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the perimeter of the proposed structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3576 00 Page 9
The non expansive select material should consist of well graded granular materials of
less than 3 inches in maximum diameter The material should have a laboratory
California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or more and should have a maximum swell of
less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1883
Prior to placement of the non expansive select fill material the existing fill soils
within the building footprint should be removed to a depth of 24 inches below the finish
subgrade elevations bottom of the cushion fill The exposed subgrade to receive non
expansive select fill should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction In general the non expansive select fill material
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95
percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch
cushion fill for more uniform support The cushion fill layer should consist of open graded
gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or basalt termite
barrier which will also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab subgrade areas
should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a 6 inch
layer of basalt base course compacted to 95 percent relative compaction be used above
the non expansive fill for a uniform support of the slab For design of structural slabs a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3576 00 Page 10
modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 per cubic inch p c i may be used for the
compacted base course select fill
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration of
moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform curing of the
slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane we recommend that a 2
inch layer of moist fine sand be placed above the membrane prior to placing the slab
concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to placing concrete
For the design of structures bearing on subgrade prepared in accordance with the
above recommendations an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 p s f may be
used The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces The
thickened edge footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade
If the foundation is designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total footing settlements under the anticipated design loads are estimated to be on
the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements of approximately inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings and the
supporting materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f for
footings embedded in the sandy soils These values assume that the concrete for

Drilled Pier Foundations
Alternatively a drilled pier foundation system with raised wood floor may be used
place drilled piers would derive vertical support from a combination of friction between the
shaft and the surrounding soils and end bearing of the drilled piers Based on the subsoil
conditions encountered in the borings we believe that an allowable compression load
capacity of up to about 15 kips per pier may be used for a 12 inch diameter drilled pier of
at least 8 feet deep Where the existing fills are encountered deeper during construction
the depth of the drilled piers may need to be extended to provide a minimum of 2 feet
socket into highly weathered rock The allowable bearing pressure value is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces An uplift capacity of up to 5 kips may be used for the drilled pier
foundation based on skin friction and weight of the drilled pier foundation Drilled piers
should have a minimum center to center spacing of 3 feet
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations can be resisted by passive resistance on
the vertical faces of pier caps where applicable Resistance due to passive pressure
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per cubic foot p c f
Pier settlements are estimated to be on the order of inch Differential settlement
between piers are estimated to be on the order of about inch We believe a significant
portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

It should be noted the subsoils at the site consist of fill soils with abundant
boulders over decomposed rocks Therefore difficult drilling should be expected for the
drilled pier construction In general drilling through the boulders and highly weathered
rock may also slow down the drilling process and may need special tools A
representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to observe the installation of drilled
piers including drilling rebar cage placement and concrete placement during
construction
A low shrink high slump concrete mix 6 to 8 inch slump range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled piers and the surrounding soils The concrete
should be placed promptly after drilling to reduce the potential of cave in The concrete
should also be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from
the concrete mix Where groundwater or perched water is encountered in the drilled
holes placement of concrete by tremie method is recommended During concrete
placement a minimum of 5 feet of concrete should be maintained above the bottom of
the tremie pipe
Site Grading
Based on the preliminary grading plans provided we anticipate that site grading
will consist of cuts and fills on the order of 3 feet for the proposed construction Site
grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is
important that a representative from our office observe whether the exposed soil
conditions during excavation are similar to those encountered in our exploration The
following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site
grading work
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation boulders debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be completely removed and properly disposed of off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3576 00 Page 13
In areas to receive fill the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For
subgrades of improvements subjected to vehicular traffic such as pavements and
driveway the compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Based on the current design concept the 24 inch non expansive select fill
recommended below the slab on grade should consist of imported granular material such
as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or higher and a
swell of not more than one percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 to 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve All non expansive select fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to 95 percent
relative compaction All surfaces should be finished to create smooth unyielding
subgrades and should be kept moist until covered by concrete slabs or pavements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3576 00 Page 14
In general the on site materials may be reused as a source of general fill and
backfill materials It should be emphasized that the on site silty and clayey soils should
not be used as non expansive select fill below slabs on grade because of the moderately
to highly expansive nature of the materials Where used as general fill and backfill
materials the on site silty and clayey soils should be properly moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
Permanent cut and fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched

will be similar to the silty soils encountered in the borings with a minimum California
Bearing Ratio CBR of 4 and swell of less than 4 5 percent Based on the above
assumptions we recommend the following pavement sections be used for preliminary
design purposes

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The base course and select
borrow should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of

Laboratory CBR tests should be performed on samples of the actual subgrade

than the above laboratory CBR test results the pavement sections recommended above

We understand that new utility lines would be required for the proposed project It
should be noted that boulders were encountered near the existing ground surface
Therefore we envision that trench excavation will likely encounter boulders In general

Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were conducted in three drilled borings Boring Nos 1 through 3
to provide data for design of on site drainage system for disposal of stormwater runoff
The falling head test method was used due to the silty and clayey soils encountered in
the borings The falling head test was performed by measuring the rate of drop of the
water level at different time intervals
Based on the percolation test results the near surface soils at Boring Nos 1
through 3 have low hydraulic conductivities ranging from to 1 9x10 feet per
minute The results of our percolation tests are presented in Appendix B of this report

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into
the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface soils The
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately
after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling eight
borings to depths of about 5 and 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Percolation Testing
Three percolation tests were performed in three drilled borings Boring Nos 1
through 3 during our field exploration The falling head test method was used due to the
silty and clayey soils encountered in the borings The falling head test was performed by
measuring the rate of water level drop after filling up the borehole with water
The test data and the corresponding calculated percolation rates of the subsoils
are presented on Plates B 1 through B 3 Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are summarized on
Plate C 1
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits Graphic presentation of the test results is provided
on Plate C 2
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM Test Method D 1883 was
performed on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the soil strength for
preliminary pavement design Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate C 3
