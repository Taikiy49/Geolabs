REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be of 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be of late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, 
lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a 
broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau. 
The project is located in the Kualoa area within the District of Koolaupoko on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The coastal area of Kualoa lies within the Coastal Plain province 
of Oahu and is located to the east of the Ko’olau Mountain province.  As a result, much 
of the generally flat land area is underlain by unconsolidated coastal sediments (coralline 
sands and silts) with pockets of hard, cemented sand dunes (sandstone) and 
coral/limestone rock formation. 
Progressing from the coastal area toward the hills of the Ko’olau Mountains, the 
subsurface conditions gradually change to reflect an increase in thickness of terrestrial 
sediments.  The terrestrial sediments include the alluvial soils derived from the hills and 
valleys located southwest of the coastline.  These alluvial soils may overlie buried coral 
formations and sand deposits in the subsurface.  The deposition of sediments has 
continued from earlier geologic times through the present.  Subsequently, development 
within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and P-1 through P-4, extending to depths of about 
3.5 and 17 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the borings 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
In general, our borings encountered a relatively thin surface fill layer about 1.5 to 
4.5 feet thick consisting of 4 inches of asphaltic concrete, medium dense sandy gravel, loose 
silty sand, and stiff clayey silt underlain by alluvium, or beach deposits and lagoonal deposits 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 17 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Asphaltic concrete and sandy gravel were encountered only in the two borings 
drilled at the driveway area. The alluvium encountered generally consisted of stiff sandy 
clay, clayey silt, and silty clay. A thin layer of weathered sandstone, consisting of dense 
moderately well-cemented sand, was encountered in the deep boring drilled at depths from 
about 4.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The beach deposits encountered 
generally consisted of very loose to medium dense silty sand.  The lagoonal deposits 
encountered varied from loose silty sand to very soft clayey silt. 
Groundwater was encountered in the deep boring drilled at depths of about 6.6 and 
7.3 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. The 
groundwater levels measured generally correspond to about Elevations +1.5 and +2.2 feet 
MSL with the estimated existing ground at about Elevation +8.8 feet MSL based on the 
topographic survey map provided. It should be noted that the groundwater level can 
fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations, storm surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, 
groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and other factors.

Our field exploration generally encountered a relatively thin surface fill layer about 
1.5 to 4.5 feet thick underlain by alluvium, or beach deposits and lagoonal deposits extended 
to the maximum depth explored of about 17 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered in the deep boring drilled at depths of about 6.6 and 7.3 feet

system with a new septic tank and leaching field system for the Kualoa Regional Park

Septic Tank 
We understand that the proposed septic tank consists of a 1,200-gallon precast 
concrete tank with plan dimensions of 8 feet 6 inches by 5 feet 9 inches and a height of 
6 feet. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings drilled at the site, 
we anticipate that the septic tank will be bearing on the very loose silty sand in the vicinity 
of the groundwater level. Therefore, we recommend providing a bedding layer consisting of 
6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) on top of a stabilization layer 
consisting of 24 inches of No. 2 Coarse Rock (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the bottom of the septic tank 
structure for uniform support and to improve the bearing resistances.  
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 1,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the concrete tank bearing on the recommended bedding and 
stabilization layers. The bearing value recommended is for dead-plus-live-loads and may be 
increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
If the foundation is designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundation to be less than 1 inch.

Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will generally consist of 
minimal cuts and fills except for the excavation and installation of the new septic tank. At the 
on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared. 
Vegetation, construction debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should 
be removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a designated area to reduce the potential 
for contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
After clearing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over-excavated to 
expose firm natural ground, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with imported 
fill materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.

believe that a temporary cut slope for open-cut excavation may be practical, provided the 
space for the cut slope is available. For the relatively thin surface fill layer consisting of stiff 
clayey silt, a temporary cut slope may be designed with a slope inclination of one horizontal

Imported fill and backfill materials should consist of non-expansive select granular fill 
material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The materials should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine, with particles no larger than 3 inches in their largest 
dimension. The material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent fines (particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve). Select granular fill should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or 
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. 
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. In areas where there will be 
pavements, the upper 3 feet of the fill/backfill below the pavement finished grade should be 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Corrosivity Testing 
One set of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
was performed by our laboratory and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a 
selected soil sample obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the 
on-site soils.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils have a 
resistivity value of about 10,000 ohm-cm. Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may 
be considered negligibly to mildly corrosive (Corrosion Category B) based on the Board 
of Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control 
Standards, dated 2021.  
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. 
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the tested 
chloride and sulfate content values are relatively low. It may be appropriate to consult

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and P-1 through P-4, extending to depths of about 3.5 and 17 
feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the
