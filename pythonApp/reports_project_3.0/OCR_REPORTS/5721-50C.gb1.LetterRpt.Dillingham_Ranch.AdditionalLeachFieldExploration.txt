In accordance with our revised proposal dated we have performed a
geotechnical engineering exploration and field percolation testing program for the
proposed agricultural subdivision at Dillingham Ranch in the Mokuleia area on the Island

percolation testing at three potential leach field sites and presents our discussion and

preliminary planning only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed agricultural subdivision project is at the foot of the north facing
slopes of the Waianae Mountain Range in Mokuleia on the Island of Oahu Hawaii We
understand the project would involve the development of about 92 large agricultural lots
on gently sloping terrain along the base of the mountains
As part of the development the proposed sewage disposal system for the
agricultural lots will consist of septic tanks at each individual lot The sewage effluent at
each lot will be conveyed to a large leach field located down slope of the lots
An initial leach field area was located in the area northwest of the intersection
between Cane Haul Road and the main ranch road This area is bounded by Cane Haul
Road on the south side the main ranch road on the east side and the drainage ditch on
the west side Based on our previous explorations the majority of the initial leach field
area is generally underlain by clayey soils to depths of about 8 to 13 feet below the

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings at
Area No 1 five borings at Area No 2 and three borings at Area No 3 The borings
designated as Boring Nos 101 and 102 at Area No 1 Boring Nos 201 through 205 at
Area No 2 and Boring Nos 301 through 303 at Area No 3 extended to depths ranging
from about 15 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings drilled at Area No 1 generally encountered medium stiff to stiff clayey
silts and medium dense silty sands extending to depths of about 6 to 10 feet below the
existing ground surface The silts were underlain by very stiff to hard clayey soils at
Boring No 101 and soft clayey soils at Boring No 102 The clayey soils extended to the
maximum depth explored of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface
In general our borings drilled at Area Nos 2 and 3 encountered clays and silts
extending to depths ranging from about 6 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface
Below the clays and silts coralline deposits consisting of sand and gravel were
encountered extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16 5 feet below the
existing ground surface
We encountered groundwater at a depth of about 10 7 feet below the existing
ground surface in Boring No 102 at Area No 1 We encountered groundwater at depths
of about 4 to 9 2 feet below the existing ground surface at Area No 2 and at depths of
about 10 1 to 10 5 feet below the existing ground surface at Area No 3
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Borings in Appendix A Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples
obtained during our field exploration and the test results are presented in Appendix B

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
We have conducted field exploration and percolation tests at Area and 3
to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary planning and design of the sewage disposal
system for the Dillingham Ranch Development project
Field Percolation Testing
Field percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The percolation tests were performed by drilling
4 0 inch diameter boreholes The depths of the percolation tests ranged from about 2 to
5 feet below the existing ground surface It should be noted that the percolation tests were
performed at depths that were at least 2 feet above the groundwater level in accordance
with standard guidelines
Approximately 1 inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to
protect the bottom of the holes from scouring and sediments During testing the holes
were carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel The time
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which calculation of the
percolation tests were based

Generally the percolation tests were performed in clays with the exception of
Boring No 101 which was performed in coralline sand and Boring No 203 which was
performed in dark brown silty sand Based on the results of the percolation tests the
clays generally have an average percolation rate of about 74 minutes per inch indicating
very low permeability which is typical for clayey soils
The faster percolation rates high permeability of the clays obtained in some test
could be attributed to the presence of localized sands and gravel within the clays These
test results have been identified with an asterisk
Boring No 101 located at Area No 1 had a faster percolation rate due to the
presence of silty sand However Boring No 102 encountered clayey silts Our
experience indicates that clayey silt soils typically have slower percolation rates Based
on the borings drilled at Area No 1 we believe that the subsurface conditions at Area
No 1 would be variable therefore the percolation rate obtained from Boring No 101
may not be indicative of the percolation rate for the entire area
One percolation test in Boring No 203 was conducted in coralline sand that was
encountered at a depth of about 0 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on
the results of the percolation test a percolation rate of about 4 minutes per inch was
obtained It should be noted that we did not perform percolation tests in the other
borings that encountered coralline deposits because the coralline deposits were
encountered at depths at or below the groundwater level
As previously mentioned preliminary analyses indicated that approximately
7 2 acres of leach field area which includes 100 percent disposal backup may be
required to accommodate the anticipated daily sewage disposal volume The leach field
size was based on using the average percolation rate in the coralline deposits obtained
from our previous reports
Based on our field exploration providing approximately 7 2 acres of leach field area
within Area Nos 1 through 3 may not be feasible since coralline deposits were not
encountered or were encountered at depths at or below the groundwater level
Over excavation of the low permeability soils to the coralline deposits and
replacement with more permeable soils is a possible option However we believe that this

Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings designated as
Boring Nos 101 and 102 201 through 205 and 301 through 303 extending to depths
of about 15 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We drilled the borings using a truck mounted drill rig
with continuous flight augers
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
