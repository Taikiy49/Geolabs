2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their
original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally blanketed by a thin
surface fill layer consisting of low to moderately expansive clay soils of about 0 to 7 feet
thick underlain by residual soil consisting of very stiff to hard clayey silts extending to
depths of about 10 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface Weathered basalt rock
was encountered underlying the residual material in the borings located at the southern
portion of the site extending to the maximum depths drilled of approximately 25 1 to
40 feet below the ground surface Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings
at the time of our field exploration
Based on the competent subsurface soil condition encountered during our field
exploration we envision that the proposed building may be supported on a shallow
foundation system However based on the grading plan we understand that the future
building will be situated over both cut and fill areas Therefore special attention should be
given to the potential for significant differential settlement across the building site To
reduce the potential for differential settlement we recommend that the upper portion of the
building site be over excavated down to about elevation 95 feet MSL and the resulting
excavation be replaced with compacted fill minimum 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the differential thickness of fill across the building site In addition we also
recommend that the fill below Elevation 95 feet MSL consist of imported select granular
fill and should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for significant settlements and differential settlements across the building
structure
The near surface soils at the site generally consist of silty clay soils of low to
moderate plasticity Therefore the near surface soils are generally susceptible to
shrinking and swelling when subjected to changes in the soil moisture contents Unless
slabs on grade constructed above these types of plastic soils are properly designed there
is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking
and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content As a result we
recommend that a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill material be

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
structures Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we
anticipate that about 2 to 4 feet of cuts and up to 35 feet of fills will be required to achieve
the proposed finished subgrade The near surface soils encountered at the proposed site
consist of clayey silt and silty clay which exhibit a low to moderate expansion potential
when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore to reduce the potential for structural
distress to lightly loaded slabs we recommend that a minimum of 12 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material be provided below the concrete slab The select
fill material should be extended beyond the footprint of the new slab on grade areas by a
minimum of 24 inches The subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth
of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The non expansive select granular fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve All select granular fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by
base course
We anticipate that the slabs on grade will be subjected to forklift traffic We
recommend that the concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer for the intended
loads and should be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate some amount of

3 3 Retaining Structures
Based on the current grading concept retaining walls retaining a fill condition
ranging between about 10 and 35 feet high will be required for the project In addition the
retaining wall will need to retain the fill materials that are supporting the foundations of the
building Based on these conditions we recommend the use of a segmental retaining wall
systems such as a Keystone retaining wall system We believe that the Keystone
retaining wall system is likely one of the most economical retaining wall systems for the
project considering the significant heights of the walls
The Keystone retaining wall system is a composite wall system which utilizes high
density polyethylene or other reinforcing elements to reinforce the backfill zone and
improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone This composite system essentially
forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and differential

3 5 Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most common
cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This process may
cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not confined can result in
significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with
little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally liquefaction is not likely to
occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface The greater
overburden pressure can be sufficient to prevent liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is mostly underlain by very stiff to
hard cohesive soils Additionally the natural groundwater table is greater than 50 feet
below the site Therefore we believe that there is no potential for liquefaction at this
project site

3 11 Drainage
As previously indicated proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations and slabs constructed on expansive soil conditions Therefore the finished
grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water away from the foundations
and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be installed
around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and
pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are
provided on Plate B 1
Three Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B 2
Eight Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the Direct
Shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 through B 10
Four Unconfined Compression tests ASTM Test Designation D 2166 were
performed on the selected samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of
the material tested The test results are summarized on Plates B 11 through B 14
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of in situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plates B 15 through B 19
One Modified Proctor Compaction tests ASTM D 1557 A was performed on bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 20
Two Consolidation tests with time rates ASTM D 2435 were performed on
samples of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility characteristics
of the materials encountered Result of the consolidation test is presented on Plates B 21
and B 22
Eight Collapse tests ASTM D 5333 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the collapsibility characteristics of the soils The test results are plotted on
Thirteen one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under
different surcharge loads The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 24 1 and
B 24 2

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the on site soils was evaluated by performing two falling head
permeability tests at depths of about 5 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface The
locations of the field permeability tests designated as Boring Nos 110 and 111 are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top of
the hole The time intervals and water drops in the borehole were then recorded to
provide data upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface
soils The test results of the field permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
