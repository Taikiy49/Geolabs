INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Onenoa Road Reconstruction Phase III between the villages
of Tula and Onenoa on the island of Tutuila American Samoa The general location of
the project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for design input and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for preparation of final construction cost estimates nor for
construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located on the northeast shore of the island of Tutuila American
Samoa An existing road presently serves as the main access between the villages of
Tula and Onenoa A portion of the existing road from Onenoa to about Station 10 00
has been paved The Phase Ill portion of the proposed road reconstruction is
approximately mile in length and majority of the existing road is unpaved The Phase
lll portion of the proposed road reconstruction begins approximately at Station 10 00
and continues to the Village of Tula at about Station 47 80
We understand that the planned project work will include construction of six culvert
crossings five retaining wails totalling approximately 1000 lineal feet installation of a
revetment structure and construction of asphalt concrete and or portland cement
concrete pavements Based on the plans and road cross sections provided to us we
understand that the project grading work will involve cuts of up to 12 feet and fills of up
to about 5 feet high We also understand that the proposed project will also include

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
On the basis of our geotechnical exploration clayey and sandy soils were
encountered at the proposed project site from the surface to the maximum depths
explored The soils generally grade from medium stiff to very stiff or medium dense to
dense with increasing depth At some boring locations loose to medium dense sandy
soils were encountered below the near surface soils The soils encountered at the site
are generally competent and should provide adequate bearing for the retaining walls
culverts and pavements planned for this project Recommendations addressing the
above considerations and other geotechnical aspects of the project are discussed in the
following sections of this report

Retaining Walls
Because of the limited space for construction of cut and fill slopes retaining wails
are planned for grade separation and to support the proposed cuts and fills We
understand that 5 retaining walls between 4 and 10 feet high totalling approximately
1 000 lineal feet are planned at the site The retaining walls will be located approximately
at Stations 25 00 through 28 50 both sides of the road Stations 30 00 through
32 00 left side of road Stations 33 00 through 34 00 right side of road and Stations
35 00 through 36 00 left side of road In general the walls will be retaining a sloping
backfill condition with slopes on the order of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V At
Station 25 50 right side of road the slope behind the wail is as steep as one horizontal
to one vertical 1H 1V
Wail Foundations
We believe that the planned retaining walls may be supported on shallow
continuous footings bearing on the stiff clayey silts and silty clays and or the
dense sandy and gravelly soils encountered at the project site An allowable
bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design
of footings bearing on the stiff and or dense soils The bearing value above is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third when considering
transient loads such as wind or seismic forces If the footings are designed in
accordance with our recommendations total settlement of wall footings is
estimated to be on the order of 1 inch Differential settlements along the wall face
generally should be less than about 1 inch in 20 feet of wall
Wall foundations should be embedded at least 2 feet below the lowest adjacent
finished grade If soft or loose materials are exposed at the footing subgrade the
soft or loose materials should be over excavated to expose firm material and the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3083 00 Page 8
over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill compacted to at least 95
percent relative compaction If the existing fills are encountered at the bottom of
the footing excavations the footings should be embedded deeper to bear on the
native soils or the existing undocumented fills should be removed and replaced
with engineered fill compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91
Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should
be embedded to a depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at
least 8 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the slope Where
footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected
upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement
is necessary to reduce the potential for surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential of foundation
settlement
Footing excavations should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations the soil should be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks and recompacted prior to concrete placement
It is important that a representative of Geolabs Hawaii observe the footing
excavations to evaluate the competency of the bearing materials and the
embedment depths If unsuitable materials are encountered in the footing
excavations these materials should be over excavated to firm soils and replaced
with compacted engineered fill

Lateral loads acting on the retaining structures may be resisted by frictional
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by
passive earth pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded
portion of footings A coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used between the base
of the footings and the supporting subgrade soil Resistance due to passive
pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per
square foot per foot of depth p c f for embedded footings These values assume
that the concrete for footings is placed directly against undisturbed soils Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches
below finished grade should be neglected
Additional resistance to lateral loads may be achieved by the contribution of
cohesion between the base of the footing and the supporting subgrade A
cohesion value of 300 p s f between the base of the footing and the subgrade
may be used for design

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The pavement and culvert subgrade soils should be moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3083 00 Page 15
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90
percent relative compaction The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill construction to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities

Requirements for Fills and Backfills
In general the on site granular soils may be used as fill and backfill material if
over sized materials greater than 3 inches in largest dimension are screened
The on site silty and clayey soils should not be used as backfill behind retaining
walls because of the low to moderately expansive nature of the clayey soils
However the on site silty and clayey soils may be used as fill material below the
pavement structures provided that they are properly moisture conditioned and
compacted as recommended herein
Where needed select fill material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have
a laboratory CBR value of 15 or more with a maximum swell of less than 1 percent
when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition the
fill material should contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Backfills
behind retaining walls should be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative
compaction Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill would exert additional
lateral earth pressures on the walls and should be avoided
All permanent fill slopes should have inclinations of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter All fills placed on slopes with a five horizontal to one vertical
5H 1V slope inclination or steeper should be keyed and benched Fill slopes
should be constructed by over filling past the design limits of the slope and cutting
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3083 00 Page 17
back to the design slope to provide adequate compaction at the edges of the
slope

Revetment Structure
A revetment is planned along the shore facing the Pacific Ocean approximately
between Stations 19 00 and 22 00 We understand that the revetment will consist of
boulder rip rap fill to provide protection of the shore from erosion by the strong wave
action This area is not accessible to exploration equipment therefore subsurface
exploration in this area was not possible Based on our visual observations this area is
presently underlain by boulder rip rap that extend to approximately the water level The
foundation for the existing boulder rip rap appears to be coralline sands of the shore As
a result wave action may still erode the foundation soils and result in erosion
For the new revetment structure we recommend that the foundation for the
revetment be founded below the lowest low tide and should be adequately protected from
scour A filter fabric Mirafi 700X or equivalent should be placed on the foundation soils
prior to placement of the revetment boulders to reduce the potential for migration of fines
through the voids of the boulders of the revetment A layer of No 3B Fine gravel of not
less than 6 inches should be placed on top of the geotextile fabric prior to placement of
the revetment boulders Care should be taken when placing the revetment boulders
above the No 3B Fine gravel and fabric to reduce the potential of puncturing the fabric
Guidelines for specifications of the geotextile fabric are presented in Appendix C
Pavement Overlay
Pavement overlays may be considered at the portion of existing paved road In
general we believe that asphalt concrete overlays would be more cost effective and more
practical when compared to concrete overlays If concrete overlays are to be used all
cracks greater than inch in width should be filled prior to placing the concrete overlay
The smaller cracks should be filled with hot liquid crack filler while the larger cracks
should be filled with asphalt hot mix or cold patch Due to the minimum concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3083 00 Page 19
overlay thickness and the cost of repair and patching work it is our opinion that concrete
overlay would not be cost effective nor practical
For asphalt concrete overlay we recommend a minimum overlay thickness of at
least 2 inches Prior to asphalt concrete overlay the existing asphalt concrete surface
should be thoroughly swept to remove all loose materials A tack coat should be placed
on the existing asphalt concrete to bond the new asphalt concrete overlay to the existing
pavements Pavement reinforcing fabric should be aligned and placed with no wrinkles
that lap If manual laydown methods are used the fabric should be unrolled aligned and
placed in increments of approximately 30 feet Adjacent borders of the fabric should be
lapped between 2 and 4 inches Pavement reinforcing fabric should be manufactured
from polyester polypropylene or polypropylene nylon material The fabric should be
non woven and heat treated on at least one side Guidelines for specification of the
pavement reinforcing fabric are presented in Appendix C of this report

due to practical considerations As a result where the elevation differential between the
existing pavement surface to the finished pavement elevation is less than about 6 inches
we recommend that full depth asphalt concrete overlays be used Asphalt concrete
should be placed in lifts not exceeding 2 inches in thickness
To provide a smooth transition from the full depth pavement overlay to the typical
roadway section we recommend that a five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V slope be
provided between the full depth asphalt concrete to the 2 inches of asphalt concrete in
lieu of an abrupt change The pavement reinforcing fabric recommended should be
placed over this slope The relatively smooth transition and the pavement reinforcing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3083 00 Page 20
fabric should help reduce the potential for reflection cracking due to an abrupt change
in the pavement thickness
As previously indicated some portions of the pavement have deteriorated and
potholes were evident Where the pavement has deteriorated the portion of the
deteriorated pavement should be repaired by removing the deteriorated pavement section
and loose to soft subgrade soils The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
aggregate base course compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Underground Utilities
For support of utility pipes we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes
Granular materials should also be used for trench backfill The backfill up to about 1 foot
above the pipes should be lightly tamped to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the finish
grade should be placed in 8 inch maximum horizontal loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction since we anticipate the utility
trenches to be below the roadway pavement
Existing Underground Utility Lines
It is anticipated that underground utilities exist beneath the existing paved and
unpaved road We recommend that all existing utility lines within the project limits be
accurately located by the contractor prior to construction The contractor has the sole
responsibility to devise and implement an effective program to protect or relocate the
utility lines during construction

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three sieve analyses tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3 Graphic presentation of the grain size
distribution is presented on Plate B 5 through B 7
Five Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the tests
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is shown on Plate B 4

SITE DESCRIPTION
The Pacific Missile Range Facility PMRF Barking Sands area is located in the western
portion of the island of Kauai Hawaii Figure 1 There are two separate sites under
investigation within the PMRF Barking Sands area the Firefighting Pit No 1 site and the
Torpedo Post Run Facility Drain Field site
The Firefighting Pit No 1 site was used from 1968 to January 1985 for the training of crash
crew firefighters The pit consisted of a shallow depression approximately 50 to 60 feet in
diameter The pit was reportedly constructed over an existing asphalt paved area The pit
area was surrounded with a low asphalt berm and covered with a thin layer of sand to
protect the asphalt from the heat Flammable liquids were placed within the pit and set on
fire to simulate a burning aircraft Crash crew firefighters then practiced extinguishing the
fire Following its closure the pit was covered with a thin layer of sand exact thickness
unknown and revegetated No visible signs of the pit remain The Firefighting Pit No 1
site is currently located within an open grassy field adjacent to an aircraft runway
Figure 2
The Torpedo Post Run Facility Drain Field site consists of a septic tank and leach field that
received torpedo cleaning wastewaters from July 1973 to 1977 The leach field consists of
four parallel drain lines approximately 60 feet long buried about four feet deep The drain
lines are reportedly constructed of clay or plastic pipe The septic tank and drain field are
_ located in a fenced lot adjacent to the Torpedo Post Run Facility building Figure 3
Wastewaters from torpedo cleaning processes reportedly contained Otto Fuel II torpedo
fuel cyanide a combustion by product of Otto Fuel II oils and solvents
