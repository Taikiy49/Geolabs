2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 to 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
both still active
The project site is situated on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield
Volcano which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows
and pyroclastic materials The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist
of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the
ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and they represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets
are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as primary water
transmission features because of their high permeability

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that
they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of the earthquakes
are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent
are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the active volcanoes in the southern portion of
the Island of Hawaii

3 1 Building Foundations
We understand the new structures for the project generally will consist of one to
two story buildings Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site
after site grading we recommend utilizing shallow spread and or continuous strip
footings to support the new structures planned As an alternative foundations for
support of the new structures one to two story structures also may consist of
thickened edge slab footings
Our borings encountered a surface layer of sandy and silty gravel soils ranging
from about 1 to 8 feet in thickness Some loose soil pockets were encountered in this
surface soil layer Therefore loose materials encountered at the foundation subgrades
should be over excavated and re compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
As an alternative to backfilling with compacted fill the foundations may be extended to
bear on the underlying competent materials
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of footings bearing on compacted structural fill or on site
materials This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on
slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured
from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the face of the slope is no less than
6 feet
In addition foundations next to other foundations utility trenches easements or
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench or the footings

of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX C
Percolation Tests
As part of our field exploration program we conducted three constant head
percolation tests at the project site In general the drilled borings had a diameter of
about 3 5 inches and extended to depths of about 10 to 12 feet below the ground level
at the time of the test
A water truck was used to pump clear water into the boreholes for the constant
head test method The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the volume as a
relatively constant flow rate was achieved Results of the percolation tests conducted
are presented on Plates C 1 through C 3
