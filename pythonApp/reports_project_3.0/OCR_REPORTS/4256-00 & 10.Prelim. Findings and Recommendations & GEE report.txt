Dear Mr Sumida
In accordance with our fee proposal dated April 2 we recently completed the
field exploration work pertaining to the proposed Kaluanui Development project in the
Hawaii Kai area of east Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This letter
summarizes our work performed thus far on the project and presents our preliminary
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the subject
project
Project Considerations
The project site is located on the west side of Hawaii Kai Drive at the
intersection with Onohi Street in the Hawaii Kai area of east Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The subject parcel is approximately 2 acres in size and is situated at the
base of the western hillside The site is generally covered with a relatively heavy
growth of trees and grasses
Based on the current design concept it is desired to develop the project site into
a residential housing project with eight townhouse buildings for a total of 30 housing
units Access roads and underground utilities are also planned to serve the new
residential development Based on the existing topography and current grading
concept we understand that fills up to about 5 to 6 feet thick along Hawaii Kai Drive
and cuts up to about 13 feet deep into the existing western hillside are planned to
create a relatively level area for the proposed residential development Retaining walls
will be required along Hawaii Kai Drive and within the buildings to provide the
necessary grade separation for the subject development

Our field exploration program consisted of excavating eight test pits designated as
Test Pit Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths ranging from about 4 5 to 17 feet below the

Based on our site reconnaissance and field exploration we believe that adobe
clay soils exist in the shallow subsurface throughout the project area including the
areas extending upslope toward the western property boundary where bedrock
exposures are overlapped by the alluvial soil and talus rock deposits The
near surface adobe clay soils encountered within the project site generally consists of
a dark brown to grayish brown silty clay with a very high percentage of stones such as
weathered gravel and cobbies as well as large boulders This soil type has been
classified by the United States Department of Agriculture Soil Conservation Service
USDASCS as Lualualei extremely stony clay LPE The soil is typically very sticky
and plastic with a very high shrink swell potential and low permeability Due to the
amount of stones within the soil mass excavation will likely be difficult The soils are
known to be susceptible to sliding or creeping on slopes generally greater than about
four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V
Our test pit excavations indicated that adobe clay soils with cobbles and
boulders were generally encountered to depths between about 3 and 5 feet below the
existing ground surface along the sloping hillside at the western portion of the site
Below the surficial adobe clay soils the majority of our test pits generally encountered
penetration refusal conditions indicating the presence of large boulders and or bedrock
formation at the shallow subsurface
Our field exploration along the narrow strip of level land and the lower slopes
towards Hawaii Kai Drive generally encountered similar near surface adobe clay soiis
but extended to greater depths of up to about 9 feet below the existing ground surface
Below the surficial adobe clay soils our test pits in these areas generally encountered
volcanic tuff deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately
17 feet below the existing ground surface
Excavation of the test pits was generally limited to less than about 20 feet deep
due to the presence of buried large boulders which precluded the deepening and
widening of the test pit trenches In general excavation became more difficult when
the cobble and boulder materials began to exceed about 50 to 60 percent of the soil
materials The relatively dense packing of the colluvial rocks limited the backhoe
penetration to greater depths
Groundwater was not encountered in the excavated test pits at the time of our field
exploration However seepage water is commonly encountered on hillsides and is
dependent upon weather conditions and site specific geologic factors Therefore it should
be noted that potential seepage or transient perched groundwater might be encountered
during construction In addition the water levels at the project site may be influenced by
seasonal precipitation run off storm surge conditions and other factors

Based on the existing topography and the proposed design finish grades of the
subject development we anticipate that the majority of the site grading work for the
proposed construction will consist of fills up to about 5 to 6 feet thick along Hawaii Kai Drive
and cuts up to about 13 feet deep into the existing hillside along the western portion of the
subject parcel Items of grading that are addressed in the following subsections include

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding
areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to
expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off site and or used in landscape areas where appropriate Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation
quantities
Laboratory tests indicate that the near surface adobe clay soils exhibit high
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in moisture content To reduce
the potential for distress to the future residential structures resulting from swelling of
the clayey soils it is recommended that the expansive adobe clay soils be capped
with a minimum of 3 feet of non expansive select capping fill material below the
building slab areas In order to reduce the amount of over excavation of the
expansive soil consideration should be given to raising the finished pad grades
above the existing ground surface during the grading concept development In
areas where the finished pad grades will be at or below the existing ground surface
over excavation for the 3 foot thick non expansive capping fill should be delayed
until the house location is finalized if practicable The non expansive select granular
fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed building
structure

Fills and Backfills
Because of the shrink swell potential and possible downslope creep susceptibility
adobe clay soils generated from cut areas should be placed in the deeper fill areas
only A minimum of 3 feet of non expansive material should be used to cap the
adobe clay soils The volcanic tuff deposits underlying the surficial adobe clay
soils encountered in cut areas may be stockpiled and re used as a source of general
fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock
fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension
Additional general fill needed for the site filling may consist of imported materials
with particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should
have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 8 or higher and a swell
potential of not more than 3 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 The 3 foot thick capping fill required under the building slab
areas should consist of imported non expansive select granular material such as
crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Non expansive select granular fill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the subgrade soils at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture is an important requirement considering the
highly expansive nature of the on site clayey soils Because moisture conditioning
and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are critical elements of earthwork
observations and soil density tests should be performed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required
degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift Where
compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be applied with
adjustment to the moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction
Cut and Fill Slopes
Permanent cut and fill slopes excluding adobe clay soils should be designed with
a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new
fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and
continue up in level compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In the event that
over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching requirements should be
implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be
provided as soon as possible to reduce potential erosion of the finished slopes
Falling Boulder Hazard
Properties adjacent to the upslope areas may be subject to boulders rolling down
the hillside The falling boulders are a general phenomena occurring on all ridge
slopes on the Island of Oahu Various catchment methods and boulder barriers can
be used to reduce the hazard of falling boulders Some methods that may be
considered include but are not necessarily limited to the following

of the art of geotechnical engineering practice has not advanced to a point that a solution is
available for this area It is our opinion that no matter what one does there is always the
risk of boulders rolling down the hill Whatever measure is chosen it only reduces the risk
It should be pointed out that no guarantee against future falling boulders can be made due
to changes in soil and rock conditions with time weathering erosion earthquake and
construction vibration and improvements and construction activities at and around the site
Slab On Grade Foundations
In general we anticipate that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge
footings will be used to support the proposed two story residential structures planned for
the subject development Our exploration indicates that the site is generally underlain by
highly expansive clayey soils at shallow depths Unless slab on grade foundations
supported by these expansive soils are properly designed there is potential for future
distress to the residential structures resulting from wetting and subsequent swelling of the
expansive clayey soils To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the
swelling of the on site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select capping
fill material be provided for a minimum depth of 3 feet below the slab cushion The capping
fill should also extend beyond the slab perimeter a minimum of 2 feet
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the non expansive select capping
fill materials This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one
third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of

of shrink swell effects on the slab due to potential heaving of the underlying expansive
clayey soils when wetted
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and
concrete To reduce the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce the costs

bearing wall footing

pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation

discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall

material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of relatively impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the walls

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu encompasses an area of about 604 square miles and generally
consists of four major geomorphic provinces 1 Koolau Range 2 Waianae Range
3 Schofield Plateau and 4 Coastal Plain Of the four provinces Kamilonui Valley is located
within the Koolau Range province The Koolau Range was formed by the Koolau shield
volcano which comprises the majority of the eastern and northeastern portions of the Island
of Oahu The elongate shape of Koolau Range is approximately 37 miles in length and has
been deeply eroded by stream activity over the past several million years The Koolau
Range generally consists of massive accumulations of thinly bedded lava flows which began
to build after the older Waianae Range of western Oahu was established
The Koolau Range erupted in Tertiary time approximately 2 to 5 million years ago
Following the formation of the Koolau Range the Island of Oahu began to submerge under
the weight of the volcanic mass This slow submergence combined with long term sea
level fluctuations as a result of the worldwide continental glacial advance and retreat
worked to erode the Island of Oahu to what it is now today For example during periods
of higher than normal today s sea level the raised sea level served to promote the
deposition of shallow water marine sediment and coral deposits on ground which today
may be exposed at or above the present day sea level In contrast during periods of low
sea level terrestrial streams were forced to cut deeper into the exposed ground to carry
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 6
the terrestrial sediment further out into what once was a marine or shallow lagoonal
environment Thus a complex series of marine and terrestrial deposits were formed
around the coastal and relatively low elevation portions of the Island of Oahu The varying
types of deposits can be found interlayered making reconstruction of the coastal area
development history of the Island of Oahu very complex
Kamilonui Valley is described as a relatively mature stage U Shaped erosional
valley formed as the streams draining the Koolau summit cut down through the rock layers
to incise and form the present day valley walls and floor The walls of the valley provide
exposure of the bedded lava flows of the Koolau shield volcano The valley floor is
generally composed of alluvial and colluvial deposits of volcanic material that had been
weathered eroded and transported downslope for deposition The valley floor typically
contains soil and rock materials that have been transported by stream activity and buried
over long periods of time forming thick deposits of mixtures of clay and boulders The
buried clayey cobbles and boulders have since undergone additional chemical weathering
in the depositional environment As a result of in situ weathering and burial the older
deeper deposits have become somewhat consolidated and tightly packed to form a
sedimentary conglomerate
More recent Quaternary time volcanic activity occurred in the vicinity of eastern Oahu
after the main Koolau volcano activity ceased The most recent volcanic activity is part of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series and is represented by the eruption of pyroclastic material ash and
cinder and some lava flows The cinder and ash cones located nearest the Kamilonui
project site comprise the Koko Head fissure which forms the extreme southeastern portion
of the Island of Oahu Although the cone and crater deposits are concentrated to the
southeast of the project site some of the pyroclastic materials are believed to have been
locally deposited in the vicinity of the site These localized deposits may be buried beneath
some of the more recent alluvial and shallow marine deposits of the area

Our field exploration program consisted of excavating 15 test pits designated as Test
Pit Nos 1 through 15 extending to depths ranging from about 4 to 26 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the excavated test pits are shown on the Site

Based on our site reconnaissance and field exploration we believe that adobe
clay soils exist in the shallow subsurface throughout the project area including the areas

creeping on slopes generally greater than about 4H 1V

Characteristics of Adobe Clay Soils
The adobe clay soils encountered at the project site which are classified as CH
according to the Unified Soil Classification System exhibit high shrink swell potential when
subjected to moisture fluctuation In addition the adobe clay soils are very weak soils
which are significantly affected by an increase in moisture content i e it will lose
considerable shear strength when saturated As a result they are subject to slope creep or
landslide movement in a sloping terrain during extended wet periods
Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping the project site is generally
underlain by adobe clay soils with a high concentration of cobbles and boulders extending
to a depth of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface Due to the limited thickness
of the adobe clay soils encountered at the project site and the relatively flat terrain under
the current grading concept we believe that the potential for slope instability would be
relatively small However it is our opinion that the site grading and slope stabilization
measures should be implemented to reduce the potential for future distresses to the planned
development due to the presence of these adobe clay soils
As discussed above the adobe clay soils encountered at the project site possess
a tendency to creep downhill and result in potential slope failures under certain conditions
We believe that the major causes of these downhill slope movements are 1 poor subsoil
conditions 2 steep hillside terrain and 3 excessive water infiltration Therefore site
grading and slope stabilization measures should be directed to these three areas in order

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Non expansive select granular fill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the subgrade soils at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture is an important requirement considering the
highly expansive nature of the on site clayey soils Because moisture conditioning
and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are critical elements of earthwork
observations and soil density tests should be performed by a representative of
Geolabs during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree
of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift Where compaction is
less than required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment to
the moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

Slab On Grade Foundations
In general we anticipate that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings
will be used to support the proposed two story residential structures planned for the subject
development Our exploration indicates that the site is generally underlain by highly
expansive clayey soils at shallow depths Unless slab on grade foundations supported by
these expansive soils are properly designed there is potential for future distress to the
residential structures resulting from wetting and subsequent swelling of the expansive clayey
soils To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on site
expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select granular capping fill material be
provided for a minimum depth of 3 feet below the slab cushion The capping fill should also
extend beyond the slab perimeter a minimum of 2 feet
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the non expansive select granular
capping fill materials This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the new non expansive select
granular capping fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted The passive pressure should

relative compaction Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways sidewalks with expansion joints at right angle intersections The subgrade soils
beneath the walkways sidewalks and patio slabs should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report
Areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with

Flexible and rigid pavements are anticipated for the new access road planned at the
project site We anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed roadways will consist
primarily of passenger vehicles and light trucks with occasional heavy truck traffic We have
made our preliminary pavement designs assuming the pavement subgrade soil will generally

2 foot non expansive select granular soil may be reduced to 6 inches if volcanic ash soils is
exposed at the pavement subgrade level If site grading exposes soils other than those
assumed additional tests should be performed to confirm or revise the recommended
pavement sections for actual field conditions Based on the above assumptions we
recommend the following pavement sections be used for preliminary design and budgeting

Granular Fill over Moist Compacted Clayey Subgrade
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a
high expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content Therefore we
recommend that the subgrade be properly prepared and kept moist prior to placement of
base course and capping fill material The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a
depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
All subgrades should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered by the
non expansive select granular capping fill If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade
GEOLABS INC

soils prior to placement of the capping fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The non expansive select granular soil under the
pavement areas should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The backfill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optinum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level

loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from
the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the
appropriate sample depths
Thirteen one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plates B 1 and B 2
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 3
Two residual direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples
of the clayey soils to evaluate the residual shear strength of the material tested Results of
the residual direct shear tests are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on the volcanic ash soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The
test results are presented on Plate B 6
