2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch
by the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Koolau is the younger
of the two volcanoes and reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million
years ago
Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from northwest to southeast
trending rift Zones These lava flows are interbedded pahoehoe and a a flows with pahoehoe
being the dominant form After the cessation of the main shield building phase of Koolau
Volcano there was a long period of erosion The project site is situated in Manoa Valley
which is an amphitheater headed valley deeply eroded into the Koolau Shield
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge on the westerly
side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf vents When these
vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down the westerly wall of
the valley and moved across the valley floor The lava flows ponded and filled the lower
portions of the valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it cooled forming dense
massive rock
Other eruptive events of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled these lava flows with
pyroclastic material particularly sand sized cinder materials Subsequent fluvial action has
transported minor amounts of alluvial soils onto the site During the development of the school
campus man made fills were placed to grade the site to its present condition

3 1 Foundations
Based on the anticipated subsurface soil conditions at the project site we recommend
that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on structural
fill be used for support of the proposed new structures planned at the project site An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the
design of foundations of the new structures bearing on a minimum of 18 inches of structural
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Due to the substantial building loads anticipated for the project and the presence of
differing materials at the foundation subgrade level we recommend that an 18 inch thick layer
of compacted structural fill material be provided below the bottom of footings for uniform
support The 18 inch thick structural fill generally would serve to improve the foundation
bearing conditions and to reduce the amount of differential settlement due to differing
subgrade materials Over excavation for the compacted structural fill should extend beyond
the edges of the footings a minimum of 12 inches Prior to placement of the structural fill the
bottom of the footing over excavations should be compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the
over excavated subgrade should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials

The over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction
The compacted structural fill should consist of imported select granular fill material
such as crushed coral or basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should also contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less
when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Compacted structural fill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
conditions should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope
Footings located adjacent to planned or existing retaining walls should be embedded deep
enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below
a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench As
an alternative the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by variable fill
materials overlying saprolitic soils with cobbles boulders and basalt rock ledges embedded
at varying depths across the project site To reduce the potential for differential movements
and cracking of the slabs resulting from differing subgrade materials we recommend that the
subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with select granular fill The select granular
fill capping fill material should extend to a depth of at least 12 inches below the slab cushion
elevation The capping fill should also extend beyond the footprint of the new slab on grade
by a minimum of 24 inches

Our laboratory test results indicated that the clayey soils encountered at the site exhibit
moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the
potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade we recommend that provisions
be made in the construction documents to increase the capping fill layer underneath the
slabs on grade from the minimum of 12 inches to 18 or 24 inches in areas where the highly
expansive clayey soils are exposed at the slab subgrade level Determination of the increased
thickness in the capping fill layer from 12 inches to 18 or 24 inches should be made by a
representative from Geolabs in the field during construction
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are properly prepared The prepared capping fill and the underlying subgrade
soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of concrete Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be
thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
For the building slabs we recommend that the building slabs on grades be reinforced
with reinforcing bars spaced at least 18 inches in each direction in lieu of the standard
welded wire fabric This recommendation is provided to reduce the potential for structural
distress due to differential movements of the slabs due to the expansive nature of the on site
soils
Due to the sloping terrain and the potential for seepage conditions at the project site
we recommend that underdrains be installed under the building slabs on grade excluding the
deck areas outside the building to relieve the potential build up of subterranean seepage
water The underdrains should consist of 4 inch diameter perforated pipes wrapped in a
non woven filter fabric with perforations placed facing down The underdrains should be
spaced at a maximum of 40 foot intervals and daylighted into appropriate drainage structures
for proper discharge A sketch of the Typical Underdrain Detail below slabs on grade is
presented on Plate 3 of this report

testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 23

accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by

In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Thirteen one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples of the soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The
results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on
Plate B 4
Four sieve analysis tests ASTM C were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 5
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 6 through
B 8
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plates B 9 through B 12
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 13 through B 16
