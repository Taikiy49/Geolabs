INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical
reconnaissance performed at the subject site The work was performed in
general accordance with our proposal of with the
exception that a helicopter was used to transport our personnel to the
site and to make observations of the erosion from the air
In the way of background an erosional scar was noted on the
escarpment face below Omega Station Anchor P 1 in early to mid December
1987 This scar caused concern for the stability and support of Anchor
P 1 Therefore this reconnaissance was requested to make a preliminary
evaluation of the effect of the erosion on the anchor Subsequent to our
retention to perform this reconnaissance an additional erosional scar
formed adjacent to Anchor P 2 see Plate A 4

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our reconnaissance was to make visual observations of
the erosional feature near Anchor P 1 in order to determine its possible
origin and to obtain data for recommendations pertaining to additional
exploration and or potential remedial actions if required
The scope of our work included but was not limited to visual
inspection of the face of the P 1 erosional feature by rappelling see
Plate A 1 aerial observations of the erosional scars near both P 1 and
P 2 Visual observations of both scars from ground level along the
stairway and detailed observations of the existing surface conditions at
both Anchors P 1 and P 2 Although the additional work for Anchor P 2 was
not included in our originally proposed scope of work the work was
performed for the sake of expediency while our personnel were on the site
OBSERVATIONS
The rappelling and aerial observations of the erosional scar adjacent
to Anchor P 1 indicated that a mass of soil and extremely weathered basalt
approximately 20 feet in width 15 feet in height and three 3 feet in
depth had become detached from the existing steep about slope
The majority of the debris slid approximately 200 feet downslope to form a
debris fan Additional debris apparently moved further downslope
stripping the vegetation in its path creating the visual impression of a
scar which is of much greater extent than the actual amount of movement
involved
Visual observations conducted while rappelling the face of the scar
indicated that the original soil profile consisted of approximately eight
8 inches of organic soil overlying about 12 inches of silty gravel over
about 12 inches of clayey soil grading to saprolite a residual soil
retaining the relict structures of the parent rock Below this horizon
extremely to highly weathered basalt formation was noted in the face of
the scar Portions of the formation exhibited blocky jointing which

DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In Hawaii one of predominant forces in weathering and erosion is the
geologic process known as mass transfer This process is the movement
of material down the side slopes of valleys primarily under the influence
of gravity The process of mass transfer ranges from very slow movement
of less than one inch per year soil creep to very rapid movement such
as the landslides which occurred on New Year s Eve in Kuliouou Niu and
Hahaione Valleys The erosion which has occurred in the vicinity of Omega
Station Antenna Anchors P 1 and P 2 is an example of the rapid form of
mass transfer and is referred to as a soil avalanche

In the wet regions of Hawaii the principal agent of mass
transfer is a type of landslide known as a soil avalanche in which
the soil cover on a steep slope simply pulls loose from the
underlying bedrock and slides downward Sometimes the avalanche
travels all the way to the foot of the valley wall and adds to the
bulk of the colluvial apron which indeed has been almost wholly
built by slides of this sort At other times it travels only part
way down the wall before it is checked The bright red scars left by
these slides are very conspicuous on the green hillsides for a few
months after the avalanche occurs
Most soil avalanches are small commonly less than 100 yards
long downslope and less than 100 feet wide though rarely they may
be two or three times that large The mass of soil removed generally
is only 2 or 3 feet thick but rarely may be as much as 10 feet
The frequency of soil avalanches is closely related to heaviness
of rainfall Some are caused by nearly every exceptionally heavy
rain The additional weight of the water saturated soil possibly
together with some degree of lubrication by the water causes less
stable portions of the mantle to pull loose from the bedrock and
Slide downhill Sometimes the actual tearing loose seems to be
initiated by a single large tree that is leaning outward and pulls
the soil loose with its roots Following a heavy Kona storm in
1930 Stearns counted 14 new avalanches in the head of Nuuanu Valley
alone

As can be seen in the above discussion such soil avalanches are part
of the normal weathering and erosion processes in Hawaii Also these
avalanches are generally small surficial slumps or skin tears of the thin
soil cover which occurs on steep slopes and are generally not indicative
of mass instability of the slope itself
During our field work at the Haiku site several other scars were
observed in addition to the scars in the vicinity of Anchors P 1 and P 2
During the helicopter flights to and from the site numerous scars could
be seen in all of the valleys flown over i e Halawa Moanalua and
The preponderance of slides and slumps which have occurred during
this winter of 1987 1988 are most likely the result of having had a few
years of abnormally dry weather followed by the prolonged and the
torrential rains of the current wet season During the dry periods the
soil cover on the steep slopes desiccated and began to crack forming
fissures for water to enter and accumulate This season s long periods of
rainfall allowed for deep saturation of the soils and the heavy rainfall
events added water faster than the soils allowed percolation and runoff
This caused portions of the saturated soils to become overloaded and to
fail or slide
Based upon our field observations at Anchor P 1 it is our opinion
that the erosional scar is merely a surficial soil slump The degree of
stability and support of P 1 which existed prior to the occurrence of the
slump does not appear to have been significantly reduced on a short term
basis However it is likely that the soil covering the highly to
extremely weathered basaltic bedrock will continue to unravel both
horizontally and vertically This will eventually lead to the exposure
and gradual weakening of the bedrock by weathering and erosion In a
long term view the continued effects of the slump may eventually lead to
a reduction in the stability and support of the anchor

We recommend that the erosion be closely monitored to determine
whether the weathered basalt formation is becoming unstable
This monitoring could be accomplished by the installation of a
visual gauge such as staked in targets which could be observed
from the valley floor or by helicopter inspections
If the estimated remaining life of the anchor does not extend
beyond the next several years and the continued monitoring of the
erosion does not indicate a rapid encroachment on the base of the
anchor we recommend a no action policy towards remedial work
and or attempts to improve the existing degree of stability and
support
If the estimated remaining life is long term and funding is
available then we recommend that additional exploration in the
form of deep borings be performed as soon as possible to obtain
geotechnical data to better assess the situation for the
development of recommendations for the improvement of the anchors
by the use of tie back and or deep foundation systems such as
rock anchors or caissons
Based upon our visual observations of the erosional scar adjacent to
Anchor P 2 it is our opinion that the scar is of similar origin The
potential impacts of this slump to P 2 are most likely similar to the
potential impacts of the original slump to P 1 Therefore a similar
course of action is recommended
