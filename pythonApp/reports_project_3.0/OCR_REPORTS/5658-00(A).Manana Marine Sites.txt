2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of
their original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years
The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new residential
structures constructed at or near the existing grades Based on the laboratory test
results the clayey silt soils encountered at the project site exhibit a low potential for
shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the surface clayey silt soils we recommend
properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to the placement of slab cushion The
clayey subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In lieu of providing the conventional non expansive select granular capping fill
underneath the slab cushion we recommend using lightly reinforced slabs on grade for
the residential buildings to reduce the potential for appreciable slab cracking resulting
from expansion of the near surface clayey silt soils The lightly reinforced
slabs on grade should be a minimum of 5 inches thick and reinforced with No 3
reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center in both directions

Drainage
In general retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for
build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a
12 inch wide zone of permeable material such as open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed directly around a perforated pipe

As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or

asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious materials such as the on site clayey soils to reduce the potential for

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials including the crushed concrete and asphaltic concrete pavements
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optinum moisture and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement for the last lift of the
fill finished subgrade below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be
increased to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Compaction of fill materials should be accomplished using
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Surface water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be
provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the
finished slopes

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the project
site on for the field survey Twenty eight soil resistivity surveys were
conducted at seven locations designated as R 1 through R 7 on the Site Plan Plate 2
using the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter
manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Our field resistivity work was
performed under sunny conditions with generally dry to damp surface conditions
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of various distances based on the anticipated utility
depths The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode
spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The average soil
resistivity in ohms cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then
be computed Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on Plate C 1 It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions soil
moisture existing underground utilities and other factors
