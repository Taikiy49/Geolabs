SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a pavement structural section 
consisting of 20 inches of concrete overlying 4 inches of aggregate base material 
consisting of medium dense to dense sandy gravel. The pavement structural section was 
underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft to medium stiff sandy silts, clayey 
silts, and silty clays extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths of about 7.5 to 9.5 feet 
below the ground surface at the time of the field exploration, corresponding to about 
Elevation +2.5 to -0.5 feet MSL. However, groundwater levels likely will change due to 
tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors 
considering the proximity of the site to Nawiliwili Harbor and the Pacific Ocean. 
Based on our field exploration and the traffic loading information provided, we 
believe a rigid pavement structural section consisting of 20 inches of Portland Cement 
Concrete over 6 inches of aggregate subbase placed on a properly prepared subgrade 
may be used for to replace the distressed concrete panels. The subgrade soils under the 
new pavement structural section should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The aggregate subbase should be moisture-
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Where soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the pavement 
structural section following removal, we recommend providing a stabilization layer at the 
bottom of the pavement section. The stabilization layer should be constructed by over-
excavating the subsurface materials to a minimum depth of 12 inches below the bottom 
of the design pavement section. The over-excavation should be backfilled with a 
stabilization layer consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) with 
one layer of geogrid (Tensar TX7, Tensar InterAx NX850, or equivalent) wrapped in a 
non-woven geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent). The layer of geogrid should be 
placed at the middle of the 12-inch open-graded gravel stabilization layer. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations for design.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Nawiliwili Harbor Concrete Repair at Pier 3 project at 
Nawiliwili Harbor in Lihue on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of the proposed repair or replacement project. These recommendations are 
intended for the design of pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed project site is located at Nawiliwili Harbor in Lihue on the Island of 
Kauai. Several concrete pavement panels located at the south side of Pier 3 have 
experienced distress including corner spalls, durability issues, longitudinal and transverse 
cracking, and spalling. Based on the information provided, a broken fire line may have 
contributed to saturation and softening of the underlying subgrade below the concrete 
pavement, resulting in pavement distress. 
We understand the distressed concrete panels will be removed and replaced with 
new concrete panels. The distressed concrete panels generally are subjected to 27-ton, 
37-ton, and 40-ton capacity forklifts operated by Young Brothers to load and unload the 
shipping barges. Geotechnical recommendations on the pavement subgrade preparation 
and treatment also are desired.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical engineering analysis are the subsurface materials 
encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, 
and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the 
following subsections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site and 
the surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch 
(more than 2.5 million years ago). Following the cessation of this main shield building 
phase, renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the post 
erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments 
of the Palikea Formation. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks generally are characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the 
vents are pyroclastic materials that usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series 
generally is characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin 
clinker layers. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils, which grade 
to saprolite with increased depth. In general, saprolite primarily is composed of silty 
material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades 
to basaltic rock formation (less weathered) with increased depth. 
The project site is located on the southeastern coast of the Island of Kauai near 
Huleia Stream - Nawiliwili Bay estuary. In general, the subsurface materials underlying 
the project site consist of fills overlying lagoonal deposits extending to the maximum 
depths explored. Land development and reclamation projects have added man-made fills 
along the shoreline, bringing the site to its present configuration.

Existing Site Conditions

3.1.3 Design Pavement Section 
Based on the above assumptions and a properly prepared subgrade, we believe

The Portland Cement Concrete should have a minimum flexural strength of

We recommend performing additional California Bearing Ratio (CBR) and field

construction to confirm the adequacy of the above pavement section. 
3.1.4 Subgrade Preparation and Aggregate Subbase Placement 
The subgrade soils under the new pavement structural section should be scarified 
to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The aggregate subbase should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction.

Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction should be 
accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. 
As mentioned previously, we anticipate medium dense to dense silty sands (fill) 
overlying soft to medium stiff sandy silts (lagoonal deposits) at relatively shallow 
depths. Where loose and/or soft materials are encountered at the bottom of the 
pavement structural section, we recommend providing a stabilization layer at the 
bottom of the pavement section. The stabilization layer should be constructed by 
over-excavating the subsurface materials to a minimum depth of 12 inches below 
the bottom of the design pavement section. The over-excavation should be 
backfilled with a stabilization layer consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) with one layer of geogrid (Tensar TX7, Tensar InterAx NX850, 
or equivalent) wrapped in a non-woven geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent). The layer of geogrid should be placed at the middle of the 12-inch 
thick open-graded gravel stabilization layer. 
The need for additional over-excavation due to the soft and/or loose subgrade soil 
conditions should be evaluated by a Geolabs representative in the field during 
construction. Therefore, contract documents should include unit prices for 
additional over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations 
in the over-excavation quantities. For preliminary budgeting purposes, we 
recommend a contingency fund be allocated for an additional over-excavation of 
12 inches below the aggregate subbase layer and replacement with compacted 
aggregate subbase with another layer of geogrid (Tensar TX7, Tensar 
InterAx NX850, or equivalent) wrapped in a non-woven geotextile fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent). The layer of geogrid should be placed at the middle of 
the 12-inch thick, open-graded gravel stabilization layer.

3.1.5 Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface should be 
sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the 
pavement to appropriate drainage features. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on the site during or after construction. The recommended pavement 
section assumes that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations presented in this report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from the borings drilled as part of our geotechnical exploration 
efforts. Variations of the subsurface conditions between and beyond the borings drilled 
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided in this report. 
The boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been obtained 
using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device. Elevations noted on the 
borings were estimated from the as-built Container Yard Grading Plan Area “A” dated 
May 7, 1992. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be 
noted that groundwater fluctuations may occur due to variation in tides, rainfall, 
temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, each extending to a depth of 
about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk soil samples, 
designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained from near the existing ground surface to 
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The borings were 
drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. The borings 
were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made 
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.
