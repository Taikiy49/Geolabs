GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is long and relatively narrow encompassing approximately 875
acres located in the Kukio 1 Section of the North Kona District on the west coast of the
island of Hawaii It is approximately thirteen 13 miles north of Kailua Kona by the Queen
Kaahumanu Highway The location of the project site is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
The terrain is generally rugged with localized topographic relief of up to fifteen 15
feet The terrain typically reflects the original surface of thick basaltic lava flows of the a a
and pahoehoe forms The ground surface elevations range from about 675 feet above
sea level in the southwest corner of the site to near sea level along the northern site limits
at Kukio Bay
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2814 00 Page 4
The site is generally barren rocky land with little vegetation cover except along the
portion adjacent to Kukio Bay Access to the site is provided by an unimproved roadway
which runs from south to north and a few bulldozer trails extending laterally from this
access roadway Queen Kaahumanu Highway runs from east to west across the mid
portion of the site at an approximate elevation of 200 feet above sea level An ancient
Hawaiian trail also traverses the length of the site from its southeast to northwest corners
Most of the ground surface is covered with rough a a lava and smoother pahoehoe
basaltic lava flows Basaltic boulders cobbles and gravel clinker with exposures of in
situ basalt rock formations characterize the project site A collapsed lava tube is evident
in the lower portion of the site and it is likely that an extension of this feature progresses
below the surface through the upper portions of the site Muheeni cinder cone presently
being mined is a prominent feature in the southwest corner of the site

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has been active
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the northern flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes
up the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric basaltic lava
flows of the Hualalai Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late
Pleistocene epoch Stearns and MacDonald 1946
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The project site is situated in the
northwest rift zone of Hualalai
The lava flows at the site consist of both a a with zones of dense hard rock and
zones of loose clinker and pahoehoe types The a a clinker phase is highly permeable
Lava tubes are not uncommon in pahoehoe flows but are relatively uncommon in a a
flows Collapsed lava tubes cinder cones underlain and surrounded by depositional
scoriaceous cinder and spatter cones are located in near vicinity of the project area

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that development of the
proposed golf course and infrastructure installation is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view The clinker and basalt formations encountered at the site
should provide adequate bearing for the proposed development The excavated volcanic
materials should provide a suitable source of fill material if reduced to acceptable sizes
and processed as necessary
Typically in areas which are composed of pahoehoe lava flows methods are
employed during the various stages of construction to attempt to determine the location
of subsurface voids These methods include probing and grouting ripping and or more
site specific geophysical surveys
Giving consideration to the geology of the project site and of the nature and
purpose of the proposed developments it is our opinion that the optimal method for
dealing with the possible existence of subsurface voids and their potential impact on the
constructed facilities is to perform the mass grading as recommended hereinbelow and
to perform detailed geophysical surveys in areas to receive roadways or other pavements
which are more subject to damage due to subsidence or collapse of subsurface voids

Site Preparation
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the grading limits to receive fill
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grubbed and
spoil material should be disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping
Soft or yielding areas or cavities which are encountered during clearing and
grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to firm
natural material and the resulting depression backfilled with properly compacted
engineered fill
Subgrade Preparation for Structures and Pavement Areas
Prior to the placement of fill areas where the finish subgrade elevation at the
bottom of footings slabs on grade or pavements is more than three 3 feet above the
existing ground surface the existing surface should be proofrolled with a vibratory roller
or D 9 Bulldozer with a static weight of at least ten 10 tons
As an aid in detecting potential cavities in all structural areas where subgrade
elevations are less than three 3 feet above the existing ground surface and composed
of massive hard basalt rock or where massive hard basalt rock is exposed by excavation
to attain finish subgrade elevation the subgrade should be proofrolled as indicated above
In all areas where the existing material or the material exposed by excavation is in
a loose or fractured state such as clinker or where subsurface voids may be suspected
the area shall be ripped with a D 9 bulldozer or equivalent with a single tooth ripper to a
depth of three 3 feet or to practical refusal whichever is shallower and the ripped area
should then be proofrolled as recommended above

Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site indicate that the site
is underlain by clinker with ledges of dense basalt at relatively shallow depths
It is anticipated that some hard excavation and or ripping may be required in
localized cemented areas or when ledges are encountered Cemented clinker and ledge
basalt formations will most likely also require some hard excavation such as heavy
ripping hoerams or blasting
Our preliminary exploration was not structured to identify specific locations where
difficult excavation may be encountered outside the immediate vicinity of the boring
locations It is recommended that if this information is desired the final grading plans
should be reviewed by the geotechnical engineer and a geophysical survey performed
at the areas to undergo excavation It is our opinion that seismic refraction surveys would
be the most efficient and cost effective method of obtaining a rippability index of materials
at specific locations
Generally the selection of excavation methods should be left to the option of the
contractor Blasting should be considered as a means to expedite the excavation
process Generally consideration should be given to the economics of blasting to initially
reduce the size of the excavated rock material and to facilitate excavation as opposed to
extended periods of heavy ripping at high equipment rates However care should be
exercised to avoid overshooting which could disrupt the clinker or rock formation resulting
in a loss of strength

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of ten 10 borings to depths ranging between 11 5 and 25 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Truck
mounted rotary drilling equipment was utilized to drill the borings
Samples of the rock material encountered at the project site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination description and evaluation The recovered
cores were logged by a Field Engineer from our firm Descriptions of the recovered rock
cores are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 10
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the evaluation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 75 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks Thus if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following guide is used to evaluate the relative quality based

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its

would be very difficult to rip and would probably require blasting or splitting for
