INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Water Transmission Replacement Line for Molokai Ranch
Ltd on the island of Molokai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the General Site Plan Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted
at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that it is desired to install 8 inch and 12 inch diameter replacement
waterlines for Molokai Ranch Ltd on the island of Molokai Hawaii The proposed project
will consist of replacing approximately 48 000 lineal feet of waterline starting from about
the existing water meter connection at Kualapuu Reservoir to the existing Kaluakoi
Reservoir The approximate locations of the proposed waterlines are shown on the
General Site Plan Plate 1
In general the proposed project will include approximately 48 000 lineal feet of
trench excavation water pipe installation and trench backfilling We understand that the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 3
proposed waterline inverts will generally be about 4 to 8 feet below the existing ground
surface In addition the proposed waterline alignment will traverse Manawainui and
Kaluapeelua Gulches Based on the preliminary information provided we understand
that the proposed waterlines will be installed by cut and cover methods including the
portion at the gulch crossings

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch Ice
Age by basaltic andesitic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai Mountain
and West Molokai Mountain The project site stretches from the eastern end of West
Molokai Mountain through Hoolehua Plain to the western end of East Molokai Mountain
The Hoolehua Plain or Isthmus of Molokai is composed of lava flows from the East
Molokai Volcano banked against the older West Molokai Volcano
The West Molokai Volcano built a shield shaped dome of primitive basalts from a
northwest rift and a southwest rift After the dome was built about 1 400 feet above sea
level faulting dropped much of the northeast slope Prior to erosion the East Molokai
Mountain was a typical elongated basalt andesitic shield shaped dome It was built over
northwest and east trending rifts with a steep slope on the north side where the lava
flows plunged into deep water and a gentle slope on the west side where the lava flows
banked against the West Molokai dome
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 5
rose because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Molokai
Subaerial erosion of the emerging East and West Molokai Volcanoes and
subsequent deposition of the clastic debris as coalescing alluvial fans formed the present
configuration of the Hoolehua Plain Weathering from the late Pleistocene to recent times
has produced a residual lateritic soil over saprolite and highly weathered basalt andesite
bedrock underlying the site
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed 8 inch and 12 inch diameter waterlines start from the existing water
meter connection at Kualapuu and extend through Hoolehua Plain to Kaluakoi Reservoir
on the island of Molokai Hawaii Based on the preliminary plans provided the proposed
waterlines will cross Manawainui Gulch at about Station 99 50 and will cross
Kaluapeelua Gulch at about Station 229 50 as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 1
Elevations of the existing ground surface along the proposed waterline alignment vary
from about 255 to 1 372 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
At the eastern end of the proposed waterline from about Station 00 00 to
Station 94 00 the terrain slopes slightly down along the alignment Much of the
alignment appears to run along the existing dirt road Where the waterline diverges away
from the existing dirt road moderately dense vegetation was observed
Outcrops of basalt andesite rock formation are abundant in areas around
Manawainui Gulch between Station 94 00 and Station 106 00 The vegetation is
generally moderate to heavy and the slope banks of the gulch are as steep as
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 6
Maunaloa Highway and runs nearly straight to the existing control tank and pump station
at about Station 373 00 Most of the area between Station 106 00 and Station 373 00
is existing farmland and the terrain is nearly level
The proposed waterline alignment travels up a hill from the control tank area to the
existing Kaluakoi Reservoir at Puu Nana Although the areas are only lightly vegetated
slopes as steep as exist and boulders on the order of 1 to 2 feet in diameter are
prominent Most of the existing dirt roads have been abandoned and access is generally
by foot or by horse only
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program for this project consisted of drilling and sampling
24 borings to depths ranging from about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface
along the proposed alignment In addition a site reconnaissance was conducted along
the proposed alignment The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
General Site Plan Plate 1 and the Detailed Site Plans Plates 2 through 4
In general our borings encountered stiff to very stiff residual soils overlying very
stiff saprolites and highly weathered basalt andesite formation extending to the maximum
depth explored of about 10 feet In some borings medium hard highly weathered
basalt andesite formation was encountered at shallow depths In particular Boring Nos
and 16 encountered the basalt andesite formation at relatively shallow depths
In addition based on our site reconnaissance of the proposed alignment areas
near Manawainui and Kaluapeelua Gulch crossings show outcrops of basalt andesite
formation The near surface residual soils and saprolites appear to have been scoured
away by storm water in some areas In most cases the basalt andesite formations near
the gulches may be considered intact medium hard rock Furthermore surficial boulders
GEOLABS HAWAII

were observed in areas between about Station 380 00 and Station 405 00 These
boulders were typically 1 to 2 feet in diameter with some as large as 5 feet in diameter
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels may fluctuate
depending on rainfall time of year seepage conditions water levels in the gulches and
other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the borings site reconnaissance available geologic information and our
experience in the vicinity of the project site the proposed waterline alignment is underlain
by stiff to very stiff residual soils over very stiff saprolite and highly weathered
basalt andesite Highly weathered basalt andesite formations and boulders were found at
shallow depths in localized areas Therefore based on our evaluation of the subsurface
conditions with respect to the proposed new waterline construction the principal
geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed project include the excavation
conditions for the trenches of proposed waterline alignment the design of the thrust
blocks and earth pressures acting on the pipes
In general we understand that the proposed waterline inverts will be about 4 to
8 feet below the existing ground surface therefore we envision that the trench
excavations will need to be about 5 to 10 feet deep in most cases Detailed discussions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 8
of the geotechnical aspects of the project and our design recommendations are
presented in the following sections of this report
Trench Excavation
Based on the information provided for the proposed waterline alignment the
waterline inverts will be about 4 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our
borings and site reconnaissance the waterline alignment is generally underlain by stiff to
very stiff residual soils over very stiff saprolites and highly weathered basalt andesite In
some areas friable to medium hard basalt andesite formation and boulders were present
at shallow depths Therefore hard rocky material should be expected in some areas
during trench excavations
Excavation Method
In general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
As previously indicated some areas along the waterline alignment are underlain
by highly weathered basalt andesite formation at shallow depths Therefore some
portions of the waterline alignment may encounter rocky materials and or boulders
The contractor should be made aware of this condition The excavated soils from
the trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the
potential for ground movement
Excavation Support
We envision that the bottom of the trenches will generally extend to depths of
about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface Since the waterline trenches
will generally be in open areas sloping back of the sides of the trenches deeper
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 9
than 5 feet may be considered during construction All trench excavation and
construction works should be conducted in conformance with all applicable
Federal State and other safety requirements
In areas where sloping back of the trench sidewalls is not practicable shoring of
the sides of the trenches will be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench
excavations and to protect personnel working in the trench The excavation
support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable safety
requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used The
adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor Even if good construction procedures are followed
some movement of the shoring system and the adjacent ground may still occur
due to changes in earth stresses during excavation Due to the complexity of the
stress changes it is difficult to estimate accurately the magnitude of ground
movement The magnitude of ground movement also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring supports are installed
the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Thrust Blocks and Earth Pressure on Pipes
We envision that lateral and vertical thrust blocks will be required at some locations
along the waterline alignment to provide resistance to the thrust forces due to the
pressurized pipes For preliminary design of the lateral thrust blocks the Pressure
Diagram provided on Plate 5 may be used as a guide For the lateral thrust blocks the
thrust force acting on the pipes may be resisted by two components of passive earth
pressure friction and cohesion acting against the near vertical faces of the thrust block
Resistance due to frictional passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 170 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f above the groundwater
table for thrust blocks embedded in the in situ soil In addition the passive pressure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 10
component from cohesion may be taken as 2 500 pounds per square foot p s f Both
components of the passive earth pressure may be used in combination
To estimate the sliding resistance between the base of the thrust block and the
supporting soils a coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used In addition an adhesion
factor of 200 p s f may also be used in conjunction with the frictional resistance Based
on our site reconnaissance the soils surrounding the pipes were found to have been
scoured away and the existing waterlines were exposed in many areas Therefore
resistance to lateral loads should only be considered from the base of the thrust blocks
Shearing resistance due to the side walls and sliding on the top of the thrust block should
be neglected These values assume that the subgrade soil is well compacted and the
thrust block concrete is cast neat against the sides of the trench
For vertical thrust blocks resistance to movement may be calculated from the
dead weight of the concrete thrust block only As previously indicated the soils
surrounding the pipes were observed to have been scoured away and the existing
waterlines were exposed in many areas Therefore resistance due to the weight of the
overburden soils sliding on the top of the thrust block and shear resistance along the
side walls should also be neglected
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of the backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls For preliminary estimates of the vertical loads on
the buried waterline pipes an average unit weight of 120 p c f for the backfill material and
a coefficient of friction of 0 3 between the backfill and trench sidewalls may be used
Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with increased width of the trench
Therefore the width of the waterline trench should be kept to a minimum Traffic loads on
the buried pipes should also be considered where the proposed waterline alignment is
located within traveled ways

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the stiff to very stiff residual soils saprolites and
basalt andesite formation a minimum of 6 inches of granular bedding material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or S4C sand should be provided
below the pipe Prior to placement of the granular bedding material the bottom of the
trench should be firm and unyielding If soft loose soils are encountered during
construction an additional stabilization layer consisting of at least 18 inches of No 3B
Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be
provided below the 6 inch bedding layer for adequate support of the pipes A Typical
Trench Detail is presented on Plate 6 for reference The granular bedding should extend
beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and
should extend half way up the sides of the pipe
We understand that the waterline pipes will be jacketed with reinforced concrete at
the Manawainui Gulch crossing Due to the presence of rocky material at the invert
elevations of the pipes at the Manawainui Gulch crossing we recommend that a bedding
layer consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be
provided below the concrete jacket to provide more uniform support of the pipes at these
locations
Backfill
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least one half
the diameter of the water pipe should consist of select free draining granular material to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of the pipes The granular
material should be less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less than
10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not
be allowed considering the clayey nature of the subgrade soils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 12
The trench backfill from the springline of the pipe to the finished subgrade in open
areas not within paved traveled ways may consist of the approved excavated on site
soils The excavated on site soils should be screened of over sized materials such as
cobbles and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in largest dimension prior to using the
materials as trench backfill In general the backfill should be placed in lifts of about
8 inches in loose thickness with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 For paved traveled ways the
trench backfill within 2 feet below the finished subgrade elevation up to the subgrade
elevation should consist of select borrow compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction A Typical Trench Detail is presented on Plate 6
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new waterline is normally caused by the difference in the
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill
material placed over the water pipes The net increase in loading will cause settlement of
the underlying subsoils below the pipe invert Based on the results of our preliminary
exploration and engineering analyses negligible primary settlement may be anticipated
for this project considering the stiff to very stiff subsoils at the site
Pavement Section
In general the completed waterline trenches will be in open areas where paving of
the top of the waterline trenches is not anticipated However it should be noted that
protection of the finished waterline trench surfaces from erosion should be implemented
It is anticipated that asphalt concrete pavements will be used to repave the waterline
trenches where the alignment traverses existing paved roads Imported select borrow fill
material is anticipated for use in the upper 2 feet below the road pavement subgrade
within the waterline trenches as discussed in the preceding subsection The select
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3764 10 A Page 13
borrow material should be placed in 8 inch level loose lifts moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
In general the new pavements along the waterline trenches should match the existing
pavement sections

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that Geolabs Hawaii be retained to provide geotechnical
engineering services during construction of the proposed project The items of
construction monitoring that are critical which require Special Inspection include
observation of the trench excavation bedding material placement and trench backfill
Other aspects of earthwork construction should also be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii This is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications
or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface conditions
differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared The recommendations
provided in this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed soil conditions encountered during construction are different from
those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the design
should be made

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field borings site reconnaissance and available local
geologic information Variations of conditions between the borings may occur and the
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is
underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate the
recommendations provided in this report

taping from features indicated on a set of project plans and profiles dated March 26 1997
provided by Warren S Unemori Engineering Inc and information provided by Tom
Nance Water Resource Engineering Elevations of the borings are also approximate
having been estimated by interpolation from the profiles on the same plan The physical
locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered accurate only to the
degree implied by the methods used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field borings depict
the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of
this report

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling 24 borings to depths
of approximately 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the General Site Plan Plate 1 and Detailed Site Plans Plates 2
through 4 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineers who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 24
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with _
ASTM Test Designation Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the near surface soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are
provided on Plate B 1
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the grain size distribution of the soils encountered Results of the sieve analysis
tests are summarized on Plate B 2
Two_ring swell tests were performed on selected sampres to evaluate the swelling
potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 3
Four unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate its unconfined shear strength characteristics Results of
the test are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate their drained shear strength characteristics Results of the tests are
presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two sets of pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B 6
