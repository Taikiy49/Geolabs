Dear Mr. Leong: 
This letter report presents our findings of the geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the HART LCC Parking Lot Settlement Evaluation project at the Leeward 
Community College in Pearl City on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering assessment related to 
the possible cause of settlement and pavement cracks at the parking lot based on the 
field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The findings and 
conclusions presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter 
report.   
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at an asphaltic parking lot in the southeastern end of the 
Leeward Community College campus in the Pearl City area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that this parking lot was constructed 
on about 5 to 10 feet of fills (in 2015) over an open area that was initially mass graded 
with 20 to 40 feet of fills (in 1972). Based on the as-built drawing information provided, 
we understand that landfill materials existed in the area before the fill placement; 
however, these materials were supposed to have been removed as part of the 
construction that took place in 1972. 
The asphalt concrete parking lot has experienced substantial settlement up to 
about 20 inches with horizontal separation along the concrete curb and gutter. The 
settling asphalt pavement was observed generally along the south and southwest sides 
of the parking lot.  The settlement has resulted in cracking of the asphalt surface 
extending nearly 60 feet from the south edge of the parking area.  We also understand 
that the size of the cracks continues to increase, even where the cracks have been 
patched.

compressible soils within the fills placed in 1972 and 2015.

EXISTING PARKING LOT CONDITIONS 
A site reconnaissance was conducted throughout the project site including the 
down slope side of the parking lot. Based on our field observations and the information 
provided, we understand that the southern edge of the existing parking lot has 
experienced substantial distress, such as settlement and horizontal movement. Water 
ponding, curb separation, and pavement cracking were observed primarily within about 
20 to 60 feet from the southern edge of the parking lot. Some of the pavement cracks 
were sealed previously and re-opened recently, indicating on-going ground settlement. 
Some new cracks were observed on the north side of the sealed old cracks. The 
approximate crack locations and limits were sketched at the time of our site 
reconnaissance and are shown on the Cracking Map, Plate 3. 
Based on our site reconnaissance observations, concrete drainage ditches were 
installed along the southern skirt boundary of the property line, either on the slope or near 
the bottom of the slope. We observed a section of the concrete ditch was 
severely undermined exposing about a 5 feet deep cavity. One section of the slope 
was observed with steeper inclination. Some cracks and/or separation were also 
observed along the concrete ditches. The above observations may be evidence of 
ground movement and/or on-going erosion. 
Four grated drain inlets were installed along the southern edge of the parking lot. 
However, we found one outlet near the concrete drainage ditch was not properly finished

SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southwestern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. The lava flows on the plateau 
have weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors 
of the soil. The deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalua, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are 
a result of this weathering. The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient 
alluvial deposits overlying the weathered rock. The project site is located on the southern 
side of the Schofield Plateau, north of the Pearl City Peninsula, which is located between 
Middle and East Lochs. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling eight borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 108, extending to depths of about 41.5 to 
61.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Boring Nos. 105 and 106 were converted 
to inclinometer monitoring wells, to evaluate the long-term slope creep and/or instability. 
Boring Nos. 103 and 107 were converted to groundwater monitoring wells, to measure 
seepage water level and observe the groundwater fluctuation at the site. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.   
As mentioned previously, five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, 
were drilled to depths of about 10.5 to 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface in 
2018.  For ease of reference, the approximate boring locations are also shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 4 inches 
of asphaltic concrete and about 5 inches of base course material.  Below the pavement 
structure, medium stiff to stiff clayey soils (fills) were encountered to depths ranging from 
about 19 to 40 feet below the ground surface, overlying either stiff clayey/silty alluvium or

groundwater measurements are presented on Plates C-3 and C-4 of Appendix C.  Based 
on the piezometer monitoring, the groundwater levels encountered may vary from 
approximately +10 to +13 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
GROUND SETTLEMENT EVALUATION 
Based on the pattern of pavement distresses and the results of our field exploration 
program, we believe that the ground settlement may be caused by a combination of 
consolidation settlement of the fills (1972 fill and 2015 fill) due to surcharge, inadequately 
compacted fill placement, and saturation of the fill after the parking lot construction.

Based on our settlement analysis, we estimate total settlement from the 
inadequately compacted new fills under a traffic surcharge of 100 pounds per square foot 
(psf) may be up to about 6 inches. It should be noted that total settlement is typically less 
than 1 inch at the parking lots with about 100 psf of the wheel load, if the fill materials 
were compacted sufficiently to 95 percent relative compaction. We also estimate total 
settlement from the old fills under the new fill plus traffic surcharge (about 1,200 psf) may 
be up to about 28 inches. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that ground settlement of up to 
about 20 inches was measured by surveyors in 2018. The survey data retrieved in 2020 
suggests that additional settlement of up to about 4 to 6 inches occurred between 2018 
and 2020.  
The results of the consolidation testing conducted on the soil samples retrieved 
from this exploration indicated settlement of up to 8 to 10 inches may occur in the future 
under the assumed traffic loading and new fill surcharge. It should be noted that the total 
settlement was estimated based on compression ratios of 0.05 to 0.2 for 15 feet of the 
2015 fill and 25 feet of the 1972 fill, respectively. The above results suggest approximately 
70 to 75 percent of the total consolidation settlement estimated has been completed. 
We believe majority of the settlement is from primary consolidation under traffic 
wheel loads and weight of the 2015 fill. Amount of secondary compression may be 
minimal to none. Up to 90 to 95 percent of the total consolidation may be completed in 
the next 5 years.

We recommend implementing a surveying program on a regular basis, about every 
four months, to provide a more accurate rate of the ground settlement. 
Based on the results of the laboratory testing, the moisture contents of the soil 
samples retrieved from the new and old fills ranged between about 30 to 35 percent or 
higher, which are significantly higher than the typical clayey soil optimum moisture content 
(typically 20 to 25 percent). This condition may have resulted from inadequate/improper 
fill placement and/or post-saturation from excessive water infiltration, introducing ground 
settlement from hydro-compression. The excessive water infiltration may be initiated by 
rainfall and/or run-off water infiltrating through cracks at the pavement surface. 
Based on our experience in the project vicinity, collapsible soils may induce 
significant settlement due to post-saturation under the surcharge loading. However, there 
is no evidence that collapsible soil was encountered in the borings drilled at the project 
site. Therefore, collapsible soil is not considered as the cause of the ground settlement 
at the project site.

SLOPE STABILITY ANALYSES 
Based on the available original grading plan and subsurface information obtained 
from our field exploration, finite element and limit equilibrium slope stability analyses were 
performed at a select cross-section (A-A’), as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2, using the 
computer program Slide 2018 from RocScience, Inc. Finite Element Method (FEM) is a 
numerical technique for finding approximate solutions to boundary value problems for 
partial differential equations.  The model for the slope stability analyses is established 
using the two dimensional (2D) finite element stress analysis method and/or Bishop 
Method to compute the factor of safety of the critical slip circle in 2D plane strain state 
based on Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion. 
The slope stability analyses were performed under static and seismic conditions 
for long-term and short-term transient considerations. A surcharge load of 100 pounds 
per square foot (psf) was modeled for traffic loads in the parking lot. Results of our global 
slope stability analyses are presented on Plates 4 and 5. 
In general, the computed factors of safety for the slope stability are greater 1.5 and 
1.05 for the critical slop circle in rotational slope failure mechanisms under the static and 
seismic conditions, respectively. Typical slope rotation critical slip circles are illustrated 
on Plates 4 and 5. 
It should be noted that the slope stability analyses modeled the subsurface profiles 
as homogeneous layers using minimal soil shear strength values from the laboratory 
testing for a conservative analytical approach. The minimum factors of safety of 1.5 and 
1.05 under the static and seismic conditions, respectively, are generally considered 
sufficient against the global slope rotational failure. 
CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of geotechnical engineering exploration and engineering 
analyses, the following summarizes our conclusions. 
1. 
No landfill material was encountered in the borings drilled. 
2. 
The settlement may be primarily attributed from the consolidation of the 
1972 fill under the weight of the new fill placed in 2015. 
3. 
Additional settlement may be attributed from the 2015 fill compressed by 
the traffic loading and saturation. 
4. 
Future settlement on the order of 8 to 10 inches could occur gradually with 
time. 
5. 
Slope appears to be stable with minimum factors of safety of 1.5 (static) and 
1.05 (seismic) against the global slope rotation failure.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from field borings. Variations of subsoil conditions between and 
beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been estimated 
by taping from visible features shown on the Topographic Survey Map overlaid onto the 
CDL Parking Lot Grading Plan dated December 13, 2016 transmitted by SSFM 
International.  Elevations of the borings were estimated from spot elevations shown on the 
same plan. The field boring locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to 
the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition.  Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report.  These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides, rainfall, temperature, and other 
factors.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 108, extending to depths of about 41.5 to 61.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface.  Five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 
5, were drilled to depths of about 10.5 to 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface 
during our initial geotechnical engineering exploration performed in 2018. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-13.1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM  D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound rope cathead hammer falling 
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The 
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of 
blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored 
to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Boring Nos. 105 and 106 were converted to inclinometer monitoring wells by 
installing 2.75-inch diameter inclinometer casings manufactured by DGSI (Durham Geo 
Slope Indicator) to evaluate the long-term slope creep and/or instability. It should be noted 
that the inclinometer casings were installed with the A-Axis perpendicular to southern 
edge of the parking lot, while the B-Axis parallel to the southern edge of the parking lot.  
A Digitilt AT probe manufactured by DGSI was used to retrieve data from both the A-Axis 
and B-Axis on an approximately monthly basis.

Boring Nos. 103 and 107 were converted to groundwater monitoring wells by 
installing vibrating wire piezometers (Model 4500 series manufactured by Geokon) to 
measure seepage water level and observe the groundwater fluctuation at the site.  Each 
borehole was backfilled with cement-bentonite grout with a traffic rated hole cover over 
the monitoring point.  Data were recorded on a near-continuous basis using a Model LC 
datalogger manufactured by Geokon and downloaded for processing from the time of 
installation through the monitoring period.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Twelve Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test results 
are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-3. 
Six Consolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D4767) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the long-term shear strengths of the 
clayey soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 through B-9. 
Six consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D2435) were performed on selected 
samples of the existing fill materials to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered.  Consolidation test results are presented on Plates B-10 through B-
15.
