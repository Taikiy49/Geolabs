EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This basis of design report was developed in support of the design of the
recommended improvements to reduce the potential for hazards associated with rockfall
from the hillslopes at the Kalawahine Streamside Subdivision project site The rockfall
protection project site consists of 35 individual house lots of the subdivision located on the
upslope side of the subdivision roadways that are exposed to a hillside which contains
various potential rockfall hazards loose rocks and scattered boulders
The project essentially consists of two regions of potential rockfall hazard concern
Region No 1 encompasses some graded slopes and rock cut slopes located at the rear of
the homes just below the existing concrete drainage ditch that traverses the project site
Potential rockfall concerns in this area pertain to some localized areas of potentially
unstable rock composing the rock cut slope In addition some of the graded slopes in
Region No 1 are composed of fill materials with rock fragments and decomposed rock
materials that may be susceptible to erosion and the generation of potential rockfall
Region No 2 encompasses the natural hill slopes located above the existing concrete
drainage ditch extending toward the top of the hillside ridgeline There are a significant
number of boulders and rock formation outcroppings on the hillside that have the potential
to produce rolling boulders that may roll or bounce toward the existing homes
Based on further observations and additional mapping of the existing site
conditions we believe that a moderate level of potential rockfall hazard exists at portions
of the project site and that some proactive measures should be undertaken to reduce the
potential rockfall hazards from the impacting the existing homes from the identified
potential rockfall hazards and slope weathering conditions
In lieu of the removal of individual rock and boulder hazards from the slopes within
Region No 2 at the project site we believe that it is viable to construct a rockfall impact
barrier at the rear of the homes The rockfall barrier would consist of a specialized cable
net fence that is designed to catch rolling boulders and absorb the impact energy from
small to large rockfall impacts Furthermore removal of the individual rock and boulder
hazards from the slopes would result in significant disturbance to the existing ground
surface conditions vegetated and potentially result in additional hazards
Within Region No 1 we recommend that the potentially unstable rock outcroppings
from several of the lots where the exposed potential rockfall hazards exist be removed
In addition we also recommend that the ground surface exposing loose surface rocks and
rock fragments be removed Following the rock and debris removal work on the slopes
the installation of erosion control matting is proposed to temporarily reduce the effect of
surface erosion on the exposed ground surfaces until other permanent improvements
such as landscaping planting of ground cover or retaining structures may be installed by
the affected residents

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older shield volcano Waianae Volcano is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western
third of the island The younger shield volcano Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene Epoch to early Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the Koolau
Volcano remained active Therefore the older Waianae Volcano s eastern flank was

The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by a
long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the dome
shaped Koolau Shield Volcano The erosional valleys grew larger by the processes of

Approximately 850 000 to years ago some limited volcanic activity
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents scattered
across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu The eruptions occurred along the
established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano Some of the most notable
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head Craters
were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series In addition some Honolulu
Volcanic Series eruptions occurred in the Tantalus area that blanketed the Koolau
mountainside and Makiki vicinity with cinders Roundtop and Sugarloaf pyroclastic
deposits The eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series continued over a period of several
hundred thousand years which might have ended as recently as approximately
30 000 years ago

Surface boulders and rock fragments generated by the erosion and breakdown of
the parent rock formation are encountered as widely scattered deposits throughout the
project site The boulders and rock fragments typically break from the parent rock
formation which may become cyclically unstable with the passage of time and effects of
erosion Some of the detached boulders and rock fragments may be re mobilized
downslope to form colluvial deposits consisting of loose rock materials or individual
boulder deposits The boulders and rock fragments typically come to rest as relatively
stable colluvial deposits on the hillside Less frequently unstable and precariously
positioned boulders may be initiated to roll downslope for considerable distances
especially on slopes that have steep slope segments or ledges that promote gains in
rolling energy
Boulder deposits that are positioned at rest on the ground surface typically require
an external force or significant change in environmental conditions to induce toppling and
rolling movement The rolling motion of boulders may be initiated by a variety of external
factors The induced rolling motion may continue or increase depending on the steepness
of the slope and presence of barriers on the slope In general slopes flatter than about
three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V are not conducive to the initiation of rolling rock

The a a lava flows that compose the hillside form generally low relief outcrops at the
ground surface The rock outcrops observed at the site generally have about 3 to 6 feet of
vertical relief with some prominent ledge outcroppings extending up to about 15 feet in
vertical height The rock formation outcrops tend to create a stepped hillside character
with sporadic bare rock ledges or scarps interspersed with flatter terrace features covered
with surface soils The a a lava flows are typified by the presence of hard rock layers
interbedded with clinker seams that are composed of weaker more erosive fragmented
rock materials The bounding clinker seams are generally thinner than the more massive
hard rock layers that represent the core of the individual lava flows
The hard rock layers usually form more erosion resistant ledge outcroppings with
near vertical scarp faces The underlying clinker seam is usually more susceptible to
erosion and weathering Therefore the weaker clinker seams erode faster than the
overlying hard cap rock creating undercut ledges and cave features As the erosion of the
clinker seam progresses the hard rock ledge becomes increasingly unsupported and is
susceptible to breakage If the undercut becomes pronounced the hard rock ledge may
fracture from the parent outcrop and fall to the ground thus initiating downslope movement
as a boulder Other possible causes of rockfall boulder generation include failure along
open fractures within the rock formation and the presence of voids and lava tubes which
may initiate instability in the overlying rock materials
It should be noted that rockfall and mass wasting of steep slopes are a fundamental
part of the geologic evolution of hillslopes within valleys It remains very difficult if not
impossible to predict when rockfall may occur as many variables have an effect on the
generation of rockfall events However experience has shown that rockfall hazards are
heightened most during and following periods of moderate to heavy rainfall where runoff is
produced and when disturbance to the slope surface is incurred

undertaken within a prudent time frame to address the potential rockfall hazards

3 1 Climatic Considerations
The project site is located in the Papakolea area on the Island of Oahu Papakolea
is located on the lower south facing slopes of the Koolau Mountains as shown on the

The project site has a southwesterly exposure and is located on the leeward side of
the Koolau Mountains at elevations ranging from about 300 to 450 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL Therefore the climate of the project site generally may be characterized as sunny
interspersed with passing rain showers This climatic condition allows for frequent wetting
and drying of the exposed ground surfaces
As a result the dark brown native clayey soils encountered at the surface of the site
may experience some shrinkage and swelling cycles in relation to the cyclic wetting and
drying of the ground surfaces The expansion and contraction of the surface soils create
cracks in the soils that open when dry and close when wet The soil shrink swell cycles
can be significant enough to cause the near surface soils to slowly overturn or creep on
inclined slope surfaces Therefore the potential exists for the slow moving soils to alter
the stability of existing boulder deposits located on steep slopes
3 2 Vegetation Considerations
The project site is located on a lower hillside area below the densely forested
Tantalus Round Top forest reserve The site receives a moderate amount of rainfall
which supports a fairly dense growth of vegetation ranging from brush to large trees
Several areas of dense bamboo growth exist mainly in the eastern through central portions
of the site above the existing concrete drainage ditch Some of the vegetation tall
grasses woody vines and haole koa brush appears to respond to the seasonal variation
in rainfall by becoming dormant during the drier summer months Therefore better
exposures of the ground surface boulders and rock outcroppings may be encountered
during the summer months

3 3 Rockfall Mechanism
The potential rockfall hazards located within Region No 2 at the project site are
related to two possible sources which include the following 1 individual boulders and
groups of boulder deposits and 2 rock formation outcroppings The greater risk appears
to involve the possible re mobilization of existing boulder deposits located on sloping
terrain The existing boulder deposits are believed to have been produced by the erosion
of upslope rock outcroppings over very long periods of time The most likely causes for
the possible initiation of movement of the boulder deposits are flooding rains and
associated runoff or disturbance to the natural environmental conditions drainage and
vegetation surrounding the boulders Other causes may include earthquake shaking
ground vibration and impact by falling rock from above
As mentioned previously several hundred boulders in Region No 2 were identified
mapped and subjectively rated either low moderate or high potential hazards based on
their condition at the time of our field evaluation The approximate location of these
boulders is shown on the Potential Rockfall Hazards Plan Plates 3 1 and 3 2 In addition
to the mapped boulders we estimate that several hundred additional boulders were not
recorded because of the their embedment and relative insignificance for potential
movement Other boulders may have been missed due to the thick vegetation coverage
that may have obscured them from view
Another possible source of rockfall is the existing rock formation outcroppings
located on the hillside Over time the erosion of less stable rock outcrops may
occasionally produce blocks of rock or rock fragments that fall from the outcrop If the
outcrop is located on sloping hillsides steeper than about 3H 1V the fallen rock may roll
downslope provided the shape of the rock is favorable for rolling motion rounded to

Due to the widespread coverage of the potentially hazardous boulders and rock

limited impact on the hillside and vegetation characteristics is needed to provide some
level of protection to the homes and residents from potential future rockfall Based on our
evaluation of various remedial alternatives we believe that a rockfall impact barrier is the
most viable alternative to provide long term and comprehensive protection of the homes
from the potential rockfall sources located on the hillside without causing widespread
disturbance to the natural slope conditions
Other remedial alternatives such as the removal destruction and or stabilization of
individual boulders is not feasible except for a few lots on the project site due to the large
amount of potential hazards spread across the densely vegetated project site The
targeting of individual boulders for removal or stabilization is generally discouraged unless
a limited number of targeted rocks can be clearly identified in readily accessible settings
3 4 Rockfall History
We understand that some residents have expressed concern over the possibility
of rocks rolling from the hillside especially following recent reports of other rockfall
incidents that occurred in similar hillside residential neighborhoods In addition several
incidents involving smaller rocks and boulders falling rolling from the slopes at the rear
of the homes have been reported The incidents did not result in significant property
loss or personal injury however the events have heightened the overall awareness of
the potential for rockfall to occur at the project site

4 1 Overview of Rockfall Protection Work
The primary purpose of this rockfall protection project is undertaken for the
following purposes
e Reduce the potential rockfall hazards associated with the existing boulder
deposits and rock outcrops located on the hillside within Region No 2
Stabilize some of the graded slopes located in Region No 1 which
contain some potential rockfall hazards related to the existing rock
outcrops and the near surface fill soils containing rock fragments
Based on our rockfall evaluation the natural hillslopes encompassed by Region
No 2 have a large number of boulder deposits and rock formation outcrops with varying
potential for producing rockfall Protection from the potential rockfall produced by the
widely scattered rock source areas may be accomplished by the construction of a
specialized rockfall impact barrier that is designed to flex and capture rockfall impacts
The general location of the proposed barrier may range between about 8 and 55 feet
upslope of the existing concrete drainage ditch structure depending on the existing
ground surface terrain and position of existing features such as rock formation
outcrops large trees and boulder deposits The approximate locations of the proposed
rockfall barrier at the affected lots are shown on the Conceptual Improvements Plan
Plates 4 1 through 4 4
It should be noted that the directional alignment and positioning of the rockfall
barrier will vary from lot to lot to allow for limited resident foot traffic access around a
margin of the barrier In general the barrier will be constructed as individual segments
spanning two lots at a time Openings of about 8 to 10 feet wide will be provided at
each end of the barrier segment spanning the two lots In addition the barrier will be
positioned in an effort to avoid large topographic changes and existing rock ledge
outcrops that would require heavy excavation and grading of the ground surface
because significant grading of the ground surface is not desirable along the rockfall

4 3 Rockfall Impact Barrier
The rockfall impact barrier proposed for construction within Region No 2 of portions
of the project site consists of a specialized fence structure which is designed to absorb the
energy from rockfall impact and retain the fallen material The rockfall impact barrier
consists of cable wire rope ring nets spanning between support posts that are held in
place by anchor plates and lateral restraint cables The barrier is designed to absorb the
energy of a rock impact and dissipate the energy through the use of flexible nets which
act as a braking element The rockfall impact barrier thereby ensures rocks are caught as
opposed to bouncing or striking with enough force to topple the retention system The
barrier may be designed for varying load impacts that are estimated based on the existing
site conditions such as boulder size slope inclination and composition computed rolling
velocities and possible boulder bounce heights
The barriers are commonly used along highways and other areas where structures
located along lengthy slope segments must be protected from rockfall The barriers are
suitable for sites where rockfall sources are widely scattered and where it may be
generally impractical to locate and stabilize the individual hazardous blocks of rock
because of the quantity of hazards difficult slope access and other restrictions The
barriers would require some periodic maintenance to check the performance of the system
and remove accumulated rock material behind the barrier
The primary advantage of the rockfall impact barrier is that it is a comprehensive
rockfall protection system which should provide a high level of protection against most of
the potential rockfall hazards on the slopes In addition man induced disturbance to the

The use of a rockfall impact barrier is excluded from some portions of the project
site where the homes are believed to have only limited exposure to potential rockfall
hazards For instance Lot Nos 55 through 58 have lengthy slopes of flatter inclinations
with a limited number of upslope boulders contained therein Therefore a rockfall impact

previously graded to remove most of the boulders and rock formation outcrops from the
ground surface therefore a rockfall barrier is also not warranted Finally due to the finite
number of readily identifiable boulder hazards encountered at Lot Nos 78 through 81 it is
believed that the removal of the potential hazardous rocks within these lots could be
performed in lieu of the installation of a rockfall impact barrier system
The main components of the rockfall impact barrier include the support posts wire
ring nets anchorage ground plates wire retaining ropes braking rings and the spiral rope
anchors The steel components are typically hot dipped galvanized and the wire ropes are
DIN 2078 coated A special protective zinc aluminum coating may be applied where
environmental corrosion is of concern Under normal field conditions the lifespan of the
steel material components is estimated to range on the order of about 40 to 50 years It is
recommended that some periodic inspection occurring on the order of about 5 to 10 year
intervals be implemented to check the condition of the rockfall impact barrier system for
possible signs of corrosion
It should be noted that the ring nets which span between the posts may be
temporarily disconnected in order to remove boulders that may be caught by the rockfall
impact barrier Larger boulder impacts may require that some net panels be replaced to
maintain the integrity of the system It is difficult if not impossible to predict the frequency
of possible boulder impacts on the barrier

The proposed barrier height and energy absorption capacity of the rockfall impact
barrier has been designed based on the use of the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program
CRSP which provides an estimation of the bounce heights rolling velocities and rolling
energies of various size boulders with respect to a formulated model that is generally
representative of the existing site conditions The safety goal for the barrier performance
is to capture and retain 90 percent of the potential rockfall based on the existing site
conditions The selection of the rockfall barrier system instead of individual boulder
destruction and removal alternative for the majority of the project site allows for the desired
safety goal to be attained
Based on our analyses of the simulated rockfall behavior at the project site based
on CRSP the proposed rockfall impact barrier should consist of an 8 foot high barrier
rated at low impact Type and at moderate to high impact Type Il energy absorption
capacity The two different types of rockfall impact barrier designated for the various

4 4 Other Considerations
The location of the proposed remedial work area is behind the existing homes at
the project site There is only limited available access between the buildings to reach the
work area from the street The distance between the existing buildings ranges from about
10 to 15 feet wide and consists of sloping ground surfaces Some homes have
landscaping and other improvements located between the existing buildings that further
reduce the available access We anticipate that access between the buildings will be
needed to deliver tools and construction supplies to the slopes and to extricate cut
vegetation rock debris and other materials generated by the construction activity It may
be necessary to designate specific construction access points at selected locations along
the row of buildings
The project site is part of an occupied residential subdivision Since moving in
some residents have performed additional landscaping and have constructed other
improvements such as irrigation systems on the slopes encompassed by Region Nos 1
