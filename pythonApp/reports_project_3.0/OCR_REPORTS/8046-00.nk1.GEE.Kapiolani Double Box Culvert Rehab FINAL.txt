SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of 
about 7 to 8 inches of AC underlain by about 7 to 10 inches of fill material consisting of 
basaltic sandy gravel. Below the pavement structure, very soft to very stiff alluvial 
deposits were encountered to a depth of about 8 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. The alluvial deposits were underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of very 
loose to loose silty/clayey gravel or very soft silty clay to depths of about 18 to 25 feet 
below ground. Medium dense coralline detritus, medium hard coral formation, and 
medium hard sandstone were encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 
38 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was encountered in our borings 
at depths of about 4 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface, corresponding to 
elevations of about ±0 to +1 feet MSL at the time of our field exploration. However, it 
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of 
year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We recommend supporting the new traffic signal pole on a cast-in-place concrete 
drilled shaft foundation. Based on the typical dimensions of the base plate and anchor 
bolts, we envision that a 36-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft would be 
required for the proposed traffic signal pole. Due to the root system of the existing 
monkeypod tree at the new traffic signal pole location, we recommend over-excavating 
the area at least 4.5 feet below the bottom of the shaft cap within a minimum 7.5-foot 
radius measured from the center of the drilled shaft. The over-excavation should be 
backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) prior to drilled shaft 
construction. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Kapiolani Double Box Culvert Rehabilitation project in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of traffic 
signal pole foundations and utilities only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located near the intersection of Kapiolani Boulevard and 
Isenberg Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The culvert consists of an 
8-foot wide by 3-foot tall double box drain structure below Kapiolani Boulevard. We 
understand that the existing culvert soffit slab will be removed and replaced in sections. 
In addition, repairs will be performed on portions of the culvert side walls.  
To mitigate traffic congestions on Kapiolani Boulevard during construction, a 
temporary bridge structure will be installed over each section where the culvert soffit is 
removed to keep all lanes of traffic open. Based on the information provided, we 
understand that the access bridge footings will be about 11 feet wide by 5.5 feet long, 
embedded approximately 3.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
In addition to the culvert repairs, a new traffic signal structure will be installed in 
the median area at the intersection, at the location of an existing large monkeypod tree. 
We understand that the existing tree will be removed, but the root system will remain 
in-place.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The bulk of the Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of 
two extinct shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau. Koolau is the younger of the 
two and forms the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waikiki/Moiliili area resides on 
the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano where relatively young coastal and marine 
deposits comprise the low gradient Honolulu Coastal Plain of Oahu. These deposits 
overlie buried (older) seaward sloping Koolau Volcano deposits. Because of the coastal 
plain location, the geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the region were directly 
affected by past global glacio-eustatic fluctuations (rise and fall) of the sea level during 
the Pleistocene age (~2 million years ago). These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in 
stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on 
Oahu resulting in the repeated deposition and erosion of a variety of coastal and marine 
deposits over a long period of time. Some of these intercalated deposits include soft 
lagoonal and marsh sediments, broken granular coralline detritus, and hard coralline 
reef ledge materials related to varying stands of the sea during deposition. 
In addition, with the passage of time, the Honolulu Coastal Plain was incised by 
multiple seaward trending drainage channels (streams) emanating from higher 
elevations of the Koolau Volcano. These prominent drainage channels cut deeply into 
the layered terrestrial, coastal, and marine deposits existing at the time and locally 
removed (scoured) the native deposits and replaced them by deposition of various 
younger age alluvial soil products derived from inland terrain. It should be noted that at 
depth, the base of the Honolulu Coastal Plain stratigraphic section is the basaltic lavas 
of the Koolau Volcano, identified as Tertiary Koolau Basalt. 
During the Pleistocene (a.k.a. last Ice Age), many glacio-eustatic sea level 
changes occurred as a result of worldwide continental glaciation. The higher sea level 
stands caused the accumulation of sediments in the heads of the old bays, 
accumulation of reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and deposition of 
lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing coralline reefs. The

Rapid rises in the base level erosion-deposition equilibrium caused the hasty

Manoa – Launiu – This system of channels generally runs from the 
University Avenue and King Street intersection to Waikiki along 
Beachwalk. The system appears to consist of meandering channels 
following the old drainage trend of Manoa Stream. In the Waikiki area, the 
system appears to extend to depths of about 150 to 200 feet below the 
ground surface.

2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located on the southeastern side of the Kapiolani Boulevard 
and Isenberg Street intersection in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
T-intersection is bounded by single-family homes and two-story walk-up apartments to 
the northwest and northeast, and the Marco Polo Condominiums to the south.  
The existing double box culvert structure traverses diagonally beneath Kapiolani 
Boulevard, extending from Hausten Ditch towards the Marco Polo Condominiums 
driveway. Based on our field observations and the topographic survey information 
provided, the existing ground surface is relatively flat, sloping downward gently from 
west to east. Existing pavement surface elevations range from about +5 to +6 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL) in the vicinity of the existing double box culvert. The location of the 
double box culvert is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Within the project limits, Kapiolani Boulevard is paved with asphaltic concrete 
(AC) pavement. The existing pavement was observed to be in fair condition, with 
occasional block fracturing, shallow ruts, and patched potholes. On the eastern side of 
the intersection, between the eastbound and westbound lanes of Kapiolani Boulevard, a 
grassed median with a large monkeypod tree was noted. The monkeypod tree was 
observed to be about 30 feet tall with a tree canopy approximately 50 to 60 feet in 
diameter.

soils. Total settlement of the drilled shaft is estimated to be on the order of less 
than 0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the settlement is elastic 
and should occur as the loads are applied.

3.2.3 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
In general, the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the 
contractor’s method of installation and construction procedures. The following 
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the 
drilled shaft foundations. 
The load-bearing capacities of the drilled shaft depend, to a significant extent, on 
the frictional resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soils. Therefore, 
proper construction techniques, especially during the drilling operations, are 
important. The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft hole and in 
placing concrete into the drilled hole. 
The subsurface materials within the depth of the proposed drilled shaft generally 
consist of very soft to very stiff alluvium consisting of gravelly/clayey silt. The 
alluvial soils encountered within the depth of the drilled shaft may contain 
cobbles and boulders. Therefore, some difficult drilling conditions may be 
encountered and should be expected in these soils. The drilled shaft contractor 
will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill through the cobbles 
and boulders that may be encountered during drilled shaft installation operations. 
Based on our field exploration and the estimated length of the drilled shaft, 
groundwater is expected in the drilled hole during the shaft installation work. 
Therefore, concrete placement by tremie method will be required. 
A low-shrinkage concrete mix with a high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should 
be used to provide close contact between the drilled shaft and the surrounding 
soils. In addition, the concrete should be placed promptly after drilling (within 
24 hours after drilling of the holes) to reduce the potential for softening of the 
sidewalls of the drilled hole. 
It is imperative that a Geolabs representative is present at the project site to 
observe the drilling and installation of the drilled shafts during construction. 
Although the drilled shaft design is primarily based on skin friction, the bottom of 
the drilled hole should be relatively free of loose materials prior to placement of

3.3 
Utility Trench 
We anticipate that underground utilities, such as new traffic signal lines, may be 
installed for the project. In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the 
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities. The contractor should 
determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to 
practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply 
with the applicable federal, state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should 
be responsible for trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the conduit for uniform 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to 
use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the 
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper 
trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
We envision that soft and/or loose alluvial or lagoonal deposits will be 
encountered at or near the invert elevations of the proposed utility lines. Where these 
soft and/or loose materials are encountered, we recommend providing a subgrade 
stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation) 
wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the bedding layer 
to provide uniform support. The stabilization layer should extend beyond the sides of the 
pipe a minimum width of one-fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or 12 inches, 
whichever is greater. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select granular fill material.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 
18 and 38 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicate the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. It should be noted that RQD 
does not apply to coral material. However, the RQD values of the coral material are 
included in the Boring Logs for rough characterization of the coral material. Generally, 
the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical 
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael 
(1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils, 
two Unconfined Compression tests were performed on selected soil samples in 
accordance with ASTM D2166. The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. The individual stress-strain curves of the unconfined 
compression tests are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Results of the direct 
shear test are presented on Plate B-5.
