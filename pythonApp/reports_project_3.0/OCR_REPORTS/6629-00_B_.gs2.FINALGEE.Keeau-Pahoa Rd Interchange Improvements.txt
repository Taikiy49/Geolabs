SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our exploratory borings at the proposed roadway improvement site generally 
encountered a surface fill layer underlain by basalt rock formation extending to the 
maximum depth explored of 20 feet. The surface fill layer was about 0.5 to 5.5 feet thick 
and was composed of loose to very dense sands and gravels with some cobbles. The 
basalt rock formation was severely fractured to massive and hard. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Because of the high seismic loading to a gravity wall structure at the project site, 
the use of a reinforced soil slope is recommended for the embankment at the northern end 
of the new roundabout.  We understand that a steepened slope with slope inclinations up 
to 3/4H:1V is required. In general, we believe that fill slopes may be designed with slope 
inclinations up to 3/4H:1V provided that the earth materials are reinforced with sufficient 
layers of geotextiles and/or geogrids to strengthen the soils. 
A woven geotextile fabric (Mirafi HS400 or equal) with minimum length of 15 feet 
and vertical spacing of 18 inches should be used for the reinforced soil slope.  The 
reinforced fill material should consist of aggregate base course compacted to a minimum 
of 95 percent relative compaction.  A fiber-reinforced shotcrete facing about 8 inches thick 
should be used on the slope face to protect the fabric from damage.  At the toe of the 
reinforced soil slope, a 3-foot deep keyway should be installed to reduce the potential for 
scouring the slope toe. The installation of geogrids is recommended to facilitate 
compaction at the slope face and provide anchorage of the shotcrete at the slope face.  
Site grading generally will consist of cuts and fills up to about 9 feet thick.  The large 
fill area is on the north side of the new roundabout.  In general, we anticipate the 
excavations will encounter fill and basalt rock.  The excavated fill and basalt rock materials 
may be used as a source of general fill and backfill materials provided that the materials 
are processed to meet the material requirements discussed herein.  Fill slopes (other than 
the reinforced soil slope) should be designed with a maximum inclination of 2H:1V or 
flatter.  Cut slopes in the fill and basalt rock formation may be designed with a maximum 
inclination of 2H:1V and 1H:1V or flatter, respectively.  
Extension of the existing 48-inch and 60-inch Corrugated Metal Pipe drainage pipes 
will be required.  Because of the high seismic loading, the use of headwall structures is not

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Keeau-Pahoa Road Intersection Improvements at Old 
Government Road project in the District of Puna on the Island of Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and 
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for design of reinforced 
soil slope, site grading and culvert extension only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project consists of a new intersection at Keeau-Pahoa Road (Old 
Government Road), Pahoa Bypass Road, and Pahoa Village Road in the District of 
Puna on the Island of Hawaii. The project consists of the installation of a modern 
roundabout at the intersection. The project improvements include site grading, 
installation of new pavements, drainage improvements, sidewalks, and highway lighting.

8 inches of aggregate base course and 6 inches of aggregate subbase.

outside diameter of 98 and 135 feet, respectively.  The circular median in the middle of 
the roundabout will include a truck apron about 15 feet wide at the perimeter and 
landscaping in the center.  The roadways approaching the roundabout will be divided in 
the different traffic directions.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 10,360 square kilometers (4,000 square miles). This island 
was formed by the activity of the following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), 
Mauna Kea (activity during recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), 
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active). 
The project site is on the eastern flank of the Kilauea Shield Volcano, which has 
been built up by basaltic lava flows. These flows are of the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age) 
and of the Recent Age, and belong to the Puna volcanic series. 
The lava formation at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow, which is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of 
vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava. Cavities 
form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes 
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when 
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may 
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Inter-flow cavities are generally 
smaller in horizontal extent.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the design criteria provided by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, the project site will need to be designed based on a 
seismic acceleration coefficient of 1.127g.  The following sections provide discussions 
on the seismicity of the Island of Hawaii and the Site Class for seismic design at the 
site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates.  In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity.  Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before 
or during volcanic eruptions.  In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt.  The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by 
instruments.  However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and 
a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii.  Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii.  Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 
90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of 
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii.  The 
Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 
(M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5.  Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui.  The effects of earthquakes occurring on the

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our borings encountered a surface fill layer underlain by basalt rock 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of 20 feet below the existing 
ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of 
our field exploration. 
For the construction of the new roundabout, a reinforced soil slope with 
inclinations up to 3/4H:1V will be installed.  The reinforced soil slope will consist of 
woven geotextile fabric, geogrids, aggregate base course, and shotcrete. 
In general, permanent fill slopes (other than the reinforced soil slope) may be 
designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Detailed discussions of these items 
and our geotechnical recommendations for design of the project are presented in the

3.1 
Reinforced Soil Slope 
Because of the high seismic loading to a gravity wall structure at the project site, 
the use of a reinforced soil slope is recommended for the embankment at the northern 
end of the new roundabout.  An existing drainageway is located at the toe of the 
embankment.  We understand that a steepened slope with slope inclinations up to 
3/4H:1V will be required to reduce the extent of the embankment slope into the 
drainageway.  In general, we believe that fill slopes may be designed with slope 
inclinations up to 3/4H:1V provided that the earth materials are reinforced with adequate 
layers of geotextiles and geogrids to strengthen the fill soils. 
The reinforced soil slope system is a composite slope system which utilizes 
woven geotextile fabric as primary reinforcement to reinforce the backfill zone and to 
improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone.   Reinforced slope geotextiles are 
woven polyester fabric with high tensile strength.  The reinforced soil slope system 
generally provides a cost-effective solution to slope stability problems, which may 
include the following:  insufficient right-of-way, high surcharge loads, poor-quality fills, 
high seismic forces, steep slopes, or difficult landslide repairs.  When geotextiles are

placed in soil, the geotextile interlocks with the adjacent soil, creating a soil-geotextile 
composite with greatly enhanced engineering properties.   
We recommend that the woven geotextile fabric used in the reinforced soil slope 
consist of Mirafi HS400 or equal.  The vertical spacing of the geotextile fabric should be 
18 inches.  The front face of the geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 5 feet from 
the face of the slope.  Based on our analysis, the reinforced zone of the geotextile fabric 
soil slope should extend laterally behind the 3/4H:1V slope face to a minimum distance 
of 15 feet. 
The reinforced fill material should consist of aggregate base course material with 
maximum particle size of 3 inches.  The aggregate base course material should have an 
angle of friction of at least 38 degrees when compacted to 95 percent relative compaction.  
Fill materials for the reinforced earth slopes should be placed in loose lifts not exceeding 
8 inches thick, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent of the maximum dry density established in accordance 
with AASHTO T-180 test methods (ASTM D 1557). 
A fiber-reinforced shotcrete facing of approximately 8 inches in thickness should 
be provided along the slope face of the geotextile fabric soil slope to protect the fabric 
from damage.  In addition, weepholes also should be provided to allow for drainage of 
water trapped behind the shotcrete facing.  To reduce the potential for scour at the toe 
of the reinforced soil slope, a 3-foot deep keyway should be provided at the toe of the 
reinforced soil slope. 
Bi-axial geogrids (Tensar BX1200 or equal) should be used in the middle of the 
geotextile fabric layers at the slope face to facilitate compaction of the reinforced fill 
material near the slope face.  To provide anchorage of the shotcrete at the slope face, 
the installation of geogrid (Tensar BX1200 or equal) between two of the reinforced 
layers may be considered.  The geogrid will be embedded into the slope for anchorage 
and a portion of the geogrid will extend beyond the slope face to provide anchorage for 
the shotcrete facing.

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and grubbed 
thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or 
disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials. Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and 
grubbing below areas designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should 
be over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. 
Prior to filling, the existing ground exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches (where possible), moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where 
shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend

3.2.2 Fills and Backfills

and basalt rock. The excavated fill and basalt rock materials may be used as a

no greater than 3 inches and have no more than 30 percent passing the No. 200 
sieve. In addition, materials within the upper 2 feet of the finished subgrades should 
have a laboratory CBR value of 25 or greater with a maximum swell value of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.

3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Granular fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement 
of the fills and backfills within 3 feet of the finished grades should be increased to at 
least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during site 
grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and 
the proper moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional 
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.

3.2.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it appears that cut 
slopes would expose fill materials consisting of sands and gravels. Permanent cut 
slopes exposing the medium dense to very dense existing fill may be cut at an 
inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
Fill slopes constructed of the processed on-site materials or imported fill materials 
should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on 
slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing benches 
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at 
the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes is not 
acceptable. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching 
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and 
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
significant erosion of the finished slopes. 
3.2.5 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by hard basalt 
rock formation near the ground surface. Therefore, the contractor likely will 
encounter some difficult excavation conditions during construction where the hard 
basalt rock formation is encountered.

recommended.  Instead, the ends of the pipe extension should be mitered to the 
finished slope face.

3.4 
Corrosion Potential 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regards to the corrosivity of the soil.  In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure.  
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion.  
Corrosion testing, including pH and minimum resistivity were performed on selected soil 
samples obtained during our field exploration for corrosivity evaluation.  Based on the 
test results, the soil samples may be considered to be mildly corrosive. The test results 
are presented in Appendix B.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions along the roadway improvements were explored by 
drilling and sampling eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8, to a depth of 
about 20 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with auger and coring equipment. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-8. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using 
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a 
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the 
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74.
