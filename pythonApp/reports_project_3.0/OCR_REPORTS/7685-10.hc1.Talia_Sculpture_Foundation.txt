SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff residual and saprolitic materials 
extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 29.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. It should be noted that some of the near-surface clayey soils may exhibit soft 
consistency due to softening caused by high precipitation in the mountain range. We did 
not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the generally stiff subsurface conditions, we recommend using shallow 
spread footing to support the new sculpture structure planned for the project. An 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf may be used for the design of shallow 
footing bearing on the recompacted on-site soils. The allowable bearing pressure may be 
increased to 3,000 psf provided that the bottom of the footing is over-excavated at least 
24 inches and backfilled with compacted select granular fill materials to improve the 
bearing support.  For footing bearing on the recompacted on-site soils, the bottom of the 
footing excavation should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to 
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing 
steel and/or concrete. 
To reduce the potential for sculpture distresses due to differing foundation 
subgrade materials, we recommend that the entire foundation footprint be situated in a cut 
condition to provide a uniform bearing support.  We also recommend embedding the 
bottom of the shallow footing a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished 
grade.  The total settlement of the sculpture foundation is estimated to be on the order of 
about 1 inch or less if the foundation is designed and constructed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein. 
Based on the results of the slope stability analyses, it is our opinion that the 
planned cut slope and the proposed sculpture foundation constructed in accordance with 
the recommendations presented in this report would have sufficient factor of safety against 
the global slope instability.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Talia Sculpture Foundation, Koele Sculpture Garden project located 
northeast of Lanai City on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, site grading, and corrosion protection only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the development of the Talia sculpture for the Koele 
Sculpture Garden at the Koele Golf Course on the northeast side of Lanai City. Based 
on the information provided, the project involves the development of a 46-foot tall 
sculpture structure.  
Details of the structural and grading plans were not available at the time this 
report was prepared. Based on Google Earth™, the project site is located at the ridge of 
the Kaiholena Gulch next to the Practice Area of the Upper Koele Golf Course with 
ground surface elevations ranging from about +1,960 to about +1,800 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL). We envision cuts up to about 15 feet will be required to achieve the design 
grades for the project.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is 
the result of volcanic extrusion on the southwest rift zone. The rift zones intersect at the 
island summit. The summit of the shield volcano collapsed to form a caldera from which 
a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends south-southeast 
toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed to the sea cliff indicate that this Manele 
Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly-filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of the Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where 
the most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. 
The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe 
tholeiitic basaltic flows that are generally inclined at about 6 to 15 degrees from 
horizontal (where not disturbed by faulting). Volcanic rocks on the Island of Lanai are 
grouped as the Lanai Volcanic Series. The near-surface soils generally consist of 
well-drained, fine-textured and moderately fine-textured soils derived from volcanic ash 
and the in-situ weathering of the igneous rocks. The formation of the Island of Lanai is 
also the result of large changes in sea-level that caused submergence and 
re-emergence of the land mass through geologic time. Faulting and stream erosion in 
conjunction with changes in sea-level are also responsible for the high sea cliffs along 
the southern and western coastlines. 
The project site is northerly from the Palawai Basin and is near the foothills of the 
central mountain portion of the island. The existing Four Seasons Resort Lanai, The

Site Description 
The project site is located at the Koele Golf Course on the north and east sides 
of Lanai City on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. It is approximately bounded by the Four 
Seasons Resort Lanai and the Upper Koele Golf Course as shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1.  
Based on our field observations, the project site is located on an existing slope 
that is heavily vegetated with mostly ironwood trees and covered with ironwood needles 
and other leaf litter on the forest floor.  An existing 3-foot wide nature trail, consisting of 
a simple dirt path with a log border, currently traverses to the west of the proposed 
sculpture location. 
The existing slope generally slopes down on a southwesterly direction with a 
gradient of about four horizontal to one vertical (4H:1V) at the upper boring locations 
(B-1 to B-3) to about 3H:1V around B-4 near the proposed sculpture location. The 
terrain becomes slightly steeper below the proposed sculpture location. Based on 
Google Earth™, the existing ground elevations range from about Elevation +1,960 feet 
MSL at the top to about Elevation +1,800 feet MSL at the bottom.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity that can impact the planned sculpture structure and seismic 
design considerations may need to be addressed. 
The geotechnical data obtained by our field exploration and a literature review of 
the geological information in the project vicinity were used to characterize the 
subsurface conditions for seismic design considerations. Generally, the boring 
exploration encountered stiff to very stiff residual and saprolitic soils extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 29.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We 
anticipate the saprolitic soils will likely be underlain by basalt formation at greater 
depths. Because the depth to the basalt formation is unknown, we have assumed that 
the upper 100 feet of the soil profile would consist of the stiff residual soils and saprolitic 
materials. Based on this assumed subsurface soil profile, we believe that the site may 
be classified as a “Stiff Soil Profile” corresponding to a Site Class D in accordance with 
the International Building Code (Table No. 1613.5.2), 2006 Edition.

3.1 
Foundations 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the proposed Talia 
sculpture will be up to about 46 feet in height. We envision that the new sculpture 
foundation subgrades will likely consist of stiff clayey silt soils as encountered in our 
drilled borings. Therefore, we recommend using shallow spread footing to support the 
proposed sculpture structure. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf may be used for the design of 
the spread footing foundation bearing on the recompacted on-site soils.  To reduce the 
potential for sculpture distresses due to differing foundation subgrade materials, we 
recommend that the entire foundation footprint be situated in a cut condition to provide a 
uniform bearing support.  The allowable bearing pressure may be increased to 
3,000 psf provided the bottom of the footing is over-excavated at least 24 inches and 
backfilled with compacted select granular fill materials to improve the bearing support. 
The allowable bearing pressures recommended above are for dead-plus-live loads and 
may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
Based on our field exploration results and experience at the project site, we 
anticipate that the subsurface clayey silt soils may exhibit softening due to high 
precipitation in the mountain range, as well as a slight potential for shrinking and 
swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for future 
distress to the sculpture resulting from softening, shrinking and swelling of the 
near-surface clayey silt soils, we recommend embedding the bottom of the shallow 
footing a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. 
Where sculpture footing is located adjacent to other below-grade structures, 
utility trenches or easements, the footing should extend to a depth below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane projected upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench,

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Surface vegetation and other unsuitable materials 
should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and yielding areas 
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill 
and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm material and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The 
excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in 
landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth 
of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
compaction requirements for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such 
as the golf cart pathway subgrades, should be increased to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation, the soft subgrade 
should be removed and replaced with well-compacted select granular fill.  Where 
shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction, we recommend 
preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and replacing these areas with 
well-compacted select granular fill. A Geolabs field representative should evaluate

3.4 
Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content 
and sulfate content, were performed on a selected sample obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. The 
test results are summarized and presented on Plate B-8 of Appendix B.  
The pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed in general conformance 
with ASTM G51 and G57, respectively, for laboratory pH and resistivity tests using a 
Miller box. Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. 
Generally, a lower resistivity results in a greater potential for corrosion of buried metallic 
objects. Conversely, a higher resistivity means the soil is less likely to contribute to 
corrosion of metallic objects. Based on the test results presented on Plate B-8, the 
subsurface soils at the project site exhibit minimum resistivity value of about 
4,900 ohm-cm, indicating that the subsurface soils are corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 3) 
to buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly designing metallic 
substructures (such as piping) for protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent, in part, on the chloride content and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm) and 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm may be considered “non-corrosive” to 
underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the relatively low values of 
chloride content and sulfate content tested on the in-situ materials, we believe that the 
near-surface soils at the project site may be considered “non-corrosive” and either 
Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the concrete in contact with the 
ground.

3.5 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the sculpture should be sloped to shed water away 
from the foundation and to reduce the potential for ponding.  Excessive landscape 
watering near the foundation should also be avoided considering the slight to moderate 
shrink/swell potential of the on-site soils.  Planters next to foundations should be 
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration 
into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface soils.  
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footing 
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.  In 
addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to 
drain surface water runoff away from the foundations.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths ranging from 
about 11.5 feet to 29.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a portable 
drilling equipment with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
