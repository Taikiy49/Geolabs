In accordance with the request from Hensel Phelps Construction Company, 
Geolabs, Inc. observed the installation of the drilled shafts for the above project. This letter 
report summarizes our geotechnical observations during the drilled shaft foundation 
installation for the Tunnel Influent Pump Station and Headworks Facility at the Kailua 
Regional Wastewater Treatment Plant (RWWTP) project in Kailua on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1.  
Geolabs, Inc. provided geotechnical engineering consultation services for the 
project. The purpose of our geotechnical observations was to monitor the geotechnical 
engineering aspects of the drilled shaft foundation installation for conformance with the 
plans and specifications and to provide subsequent recommendations, as appropriate. 
PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
The primary component of the project involves various improvements to the 
wastewater collection, treatment and disposal system in the Kailua-Kaneohe-Kahalu’u 
wastewater service area in the District of Ko’olaupoko on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
proposed improvements will consist of the construction of a Tunnel Influent Pump Station 
(TIPS) and a Headworks (HDWKS) Facility, which will lift the wastewater from the newly 
constructed sewer infrastructure to the surface for treatment.  A layout of the various 
improvements is shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2.1. 
Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer, the 
maximum column load for the project construction may be on the order of about 
120 kips. The new buildings are designed to be supported on deep foundations, 
consisting of 24-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts. The drilled shafts 
were designed to derive vertical support primarily from skin friction between the drill 
shaft and the upper coralline materials and/or the underlying stiff to very stiff alluvial

DRILLED SHAFT INSTALLATION 
As part of the project requirements, two sacrificial load test shafts were constructed 
and tested. In addition, Low Strain Impact (LSI) testing was performed on several 
production drilled shafts. The following subsections provide a brief description of the load 
test shaft construction and discussions on the production drilled shaft installation and LSI 
testing. 
Load Test Shafts 
As part of the project requirements, two sacrificial 24-inch diameter load test 
shafts, designated as Test Shaft No. 1 and Test Shaft No. 2, were installed at the 
locations shown on the Drilled Shaft Location Plan, Plate 2.2. Test Shaft No. 1 and Test 
Shaft No. 2 were drilled to tip elevations of approximately -65.2 feet and -64.6 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL), respectively. 
Test Shaft Nos. 1 and 2 were load tested on July 7 and 12, 2016, respectively. 
Results of the load tests are contained in the Drilled Shaft Load Test Reports entitled 
“Drilled Shaft Load Test Report, Test Shaft No. 1, Tunnel Influent Pump Station and 
Headworks Facility, Kailua Regional Wastewater Treatment Plant (RWWTP), Kailua, 
Oahu, Hawaii” and “Drilled Shaft Load Test Report, Test Shaft No. 2, Tunnel Influent 
Pump Station and Headworks Facility, Kailua Regional Wastewater Treatment Plant 
(RWWTP), Kailua, Oahu, Hawaii,” dated July 22, 2016.  
Low Strain Impact (LSI) Testing 
Low Strain Impact testing was performed on load test shafts and several production 
shafts in accordance with ASTM D5882 utilizing a Pulse Echo Tester (PET) manufactured 
by PileTest. The PET system meets the project specification requirements with an analog 
to digital resolution of at least 24 bits, a sampling frequency exceeding 50,000 Hz, 
contains available data storage so additional processing and further wave analysis is 
possible, and contains a display to present results in the field for evaluations of preliminary 
data quality and interpretation. Tested drilled shafts are shown in the “PET” column of the 
Drilled Shaft Installation Summary, Plates 3.1 through 3.7.

Production Drilled Shafts 
The drilled shaft subcontractor installed 240 production shafts from July 26 
through December 19, 2016 at the locations shown on the Drilled Shaft Location Plans, 
Plates 2.2 through 2.6. The as-built tip elevations and lengths of the production drilled 
shafts installed for the project are summarized on Plates 3.1 through 3.7 of this report. 
Representatives from Geolabs, Inc. observed the drilled shaft excavation, placement of 
reinforcing cage, and concrete operations during the installation of the production drilled 
shafts. 
In general, the production drilled shafts were excavated using a Watson 3100CM 
track-mounted drill rig. Excavation tools used for the 24-inch diameter shafts included 
24, 27, and 30-inch diameter augers, digging buckets and core barrels, and a 24 and 
27-inch clean-out buckets. For all shafts, 27 and 30-inch diameter temporary steel 
casing was installed to depths of about 30 to 80 feet below the ground surface for 
excavation stability. 
During excavation, sandy gravel were generally encountered at depths of about 
0 to 5 feet, silty clay lagoonal deposit from about 5 to 15 feet, sandy gravel coralline 
detritus layer from about 15 to 35 feet, sandy gravel alluvial layer from about 35 to 
70 feet, and a highly weathered basalt layer from about 70 to 80 feet below the working 
platform elevation. The drilled shafts were extended to varying depths as required by 
the project specifications. The temporary casing was used for the upper portion to full 
depth of the drilled shaft length based on the conditions of the underlying soil stratum

After excavation of the production shafts to their designated tip elevations, the 
reinforcing cages were lowered into the drilled holes to the appropriate elevations with 
the use of a crane. After the reinforcing cage was installed in the drilled hole, we 
observed that concrete was placed within an acceptable time frame from substantial 
completion. Concrete was placed through a 4-inch diameter steel tremie pipe starting 
from the bottom. During concrete placement, we observed that at least 5 feet of 
concrete head was maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe.  Concrete with 
slump ranging from about 7 to 9 inches was used for the drilled shafts. Slump tests and 
compression tests of concrete were performed by others.  
As with most foundation installation projects, some problems did arise during 
construction. The various problems encountered and their remedial action, where 
applicable, are shown in the “Remarks” column of the Drilled Shaft Installation Summary, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.7. 
Based on our observations, it is our professional opinion that the production 
drilled shafts supporting the Tunnel Influent Pump Station and Headworks Facility project 
were installed in general accordance with the intent of the project plans and 
specifications. In addition, it is our professional opinion that the drilled shafts have been 
installed to sufficient depths to provide the design compressive load capacity.
