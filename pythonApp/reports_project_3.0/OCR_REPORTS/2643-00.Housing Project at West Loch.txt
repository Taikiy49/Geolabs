INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
percolation testing for the Preliminary Site Study of the proposed FY 91 Public Private
Venture Housing Project at West Loch The project site is located at the northeasterly
end of Ewa Beach on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site
and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work on the project was performed in general accordance with our revised
proposal to Belt Collins and Associates Incorporated dated This
report summarizes our findings for our preliminary field exploration and percolation
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Ewa District of the island of Oahu Hawaii at the
northeasterly end of the community of Ewa Beach The subject parcel is a relatively
narrow 30 acre strip along the Naval Magazine Lualualei West Loch Branch ESQD arc
The northerly third of the site is currently used for the cultivation of sugar cane and the
southerly two thirds is fallow land with a thick cover of keawe and haole koa trees
It is proposed to develop the site as a turnkey housing project jointedly developed
by the U S Navy and by private industry It is estimated that the project will involve the
design and construction of 300 to 500 one and two bedroom housing units with
appurtenant site improvements and infrastructure installations such as water sewer roads
and drainage

The topography of the site is nearly level It is anticipated that development of the
site would not require substantial amounts of earthwork However since the region of
the site is at a relatively low elevation with little slope drainage would be a key
consideration Based on the information provided to us it is desired to include field
testing and engineering analyses in the preliminary exploration to obtain data pertaining
to utilizing on site drainage disposal such as dry wells and pertaining to the potential
use of the abandoned coral pit to the north of the site as a drainage disposal sump

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Waianae and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Ewa Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the majority of the coastal plain are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during high stands of the sea which
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2643 00 Page 6
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Waianae Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau voicanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
Stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2643 00 Page 7
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials
Agricultural land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our findings development of the proposed project site is feasible
provided that our recommendations are followed The competent coral algal limestone
formation encountered at shallow depths below the project site should provide adequate
support for the proposed housing development
Spread or continuous strip footings may bear on the upper existing fill after
proofrolling and recompaction in accordance with the recommendations presented herein
Alternately spread or continuous strip footings may bear on the competent coral algal
limestone if encountered at shallow depths
Our laboratory testing indicates that the fill soils in the vicinity of Borings 6 and 7
are moderately expansive Special consideration should be given to foundation
construction in portions of the site underlain by moderately expansive soils

Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of earthwork the site should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed of the existing cover of trees shrubs and other vegetation This material
should be removed from the site and disposed of properly Care should be exercised to
avoid contamination of the subsoils with this material
Following clearing and grubbing of the site the ground surface should generally
be scarified brought to near optimum moisture content and proofrolled with a heavy
vibratory roller Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during proofrolling operations
should be over excavated to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted engineered fill
Site Grading
No grading plans are available for the subject project It is our understanding that
grading plans will be developed at a later date by the turnkey developer of the proposed
housing project
Based on our observations of the site we anticipate that grading will be minimal
The following recommendations are presented to give general guidelines for the initial
design of the site grading and to allow the development of preliminary engineering cost
estimates
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grub
and spoil material should be disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for use in
landscaping Care should be exercised during clearing and stockpiling operations
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2643 00 Page 10
to avoid contamination of fill material with organic matter or other deleterious
materials
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operations should
be over excavated to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted engineered fill
In areas to receive fill the ground should be scarified to a depth of six 6 inches
brought to near optimum moisture content and recompacted to 90 of its
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
In cut areas or areas at existing grade we recommend that the soils be scarified
to a depth of twelve 12 inches brought to near optimum moisture content and
recompacted to 90 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
Fill Placement
Generally the on site soils should be suitable for use as engineered fill If
additional imported material is required the material should be nonexpansive
granular material such as crushed coral mudrock or cinder The material should
be less than 3 inch in size with a laboratory CBR swell value not exceeding 1 and
CBR value of 8 or more It should not contain large percentages of boulders
organic or other deleterious material should contain sufficient fines so that there
is a minimum of 20 passing the No 200 sieve and should be Capable of
forming a competent stable mass when compacted
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2643 00 Page 11
All off site borrow should be tested by the project geotechnical engineer prior to
its delivery to the site to determine whether it is suitable for its intended use
In general fills or backfills required for the project site grading should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding ten 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet below finish subgrade of trench backfills in
paved areas should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density as
established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site and in its vicinity
indicate that the upper portion of the site is most likely underlain by competent
coral algal limestone formation at relatively shallow depths Portions of the material
appear to be moderately to well cemented It is anticipated that hard excavation
and or ripping may be encountered for cuts and infrastructure installation
Generally hard excavation is considered to be techniques such as heavy ripping
hoerams or splitting Generally the excavation method should be left to the
contractor
Based on previous experience there is a tendency for the coral algal material to
generate large boulders and cobbles when ripped and excavated Some
contractors have mobilized portable crushers to job sites and used them to reduce
the coral material to smaller sizes thereby generating excellent fill and select
borrow materials On other projects belt trenchers have been used with good
results generating good fill and backfill material while excavating utility trenches
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2643 00 Page 12
In general design slope ratios of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may be
used for the design of cut and fill slopes in areas where the cut encounters
competent coral formation the design cut slopes may be steepened to a ratio of
one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
Cesspools
The formerly occupied portions of the project site were originally developed prior
to the installation of a sewerage system in the area There may be buried
abandoned cesspools on the project site The available county and state records
should be researched to determine their locations Any cesspool found on the site
should be opened cleaned of loose soft materials and backfilled with granular
material such as 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to two 2 feet
below finished subgrade Above this level the cesspool excavation should be filled
with approved backfill material compacted to 90 of its maximum dry density as
established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Foundation Support
An allowable bearing capacity of 3 000 p s f may be utilized for the design of
foundations bearing on the existing fill which has been proofrolled and recompacted in
accordance with the recommendations presented herein or on_ in situ coral algal
limestone This value may be increased by one third for temporary wind and seismic
loads The footings should be embedded a minimum of two 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent grade

Slabs On Grade
It is anticipated that the floors of proposed buildings on the project site will be
concrete slabs on grade
Building slabs and their cushions may be supported by the upper fill provided the
subgrade is proofrolled and recompacted as recommended above A 4 inch layer of 3B
Fine gravel or basaltic termite barrier should be provided below the slabs as cushion fill
Slab thickened edges should be embedded a minimum of eighteen 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent exterior grade
Laboratory testing of the near surface soils from Borings 6 and 7 indicates that
they generally have moderate swelling potential This moderate swelling potential could
cause distresses to slabs unless the slab subgrade is properly prepared to minimize the
potential effects of the swelling soils
In general the bottom of the slabs should be separated for these moderate
swelling soils by a minimum of two 2 feet of select nonexpansive material such as
subbase course or on site coral material This separation may be accomplished by either
removing the clayey swelling soils and replacing them with select material or by capping
the clayey soils with select material
Although it appears that the swelling potential of the remainder of the on site soils
appears to be low we recommend that the subgrade soils be kept moist prior to concrete
placement to minimize the potential of the subgrade soils from heaving due to subsequent
saturation This practice also aids concrete curing by reducing dehydration of the
concrete during its curing period
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2643 00 Page 14
In order to prevent future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor
coverings an impervious moisture membrane may be considered below the slabs
provided an inch of moist fine sand is placed above the plastic membrane to help
concrete curing
Infrastructure
It is anticipated that the infrastructure for the development will consist primarily of
water drainage and sewerage for the housing areas
It is anticipated that conventional construction methods may be utilized for the
installation of the proposed infrastructure
Pipes should be bedded and cushioned in a granular material such as 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation Proper backfill placement and compaction is
critical for soil supported pipes such as corrugated or spiral metal pipes for drainlines
Improper backfilling could result in structural distress and deflection of the arch of the
pipe causing settlements of the soil above the pipe It is our opinion that only granular
material should be used for backfill from the cushion level to one 1 foot above the top
of the pipe This opinion holds for all types of pipe
The trenches should be excavated and backfilled in accordance with Section 11
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction 1986 Trench Excavation and
Backfill
Pavement Design
Our preliminary laboratory testing and analyses for pavement design indicate that
the on site soils generally have good pavement support characteristics Testing and
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2643 00 Page 15
analyses should be conducted on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction The final pavement design will be contingent upon these analyses
For preliminary design purposes we recommend that the following section be
used
2 Inches Asphaltic Concrete
6 Inches Base Course
8 Inches Total Pavement Thickness
on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The recommended section considers medium traffic and should be revised to
include an additional one 1 inch of asphaltic concrete for heavy traffic areas e g main
collector roads and entrance roads
The recommended section also assumes that adequate drainage will be provided
for the paved areas Weepholes should be provided in drainage inlet structures in order
to relieve the water which may accumulate in the subgrade and cause pumping failure of
the pavement The weepholes should be installed at both base course and subbase
course elevations
The use of coral material for base course is not recommended Coral algal
limestone is formed by the growth of small organisms and plants which secrete a calcium
Carbonate framework This framework is porous and is composed of relatively fragile
honeycombed cells It has been our experience that this framework tends to break down
under repeated loading leading to rapid deterioration of the pavement section Crushed
basalt aggregate is preferable as base course material

Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were conducted in four 4 of the drilled 12A
16 and 17 In general the field testing indicated that the deeper subsoils within the
proposed housing development area and in the abandoned coral pit have rapid
permeability or infiltration characteristics
Based on our field percolation testing it is our opinion that a range of hydraulic
conductivity between about 100 and 800 feet per day may be used for design of drywells
in the proposed housing development area and for the abandoned coral pit These
values do not include any factor of safety
It should be recognized that the floors of the drywells and the pit will accumulate
sediment over time This accumulation of sediment will reduce the infiltration Capacity of
the drainage system Therefore allowance should be made for maintenance of the
system including removal of accumulated sediments
Geoenvironmental Concerns
As discussed above the southerly portion of the site was apparently used for
military industrial purposes This belief is based on statements by persons residing in the

parts
Conducting environmental assessments and finding identifying contamination are
neither part of our scope of work nor our area of expertise However we feel that it is
our responsibility to alert our clients of the potential of contamination possibly being
encountered at old industrial sites

or hydrocarbon products in the soil samples were noticed However this does not
preclude the potential existence of contamination existing at the site as a result of former
land use

Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to determine the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in
the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering
properties The results of these tests are summarized on Plates C 3 through C 6
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate C 1
Proctor ASTM D 1557 78 tests and California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883 tests
were performed on selected samples as an aid to Evaluating their embankment fill and
pavement support characteristics The results of these tests are summarized on Plate
C 2
