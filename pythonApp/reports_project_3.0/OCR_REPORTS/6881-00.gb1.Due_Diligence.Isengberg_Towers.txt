REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is located on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of 
the Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium 
derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated 
deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles 
of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess 
of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site generally is underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments 
and lagoonal deposits. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over these deposits 
for development of the area within the last century.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Based on our preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration, the project site is 
generally underlain by approximately 4 feet of stiff clayey silt fills placed over medium 
hard to hard coral.  The coral extended to a depth of about 29 feet below the existing 
ground surface; however, medium dense coralline detritus consisting of silty sand was 
encountered between depths of about 14 and 21 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Below the medium hard to hard coral, our boring generally encountered alluvial 
deposits to a depth of about 57 feet below the existing ground surface.  The alluvial 
deposits consist of very stiff silt and clay.  The alluvial deposits were underlain by 
medium dense coralline detritus extending to the maximum depth explored of about 
78 feet below the existing ground surface 
We encountered groundwater in the boring drilled at a depth of about 4 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. In general, we 
envision the groundwater levels will be near sea level, ranging from about -1 to +2 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) considering the proximity of the site to Honolulu Harbor and the 
Pacific Ocean. It should be noted that groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on 
tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and 
other factors.

Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the 2006 International Building Code (UBC) (code adopted by the 
City and County of Honolulu), the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and 
the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be evaluated. 
In general, the project site is underlain by medium hard to hard coral and medium 
dense silty sand to a depth of about 29 feet below the existing ground surface.  Very 
stiff silt and clay were encountered below the coral formation.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration and our 
preliminary engineering analyses, we believe the potential for soil liquefaction at the 
project site is low to negligible. However, this assessment is based on only one boring 
drilled at the project site. Additional engineering analyses should be conducted during

Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design 
The project will be designed in accordance with the requirements of the 2006 
International Building Code (IBC 2006). Based on the average penetration resistance 
(N-values) of the subsurface materials encountered and the geologic setting of the area, 
the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as a Stiff Soil 
Profile corresponding to a Site Class D based on IBC Table 1615.1.1, 2006 Edition. In 
addition, the maximum considered earthquake spectral response acceleration at short 
periods (Ss) and the 1.0-second period (S1) may be estimated as 0.61g and 0.18g, 
respectively. We highly recommend conducting site-specific shear wave velocity testing 
during detailed design to confirm the site class for seismic design considerations.

Foundation Considerations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated structural 
loads for the project, we believe a mat foundation bearing on the medium hard to hard 
coral or a drilled shaft foundation may be used to support the proposed structures.  It 
should be noted that groundwater was encountered near the top of the coral layer; 
therefore, significant dewatering will be required for construction of the mat foundation.  
Due to the potential difficulties associated with dewatering, such as disposal of effluent, 
potential adverse effect to neighboring properties, etc., it is our opinion that drilled shaft 
foundations to support the proposed structures is preferable. Detailed discussion 
pertaining to the preliminary design of the building foundations utilizing mat foundations 
or drilled shaft foundations is provided below.

Mat Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the building structure may be 
supported on a mat foundation bearing on the medium hard to hard coral 
encountered at about 4 feet below the existing ground surface. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 6,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
preliminary design of the mat foundation bearing on medium hard to hard coral. 
This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. A 
modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of 
deflection (pci) may be used in the preliminary design of the mat foundation. 
As previously mentioned, groundwater was encountered near the top of the coral 
layer. Due to the shallow groundwater and the potential presence of voids and/or 
cavities in the coral encountered at the site, we anticipated dewatering, involving 
large volumes of water, would be required during the construction of the mat 
foundation. 
Drilled Shaft Foundations 
To reduce the amount of dewatering for the proposed development, a deep 
foundation system consisting of drilled shaft foundations may be used to support 
the proposed structures. In general, we believe each column location for the 
building structure may be supported by one drilled shaft to reduce the size of the 
footing at each column location. Based on the anticipated structural loads (on the 
order of about 1,000 to 1,500 kips per column), the proposed building structure 
will likely be supported by a combination of 30, 42, and 48-inch diameter 
cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts, depending on the axial and lateral load 
demands on the foundations. The drilled shafts would be embedded in the coral, 
alluvial deposits, and coralline detritus. The drilled shafts would derive vertical 
support primarily from adhesion between the shaft and the coral, alluvial 
deposits, and coralline detritus. 
Based on our preliminary analyses, the 42 and 48-inch diameter drilled shafts 
with a depth on the order of about 60 to 80 feet would be required to develop 
allowable compressive loads of about 1,000 to 1,500 kips per drilled shaft to 
support the structural loads anticipated at the column locations of the proposed 
building structure. For the lighter structural loads (less than 1,000 kips per 
column), we recommend utilizing drilled shafts with a smaller diameter, such as 
30 inches, to support the columns with lighter structural loads.  The 30-inch 
diameter drilled shafts would need to extend to an average depth of about 
50 feet. 
It should be noted some difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during 
installation of the drilled shafts due to the shallow groundwater levels and the 
medium hard to hard coral present at the project site. Placement of concrete by

Mat Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the building structure may be 
supported on a mat foundation bearing on the medium hard to hard coral 
encountered at about 4 feet below the existing ground surface. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 6,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
preliminary design of the mat foundation bearing on medium hard to hard coral. 
This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. A 
modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of 
deflection (pci) may be used in the preliminary design of the mat foundation. 
Cavities and/or voids are encountered commonly in coral; therefore, cavities 
and/or voids may be present in the coral encountered at the project site.  Due to 
the anticipated high structural loads, a program of cavity probing and grouting 
within the mat foundation footprint is recommended to reduce the potential for 
loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities and/or voids 
below the mat foundation. In general, a probe hole should be drilled for every 
100 square feet of foundation area.  The probe holes should be at least 3 inches 
in diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned 
bottom of foundation elevation.  A low strength sand-cement grout mixture with a 
slump range of 6 to 9 inches should be utilized for the grouting operations.  As an 
alternative to sand-cement grout, Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) may 
also be used for the grouting operations. 
As previously mentioned, groundwater was encountered near the top of the coral 
layer.  Due to the shallow groundwater and the potential presence of voids and/or 
cavities in the coral encountered at the site, we anticipate dewatering, involving 
large volumes of water, would be required during construction of the mat 
foundation.

Dewatering 
Since groundwater was encountered at a relatively shallow depth, we anticipate 
dewatering for construction of a mat foundation and some underground utility 
installations. Special attention should be given to the effects of dewatering on the 
adjacent properties and beyond. 
Based on the generally permeable nature of the coral (due to the presence of 
cavities and/or voids) encountered at the site, the dewatering operations may involve 
large volumes of water.  The coral encountered at the site may have cavities and/or 
voids filled with loose coralline deposits.  Because the project is in a developed area 
and there is a potential presence of loose coralline deposits in the coral formation in the 
vicinity of the site, the dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that 
the dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence.  The resultant areal subsidence 
of the natural groundwater table could induce consolidation of the loose coralline 
deposits resulting in potential ground settlements, which could affect the adjacent 
existing structures. 
We recommend installing groundwater monitoring wells during the field 
exploration program for the performance of field permeability tests, collection of 
groundwater samples for National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) 
permit application, and groundwater level monitoring purposes. 
Excavation 
As mentioned previously, the site is underlain by coral at shallow depths.  The 
coral encountered at the site appears to be moderately weathered and medium hard to 
hard in nature.  It is anticipated that the majority of the coral may be excavated with 
heavy excavation equipment, such as the large backhoe excavator.  However, coral 
typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones. Therefore, we anticipate that 
difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas during construction. The 
contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of 
the coral resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for a mat foundation. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
field exploration and general knowledge of the coral present at the project site.  A 
detailed field exploration program should be conducted to evaluate the hardness of the 
coral encountered at the project site.

Field Exploration 
Our preliminary field exploration at the project site consisted of drilling and 
sampling one test boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 78 
feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate location of the boring drilled for 
the project is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Boring, Plates A-1.1 through A-1.4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.

Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 and C 136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil

of the grain size distribution of the soils are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 7012) were performed on selected 
core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the coral formation 
encountered at the project site. The unconfined compression test results are presented 
on Plate B-3. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plate B-4.
