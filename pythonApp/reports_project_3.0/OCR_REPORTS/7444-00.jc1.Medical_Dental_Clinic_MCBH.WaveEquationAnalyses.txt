PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is at Marine Corps Base Hawaii on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We 
understand it is planned to demolish the existing one to two story buildings and replace 
them with a new two-story medical/dental facility. The new two-story building will be 
supported on 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete piles. 
Based on the information provided, a total of 347 piles are specified, including 
35 test piles and 312 production piles. RFP (request for proposal) documents require the 
test and production piles to be cast at a length of 60 feet. In addition, each pile location will 
be predrilled to a depth of about 10 feet below the cut-off elevation prior to driving. 
One of the test piles will be selected to compressive static load test in accordance 
with ASTM D1143. The maximum testing load is about 400 kips. Results of the testing pile 
driving and the static load test will be utilized to determine production pile tip elevation. A 
refined wave equation will be conducted to determine production pile driving criteria.

Hammer and Driving System 
We understand that it is proposed to use a Pileco D30-32 single acting diesel hammer 
for the pile driving operations. Based on the pile driving equipment data forms provided, the 
Pileco D30-32 hammer has a ram weight of 6,615 pounds and a maximum stroke of 10.57 feet. 
The maximum rated energy of the Pileco D30-32 is 69,925 foot-pounds at the maximum pump 
setting. We envision the use of an efficiency of 72 percent for diesel hammers in a wave 
equation analysis. 
A pile cushion consisting of 9 inches of plywood cushion block is proposed for the 
pile driving operations. The plywood cushion block generally has a cross section area of 
approximately 560 square inches for the 16.5-inch octagonal concrete piles. Schematic 
details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and Driving Equipment Data Forms 
in the Appendix (Page 1). 
Pile Details 
We understand the piles will be cast from concrete with a minimum 28-day 
unconfined compressive strength of 6,000 pounds per square inch (psi) for the 16.5-inch 
octagonal concrete piles. The precast shop drawings propose using 170 kips of working 
force after all losses. The effective prestress after all losses is computed to be about 754 psi. 
Therefore, the maximum allowable stress (compression) in the 16.5-inch octagonal 
precast, prestressed concrete pile during pile driving is about 4,300 psi, while the maximum 
allowable stress (tension) is approximately 980 psi.

and the idealized subsurface conditions interpreted from the boring logs presented in the

Graphs showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during

Based on our analyses, the compressive stresses during the pile driving are less than 
the allowable compressive stress of 4,300 psi for the 16.5-inch octagonal concrete piles. 
The compression stresses developed during pile driving did not exceed the allowable 
compressive stresses for the full range of driving resistance at Pump Settings 1 through 4. 
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving estimated for the test piles and 
production piles also are less than the allowable tensile stress of 980 psi for the 16.5-inch 
square concrete piles for the full range of driving resistance at Pump Settings 1 through 4. 
Special attention should be given to the pump settings in order to control the energy 
during pile driving within the predrilling depth. The pile should be removed from the predrilled 
hole when hard driving resistance is encountered before achieving the predrilled tip 
elevation. The pile driving operation may be resumed after the hole is re-drilled. 
Based on the above results and the anticipated subsurface conditions, it is our 
professional opinion that the proposed pile driving equipment is capable of installing the 
16.5-inch octagonal precast and prestressed piles to the specified tip elevation without 
over-stressing the concrete piles.

                   ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS                      
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by         
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer.  The program is based on          
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving         
system, pile and soil under the hammer action.  Under certain conditions,        
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.          
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents        
normal situations.  In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the        
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order.  All of the input      
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time      
when the analysis is performed.  However, input data and therefore results       
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.                           
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.     
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or         
dynamic testing and measurements.  Estimates of bending or other local           
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the       
user.                                                                            
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,       
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity         
assessment of a driven pile.  Soil setup occurring after pile installation       
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those         
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation.  This    
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers.  The GRLWEAP user   
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow    
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil           
resistance and blow count during early restriking.                               
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values.  They MUST be reduced by    
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.      
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the           
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties      
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.
