SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills consisting of medium 
dense to very dense granular materials with varying amounts of silt at the Improve 
Wharf Refueling Capability project site. The surface fills extended down to depths of 
about 0.9 to 2.1 meters below the ground surface, which often corresponded, with the 
maximum depth explored. In some test pits, dune deposits, beach deposits, and 
lagoonal deposits consisting of loose to dense granular materials and very soft to soft 
sandy silts were encountered beneath the surface fills. The granular portions of the 
submerged fill, beach, and dune deposits are susceptible to soil liquefaction when 
subjected to a seismic event with a peak ground acceleration of 0.28g corresponding to 
the Maximum Considered Earthquake. Groundwater was encountered at an elevation of 
approximately +0.2 and +0.9 meters MSL at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on discussions with the project mechanical engineer, we understand the 
IFO fuel line will be designed to accommodate seismically-induced ground settlements. 
The JP-5 fuel line will not be designed to accommodate liquefaction settlement; 
however, the fuel line will not normally contain fuel and will be checked after each 
seismic event. Therefore, the above ground portion of the proposed fuel line may be 
supported on a shallow foundation system bearing on the re-compacted on-site soils 
and/or new compacted fills with the understanding that some footing settlement may 
occur due to soil liquefaction under seismic conditions. Based on this design concept, 
we recommend using shallow isolated spread footings to support the fuel line. An 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 120 kPa may be used for the design of shallow 
foundations bearing on the re-compacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills 
needed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing pressure is for supporting dead-
plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those 
caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, the bottom of footings should be 
embedded a minimum of 450 mm below the lowest adjacent finished grades. Bottom of 
footing excavations should be re-compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
Based on the relatively flat site topography, we anticipate relatively small cuts 
and fills will be needed to achieve the finished grades. Conventional earthwork and 
construction methods may be used for the proposed project grading. The text of this 
report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific design 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Improve Wharf Refueling Capability project (P-1705) at 
Naval Support Facility, Diego Garcia in the British Indian Ocean Territory (B.I.OT.). The 
approximate location of Diego Garcia is shown on the Area Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our literature review, field exploration, 
and laboratory testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from 
our analyses for the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the 
design of the fuel lines and associated appurtenances only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.  
It should be noted that our recommendations presented herein are based, in part, 
on a limited subsurface investigation program consisting of excavating test pits and 
performing relatively shallow field-testing. Ideally, a more comprehensive field 
investigation consisting of deeper borings would be preferred. However, due to the 
remote location of Diego Garcia and the associated costs with mobilizing a drill rig and 
crew to the project site, the Government determined a more comprehensive field 
investigation was not economically viable for this project. 
1.1

Diego Garcia. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project

The new wharf JP-5 fuel line will remain empty except when actively used. After

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Diego Garcia, part of the British Indian Ocean Territory (B.I.O.T.), is a horseshoe-
shaped atoll of the Chagos Archipelago located at 7 degrees south latitude, 
approximately 1,368 kilometers south of India in the Indian Ocean. The atoll is at the 
southern terminus of the Chagos-Maldive-Laccadive Ridge, a chain of submarine 
mountains that extends south from India to the Indo-Australian tectonic plate. Current 
theory suggests the ridge was built by a mantle hotspot that is presently active at 
Reunion Island. 
The origin of coral atolls was first postulated by Charles Darwin in 1842. His 
theory was that an atoll began as a fringing reef around a volcanic island. The island 
begins to subside tectonically at a slow rate so that the coral growth maintains a 
reef-line. As the island subsides and becomes smaller, the reef grows larger and 
becomes a barrier reef. When the island finally disappears below sea level, the upward-
growing reef encloses only a lagoon and becomes an atoll. 
This basic theory has been somewhat modified by other considerations such as 
eustatic sea level changes and cooled waters brought on by glacial activity. Generally, 
the ocean side of an atoll is built of larger pieces of rock that are ripped off of the hard 
reef and worked up during storms while the lagoon side is composed of finer material. 
The usual atoll island stands between 1.5 to 10 meters above the sea level and Diego 
Garcia is no exception, with a maximum elevation of about 9 meters above Mean Low 
Water.  
The subsurface conditions in a coral atoll vary substantially in both the horizontal 
and vertical dimensions. Much of the large-scale vertical variation is the result of 
worldwide glacio-eustatic sea level changes that occurred during the Pleistocene Epoch 
(Ice Age). One significant low stand of the sea was a stand of relatively long duration at 
about 18 meters below the present sea level. Throughout the world, many of the coral 
atolls were eroded to this low stand and the materials above this level are the result of 
rapid growth and accumulation of coralline deposits since the mid to late-Pleistocene

Existing Site Conditions 
The atoll is a roughly footprint shaped island of approximately 24 kilometers in 
length with a central lagoon of up to about 11 kilometers in width. The proposed fuel line 
alignment begins near the existing POL Tank Farm and terminates at the Deep Draft 
Wharf within the Naval Support Facility. In general, the majority of the fuel line 
alignment runs parallel with Portsmouth Road. We understand the proposed fuel line 
alignment is located roughly 25 meters southeast of the roadway centerline.  
Based on the topographic information provided, the ground elevations along the 
alignment range from approximately +1.7 meters MSL near the POL Tank Farm to 
about +3.4 meters MSL near the Deep Draft Wharf. In general, the alignment traverses 
primarily grassed or vegetated areas except at roadway crossings and the Deep Draft 
Wharf. At the time of our field exploration, heavy vegetation was observed to the 
southeast of the proposed fuel line alignment and generally consisted of large trees and 
shrubs.

have occurred since 1940.

2.3.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Liquefaction is a phenomenon during which loose, saturated, cohesionless soils 
(such as sands and non-plastic silts) temporarily lose their shear strengths due to 
the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress 
applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated 
sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil 
tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up 
of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is 
reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could 
equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to 
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil 
acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and 
if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Significant factors known to affect the liquefaction potential of soils are the 
characteristics of the materials such as grain size distribution and relative 
density; the plasticity of the fine-grained soils, the degree of saturation; the initial 
stresses (overburden pressures) acting on the soils; and the characteristics of 
the earthquakes such as the intensity and duration of ground shaking. The major 
factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of soil deposits are as follows: 
1. 
Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more 
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands. 
2. 
Initial Relative Density - Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to 
liquefaction. Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative 
density.

unlikely due to the thickness of the non-liquefiable layer above the submerged 
granular material, which translates to relatively uniform settlement for the above-
ground portions of the new fuel line during a seismic event. 
We have assumed the reef deposit (underlying the fill material) is weakly 
cemented and, therefore, may be considered non-liquefiable. There has been at 
least one previous geotechnical exploration in the area (C.W. Associates, Inc., 
1990) that encountered weak cementation in the granular reef deposits to 
support this assumption. However, if we assumed the reef deposit is liquefiable, 
the estimated seismically induced ground settlement due to soil liquefaction 
would be upwards of 250 mm along the new fuel line.

explored. Groundwater was encountered at an elevation of approximately +0.2 and

In some test pits, dune deposits, beach deposits, and lagoonal deposits 
consisting of loose to dense granular materials and very soft to soft sandy silts were 
encountered beneath the surface fills. The granular portions of the submerged fill, 
beach, and dune deposits are susceptible to soil liquefaction when subjected to a 
seismic event with a peak ground acceleration of 0.28g corresponding to the Maximum 
Considered Earthquake.  
Based on discussions with the project mechanical engineer, we understand the 
IFO fuel line will be designed to accommodate seismically-induced settlements. The 
JP-5 fuel line will not be designed to accommodate liquefaction settlement; however, 
the fuel line will not normally contain fuel and will be checked after a seismic event. 
Therefore, the above ground portion of the proposed fuel line may be supported 
on a shallow foundation system bearing on the re-compacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills with the understanding that some footing settlement may occur due to 
soil liquefaction under seismic conditions. Based on the grading concept, we 
recommend using shallow isolated spread footings to support the fuel line. Footings 
should be embedded a minimum of 450 mm below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
Bottom of footing excavations should be re-compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction.

3.1 
Shallow Foundations 
In general, we understand the proposed new fuel line will be partially above 
grade and thus may be supported on a shallow foundation system with the 
understanding that some footing settlement may occur due to soil liquefaction under 
seismic conditions. Based on this design philosophy and the near-surface soil 
conditions anticipated at the project site, shallow isolated spread footings may be used 
to support the above-ground portion of the fuel line.  
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 120 kilopascals (kPa) may be used for the 
design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in-situ coralline materials or 
new compacted fills. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
For footings bearing on the in-situ granular materials, the bottom of footing 
excavations should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction to 
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing 
steel and/or concrete. Relative compaction, in this report, refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture content, in this report, 
is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Soft and/or loose materials (as determined by a Geotechnical QC Specialist) 
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be over-excavated to 
expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with 
on-site coarse-grained soils (as defined per ASTM D2487) compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction, or the footing bottom may be extended down to bear on 
the underlying competent materials.  
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 450 mm 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades. Footings adjacent to planned (or existing) 
retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining 
wall foundations, or the planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the 
additional structural loads.

Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. 
Generally, a lower resistivity results in a greater potential for corrosion of buried metallic 
objects. Conversely, a higher resistivity means the soil is less likely to contribute to 
corrosion of metallic objects. Based on the resistivity values of the in-situ soils 
encountered, the subsurface materials may be classified as mildly corrosive to 
moderately corrosive for buried metallic structures. A corrosion engineer should be 
consulted for detailed recommendations on corrosion protection of metallic 
substructures, if appropriate. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the test 
results, the in-situ soils may be considered “non-corrosive” to underground concrete 
pipelines and structures; therefore, the typical Type I or Type II cement and a minimum 
concrete cover of 75 mm over reinforcing steel should be adequate for concrete 
substructures in contact with the on-site soils.

upon information obtained from the test pits from our limited site-specific field

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of excavating and sampling seven test 
pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths between about 
1.7 and 2.4 meters below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit 
locations are shown on the Overall Site Plan (Plate 3) and Site Plans (Plates 4.1 
through 4.5). An Airman AX 40 track-mounted excavator was used to excavate the test 
pits. 
Our engineer observed the excavation operations on a near-continuous basis 
and classified the materials encountered in the test pits by visual and textural 
examination in the field. These classifications were further refined by visual observation 
and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the 
Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits (Plates A-1 through A-7). 
In order to evaluate the consistency or relative density of the near-surface soils, 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) tests were conducted at the test pit locations. The 
procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with ASTM D6951, 
Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow 
Pavement Applications. The procedures used in our data processing generally were 
derived from the publication entitled “Application of Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) 
for determination of the engineering parameters of sandy soils” prepared by 
S.D. Mohammadi, et al. The DCP field data is presented in Appendix C and also is 
presented graphically on the Logs of Test Pits in Appendix A.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) determinations were performed on selected soil 
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties. The test results 
are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test result is 
summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depth, and graphic 
presentation of the test result is provided on Plate B-1. 
Ten Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
samples obtained from the test pits to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the near-
surface soils and to aid in soil classification. The fines content of the samples tested 
(percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve) are indicated on the Logs of Test Pits at the 
appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided 
on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils obtained from the test pits to evaluate the dry density 
and moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through 
B-6. 
Six laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils obtained from the test pits to evaluate the quality of 
the materials tested for use as fills. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through 
B-12. 
Three sets of pH (ASTM G51) and minimum resistivity (ASTM G57) tests were 
performed by our office on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. In 
addition, three sets of chloride (EPA 300.0) and sulfate (EPA 300.0) content tests were 
performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from our 
field exploration. The test results are presented on Plate B-13.
