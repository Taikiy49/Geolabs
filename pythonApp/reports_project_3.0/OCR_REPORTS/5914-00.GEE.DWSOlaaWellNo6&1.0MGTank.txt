2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist of
both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the
ocean Ava lava typically is characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are encountered commonly in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state and they represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and
pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock formation and can transmit
water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions where cavities clinker
layers and large fractures are present In general water is transmitted through cavities
along clinker layers through the porous rock matrix and along joints fractures and
inter flow contacts The permeability of the cavities and clinker layers is high therefore
they serve as the primary water transmission features

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that
only sensitive instruments can detect the tremors However some of the earthquakes
are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of
Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent are related
to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes
with increased distance from the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii The Island of

2 5 Site Specific Response Spectrum Analysis
As requested we performed a seismic hazard analysis and site response
evaluation with respect to seismic design considerations for the proposed project The
purpose of our seismic hazard analysis and site response evaluation is to establish
appropriate design elastic response spectra taking into consideration the proximity of
the site to seismic sources regional seismicity and site specific subsurface conditions
The structural engineer utilizes a response spectrum to perform an analysis of the
structure given the dynamic loading conditions and the ground motions at the
foundation elevations
The response spectrum is a graphical representation relating to the maximum
response of a single degree of freedom damped elastic system with different
fundamental periods to dynamic forces The anticipated earthquake ground motions and

Design Ground Motion
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC the Island of Hawaii is within
Seismic Zone 4 with an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 40g
Alternatively site specific design ground motions may be developed based on a
site specific probabilistic seismic hazard analysis PSHA or determined based on
seismic hazard maps In any case the site specific design ground motions should
be based on a 10 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years This level of
seismic hazard is equivalent to a return period of approximately 475 years or an
annual probability of occurrence of 0 002107 events per year
As previously mentioned the subsurface conditions generally consist of surface fills
and volcanic ash underlain by interbedded layers of dense basalt formation and a a
clinker materials Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field
exploration the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as
being a Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock site corresponding to an Sc soil profile
type based on the Uniform Building Code Table No 16 J 1997 Edition
Based on our review of the site specific ground conditions and the specified annual
probability of occurrence we believe the design ground motions may be adequately
determined based on the seismic hazard maps produced by the United States
Geological Survey Seismic Hazard Mapping Project 1996 Based on the seismic
hazard maps for 10 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years we believe a
design peak ground acceleration of 0 80g may be adopted as the basis of ground
motions at the project site

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area voids and or cavities
appear to be present in the basaltic lava flows To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below foundations we
recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the reservoir tank
foundations and tank floor during construction Foundation probing and grouting may be
omitted for other minor structures planned due to the light structural loads and reduced
risk of damage due to cavity collapse
For estimating purposes we recommend drilling probe holes at 10 foot on
centers for the perimeter wall foundation In addition probe holes should be drilled at
each isolated spread footing location Probe holes should also be drilled within the tank
floor area at about 10 foot on center starting at the center of the tank and radiating out
at 10 foot radial increments The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter
and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation
If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM with a
slump of about 6 to 9 inches The CLSM should be injected by pumping at low to
moderate pressures As an alternate to CLSM sand cement grout with a slump of about
6 to 9 inches may also be used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding In
addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are completed The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag
