SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered residual soils and alluvial deposits 
underlain by clinker materials and basalt formation. The thickness of the residual soils and 
alluvial deposits ranged from about 0.5 to 12 feet at the boring locations. The residual soils 
and alluvial deposits generally consisted of medium dense to dense silty sands, silty gravel, 
and sandy gravel and medium stiff to hard sandy silts. The residual soils and alluvial 
deposits generally were underlain by clinkers and basalt formation extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 13.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The clinker 
materials generally consisted of medium dense to very dense sandy gravel, silty gravel, and 
gravelly cobbles. 
The basalt formation at the project site generally ranged from medium hard to very 
hard and slightly weathered to unweathered. The basalt formation was encountered from 
depths of about 0.5 to 9.5 feet below the ground surface and extended down to the 
maximum depth explored of about 13.5 feet below the ground surface. Based on the field 
exploration conducted, it appears that the depth to basalt formation varies across the project 
site. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new buildings planned. As an 
alternative, foundations for support of the new building structures planned also may consist 
of thickened-edge slab footings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on the recompacted on-site materials or basalt 
formation. Bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the 
lowest adjacent exterior grade. 
Due to the presence of cobbles and boulders in the clinker layers and basalt 
formation at relatively shallow depths, we envision the traditional driven pile system may 
encounter difficult driving conditions in some areas. As a result, we recommend 
supporting the solar panel arrays on a ballasted ground mounting system. Alternatively, 
we recommend considering a hybrid ballasted ground mounting system for support of the 
solar panel arrays. The hybrid ballasted ground mounting system consists of anchoring 
concrete blocks to the ground using a ground anchor system.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Paeahu Solar Project located near Wailea on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. 
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, site grading, and pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
Project Considerations 
Paeahu Solar LLC is proposing to build and operate the Paeahu Solar Project 
(Project) located near Wailea on the island of Maui. The Project will consist of a 
15-megawatt solar photovoltaic system coupled with a 60-megawatt-hour battery energy 
storage system (BESS). The Project would consist of a solar photovoltaic system, a 
network of electrical collector lines and inverters, a BESS, a Project collector substation, 
an overhead generator-tie line, a temporary construction staging/laydown area, and

 SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis are the subsurface materials encountered 
at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, and the 
variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following 
subsections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site, the surface

Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major shield volcanoes, the older as West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the younger as East Maui, or Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). The 
broad gently sloping low elevation plain, or Isthmus, in between the two volcanoes was 
formed by the younger Haleakala lavas banking against the older West Maui volcano. 
West Maui is classified as an extinct volcano while Haleakala is regarded as dormant, 
having erupted within the last several hundred years. The Island of Maui is actually part 
of a larger volcanic massif comprising about seven volcanoes and the Islands of Maui, 
Kahoolawe, Lanai and Molokai are separated by relatively shallow sea depths. 
The West Maui Mountain volcanic deposits are divided into three parts from oldest 
to youngest and are identified as the Wailuku, Honolua, and Lahaina Volcanic Series. 
Each volcanic series contains distinct lava types and distribution on West Maui. Similarly, 
Haleakala volcanic deposits are divided into three parts from oldest to youngest and are 
identified as the Honomanu, Kula, and Hana Volcanic Series. 
The dominant erosional processes acting on the volcanic island mass is the deep 
incision of streams and subsequent detachment of soil and rock masses from the 
steepened valley bounding walls (mass wasting process). The detached soil and rock 
materials are transported down slope toward the valley axes primarily by gravity as 
colluvium or colluvial deposits. Once these eroded materials reach streams at the central 
portion of a valley, alluvial transport processes become dominant, and the sediments are 
further re-worked, transported and deposited as alluvium or alluvial deposits.

The project site is along the lower elevation foot of the southwest facing slopes of 
Haleakala near the town of Wailea. The bulk of the Haleakala Shield Volcano was built

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (current building code in 
effect), the project site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design 
considerations will need to be addressed. The following sections provide discussions on 
the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic design, and the potential for liquefaction at the 
project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic activity; 
therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of 
magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they 
can be detected only by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes 
are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) 
are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground 
shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of 
Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); 
however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in the 
County of Maui. Due to the relatively short period of documented earthquake 
monitoring in the State of Hawaii, information pertaining to earthquakes on the Island 
of Maui may not be complete. However, the following are two examples of reported 
earthquakes within the County of Maui. A Magnitude 6.8 earthquake occurred on the 
Island of Maui in 1938, and the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 was estimated at

2.4.2 Liquefaction 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of 
strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose 
saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the 
soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. 
Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the 
overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing 
it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility 
sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, will 
result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands 
and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by residual 
soils and alluvial deposits, on the order of about 0.5 to 12 feet thick, consisting of medium 
dense to dense silty sands, silty gravel, and sandy gravel and medium stiff to hard sandy 
silts. The residual soils and alluvial deposits generally were underlain by clinkers and 
basalt formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 13.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The clinker materials generally consisted of medium dense to 
hard sandy gravel, silty gravel, and gravelly cobbles. The basalt formation was 
encountered from depths of about 0.5 to 9.5 feet below the ground surface and generally 
ranged from medium hard to very hard and slightly weathered to unweathered. We did 
not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings and excavated test pits at the time of our 
field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new equipment and 
structures planned. As an alternative, thickened-edge slab footings also may be 
considered for support of the new equipment and structures planned.  
Due to the presence of cobbles and boulders in the clinker layers and basalt 
formation at relatively shallow depths, we envision that the traditional driven pile system 
may encounter difficult driving conditions in some areas. As a result, we recommend 
supporting the solar panel arrays on a ballasted ground mounting system. Alternatively, 
we recommend considering a hybrid ballasted ground mounting system for support of the 
solar panel arrays.

easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the utility trench (or retaining wall). Alternatively, the footings 
should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines or bottom of the 
retaining wall.

Solar Array Foundations 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the solar panel arrays will 
be supported on steel poles consisting of C-Pile or H-Pile sections. Details of the steel 
poles and structural loading were not provided at the time this report was prepared. 
In general, we believe that pile foundations consisting of C-Pile or H-Pile sections 
driven into the ground may be used to support the solar panel arrays for only a portion of 
the project site. As mentioned above, basalt formation was encountered at depths as 
shallow as 0.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, cobbles and boulders 
likely are present in the clinker layers. Therefore, we envision the traditional driven pile 
system likely will encounter difficult driving conditions. As a result, we recommend 
supporting the solar panel arrays on a ballasted ground mounting system. 
In general, ballasted ground mounting system consists of a series of pre-cast or 
cast-in-place concrete blocks set on-grade to serve as a foundation for the solar panel 
arrays. The ballasted ground mounting system should be sized appropriately to develop 
the required bearing, uplift, and lateral load resistance to resist loads induced by the solar 
panel arrays. 
Based on the existing ground surface conditions, we believe there is potential for 
erosion of the near-surface residual soils around the on-grade concrete blocks that could 
result in a loss of foundation stability. Therefore, we recommend implementing a hybrid 
ballasted ground mounting system by anchoring the concrete blocks to the ground using 
a ground anchor system.

than 6 inches), drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The ground anchor

Settlements of the ground anchors will result primarily from elastic compression of

3.4.3 Ground Anchor Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the compressive, uplift, and lateral load capacities of the 
ground anchors are highly dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting

load tests on anchors is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the anchors to the depths required. 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grout injection. 
To confirm the production anchor length and to evaluate the degree 
of long-term settlements of the anchors embedded in the on-site

Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Items of corrosion potential that are addressed in the subsequent 
subsections include the following:

Corrosivity Laboratory Testing

3.9.1 Field Resistivity Survey 
A total of 64 field resistivity tests were conducted at a maximum electrode spacing of 
about 20 feet at eight locations, designated as R-1 through R-8, using the in-situ 
Wenner 4-Pin Method and an AEMC Model 4610 resistance meter manufactured by 
AEMC Instruments. Locations of the field resistivity tests are presented on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the average 
resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the underlying soil mass. The field resistivity 
surveys were used to evaluate the average resistivity values of the soil mass at the 
approximate survey locations. Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented in 
Appendix D. It should be noted that the field resistivity survey may be influenced by 
temperature, weather conditions, soil moisture, existing underground utilities and 
other factors.

for protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete structures is 
dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the soil. In 
general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered "non 
corrosive" to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the values of the chloride and sulfate content tested on the in-situ 
near-surface soils, we believe that either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be 
used for the concrete in contact with the ground planned.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
25 test borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 25, extending to depths of about 
8.5 to 13.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  In addition, six test pits, designated 
as TP-1 through TP-6, were excavated to depths of about 1 to 5.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface to supplement the boring data. The approximate boring and test pit 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The test 
pits were excavated using a CAT 303.5e Excavator.  
Our geologist and engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings and 
test pits by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM 
D2488, Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the 
drilling operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were 
further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM 
D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-25, and the Logs of Test Pits, Plates B-26 through B-31. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs 
of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Nine Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Fifteen Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012) were performed on 
selected rock core samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the basalt 
rock formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-5 and B-6. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the relationship between the moisture 
content and the dry density of the near-surface soils as fill materials.  The test results are 
presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Four sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH Value (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-11.

Field Resistivity Surveys 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on January 20, 2020 for the field resistivity survey.  Sixty-four (64) field resistivity 
surveys were conducted at eight locations, designated as R-1 through R-8, using the 
in-situ Wenner 4-Pin Method and an AEMC Model 4610 resistance meter manufactured 
by AEMC Instruments. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to 
evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values 
of the soil mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, was 
assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at 
the approximate survey locations shown on the site plan. The average soil resistance 
along the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes 
aligned at equal spacing of generally 2.5, 5, 10, and 20 feet. The depth of penetration of 
a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (AEMC Model 4610) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the 
soil resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity 
(in ohm-cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be 
computed. The evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented 
on Plates D-1 through D-4. In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing 
at the test locations are presented in the graph on Plate D-5. At the time of resistivity 
surveying, the temperature was about 75º to 80º F. The existing ground surface was dry 
to damp. It should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature, 
weather conditions, existing underground utilities and other factors.
