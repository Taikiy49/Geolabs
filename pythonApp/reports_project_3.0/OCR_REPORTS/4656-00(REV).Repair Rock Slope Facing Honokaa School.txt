PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to repair an existing rock facing located at the Honokaa
High Intermediate School campus football field in the Honokaa area on the Island of
Hawaii The existing slope along the southern and eastern sides of the football field is
covered by a rock facing Rocks from this facing began to loosen and fall off throughout
the past five years Some rocks at the top of the wall used as a seat have been
replaced and regrouted Planned improvements to the slope area include removal of
rocks from the slopes and replacement with a new rock or gunite slope facing retaining
wall or an equivalent stabilization system surface drainage improvements correction of
drainage and erosion deficiencies around dugouts and repair of the top portion of the
wall
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical reconnaissance survey was to obtain an overview
of the existing slope surface conditions at the site to evaluate the feasibility for slope
stabilization To accomplish this we conducted an exploration program consisting of the
following tasks and work efforts

SITE CONDITIONS OBSERVATIONS
The project site is located at the Honokaa High and Intermediate School in the
Honokaa area of the Island of Hawaii The football field is located on a flat area on the
northern side of the campus The field level is approximately 10 to 20 feet lower than the
classroom buildings to the south and east The grade difference between the field and
classrooms is provided by a steep slope currenily at a ratio of about one horizontal io
one vertical 1H 1V The southeastern portion of the steep slope is covered with
grouted rock facing However much of the lower portions of the rock facing was
missing The absence of the lower portions of the rock facing appears to have
destabilized the remainder of the facing
A grandstand with a dugout on either side is present along the southern portion
of the slope The grandstand has concrete bleachers and a metal roof The eastern
dugout is located at the toe of the steep slope The rock facing behind the dugout is
raveling and depositing loose rocks behind the dugout It appears that a near vertical
cut was made into the slope to constrict the western dugout Material from the slope
has ravelled and piled up against the dugout rear wall
At the eastern end of the slope the slope height is generally 5 to 8 feet and the
condition of the rock facing is fair to good i e the rocks and mortar are still in place
The northern portion of the slope is unlined with volcanic ash with cobbles and
gravel visible in the slope exposure Signs of surface erosion were visible in several
areas along the slope
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our site observations indicate that the steep slope is composed of stiff volcanic
ash soils with gravel and cobbles It appears that the slope has performed well over the
years aS our visual observations did not disclose obvious signs of global instability

GRP Slope Protection
Grouted Rubble Paving GRP may be placed on the existing slope face The
GRP should have a minimum thickness of 12 inches with a 24 inch deep and 24 inch
wide socket at the bottom and at mid slope The top of the GRP should be embedded
a minimum of 24 inches below the adjacent finished grade Schematic sections
illustrating the GRP facing are shown on the attached Typical Sections Plates 3 4 and
5
Portions of the rock facing at the eastern end of the site are in relatively good
condition In order to reduce costs it may be possible to rehabilitate the existing facing
by installing a socket footing at the bottom of the facing and replacing the mortar in
deteriorated areas
The toe of the slope was excavated to install the western dugout This
excavation has reduced the stability of the slope and facing by oversteepening the
slope and removing the toe support Ideally the dugout should be relocated away from
the toe of slope to allow reconstruction of the slope and facing to match the existing
conditions If this is not practical a retaining wall may be constructed behind the
dugout to retain the cut slope Design parameters for the retaining wall design are
presented in the following section Due to the steep slope and ash soil conditions we
anticipate that a large wall footing would be required to retain the slope
Retaining Walls
Wall Foundations
Based on our visual observations we recommend that a shallow foundation
system consisting of continuous footings be used to support the retaining wall
structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot
psf may be used for the design of the retaining wall footings bearing on the
natural ground This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by
temporary wind and or seismic forces Wall footings should have a minimum
width of 18 inches
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement
of reinforcing steel and or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures

Lateral Earth Pressures
Retaining structures should be designed to resist lateral earth pressures due to the
adjacent soils and surcharge effects The recommended lateral earth pressures
for design of retaining structures expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of
pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf are presented below
Walls unrestrained at top active condition 50 pcf horizontal
50 pcf vertical
The values provided above assume that granular materials less than 3 inches in
maximum dimension will be used to backfill behind the retaining structures It is
assumed that the backfill behind retaining structures will be compacted to
between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over compaction of the
retaining structure backfill should be avoided In general an active condition
may be used for gravity walls and retaining structures that are free to deflect by
as much as 0 5 percent of the wall height If the tops of walls are not free io
deflect beyond this degree or are restrained the retaining structures should be
designed for the at rest condition These lateral earth pressures do not include
hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the
structures
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the height of the retaining structures should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the
loaded side of the structure a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure
equal to 35 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height
of the wall which is free to deflect cantilever may be used in design For
retaining structures that are restrained a rectangular distribution equal to
50 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the
structures may be used for design Additional analyses during design may be
needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the
base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain
may be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
