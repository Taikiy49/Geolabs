PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at the northern embankment of Hana Highway and is 
approximately 50 feet east of the intersection with West Kuiaha Road in the Haiku area on 
the Island of Maui, Hawaii. On March 8, 2021, a severe rainstorm occurred on the Island 
of Maui, which caused the Kaupakalua Dam to over crest and resulted in flooding 
downslope of the dam. The stormwater encountered the 6 feet by 4 feet concrete box 
culvert traversing under the Hana Highway and was soon overwhelmed. The water then 
flowed over the Hana Highway and down the northern roadway embankment and eroded 
the roadway embankment. The rough estimates of the erosion area were approximately 
50 feet long, 20 feet wide by 20 feet deep. 
The original design of the project consisted of the installation of a reinforced soil 
slope. We understand that it is desired to develop an alternative design consisting of soil 
nails and shotcrete facing.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at the northern embankment of Hana Highway and is 
approximately 50 feet east of the intersection with West Kuiaha Road in the Haiku area on 
the Island of Maui, Hawaii. Due to a recent severe rainstorm on March 8, 2021, a portion of 
the northern embankment, approximately 50 feet long, 20 feet wide by 20 feet deep, was 
eroded exposing steep slopes with gradients ranging from near vertical to approximately one 
horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V). Based on our field observation, several waterlines were 
exposed dangling on the surface of the eroded slope with temporary support ties to the 
guardrail posts along the Hana Highway. Numerous tree roots were also observed in the 
eroded area.  It appeared that the existing headwall of the 6 feet by 4 feet box culvert and 
the concrete drainage ditch at the bottom of the eroded slope were undamaged. Numerous 
cobbles and boulders were observed near the bottom of the eroded slope. 
The Hana Highway pavement in the vicinity of the project site is generally flat and in 
good condition. Based on the Topographic Survey Map dated March 10, 2022, the existing 
ground surface in the project site of slope repairs ranges from approximate elevation of +301 
to +281 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site are presented in our Geotechnical 
Engineering Exploration Report entitled “Hana Highway Emergency Slope Repairs, Vicinity 
of West Kuiaha Road Intersection, Haiku, Maui, Hawaii” dated August 12, 2022. The 
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the borings encountered a thin surface fill layer, about 1.5 and 2 feet thick, 
underlain by residual soils and saprolite extending to the maximum depth drilled of 51.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The surface fills encountered generally consisted of 6 to 
11 inches of asphaltic concrete pavement underlain by about 12 to 18 inches of medium 
dense sandy gravel and gravel. The residual soils and saprolite generally consisted of soft to 
hard sandy silt with occasional cobbles and boulders. Groundwater levels were encountered 
in the borings at depths of about 34 and 36 feet below the existing ground surface at the time 
of our field exploration. The groundwater levels measured generally correspond to Elevations 
of about +265 and +266 feet MSL. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are 
subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, 
and other factors.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the available information and the stability analyses performed, we 
recommend using a soil nail slope system as an alternative design for the proposed 
emergency slope repair. Design of the soil nailed slope system considers both the internal 
and external stability of the reinforced mass. The design of the internal stability includes 
establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted reinforcing bars. The 
external stability includes the slope stability of the reinforced mass. 
Items pertaining to the soil nailed slope system are addressed in the subsequent 
subsections and include the following: 
 Slope Preparation 
 Soil Nailed Slope 
 Soil Nail Installation 
 Soil Nail Testing 
 Shotcrete Facing 
 Drainage 
Construction of permanent soil nailed slope system should be performed by a 
specialty contractor experienced in the construction of soil nailed slope systems. Due to the

Slope Preparation 
At the on-set of construction, surface vegetation, roots, loose boulders, and debris 
should be removed from the face of the eroded slope. Care should be taken in removing 
materials so that the stability of the existing slope and Hana Highway is not compromised. In 
addition, there are several waterlines exposed dangling from and along the face of the eroded 
slope; the waterlines should be properly identified and protected from damage during the 
installation of the soil nailed slope for the proposed emergency repair. 
Soil Nailed Slope 
A soil nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted 
into drilled holes to stabilize the eroded slope. A shotcrete facing is generally applied to the 
face of the reinforced slope. The reinforcing bars provide additional tensile and shear 
strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the slope. The reinforced soil mass behaves 
in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing.  The face of the soil nailed 
slope system will have a finished slope of about 0.25H:1V to 1.5H:1V inclination with slope 
heights up to about 20 feet.  
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 6.0-inch diameter hole with an 
inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The soil nail bar should consist of 
ASTM A615 Grade 75 solid threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter of 1.125-inch. We 
anticipate that the subsoil may be very corrosive. Therefore, we recommend using a triple 
corrosion protection system for the nails. The epoxy coated bars should be surrounded by 
neat cement grout or sand-cement mixture with a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 
4,000 pounds per square inch (psi). 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using a design nail length of 12 feet 
extending into the stiff silty soils encountered in our field exploration. The soil nails should be 
spaced at 8 feet on-center horizontally and 6 to 8 feet on-center vertically in a grid pattern. 
The first row of nails should be installed about 2 to 3 feet below the top of the slope. A soil 
nail numbering plan is presented on Plate 3. A typical soil nailed slope section is presented 
on Plate 4. 
Based on the above design parameters, the soil nailed slope system should have a 
factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external stability for the static 
loading condition considering a surcharge load of 250 pounds per square foot (psf) applied 
on the roadway travel lanes.

Soil Nail Installation 
Construction of permanent soil nails should be performed by a specialty contractor 
experienced in the construction of soil nails. Due to the specialized nature of the soil nail 
construction, a Geolabs representative should be present to observe the geotechnical 
aspects of the soil nail construction. Potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered 
during the installation of the soil nails due to the presence of cobbles and boulders within the 
residual soils and saprolite anticipated at the project site. Utilizing a temporary casing may 
be required to maintain an open hole for the soil nail installation. In addition, due to potential 
variations of the residual soils and saprolite within the subsurface, variable drilling conditions 
should be anticipated. The contractor should fully investigate the project site and make 
his/her own interpretation as to the character of the materials to be encountered. The 
contractor should provide sufficient contingency taking into account potential caving-in, hard 
drilling, and difficult access conditions.  
Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nailed slope, we 
recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification test on a sacrificial soil 
nail at the project site. In addition, proof tests should be performed on at least 10 percent of 
the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load for 
the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent of the design 
load. In addition, the load at every loading increment should be held for at least 10 minutes, 
and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes. The proof tests should consist of 
subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held 
for at least 10 minutes (until stable). The proof test nails may be incorporated into the 
permanent soil nail slope, provided they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests 
on the soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nailed slope system. 
Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout tests and creep tests in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative.  
Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete be 
constructed for inspection. A bottom key with a minimum width of 12 inches and a minimum 
depth of 24 inches below the adjacent finished grade should be provided for the shotcrete 
facing.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 32 to 
51.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig with 
continuous flight augers and PQ coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock cores were described in general accordance with 
the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock 
Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative 
Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock 
Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that

Core Recovery may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Core Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as Core 
Recovery = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound rock material is recovered from 
a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the 
Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative 
quality of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
