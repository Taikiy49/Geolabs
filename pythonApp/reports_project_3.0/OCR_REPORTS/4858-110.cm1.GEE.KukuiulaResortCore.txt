This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Kukuiula Resort Core in the Kukuiula Resort Community
development in Koloa on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering

engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of
the mass grading one and two story resort facility structure foundations retaining walls
and foundations pavements utilities excavation and fill placement only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about
5 to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2 million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion Following the
cessation of this main volcano shield building phase about 17 million years ago there
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of
the Palikea Formation
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in
north south trends at various locales Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials volcanic ash and cinders which
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project site
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai
The project site is situated on ground underlain by hard basaltic rock lava flows
of the Koloa Volcanic Series In some places generally thin surficial soils consisting of
silty and clayey residual and alluvial materials may mantle the underlying rock
formation The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa area appear to be

representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type which spread and ponded as it
approached the ocean from inland areas Pahoehoe lavas are typically characterized
by a smoother billowy surface and internal structure of vesicular porous rock with
limited clinker materials In contrast a a lavas are typically characterized by porous
rough and irregular flow surfaces resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments
including cobbles and boulders A a lavas typically contain thick sequences of clinker
rock rubble which bound a massive rock internal flow core
Buried cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows especially the
pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and localized blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains quickly from the
feeding conduit of a cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend
for large distances longitudinally along the flow Lava tubes may also consist of a
network of branching and merging segments that may change directional course over
relatively short distances Lava tubes may extend from far inland all the way to the
ocean since they serve as the primary lava conduit for advancing flows Blisters and
pockets are more isolated elliptical shaped features which are generally smaller in
horizontal and vertical extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features
Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work
has brought the area to its present form

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials
Prior to filling the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be
thoroughly moistened to close cracks or prepared again as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted engineered fill Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density

Boulder Fill
For the purposes of discussion on this project boulders are defined as rock
fragments larger than 12 inches in largest dimension We believe that a significant
amount of boulders may be generated from rock cuts in the golf course grading

incorporated into the fill Guidelines for use of boulders in the fills are presented

methods if performed in accordance with the following guidelines
Prior to placement of boulders the subgrade soils should be prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section
of this report
1 Boulders must not be nested together They should be placed in
such a manner that compaction equipment would be able to
operate around them
2 Based on the anticipated utility inverts boulders should not be
utilized within 5 feet from finished grades beneath structural areas
and within 10 feet from finished grades beneath roadways
3 Boulders should not be utilized within 6 feet from slope faces
4 No boulder larger than 3 feet in its largest dimension should be
placed in the fill It may be necessary to reduce the size of larger
boulders or dispose of them off site
5 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the
proposed utility alignments to prevent difficulty in the excavation of
the utility trenches at a later date

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed immediately around the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
in lieu of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall Unless
covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of impervious
material such as the on site silty clay material to reduce water infiltration behind
the walls

In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that granular bedding
