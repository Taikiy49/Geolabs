INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new Rolph Residence at 204 D Hao Street in the Aina Haina
area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
The project area is located on the southeastern flank of Koolau and is composed
of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These
rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with
interbedded thin clinker layers For the last 1 to 2 million years in situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred forming a mantle of residual soils
which grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty
GEOLABS HAWAII

material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to
basaltic rock formation with depth Grading operations for the housing developments in
the area have brought the site to its present form

Foundations
The proposed new residence will consist of a two story residential structure
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used
to support the proposed structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds
per square foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on the medium stiff
to stiff near surface soils encountered in the borings and or on 6 inches of select borrow
fill This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general perimeter footings for the residential structure should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade and interior footings
should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend a minimum of 5 feet below the
lowest adjacent grade In addition a minimum set back distance of 8 feet measured
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope should be
maintained for footings on sloping ground Footings oriented parallel to the direction of
the slope should be constructed in stepped footings
If foundations are located next to utility trenches easements or other below grade
structures they should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we estimate
total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of inch

In general the footing subgrade should be recompacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction For the retaining wall footing which will be embedded a

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade are proposed for the interior floor and
driveway of the proposed new residence To reduce the potential for structural distress
resulting from swelling of the on site moderately expansive soils we recommend that a
minimum 12 inch thick non expansive capping fill layer consisting of select granular fill be
provided below the cushion fill layer for slabs on grade The capping fill should also
extend beyond the slab edges a minimum of 12 inches
The non expansive select granular fill material should provide a capping layer of
moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the
loss of moisture from the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential
drying and wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects
on the slab due to potential heaving of the underlying moderately expansive soils when
wetted
Prior to placement of the capping fill the clayey subgrades should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of the capping fill If
shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade soils they should be scarified moisture
conditioned and recompacted as recommended above
For more uniform support of interior slabs we recommend that the concrete
slabs on grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch cushion fill layer The cushion fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3782 00 Page 10
layer should consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation which will also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion
fill should be placed as soon as possible after the placement of the capping fill layer
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration of
moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform curing of
the slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane we recommend
that a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand be placed above the membrane prior to placing
the slab concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to placing concrete
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the proposed
driveway slab we recommend that the slab be supported on a 6 inch layer of select
borrow subbase compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in lieu of
the 4 inch cushion fill layer and 2 inch sand layer The select borrow subbase should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate The cushion fill layer and subgrade beneath the
slabs should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per
cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted select borrow subbase The thickened
edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches
below the lowest adjacent grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly
against the slab with non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water away from
the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations
Exterior concrete walkways around the house structure may be required for the
proposed project The 12 inch thick non expansive capping fill layer may be omitted for
exterior walkway slabs However we recommend that a minimum 6 inch thick cushion
layer of select borrow or No 3B Fine gravel be provided below the concrete walkway

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new residence should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations

APRENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings to depths of approximately 18 to 28 feet below the existing ground surface using
a truck mounted drill rig The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling and sampling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Logs of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
Penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality ROD
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation
