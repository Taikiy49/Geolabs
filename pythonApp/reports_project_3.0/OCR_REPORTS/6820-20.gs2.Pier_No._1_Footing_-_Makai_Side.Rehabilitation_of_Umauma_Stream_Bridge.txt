This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Pier No. 1 Footing – Makai Portion, Rehabilitation of Umauma Stream 
Bridge project in the North Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii. 
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our field exploration and engineering analyses for the 
proposed project.  The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The bridge rehabilitation project is along Hawaii Belt Road at the Umauma Stream 
crossing in north of Hilo on the Island of Hawaii.  A Value Engineering Cost Proposal 
(VECP) which includes redesign of the pier and abutment structures, and the installation of 
a temporary bypass road upstream of the existing bridge structure was proposed and 
accepted.  
The existing bridge structure will be rehabilitated with the installation of two new 
pier column structures located between existing Bent Nos. 1 and 2 and between existing 
Bent Nos. 3 and 4.  We understand that existing Bent No. 5 will not carry structural 
loading.  At the existing abutments, geotextile fabric reinforced walls will be constructed 
behind the abutments.  In addition, new wingwalls will be constructed. 
Pier No. 1 is located between existing Bent Nos. 1 and 2.  The mauka portion of 
Pier No. 1 footing has been constructed to the design bottom of footing elevation of 
+72.15 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  Previously, foundation recommendations for the 
mauka portion of the footing were provided in our revised report entitled “Geotechnical 
Engineering Exploration, Pier No. 1 Footing, Rehabilitation of Umauma Stream Bridge,

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling one boring, designated as Boring No. 201, to a depth of about 38.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring location is shown on the Pier 
No. 1 Footing – Plan and Geologic Profile, Plate 1.   
Our field exploration generally encountered a recent alluvial deposit, about 
24 feet, underlain by medium dense clinker and medium hard to hard basalt rock 
formation extending to the maximum explored depth of 38.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
The alluvial deposit consisted of stiff clayey silt with some gravel and very dense 
gravelly cobbles and cobbly boulders.  The clinker was composed of gravelly cobbles 
with some sand.  The basalt rock formation was medium hard to hard. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled boring at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may fluctuate 
depending on seasonal precipitation, and other factors.  Detailed descriptions of the 
materials encountered in the boring are presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-4.1 
and 4.2 in Appendix A.  Photographs of the recovered core samples are presented in 
Appendix B. 
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our exploration, the makai portion of the Pier No. 1 footing 
is generally underlain by stiff and very dense alluvial deposits about 24 feet thick followed 
by medium dense clinker and medium hard to hard basalt rock formation extending to the 
maximum depth explored of 38.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled boring at the time of our field exploration. 
We understand that the bottom of footing elevation for Pier No. 1 will be at 
Elevation +72.15 feet MSL.  In addition, we understand that scour need not be considered 
for the foundation design.  Spread footing foundation recommendations for the makai 
portion of Pier No. 1 footing are provided below. 
Based on the additional boring performed on the makai portion of Pier No. 1 
footing, very dense alluvial deposits are located near the design bottom of footing 
elevation.  The surface alluvium consisting of clayey silt should be removed to expose the 
underlying very dense gravelly cobbles and cobbly boulders.  It appears that the

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings.  Variations of conditions beyond the field 
borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident 
until construction is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
reevaluate the recommendations presented in this report. 
The field boring location was determined by Geolabs for Boring No. 201 and the 
boring location in relation to the new footing was provided by Hawaiian Dredging 
Construction Company, Inc. on September 9, 2015.  The field boring location should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the boring depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition.  Water level data from the boring was measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report.  These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made to formulate this report.  However, it must be noted that 
fluctuation may occur due to variation in the seasonal rainfall and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling one boring, 
designated as Boring No. 201, to a depth of about 38.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface at the approximate location shown on the Pier No. 1 Footing - Plan and Geologic 
Profile, Plate 1. The boring was drilled using a skid-mounted drill rig equipped with auger 
and coring equipment. 
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Boring, 
Plates A-4.1 and A-4.2. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with 
ASTM  D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
O.D. standard penetration sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling-induced 
fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the 
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%.
