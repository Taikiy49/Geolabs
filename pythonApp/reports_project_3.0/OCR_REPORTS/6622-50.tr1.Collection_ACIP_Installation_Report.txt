PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
The project is situated at the former CompUSA site in the Kaka`ako area of 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bounded by Auahi Street to the north, 
Keawe Street to the east, Ala Moana Boulevard to the south, and South Street to the west. 
In general, the project involves the construction of a 43-story high-rise building, three and 
four-story townhomes and mid-rise residential buildings, and a seven-level parking 
structure. 
Based on the geotechnical engineering report referenced above for the project, we 
recommended supporting the new townhomes, mid-rise structures, and parking structure 
on a deep foundation system consisting of 18 and 24-inch ACIP piles with allowable 
compressive load capacities ranging from 120 to 600 kips per pile. Most of the ACIP piles 
would extend through the soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits (near the surface) and derive 
support primarily from skin friction and end-bearing within coral ledges and/or coralline 
detritus materials encountered below the lagoonal deposit.  
The scope of this letter report is limited to our geotechnical observations of the 
ACIP pile foundation construction for The Collection project only. Hawaiian Dredging 
Construction Company, Inc. (HDCC) was the general contractor for the project and also 
installed the ACIP pile foundation support system. 
ACIP PILE INSTALLATION 
As part of the project requirements, one sacrificial load test pile was constructed 
and tested. Following completion of the load test, the ACIP pile lengths recommended for 
the project were evaluated based on the results of the instrumented load test. The 
following subsections provide descriptions of the load test pile construction and 
discussions on the production ACIP pile installation. 
Load Test ACIP Pile 
In order to evaluate the adequacy of the recommended design lengths of the 
ACIP pile foundation, a bi-directional axial pile load test (Osterberg Method) was 
conducted on a sacrificial ACIP pile, designated as LT-2, on November 4, 2014. The pile 
load test was conducted on a 24-inch diameter ACIP pile with a length of approximately 
72.2 feet relative to the existing ground surface. To better understand the load transfer 
mechanism at the pile-soil interface, the load test piles were instrumented with five levels 
of vibrating wire strain gages installed at various depths for the load test pile. 
A Manitowoc 3900 Crawler Crane equipped with an APE75C top drill drive with 
automatic monitoring equipment and a continuous flight hollow-stem auger was used to

Test Method. A combined side shear and end-bearing resistance of 1,647 kips was 
mobilized during the test.  Complete details of the load test program are presented in the 
report prepared by Loadtest USA. 
Based on the load test results, it appears the load test pile generally behaved like a 
friction pile with the majority of the maximum test load being resisted by adhesion between 
the ACIP pile and the surrounding soils. Based on our field observations and the results of 
the load test, we recommended embedment lengths between 40 and 75 feet below bottom 
of pile caps to achieve the design capacities. 
Production ACIP Pile Installation 
The production ACIP pile installation program, consisting of installing 198 ACIP 
piles, was conducted from November 10, 2014 through September 14, 2015 under the 
near-continuous (full-time) observation of our field personnel. Automatic monitoring 
equipment was mounted on the drilling/pumping equipment with the intent of providing 
auger depth, grout flow rate, grout pressure, etc., on a near-continuous basis.  
During the pile installation operation, logs were maintained for each ACIP pile, 
which included information pertaining to pile number, date installed, depth of pile, grout 
volume used, grout return depth, etc. The identifications of the production ACIP piles 
installed are shown on the ACIP Pile Location Plan, Plate 2. The production ACIP pile 
installation summary is presented by pile number on the attached Plates 3.1 through 3.7. 
As with most foundation installation projects, some problems did arise during 
construction.  The various problems encountered and their remedial action, where 
applicable, are shown in the “Remarks” column of the ACIP Pile Installation Summary. 
Based on our observation of the equipment performance and subsurface conditions 
encountered, it is our professional opinion that the production ACIP piles have been 
installed to sufficient depths and/or resistance to provide the design load capacities.
