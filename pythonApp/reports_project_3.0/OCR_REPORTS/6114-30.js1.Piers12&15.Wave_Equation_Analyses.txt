PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed waterfront improvements project is at Piers 12 and 15 along
Nimitz Highway in Honolulu Harbor on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project consists of
construction of the new berthing as mooring structures to create approximately 300 and
500 linear feet of berthing spaces adjacent to the existing Piers 12 and 15 respectively
The project requires installing sixteen 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete piles to support the structural slab at Pier 15 The piles will extend to a tip
elevation of 97 feet Mean Sea Level MSL for load resistances of 160 and 360 kips for
strength limit state and extreme event limit state design
To support new mooring dolphins and loading platforms eighty seven 87 20 inch
octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles will be driven to tip elevations of 90 and
110 feet MSL at Piers 12 and 15 respectively The 20 inch concrete piles will be
installed in batter configurations from one horizontal to twelve vertical 1H 12V to 3H 12V

Hammer and Driving System
We understand that a Junttan HHS9 hydraulic impact hammer is proposed for the pile
driving operations Based on the pile driving equipment data forms provided the Junttan
HHS9 hammer has a ram weight of approximately 19 500 pounds for a maximum rated
energy of 79 200 foot pounds at a maximum stroke of 4 feet
A pile cushion consisting of at least 9 inches of CDX plywood cushion block is

concrete piles Schematic details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and

Based on precast shop drawings we understand that the effective prestress level
of the 16 5 inch and 20 inch octagonal piles are approximately 1410 psi and 1580 psi
respectively The piles will be casted from concrete with a minimum 28 day unconfined
compressive strength of 6 000 pounds per square inch psi The maximum allowable
compression stresses in the 16 5 inch and 20 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete piles can be calculated to be 3 600 and 3 500 psi respectively The maximum
allowable tensile stresses in the 16 5 inch and 20 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete piles can be calculated to be 1 640 and 1 810 psi respectively
It should be noted that the maximum allowable compressive stress is defined as
85 percent of the 28 day concrete unconfined compressive strength minus the effective
prestress value while the maximum allowable tensile stress is defined to be three times of
the square root of the 28 day concrete unconfined compressive strength plus the effective
prestress value

Driving Stresses
Graphs showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during
driving are provided on Plates 2 1 through 2 3 for the three analyzed cases Please note
that the graphs were prepared based on kips per square inch ksi which is equivalent to
1 000 psi
Based on our analyses the compression stresses developed during pile driving for
the 16 5 inch and 20 inch octagonal concrete piles did not exceed the allowable
compression stresses of 3 600 psi and 3 500 psi respectively for the full range of driving
resistance when using the Junttan HHS9 hydraulic impact hammer
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving estimated for 16 5 inch and
20 inch octagonal concrete piles are less than the allowable tensile stresses of 1 640 psi
and 1 810 psi respectively for the full range of driving resistance except when using the
Junttan HHS9 hydraulic impact hammer with stroke heights between 3 and 4 feet in Case
No 1
Based on the above results and the anticipated subsurface conditions it is our
professional opinion that the proposed pile driving equipment HHS9 hydraulic hammer is
capable of installing the 16 5 inch and 20 inch octagonal concrete piles to the specified tip
elevations and achieving the ultimate design compressive capacities without
over stressing the concrete piles provided that the Junttan HHS9 hydraulic hammer is
operated with a stroke height of no more than 2 feet while driving the 16 5 inch octagonal
concrete piles at Pier 15 Case No 1
A pile may be rejected when the pile driving operation is interrupted for more than
4 hours when the pile cannot be driven to the required depth The wave equation analysis
may be refined after completion of the test pile program to establish production pile
driving criteria for implementation
Based on our analysis we recommend that the following test pile driving criteria be
used for the 16 5 inch and 20 inch octagonal concrete test pile installation The pile driving
criteria should be refined after the test pile program
Case No 1 Pier inch octagonal concrete pile

tip elevation of 97 feet MSL and the driving resistance is greater than 36 blows per
foot while driving with a Junttan HHS9 hydraulic impact hammer with a stroke height
of 2 feet and the pile tip achieves the estimated pile tip elevation of 97 feet
Case No 2 Pier 15 20 inch octagonal concrete pile
The pile driving operation may be terminated when the pile tip achieves the
specified tip elevation of 110 feet MSL and the pile driving resistance is greater

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer The program is based on
mathematical models which describe motion and forces of hammer driving
system pile and soil under the hammer action Under certain conditions
the models only crudely approximate often complex dynamic situations
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data which represents
normal situations In particular the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed However input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions
Therefore the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and or
dynamic testing and measurements Estimates of bending or other local
non axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user
The calculated capacity blow count relationship i e the bearing graph
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking
Finally the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control the variability of the site conditions uncertainties
in the loads the importance of building and other factors

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer The program is based on
mathematical models which describe motion and forces of hammer driving
system pile and soil under the hammer action Under certain conditions
the models only crudely approximate often complex dynamic situations
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data which represents
normal situations In particular the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed However input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions
Therefore the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and or
dynamic testing and measurements Estimates of bending or other local
non axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user
The calculated capacity blow count relationship i e the bearing graph
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking
Finally the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control the variability of the site conditions uncertainties
in the loads the importance of building and other factors
