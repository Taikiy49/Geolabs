SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration is generally underlain by medium stiff to very stiff alluvial soils 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings bearing on 
the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills for support of the structures 
planned at the site with an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square 
foot (psf). In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the 
lowest adjacent grades. The bottom of footing excavations in on-site soils should be 
recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
We envision that the high moisture content subgrade conditions may likely cause 
pumping/yielding during the compaction process without additional stabilization efforts. 
The subgrade stabilization efforts may consist of aerating on-site soils prior to the 
compaction process. Considering the high rainfall environment that the aerating process 
would likely cause construction delay, an alternative may include soil-cement 
stabilization. 
Based on the results of the field in-situ permeability testing pertaining to the 
preliminary design of the wastewater disposal system, the percolation rates presented at 
the testing locations indicate that a leach field system for disposal of wastewater effluent 
may be feasible for this development. Considering the high variability of the subsurface 
conditions, the absorption capacity of the selected disposal system should be confirmed by 
conducting additional permeability tests during construction. 
Based on the results of the field in-situ infiltration testing, the average infiltration 
rate is generally greater than 0.5 inches per hour, indicating that an infiltration/detention 
system and similar methods of stormwater management would be feasible for the 
development. Due to the high variability of the subsurface conditions, the absorption 
capacity of the selected stormwater disposal system should be confirmed by conducting 
additional infiltration tests during construction. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Fleet Yard Redevelopment project on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, pavements, and site grading only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site encompassing 8.5 acres is generally situated in the center of Lanai 
City, Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The existing fleet yard maintenance facility is bounded by 
Lanai Avenue to the east, 11th Street to the south, Ilima Avenue to the west, and 9th Street 
to the north. We understand that it is planned to redevelop the existing facility. Details of 
the redevelopment were not available at the time this report was prepared. 
Based on Google Earth™, the project site terrain was relatively flat with existing 
ground surface elevations ranging from about +1,594 to +1,612 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL). We envision minimal site grading will be required to achieve the design grades for 
the project.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is the 
result of building in the southwest rift zone. The summit of the shield collapsed to form a 
caldera from which a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends 
south-southeast toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff indicate 
that this Manele Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows, and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. The 
project site is located within the Manele Graben.

extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground

3.1 
Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, a shallow 
foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings may be used to 
support the newly proposed structures planned for the project. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of 
the foundations bearing on recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed 
to achieve the design finished grades.

This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, we 
recommend embedding the footings a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent 
grades. 
Where footings are located adjacent to other below-grade structures, utility trenches, 
or easements, the footings should extend to a depth below a 45-degree imaginary plane 
projected upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench or the footings should 
extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to 
avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to 
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
The bottom of footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at 
the bottom of the footing excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying 
firm materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the bottom of the footing 
may be extended down to bear on the underlying competent materials. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations 
to be less than 1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on 
similar materials may be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the building structure may be resisted by friction between 
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and/or by passive earth pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the footing. A coefficient of friction of 0.30 may be used 
for footings bearing on the stiff on-site soils or new compacted fills. Resistance to lateral 
loads due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure 
of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This value assumes that the soils 
around the footings are well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the 
passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We envision slabs-on-grade building structures may be required for the proposed 
facility redevelopment. Based on the results of our field exploration, the on-site material 
has a low expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. Near-surface 
on-site material has a relatively higher moisture content, probably due to the high rainfall 
environment and poor drainage conditions. We envision that the high moisture content 
subgrade conditions may likely cause pumping/yielding during the subgrade compaction 
process without additional stabilization efforts. 
If the weather is permitted, the subgrade stabilization may consist of aerating the 
on-site subgrade soils to the moisture content about 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content prior to the subgrade compaction process. 
Considering the high rainfall environment in the vicinity of the project site that the 
aerating process would likely require excessive time without causing construction delay, 
an alternative may be to apply cement stabilization on the subgrade with high moisture 
content. A typical ratio of the cement stabilization may be about 2 percent of cement by 
weight of the in-situ soils, for example, five bags of 94-pound cement to stabilize a 12-inch 
deep scarified 15 feet by 15 feet area. The site-specific ratio of the soil-cement 
stabilization should be further defined by performing a test section prior to the 
construction. 
We recommend moisture-conditioning the subgrade soils to about 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture content and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction, where possible. The underlying subgrade soils and imported fill should be 
wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete.

For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), 
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch-thick layer of cushion fill consisting of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support to the slabs and would serve as a 
capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for appreciable future moisture infiltration 
through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, we recommend placing an 
impervious moisture barrier on top of the open-graded gravel cushion fill layer. Flexible 
floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered because they can 
better mask minor slab cracking. In addition, we recommend designing interior walls to 
incorporate some flexibility in accommodating a small amount of possible ground 
movements. 
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic (such as driveways) or 
machinery vibration, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below 
the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer. The moisture barrier also may 
be omitted for these slabs. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic 
aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Where slabs are 
intended to function as rigid pavements, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be 
used for preliminary design purposes. Provisions should be made for proper load transfer 
across the slab joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled 
tightly against the slab edges with low expansion, relatively impervious soils, or cast neat 
against the vertical cut face. These areas should also be graded to divert water away 
from the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor slab-on-grade foundation excavations 
prior to placing the reinforcing steel and/or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing 
conditions and the required embedment depths and observe backfill placement to 
evaluate the compaction quality of the fill material. Observation of the foundation 
excavations should be designated as a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with 
International Building Code (2012).

3.3 
Retaining Structures 
We envision that retaining structures may be required to serve the proposed 
project. The following guidelines may be used for the design of the retaining structures. 
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the “Foundations” 
section. 
In addition, retaining wall foundations should be at least 18 inches wide, and the 
bottom should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished 
grades. For sloping ground conditions, the bottom of the footing should extend deeper to 
obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge 
of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the 
slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects. The recommended lateral earth pressures for 
the design of retaining walls, expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of pounds per

3.4.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding 
to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the fills. Where compaction is less than required, 
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment moisture content, as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. 
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils and 
general fill materials are critical elements of the earthwork, Geolabs should perform 
observations and soil density tests during the site grading operations to assist the 
contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture 
content on each fill lift. Where compaction is less than required, additional 
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment in the moisture content as 
necessary to obtain the specified compaction. It should be noted that the moisture 
requirement of the on-site fills and subgrades (at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture) is an important requirement considering the slightly expansive nature of 
the on-site clayey soils.

3.5 Leaching Field 
We understand that wastewater effluent generated from the new fleet yard 
maintenance facility development may be disposed of on-site. Therefore, we envision that 
the disposal of wastewater effluent will be by the leach field method. Field percolation

preliminary design of the wastewater disposal system. Two field percolation tests were 
performed at locations P-1 and P-2, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the 
provisions of the State of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 
11-62-31.2, “Wastewater Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to 
evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. 
The tests were performed by drilling 4-inch diameter boreholes extending to a depth of 
about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Approximately 6 inches of gravel was then 
placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect the bottom of the holes from scouring. 
Because clayey soil was encountered, the test holes were presoaked for at least 4 hours. 
During the tests, the holes were carefully filled with water to a depth of about 
12 inches above the gravel layer. The time intervals and water drops were then recorded 
to provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation rate was based. Based on 
the percolation tests conducted, the percolation rates at locations P-1 and P-2 were 
generally about 30 and 120 minutes per inch, respectively. It should be noted that 
percolation rates in excess of 60 minutes per inch are considered unsuitable for leach

materials encountered. The tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix C of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent 
with other states’ procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
Results of the infiltration tests performed at selected locations are presented in Appendix D.

Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH value, minimum resistivity, chloride 
content, and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our 
field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. 
The test results are summarized and presented on Plate B-10 of Appendix B. Detailed 
results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests 
performed by Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. are presented in Appendix E. 
Based on the results of laboratory corrosivity tests, the near-surface soils at the 
project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of approximately 2,300 to 3,400 ohm-cm, 
indicating that the near-surface soils are very corrosive to corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 
2 to 3) to buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly designing near-
surface metallic substructures (such as piping) for protection against the potential for 
corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent, in part, on the chloride content and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate content tested 
on the in-situ materials, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used 
for the concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult with a

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
nine borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the

designated as I-1 through I-3 and P-1 and P-2, were drilled for infiltration and percolation 
testing access. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil

are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-14.

ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified

were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Six one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under 
different surcharge loads.  The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B-2. 
Four Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the in-situ 
soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-3 through B-6. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method A) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Two sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-11.
