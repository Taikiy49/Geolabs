SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered beach sands underlain by basalt formation at 
greater depths.  Beach sands consisting of medium dense sands were generally 
encountered at the ground surface extending down to about 5 and 9.5 feet below the 
ground surface.  Beach sands were underlain by basalt formation extending down to the 
maximum depth explored of about 12.5 feet below the ground surface.  Basalt formation 
encountered were generally hard and slightly weathered. Groundwater was encountered 
in our borings at about 6 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Due to the seasonal erosion of beach sands anticipated at the site, we recommend 
that a cut-off wall be constructed along the makai side of the walkway.  Ideally, the cut-off 
wall should extend down until hard basalt formation is encountered.  Due to the relatively 
shallow groundwater level, we envision that it would be difficult to construct the cut-off 
wall below the groundwater level.  Therefore, we recommend that the cut-off wall be 
extended down to about 5 feet below the ground surface. 
In lieu of constructing a concrete cut-off wall as described above, consideration 
may be given to installing a synthetic sheet pile wall, such as ShoreGuard, along the 
makai side of the new walkway.  The synthetic sheet piles should be installed down to the 
basalt formation.  Due to the access restrictions, constructability of the synthetic sheet 
pile wall at this project site would need to be further explored with local contractors. 
In addition to providing the concrete cut-off wall or synthetic sheet pile wall for the 
walkway, construction of a short free-standing barrier wall along the mauka side of the 
walkway may be considered.  In general, the barrier wall would keep the beach sands on 
and in front of the walkway and reduce the amount of beach sands that get washed further 
inland below the existing bungalow building. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the new Shoreline Walkway project within the Hualalai Resort in the District 
of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field 
exploration and laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations for 
the design of the new walkway. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of the new 
walkway and site preparation only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is within the existing Hualalai development in the District of North Kona 
on the Island of Hawaii.  A portion of the walkway, about 170 feet in length, fronting the 
shoreline was undermined by wave surge during the winter months.  We understand that the 
damaged walkway was recently repaired.  However, it is desired to design the walkway to 
withstand future wave surges. 
1.2

subsurface conditions to develop a soil and/or rock data set to formulate geotechnical 
recommendations for the design of the new walkway and site preparation.  In order to 
accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks 
and efforts:

2. 
Mobilization of a portable drill rig and operators to the site and back. 
3. 
Drilling and sampling of two borings to depths of about 9 and 12.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. 
4. 
Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our 
geologist.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Loa Mountain, which 
makes up the south-central portion of the island.  The lava formation encountered at the 
site appears to be of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as 
they approached the ocean. A’a lava typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and 
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including 
cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy 
surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets generally 
are smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted 
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, 
and along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as the primary water transmission 
features because of their high permeability.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project is within the existing Hualalai Resort in the Kaupulehu area in the 
District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The portion of the walkway that will be 
reconstructed is in front of Bungalow 25 at the central portion of the existing resort.  A 
Site Plan is provided as Plate 2. 
Based on our field observations of the site conditions in December 2017, the 
walkway was undermined by ocean waves.  The beach sands appeared to have been 
washed inland below the existing bungalow building that is supported on a post and pier 
system.  We understand that the damaged concrete walkway was demolished, and a new 
temporary walkway was constructed. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 9 and 12.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our borings drilled, the subsurface conditions at the site generally consist 
of beach sands underlain by basalt formation at greater depths.  Beach sands consisting 
of medium dense sands were generally encountered at the ground surface extending 
down to about 5 and 9.5 feet below the ground surface.  Beach sands were underlain by 
basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 12.5 feet below 
the ground surface.  Basalt formation encountered were generally hard and slightly 
weathered.  It should be noted that cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the 
basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should 
be expected during construction. 
Groundwater was encountered in our borings at about 6 feet below the existing 
ground surface at the time of our field exploration. Based on the proximity of the project 
site to the Pacific Ocean, groundwater should generally be encountered near Mean Sea 
Level and vary with tidal fluctuations. In addition, groundwater levels may change due to 
storm surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered beach sands underlain by basalt formation at 
greater depths.  Beach sands consisting of medium dense sands were generally 
encountered at the ground surface extending down to about 5 and 9.5 feet below the 
ground surface.  Beach sands were underlain by basalt formation extending down to the 
maximum depth explored of about 12.5 feet below the ground surface.  Basalt formation 
encountered were generally hard and slightly weathered. Groundwater was encountered 
in our borings at about 6 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Due to the seasonal erosion of beach sands anticipated at the site, we recommend 
that a cut-off wall be constructed along the makai side of the walkway.  Ideally, the cut-off 
wall should extend down until hard basalt formation is encountered.  Due to the relatively 
shallow groundwater level, we envision that it would be difficult to construct the cut-off 
wall below the groundwater level.  Therefore, we recommend that the cut-off wall be 
extended down to about 5 feet below the ground surface. 
In lieu of constructing a concrete cut-off wall as described above, consideration 
may be given to installing a synthetic sheet pile wall, such as ShoreGuard, along the 
makai side of the new walkway.  The synthetic sheet piles should be installed down to the 
basalt formation.  Due to the access restrictions, constructability of the synthetic sheet 
pile wall at this project site would need to be further explored with local contractors. 
In addition to providing the concrete cut-off wall or synthetic sheet pile wall for the 
walkway, construction of a short free-standing barrier wall along the mauka side of the 
walkway may be considered.  In general, the barrier wall would keep the beach sands on 
and in front of the walkway and reduce the amount of beach sands that get washed further 
inland below the existing bungalow building. 
Detailed discussions and specific design recommendations for the design of the 
new walkway, site preparation, and other geotechnical aspects of the project are 
presented in the following sections.

3.1 
New Walkway 
We understand that seasonal storm surge conditions have caused significant 
erosion of the beach sands below and around the existing walkway.  Therefore, design 
of the new walkway will need to take into consideration the highly erodible nature of the 
clean beach sands. 
We understand walkway slabs with a uniform thickness of 6 inches were utilized 
for the project. Considering the proximity of the walkway to the shoreline, steel 
reinforcement in the concrete should be avoided to reduce the potential for corrosion of 
the steel reinforcement embedded in the concrete. Therefore, we recommend utilizing 
fiber reinforcement in the concrete (fiber-reinforced concrete) instead of steel 
reinforcement for the walkways. 
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, 
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with 
expansion joints at right-angle intersections. The crack control joints should be made at 
the appropriate time following concrete placement to reduce the potential for uncontrolled 
cracking of the concrete slab. 
In general, we believe the new concrete walkway may be constructed directly on 
the compacted sandy subgrades, provided that the recommendations presented in the 
“Site Preparation” section herein are followed.  
Due to the seasonal erosion of beach sands anticipated at the site, we recommend 
that a cut-off wall be constructed along the makai side of the walkway.  Ideally, the cut-off 
wall should extend down until hard basalt formation is encountered.  Based on our 
borings, basalt formation was encountered at depths of about 5 and 9.5 feet below the 
ground surface.  In addition, groundwater was encountered at about 6 feet below the 
ground surface.  Due to the relatively shallow groundwater level, we envision that it would 
be difficult to construct the cut-off wall below the groundwater level.  Therefore, we 
recommend that the cut-off wall be extended down to about 5 feet below the ground 
surface.

In lieu of constructing a concrete cut-off wall as described above, consideration 
may be given to installing a synthetic sheet pile wall, such as ShoreGuard, along the 
makai side of the new walkway.  Synthetic sheet piles are preferable to steel sheet piles 
in marine application due to their non-corrosive properties.  The synthetic sheet piles 
should be installed down to the basalt formation.  Due to the access restrictions, 
constructability of the synthetic sheet pile wall at this project site would need to be further 
explored with local contractors. 
In addition to providing the concrete cut-off wall or synthetic sheet pile wall for the 
walkway, construction of a short free-standing barrier wall along the mauka side of the 
walkway may be considered.  In general, the barrier wall would keep the beach sands on 
and in front of the walkway and reduce the amount of beach sands that get washed further 
inland below the existing bungalow building.  We understand that short rock walls of about 
2 feet in height have been in place on the mauka side of the walkways in other parts of 
the resort.  These areas have generally kept the beach sands in place and have 
performed well over the years. 
3.2 
Site Preparation 
Based on the proposed scope of work anticipated at the site, we envision that site 
grading consisting of cuts and fills of less than about 5 feet thick will be required for the 
project. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the site grading operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered and to confirm whether the exposed 
subsurface conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, existing 
walkway, and other unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of properly 
off-site. 
Subgrade preparation is a critical item for the performance of the new walkway. It 
should be noted that the poorly-graded clean beach sands are generally difficult to compact 
to a dense state and maintained in that dense state unless adequate moisture is preserved

in the material. Thorough wetting of the beach sands will be required to compact the 
subgrade properly utilizing a vibratory plate tampers or similar compaction equipment. In 
addition, if the materials are allowed to dry out following compaction, the sandy soils will 
have a tendency to loosen.  Therefore, concrete should be poured as soon as practicable 
after the subgrade has been properly prepared. 
The sandy subgrades should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by vibratory plate tampers or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
In general, the on-site beach sands may be used as general fill materials.  Imported 
fill materials (where required) should be free of organics and deleterious materials and 
should be suitable for the intended use. In addition, imported fill materials should be 
observed and/or tested by Geolabs prior to being transported to the site for the intended 
use. 
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by beach 
sands and basalt formation at greater depths. It is anticipated that the beach sands may 
be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators. 
However, it should be noted that beach sands are highly susceptible to raveling and 
cave-ins, especially under groundwater level.  Therefore, the contractor should anticipate 
encountering difficult excavation conditions.  Shoring of excavations or flattening of 
excavation sidewalls will likely be required.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the drilled borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations 
may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, 
it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated herein are approximate, by overlaying GPS 
coordinates on Google Earth satellite image. The physical locations of the borings should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation tides, rainfall, temperature, and other 
factors.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 9 and 
12.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a portable drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
A soil sample was obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow count needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive is shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any

probably require more arduous methods of excavation.
