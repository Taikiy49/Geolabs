Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosion processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls which are then transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit
large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport of sediments is
intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture
of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosion base levels change
these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local
base level and rate of transport these new sediments are generally transient in terms of
geologic time In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of
older partially consolidated deposits

The project site is situated at the base of the Koolau Pali a high cliff that extends
along most of the windward side of the Koolau Range on the Island of Oahu The
climate along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average annual rainfall
Following the main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene Epoch stream erosion landslide and deep weathering of the basalt
rock became the dominant agents affecting the morphology of the area
The project site is on the large colluvial apron deposited near the base of the
Koolau Range The colluvial apron formed as a result of the accumulation of soil and
rock materials derived from large scale erosion and landslide along the Koolau Pali
Subsequently stream erosion incised these deposits to create the present day
topography of the region as evidenced by the occurrence of multiple hill and gully
terrain
The project site generally exists as a prominent ridgeline composed of alluvial
and colluvial soil deposits The ridge is separated by an incised drainage gully to the
east and a well developed stream channel located to the west of the ridge The
ridgeline merges northerly into the developed landscape of the existing Garden of
Patriotism at the Valley of the Temples Memorial Park

underlain at relatively shallow depths by alluvial and colluvial soils consisting of
medium stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts with interbeds of highly to
completely weathered gravel cobbles and boulders Based on our evaluation we
believe that the in situ soils should provide adequate support for the proposed site
grading work and retaining wall structures planned for the mortuary expansion provided
that the recommendations presented herein are closely followed during the design and
construction of the project

relatively high moisture content which is generally associated with poor workability and

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials Due to the relatively large acreage of the site it is anticipated
that the clearing and grubbing work may need to be performed incrementally Care
should be exercised during the clearing and grubbing operations to avoid
contaminating previously cleared and or filled areas with unsuitable materials such
as organic matter and or soft soils
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below finished
subgrades and areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose
firm natural material The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted general fills The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
disposed of off site and or used in landscape areas where appropriate Special
attention should be given to the potential presence of soft and or loose soils along
the bottom of gullies where deep fills are planned Contract documents should
include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing the over excavated subgrades finished subgrades
and areas designated to receive fills should be proof rolled to a minimum of
85 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close cracks or moisture conditioning
and recompacting the subgrade Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require

Keying and Benching
Although the average slope at the project site is on the order of about six horizontal
to one vertical 6H 1V there are local areas of steeper slopes Therefore
extensive keying and benching is recommended for the earthwork to be performed
at the site These keys and benches should be firmly seated in the native subsoils
underlying the site to reduce the potential for sliding and or slope failures
At the toe of large fill segments keyway should be cut down to a minimum of 5 feet
in depth at the toe of the fill and should be a minimum of 15 feet in width extending
back under the toe of the fill The floor of the key should be sloped back under the
base of the fill at about 10H 1V to bond the new fill into the existing slope
As fill placement progresses up the existing slope benches should be cut into the
existing slope to bond the mass of the fill to the existing ground These benches
should be a minimum of 4 feet in height and approximately 10 feet in width A

Fills and Backfills
General fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed
finished subgrades may consist of the excavated on site soils provided that they
are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock fragments greater than
6 inches in maximum dimension Since the project site is in a high rainfall area the
on site materials have very high in situ moisture contents of about 15 to 30 percent
above optimum Therefore significant drying of excavated materials will be
required prior to their use as fill and or backfill material
Imported general fill materials needed for the site filling should consist of materials
with a low expansion potential Imported general fill materials should consist of
materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension and should
be free of deleterious materials The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio CBR value of 8 or higher and a swell potential of not more than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Aggregate subbase materials required under the retaining wall foundations and
rigid pavement section should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should
conform to Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction
Department of Public Works City and County of Honolulu September 1986
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use

compaction normally required This field method of reducing the moisture content in
the soil may not be feasible at all times due to the high rainfall frequency

Subdrains
Based on our explorations and our understanding of the subsurface conditions at
the site there is a potential for seepage to develop along the joints within the

precipitation In addition there is a potential for the moisture content of the fill
materials to increase over time as a result of the planned use of the site from
sources such as landscape irrigation and minor utility leaks
The accumulation of water along the base of the fill could adversely affect the
performance of the fill Therefore we recommend installing subdrains in the base
of the fills to drain at the toe In general the subdrains should follow the existing
drainage paths on the site Detail of a typical subdrain installation is shown on
Plate 4 of this report

affect the construction schedule and the earthwork quantity estimates for the project
Based on the results of our exploration the anticipated ground settlements are primarily
the result of the following two processes
Compression of the fill material under its own weight especially since the

Rockfall Hazards
Rockfall is a type of natural mass wasting process that is in part responsible for the
evolution of mountain slopes Rockfall is a common natural hazard due to the steep
volcanic terrain comprising the mountains and valleys of the Islands of Hawaii
Rockfall activity involves the detachment of a rock mass from the slope face and
the fall of rock materials under the force of gravity Rockfall may involve a single
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
GEOLABS INC
Page 28
Hawaii California
unstable block of rock outcrop or it may involve a rockslide which encompasses
multiple rock components
Falling rock behavior can be complicated by the varying character of the ground
surface and topography encountered along the rock fall line Rocks may either roll
bounce or have combined movement vectors Falling rock may also hang up
temporarily or permanently on the slope without reaching the bottom under the
effects of terrain roughness topography vegetation and the presence of natural
features such as berms and terraces formed by other rock outcroppings
The nearest rock source that can impact the project site would originate from the
Koolau ridge system We believe that the 800 to 1 000 feet separation distance
between the project site and the base of the mountain provides a significant buffer
for rockfall hazard mitigation Based on observed general site conditions the
near vertical faces of the closely spaced and semi parallel basalt dikes buttressing
the slopes of Koolau Pali would restrict the trajectory of a falling rock within the
confines of these walls and consequently its ability to reach the project site The
relatively smooth and slightly weathered facade of the basalt dikes behind the
project site are generally devoid of rock outcrops that could readily detach and
potentially fall Photo No 2 We envision that rocks falling from the upper reaches
of Koolau would end up at the basal intersections Rocks reaching the base of
Koolau will likely accumulate at the bottom of the valley southwest of the project site
where the stream tributaries converge The southern segment of the ridgeline that
traverses the central portion of the project site provides a natural rock fall barrier by
virtue of its height and orientation relative to the potential rockfall zone
On the east side of the study area the head gully formed by the central ridgeline
and adjacent east ridgeline rises to approximately 300 feet high relative to the
project site merging at the foothills of Koolau Pali The drainage gully formed by

lying portions of the project site However the southernmost terminus of this head
gully is separated from the main Koolau mass by a very narrow 500 foot long ridge

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by our office in 1998
and 2003 The latter field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths of about 9 to 41 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the excavated test pits and drilled
borings previous and current field explorations relative to the new grading plan are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using track mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
Data from the above borings Boring No 2 of the 1998 exploration and Boring No 6
from the 2003 exploration were used for the slope stability analysis at the location of
Walls 2 and 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear test results are presented on Plates C 2 through C 5

sample of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered Consolidation test results are presented on
Plate C 6

bulk samples of the in situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils Results of the CBR tests were also used to evaluate the quality of the materials
tested for use as fills The test results are presented on Plates C 7 and C 8
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the in situ soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 9 and C 10
