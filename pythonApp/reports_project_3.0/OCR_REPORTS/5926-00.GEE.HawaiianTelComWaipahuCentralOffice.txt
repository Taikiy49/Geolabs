DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We have reviewed the information obtained from the field exploration and our
in house soils reports relevant to the project site The subsurface conditions at the project
site generally consist of hard silty clay clayey silt with a layer of weathered basaltic cobbles
and boulders Based on our engineering experience and slope stability analyses we
believe that the project site is generally stable for future developments The preliminary
recommendations presented below should be confirmed when details of the future
structures are available
Slope Stability
Based on our past project experience slopes that are susceptible to failures or
movements are usually underlain by soft loose soils or highly plastic clay soils or are
over steepened
Slope stability is an equilibrium state of driving force and resisting force within soils
of a slope When a slope experiences an increase of driving force such as seismic loads
during an earthquake event and or a decrease of resisting force such as excavation at
toe of slope shear stress will increase within the slope soils Because the loose soft soils
have very little shear strength shear stress will easily exceed the shear strength of the soil
and cause shear slip surface to develop within the loose soft soil layer Depending on
the magnitude of shear stress within the soils the slope may experience movement along
the shear slip surface or slope failure
In addition slope movements may be caused by underlying highly plastic clay soils
which are classified as CH according to the Unified Soil Classification System locally
referred to as adobe clay The highly plastic clay exhibits high swelling shrinkage
potential and will lose considerable shear strength when saturated When the CH clay
soils experience significant downslope movement shear slip surfaces will then develop
which will further reduce the shear strength along the slip surfaces to a very low residual
shear value which causes the slope to continuously move over time creep
Our field exploration work indicated that loose soft soils and highly plastic adobe
clay which are major components in the driving forces for slope failures were not
encountered within the borings drilled at the project site
Based on the information obtained from our field exploration and laboratory tests
we have conducted slope stability analyses using the Reinforced Slope Stability Analysis
computer program The Bishop s simplified method of limit equilibrium was
used to evaluate the balance of forces and moments acting within the slope to determine
the factors of safety for the slopes based on the generalized subsurface profile and the

top of slope may be susceptible to near surface slope creep and therefore future
structures should be situated a minimum of 10 feet from the top of slope
Preliminary Recommendations for Future Structures
Because structural details were not available at the time we prepared this letter
report the recommendations pertaining to future building structures and retaining
structures are preliminary and should not be used for final design purposes without
Geolabs confirmation
Building Foundations
We anticipate that the future development will involve construction of relatively light
structures up to two stories high We assume that the site grading will consist of
very thin cuts and fills of less than 1 foot to achieve design finish grade
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration we believe that shallow
spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the new building
structure at the site An allowable bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot
psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the compacted
subgrade
The bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavated area may be backfilled with new fill material compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be
extended down to bear on competent materials at a greater depth
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum

horizontal setback distance of 10 feet measured from the outside edge of the
footings to the face of the slope Footings adjacent to planned or existing retaining
walls should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall
foundations or the planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the
additional structural loads
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent below grade utility lines
or structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
appreciable foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the building structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the on site soils or
new compacted fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected In addition the passive earth pressure for foundations
located on slopes should be reduced
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
The project site is underlain by hard silty clays extending to about 11 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The near surface clayey materials may exhibit a slight
expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the
potential for future structural distress resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils
we recommend properly preparing and capping the slab subgrades with structural
fill
In general we recommend providing a minimum of 6 inches of structural fill material
below the building slab areas The structural fill should also extend beyond the
perimeter of the building a minimum of 24 inches or extend to the edge of the
concrete sidewalk or concrete apron surrounding the building if applicable The
structural fill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

In addition we recommend scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the
placement of the structural fill Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
For interior building slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs The open graded
gravel cushion fill would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for
uniform support of the slabs To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration
and subsequent damage to the floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as trucks and or forklifts
we recommend providing a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs
in lieu of the 4 inch cushion fill layer The aggregate subbase should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95percent relative
compaction
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled
tightly against the slab edges with low expansion relatively impervious soils such
as the on site soils These areas should also be graded to divert water away from
the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs and
foundations
Retaining Structures
Retaining structures may be required for the future development at the project site
These discussion and recommendations are intended to provide general guidance
for the design and construction of such walls
Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the Building Foundations section presented herein The
foundations should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
on relatively flat areas For sloping ground conditions the footings should
extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback distance measured
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Wall
footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed
in stepped footings

Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for
build up of hydrostatic pressures
For site retaining walls the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic
foot of permeable material such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation wrapped with non woven filter fabric placed at each of the
weephole locations The weepholes should be spaced no more than 6 feet
apart As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain
or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the permeable drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall
Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of relatively impervious material well graded materials to reduce the
potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure
