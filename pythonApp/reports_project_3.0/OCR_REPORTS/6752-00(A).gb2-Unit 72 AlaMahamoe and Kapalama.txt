SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the existing PCC pavement thickness at various locations along 
Ala Mahamoe Street generally ranged from about 5 to 7 inches.  The PCC pavement was 
underlain by stiff clayey soils.  The consistency of the subgrade at Onipaa Street was 
generally soft to medium stiff.  The existing PCC pavement thickness at the upper 
Kapalama Avenue ranged from about 6 to 8 inches.  The existing PCC pavement 
thickness at the lower Kapalama Avenue was about 5 inches thick.  The PCC pavement 
was underlain by stiff clayey soils and dense gravel. 
Based on the assumed traffic data and the subgrade conditions encountered, the 
new rigid pavement sections for the project should consist of the following:  
8.0 inches of PCC on compacted subgrade (Ala Mahamoe Street) 
6.5 inches of PCC over compacted subgrade (Kapalama Avenue) 
Where soft soils are encountered at the pavement subgrades, we recommend 
over-excavating the soft soils to a maximum of depth of 12 inches and should extend a 
minimum of 24 inches beyond the lateral limits of the soft zone.  A woven geotextile fabric 
(such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) should be placed at the bottom of the 
over-excavation and select borrow subbase course should be used to backfill up to the 
pavement subgrade.  
Based on our experience, we have observed soft soils near the downslope end of 
roadways located on steep inclines.  We believe that the soils become saturated as a 
result of seepage water flowing down the slope and collecting at the point where the 
roadway gradient flattens out.  To reduce saturation of the subgrade soils at the 
downslope end of the roadways, we recommend placing a transverse subdrain (across the 
entire roadway width) near the bottom of the sloping roadway. The text of this report 
should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific pavement design 
recommendations.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering analyses 
performed for the Rehabilitation of Concrete Pavements, Unit 72, Ala Mahamoe Street 
and Kapalama Avenue project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our 
analyses for the concrete pavement rehabilitation project. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Rehabilitation of Concrete Pavements, Various Streets, Unit 72 project 
covers various roadways, consisting of Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) pavements, 
across the City and County of Honolulu.  
This geotechnical engineering exploration covers Ala Mahamoe Street at 
five locations and Kapalama Avenue at two locations.  Ala Mahamoe Street is in the 
Moanalua area of Honolulu and Kapalama Avenue is in the Kalihi area of Honolulu.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  The project site is at the southern flank of 
the Koolau Mountain Range. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.  
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited 
as alluvium.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the existing PCC pavement thickness at various locations along 
Ala Mahamoe Street generally ranged from about 5 to 7 inches.  The PCC pavement 
was underlain by stiff clayey soils.  The consistency of the subgrade at Onipaa Street 
was generally soft to medium stiff.  The existing PCC pavement thickness at the upper 
Kapalama Avenue ranged from about 6 to 8 inches.  The existing PCC pavement 
thickness at the lower Kapalama Avenue was about 5 inches thick.  The PCC pavement 
was underlain by stiff clayey soils and dense gravel.

new rigid pavement sections for the project should consist of the following:  
8.0 inches of PCC on compacted subgrade (Ala Mahamoe Street)

The existing roadways are located on steep inclines.  Based on our experience, 
we have observed soft soils near the downslope end of roadways located on steep

out.  To reduce saturation of the subgrade soils at the downslope end of the roadways, 
we recommend placing a transverse subdrain (across the entire roadway width) near 
the bottom of the sloping roadway. In general, the subdrain should be a minimum of 
18 inches wide and 12 inches deep below the pavement subgrade.  The subdrain 
should consist of a 6-inch PVC perforated pipe (with perforations facing down) near the 
bottom of the trench.  The perforated pipe should be surrounded by No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) to reduce the potential for migration of fines into the drainage material.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The design procedures used in determining the new rigid pavement sections 
discussed herein are based on the “Guide for Design of Pavement Structures” (1993) 
developed by the American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials 
(AASHTO). 
3.2 
Design Traffic Loading Conditions 
We understand that traffic counts and truck distribution information are not 
available for the project. Kapalama Avenue is not subjected to bus traffic.  However, 
Ala Mahamoe Street is currently being utilized as a bus route for the City and County of 
Honolulu.  We have assumed that the new pavement for Kapalama Avenue will be 
designed for Equivalent Single Axle Loads (ESALs) of no more than 1,000,000 
(25,000 ESALs per year) over 40-year design life of the pavement. 
We have assumed ESALs of about 2,000,000 (50,000 ESALs per year) over the 
40-year design life of the new pavements at various locations along Ala Mahamoe 
Street.  
3.3 
Design Subgrade Conditions 
Laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of each area to evaluate the swelling potential and pavement support 
characteristics of the pavement subgrade soils.  The samples were remolded to near 
the optimum moisture content and saturated.  In addition, DCP testing was conducted in 
the borings to estimate the in-situ CBR value of the subgrade soils.   
Based on our laboratory tests, the in-situ moisture content of the soils are on the 
order of about 20 to 30 percent.  We believe that the subgrade soils may be compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction without significant aeration of the soils.  
Therefore, we have used the laboratory CBR values to correlate an Effective Modulus of 
Subgrade Reaction, k, for use in our rigid pavement analysis.  Since the subgrade soils 
consist of fine-grained soils, a Loss of Support value of 2 was for used for 
Kapalama Avenue to determine a Reduced Effective Modulus of Subgrade Reaction.  
The subgrade soils encountered at Ala Mahamoe Street appear to have a moderate to

Design Pavement Section 
To reduce the potential for encountering existing underground utilities during 
pavement construction, we recommend placing the new PCC pavement section directly 
on the clayey subgrade soils.  Placing the PCC pavement section on the subgrade soils 
in lieu of placing on a layer of select borrow would reduce the amount and depth of 
excavation required. Based on the ESALs and the reduced effective modulus of 
subgrade reaction, the following rigid pavement sections may be used for the project. 
Ala Mahamoe Street Rigid Pavement Section 
8.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete (Flexural Strength = 650 psi) 
8.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness Over Compacted Subgrade 
Kapalama Avenue Rigid Pavement Section 
6.5-Inch Portland Cement Concrete (Flexural Strength = 650 psi) 
6.5-Inch Total Pavement Thickness Over Compacted Subgrade

over-excavating the soft soils to a maximum of depth of 12 inches.  The over-excavation

3.5 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subgrade soils. It should be noted that the silty/clayey subgrade soils 
are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. Therefore, special 
attention should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements. As 
a minimum, the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.  
The existing roadways are located on steep inclines.  Based on our experience, 
we have observed soft soils near the downslope end of roadways (transition between 
AC and PCC pavements) located on steep inclines.  We believe that the soils become 
saturated as a result of seepage water flowing down the slope and collecting at the 
point where the roadway gradient flattens out.  To reduce saturation of the subgrade 
soils at the downslope end of the roadways, we recommend placing a transverse 
subdrain (across the entire roadway width) near the bottom of the sloping roadway. In 
general, the subdrain should be a minimum of 18 inches wide and 12 inches deep 
below the pavement subgrade.  The subdrain should consist of a 6-inch PVC perforated 
pipe (with perforations facing down) near the bottom of the trench surrounded by 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation). A non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be placed around the No. 3B Fine gravel to reduce 
the potential for migration of fines into the drainage material.  A typical subdrain detail is 
provided on Plate 5 for reference.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation by drilling 
and sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 106, and 401 through 
404, extending to depths ranging from about 4.0 to 5.3 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. The approximate boring locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 2.1 through 2.4. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10. Rock cores were described in general accordance with 
the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing was performed in the ten borings 
locations (Boring Nos. 101 through 106 and Boring Nos. 401 through 404) to obtain 
pavement support characteristics of the in-situ soils. The tests were performed using a 
Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone Penetrometer to a depth of about 1.4 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the lower 
rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The upper rod 
holds a 17.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the 
cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone is just below the 
testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts 
and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the strength of the soil at the test 
location.  The number of blows for a minimum of 1-inch penetration is recorded.  The 
process is continued until the desired depth of testing is reached or the full length of the 
lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as millimeters 
per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the DCP Logs, 
Plates C-1.1 through C-10.2.
