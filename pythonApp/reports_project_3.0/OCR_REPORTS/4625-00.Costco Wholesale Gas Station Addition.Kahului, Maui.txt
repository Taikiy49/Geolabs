for the Costco Wholesale Gas Station Addition project located at 540 Haleakala Highway in
the Kahului area on the Island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project

limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The
isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two volcanoes
The project site is located at the northern portion of this gently sloping plain
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on West
Maui It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui
GEOLABS INC
W O 4625 00 Page 5
Haleakala The erosional processes were dominated by the detachment of soil and rock
masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials were transported downslope toward
the Isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reached the stream
in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes became dominant and the sediments
were transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of this situation the
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists
of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the
erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the materials
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial soil deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In
addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older partially
consolidated deposits
Underlying the alluvial soil deposits are overlapping lava from the West Maui and
East Maui Volcanoes The bulk of the Haleakala Shield was built during the late Pliocene
and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu
Volcanic Series During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the lava changed to
very hard thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition These lavas have been grouped
as the Kula Volcanic Series Typically the basalt rock formation consists of thinly to thickly
bedded a a and pahoehoe type lava flows

Shallow Foundations
Our field exploration indicated that the proposed gasoline facility site is generally
underlain by variable fill soils on the order of about 6 to 14 feet thick below the existing
GEOLABS INC
W O 4625 00 Page 9
pavement section Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe
that spread footings may be used for support of the open canopies planned at the site An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the
foundation design of the new open canopies bearing on 12 inches of structural fill material
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Due to the potential for presence of loose soft fill soils at shallow depths as encountered
in our field exploration we recommend that a 12 inch thick layer of structural fill material be
provided below the bottom of footings for uniform support Because the footings for the open
canopies may extend to depths of over 5 to 6 feet deep below the existing pavement surface
we anticipate that cobbles and boulders will likely be encountered at the design footing
subgrade elevations Therefore the 12 inch thick layer of structural fill material recommended
above would also serve to reduce the potential for concentrated loading resulting from footings
bearing on sharp points of the cobbles and boulders The 12 inch thick structural fill layer
below the footings should extend beyond the edges of the footing a minimum of 12 inches
Prior to placement of the structural fill material the bottom of the footing
over excavations should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches where possible
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to not less
than 90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the
over excavated subgrade levels deeper over excavation may be required to expose the
underlying firm material Alternatively the bottom of the over excavation may be compacted
to a firm condition and lined with a layer of filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent prior
to placement of the structural fill
Structural fill required below the footing subgrade should consist of well graded select
granular fill material with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or less Structural fill materials
should meet the requirements for imported fill materials presented in the Site Preparation
section of this report Structural fill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4625 00 Page 10
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We anticipate that the pump island canopies may be subjected to uplift forces created
by wind conditions Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the following 1 dead
weight of the structure 2 weight of backfill material above the footing where applicable and
3 side shear between the sides of the footings and surrounding soils Contribution of dead
weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of 110 pounds per cubic
foot pcf For side shear between the sides of the footings and the adjacent soils a shear
value of 150 psf may be used
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 40
may be used for footings bearing on the 12 inch thick structural fill material Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around footings are well
compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls or below grade
structures should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining or basement
wall foundations or the retaining walls or below grade structures should be designed to resist
the additional structural loads
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below

should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the pump islands
above the underground fuel storage tanks and at the fuel truck dispensing pad Based on our
field exploration the project site is generally underlain by variable fill soils consisting of medium
stiff to very stiff clayey silts over loose to very dense silty basalt gravel extending to depths of
about 7 to 15 feet below the existing pavement surface
Due to the variable types of fill soils encountered near the existing ground surface we
recommend that the concrete slabs be supported on 12 inches of aggregate subbase for
uniform support For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of
250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
aggregate subbase The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregates
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Our laboratory tests indicated that the near surface fill soils exhibit a slight to low
expansion potential when subjected to normal moisture fluctuations To reduce the
potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and
swelling of the near surface soils due to changes in the moisture content we recommend
GEOLABS INC
W O 4625 00 Page 12
that the subgrade soils be properly prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented below and in the Site Preparation section of this report
The soil subgrades below the 12 inch thick aggregate subbase layer should be scarified
to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The prepared
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of aggregate subbase
and concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the subgrade soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or that the
subgrade be prepared again Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should
be backfilled tightly against the slab edge with relatively impervious soils on site soils and
graded to divert water away from the slab edges to reduce the potential for water ponding
around the foundations

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations will result primarily from elastic

of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe

Workmanship

contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes Because the drilled
shaft foundations are designed based on an end bearing component proper
cleaning of the bottom of the drilled shaft hole will be required prior to the placement
of reinforcing cage and concrete

Underground Fuel Storage Tanks
Based on the results of our field exploration we anticipate that the bottom of the new
underground fuel storage tank excavation will likely expose dense basalt boulders to hard
basalt rock formation Therefore we believe that the new underground fuel storage tanks
should be supported on a minimum 6 inch thick layer of granular bedding material The
granular bedding material should consist of open graded granular material such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or similar material The excavated tank subgrade
should be compacted to provide a firm base prior to placement of the bedding material
If soft and or loose subsoils are encountered at the tank subgrade elevation a
stabilization layer should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support and to serve
as a working platform for construction As a guide the stabilization layer may consist of 12 to
18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped with a filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent
The filter fabric should be lapped a minimum of 24 inches at the ends of each roll and along

Site Preparation
Based on the existing site conditions and the proposed design layout we anticipate
only minor grading with cuts and fills on the order of less than about 5 feet will be required

Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It is

design of site grading work
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site Slabs and foundations of the existing structures and pavements
to be demolished should be completely removed Soft weak or yielding areas disclosed
during clearing operations or over excavations resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with well compacted fill Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and
the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with well compacted fill Utilities to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
After clearing and demolition areas beneath the future slabs on grade should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick layer of aggregate

should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the

Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend

recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to

replacement with well compacted fill

soils may be reused as a source of general fill material up to the bottom of the aggregate

Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of

C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths ranging from about
16 5 to 35 5 feet below the existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches in addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
