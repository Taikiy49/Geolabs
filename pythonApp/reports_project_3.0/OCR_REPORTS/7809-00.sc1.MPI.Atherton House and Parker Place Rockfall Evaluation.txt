REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
two primary shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older volcano, Waianae, is 
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, 
during which erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is located on land that was mapped by others as being partially 
underlain by relatively young volcanic products belonging to the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. The volcanic products include localized hard rock lava flows and vent deposits 
that erupted from late stage volcanic vents in the Tantalus and Manoa area. The 
near-surface deposits may include dense basalt rock and cinder materials with derived 
soils.

Atherton House Slope Site 
The subject rock slope spans discontinuously for a distance of about 150 linear feet 
along the western property boundary between the Atherton House and an adjacent church 
property. An existing chain link fence separates the properties and the majority of the rock 
outcrop resides within the MPI property. It should be noted that the existing fence line

The rock slope spanning along the property line traverses from the Atherton front 
yard to the side yard, adjacent to the house, and consists of three distinct “mounds” of rock

For discussion purposes, the length of rock slope was divided into three rock

Driveway Outcrop 
The existing outcrop measures approximately 30 linear feet long along the property 
boundary and extends about 14 linear feet into the Atherton front yard from the 
property boundary. The toe of the rock slope is about 20 to 25 linear feet away from 
the paved driveway edge. The rock outcrop has the appearance of a jumbled pile of 
large, angular basalt blocks ranging in dimension between about 2 and 6 feet and 
includes some smaller accessory cobbles and stones. Based on our observations, 
the individual blocks comprising the outcropping appear to be well keyed and 
nested such that movement of the larger blocks is restricted and the mass appears 
to be firmly held in-place.  
A portion of the rock outcropping resides on the opposite side of the chain link

appear to have a potential to shift and encroach on the church property.  Existing

risk for potential movement, unless disturbed by a large external force.  Based on 
our evaluation, there appears to be very limited potential for a hazardous rockfall 
condition to affect the Atherton House and church property and rockfall hazard 
mitigation measures do not appear necessary at this time. 
However, we observe a single boulder of about 3-feet in dimension at the southern 
tip of the outcropping. The boulder appears to be found in a shallow pocket setting 
on some alluvial soil and small rock fragments that could erode over time and

Observatory Outcrop

Garden Outcrop 
The existing rock outcrop generally consists of a large equidimensional and 
bulbous appearing basalt rock mass spanning about 20 linear feet in length along 
the property boundary. The outcrop extends about 15 linear feet from the property 
boundary towards the house and averages about 17 linear feet in height with 
respect to the lower house patio elevation. The overall rock mass appears to be 
comprised of multiple angular blocks of dense basalt rock that represent in-situ rock 
formation material. Embedded clinker and soil seams were not observed on the 
exposed surfaces of the outcrop at the time of our reconnaissance. 
The rock outcrop was completely encased with dense vine growth at the time of our 
site visit. Therefore, we hand-cleared some of the encasing vine vegetation to 
observe the general character of the lower portions of the outcrop. Based on our 
observations, it appears there are some overhanging blocks of rock that have some 
compromised basal support. The overhanging blocks that we observed appear to 
extend deeper into the interior of the outcrop; therefore, they appear to be held in 
place by the weight of the adjoining and overlying blocks of rock. We observed 
some open rock fractures on the order of about 2 to 3 inches wide between the 
adjacent blocks of rock. We did not observe loose rock material in the exposed 
lower portion of the outcrop.  
We observed an existing Banyan (Ficus) tree growing on top of the rear portion of 
the outcrop adjacent to the fence line. We also observed several sprouts of invasive 
Rubber trees gaining a foothold at scattered locations on top of the outcrop. These 
woody-stem shrubs and trees should be completely cut and poisoned as the 
penetrating growth of the root systems may pry into the existing fractures in the 
rock mass and create rock instability.

Parker Place Slope Site 
A portion of the existing slope appears to consist of a rock cut slope that is 
experiencing signs of erosion and loosening of the rock block components comprising the 
cut slope face. The basalt rock is generally moderately fractured, slightly weathered and 
hard to very hard with a blocky appearance and lack of exposed clinker or embedded soil 
seams. The cut slope stands at about a 0.25H:1V to 0.5H:1V inclination. 
The overall slope is approximately 140 linear feet in length and consists of two 
slope segments of differing rock character. The western slope segment extends 
approximately 80 linear feet along the driveway and contains the steeper and taller rock 
cut slope. The cut slope extends to heights of about 11 to 19 feet above the adjacent 
ground surface fronting the slope. The eastern slope segment extends an additional 
approximately 60 feet in length and consists of an elongated pile of individual nested 
boulders which tapers from a height of about 8 feet to about 3 feet at the eastern end of 
the slope segment. The location of the slope site in relation to the existing driveway and 
parking stalls is shown on Site Plan 2, Plate 3.2.

segment. The boulder pile segment does have a 6-foot chain link fence and lawn area to 
act as a buffer to contain possible shifting rocks away from the driveway; therefore, the 
rock pile segment does not appear to be of concern for a dangerous rockfall hazard.

the Atherton House and Parker Place slope sites, the following discussions are presented

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations of potential rockfall activity only. 
Variation in the surface and subsurface conditions between our observations may occur, 
and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field 
exploration or construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold 
cycles, and erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting 
from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), 
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore,
