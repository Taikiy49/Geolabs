2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava
flows from Koolau Volcano banked against the eroded eastern slope of Waianae
Volcano forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau
The project site is located near the southwestern flank of the Aliamanu vent and
Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series part of the Koolau Volcano
As the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the groundwater
generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low cones
of ash material that have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as
mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream After this phase of volcanic
activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the
next phase of volcanic activity began Then the Salt Lake eruptions began when the
sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its present level The Salt Lake eruptive
event deposited its material over earlier tuffs generated from the older Aliamanu events

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused solely by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that they can only be detected by instruments However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanism the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground
shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The Island of
Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5
however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in

2 5 Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered stiff silty clay soils
overlying dense volcanic tuff formation extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the average
penetration resistance N values of the subsurface materials encountered below the
foundation subgrade level and the geology of the area we believe that the project site
may be classified as a Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock Profile Therefore we believe
that the seismic design of the building structures at the project site may be designed
based on a Soil Profile Type Sc in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building
Code 1997

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration and the design finished floor elevations for the
project we anticipate that the proposed building slabs likely will straddle across the
surface clayey fill soils and dense volcanic tuff formation Our laboratory test results
indicated that the surface clayey fill soils exhibit highly variable swelling potential
ranging from about 3 to 21 percent slight to very high when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of
the on site expansive clay soils and differing subgrade materials we recommend that a
minimum of 8 inches of non expansive select granular fill material be provided below
the slab cushion
In addition we recommend that a cushion fill layer consisting of 4 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the slab for
uniform support and to serve as a capillary break for moisture infiltration A moisture
membrane should be incorporated above the cushion fill layer to reduce the potential for
damage to the moisture sensitive floor coverings by moisture infiltration
In addition to the 8 inch non expansive select granular fill layer we also
recommend that structural slabs be used for the buildings to reduce the potential for
appreciable slab cracking considering the highly variable expansion potential of the
near surface clayey fill soils The structural slabs should be a minimum of 5 inches
thick and reinforced with No 3 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center in both
directions

In lieu of providing the 8 inch non expansive select granular fill layer
consideration was given to chemical stabilization of the expansive soils at the project
site Based on the available information we believe that chemical stabilization using
the Electrochemical Soil Stabilizer EcSS 3000 may be an effective alternative to
reduce the shrink swell effects of the expansive soils on the slabs on grade at the
project site In general the EcSS 3000 chemical stabilization involves the injection of a
diluted chemical diluted in water into the ground to chemically stabilize the expansive
nature of the soils The chemical injection reacts with the clay particles in the soil to
form a soil mass that is less susceptible to shrink swell effects when subjected to
moisture fluctuations Due to the variable depth to the mudrock surface varying from
1 to 6 feet below the ground level we believe that it would be very difficult to implement
the chemical stabilization alternative for this project site Therefore we did not give
further consideration to the use of chemical stabilization using the Electrochemical Soil
Stabilizer ECSS 3000 on this project
Prior to the placement of the select granular fill layer or slab cushion the clayey
subgrades if exposed at the over excavated subgrade level should be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In our
opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the subgrade soils should
reduce the impact of the shrink swell effects on the slab due to potential heaving of the
underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We

General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive select
granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness

90 percent relative compaction The aggregate base and aggregate subbase course
materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel pneumatic tired
rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed for compaction of the fills

3 5 Utility Trench
We envision that some new utility lines and utility connections will be required for
the project In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the pipes for
uniform support Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to
use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to
the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils
or select granular fill materials with a maximum particle size of about 3 inches The
backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new buildings should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs also should be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for appreciable
water infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 extending to depths
between about 5 8 and 20 1 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the volcanic tuff formation encountered at the project site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is
a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the volcanic tuff formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Nine one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface clayey fill soils The results of these
tests are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
An Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample of the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 3
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
