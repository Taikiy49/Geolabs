SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the new Fire Station within Schofield Barracks in the Wahiawa area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the new
Fire Station project within Schofield Barracks These recommendations are intended for
the design of foundations slabs on grade site preparation and pavements only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located at the existing Building 489 within Schofield Barracks
Building 489 is located at the northeast intersection of Kolekole Avenue and Heard
Avenue in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii We understand that a new
two company fire station is planned to be constructed at the project site
The new fire station will include stalls for housing apparatus office storage
mechanical room and dormitory space In addition new parking areas are planned at
the project site In general we understand that Portland cement concrete will be used for
pavements supporting vehicular traffic consisting of heavy fire trucks and tankers
Based on the topographic survey the existing ground surface elevation ranges
between about 866 and to 869 feet Mean Sea Level MSL We anticipate that minor
site grading work will be required to achieve the design finished grades

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The
dips of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees near horizontal The lava flows
on the plateau have weathered to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the
red colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and
Kaukonahua are a result of this weathering The brown and reddish brown soils generally
consist of ancient alluvial deposits and residual soils overlying the weathered rock

Kolekole Avenue and Heard Avenue within Schofield Barracks in the Wahiawa area on the

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that the new structure be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread
and or continuous footings bearing on the clayey silts encountered at the project site The
footings should extend down a minimum depth of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade In general the bottom of footing elevations should be compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing elevations
the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm or
dense materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with select granular
fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In lieu_ of
backfilling the over excavation with compacted fills the footings may extend down to bear
directly on the firm or dense materials
Based on our laboratory tests the clayey silt soils at the site exhibit a slight
potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting
from shrinking and swelling of the clayey silt soils we recommend that the subgrade
soils underneath the slab areas be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In addition the slab subgrades should be
capped with at least 6 inches of non expansive select granular material The
non expansive select granular fill material should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
recommendations for design are presented in the following sections of this report

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous
footings be used to support the new structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the
clayey silt soils encountered at the site This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
In general footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches In addition
footings should be embedded a minimum depth of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade Foundations located next to utility trenches and or easements should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or the footing should be embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement
The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered
at the footing subgrade level the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation should be backfilled
with select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the footings may be extended down to bear directly on the firm or dense
materials
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the
recompacted clayey silts may be on the order of about 1 inch or less We estimate that
differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on the recompacted clayey
silts to be on the order of about 0 5 inches

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the clayey silt soils Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive pressure resistance

water should be diverted away from the foundations

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our laboratory test results the clayey silt soils encountered at the site
exhibit a slight potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey silt soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In addition
the slab subgrade area should be capped with at least 6 inches of non expansive select
granular fill materials The non expansive select granular fill materials should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The non expansive select granular fill materials should be placed as soon as
possible after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the above discussions The non expansive select granular fill materials and
the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or the subgrades

In general the excavated on site soils may be used as general fill up to the bottom
the non expansive select granular fill or aggregate subbase course layers provided that
deleterious materials vegetation are removed and over sized materials greater than
3 inches in maximum dimension are screened Imported fill materials required for the
project construction should consist of non expansive select granular material such as
crushed coral and basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of one percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should
be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site
for the intended use
Aggregate subbase and base course materials should consist of crushed basaltic
aggregates and should conform to Subsections 703 17 and 703 06 of the State of Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness

select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The aggregate subbase and
base course materials should be moisture conditioned to near the optimum moisture

project In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for parking areas will consist
primarily of passenger vehicles and light to medium trucks We anticipate that the vehicle
loading for certain areas will consist of heavy trucks such as the fire trucks and tankers

3 5 Underground Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections will be required for the
proposed project In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches
of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used to support the utility lines
The initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should also consist of free
draining backfill such as open graded gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use this free draining material to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with
particles less than 6 inches in maximum dimension or select granular fill materials The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in

3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structure should be sloped to shed surface
water runoff away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding
It is also recommended that gutter systems be installed around the new structure and that
the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations
should also be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for
water infiltration into the subsurface soils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water
infiltration in to the subsurface In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about
26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples of
the surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test
results are presented on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a selected sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
