PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site generally is located near the intersection of Hobron Lane and 
Lipeepee Street in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that a fire hydrant adjacent to an 
existing wood power pole, designated as P9, was hit by a car in 2017, causing the 
surrounding soils to become saturated and resulting in the pullout of the deadman on the 
guy anchors.  Since the deadman for the guy anchors could not be set due to the saturated 
soils, we understand that it is desired to replace power pole P9 with a new power pole 
supported on a single cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft foundation. 
In addition to P9, two other nearby power poles, designated as P8 and P10, are 
planned to be replaced. Due to lower load demands onto the foundations for power poles 
P8 and P10, a drilled shaft foundation consisting of directly embedding the steel pole into

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and

forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano reached 
the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its geomorphology 
and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea 
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The 
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea, which 
are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in this 
area is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level on Oahu. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise 
in sea level occurred. During that rise, the valleys in the project area were drowned. In 
the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous 
and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal 
environments resulting in thick deposits of loose and/or soft sediments along the coast in 
the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways. 
In the early part of this century, the Waikiki area consisted of low, marshy areas. 
As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized, man-made fills were 
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons. The fill was placed upon lagoonal 
deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments. The lower clayey material was 
deposited during periodic influxes of non-marine alluvial debris, which apparently 
smothered the coral reef development. Land development and reclamation projects within 
the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki area to its present form. Many of the resulting 
fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads.

replacement power poles P8 and P10 may be supported by a deep foundation system

shaft foundations will be embedded beneath the ground surface by about 1 foot and will

Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the drilled shaft, allowable deflection at the top of the 
drilled shaft, and the induced moment in the drilled shaft. The lateral load analyses were 
performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a microcomputer 
adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation program originally 
developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program solves for deflection and 
bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral loads as a function of depth. The 
analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. 
The lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various 
depths.

Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered at the 
site. The total settlement of the drilled shaft is estimated to be less than 0.5 inches. We 
believe that a significant portion of the settlement will be elastic and should occur as the 
loads are applied.

Soft/Loose Soils 
As mentioned above, the project site at power pole P8 and P10 is likely underlain 
by surface fills over lagoonal deposits, consisting of very loose silty sands and 
sandy gravel and very soft sandy clays, extending down to about 23.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface.  In order to provide safe access by the workers and 
to reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, permanent steel casing 
should be provided for the drilled shaft foundation construction work. 
Workmanship 
The load-bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper 
construction techniques are important.  The contractor should exercise care in 
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended.  Placement of 
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required due to 
the groundwater table anticipated within the drilled shaft depth at the site.  The 
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an 
upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used for 
the concreting operation.  The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to 
reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix.  If 
pre-cast concrete pole bases are used, the gap between the pole base and the 
surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete. 
A specialty subcontractor, experienced in the construction of the cased drilled shaft 
foundation system (minimum five projects), should perform the drilled shaft 
installation. Due to the urban nature of the project site, it should be noted that hard 
drilling, such as cobbles, boulders, concrete, and other obstructions, may be 
encountered locally within the fill materials at the site. In addition, coralline cobbles 
and/or coral formation will be encountered during drilling of the drilled shafts. 
Due to the specialized nature of the drilled shaft foundation construction, 
observation of the drilled shaft foundations should be designated as a “Special 
Inspection” item. Therefore, observation of the drilled shaft installation operations 
by Geolabs (Special Inspector) is necessary to confirm our design assumptions 
and should be designated as a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with 
Section 1704 of IBC 2012.
