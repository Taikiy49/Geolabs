recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below the
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene
Epoch The dips of the lava beds are near horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees from
horizontal The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering to depths
of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil
This in situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that
grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks Spheroidally weathered
boulders are typical in the Schofield Plateau The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with depth The project site is located on the Schofield Plateau and is
underlain by residual soils and saprolite

Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered fill materials
consisting of stiff to hard silty clay underlain by stiff to very stiff silty clay and clayey silt
extending to the maximum depths explored of about 51 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Groundwater was not encountered in our borings during the time of our
exploration
Based on our field exploration the site is underlain by stiff to hard fill materials
extending to depths of about 20 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface Due to the
variation in consistency of the fill materials we recommend that the bottoms of footing and
the tank floor be over excavated a minimum of 24 inches The resulting over excavation
should be backfilled with the select borrow compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Based on the laboratory test results we recommend that the slab subgrade be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction After preparing the slab subgrade the subgrade soils should be capped with
a minimum of 24 inches of compacted select borrow below the reservoir slab areas This
serves to reduce the potential for distress to the future slab on grade resulting from
swelling of the near surface soils and to provide more uniform support for the tank slab
The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of
select borrow and slab concrete

3 1 Reservoir Foundations
Based on our field exploration the project is underlain by stiff to hard fill materials
extending to depths of about 20 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface Due to the
variation in the consistency of the fill materials we recommend that the bottom of footing
elevations be over excavated a minimum of 24 inches The resulting over excavation
should be backfilled with select borrow compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction The select borrow should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
Based on the over excavation recommendations we believe that shallow
foundations may be used for support of the proposed new reservoir An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of
shallow foundations bearing on the compacted select borrow over the recompacted fills at
the project site This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
As previously mentioned the new reservoir site has settled about 3 8 to 5 8 inches
due to placement of the fills Based on the settlement monitoring program the settlement
have remained constant We believe that new settlements at the site will be due to the
loads imposed by the new reservoir Therefore if foundations are designed and
constructed in accordance with our recommendations we estimate total settlements of

3 3 Settlement Monitoring
As previously mentioned the existing Reservoir No 1 has settled between 7 8 and
inches from November 1993 to February 1998 Due to the magnitude of settlement
that has occurred for Reservoir No 1 we recommend that a settlement monitoring
program be implemented after construction of the new reservoir To monitor the actual
settlement we recommend that three settlement points be evenly spaced around the
reservoir perimeter footing The settlement points should be read optically by a
qualified professional surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs for
review in a timely manner We recommend that two readings minimum 24 hours apart
for each settlement gauge be taken after construction of the reservoir to establish a
baseline Subsequent readings of the settlement gauges should be taken during filling
of the reservoir with water
We recommend that settlement readings be taken when the reservoir is one third
full two thirds full and when the reservoir is completely full The settlement gauges
should be monitored for a week after each filling stage After the reservoir is full the
settlement gauges should be monitored until the settlement has remained constant We
estimate about three weeks of settlement monitoring may be required after the reservoir
has been filled completely

3 4 Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the design finish floor elevation of the new
reservoir we anticipate that the site grading work will consist of minor cuts and fills As
previously mentioned a 3 to 4 foot high surcharge was placed at the site therefore
removal of the surcharge will be required for construction of the project
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath future slabs and footings should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 24 inch thick compacted select
borrow layer below the slab concrete and footings Prior to placement of the select borrow
layer the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and recompacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after
preparation of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close
the cracks and recompacted Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the
soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
The on site soils generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of
general fill material Imported fill material if required should consist of non expansive
imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a

3 5 Service Road Pavements
Asphalt concrete pavements are anticipated for the service roads around the
perimeter of the new reservoir and between the existing and new reservoir In addition it
is anticipated that the service roadways will be subjected to light vehicular traffic
Assuming that the pavement subgrade will consist of the clayey soils encountered
in our borings CBR value of 16 and a swell of 2 0 percent and the vehicular traffic will be
relatively light the following pavement section may be used for preliminary budgeting
purposes until the finish subgrade material is verified and laboratory testing of this material
is conducted
Asphaltic Concrete Pavements
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
It should be noted that the clayey soils have a low to moderate potential for swelling
when subjected to a fluctuation in moisture Typically pavement subgrades are scarified
moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction To reduce the potential for swelling of the pavement
subgrade soils we recommend that the pavement subgrades be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on
the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
above section The recommended section considers only light traffic conditions and
assumes that good drainage will be provided adjacent to paved areas

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of about 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the in situ soils The results of the test are summarized on
Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear test are presented on Plate B 3
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 with time rates of consolidation was
performed on a sample of the soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered The results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 5
