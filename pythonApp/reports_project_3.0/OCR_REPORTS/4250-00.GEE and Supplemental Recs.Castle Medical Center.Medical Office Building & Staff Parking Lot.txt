REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava banking against its
eastern flank
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Basaltic rock built by the extrusion
of lava of the Honolulu Volcanic Series are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense
vesicular basalt rock formation with inter bedded thin clinker layers in situ chemical
weathering of the lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering
process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth
In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical
weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth
In addition sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental glaciation
As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such
that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and
receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the
present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and vaileys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level
W O 4250 00 Page 5
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and the soil materials are
transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general stream
flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit large volumes
of water for very short duration Because of this transport of sediments is intermittent and
the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders
cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change these sediment
loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin to
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of
the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously
erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This
continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older
alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time in addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the
drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils
Considering that the drilled shafts are designed based on adhesion between the
shaft and the surrounding soils we recommend that an uplift load capacity of
180 kips be used to resist uplift loads The uplift load capacity provided include the
weight of the drilled shaft The structural capacity of the shaft member in tension
should be checked by the project structural engineer

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The following
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the frictional
resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soils and the end bearing
Therefore proper construction techniques are important The contractor should
exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the drilled holes
The drilled shafts are designed to be embedded into the stiff clayey soils encountered
in the borings Some boulders should be expected in the clayey soil horizon given the
geologic conditions at the site The drilled shaft contractor will need to demonstrate
that the proposed drilling equipment and coring tools will be capable of installing the
drilled shafts to the recommended depths and dimensions by performance of a trial
shaft program
Due to the high structural capacities recommended for the drilled shafts the sidewalls
of the drilled shaft holes will need to be rifled with grooves to provide the necessary
contact and side shaft resistance assumed in our analyses The grooves should be
at least 2 inches deep by 3 inches wide and should run spirally along the shaft
circumference at a pitch of about 12 inches The rifling procedure will be required
throughout the depth of the drilled shaft
Our field exploration generally encountered medium stiff to hard clayey soils We
anticipate that some minor sloughing and squeezing of the medium stiff clayey soils
may occur The need for temporary casing of the drilled holes should be determined
in the field on a shaft by shaft basis We recommend that unit prices for temporary
casing be obtained during bidding in the event that temporary casing will be
necessary based on the field conditions encountered
W O 4250 00 Page 12
Based on the estimated lengths of the drilled shafts groundwater is not expected in
the drilled holes during the shaft installation In general placement of concrete may
be accomplished by free fall methods provided that the concrete is placed in a
suitable manner that reduces the potential for segregation of aggregates from the
concrete mix However due to factors such as seasonal rainfall and perched water
groundwater may be encountered in the drilled holes In addition there is the
potential for water seepage into the drilled holes considering the relatively wet
environment in this area If groundwater is encountered in the drilled holes then
concrete should be placed by tremie methods A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch
slump range concrete mix should be used to provide close contact between the
drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed promptly after
drilling within 24 hours to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled
holes
It is imperative that a representative of Geolabs Hawaii be present at the site to
observe the drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction Drilling by
methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended because use of drilling fluids may
reduce the frictional resistance between the drilled shaft and surrounding soils As
previously mentioned a portion of load bearing capacity of the drilled shaft is from end
bearing therefore it is essential that the bottom of the drilled hole be relatively free
of loose materials prior to placement of concrete
Trial Shaft Program
A trial shaft program is highly recommended for this project considering the high
structural loads Considering the diameter and structural load capacities of the
drilled shafts we recommend that a trial shaft program including the performance
of an instrumented load test be undertaken to fulfill the following objectives
e To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the drilled shafts to the depths required
To confirm or modify the estimated tip elevations of the drilled shafts
W O 4250 00 Page 13
e To assess the contractor s method of rifling the sidewalls of the drilled shaft
holes prior to placing concrete
To assess the contractor s method of placing and extracting the temporary
casing if required for the drilled shaft
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the trial shaft program consist of
drilling a 36 inch diameter trial shaft extending to a depth of about 32 feet below the
ground surface The location of the trial shaft should be near but outside of the
building foundations After drilling of the trial shaft the trial shaft should be
structurally reinforced and instrumented with embedment strain gages for load
testing purposes The embedment strain gages should be placed starting from the
bottom at an elevation of about 4 feet above the tip of the trial shaft and
subsequently at 8 foot intervals
After installation of the trial shaft a load test should be conducted on the reinforced
trial shaft The drilled shaft load test should be performed in general accordance
with the Quick Load Test Method of ASTM Test Designation D 1143 The trial shaft
should be loaded to an ultimate load of about 1 000 kips and should be held for a
minimum of 8 hours
We recommend that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present during the
trial shaft program to evaluate the contractor s method of drilled shaft installation
and to evaluate the subsurface materials encountered In addition Geolabs Hawaii
should observe the instrumented load test on the reinforced trial shaft It should be
noted that the drilled shaft design was developed from our analysis using the field
exploration data Therefore observation of the drilled shaft installation operations
by Geolabs Hawaii is a vital part of the foundation design to confirm the design
assumptions

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should
not be allowed to compact the on site clayey soils General fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established
in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The non expansive select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Select
borrow subbase and aggregate base course materials below pavement areas should
be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented above Excess water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be
provided as soon as the cut and fill slopes are completed to reduce the potential for
erosion of the finished slopes

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of these
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the soils
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test are presented
on Plate B 3
One unconfined compression test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics Results of the
test are presented on Piate B 4
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate compressibility characteristics of the on site soils Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate B 5
One California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk sample
collected from the near surface soils for pavement design Results of the CBR test are
presented on Plate B 6
