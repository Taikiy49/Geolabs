This letter report presents the results of the field infiltration testing performed at the 
Kamehameha Schools Community Learning Center, Maili – Phase 1 project site in the 
Maili area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The findings presented herein are intended to assist in the design of the storm 
water runoff detention and disposal systems only. The findings presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves development and construction of a community educational 
facility for Kamehameha Schools.  As part of the development, it will be necessary to 
implement Best Management Practices (BMP) for storm water runoff generated at the site.  
Generally, these BMP would include features such as detention basins and disposal units 
such as infiltration areas. 
In accordance with current City and County of Honolulu requirements, information 
from subsurface infiltration testing is required to aid in the design of storm water detention 
and disposal systems.  A lower factor of safety may be applied to the design when in-situ 
testing is performed rather than using the presumptive values provided in the City and 
County Storm Water BMP Guide.  To evaluate the subsurface percolation characteristics, 
we drilled six boreholes to depths ranging between about 2.9 and 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface and conducted falling head infiltration tests in the borings.  The results 
from these tests are presented in Appendix B.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE 
The purpose of our exploration was to perform field infiltration testing at selected 
locations within the site to obtain infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials. 
This information may be used to assist in the design of the storm water runoff disposal 
system. Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated 
October 15, 2013. The scope of our work included the following tasks and work efforts: 
1. 
Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig, water tank, and 
two operators to and from the project site. 
2. 
Drilling of six borings at selected locations within the project site to depths 
ranging between about 2.9 and 5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
3. 
Performance of falling head infiltration tests in the drilled borings.  Each test 
consisted of 4 one-hour infiltration cycles. 
4. 
Coordination of the field exploration and performance of the field infiltration 
testing by our geologist. 
5. 
Analyses of the field data to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the 
near-surface materials for preliminary design of the storm water runoff 
management systems.

Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and 
presenting our findings and recommendations.

Coordination of our work on the project by our senior engineer. 
8. 
Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our 
principal engineer. 
9. 
Miscellaneous work efforts, such as drafting, word processing, and clerical

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The shallow subsurface infiltration conditions at the site were explored by the 
drilling and testing of six borings, designated as Boring Nos. P-1 through P-6, to depths 
ranging between about 2.9 and 5.0 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The test locations were 
selected by the Project Civil Engineer as the approximate locations of storm water 
management systems, such as detention and infiltration basins. 
The borings generally encountered a surface layer of brown clay soils ranging from 
about 0.5 to about 3 feet in thickness overlying hard and dense tannish white coralline 
materials.  The surface clay soils were not encountered in one of the borings.

Groundwater was encountered only in Boring No. P-5 (original location) at a depth of

FIELD INFILTRATION TESTING 
Six field infiltration tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered at the boring locations. The 
infiltration tests were performed in Boring Nos. P-1 through P-6; and the approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  These tests were performed in 
general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland 
Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009).  
These procedures are consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be 
considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering a boring to the selected test 
depth, generally about 5 feet below the existing ground surface except for Boring No. P-5, 
which was drilled to a depth of about 2.9 feet below the existing ground surface due to the 
presence of groundwater at a depth of 3.0 feet at that location.  Upon reaching the test 
depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow 
infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head 
infiltration tests were performed to determine the average infiltration rates of the underlying 
subsurface materials.  Each test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches 
of water and taking periodic readings over a one hour trial period.  The infiltration rates are 
then calculated based on the result of the fourth and last trial for each test location.  The 
results are summarized in the following table. Detailed information of the tests is presented 
in Appendix B.

It should be noted that the testing conducted in Boring No. P-4 tended to 
increase with each trial; and, it is believed that this is indicative of a subsurface 
discontinuity, such as a crack or void, which was being flushed out by the infiltrating 
water.  It should also be noted that the infiltration values indicated are the rate of 
infiltration through the soil exposed in the bottom of the tested borehole and reduces the 
lateral spreading of the water in the subsurface materials. 
Based on the results of the field infiltration testing, the average infiltration rates at 
the majority of the site indicate that retention systems, such as infiltration basins, 
infiltration trenches and similar methods of storm water management are feasible for 
this development.  No infiltration was measured in Boring No. P-6 due to the relatively 
high clay content of the weathered coral in the boring.  The addition of infiltration 
chambers to such systems would increase storage capacity and provide for additional 
usable surface area within the development.
