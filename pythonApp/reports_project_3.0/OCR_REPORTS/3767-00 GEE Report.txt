INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Piikoi Chevron Service Station project located at the
intersection of South King and Piikoi Streets in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation site grading and
excavation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to demolish the existing Chevron Service Station and to construct a
new service station at the intersection of South King and Piikoi Streets in Honolulu on the
island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided we understand that the
existing convenience store pump islands pavements and underground fuel storage
tanks will be demolished in order to construct the new facility Ve also understand that
the new facility will consist of a one story building several pump islands a car wash
facility and three underground fuel storage tanks side by side
Detailed grading plans for the project were not available at the time this report was
prepared However we anticipate that the proposed new structures will be constructed
near the existing grades Therefore we envision that site grading for the proposed
construction will involve minor cuts and fills on the order of less than 2 feet relative to the
existing ground surface

order to remove the underground storage tank structures and install the new underground

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against it
These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3767 00 Page 5
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulat
ed terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The proposed project site is generally underlain by very stiff clayey soils over
medium dense to dense sand over coral detritus formation to the maximum depths drilled
of about 9 to 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the results of our
field exploration and laboratory testing we believe that a shallow foundation system
bearing on the near surface very stiff clayey soils or compacted fills may be used to
support the proposed structures
Laboratory test results indicate that the near surface soils exhibit slight to
moderate swelling and shrinking potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore we recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on at least
6 inches of non expansive select granular fill material to reduce the effects of shrink swell
potential of the slightly to moderately expansive near surface soils

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used to support the proposed one story building food mart car wash facility and pump
island canopy An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the
design of footings bearing on the very stiff near surface soils and or compacted fill
material The allowable bearing pressure recommended is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as temporary wind and or
seismic forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade
Where located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the footings should
extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom
of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the very stiff in situ soils or compacted fills as recommended herein to
be less than one inch total with differential settlements on the order of inch
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3767 00 Page 8
For footings bearing on the in situ soils or new engineered fills the bottoms of the
footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Any soft and or loose
materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
select granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the spread footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of
footings A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings
and the supporting materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f
for footings embedded in the in situ soil These values assume that the concrete for
footings is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be
neglected
We anticipate that the proposed structures such as the canopy for the pump
islands will be subjected to uplift forces Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of
the following 1 dead weight of the structures 2 weight of backfill material above the
footing where applicable and 3 side shear between the sides of the footings and
surrounding soils Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a
unit weight of 110 p c f For side shear between the sides of the footings and the
adjacent soils a shear value of 200 p s f may be used
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the competency of the
bearing materials and embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures such as the one story building car wash facility and pump islands The near
surface soils encountered at the site exhibit slight to moderate expansion potential when
subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content Therefore to reduce the potential of
structural distress to lightly loaded slab on grade construction resulting from swelling of
the on site slightly to moderately expansive soils we recommend that the subgrade soils
be properly prepared and a minimum of 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill
material be provided below concrete slabs on grade
The subgrade to receive the non expansive select granular fill should be scarified
to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The 6 inch non expansive select fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 to
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve All select granular fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by
the cushion fill or base course
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3767 00 Page 10
For more uniform support of interior slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic such as
for the one story building food mart we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of
cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 6 gradation be placed
below the slab above the 6 inch thick select granular fill The cushion fill layer may also
serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic
membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine
sand should be placed above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the
membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete The sand should be moistened
slightly prior to concrete placement Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet
vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as at the car wash
facility and pump islands we recommend that a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction be provided below the slabs in lieu of the
4 inch thick cushion layer The 2 inch moist fine sand and vapor barrier may be omitted
for these slabs The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For design of structural slabs a
modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for
the compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across
the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs
adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 6 inch thick layer of select granular material or select borrow
subbase be provided below the concrete walkway slab Contraction joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right
angle intersections

Underground Fuel Storage Tanks
Based on the results of our exploration we anticipate the bottom of the new
underground fuel storage tanks to consist of medium dense sand or dense coral
detritus formation Therefore we believe that the new underground fuel storage tanks
may be supported on a minimum 6 inch thick open graded granular material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation sand or similar material to serve as
bedding material The excavated subgrade should be compacted to provide a firm base
prior to placement of the bedding material If soft loose soils are encountered at the tank
subgrade elevation they should be over excavated to expose firm ground and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the dead weight of the tank
structure and the weight of the backfill material above the tank structure where
applicable Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit
weight of 110 p c f above the groundwater table

during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of
2 inches into the subgrade below the aggregate base course to reduce migration of
landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we recommend a_ subdrain system be constructed adjacent to
paved landscape areas
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines would be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 6 gradation be used under the pipes
The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills
such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or the
on site soils The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3767 00 Page 18
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slabs and footing
foundations
