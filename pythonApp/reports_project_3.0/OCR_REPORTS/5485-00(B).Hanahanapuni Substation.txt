SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the substation site generally encountered residual soil
extending to the maximum explored depth of 36 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The residual soil generally consisted of very soft to very hard silty clay and clayey silt with
traces of highly weathered gravel In one of the borings we encountered a 2 5 foot thick
surface fill layer consisting of very stiff silty clay We encountered groundwater in the
borings at depths ranging from about 17 6 to 24 8 feet below the existing ground surface
at the time of our field exploration These groundwater levels correspond to elevations
ranging from about 449 3 to 450 feet Mean Sea Level
Based on our field exploration and relatively light foundation loading anticipated we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footing foundations and _ concrete
slabs on grade may be used to support the new electrical substation structures and
equipment The existing subgrade soils below foundations should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design
of footing foundations bearing on compacted fill or recompacted on site materials In
addition footings for the structures should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below
the lowest adjacent exterior grade
Due to the presence of soft residual deposits we anticipate that significant
settlements may occur due to loading from fill materials greater than 3 feet thick
Therefore fill heights should be limited to 3 feet Should it be necessary to place fills
greater than 3 feet at the project site we recommend establishing a settlement monitoring
program to monitor the ground settlements in these fill areas In order to reduce the
magnitude of post construction settlements a settlement waiting period of about 3 to
6 months after placement of the fills should be required for fills greater than 3 feet
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed discussions and
specific site preparation recommendations

1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Hanahanapuni Substation project in the Lihue Koloa Forest Reserve
area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations slabs on grade and _ site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project consists of a new electrical substation for the Kauai Island Utility
Cooperative in Hanahanapuni on the Island of Kauai Hawaii We understand that the
foundations for the new substation will be similar to the Kukuiula Substation We
anticipate that the new substation will be approximately 228 feet by 188 feet in plan
dimensions The substation will contain a power transformer circuit breakers an
A frame structure an operating bus and voltage regulators In addition we anticipate
the construction of a control building about 20 feet by 20 feet The new substation will
be built to accommodate future electrical equipment The topographic survey plan of the
new substation site is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
We understand that the estimated substation loading is up to about 45 tons We
anticipate that the majority of the equipment will bear on concrete footing and pad
foundations

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this
main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon
Volcanic Series These lavas consist of four distinct formations Napali Olokele
Haupu and Makaweli These formations are comprised of thin bedded a a and
pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with
the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pliestocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level of Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenious sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is mainly composed of silty material

basaltic rock formation with depth

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by a
very soft to very hard residual soil deposit extending to the maximum depth explored of
36 5 feet below the existing ground surface We encountered a 2 5 foot thick surface fill
layer in one of the borings We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths
ranging from about 17 6 to 24 8 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of the
field exploration
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site and the relatively
light foundation loading anticipated we believe that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings and concrete slabs on grade bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fill materials may be used to support the
new electrical substation structures and equipment An allowable bearing pressure of up
to 1 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footing foundations
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills placed to achieve the
design finished grades The existing subgrade soils below foundations should be
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
Due to the presence of soft residual soils we anticipate that significant
settlements may occur due to loading from fill materials greater than 3 feet in thickness
Therefore fill heights should be limited to 3 feet Should it be necessary to place fills
greater than 3 feet at the project site we recommend establishing a settlement
monitoring program to monitor the ground settlements in these fill areas In order to
reduce the magnitude of post construction settlements a settlement waiting period of
about 3 to 6 months after placement of the fills should be required for fills greater than
3 feet Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering

3 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and relatively
light foundation loading anticipated we believe that shallow spread and or continuous
footings may be used to support the new electrical substation structures An allowable
bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design of shallow foundations
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills placed to achieve the
design finished grades This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
For footings bearing on the medium stiff on site soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and recompacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with the on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the bottom of footing may extend down to the underlying competent
materials For footings bearing on new compacted fills the bottom of the footing
excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
In general the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or
on sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of
the slope Footings located adjacent to planned or existing retaining walls should be

3 4 Utility Trench
New utility lines may be required In general for support of the utility lines we
recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the pipes The initial trench backfill up to about
1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes
from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular material to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes We do not recommend using the on site
clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around the utility pipes
In general we recommend providing a minimum cover of 3 feet between the top
of the utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate compaction of the trench backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 1 foot above the pipes to the
finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or select granular
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to
compact the materials Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to
compact the backfill material considering the clayey nature of the on site soils
3 5 Drainage
The finished grades outside the foundations and slabs should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away
from the slabs and foundations

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths ranging from
about 21 5 to 36 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck
mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The tests
results are summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results
are summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 2
Two Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
in situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 3 and B 4
One Consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered The test
results are presented on Plate B 5
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 6
