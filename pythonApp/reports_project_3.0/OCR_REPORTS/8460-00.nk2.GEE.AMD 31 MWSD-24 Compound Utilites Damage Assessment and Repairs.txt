SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of 
about 7 to 9 inches of PCC pavement underlain by 3 to 20 inches of the basaltic base 
material. Beneath the pavement structure, fill material was encountered in some of the 
borings extending to depths of about 3 to 6.5 feet. The fill generally consisted of medium 
stiff to stiff elastic silt and sandy clay, very loose to loose silty/poorly-graded gravel, and 
loose to medium dense silty/poorly-graded sand. Underlying the fill, alluvium consisting 
of soft to stiff fat clay and elastic silt was encountered extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 11.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In Boring Nos. 7 through 
9 (near the central portion of the site), lagoonal deposits were encountered below the 
alluvium at depths of about 5.5 to 9 feet. The lagoonal deposits generally consisted of 
loose to medium dense silty sand and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 
11.5 feet.  
Based on our literature review, field observations, and DCP testing, it appears that 
the existing exposed waterline is underlain by relatively stiff and/or loose to medium 
dense materials in the upper 2 to 3 feet, followed by very stiff or medium dense to dense 
materials extending to the maximum depth explored of about 9.9 feet below the existing 
subgrade surface. 
We encountered groundwater at depths of approximately 8.7 to 9.8 feet below the 
existing ground surface in our drilled borings at the time of our field exploration, except 
for Boring No. 1. The groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to elevations 
of approximately +0.7 to +1.7 feet MSL. Due to the proximity of the project site to the 
Pacific Ocean, groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations, storm 
surge conditions, seasonal precipitation, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and 
other factors. 
In general, the height of the voids measured in our drilled borings directly beneath 
the PCC pavement was greatest near the location of the water main break – about 2 to 
3 inches. The measurable size of the void decreased with increasing distance from the 
location of the water main break. Voids were not observed in three of the furthest borings. 
A Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) survey was performed by others to aid in determining 
the extent of the washed-out areas beneath the concrete slabs. However, we understand 
that the survey did not produce any usable data. Therefore, the size and extent of the 
voids beneath the PCC pavement have been estimated solely on the observed surface 
cracking and the observed voids in our thirteen drilled borings. The remediation contractor 
should consider the above methods for evaluating the extent and size of the void(s) when 
bidding on the project.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed MWSD-24 Compound Utilities Damage Assessment and 
Repairs, B373 project (Contract No. N62478-18-D-5030) at the Marine Corps Base 
Hawaii (MCBH) in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our field 
exploration and engineering analyses for the proposed project. These recommendations 
are intended to evaluate design alternatives for the remediation of the existing concrete 
slabs that were undermined by a previous waterline break. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located near Building 388 at the MCBH facility on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. An existing 12-inch waterline broke within the MWSD-24 Compound in 
June 2021. The pressure of the waterline break washed out the base course under the 
adjacent pavement to the west of the break, and likely washed-out additional base course 
throughout the site due to the extensive cracking observed on the surface of the Portland 
cement concrete (PCC) pavement.  
We understand that an investigation is desired to determine the extent of the base 
course washout and determine the best repair methods. A Ground Penetrating 
Radar (GPR) survey was performed by others to aid in determining the extent of the 
washed-out areas and voids beneath the concrete slabs. However, we understand that 
the survey did not produce any usable data.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that a minimally invasive 
remediation method, such as pressure grouting or probing and grouting, is desired for the 
project. We also anticipate that the existing exposed section of the waterline will be 
backfilled, and a new section of PCC pavement (matching the thickness of the existing

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical engineering analysis are the subsurface materials 
encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, 
and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the 
following sections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site and the 
surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau Volcano lavas banking against its eastern flank. The Koolau Volcano 
reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The project site is on the northeast flank of Koolau Volcano, which has eroded 
away. After the main shield building phase and a long period of erosion, volcanic activity 
was renewed on the Koolau side with the extrusion of lavas of the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. These rocks are generally characterized by tuff and cinder cones in the 
southeastern part of the Koolau dome. The site is situated to the west of Puu Hawaiiloa, 
which is a cinder cone of the Honolulu Volcanic Series.

Possible Extents of Undermined PCC Pavement

within the MWSD-24 Compound, causing the aggregate base course to wash out from 
beneath the existing PCC pavement. The extent of the observed cracking at the

3.2.1 Alternative No. 1 
This alternative consists of removing the existing pavement that is underlain by 
voids and reconstructing a new pavement section. The pavement removal should 
start at the open pit location and work outwards to expose the underlying voids 
until there are no voids below the existing pavement. The aggregate base course 
material is prepared and compacted prior to PCC pavement installation. This 
alternative is the most disruptive remediation measure to the Compound’s 
operation. 
3.2.2 Alternative No. 2 
This alternative consists of probing and grouting below PCC pavement using a 
chemical grout product. Chemical grout is advantageous when grout permeation 
is needed through soils. However, the existing voids beneath the PCC pavement 
are above the base material layer. As previously mentioned, the locations of the 
voids are near existing underground utility trenches, which are normally backfilled 
with permeable material (open-graded gravel). Therefore, the chemical grout 
product may permeate through the underlying base and backfill material. Chemical 
grout is more costly compared to cementitious grout.   
3.2.3 Alternative No. 3 
This alternative consists of probing and grouting below the PCC pavement using 
a non-shrink sand-cement grout mixture. The grout mixture can be made less fluid 
and more viscous than chemical grout and, therefore, would be less likely to 
permeate through the underlying base and backfill material. The contractor should 
utilize a grout mixture of sufficient viscosity to fill the voids beneath the PCC 
pavement without incurring significant grout loss volumes. 
Four-inch diameter probe holes are typically drilled in a grid pattern with spacing 
of about 5 feet center-to-center. The probe holes would extend to just below the 
bottom of the PCC pavement. After the probe holes are drilled, a non-shrink

3.3 
Existing Waterline 
We understand that the existing 12-inch waterline pipe in the open pit area may be 
underlain by two crossing pipes below the existing waterline. Since the crossing pipes 
exert a point loading below the 12-inch waterline above, the excessive loading to the 
12-inch waterline pipe may have been the cause of the waterline distress. We recommend 
that the two crossing lines be lowered below the 12-inch waterline. In addition, the 12-inch 
waterline should be concrete jacketed at the crossing locations.

3.4.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared. Demolished man-made debris, surface vegetation, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill 
and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm material and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The 
excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in 
landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and demolition, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth 
of about 8 inches (where possible), moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to 
the maximum dry density.  
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again, as described above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and replacing these areas 
with engineered fill. A Geolabs field representative should evaluate the need for 
over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
3.4.2 Fills and Backfills 
We envision that backfill material will need to be imported for the project. 
Non-expansive, select granular fill materials should consist of crushed coral or basalt. 
Select granular fills should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater 
than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have 
a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential 
of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs

minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate subbase materials should be

exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be 
allowed to compact the fills. Where compaction is less than required, additional 
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content, as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
13 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 13, extending to depths of about 10.2 to 
11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig with 
continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our field engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-13. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions around the existing exposed section of 
waterline by performing 4 Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests extending to depths of 
about 5.6 to 9.9 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate DCP locations 
are shown on the Site Plan (Plate 2) Field Logs of the Dynamic Cone Penetration tests 
are presented on Plates B-1.0 through B-4.6. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications. The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter steel 
rod with a 60-degree conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a 
second steel rod. This rod is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be repeatedly 
raised and dropped from a height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods 
consists of an anvil to allow for quick connections between the rods and for efficient 
energy transfer from the falling weight to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and 
the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the 
rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and 
dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above was 
used to record the depth of penetration per hammer drop, as shown in the field logs provided 
in Appendix B.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate

samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate C-1.

samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on
