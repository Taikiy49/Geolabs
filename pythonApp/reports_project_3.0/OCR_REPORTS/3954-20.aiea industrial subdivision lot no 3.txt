AIEA INDUSTRIAL SUBDIVISION
LOT NO 3
Dear Mr Criz
This letter report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for Lot No 3 of the former Aiea Industrial Subdivision The
subdivision is located within the former Aiea Sugar Mill site located northeast of Ulune
Street northwest of Kulawea Street and southeast of Hakina Street in the District of
Aiea on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This letter report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical
engineering recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These preliminary recommendations are intended for the
preliminary design associated with site grading foundations retaining walls asphalt
pavements and slabs on grade only The findings and preliminary recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report

northeast from the intersection of Roads A and B Specific details of the structure that will

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Shallow Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings would be suitable for support of
the structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pound per square foot psf
may be utilized for the design of shallow foundations bearing on properly compacted
select granular fill material or recompacted on site material This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces
Footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches and should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade For footings constructed near
tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to
provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge
of the footings to the slope face

If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down
to the underlying competent material
Footings located adjacent to retaining walls should be embedded below a 1H 1V
imaginary plane extended upward from the heel of the retaining wall foundation or the
retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around the
footings is relatively undisturbed or well compacted Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the subgrade soil and foundation
concrete
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Inc prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Retaining Walls
In general retaining wall foundations should be designed in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this letter report except for
the following Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be
embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Wall footings
parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed with stepped footings
For the design of free standing retaining walls with level backfill an equivalent fluid
earth pressure of 45 pcf may be utilized Note that free standing walls are defined as
walls that are allowed to rotate about 0 5 percent of the wall height For restrained
retaining walls with level backfill i e split level retaining wall or basement wall an
equivalent fluid earth pressure of 65 pcf may be utilized For sloping backfill conditions
the equivalent fluid earth pressure should be increased by 25 percent

Surcharge and hydrostatic loads are not accounted for in the earth pressures
given Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges live loads and point loads should be
considered in the design
Backfill behind retaining walls should consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around
the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to appropriate
outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as
MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material The prefabricated
drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base
of the wall Unless covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
impervious material such as the on site clayey materials to reduce the potential for water
infiltration behind the walls

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that Lot No 3 is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with high expansion potential Unless floor slabs constructed above the
highly expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the
lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these soils due to
changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting
from the swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be
properly prepared and capped with at least 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill
material
Consideration should be given to construction of a 3 foot wide sidewalk adjacent to
and surrounding the structures A sidewalk with a 12 inch deep thickened edge should
help to stabilize the subgrade soil moisture below the structures and therefore reduce
the potential for swelling shrinking and softening of the subgrade soils
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and
graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding around
the foundation

The select granular fill should be placed immediately after the
moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils The prepared select
granular fill and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to
final placement of concrete Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this letter report
Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and select granular fill are also
discussed in the same section
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below the slabs to serve as
a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top
of the gravel or BTB cushion layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl
should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also
recommended that interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to
accommodate a small amount of possible ground movements
For slabs subjected to vehicular traffic the vapor barrier and 4 inch gravel BTB
cushion layer may be omitted For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 200 pci may be used for the compacted select granular fill material A
minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes
Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that are
subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved area
should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
For exterior slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic the vapor barrier and 4 inch
gravel BTB cushion layer may be omitted Construction joints should be provided at
intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle
intersections

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork vegetation debris rubbish or other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas should be over excavated to expose firm material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with properly compacted fill material
The excavated soft soils should be disposed of properly off site
Prior to filling and at finished subgrades the ground should be scarified to a depth
of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For
pavement subgrades the ground should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or the

Fills and Backfills
Fill material should consist of well graded on site materials less than 3 inches in
largest dimension and free of vegetation and deleterious materials
Import fill and select granular fill materials should consist of non expansive select
granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Inc for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the fills due to inclement weather and poor drainage may
require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with properly
compacted fill material
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be
performed by Geolabs Inc during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining
the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content Where
compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be applied
with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction

Slopes
Slopes should not exceed a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the
design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched
into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Utilities
The methods and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation
should comply with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench
shoring design and installation should be made the responsibility of the contractor
Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety
requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material
be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill
immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade should consist of the on site soils or select granular fill material The
backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of trench backfill below pavement subgrade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical
compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials Water tamping jetting
or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill material
