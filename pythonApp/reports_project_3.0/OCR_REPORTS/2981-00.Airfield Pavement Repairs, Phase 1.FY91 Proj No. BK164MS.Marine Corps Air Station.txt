PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at the southern end of the Marine Corps Air Station in
Kaneohe on the island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed project consist of replacing the
existing pavements with new pavements for a parking apron along the southern Kaneohe
Bay boundary and a triangular shaped taxiway apron located south of Hangar 105
Construction of a shoreline protection system along the shoreline adjacent to the parking
apron is also considered
The existing aircraft parking apron consists of Portland Cement Concrete PCC
pavements Large portions of the existing pavement have deteriorated and signs of
extensive pavement failure were noted The existing taxiway apron consists of asphaltic

existing grades in the adjacent areas we anticipate that the existing pavement sections
will need to be removed and replaced by new pavement sections

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion
cut valleys deep into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formation These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the MCAS at Kaneohe Bay is located on Mokapu Peninsula which was
built by three 3 volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series These vents are believed
to be among the oldest of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from vents
formed salients permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of
the island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Ice Age lowering of the sea level in response to
continental glaciation sands from emerged coral reefs were blown inland by the prevailing
winds to form sand dunes along the northern portion of the airfield
An extensive deposit of unconsolidated calcareous marine sediments primarily
consisting of beach sand made up of water worn coral shells and organisms covers the
western tip of Mokapu Peninsula Man made fills consisting of marine sediments dredged
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2981 00 Page 8
from the ocean floor were used to build up the area around the southern and western
shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula
Beach sand deposits and dredged fill materials were encountered in the borings
drilled for our exploration In general the subgrade materials at the project site consist
mainly of silty coral gravels and sands

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The airfield pavement improvement will include construction of new pavements to
replace the existing aircraft parking apron areas along the southern Kaneohe Bay
boundary and the triangular shaped taxiway apron located near Hangar 105 Also
included in the improvement project is the construction of a shoreline protection system
along the parking apron adjacent to Kaneohe Bay
The existing concrete pavements at the site range from about six 6 to twelve
inches 12 thick with steel reinforcement The concrete surface has deteriorated with
cracks through the center of the joint blocks Some spalling at the joints were also noted
Due to the deteriorated condition of the existing pavement and the need to match the
existing grades of the apron it appears that replacement of the pavement is necessary
The pavement replacement would allow improvement of the present poor surface
drainage of the area
The airfield has one major runway 4 22 which is about 7 769 feet long and 200
feet wide oriented in a general northeast southwest direction The runway consists of a
GEOLABS HAWAII

narrow strip of land bounded by the bay on either side The five 5 hangars and their
surrounding apron pavements provide storage maintenance and administrative space
for the Marine Air Group Hangars 101 and 105 are primarily used for
helicopters while Hangars 102 103 and 104 support fixed wing aircrafts
All apron areas are considered standard critical areas and are designed only for
rigid pavements The triangular shaped taxiway apron is treated as non critical and as

Design Modulus of Subgrade Reaction
Two 2 test pits were performed in general accordance with ASTM D 1196 64 test
method to evaluate the modulus of subgrade reaction K value of the underlying
subgrade material
The existing concrete pavement was saw cut and removed and a backhoe was
used to excavate the test pits to depths of 3 3 and 3 8 feet below the existing pavement
surface In situ plate load tests were performed on the natural soils exposed by
excavating at various selected depths to evaluate the variation of strength properties with
depth It is believed that the natural soils could be disturbed by excavation Therefore
the plate load tests were repeated two 2 times at the same depths in order to eliminate
the effect of poor seating of the plates soil inelastic deformation and or disturbance of
soil caused by excavation In situ plate load tests were also performed on the
compacted backfill at different depths to obtain strength properties for the recompacted
subgrade soils
Detailed descriptions of the testing procedure and data analysis are presented in
Appendix B The resulting load deflection curves are presented on Plates B 2 through
B 9 and a summary of the K values obtained are presented on Plate B 10
The modulus of subgrade reaction K values on the natural soil conditions ranged
from 125 to 330 pounds per cubic inch pci with an average of about 233 pci The low
K values are likely due to disturbances caused by excavation and may not be
representative for use in our pavement design The K value for soil conditions shallower
than 18 inches from the surface ranged from 282 to 330 pci with an average value of 306
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2981 00 Page 12
pci The K values for the soils at depths deeper than 18 inches from the surface ranged
from 125 to 325 pci with an average value of 197 pci The K values appear to decrease
with increasing depth as evidenced by the difference between the shallower and deeper
K values This trend correlates well with our field observations of penetration resistance
SPT values obtained from soil sampling in the borings
As shown on Plate B 10 the modulus of subgrade reaction values obtained by
repeating the plate load tests at the same test locations generally were found to increase
significantly with the repeated K values ranging from 280 to 565 pci with an average of
424 pci This increase appears to reflect the densification of the subgrade soils by
repeating the plate load tests at the same location We believe that the in situ modulus
of subgrade reaction of the soils are between 250 and 350 pci based on our tests
Therefore we believe that an undisturbed in situ K value of 300 pci can be used for
design of pavements
Using the same soil excavated from the test pits for backfill K values were found
to range from 235 to 425 pci on the recompacted backfill subgrade The average for the
K values is about 380 pci For pavement thickness design purposes a subgrade
modulus of 380 pci was used since the subgrade soils would be scarified and
recompacted in the pavement construction
Design Subgrade California Bearing Ratio CBR
Laboratory CBR tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D 1883 test
method on bulk samples obtained from the test pits Composite samples from test pits
TP 1 and TP 2 were used to carry out a series of tests in order to obtain the design CBR
value in accordance with the procedure outlined in Section 3 of the Navy Design Manual
DM 21 3 Flexible Pavement Design for Airfields of August 1978 The individual CBR
test results are presented on Plates C 29 through C 43 and a summary is presented on
GEOLABS HAWAII

Plates C 44 and C 45 A design CBR of 44 was obtained for a minimum compaction of
95 percent and moisture content between 17 and 19 percent A lower design CBR value
of 40 was selected for use in our analysis to account for some localized soil variability
From the approximate relationship between CBR and modulus of subgrade
reaction Figure 1 of MIL April 1987 a CBR of 40 correlates
approximately to a modulus of subgrade reaction of 420 pci which is slightly higher than
the design modulus of subgrade reaction of 380 pci obtained from the field plate load
tests on the compacted soil The modulus of subgrade reaction obtained from the
correlation chart appears to be consistent with the values obtained from our field and
laboratory tests

exceeding eight inches 8 in loose thickness A summary of the compaction
requirements for base course and subbase materials is presented on Plate D 3 of
Appendix D

For the rigid pavements to provide uniform support and strength for the
pavements we recommend that the pavement structural section be supported on a 12 inch
layer of subbase material The upper six inches 6 of the subbase material should be
compacted to not less than 100 percent of the maximum dry density as established by
ASTM D 1557 78 The lower six inches 6 of the subbase material should be compacted
to at least 95 percent compaction The on site sandy and gravelly soils may be re used
as the subbase material We anticipate that compacting the subgrade below the subbase
may be difficult due to the high groundwater levels Therefore we recommend that a
reinforcing fabric such as Mirafi 600X or equivalent be placed on the subgrade as
shown on Plate D 3 The reinforcing fabric should have minimum overlap of two feet 2
and should extend at least two feet 2 beyond the edge of the pavement on the
shoreline side Prior to placement of the reinforcing fabric the subgrade should be proof
rolled with a minimum ten ton smooth drum roller minimum four passes Since the
reinforcing fabric will provide additional strength to the subgrade soils the compaction
requirements of the subgrade soils have been reduced
For flexible pavements we also recommend that a 12 inch layer of subbase
material be provided below the base course material for uniform support of the pavement
section The upper six inches 6 of the subbase material should be compacted to not
less than 100 percent compaction with the lower six inches 6 of the subbase
compacted to at least 95 percent compaction We recommend that a layer of geogrid
reinforcements such as Tensar BX1100 or equivalent be placed on the subgrade below
the subbase material as shown on Plate D 3 The geogrid reinforcement should have
a minimum overlap of two feet 2 and should extend at least two feet 2 beyond the
edge of the pavement on the shoreline side Prior to placement of the geogrid

To reduce the potential for subgrade pumping heavy or rubber tired equipment
are not recommended In addition vibratory action of the compaction equipment should

If soft clayey spots are encountered at the subgrade level the soft clayey materials

From field and laboratory tests the recompacted subgrade was found to have a
relatively high design K value of 380 pci and design CBR of 40 Therefore cement or
asphalt stabilization of the subgrade soils is not necessary If import soils are needed
they should meet the minimum K value of 380 pci and CBR of 40
Due to the granular nature of the subsoil the subgrade soil can be classified as
permeable and therefore impeded drainage is not anticipated From the grain size
distribution analysis and rough correlation between capillary rise and effective grain size
the anticipated height of capillary rise would be on the order of one foot or less The
capillary rise effect combined with the high groundwater level may cause compaction
difficulties
Pavement Joints
Joints in concrete pavements should be provided 1 to reduce potential curling and
warping stresses in the pavement caused by temperature and moisture gradients across
GEOLABS HAWAII

the pavement slabs 2 to reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the
concrete 3 to reduce damage to immovable structures and 4 to facilitate construction
revision Joints Reinforcements and Mooring Eyes in Concrete Pavement should be
referred to for joint details and construction requirements
Joint sealants should be used to provide a seal to reduce potential infiltration of
water and to provide a compressible joint Joint sealing compounds used should be jet
fuel resistant and blast resistant and should follow the recommendations of Federal
Portland Cement and Asphalt Pavements Detailed joint sealant design are contained in
NFGS 02561
Pavement Drainage
Surface runoff from the project and pavement areas should be collected and
drained to a suitable discharge point The site should be graded to provide positive
drainage away from the pavement areas and to reduce ponding of water adjacent to the
pavement areas
Shoreline Protection System
It is our understanding that a rock revetment system is planned for the protection
of the shoreline and sea plane ramps along the southern boundary adjacent to Kaneohe
Bay We understand that the rock revetment system for the sea plane ramps will be
constructed along the sides of the ramp and consist of a three foot 3 layer of spalls to
ten inch 10 stones underlain by a geotextile fabric at a slope ratio of one and one half
horizontal to one vertical
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2981 00 Page 20
The rock revetment along the shoreline will consist of 500 to 800 pounds armor
stones with an underlayer of spalls to ten inch 10 stones underlain by a geotextile fabric
at a slope ratio of one and one half horizontal to one vertical
To reduce the migration of the existing fine soils and backfill into the revetment
stones we recommend that a geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 700X be placed between
the fine soils and revetment stones A layer of 3B Fine gravel of not less than six inches
6 should be placed on top of the geotextile fabric prior to placement of the ten inch
10 stones Care should be taken when placing the ten inch 10 stones above the
3B Fine gravel and fabric to reduce the potential of puncturing the fabric
The voids caused by the erosion of the materials under the sea plane ramp
concrete slab should be properly backfilled prior to construction of the revetment system
Because of the difficulty in performing compaction on the backfill beneath the existing
concrete slab we recommend that the voids beneath the concrete slab be backfilled with
a self compacting material such as 3B Fine gravel or with lean concrete
Since portions of the backfill behind the revetment along the shoreline is near the
high water level we recommend that the backfill be placed and compacted during low
tide or that a material such as 3B Fine gravel be used below the water level If the
backfill is placed and compacted above the water level the on site coralline gravel and
sand may be used The backfill material should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
of its maximum dry density established in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
An alternative using articulated precast or cast in place block mat for erosion protection
is being considered The block mat will be placed on an embankment with a geotextile
fabric below the mat We recommend that a geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 700X or
equivalent be placed below the mat The backfill recommendations above may be used
for the embankment

APPENDIX B
Field Testing
In each of the two 2 test pits excavated in situ density and plate load tests were
performed at selected depths below the existing ground surface The test pits were
excavated to depths of 3 3 to 3 8 feet below the ground surface with a hoptoe The soil
conditions exposed by excavation for in situ tests were treated as natural and
undisturbed The test pits were then backfilled and compacted with a hand operated
compactor to the original ground elevation In situ tests were also performed on the
backfilled and recompacted soil conditions in order to determine soil properties for a
recompacted soil conditions in order to determine soil properties for a compacted
subgrade condition Plate load tests on the natural soil conditions were repeated two
times Test Run Nos 2 and 3 in order to determine the relative amount of inelastic
deformation in the first test Test Run No 1 and to reduce the effects of disturbance
caused by the excavation
In situ plate load tests for determination of the modulus of subgrade reaction K
value were carried out in general accordance with ASTM D 1196 64 test method A
trailer truck with approximately ten 10 tons of dead weight on its bed was used for the
load reaction The truck was placed over the test pit so that one of the I beams in its bed
was located directly above the center of the test pit A hydraulic jack was then used to
jack against this beam with four 1 inch thick steel plates stacked on the bottom of the
jack as support The steel plates had diameters of and 30 inches and were
stacked in this sequence with the largest plate at the bottom Two dial gages graduated
in units of 0 001 inch were used to measure the deflections near the circumference of the
30 inch diameter plate A capacity proving ring was used to measure the
applied load
An initial load of psi was used to seat the loading system and bearing
plates This seating load was also considered to be the zero load Then without
releasing the seating load loads were applied in increments of psi with each
load increment held until the rate of deformation was less than 0 001 inch per minute
Readings of the dial micrometers at the end of each load increment were recorded
Following the completion of the 10 psi load increment the average deflection
was determined by averaging the total movement between the zero and 10 psi
increment for each dial The modulus of subgrade reaction K value was taken as the
value of 10 psi divided by this average deflection Following recommendations by the
Military Standard 621A Method 104 Modulus of Soil Reaction computed K values larger
than 200 pci were corrected for poor seating of the plates and or nonlinear load
deformation relations The correction consists of drawing a straight line parallel to the
straight line portion of the plotted curve through the origin of the load deformation
relation The deflection at 10 psi indicated by this straight line was used in computing K
values The resulting K values larger than 200 pci were further corrected for plate

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Log of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Forty seven 47 sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of the underlying
sandy soils to evaluate its grain size distribution The test results are summarized on
comparison purposes
To further evaluate the index properties of the soil samples two 2 specific gravity
tests were performed and the test results are summarized on Plates C 10
Two 2 moisture density determinations were performed on selected samples to
determine the compaction characteristic of the subsoils These test results are
summarized on Plate C 10 and plotted on Plates C 27 and C 28
Two 2 laboratory CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of the subsoil to
evaluate the suitability of the soil for pavement support The test results are summarized
on Plate C 10
One full series CBR tests were performed on composite bulk samples obtained
from the test pits in accordance with the method outlined in Section 3 of the Navy Design
Manual DM 21 3 in order to evaluate the relationships between the moisture density and
CBR values of the compacted soil and to estimate the design CBR value for use in flexible
pavement analysis The test results are plotted on Plates C 29 to C 45
