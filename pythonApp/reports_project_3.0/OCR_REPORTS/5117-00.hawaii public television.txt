GEOTECHNICAL DUE DILIGENCE STUDY
HAWAII PUBLIC TELEVISION
HONOLULU, OAHU, HAWAII
Dear Mr. Wong:
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed in support of the due diligence study for the proposed. Hawaii Public Television
development located in the Ala Wai area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
This letter report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration and engineering analyses in support
of the due diligence study for the proposed television station project. We understand that
a more detailed geotechnical engineering exploration will be performed when additional
information for the proposed development becomes available. The findings and
preliminary recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this letter report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located adjacent to the existing Hard Rock Café along Kalakaua
Avenue in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The subject parcel is approximately
trapezoidal in shape and encompasses approximately 28,761 square feet in area. The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map,
Plate 1.
Based on the information provided, it is proposed to construct a new television
station for Hawaii Public Television at the project site. The new television station will be a
three-story building with on-grade parking and some office space at the ground floor level.
Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer, maximum column
loads on the order of about 350 kips are anticipated for the building construction. Detailed
information pertaining to the proposed development was not available at the time this due
diligence report was prepared.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain a general
overview of the surface and subsurface conditions at the project site. The subsurface
information obtained was utilized to develop a generalized soil/rock data set for the
formulation of preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations in support of the
due diligence study for the proposed television station project. To accomplish this, we
conducted a program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts:
1. Research and review of available reports and in-house _ information
pertaining to the geology and subsurface conditions in the vicinity of the
project site.
2. Reconnaissance of the project site by a senior engineer from our firm to
evaluate the existing site conditions.
3. Coordination of the stake-out, utility toning, and clearances of the boring
location by an engineer from our firm.
4. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and operations to
and from the project site.
5. Drilling and sampling of one boring extending to a depth of about 54 feet
below the existing ground surface for one day of field exploration.
6. Engineering analyses of the field data for the formulation of preliminary
geotechnical recommendations pertaining to the due diligence study for the
new television station project.
7. Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and preliminary geotechnical recommendations.
8. Coordination and quality assurance of our overall work on the project by a
principal engineer from our firm.
9. Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 54 feet below the
existing ground surface. The approximate location of the boring drilled is shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight, solid-stem augers and coring tools.
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by a surface fill
layer consisting of medium dense to dense silty basaltic gravel and coralline sands with
some clayey silts extending to a depth of about 4 feet below the existing ground surface.
Below the surface fill layer, lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft silty clays were
encountered to a depth of about 31 feet below the existing ground surface. Alluvial soil
deposits consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays overlying medium dense coralline detritus
were then encountered extending to the maximum depth explored of about 54 feet below
the existing ground surface.
Shallow groundwater conditions were encountered in the boring drilled at depths
between about 3.3 and 5.9 feet below the existing ground surface. Due to the proximity of
the project site to the Ala Wai Canal and the Pacific Ocean, groundwater levels will likely
be affected by tidal fluctuations and other factors, such as seasonal precipitation and
storm surge conditions. Detailed descriptions of the subsurface materials encountered
from our field exploration are presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates 3 and 4 of this
report.
FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our field exploration, we believe that medium dense to dense surface fills
on the order of about 3 to 4 feet thick may be encountered across the project site. The
surface fills were generally underlain by soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits extending to a
depth of about 30 feet below the existing ground surface. Below the highly compressible
lagoonal deposits, stiff alluvial soil deposits overlying medium dense coralline detritus were
then encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 54 feet below
the existing ground surface. Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 3.3 to
5.9 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration program.
Based on the highly compressible lagoonal deposits encountered at shallow depths
and the moderate to high structural loads anticipated for the planned development, we
believe that appreciable settlements would occur if a shallow foundation system is used to
support the proposed television station building. Therefore, we recommend that the
proposed structure be supported on a deep foundation system consisting of either 12-inch
square or 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete piles. An allowable
compressive load capacity on the order of about 30 to 70 tons per pile with a preliminary
pile length of about 50 to 70 feet may be used for the recommended pile design. In order

Predrilling
The upper 5 feet below the existing ground surface may need to be predrilled to
seat the pile and to facilitate the pile driving operations through the fill crust. The
predrilled holes should have a maximum diameter of about 16 inches for the
12-inch square piles and 22 inches for the 16.5-inch octagonal piles. The annular
space between the piles and predrilled holes should be backfilled with pea gravel,
S4C sand, or grouted with tremie concrete to fill in the voids. The required
predrilling size and depth should be verified and/or modified by Geolabs during the
test pile driving operation depending on the conditions encountered and pile driving
equipment used.
Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and the subgrade response. We estimate that the total settlement of
the pile-supported foundation to be less than 0.5 inches. Differential settlements
between adjacent piles should not exceed about one-half of the total foundation
settlement. We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should
occur as the loads are applied.
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 30,000 foot-pounds of energy per blow. The hammer should be equipped
with an energy control level such that driving within the soft/loose soils may be
controlled at a lower hammer energy to reduce the potential for tensile stress
development within the piles, which could damage or cause cracking of the piles.
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the "matching" of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven. This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles. Geolabs
should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate whether the
equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity.
Test Pile Program
We recommend that a test pile program, which includes at least one load test, be
undertaken for the project. In general, we recommend that the test pile program
consist of approximately 10 percent of the total number of piles for the project. The
test piles may be incorporated into the actual foundation system upon evaluation of
the load test results. When the final foundation plan is available, Geolabs should
be consulted for selection of the test pile locations.

Test piles should be cast at least 10 feet longer than the estimated pile lengths to
account for potential variations. In addition, the test pile program should provide
flexibility to allow for addition or relocation of the test piles, as may be deemed
necessary by our office, to suit the conditions encountered during the test pile
driving. Re-striking of all the test piles may be required as part of the test pile
program. The pile load test should be performed in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1143, loading the test pile to 200 percent of the design load.
We recommend that a representative from Geolabs be present during pile driving
operations to observe the actual driving behavior and to further evaluate the field
performance. It should be noted that the pile design and driving criteria
recommended were developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration
data. Therefore, observation of the pile driving operations by Geolabs is necessary
to confirm our design assumptions and should be designated a “Special Inspection”
item.
Site Preparation
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will involve minor cuts and
fills on the order of up to about 5 feet thick. Site grading and earthwork operations should
be observed by a representative from Geolabs.
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, and other deleterious and organic
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and/or yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and
compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities.
After clearing and grubbing, existing structures and pavements that are to be
demolished should be completely removed. Any over-excavation resulting from demolition
should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material. Existing utilities to be
abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled
with select granular fill material placed in 8-inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction. Utilities to be abandoned in-place under the proposed
structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure.
After clearing, grubbing, and demolition, areas at grade or areas designated to
receive fill should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test

Based on our field exploration, the near-surface excavated on-site soils, excluding
the soft clays and other saturated materials below the water table, may be re-used as a
source of general fill and backfill material. Where needed, imported materials should
consist of non-expansive, select granular fill materials, such as crushed coral and basalt
gravel. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater
than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should have a California Bearing Ratio
(CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883. It should also contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.
Fill or backfill below the water level should consist of free-draining granular
materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), up to a minimum
of 12 inches above the groundwater level. If fill or backfill below the water level is placed
on clayey materials, we recommend that the open-graded gravel be wrapped in a
non-woven filter fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent.
Pavement Design
We understand that asphaltic concrete pavements are planned at the ground floor
level of the new television station building. We anticipate that the vehicle loading for this
project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles, light trucks, and maintenance vehicles.
We have made our preliminary pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil
will consist of the near-surface sandy soils or imported fill materials. Based on the above
assumptions, we believe that the following pavement section may be used for preliminary
design and cost estimating purposes:
Flexible Pavement
2.5-Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)
8.5-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches,
moisture-conditioned to above the optinum moisture, and compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction. CBR and
field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section.
In general, paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the site. Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction. Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
