performed for the Reconstruction of Wastewater Systems at Waimanalo District Park
project within the existing Waimanalo District Park in the Waimanalo area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1

testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of the new sewer manhole

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years
ago
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When
the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits

When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary
rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in areas above the valley
floors and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the friction between the backfill
material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads acting on the buried
pipes an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot under
submerged condition may be used for compacted backfill material within the trench
excavation and above the pipes In addition a coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be
used to calculate the frictional resistance between the compacted backfill and the
sidewalls of the excavated trench If the trench excavations are shored using steel
sheet piles or similar type shoring the frictional resistance between the backfill
material and the sidewalls of the trench should be neglected
For the underground pipes earth forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with the
width of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum
Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered for the portion of the
pipes located in roadway areas In general the traffic load may be modeled as
2 feet of surcharge fill or about 240 pounds per square foot psf

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded
gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or similar
free draining type material is recommended under the pipes The granular bedding
should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of one fourth of the
outside pipe diameter Based on our field exploration we do not envision the
presence of soft loose soils at or near the invert of the pipes If soft loose soils are
encountered at or near the invert of the pipes a stabilization layer consisting of
additional 18 inches of open graded gravel wrapped in a non woven filter fabric
Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform
support A typical section of the trench detail is presented on Plate 3

Grinder Pump Stations
Based on the information provided the bottom of the grinder pumps at the
underground pump stations are set at about 8 feet below the existing ground surface
Therefore construction of the pump stations may involve an excavation on the order of
about 9 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface
In general we recommend placing the grinder pumps on a minimum 6 inch thick
layer of cushion fill to provide for uniform support The cushion fill should consist of
open graded gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which
would also serve as a leveling course In addition the cushion fill should extend a
minimum of 8 inches beyond the edges of the pump station structure The subgrade
soils below the cushion fill at the bottom of the pump station structure should be firm
and unyielding prior to placement of the cushion fill Soft and or loose subsoils
encountered near the foundation level should be over excavated to expose competent
in situ material and be replaced with free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric such as
Mirafi 180N or equivalent
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered it is our opinion that the lateral
earth pressures at rest conditions and lateral load resistance presented in the subsection
New Sewer Manhole may be used for the design of the new pump stations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered we estimate the
settlement of the pump station structures to be less than 1 inch total We believe that
most of the settlements should occur during the construction period However some
movement of soils around the new pump station excavation should be anticipated due
to changes in the earth stresses during and after construction

excavation for the low pressure gravity sewer line and excavations for the new sewer
manhole structure and grinder pump stations A Geolabs representative should monitor
site grading and earthwork operations to observe whether undesirable materials are
encountered during the excavation process and to confirm whether the exposed
soil rock conditions at the invert elevations are similar to those encountered in our
exploration
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared thoroughly Vegetation debris and other deleterious and organic materials
should be removed and disposed properly off site Soft and or yielding areas
encountered during clearing should be over excavated to expose firm material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fills The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed off site and or used in landscape
areas where appropriate Contract documents should include additive and deductive
unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in
the over excavation quantities

source of fill and backfill material provided that they are processed to 3 inch minus

aeration of the excavated materials may be required prior to their use as backfill
material

Excavation
In order to install the underground structures and new sewer line we anticipate
that excavations below the existing ground pavement surface will be required for the
project construction Our field exploration at the project area generally encountered
very stiff to hard silty clay and clayey silt soils extending to depths of about 22 feet
below the existing ground pavement surface
Excavation Method
In general the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used
for excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on our

field exploration the on site clayey soils encountered in the borings may be
excavated with conventional earthmoving equipment
In general the excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal
distance equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of
the excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In
addition the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from
underground utilities to reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence
which may damage the existing underground utility lines
Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 11 feet may be required for the
project construction In areas where open excavations are feasible we believe that
a temporary cut slope inclination of or flatter may be used in areas
exposing the very stiff to hard clayey soils encountered at the project site In areas
where open excavations are not feasible we believe that shoring of the sides of the
excavations will be necessary to protect personnel working in the excavations
based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the excavation depths
Based on the stiff subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated depth of
excavation we believe that steel plates with horizontal struts may be considered for
temporary shoring of the trench excavations for the new underground piping
planned for the project For the deeper excavations and excavations adjacent to
existing structures and other utilities we recommend considering soldier pile with
lagging or interlocking steel sheet piling with or without horizontal bracing for
excavation support to reduce the potential for significant adjacent ground
movement In addition it should be noted that the planned excavations may
traverse other utility lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating
the existing utility lines if sheet piles are used as shoring
The presence of the very stiff to hard clayey soils may require that the sheet pile
locations are predrilled prior to the installation of the steel sheet piles to the desired
depths for shoring stability Therefore the contractor should carefully evaluate the

It is important to install adequate shoring prior to the excavation and to maintain it
tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation The
properly braced shoring is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral
movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in
potential settlements or distress to the adjacent structures or other improvements
such as pavements or existing utilities
The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the
shoring installation His her representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the
best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction such
as unforeseen subsurface soils unexpectedly high groundwater conditions
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring
stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground still may occur due to changes in the earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are
installed the subsurface soil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation In addition it should be noted that settlement of the existing ground
may occur as a result of the vibrations generated during the installation and
extraction of sheet pile shoring Therefore special attention should be given by the
contractor during the sheet pile installation and removal process to reduce the
potential for appreciable ground settlement

Thrust Block
We anticipate thrust blocks will be required at the bends of the pressurized
underground piping The following recommendations may be used for the preliminary
design and sizing of the thrust blocks Lateral loads acting on the thrust blocks may be
resisted by friction developed between the bottom of the thrust blocks and the bearing
materials and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the
thrust block A coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used for the concrete thrust blocks
bearing on the stiff in situ soils
In general we anticipate the bottom of the thrust blocks will be embedded at
least 3 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our field exploration stiff
clayey soils are anticipated down to a depth of about 22 feet below the existing ground
surface Therefore we believe that a lateral bearing pressure of up to may be
used to size the thrust blocks based on encountering stiff clayey soils at the thrust block
locations In addition this assumes that the soils around the thrust blocks are well
compacted minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 11 5 to
22 feet below the existing ground pavement surface The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a track mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil
samples in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
