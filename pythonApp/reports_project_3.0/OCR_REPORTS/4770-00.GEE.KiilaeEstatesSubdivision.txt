2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which had activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes
up the west central part of the island The lava formations encountered at the site appear
to be of a a and pahoehoe type lava flows which spread and ponded as they approached
the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow
surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Typically a denser and more layered lava rock material is contained within the
lava flow core
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface with an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in
pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state
and they represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and or pockets
inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow
leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Blisters and or pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt formations are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit
water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock mairix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts
cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high
therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

3 3 Surface Runoff Disposal Systems
Four falling head percolation tests were conducted in the borings drilled to provide
data for design of the proposed drywells planned at the site The percolation tests were
performed at depths ranging from about 15 4 to 16 feet below the ground surface to
evaluate the permeability characteristics of the on site basalt formation The results of the
percolation tests performed at the site are presented in Plate 3 through 6 of this report
The percolation tests indicated that the on site basalt formation generally have
coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 2 5 x 10 to 4 5 x 10 feet per minute
which corresponds to about 1 3 x 10 to 2 3 x 10 centimeters per second Based on the
results of our field percolation tests we believe that an average hydraulic conductivity of
about 0 03 feet per minute may be assumed for the in situ materials
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava
or the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of a
drywell In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or voids encountered by
the drywell excavation the higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity
In general drywells are typically 5 feet in diameter and 20 feet in depth The actual
size and depth of the subsurface disposal system will depend on the design quantity of the
future surface runoff Extension of the drywell from the planned 20 foot depth may be
required depending on the specific site conditions at the proposed drywell locations
Therefore we recommend that unit prices for additional drilling be obtained during bidding
Provisions should be incorporated into the construction documents to extend the drywell
depths from the planned 20 feet to about 40 feet below the ground surface using a
minimum 15 inch diameter extension hole or 5 foot diameter drilling equipment The unit
prices for drywell extension should be obtained in 20 foot increments Considering the
highly variable disposal percolation rates of the subsurface conditions at the project site
we strongly recommend testing of all the drywells be performed during construction

should also be given to the installation of concrete rings in the clinker layer to reduce the

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of about 15 5 to
20 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings
drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who observed the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through
A 5
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 21138 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
