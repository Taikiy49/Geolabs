INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of the Church of Latter Day Saints we have made
visual observations upon completion of construction and tested two 2 new injection wells
at the project site This report has been prepared in support of Underground Injection
Control permit application No UH 1702
The approximate location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal
to the Church of Latter Day Saints dated March 13 1992
This report summarizes our findings from our field observation and testing of the
new injection wells at the project site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in Hilo in the District of South Hilo on the island of
Hawaii It is our understanding that County of Hawaii requires that new development
create no net increase in discharge of surface or storm water runoff The general
practice on the island of Hawaii is to utilize drywells as a disposal method for handling
surface runoff
GEOLABS HAWAII

The project is a new chapel to serve the 2 and Ainaola Wards
of the Hilo Hawaii Stake of the Church of Latter Day Saints The project involves the
construction of a new chapel and related buildings along with paved parking areas
Surface runoff within the project site will be drained to two 2 drywells
Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our work on the project has been to make visual observations of
the drywells after their construction and to conduct testing on the wells and to prepare
and submit this engineering report as required under Chapter 11 23 Department of
Health Administrative Rules Underground Injection Control
In order to accomplish these tasks we have conducted a program consisting of
the following work efforts
1 Visual observation of the wells after their construction and supervision of the
injection testing by a Field Engineer Geologist from our firm
2 Analyses of the field data for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertinent to the hydrology of the new wells
3 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings in general accordance with Department of Health
requirements
4 Coordination of our overall work on the project and client design team
consultation by a Principal Engineer of our firm

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The site is located in Hilo in the South Hilo District on the island of Hawaii The
climate of the area is mild with an average annual temperature of about 72 5 F and an
average annual rainfall of about 150 inches per year
The site is situated on the easterly flank of Mauna Loa The topography is a
relatively gentle slope down towards the east from the central portion of the island to the
ocean
At the time of the well construction the new chapel facilities were being
constructed Formerly the site was used for the old chapel facilities which were
demolished for the new construction
Regional Geology
The island of Hawaii is composed of five 5 basalt shield volcanoes built by the
extrusion of the lavas during the late Pleistocene and the Holocene Epochs
The site is located on the easterly flank of Mauna Loa the second youngest of
these volcanoes Mauna Loa appears to still be in the primitive shield building stage and
has been active within the past decade
Rocks of Mauna Loa cover most of the central and western portions of the island
of Hawaii The easterly flank of the volcano has been channelled between a saddle
created by the older Mauna Kea and the Younger Kilauea The rocks of Mauna Loa are
generally characterized as thin flows of tholeitic basalt extruded from vents along the main
rift zones of the volcano In many locales the rocks are mantled with varying thicknesses
of ash soils Pahala Ash

Foundation Conditions
In general borings drilled for geotechnical exploration at the site encountered a
relatively thin layer of ash soil overlying competent volcanic rock formation at relatively
shallow depths below the existing ground surface The rock formation generally has very
good strength characteristics and are capable of supporting large foundation loads
Since the Pahala Ash soils are generally thixotropic in nature the geotechnical
engineering exploration report for site preparation and foundation design recommended
that the ash soil be removed from structural areas
Since the foundation aspects of the new chapel and related buildings which are
currently under construction were addressed in separate geotechnical engineering efforts
foundation considerations are not considered to be of significance for the injection wells
themselves
Earthquake Conditions
The island of Hawaii is designated in the 1985 Uniform Building Code as Seismic
Zone Three Since the wells were constructed in relatively competent rock formation and
the drywells generally consisted of simple excavated pits with a catch basin inlet at the
ground surface seismic conditions are not considered to be significant
Flood Problems
The site has been engineered and will be graded for drainage Therefore
inundation is not considered to be of significance to the operation of the wells
Land Use
The site is being used for religious activities and is situated in an urban area of
mixed commercial and residential development Therefore construction of drywells at the
site is consistent with this use

SYSTEM OPERATION
It is proposed to use the new drywells for the disposal of storm water runoff from
the roof and paved areas of the chapel facilities Use of the wells will be intermittent and
will be dependent upon weather conditions The injectate will not be treated prior to
disposal
The complete injection system will consist of two 2 wells arranged independently
in the development area Injection will be under gravity pressure i e the head pressure
that builds up within the individual drywells Details of the system are shown on Plate A 1
Since the new drywells are intended for intermittent disposal of storm water runoff
only there is no additional standby system nor are monitoring wells planned
GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The site is located at Latitude North and Longitude West
The ground surface elevation at the site at the time of well testing was approximately 33
feet above Mean Sea Level
The logs of the drywells are presented in Appendix A Generally the wells
encountered what appears to be Mauna Loa basalts
protected by concrete and masonry curbing to support the sidewalls of the excavations
These portions of the drywells could not be observed In general the visible portion of
Well No 1 encountered moderately dense fractured basalt formation from a depth of
about four and one half 4 5 feet below the existing ground surface to a depth of about
13 feet Below this depth the drywell penetrated fragmental basalt Clinker to the
maximum depth excavated of 20 5 feet below the existing ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2367 30 Page 7
The visible portions of Well No 2 similarly penetrated moderately dense fractured
basalt formation from a depth of about six and one half 6 5 feet below the existing
ground surface to a depth of about 14 5 feet Below this depth the drywell penetrated
fragmental basalt Clinker to the maximum depth excavated of 20 8 feet below the
existing ground surface
Groundwater was not observed in the excavated drywells at the time of testing
The regional characteristics of groundwater in the area is fresh to brackish water
in a basal volcanic aquifer of relatively high permeability
PERMEABILITY TESTING
When the new drywells had been excavated to their intended depths and the
curbing installed to support the upper portions of the excavations the wells were tested
by the injection of clear fresh water These tests indicated that the injection formation has
a coefficient of permeability k on the order of about 900 feet per day
The subsurface permeability conditions at the site were tested by the injection of
clear fresh water from a water tank truck at a rate of about 200 gallons per minute for a
period of about one half 2 hour per well
During the injection of water the changes in water level in the wells were recorded
with respect to time and rate of injection
The results of these tests are presented in Appendix B

The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as excavated wells and injection tests

can not assume responsibility for the accuracy of the location and or elevation of the

depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a
