PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The existing retaining wall is located along lolani Street between Prospect and
Miller Streets in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The existing retaining wall
provides grade separation between the roadway and the park below
We understand that an approximately 30 foot long section of the wall failed years
ago The face of the failed retaining wall is covered with shotcrete It is desired to evaluate
repair alternatives for the failed section and to provide stabilization measures for the
remainder of the wall It is our understanding that one of the repair goals is to keep the
appearance of the repair similar to the condition of the adjacent un failed existing wall
Details for the existing retaining wall were not available at the time this letter
report was prepared One of the considerations was to retain the existing retaining wall
with minor drainage improvements and only reconstruct the failed portion of the wall
Therefore our study and exploration focused on an effective repair design for the failed
retaining wall and the stability of the remaining retaining wall

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions These later eruptions are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present sea level
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the
sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
The project site is on the southern flank of Punchbowl Hill Puowaina which is a
tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of the Koolau Range
The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with scattered fragments of coral
limestone and Koolau basalt

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions behind the existing
retaining wall consist of loose to medium dense granular fill materials overlying weathered
volcanic tuff formation Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time
of our field exploration The probing results indicate that the existing retaining wall may
only consist of a rock veneer facing
The following design alternatives were considered during the initial conceptual
design phase
Soil Nail Retaining Wall
Conventional CRM Retaining Wall
Sheetpile Wall Supported by Tieback Anchors
Chemical Grouting
The sheetpile wall and chemical grout alternatives were eliminated during the
preliminary design phase due to aethetics long term corrosion concern and potential
environmental impact Based on cost comparison the soil nail retaining wall alternative is
a more cost effective solution than conventional cement rubble masonry CRM retaining
wall Therefore the soil nail retaining wall alternative is the selected repair method
As previously mentioned one of the considerations is to construct a soil nail
retaining wall only for the failed portion and to improve the drainage for the remainder of
the wall However our stability analyses indicate that the existing near vertical slope with
rock veneer facing would not provide sufficient lateral support to the roadway with factor of
safety greater than or equal to 1 5 Therefore we recommend reconstructing the entire
retaining wall by using a soil nail retaining wall system We also recommend capping the
soil nail wall with sculptured shotcrete as permanent facing to simulate rock appearance
Detailed discussion of our findings and geotechnical engineering design
recommendations for the soil nail retaining wall are presented in the following sections
Soil Nail Retaining Walls
The soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize slope A shotcrete facing is generally applied to the
face of the reinforced slope The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear
strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the slope The reinforced soil mass
behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing The face of the
soil nail retaining wall system is slightly battered at a slope inclination of about 1H 12V A
typical cross section of the soil nail retaining wall system is presented on Plate 3
The soil nail installed may be capped with sculpture shotcrete to simulate the
natural rock appearance

Design Parameters
Design of the soil nail retaining wall system will need to consider both the internal
and external stability of the reinforced soil mass The design of the internal
stability includes establishing the size spacing orientation and length of the
grouted reinforcing bars The external stability includes overall slope stability of
the reinforced slope The geotechnical design parameters needed to evaluate the
internal and external stability of the soil nail retaining wall system are provided in
the following table

Design Considerations
We believe that the soil nails may require some form of corrosion protection such
as a fully encapsulated double corrosion protection system
The design of the soil nail retaining wall system does not include hydrostatic
pressures that might be caused by groundwater or water trapped behind the
walls Therefore the soil nail retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the
build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of
2 foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain placed on the face of the excavation between the soil nails The
prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top to the base of the wall
and should be hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall as
shown on Plate 3 The weepholes should discharge to appropriate outlets
Soil Nail Testing
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nail retaining
walls we recommend performing a minimum of one pre production verification
test on a sacrificial soil nail We also recommend conducting verification tests on
a minimum of three production soil nails In addition proof tests should be
performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during construction
to confirm the bond stresses used in the design
The verification tests should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design
load for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about
200 percent of the design load In addition the load at every loading increment
should be held for at least 10 minutes and creep tests should be held for at least
60 minutes The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least
150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least
10 minutes until stable The test nails may be incorporated into the permanent
soil nail retaining wall provided that they satisfy the test criteria Pullout tests and
creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nail
retaining wall system Therefore we recommend conducting pullout tests and
creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs representative

Due to the loose consistency of sandy fill materials behind the existing retaining
wall as encountered in the borings caving in and or sloughing may occur during the
drill operations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary
casing of the drilled holes may be required during the soil nail installation
It is common that some large boulder or cobble may be encountered in the fill
materials Therefore hard drilling conditions may be encountered in the localized
areas In addition due to potential variation of dense tuff formation outcrop behind
the retaining wall hard drilling conditions may also be anticipated Considerations
may be given to drilling and probing behind the retaining wall to estimate the dense
tuff formation outcrop prior to the production soil nail installation
Drilling fluids including mineral and polymer slurry should not be allowed neither
used for the soil nail installation If utilizing air pressure during the drilling the
contractor should be cautious when drilling is advanced into the loose sandy
material to prevent undermining the roadway subgrade Undermining the roadway
subgrade will further cause ground settlement and loss of foundation support
Contractor should fully investigate the project site and make his her own
interpretation It is the contractor s responsibility to bid the project accordingly with
sufficient contingency taking into account potential caving in hard drilling and
difficult access conditions
