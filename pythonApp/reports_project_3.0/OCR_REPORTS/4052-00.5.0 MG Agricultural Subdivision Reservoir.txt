INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed 500 000 gallon water reservoir for Princeville Utilities
Company Inc on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 2
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the 500 000 gallon water reservoir These recommendations are intended
for the design of foundations site grading and pavements only and are not intended for
the basis of preparation of cost estimates or for bidding purposes The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
lt is proposed to construct a 500 000 gallon potable water reservoir
approximately 60 feet in diameter and 26 5 feet high with a concrete floor slab An
asphalt concrete paved service road will be provided around the reservoir structure In
addition a low retaining wall between 2 to 3 feet will also be built to allow grading of
the service road around the tank
The finish floor elevation for the new water reservoir will be at approximately
584 99 feet Mean Sea Level MSL to match the existing 50 000 gallon water
reservoir Based on the project plans provided the existing ground surface elevations
at the proposed reservoir site vary from about 584 to 594 feet MSL Therefore we
anticipate that minor site grading consisting of cuts of up to about 10 feet deep will be
needed for the construction of the proposed reservoir and appurtenant structures

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years ago Following the cessation of the formation of this main
shield there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 4
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locations Associated with
the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation As the continental glaciers accumulated the evel of the
ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the ocean basins Conversely as
the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because more water was
available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes and
the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in
stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level of
Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits of
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The project site is situated in the Makaleha Mountains Most of the soils in the
project area are residual soils derived from in situ weathering of igneous rocks Thin
beds of ash and tuffaceous soils are intercalated with the flows

and an existing dirt road on the western side as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The site was explored by drilling and sampling of two borings to depths of 11 5 and
40 8 feet below the existing ground surface to evaluate the subsurface conditions below
the proposed reservoir structure The approximate locations of the borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered reddish brown medium stiff clayey silt
to a depth of about 5 feet Below this the soils graded with blackish brown mottling and
weathered basalt fragments to the maximum depth explored at about 40 8 feet below the
ground surface
Groundwater was encountered at elevation 547 feet MSL 37 feet below the
existing ground surface in Boring No 1 This groundwater is probably in a perched
condition and is generally the result of low permeability ash beds or lenses within the
Koloa Volcanic Series
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on
selected samples and the results are presented in Appendix B
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by medium
stiff to stiff clayey silt soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 40 8 feet
below the existing ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 4052 00 Page 6
project site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of a perimeter ring footing and
individual spread footing bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils may be used for
support of the proposed new reservoir
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a slight
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
To reduce the potential for distress to the future slab on grade resulting from swelling of
the near surface soils and to provide more uniform support we recommend that the
on site clayey soils be capped with a minimum of 6 inches of compacted base course
below the reservoir slab areas In general the subgrade soils should be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement
of base course and slab concrete Our geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertaining to the design of foundations site grading and pavements are further discussed
in the following sections of this report
Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe
that shallow foundations may be used for support of the proposed new reservoir An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
the design of a perimeter ring footing and spread footing bearing on the recompacted stiff
clayey soils anticipated at the project site This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind
or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively
firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 7
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent
material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system
A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for foundations bearing on the in situ stiff soils
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for
foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 8
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils as recommended herein to be less
than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of 0 5 inch
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depth
Slab On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with a slight expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress resulting from shrinking and
swelling of these clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we recommend
that the subgrade soils below the slab concrete be properly prepared and capped with at
least 6 inches of base course materials The 6 inch compacted base course will also
serve to provide more uniform support for the reservoir slab The base course materials
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of the maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM D 1557 Test Methods for Laboratory Compaction Characteristics of
Soil Using Modified Effort The in place density of the compacted base course should be
determined in accordance with ASTM D 1556 90 Standard Test Methods for Density and
Unit Weight of Soil in Place by the Sand Cone Method or ASTM D2922 Standard Test
Methods for Density of Soil and Soil Aggregate in Place by Nuclear Methods Shallow
Depths The area adjacent to the reservoir slab should be backfilled tightly against the
slab with compacted on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce
the potential for water ponding around the slab and foundation
Drainage
We recommend that a perimeter subdrain be installed along the outside edge of
the reservoir ring footing The subdrain should consist of a perforated pipe with a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 9
minimum diameter of 4 inches and surrounded by permeable gravel such as
ASTM C 33 No 67 We recommend that the perimeter subdrain not be hydraulically
connected to the gravel cushion layer under the reservoir slab
The finished grades outside the reservoir structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography and the design finished floor elevation of the
proposed new reservoir we anticipate that the majority of the site grading will consist of
minor cuts up to about 10 feet deep to match the finished floor elevation of the existing
reservoir
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should
be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
Based on our field exploration we anticipate that the on site near surface soils will
be very moist We recommend that the exposed subgrades be proof rolled with a smooth
drum roller to attain a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction If the subgrade soils
are observed to be pumping during proof rolling the pumping areas should be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 12
over excavated to expose the firm and unyielding material or a maximum of 2 feet below
the finished grade whichever is less The resulting over excavation should be backfilled
with imported fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath future slabs should be over excavated
where necessary to provide the minimum 6 inch thick compacted base course below the
slab concrete Prior to placement of the base course layer the subgrade soils should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we recommend that
the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
Aggregate base course material should meet the material requirements for
Aggregate for Untreated Base Section 703 06 as specified in the Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Imported fill
material if required should consist of non expansive imported granular material such as
crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less
than 1 percent Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Base course and imported granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 13
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to compact the fill materials
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter All fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
fill placement recommendations presented in this report water should be diverted away
from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the fill slopes
are completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Site grading operations should be observed by a_ representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
grading operation to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
scarification and excavation process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are
similar to those encountered in our field exploration

Water Lines
We envision that new water lines will be required for this project In general for
support of the water lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes The initial
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining backfill
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes
from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site clayey soils as backfill
immediately around water line pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finish grade may consist of the on site soils or select granular
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4052 00 Page 15
placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 85 percent
relative compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the trench backfill at the
level of the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction
For utility lines located below the footprint of the reservoir tank the utility trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the base course
layer The base course layer recommended below the tank floor should be placed above
the concrete to provide more uniform support for the tank slab The concrete around the
utility lines below the reservoir tank structure should be placed neat against the sides of
the excavation

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of 11 5 and 40 8 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two direct shear tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters of the subsoils The results of these tests are
summarized are shown on Plates B 2 and B 3
