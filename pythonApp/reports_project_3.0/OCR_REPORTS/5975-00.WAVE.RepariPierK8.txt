PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at Pier K8 within the Fleet Industrial Supply Center FISC of
Pearl Harbor on the Island of Oahu Hawaii We understand it is planned to demolish and
remove the existing timber fender pile system and replace it with a new concrete fender
pile system
The new concrete fender pile system will consist of 24 inch square precast
prestressed concrete piles with pneumatic rubber marine fenders Based on the
information shown in the Contract Documents the top of pile elevation is set at about
109 3 feet U S Navy Datum 100 feet U S Navy Datum USND 0 feet Mean Lower
Low Water The pile locations will be predrilled to the specified tip elevation prior to
driving The pile tip elevation is specified at about 19 feet USND

Hammer and Driving System
A single acting diesel hammer is proposed for the pile driving
operations by Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company HDCC Based on the pile
driving equipment data form provided the hammer has a ram weight of
6 615 pounds and a maximum stroke of 10 57 feet The maximum rated energy of the
is 69 990 foot pounds at the maximum pump setting
Based on the pile driving equipment data form a pile cushion consisting of at least
12 inches of plywood cushion block will be used for the pile driving operations The
plywood cushion block generally has a cross section area of approximately 568 square
inches Schematic details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and Driving
Equipment Data Form Page 1 in the Appendix
Pile Details
The specified fender piles will consist of 24 inch square precast prestressed
concrete piles The pile lengths will be cast at about 92 and 93 feet
We understand the piles will be cast from concrete with a minimum 28 day
unconfined compressive strength of 6 000 pounds per square inch psi We also
understand the effective prestress of the 24 inch square piles is at about 1 263 psi Based
on this information the maximum allowable compressive and tensile stresses in the
24 inch square precast prestressed concrete pile developed during pile driving are about
2 337 and 1 263 psi respectively
WAVE EQUATION ANALYSES
Wave equation analyses were performed based on the pile driving system
proposed by Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company and the subsurface conditions
presented in the referenced geotechnical report The wave equation analyses were
performed using the computer program GRLWEAP Wave Equation Analysis of Pile
Foundations which is an extension of the WEAP87 program developed under the
sponsorship of the Federal Highway Administration
The wave equation analyses were performed on four differing subsurface
conditions Soil Profile Types that may be encountered during the pile driving operations
The four soil profile types are anticipated based on the boring information shown on the
plans and summarized in the following table
Complete printouts of the results of the wave equation analyses are provided in the
Appendix Pages 2 1 through 5 15 A graph showing the ultimate pile capacity versus the
driving resistance is provided on Plates 1 1 through 1 4 for the four cases analyzed

Driving Stresses
A graph showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during
driving is provided on Plates 2 1 through 2 4 for the four cases analyzed Please note that
the graph was prepared based on kips per square inch ksi which is equivalent to
1 000 psi
Based on our analyses the estimated compressive and tensile stresses developed
during the pile driving for Soil Profile Types 1 through 4 are less than the allowable
compressive and tensile stresses of 2 337 and 1 263 psi for the 24 inch square piles The
estimated compressive and tensile stresses developed during pile driving did not exceed
the allowable compressive and tensile stresses for the full range of driving resistance at
Pump Settings 1 through 4
Based on the above results it is our professional opinion that the proposed pile
driving equipment is able to install the fender piles to the specified tip elevations without
over stressing the concrete fender piles
However it should be noted that the specified fender pile tip elevation of 19 feet
USND is still within the soft mud in the area represented by Soil Profile Type 4 Therefore
the fender piles will need to be pushed into place rather than driving because of the soft
soil conditions The Government Design Engineer for the fender piles should review the
driving conditions and confirm the specified requirements for the piles located in the area
represented by Soil Profile Type 4 beyond Bent 35 through the end

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer The program is based on
mathematical models which describe motion and forces of hammer driving
system pile and soil under the hammer action Under certain conditions
the models only crudely approximate often complex dynamic situations
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data which represents
normal situations In particular the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed However input data and therefore results
may Significantly differ from actual field conditions
Therefore the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and or
dynamic testing and measurements Estimates of bending or other local
non axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user
The calculated capacity blow count relationship i e the bearing graph
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking
Finally the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control the variability of the site conditions uncertainties
in the loads the importance of building and other factors

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer The program is based on
mathematical models which describe motion and forces of hammer driving
system pile and soil under the hammer action Under certain conditions
the models only crudely approximate often complex dynamic situations
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data which represents
normal situations In particular the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed However input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions
Therefore the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and or
dynamic testing and measurements Estimates of bending or other local
non axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user
The calculated capacity blow count relationship i e the bearing graph
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking
Finally the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control the variability of the site conditions uncertainties
in the loads the importance of building and other factors
