performed for the HECO Kalaheo Substation located within the Kailua Wastewater
Treatment Plant in the district of Kailua on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two 2
extinct shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the
bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located at the base of the northeastern slope of Puu Papoa
which is essentially an erosional remnant of caldera filling lava flows of the Koolau
Volcano
After the Koolau Volcano ceased its activity it was followed by a period of erosion
and subsequent submergence during which most of the eastern flank of the volcano was
removed Later glacio eustatic sea level changes during the Pleistocene resulted in the
accumulation of several hundred feet of marine and non marine sediments
Data obtained from the exploration for the secondary clarifier tanks of the plant
indicated that the northwestern and central portions of the plant are underlain by
interbedded sediments of both alluvial origins and detrital marine over tuff to basalt
formation at greater depths The current Phase III exploration for the HECO electrical
substation located at the northwestern corner of the plant is consistent with the subsoil
conditions previously encountered at the central and northern portions of the site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The project site is underlain by a layer of fill material overlying alluvial and coral
deposits to a depth of approximately 115 feet below the existing ground Saprolite
overlying tuff formation was encountered from about 115 to 133 feet the maximum depth
explored Some of the fill encountered may have been the result of placement of surplus
material generated from the adjacent construction
Based on the moderate to heavy structural loads 80 to 90 kips provided to us
we believe appreciable total and differential settlements would occur for the proposed
transformer and switchgear pads if shallow foundations are used We understand that
very small settlements are required for the transformer and switchgear foundation pads
Therefore deep pile foundations deriving support principally from a combination of the
friction between the pile shaft and the surrounding soil and end bearing on the tuff
formation are recommended to support the pads
For the lightly loaded switch frames it is our opinion that the medium stiff fill may
provide support for the anticipated structural loads of up to three 8 kips per column and
shallow spread footing or drilled pier foundations may be utilized

Transformer and Switchgear Pads
Due to the anticipated moderate to heavy structural loads 80 to 90 kips at the
transformer pad and the relatively light structural loads 10 to 15 kips at the switchgear
pad appreciable differential settlements are anticipated for shallow foundation support
We have also evaluated the option of using a mat foundation to support both pads
However the anticipated total settlement for the mat foundation would be on the order
of about 1 inch with differential settlements not exceeding about 1 that amount
Because of the rigid interconnection between the equipment we understand that
stringent differential settlement between the two pads are required As a result pile
foundations are recommended to support the transformer and switchgear pads located
above the poor subsoil conditions
Pile Foundations
Due to the anticipated significant total and differential settlements of a shallow
foundation system we recommend that pile foundations be used to support the
transformer and switchgear pads The piles will derive support from a combination
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3072 00 Page 9
of the friction between the pile shaft and the surrounding soil and from end bearing
on the underlying tuff formation at about 120 feet below the existing ground
surface The subsoils encountered above the tuff formation consist of loose to
medium dense coral sand and gravel which are not capable of providing end
bearing support of pile foundations
Pile Capacities
Pile foundations should be driven through the surficial fill and develop its
supporting capacity from a combination of friction between the pile shaft and the
Surrounding soil and end bearing on the tuff formation at approximately 120 feet
below the existing ground surface We understand that the existing structures of
the Phase Ill modifications are supported by 15 inch square piles During our
exploration a coral ledge was encountered at about 51 to 59 feet below the
ground surface It is our opinion that hard driving in localized coral ledges may
produce high tensile stresses in the square pile resulting in potential damage to the
piles particularly the corners of the square piles As a result we recommend that
16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concretes piles be used to support the
pads
An allowable compressive load capacity of 50 tons may be used for the 16 inch
octagonal piles When piles are driven in groups they should have a minimum
center to center spacing of four feet 4 to avoid capacity reduction due to group
action

Downdrag Loads
Where fill is placed over a compressible soil the latter consolidates and moves
downward relative to the pile The downward movement results in negative skin
friction forces generally known as DOWNDRAG forces on the pile shaft The
magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the compressible
layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the pile surface area
Due to the anticipated fill placement onto the slopes to the north of the project site
consolidation of the subsoils at the northern portion of the substation site would
occur The consolidation will result in downdrag loading of deep foundations
extending through the recent alluvial and coral deposit Therefore the allowable
compressive load capacities of the piles located in and near the fill areas will need
to be reduced by the anticipated downdrag loads The approximate area where
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3072 00 Page 11
pile foundations would be subjected to downdrag forces is shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The downdrag load on the pile shaft is estimated to be about 50 kips for
the 16 inch octagonal pile Therefore we have recommended higher pile
capacities to account for the combined downdrag and structural loads
To reduce the downdrag forces coating of the portion of the pile shaft within the
compressible layer with bitumen coating may be considered The bitumen coating
should only be applied to the portion of the pile which will be embedded in the
negative skin friction zone or compressible soil layer and above However due
to the anticipated hot temperature and high humidity of this area depending on
the time of year placement of the bitumen coating may be difficult during pile
installation and is not recommended

neglected due to uncertainties regarding long term soil contact

Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four 4 hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop in the pile
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This can be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the
more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the pile has
been driven to its full length or the blow counts steadily increase with depth and
reach four 4 blows per inch for the last six inches 6 of final pile penetration or
when aggregated blow counts are 45 blows for the last foot
Appropriate driving criteria will be developed after selection of the pile driving
equipment

Test Pile Program
As previously indicated a test pile program may not be feasible considering the
limited number of piles anticipated for this project However if a test pile program
is desired the test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following
objectives
1 To evaluate the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
2 To estimate the production pile lengths
3 To assess the test pile driving criteria which may be modified for use as the
production pile driving criteria
To achieve cost savings on this project we recommend that the test pile program
be eliminated for the project provided that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii
is present during all pile driving operations to observe the actual driving behavior
and to further evaluate the field performance It should be noted that the pile
design and driving criteria recommended were developed from our pile analysis
using the field exploration data Therefore observation of the pile driving
operations by Geolabs Hawaii is necessary to confirm our design assumptions

cutting of cut slopes in order to avoid the placement of sliver fills i e thin sloping
fills on the slope faces in an attempt to repair the over excavated area In the
event over cutting occurs consideration should be given to leaving the slope rather
than attempting to backfill the slope to design grade If backfilling of an over cut
slope is required keying and benching requirements should be implemented
Inclinations of four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V or flatter may be used for the
design of fill slopes Fill slopes should be constructed by over filling past the
design slope and then cutting back
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should
be utilized to reduce the potential of erosion and or instability of the finished
slopes

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of two
2 borings with depths ranging between 17 and 133 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary and
core drilling tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D
2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD
50
