This letter report presents the findings from our preliminary rockfall potential 
evaluation conducted at 2755 Kalawao Street, Lot No. 36, in Manoa on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  This report summarizes our preliminary findings which are based on a visit to the 
project site and our experience with potential rockfall conditions in similar hillside settings.  
The intent of this letter report is to provide the prospective buyer with a general 
overview of the potential rockfall hazard conditions and possible mitigation alternatives that 
may be considered for the property.  The preliminary findings presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted in this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is on the upslope side of Kalawao Street within the existing Manoa 
Hillside Estates subdivision in Manoa Valley on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The residence 
at 2755 Kalawao Street, Lot 36, was developed by cutting into the existing hillside slope to 
create the building pad. As a result, there is an existing tall rock cut slope at the rear of the 
lot with additional natural hillside slope spanning towards the top of the ridge above the 
home. 
We understand the potential buyer is interested in the purchase of the lot. However, 
the buyer has expressed concern regarding the potential for falling rock emanating from 
the rock cut slope to encroach on the rear area of the home. As a result, we conducted a 
field reconnaissance of the existing accessible site conditions with respect to the potential 
for falling rock hazards.

Preliminary Findings 
We conducted a preliminary site visit on November 9, 2017 to observe the existing 
site conditions with respect to the potential rockfall hazard conditions at the rear of the lot. 
The lot is bounded by Kalawao Street at the front of the lot and an existing rock cut slope 
and natural hillside extending further uphill at the rear of the lot. The existing three-story 
home occupies the majority of the lot footprint with some existing narrow stepped access 
corridors at the side and rear of the home. Thus, access for remedial construction activity 
appears to be difficult and will be a factor affecting the construction cost. 
Based on our review of the available lot plot plan, the existing ground surface 
elevations range from about +160 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the front of the lot to 
about + 204 feet MSL at the top of the cut slope near the rear of the lot. Based on a review 
of available regional topographic information and aerial imagery, the natural hillside slope 
above the cut slope appears to extend upward to an elevation of about +480 feet MSL at 
the summit. 
An existing concrete drainage interceptor ditch and low height chain link fence span 
across the hillside on the upper natural slope above the cut slope.  Based on our 
observations and our experience with other residential lots residing in similar hillside cut 
(excavation) settings, it appears that the lot and rear of the home may be exposed to 
potential falling rock hazards which could emanate from two possible hillside sources. The 
first and primary potential rockfall source is from the exposed rock cut face at the 
immediate rear of the home. The secondary potential source of rockfall is from the natural 
slope extending toward the summit. 
Existing Cut Slope Conditions  
We observed that the rock cut slope is generally devoid of vegetation, sub-vertically 
inclined and extends to a height of about 30 to 34 feet above the level patio elevation at 
the rear of the home. An existing narrow corridor space on the order of about 10 feet wide 
spans between the exposed rock cut face and the rear wall of the home. The cut slope is 
generally composed of layered basaltic rock representing the sequential deposition of lava 
flows of varying hardness and competency.  
We observed that the exposed rock face is composed of interbedded soft, friable 
rock (clinker lava) and hard, erosion resistant lava rock. The softer, moderately to highly 
weathered, clinker material is readily abraded by hand and is susceptible to slow and 
continual erosion by exposure to the environmental elements. In the exposed condition, 
the clinker material may ravel to produce falling rock fragments generally ranging between 
stones and cobble size rocks (typically ranging between about 2 and 6 inches in size). This 
type of rockfall may be characterized as nuisance type erosion and debris accumulation; 
however, small rock fragments of this size falling from the upper portion of the cut slope 
have the potential for injury or light damage. Continued raveling of the exposed clinker 
layers may produce undercutting of the harder more erosion resistant rock layers. As the 
overlying fractured hard rock layers become increasingly unsupported by undercutting of

Potentially viable rockfall mitigation alternatives for this cut slope site condition may 
include the following: 
Installation of a wire mesh drapery on the cut face  
Application of reinforced shotcrete covering the cut face 
Wire mesh drapery is essentially a passive rockfall control system. The purpose is 
to control the fall of rock material toward the bottom of the slope and mesh drapery for 
periodic collection and disposal. The installation of wire mesh drapery typically involves 
the drilling and grouting of ground anchors along the top of the slope to support the 
drapery system and along the bottom of the slope to hold the wire mesh drapery against 
the slope face. The lifespan of the steel component system is anticipated to be on the 
order of about 20 to 30 years before partial or whole system replacement may be 
required. Periodic cleanup of fallen/accumulated debris at the bottom of the mesh will be 
required. 
Shotcrete facing is essentially the spray application of fiber reinforced cement to 
cover the slope face and prevent erosion and release of rock debris from the slope face. 
Appropriate rock face sub-drainage such as drain mats should be installed beneath the 
shotcrete cover to permit the drainage of slope face seepage water. Shotcrete facing is 
anticipated to be a permanent mitigation alternative. 
Due to the very tight confines of the slope location in relation to the rear of the 
existing home and with limited access for traditional heavy equipment, construction 
access is anticipated to be difficult, and additional construction cost is anticipated due to 
the need for specialized equipment to accommodate the difficult access condition. Both 
the shotcrete and wire mesh drapery alternatives will require specialized equipment for 
mobilization of the materials and construction in the difficult access slope setting. 
Upper Natural Slope Conditions 
The second potential source for falling rock hazards at the project site is the upper 
natural mountain slope above the cut slope which extends toward the top of the ridgeline. 
Although the mountain slopes were not directly observed during our site visit, our 
experience is that these undeveloped rock slopes may produce larger falling rock hazards 
including boulders larger than 3 feet in dimension. Because of the steep slope inclination 
and the typical stepped character of the basalt rock ledge outcroppings on the valley 
slopes, falling rock has the potential to gain great momentum including complex bouncing 
and rolling trajectories at high velocity. 
Although there is an existing drainage interceptor ditch and a small chain link fence 
on the lower portion of the upper slope, these existing features are likely not designed for

To address the potential hazard, a geological reconnaissance of the upper slope 
area above Lot No. 36 to obtain an overview of the existing slope conditions would assist 
in determining the relative level of risk and potential for rockfall hazard conditions. With this 
geological field information, possible mitigation alternatives would be proposed to reduce 
the potential for upslope rockfall to affect the property.  
The installation of a specialized rockfall impact barrier may be a viable mitigation 
alternative for the upper slope area provided that permission is granted by the upslope 
landowner to access the slope for study and construction purposes. It should be noted that 
issues such as land ownership, adjacent property boundaries, land use ordinances, 
topographic survey needs, possible operation and maintenance easement requirements, 
and the close proximity of other non-participating residential structures have the potential 
to impede implementation of this mitigation alternative at the project site. 
It should be noted that the construction of some rockfall hazard mitigation 
alternatives, such as rockfall impact barrier fencing or individual rock outcrop stabilization 
conducted in existing tightly developed site conditions with adjacent property owners, may 
be a sensitive undertaking since the construction activity could be perceived as slope 
disturbance for protection of a single lot. It should be noted that the installation of a rockfall 
impact barrier system across a hillside typically involves ground disturbance and may 
impart some potential hazard to down slope development during construction. This is due 
to the number of post foundations and ground anchors that require excavation, drilling and 
grouting to support the steel barrier support posts and interconnecting flexible ring net 
panels. Due to the existing difficult site access conditions, helicopter mobilization of the 
construction materials may be the only viable way to access the hillside for barrier 
construction. 
It should also be noted that the rockfall hazard mitigation measures can only be 
constructed within the homeowner’s property limits with applicable setbacks from the 
adjacent property boundaries. Therefore, there remains some potential for falling rocks 
from adjacent higher slope areas to have erratic rolling and bouncing trajectories that 
could deflect and enter the homeowner’s property from the sides of the rockfall barrier 
fence despite having the protection barrier in place. Finally, due to the tight cluster of 
existing homes along the base of the slope, there is potential for the perception of 
neighboring properties to view the on-slope improvement work as a possible cause of 
future rockfall that could affect adjacent unprotected properties.  
Limitations 
This report is based, in part, on a visual assessment of the exposed and accessible 
areas of the project site visible from the base of the slope at the yard elevation. It is not 
possible to observe all of the existing site conditions during a brief site reconnaissance.
