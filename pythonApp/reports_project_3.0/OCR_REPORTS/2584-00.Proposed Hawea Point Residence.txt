INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the design of the proposed Hawea Point Residence for the Liem Family at
The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the exploration These recommendations are intended for use in project planning
and to provide engineering design input The recommendations and findings presented
herein are not intended for other uses nor are they intended for final construction
estimation or bidding purposes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface soils
and rock conditions at the site of the proposed residence in order to provide data for the

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated at Hawea Point in the Kapalua area on the island of
Maui Hawaii The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
The site is situated to the west of the Kapalua Resort development area The
subject site is an exposed point of land overlooking the ocean below Kapalua Bay Hotel
and Villas The site is vacant land vegetated with scrub haole koa naupaka and
ironwood trees There is a navigation warning light on the site and several footpaths
traverse throughout the property
The topography of the site slopes gently towards the west Along the southern
western and northern boundaries of the site the ground falls off sharply forming sea cliffs
about 20 to 50 feet in height Along the northerly cliff the ground surface is exposed
volcanic rock formation
The existing ground surface elevation of the site varies from approximately 40 to
about 68 feet above Mean Sea Level

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a luxury residence at the subject site Based on the
plans provided to us it is anticipated that the residence will consist of several intercon
nected structures or wings and that these wings will be of concrete and masonry
construction It is also anticipated that the new buildings will impose moderate structural
loads on the soil rock formations
Based on the preliminary plans provided to us for support of our exploration it is
anticipated that most of the project earthwork will be to create level pads for the various
wings This grading will involve relatively shallow cuts and fills on the order of five 5 or
So feet in depth or thickness However a deep cut of about 16 feet below the existing
ground surface will be required to create a level depressed area for the future tennis court
area
Several retaining walls will be constructed to provide grade separation between the
various wings of the residence and between other portions of the development
The project will also include driveways and parking areas Both flexible and rigid
pavements are anticipated
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The western half of the island of Maui is composed of a single basalt shield
volcano built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Wailuku Volcanic Series during the late
Pliocene and early Pleistocene As this main shield building phase drew to a close there
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of the andesitic and trachytic basalt lavas
of the Honolua Volcanic Series
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2584 00 Page 6
Rocks of the Honolua Volcanic Series cover most of the western half of the island
of Maui These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense andesitic basalt
The subject site is situated on a point of one of the thick andesitic basalt flows
extending into the ocean
Exposed Subsurface Conditions
The cliffs along the ocean side boundaries of the property are generally dense
basalt rock and areas of indurated clinker that have been subjected to wave erosion At
the crest of the site there is a thin overburden of few feet of silty ash soil which grades
with depth to indurated clinker In some areas of the site there are pockets of wind
blown calcareous sand which have accumulated over the ash and clinker Also observed
were scattered outcrops of the underlying andesitic rock
Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
eleven 11 borings to depths ranging between 6 5 and 25 0 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
In general most of our borings at the proposed facility site encountered a thin
surface layer of windblown sand or clayey silt residual soil underlain by dense or
moderately cemented gravel and cobbles a a clinker and a massive dense basalt
formation to the maximum depths explored
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during the time of our
exploration

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our findings development of the proposed project site is feasible
provided that our recommendations are followed Our exploration encountered
competent soils and volcanic rock formation underlying the site It is our opinion that
shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to support the proposed
structures It is our further opinion that the steep slopes along the seaward boundaries
of the site are relatively stable in their current state
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting stripped and
processed material should be stockpiled for use in landscaping and or general yard fill
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operations should be
overexcavated to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill
Generally we anticipate that earthwork will involve two main types of material e g
soils and excavated voicanic rock materials Based on our exploration the majority of the
materials generated from these cuts will be volcanic rock materials including the clinker
material The upper soils are very thin and should either be blended into the rocky
excavated materials or be reserved for landscaping and top dressing over the rocky fill
materials The on site excavated rock materials should be suitable for use as engineered
fill if properly processed placed and compacted If additional imported material is
required the material should conform to the requirements stipulated in Section 30 of the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Select Borrow for Subbase
Course dated September 1986
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2584 00 Page 8
lf the volcanic rock materials encountered at the site are processed and reduced
to a relatively well graded select granular material with a maximum size of about six 6
inches the fills and embankments may generally be considered to be equivalent to
earthfill In this case no special choking layer would be required if some losses of topsoil
are considered acceptable
Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should be placed in level
lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper
two 2 feet of fills and backfills below road or structure subgrade level should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 maximum dry density
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one
vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the underlying soil or basalt formation
to lessen potential instability of the new fill A schematic presentation of the keying and
benching is provided on Plate 3
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to determine the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate
compaction

Slope Setbacks
Most of the ocean side boundaries of the site consist of nearly vertical slopes Our
reconnaissance and exploration indicates that these slopes are composed of volcanic
rock materials which are relatively competent
In general these slopes appear to be stable in their natural state However there
is a possibility of block failures in portions of these slopes as a result of wave erosion at
the bases of the slopes Therefore we recommend that site grading and future structures
be set back from these steep slopes to avoid imposing surcharge loads on them which
could potentially result in failure or sliding of the materials along joints in the rock
formation We recommend that the minimum building or embankment setback should be
defined by a line determined by an imaginary plane at a ratio of one half horizontal to one
vertical from the toe of the existing slope or a minimum of fifteen 15 feet from
the top of the slope whichever is greater
Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site and in its vicinity
indicate that the upper portion of the site is most likely underlain by clinker and dense
basalt at relatively shallow depths Portions of the clinker material appear to be
moderately cemented It is anticipated that hard excavation and or ripping may be
required in cemented areas of the clinker horizons Very hard excavation will be required
in the dense basalt rock formation Generally hard excavation is considered to be
techniques such as heavy ripping hoerams or splitting The use of blasting in deeper
cuts may be considered only if it is allowable in the area of the project site Generally
the excavation method should be left to the contractor
Design slope ratios of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may be used for cut
and fill slopes

Foundation Support
Our field exploration encountered a very competent basaltic rock formation at
relatively shallow depths below the existing ground surface This formation is capable of
supporting very large structural loads However the boring data and our visual
observations at the site indicate that the depth to the top of this rock formation is highly
variable It is anticipated that rock maybe be found in some portions of an individual
footing excavation with clinker or soil found in other portions
An allowable bearing capacity of 5 000 p s f may be utilized for the design of
foundations bearing on the clinker or basalt rock formation This value may be increased
by one third for temporary wind and seismic loads The footings should be embedded
a minimum of one 1 feet below the lowest adjacent grade
An allowable bearing capacity of 4 000 p s f may be utilized for the design of
foundations bearing on granular fill placed and compacted in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein This value may be increased by one third for
temporary wind and seismic loads Footings bearing on fill should be embedded a
minimum of two 2 feet below the lowest adjacent grade
Foundations should only bear on one type of material i e footings should not
span from soil to rock If a foundation will span between the two materials the portion
of the subgrade in rock should be overexcavated a minimum of twelve 12 inches The
overexcavated area should be backfilled with select granular material placed in six 6 inch
lifts and compacted to 90 of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 The foundation should be designed for an allowable bearing capacity
of 4 500 p s f This will provide uniform support for the foundation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2584 00 Page 11
For areas where a long span of rock is encountered in the foundation excavation
e g a strip footing the over excavation may be transitioned over a distance of five 5
feet from the above recommended twelve 12 inches to no overexcavation in the rock
formation This would reduce the amount of overexcavation while still providing uniform
support for the footing
If any soft or weak areas are encountered at the bottom of any footing excavation
they should be treated by overexcavation of the soft and or weak materials to competent
rock and backfilling of the resultant depression with select granular material placed in six
6 inch lifts and compacted to 90 of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78

2 Higher allowable bearing capacities up to 6 000 p s f may be used for

retaining walls should not bear on fill or residual soils
3 A passive earth resistance of 450 p s f per foot of depth may be used for
the design of wall footings on level areas This value assumes that the

material placed and compacted as recommended in Site Grading is used
to backfill against the foundation The passive pressure must be reduced
for walls located on slopes

against sliding of the wall
5 The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a

ground the footing should extend to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback from
the outside edge of the footing measured horizontally to the slope face
6 A subsurface drainage system subdrains and or weepholes should be
utilized behind the walls to minimize the build up of hydrostatic pressure
behind the walls Subdrains should be constructed properly to drain water
away from the wall
7 Select granular backfill material such as ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel 3B
Fine is recommended behind the retaining walls to minimize excessive
compaction requirements and to aid in the drainage of the area behind the

Slabs On Grade
It is anticipated that the floors of proposed buildings on the project site will be
concrete slabs on grade Concrete slabs subject to vehicular traffic should be considered
rigid pavements and are discussed in a subsequent section
Building slabs and their cushions may be supported by the in situ volcanic clinker

Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were conducted in the vicinity of the proposed storm drain
leachfields In general the field testing indicated that the rock materials at these locations
have moderate to good permeability or infiltration characteristics However based on our
boring data the permeable horizons were all encountered at depths of more than five 5
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2584 00 Page 15
feet below the existing ground surface In addition the movement of the water introduced
in the percolation tests appears to be predominantly along joints in the underlying rock
formation Therefore it appears that the use of shallow leachfields is not practical It is
our recommendation that from a geotechnical engineering point of view the use of other
deeper disposal methods such as drywells be investigated
Based on our field permeability testing the underlying rock formation shows
permeability values ranging from about 0 001 to about 0 1 centimeters per second
Generally the drywells should be designed in accordance with Detail No R 40 of the
Standard Details for Public Works Construction September 1984 except that inlets would
be required for water piped to the drywells from other portions of the site
The drywells should be constructed as large as possible and as deep as possible
to maximize the potential of intercepting joints in the rock formation Consideration
should be given to overfracturing the rock in the sides and base of the drywells to further
increase the potential of intercepting joints Using the median value of the calculated
range of permeability a drywell having dimensions of five 5 feet in diameter and depth
of twenty 20 feet in the basalt formation would handle a flow on the order of about 0 4
cubic feet per second and give a temporary storage volume of about 390 cubic feet
Please be advised that the use of drywells is regulated by the State of Hawaii
Department of Health as injection wells and that permits would be required to construct
and operate drywells at the project site If it is decided to utilize drywells for the disposal
of storm water runoff we would be pleased to offer a proposal for providing our services
in assisting in preparation of the permit application and for testing of the completed
drywells as required under Department of Health regulations

APENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of eleven 11 borings to depths ranging between 6 5 and 25 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Truck
mounted rotary drilling equipment was utilized to drill the borings This equipment was
mobilized to Maui from our Honolulu office
Drilling in the upper soils was accomplished by using continuous flight augers to
advance the borings Soil samples were obtained by using drive penetration samples
driven by a 140 pound hammer freefalling 30 inches The number of blows required to
drive the sampler one 1 foot or a fraction thereof are presented at the appropriate
sample depths on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 11
The drive samplers used were the 2 0 inch O D Standard Penetration Sampler and
sampler to obtain and preserve relatively undisturbed samples for laboratory testing
Samples of the rock material encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit
to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the annulus of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing The recovered cores
were logged by an Engineer or Geologist from our firm Descriptions of the recovered
rock cores are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 11
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material which is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the
Boring Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative
quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

SITE DESCRIPTION
The subject site is situated in the Kapalua area on the west coast of the island of
Maui Hawaii
The subject site is an exposed point of land overlooking the ocean below Kapalua
Bay Hotel and Villas The site is vacant land vegetated with scrub haole koa naupaka
and ironwood trees There is a navigation warning light on the site and several footpaths
traverse throughout the property
The ocean boundaries of the site are near vertical cliffs about 20 to 50 feet in
height Along the northerly cliff the ground surface is exposed volcanic rock formation
SITE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The western half of the island of Maui is composed of a single basalt shield
volcano built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Wailuku Volcanic Series during the late
Pliocene and early Pleistocene As this main shield building phase drew to a close there
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of the andesitic and trachytic basalt lavas
of the Honolua Volcanic Series
Rocks of the Honolua Volcanic Series cover most of the western half of the island
of Maui These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense andesitic basalt
The subject site is situated on a point of one of the thick andesitic basalt flows
extending into the ocean

Exposed Subsurface Conditions
The cliffs along the ocean side boundaries of the property are generally dense
basalt rock and areas of indurated clinker that have been subjected to wave erosion At
the crest of the site there is a thin overburden of few feet of silty ash soil which grades
with depth to indurated clinker In some areas of the site there are pockets of wind
blown calcareous sand which have accumulated over the ash and clinker Also observed
were scattered outcrops of the underlying andesitic rock
DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS
In general our reconnaissance of the site indicates that development of the site
for residential purposes is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view The
andesitic basalt rock formation which appears to underlie the site at relatively shallow
depths should provide adequate bearing for the proposed site improvements
Based on our visual observations the steep cliffs surrounding the site should be
relatively stable in terms of engineering design life However structural site improvements
should be set back from the cliffs a sufficient distance to provide a factor of safety against
headward erosion of the cliffs which could cause slope failure as rock falls along the cliff
line
Additional Exploration
This reconnaissance was performed on the basis visual observations of the
exposed site conditions The subsurface conditions within volcanic formations may be
variable and complex The discussions and findings presented in this report are general
in nature and as such should not be considered adequate for final design
