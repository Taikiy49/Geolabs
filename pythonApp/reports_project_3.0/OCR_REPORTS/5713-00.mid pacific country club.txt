SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the New Maintenance Buildings at Mid Pacific Country Club project on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and recommendations resulting from our
field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These recommendations
are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade and site grading only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is within the Mid Pacific Country Club in the Kailua area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The project involves the construction of two different structures
on the country club property a new maintenance building near the existing maintenance
facility and an operations support building between the existing clubhouse building and
the driving range The maintenance building site is in a flat relatively grassy area
between the 10 green and the 9 fairway The new development will consist of a
one story building with an area of approximately 7 500 square feet occupying an area
of one of the existing buildings which will be demolished and expanding into an area
identified as Alternate Siting 2 We anticipate a steel frame structure with masonry
walls and concrete floor slab
The operations support building site is on a gentle slope between the driving
range and an access road adjacent to the north of the clubhouse building The new
development will consist of a two story structure of about 2 200 square feet and will be
used for storage office space and driving range support facilities

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is largely composed of the weathered remnants of the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that
Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below the Koolau lavas in central Oahu
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell because less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Sub aerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits
and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed sub aerial erosion of
the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively
high energy conditions
During periods of insignificant sea level changes continued stream action extended
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed distinct ledges

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings extending
to depths of approximately 21 to 26 feet below the existing ground surface We drilled two
borings Boring Nos 1 and 2 each to a depth of 21 feet below the existing ground
surface for the new maintenance building We drilled two more borings Boring Nos 3
and 4 to depths of 21 and 26 feet below the existing ground surface for the new
operations support building The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
At the maintenance building site we generally encountered approximately 2 feet of
medium dense sandy fill and very stiff to hard clayey alluvial deposits extending to about
9 to 14 feet below the existing ground surface The alluvial deposits are underlain by a
layer of beach sand and coralline detritus material extending to a depth of about 21 feet
below the ground surface
At the operations support building site the soil conditions between the east and
west sides Boring Nos 4 and 3 were quite different On the hilly western side
Boring No 3 we encountered approximately 5 feet of fill over a layer of hard residual
clayey silt with native rock remnants extending to a depth of about 10 5 feet Below the
residual soil we encountered highly weathered basalt rock extending to a depth of 21 feet
below the ground surface At the eastern side Boring No 4 adjacent to the driving
range we encountered approximately 9 5 feet of fill consisting of 2 feet of very stiff
clayey silty soils and 7 5 feet of loose gravel material possibly from a previous excavation
or for drainage purposes overlying beach sand deposits to a depth of about 18 feet
below the ground surface The beach deposits were underlain by coralline sandstone and
very stiff clayey residual soil with remnant rock structures extending to the maximum
depth drilled of approximately 26 feet below the ground surface

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of fill material overlying stiff
alluvial residual soils and beach coralline reef deposits extending to the maximum depth
drilled of approximately 26 feet below the existing ground surface We did not encounter
groundwater in the borings during our field exploration
Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the new maintenance and operations support buildings An allowable bearing
pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used in the design of footings
bearing on stiff on site clayey soil or a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select
granular fill materials
Because of expansive soils present near the ground surface concrete slab on
grade should be designed properly to reduce the potential for future distress resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the near surface clayey soils We recommend properly preparing
and capping the subgrade with a 24 inch thick layer of non expansive select granular fill
material The lightly reinforced slabs on grade should be a minimum of 5 inches thick and
reinforced with No 3 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center in both directions

select granular fill materials This bearing value is for sizing footings based on

We recommend embedding the footings 24 inches below the lowest adjacent
exterior grade based on the subsurface conditions at the site Foundations next to utility
trenches or easements should be embedded below an imaginary 45 degree plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the footings should
extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If the shallow foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of footings to be on the order of
about 1 inch or less Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on
similar materials should be on the order of about 0 5 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
A Geolabs representative should monitor the footing excavations prior to placing
the reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the
required embedment depths

3 3 Site Grading
We anticipate that the site grading will involve minimal cuts and fills in order to
achieve the design grades In general a Geolabs representative should monitor site
grading operations to observe whether the exposed soil conditions during excavation
are similar to those encountered in our exploration
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site Materials
generated from the demolition should also be removed and disposed properly off site The
excavated on site soils encountered in the cut areas may be stockpiled and re used as a
source of general fill materials provided that the soils meet the requirements for general
fills presented later in this section

resulting over excavated areas should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches

compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to

3 5 Underground Utilities
We envision that new utility lines and connections may be needed In general we
recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the pipes Free draining granular materials such
as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe
damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site clayey materials or
imported granular materials To reduce the potential for future ground subsidence the
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about
8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent
relative compaction
3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new maintenance building should be sloped to
shed water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised to install gutter systems around the building structures and to divert the
discharge away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be backfilled properly against the walls or slab edges immediately

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
four borings extending to depths of approximately 21 to 26 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
laboratory testing Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples to aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test results
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Three one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on samples of the relative
undisturbed or remolded soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface
soils The test results are presented on Plate B 2
