INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of the owners representative
of Kuilima Development Company we have conducted a field observation and testing
of the standby injection wells for the Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant
Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated
and the Department of Health UIC Injection Well Status Report
Guidelines Version 1 dated June 1992
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations based on the site
observation performance of short term injection testing on the wells and interview with
treatment plant operators
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for the
disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Turtle Bay resort
development The majority of the effluent treated at the plant is used for irrigation of
the resort golf course within the Turtle Bay complex Therefore the wells are used for
the injection of treated effluent only on a standby basis during periods of time when the
effluent cannot be utilized for irrigation purposes generally due to wet weather
conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII

As acondition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit issued for the
facility UIC Permit No UO 1532 it is required that periodic monitoring of the wells be
made by a professional engineer or geologist familiar with injection well operation This
observation is intended to ascertain that the wells remain operational
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our work on the project was to conduct an inspection of the
injection wells to make visual observations of their operational status In order to
accomplish this task we have conducted a program consisting of the following work
efforts
1 A visit to the site by a geologist of our firm familiar with the operation of
injection wells Activities during the site visit included an interview with
operators of the Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant observation of
the well heads and sumps observation of the metering device for the
wells sounding of the open portion of the wells and visual observation of
water samples bailed from the wells
In addition three short term injection tests were performed to monitor the
operation of each of the three injection wells
2 Analyses of the operational data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering discussions pertinent to the performance of the injection
wells
3 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and discussions

4 Coordination of our work by a Geologist from our firm
5 Quality assurance of our overall work on the project by a Principal
Engineer of our firm
6 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
GENERAL INFORMATION
The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant had been granted a permit to
operate injection wells under the Underground Injection Control program In this permit
the facility is identified by Facility ID No and the wells are identified as Wells
A B and C
The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant located mauka or southerly of
Kamehameha Highway is a privately owned treatment facility that is used for the
treatment of sanitary wastes generated within the Turtle Bay resort development The
plant is generally of the aeration lagoon type and has a design hydraulic capacity of
about 1 32 million gallons per day mgd with a peak capacity of However
currently only two of the four ponds are being utilized with a peak hydraulic capacity
of At present the average daily flow through the plant is about 250 000 to
280 000 gallons per day of domestic wastewater The current average daily irrigation
requirement of the G 2 Links Course is approximately 400 000 gallons per day The
actual irrigation requirement of the golf course varies with seasonal changes in rainfall
temperature and wind conditions The effluent is piped from the treatment plant to the
golf course area which is located makai or northerly of Kamehameha Highway for
disposal

The wells are utilized only on a standby basis The primary method of disposal
is by irrigation of the resort golf course area Links Golf Course G 2 If necessary it
is also possible to divert effluent for the irrigation of the Turtle Bay Golf Course G 1
The current irrigation needs of Golf Course G 2 average approximately 400 000 gallons
per day The daily irrigation requirements typically exceed the amount of treated
effluent presently generated A daily operating log of the injection well system
maintained by the Turtle Bay Wastewater Plant is presented in Appendix A of this

DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
Well Site Conditions
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of
Golf Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and
the moat around Punahoolapa Marsh A photograph of the well field vicinity is
presented on Plate C 1
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults approximately 4 feet
by 4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The vaults are covered with
hinged steel plate doors
Inside of the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant representatives for the inspection and performance of the
injection tests Based on our recommendation provided in the Injection Well Inspection
Report dated two inch diameter inspection ports were installed to
eliminate the need for removal of the entire top plate during future inspection testing
GEOLABS HAWAII

At any time during the injection of effluent two of the wells are placed in use for
effluent disposal The three wells are placed into operation on a rotating basis so that
at any given time one well will not be in operation Flow to the wells are controlled by
a valve located adjacent to each well head

Injection System
The injection system is regulated by valves which can be used to isolate the
wells so that any one or combination of the wells may be used for injection At the time
of our site visit Wells A and C were currently in use for the injection of effluent
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 10 Page 9
The system is also equipped with a flow totalizing meter This meter measures
all flow to the well field rather than to individual wells Flows to individual wells may be
estimated by records of which valves are open during injection periods
At the time of our site visit the totalizing meter had an initial cumulative reading
of gallons According to in 1992 the original reading on
the meter when the well field was placed in service was 400 000 gallons
Short Term Injection Tests
The short term injection tests were performed by diverting treated effluent from
the treatment plant into each well separately for a period of about 9 minutes Each
well was isolated from the others by adjusting the valves controlling the effluent flow
Because the system was in operation at the time of our site visit initial head levels
were recorded as being higher than normal due to the need to continue diverting
effluent to an inactive well to keep the system operational in between tests
During injection the head level response was measured and recorded
Readings from the totalizing meter were recorded prior to and after each short term
injection test to obtain the total quantity of injected effluent and to estimate the average
flow rate into each well
In general it was observed that the operation of the wells are satisfactory The
average injection rates ranged from about 461 to 561 gallons per minute gpm During
injection at these rates the head levels in all three wells were observed to rise steadily
to nearly the top of the well casing within the 9 minute test duration Wells A and C
appeared to have the smaller capacity as some of the flow had to be diverted to
another well during the injection testing to avoid overflowing the well head Based on
our observations of the injection testing Well B appears to be the more permeable well

of the three The results of the short term injection testing indicate the wells may be
experiencing a slight reduction in overall capacity since our June inspection However
this may be due to the greater use of the well system in the months preceeding our
current site visit Appendix B of this report summarizes the results of the short term
injection tests
Recommendations
To maintain the injection capacity of the wells we recommend that an operation
and maintenance program be continued There is a tendency for injection wells to clog
over time as a result of suspended solids in the effluent and biological action in the
wells In general to reduce the level of biological activity the use of the wells should
be rotated so that the wells are allowed to recover between extended periods of
injection
To partially restore capacity lost by the introduction of suspended solids and gas
accumulation due to biological activity we recommend that the wells be redeveloped
on a periodic basis It is our opinion that airlift pumping could be effective for
redevelopment of the wells Other methods of redevelopment such as jetting or
scrubbing the interior of the well may be too abrasive and may damage the well screen
The method of redevelopment should be carefully evaluated by a professional
experienced with the available well development methods and the construction of
injection wells
Redevelopment should be scheduled and conducted based in part on field
observations recorded by Treatment Plant personnel of the system operation and head
levels within the wells during injection In addition the results of our bi annual short
term injection testing of the wells may be used to aid in determining whether the
injection wells could be experiencing a reduced capacity between inspection visits

Generally redevelopment should be conducted if observed head levels indicate an
average increase of about 10 to 15 percent over conditions established after a
redevelopment event
