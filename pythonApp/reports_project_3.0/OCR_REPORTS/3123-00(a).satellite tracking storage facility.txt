SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
Diego Garcia is a footprint shaped atoll of the Chagos Archipelago The atoll is
about 850 miles south of India in the Indian Ocean
The origin of coral atolls was first postulated by Charles Darwin in 1842 His theory
was that an atoll began as a fringing reef around a volcanic island The island begins to
subside tectonically at a slow rate so that the coral growth maintains a reef line As the
island subsides and becomes smaller the reef grows larger and becomes a barrier reef
When the island finaily disappears below sea level the upward growing reef encloses only
a lagoon and becomes an atoll
The basic theory has been somewhat modified by other considerations such as
eustatic sea level changes and cooled waters brought on by glacial activity Generally the
ocean side of an atoll is built up of larger pieces of rock ripped off the hard reef and
worked up during storms while the lagoon side is composed of finer material
The usual atoll island stands between 5 to 30 feet above sea level The reef rock
platform is generally about the low tide level The island detritus may spill off the reef rock
platform and build up towards the lagoon side of the island
The subsurface conditions in a coral atoll vary substantially in both the horizontal
and vertical dimensions Much of the large scale vertical variation is the result of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 A Page 6
worldwide glacio eustatic sea level changes that occurred during the Pleistocene Ice
Age One significant low stand of the sea was a stand of relatively long duration at about
60 feet below present sea level Throughout the world many of the coral atolls were
eroded to this low stand and the materials above this level are the result of rapid growth
and accumulation of coralline deposits since the mid to late Pleistocene The horizontal
variation is largely dependent upon the environment in which the coralline materials grew
or were deposited

Seismic Considerations
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during an
earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore
water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough due to a large areal extent
of the sand deposit to dissipate the build up of pore pressure the effective stress
internal strength of the soils is reduced Under sustained vibration the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear
strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil
acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not
confined will result in significant deformations Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are
loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is
generally acknowledged that liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 50
feet below the ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil are
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 A Page 8
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands are more prone to
liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands are most susceptible to liquefaction
Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
A detailed analysis for liquefaction settlements could not be performed with the
limited data obtained from our field test pit exploration However a review of the
subsurface information obtained from the deeper borings previously drilled in the vicinity
of the project site appeared to indicate that loose saturated sandy material up to
approximately 10 feet thick may be encountered below the surficial engineered fill

geotechnical engineering point of view provided that the recommendations presented in

Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the foundation design

Shallow Foundations
We understand from conversations with the project structural engineer that wall
loads of approximately 3 kips per lineal foot are anticipated for the proposed building
construction Because of the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the
site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings and slab on grade
foundations may be used for support of the proposed new facilities
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted gravelly sand
material engineered fill encountered near the existing ground surface This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 4 for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade However footings adjacent to the existing structures should extend
at least to the same depth as the existing building footings or deeper to avoid surcharging
the existing foundations The actual depths of the existing footings should be confirmed
in the field during construction
We estimate the footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils to be less than 1 inch total and inch
differential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 A Page 11
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1 horizontal to 1 vertical imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footing excavations be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over
excavated down to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should
either be backfilled with on site granular sandy material compacted to a minimum of 90
percent relative compaction or the footing bottom be extended down to the compacted
firm in situ soil Footing excavations should be observed by a qualified soils engineer
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to verify the adequacy of foundation
bearing conditions and the footing embedment depths
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 A Page 12
Special attention should be made to locate the recently backfilled test pits located
adjacent to or under the future footing areas If encountered at the proposed footing
locations the previously placed backfill material should be over excavated down to a
minimum depth of 3 feet below the footing bottom elevation and backfilled in 10 inch
horizontal lifts compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The over
excavation and backfilling operations should also be observed by a qualified soils
engineer
Slab On Grade
We anticipate concrete slab on grade will be used for the proposed new buildings
We understand that these slabs may be subjected to forklift traffic therefore a 6 inch
layer of base course aggregate is recommended below the concrete slab to serve as a
slab cushion
The base course supporting the concrete slab should consist of competent
crushed basalt aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic
inch p c i may be used for the above specified compacted base course The thickened
edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches
below the lowest adjacent grade Provisions should be made for proper load transfer
across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic
For interior slabs that are not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation gravel aggregate be provided
below the concrete slabs to provide uniform support for the slabs To reduce future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor coverings an impervious
moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 A Page 13
cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be placed above the plastic membrane
to provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel be provided
below the slabs Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width
of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Site Preparation
Minimal site grading work consisting of minor cuts and fills to provide a level
building pad is anticipated for the project construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Slabs and asphalt concrete pavings that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the
resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with horizontal lifts of compacted select
granular fill material Utilities that are to be abandoned in place should be backfilled by
pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs areas
should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Particular attention should be paid to observe the subgrade soil conditions
at the previously excavated test pit areas where a minimum of 3 feet previously placed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 A Page 14
backfill material should be over excavated backfilled in 10 inch horizontal lifts and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Imported fill material to replace the over excavated material and to raise the ground
surface to the proposed finish subgrade elevation should consist of non expansive
granular material placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
The select granular fill material should consist of either on site coral sand or off site
granular material such as crushed coral or dredged coral sand fill The material should
be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest
dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value
of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than one percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain less than 20 percent
passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill material snould be tested and approved for
its intended use by our firm prior to delivery to the project site
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of coral sand or the ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
aggregate is recommended under the pipes Self compacting granular materials such
as the ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation gravel should also be considered for the trench
backfill up to about one foot above the pipes to provide adequate support around the
pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes Select clean coral sand material may also be used to backfill around
the pipe provided all sandy material is properly compacted using water tamping method
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 A Page 15
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material such
as coral sand The backfill should be placed in maximum 10 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted
to 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations in sandy soils should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings after setting of the concrete to
develop its bearing capacity to support the design footing load
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations
