performed for the proposed Maui Central Park project located in the Wailuku area on the

subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Maui the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago was formed by the
activity of two volcanoes East Maui or Haleakala and West Maui The broad gently
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes known as the Maui Isthmus was formed
when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui
Volcano
The East Maui Volcano better known as the Haleakala Mountain makes up the
eastern portion of the island Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant with the
last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around the year 1790
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the
mountain from three rift zones probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epochs
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui have been divided into three volcanic series
The Wailuku Volcanic Series is the oldest and is comprised of the basaltic lava flows and
associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks that built the major shield volcano The
Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin discontinuous frosting of andesitic and
trachytic flows domes and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic Series
After a long period of inactivity where erosion incised deep valleys into the volcanic flows
volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and cones of the Lahaina
Volcanic Series
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts
of the two volcanoes During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fell to stands lower than
the present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean
bottom Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of
sand dunes

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located adjacent to the Maui Arts and Cultural Center
approximately one mile northwest of Kahului on the island of Maui Hawaii The site is
bounded by Kahului Beach Road to the east and Kanaloa Avenue on the other three
sides as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The site exists as partially developed land consisting of an undulating
topography of sand dunes and some level areas An operating archery range is
situated near the central portion of the site and consists of foot trails and dirt roads
connecting various shooting ranges with one another A few small wooden shelters
exist as a part of the archery range n addition a youth center botanical garden and
YMCA facilities exist along the western boundary of the site These developments
consist of some building structures with generally level surrounding areas
The eastern portion of the site fronting Kahului Beach Road has been developed
into a horseshoe pitching park skateboard park and a BMX bicycle race track In
addition an area to the west of the horseshoe park is currenily being utilized for the
storage of construction machinery and stockpiles of various soil and rock materials A
concrete slab exists at this location and is accessed via a locked gate entry
In general the site exists as native sand dunes and is densely vegetated with
Kiawe trees and brush The sand dunes exist both as loose deposits of windblown
sand and lithified bedded deposits resembling sandstone rock The site was observed
to be windy and dry with frequent occurrences of windblown dust as a result Some
areas of the site have been graded to allow for mining of sand deposits and existing
building development Along the access trail leading to the archery range are some
abandoned vehicles and stockpiles of earth materials

Slab On Grade Foundations
In general we envision that the proposed development will consist of lightly loaded
used to support the proposed one story structures anticipated for the subject project
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot p s f may
be utilized for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils
or engineered fill material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
The bottom of all footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction f soft and or ioose materials are encountered at the
bottom of footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying
firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular material
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to

Construction Considerations
Drilled piers are desirable for the subject project because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled piers will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled pier foundations
Loose Soft Subsoils
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light pole locations are generally
underlain by dense sandy materials to depths of about 20 feet below the existing
ground surface However loose sandy soils may exist in localized areas and also
within the drilled depths of the light pole foundations To reduce the potential for
cave in of the drilled holes temporary casing may be required during the
foundation construction Therefore we recommend that casing of drilled holes be
considered as a contingent item with additive and deductive unit prices included in
the contract documents Care should be exercised during removal of the
temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the concrete pier
Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained above the
bottom of the casing during removal of the casing The anticipated high
GEOLABS HAWAII

groundwater level along the shoreline may also pose further construction
difficulties since proper observation of the sides and bottoms of the drilled shaft
may not be possible
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled piers depend to a large extent on the
contact between the pier shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the pier holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended Placement of
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required where
groundwater is encountered in the drilled holes The concrete should be placed in
a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of
the drilled hole A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to observe
the installation of drilled piers during construction
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump should be used to provide close contact
between the pier shafts and the surrounding soil The concrete should be placed
in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete
mix

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas designated to receive
fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should
be backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be
abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly
backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch loose level lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping
lean concrete under low pressure
The over excavated subgrades or areas designated to receive fills should then be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a

and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to

Fills and Backfills
The on site sandy soils generated from the excavations may be reused as a
source of fill and backfill material provided they are free of vegetation and
oversized particles Large boulders or rock fragments should be buried in the
deepest portions of the fills provided that they are not nested together and an
adequate amount of fine aggregates fills the void between the boulders to reduce
the potential of loss of fines from the upper portion of the fill Care must be
exercised to avoid placement of any boulders in locations which would conflict with
future utility line installations
Imported fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed
finish subgrades should consist of non expansive select granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material should be tested by
Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to
the project site for its intended use

Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of the
fill materials will be a critical aspect during fill placement Therefore a sufficient
number of water trucks should be available to provide adequate water for moisture
conditioning during compaction The State and Federal governments have
enacted stringent regulations pertaining to the release of dust from construction
sites Therefore it is also advisable to institute a dust control program during
grading operations to reduce the amount of airborne dust

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose sandy soils in a relatively dense state In
general we believe permanent cut and fill slopes may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter For cut and fill slopes
steeper than 4H 1V with vertical heights greater than 20 feet we recommend that
a minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at the midpoint of the slope to reduce
the potential for erosion due to surface runoff water We also recommend that
V ditches or earth berms be provided at the top and bottom of slopes

Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
A sketch of typical keying and benching requirements is shown on Plate 3 Fill
slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In the event that over cutting of a

of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Alternatively cut slopes
may be buttressed with fill slopes constructed of non expansive fills
Sandy soils such as those encountered at the project site are susceptible to

erosion control mat such as Miramat or equivalent be considered to reduce the

vegetative cover is well established

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling eight
borings to depths ranging from about 6 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rock coring
tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
aS __ examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Acountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The field percolation rates of the on site soils at the proposed retention basins
We were estimated by performing four falling head percolation tests in Boring Nos P 1 and
to depths ranging from approximately 2 5 to 3 feet below the existing ground
surface The boreholes were then presoaked for about 10 minutes prior to testing
During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to the top of the boreholes
_ The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which to
base calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils The test results are presented
on Plates C 1 through C 4
