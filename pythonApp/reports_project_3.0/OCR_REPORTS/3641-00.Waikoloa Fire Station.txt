INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Waikoloa Fire Station project in the Waikoloa area in the
District of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that a new fire station is planned near the intersection of Waikoloa
Road and Puu Melia Street in the Waikoloa area in the District of South Kohala on the
island of Hawaii The subject lot is approximately triangular in shape and encompasses
an area of approximately 2 acres
Based on the information provided to us the new fire station building and parking
lot will be located on the north side of the property The new fire station building will be a
one story structure measuring approximately 130 feet long and 50 feet wide Based on
the information provided by the project structural engineer maximum column loads are
estimated to be on the order of 20 kips per column with wall loads of approximately
1 8 kips per lineal foot Concrete slabs on grades are also planned for the proposed
building An asphaltic concrete access road and parking lot are planned to serve the new
facility

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which
spread and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities
are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the
lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and
inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a
large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizonta extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the
porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

Our field exploration at the proposed fire station site generally encountered
approximately 6 to 10 feet of medium dense clinker materials over hard basalt rock
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below the existing
ground surface On the basis of the generally competent subsurface conditions
encountered at the project site it is our opinion that shallow spread and or continuous
footings may be used to support the proposed fire station building An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design of footings
bearing on the recompacted near surface clinkers and or on engineered fill materials
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic
lava flows we recommend that the excavated slab subgrades be proof rolled with a
minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy construction equipment a minimum
of 6 passes io assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities In addition a
program of probing and grouting the building footing locations should be implemented
during the project construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe the
probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities if found Special
attention should be given to the Foundation Probing section of this report
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed fire station site is generally
underlain by clinker materials consisting of basalt gravels and cobbles at shallow depths
In general we believe that the near surface clinkers may be excavated with normal heavy

Building Foundations
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we
anticipate that the proposed fire station will straddle across cut and fill areas of
approximately 5 feet thick Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions
encountered and the structural loads anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion
that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for
support of the proposed one story building An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000
pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of footing foundations bearing
on the recompacted near surface clinkers and or on engineered fill materials This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
select granular material 3 inch minus material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction or the footing bottom should be extended down to the underlying
competent material
The bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below
the lowest adjacent finished grade For footings located near the top of slopes or on
slopes the footings should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance
from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the edge of the slope is not less than
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3641 00 Page 9
8 feet If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
clinkers and or engineered fill materials Resistance to lateral loads due to passive
pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 400 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth p c f These values are based on the assumption that the soil
around footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be
neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present in the
basalt rock formation and clinkers encountered at the project site To reduce the potential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3641 00 Page 10
for loss of building foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a
footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented
during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during
the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help
delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with
a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed

Concrete slabs on grade are anticipated for the proposed fire station building Due
to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows we

drum roller or similar heavy construction equipment for a minimum of 6 passes to assist in
detecting and collapsing near surface cavities
For the interior building slabs which are not subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below

the slab concrete

crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

transfer across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic Thickened edges

pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of

discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for
adequate compaction

Excavation
Based on our field exploration the proposed fire station site is underlain by
clinkers consisting of medium dense to dense basalt cobbles and boulders at
shallow depths It is anticipated that the near surface clinkers may be excavated
with normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers

Cut and Fill Slopes

should be backfilled and or grouted if necessary
Permanent fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter All fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should
be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition
drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to
drain all surface run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

clinkers extending to a depth of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface
Therefore we recommend that the proposed drywell s be lined with concrete rings to

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths ranging from about 19 to 20 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
