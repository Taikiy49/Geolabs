SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our boring at the PTA Siren No. 1 site generally encountered surface fills underlain 
by cinder.  Surface fill generally consists of about 1 foot of medium dense silty sands with 
a little gravel. Cinder materials consisting of medium dense sandy gravel were 
encountered and extended down to the maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below 
the ground surface. 
The subsurface conditions at the PTA Siren No. 2 site generally consist of surface 
fills underlain by alluvium, volcanic ash, clinker and basalt formation. Surface fill generally 
consists of about 3.5 feet of medium dense silty sands with some gravel. Surface fills are 
underlain by interbedded layers of alluvium and volcanic ash, extending down to about 
11 feet below the ground surface. Alluvium generally consist of dense gravelly cobbles 
and medium dense to dense silty sands. Volcanic ash generally consists of stiff sandy 
silts. Clinker generally consist of dense gravelly cobbles. Basalt formation was 
encountered from about 15 feet below the ground surface. Basalt formation encountered 
were relatively unweathered and very hard. We did not encounter groundwater in the 
borings at the time of our field exploration. 
We understand that the new siren poles generally will be about 40 feet in height 
above ground with several equipment attached to them. Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered, structural loads provided, and our engineering analyses, the new 
siren pole foundations generally should consist of a single 30-inch diameter drilled shaft 
extending down to a depth of at least 10 feet below the ground surface. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the FEWR No. RB003218J Install Siren System project at the Pohakuloa 
Training Area (PTA) on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from 
our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical 
recommendations for the design of the proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of siren pole 
foundations, site preparation, and underground utility lines only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project consists of constructing two siren poles at the PTA on the Island of 
Hawaii. The siren poles will be about 50 feet in height, with about 40 feet above the ground 
surface. We envision that drilled shaft foundations may be utilized to support the new 
siren poles. 
We also envision that only minor site preparation will be required for the installation 
of the new sirens. In addition, we anticipate that new underground utility lines may be 
required for the project.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The PTA is situated in the north central portion of the Humu`ula Saddle, a broad 
plateau between Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa. Based on an available geologic map 
(USGS, 2007), the PTA area is underlain by rocks of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of 
Mauna Kea.  The map indicates that these Hamakua rocks may be mantled by recent 
alluvium that would be outwash from the slopes of Mauna Kea to the north. Since these 
outwash materials are generally cindery in nature, it is difficult to differentiate between the 
alluvial soils and air-laid cinder from adjacent cinder cones of Mauna Loa. 
In terms of engineering properties, there is not much difference between the 
cindery alluvium and air-laid cinder deposits. Both behave as granular cohesionless soils 
and are generally well-drained. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is within the PTA on the Island of Hawaii. Two new siren poles are 
planned for the project. PTA Siren No. 1 will be located between Building Nos. 177 and 
180 along Mauna Kea Loop. PTA Siren No. 2 will be located further east near Bradshaw 
Army Airfield adjacent to Building No. 390. 
The terrains at both siren sites are generally open and level. The ground surface 
generally consists of brown silty sand soils. Topographic survey map of the two sites was 
not available at the time this report was prepared.

2.4 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement 
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can

At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be

In general, fill materials required to raise the grades at the site should consist of 
structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular 
materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to 
prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In addition, structural fill 
materials should contain less than 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve by 
weight. In general, the excavated on-site materials may be reused as a source of 
structural fill materials, provided they are processed to meet the maximum particle size 
and gradation requirements presented above. 
Imported fill materials (where required) should be free of organics and deleterious 
materials and should be suitable for the intended use. In addition, imported fill materials 
should be observed and/or tested by Geolabs prior to being transported to the site for the 
intended use. 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the 
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.2.2 Foundation Settlements 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the subsurface soils. 
Total settlements of the 10-foot long drilled shafts are estimated to be on the order of 
less than 0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the settlement is elastic 
and should occur as the loads are applied. 
3.2.3 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
Based on the borings drilled at the site, medium dense sands, gravel, and cobbles 
are present at the project site. Due to the granular nature of subsurface materials and 
potential presence of loose zones, caving-in and/or sloughing of these materials may 
occur during the drilling operations. The contractor should select drilled shaft 
installation methods, such as utilization of temporary casing, which will maintain the 
integrity of the shaft hole. 
Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing, if used, to reduce 
the potential for "necking" of the drilled shaft. Therefore, a minimum 5-foot head of 
concrete above the bottom of the casing should be maintained during removal of the 
casing. 
In addition, localized zones of cobbles and boulders may be present in the alluvium 
and clinker layers. Therefore, some difficult drilling should be anticipated for the 
installation of the drilled shaft foundations. 
It is imperative that a Geolabs representative be present at the project site to observe 
the drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction and should be 
designated as a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of the 
International Building Code (2015 Edition).

3.3 
Underground Utility Lines 
We envision new underground utility lines will be required for the project 
development. We anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in 
the compacted fill, recent alluvium, volcanic ash, and cinder encountered in our borings. 
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the top of the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential 
for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support 
for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper 
placement of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
As an alternative, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill (up to about 
12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials (aggregate base materials). Where the 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials are used, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular materials 
less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are located in 
paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 19 and 20 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."
