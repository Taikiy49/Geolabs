SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a
complex series of depositional and erosional events brought on by
glacioeustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities
during the Pleistocene
Following the cessation of activity of the
Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated many times receding perhaps as much as 300
feet below and rising up to 95 feet above the present sea level
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during late Pleistocene era
eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock
layers
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of
tuff During the Recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand
the area was covered with lagoonal marine deposits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project site is underlain by a thick deposit of compressible
soil overlying a layer of boulderly material or very stiff alluvial
material at the northern part of the site
No competent bedrock
formation was encountered within the depths of our borings which
extended to 134 feet below the ground surface
Significant settlement may be anticipated due to the new fill
required for site grading
The areas adjacent to the fill boundary
which may include those for the future building the existing utility
line and Kamehameha Highway may also experience settlement due to the
new fill though to a lesser degree
Therefore special attention should
be paid to these areas during site grading
Because of the organic
nature of the compressible material we believe that long term secondary
settlement may also be anticipated
Also additional settlement in the
general area may occur as a result of lowering the regional artesian
head
It appears that pile foundation would not provide an effective
means to support the proposed building because of the absence of a
competent bearing layer within a reasonably economical depth large
amount of anticipated downdrag load and the questionable drivability of
the pile through the boulderly layer
In light of the above we would
recommend that shallow strip footings bearing on a constructed gravel
crust layer be used as building support
The footing should have
provisions to accomodate future ground settlements
It is our understanding that surcharging of the building area is
being done presently and Phase One will proceed immediately with Phase
Two following later
An extensive releveling program to correct future
settlement should be implemented
It is our understanding that the

Pile Foundation
Due
to
the
significant anticipated settlement at the site
investigation and analysis of pile foundation for the proposed
project was carried out However our findings indicated that pile
foundation
might
not
provide
an
effective
solution
to
the
foundation settlement problem because of the following
1
Negative
Skin Friction
Where
fill
is
placed
over
a
compressible soil the latter consolidates and moves downward
relative to the pile
The downward movement results in
negative skin friction forces generally known as OOWNDRAG
force on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force
depends on the thickness of the compressible layer adhesion
between the soil and pile and the pile perimeter area At the
GEOLABS HAWAII
w o 1672 00
I D 1985
Page 11
project site the average downdrag force is estimated to be on
the order of 60 tons per pile
Therefore although the
building load from the one story wood structure is anticipated
to be small the combined downdrag and building load may
require a high pile capacity Therefore relatively long piles
in excess of 150 feet may have to be used
To reduce the downdrag force coating of the entire or part of
the pile shaft through the compressible layer with bitumen may
be considered However this would add to the cost of the pile
foundation
2
Drivability of Pile
The anticipated long pile has to
penetrate through the boulderly layer and bear on the stiffer
material below
But the drivability through the boulderly
layer below the soft layers is questionable and can only be
fully determined during the
test pile stage
If found
necessary predrilling through the boulderly layer may be
required at a high additional cost
We would like to point out that if for reasons other than
geotechnical pile foundations are required as a means or an
alternative
further soil exploration should be performed to
determine the depth to possible bearing stratum and to provide a
better cost estimate for the foundation system

APPENDIX
B
Dutch Cone Penetrometer Test
Sixteen 16 dutch cone penetration tests to depths frcm 41 to 81 feet
below the ground surface were performed at the approximate locations shown
on Site Plan Plate 2
The cone penetrcmeter test consists of hydraulically pushing a steel
cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 10 square
centimeters connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 150 square
centimeters into the ground The tip and side friction resistance
induced during penetration are measured by strain gages within the
instrument and are recorded versus the depth of penetration The values
of the tip resistance and friction ratio sleeve friction over tip
resistance can be correlated to soil strength and classification They
also provide information regarding the depth to the bottcm of relatively
stiffer material
The records of the dutch cone penetrometer test and the
interpretations are shown on Plates B 1 through B 16

Laboratory Testing
The moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties Torvane tests were also performed on most
of the shelby tube sample in order to determine their undrained shear
strength The results of the moisture content Atterberg Limit and
torvane shear are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample
depths The Atterberg Limits are further summarized on Plates C 1 through
C 8
Twelve 12 sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of sand to
evaluate its grain size distribution The numerical results of the tests
are summarized on Plates C 1 through C 7 A graphical presentation of all
the results are shown on Plates C 9 through C 11
Four 4 specific gravity tests were performed on the field soil
sample to help determine the mineral content void ratio and water content
relationship The results are shown on Plates C 1 through C 7
Sixteen 16 consolidation tests were performed on undisturbed
fine grained samples to evaluate the compressibility and settlement
characteristics During most of the tests settlement time readings were
recorded in order to determine the rate of consolidation Some loadings
were allowed to act on the soil sample for a prolonged period of time in
order to study the effect of secondary consolidation The results of the
load settlement curves are presented on Plates C 12 to C 27 The
coefficients of consolidation and secondary consolidatioo resulting from
the settlement time records are summarized on Plate C 28
Seven 7 unconsolidated undrained triaxial tests were performed on
undisturbed fine grain soil to evaluate their compressive strengh The
test results are summarized on Plate C 29
Three 3 sets of total number 9 direct shear tests were carried out
on the stiff alluvial deposit to evaluate its shear strength
characteristics The results of the tests were presented on Plates C 30
through C 32

Settlement Analysis
The project site is underlain by layers of soft and compressible
clayey materials A settlement analysis was performed to evaluate the
effect of filling site
The analysis for the compressible layer was carried out by the use of
the computer program CONSOL developed by the Department of Civil
Engineering at the University of califomia at Berkeley The computer
program calculates the total and time rate of settlement for
one dimensional layered soil masses using an implicit finite differences
method
Two of the most important soil properties that the program requires as
input are the compression index and the coefficient of consolidation For
the current analysis these latter data were obtained from sixteen 16
one dimensional consolidation tests and fifteen 15 time settlement
plots
The results of the analysis are sh 7n on Plates D 1 through D 3
Plate D 1 summarized the Effect of the fill thickness on total primary
settlement of various thickness of soft deposit Plate D 2 sha ls the
variation of percentage total primary fill settlement with time
On Plate
D 3 the estimated ultimate settlement and the estimated remaining
settlement after 1 year for both the fill area and the building area are
presented
In addition to the primary settlement which occurs as a result of
consolidation long term secondary settlement due to soil creep will occur
over the design life of the structure The amount of secondary settlement
depends mainly on the thickness of the soft compressible layer and the
length of time after the completion of the primary settlement
Assuming that a soil surcharge program will be carried out to reduce
completion time of the primary settlement to one year then the estimate
of the total secondary settlement over the design life of say 50 years
will be on the order of 4 to 6 inches
