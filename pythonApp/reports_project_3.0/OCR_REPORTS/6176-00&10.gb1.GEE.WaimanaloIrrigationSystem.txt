Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load

When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes

Site Description
The Waimanalo Irrigation System is located on the Windward side in the
Ko olauloa District on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The irrigation system begins in the
Maunawili Valley area and ends in the Waimanalo area The project locations are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The source intakes for the irrigation system are from streams and tunnels in the
Maunawili Valley area The water travels through a series of ditches and pipe siphons
to Aniani Nui Tunnel which crosses Aniani Nui Ridge From the tunnel outlet the
water travels through a ditch to a concrete box that filters debris from the water The
water is taken from the box through a 16 inch diameter line to Waimanalo Reservoir
Water is distributed from the reservoir by pipes to various locations in the
Waimanalo Agricultural Farm Lot Subdivision
Access to the Maunawili side of the irrigation system is at the end of
Maunawili Road There are five intake sources Clark Tunnel Maunawili Stream Ainoni
Stream Fault Tunnel and Makawao Stream In general the irrigation ditch passes
through rugged forest terrain The typical conditions of the existing irrigation ditch
consist of an upgrade slope on one side of the ditch and a downgrade slope on the
other side In some areas the irrigation ditch is situated in a flat topography The
existing site conditions along the ditch were observed during a ground surface
reconnaissance The existing surface generally consists of stiff silty and clayey soils

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of hand drilling 14 borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 9 and 12 through 16 extending to depths of about 2 to 3 feet
below the existing ground surface
In general our field borings at the project site encountered alluvial deposits and
residual soils The alluvial deposits and residual soils consisted of stiff to very stiff
clayey silt in a moist condition The alluvial deposits and residual soils extended to the
maximum depths drilled of about 2 to 3 feet below the existing ground surface

very stiff silty clay that extended to a depth of about 4 feet below the existing ground
surface Alluvial deposits were observed below the fill and extended to the bottom of
the washout area which corresponds to about 12 feet below the existing ground
surface The alluvial deposits consisted of hard clayey silt with rounded gravel An
8 inch diameter water line which was observed at about 2 5 feet below the existing
ground surface was exposed at the washout area

SCADA Tower and Siphon Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that shallow
foundations consisting of spread footings may be used to support the SCADA towers
and the new siphon planned between Siphon 12 and Station 100 00 An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of
the foundations bearing on the in situ soils This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces
The soils encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be
re compacted to a firm and unyielding base Soft and or loose soils encountered at the
bottom of the footing excavations should be removed to expose the underlying firm
materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill or the
footing may extend down to bear on the underlying firm material
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback
distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from
the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total footing settlements may be on the order of
1 inch or less We estimate that the differential settlements between adjacent
foundations to be on the order of about 0 5 inch

Micropiles
We understand the outside edge of the new concrete irrigation ditch between
Baseline Stations 58 37 and 64 09 will be relatively close to the face of a steep slope
therefore the potential for loss of foundation support due to undermining of the concrete
irrigation ditch caused by slope erosion is relatively high To reduce the potential for
loss of foundation support we recommend supporting the new concrete irrigation ditch
on micropiles
In general a micropile consists of a small diameter usually less than 12 inches
drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile foundation is typically
constructed by drilling a hole with or without casing placing reinforcing steel in the
hole and grouting the hole Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed
readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground
conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal
disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend using a micropile with a grout bulb diameter of at least 5 5 inches to
support the new concrete irrigation ditch One micropile should be placed near the
outside edge of the new concrete irrigation ditch The micropiles should be spaced a
maximum of about 10 feet center to center along the irrigation ditch where the potential
for undermining is considered high An allowable compressive load capacity of 14 kips
may be used per micropile The allowable compressive load capacity of the micropile is

Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the micropile supported foundations to be less than 0 5 inch with
differential settlements between micropiles not exceeding about one half of the total
settlement We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should
occur as the loads are applied
Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the compressive load capacity of the micropiles is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor to install the micropile Therefore the compressive load capacity of the
micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile
foundation systems In order to determine whether the contractor s methods of
micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate compressive load
capacity we recommend performing one pre production load test at the site In
general the purpose of the pre production load test on a micropile is to fulfill the
following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
To evaluate the degree of long term settlements of the micropile

In general the pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1143 Based on experience we believe that the load test should be
conducted at least seven days after completion of the micropile installation to allow
the grout adequate time to cure Two to four additional micropiles may be used for
reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre production load test
micropile The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load
test micropile to provide adequate reaction in uplift to be determined by the
contractor
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the
allowable design load in compression In addition we recommend holding the
micropile for a minimum of 4 hours at 200 percent of the allowable design load
depending on the recorded movements of the load test micropile Please note that
the pre production load test is an integral part of the design of the micropile
foundation system
In addition to the pre production load test we also recommend performing pullout
tests proof tests on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the load
carrying capacity of the installed micropiles We recommend testing a minimum
10 percent of the total number of micropiles for pullout The pullout tests should
consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 133 percent of the design load and
the maximum test load should be held for at least 10 minutes Pullout tests on the
micropiles also are integral parts of the micropile foundation system design

3 6
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration we
believe that planned cut slopes will expose stiff to very stiff silty and clayey soils In
general planned cut slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 1H 1V or flatter
for slope heights less than 6 feet Cut slopes having an inclination of 1H 1V should be
covered with an Erosion Control Matting ECM on the slope face immediately after
cutting the slope face Planned cut slope heights greater than 6 feet should be designed
with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on site materials or imported fill
materials may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed
on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to
provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with
the above fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by
overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope
face In addition slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the
potential for erosion of the finished slopes
3 7
Excavation
Based on our field exploration the majority of the site grading work for the project
will involve cuts into the alluvial deposits and residual soils It is anticipated that the
alluvial deposits and residual soils may be excavated readily with normal heavy
excavation equipment
We believe there is a high potential for encountering relatively unweathered
cobbles and large boulders within the alluvial deposits and residual soil layers

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs also should be avoided These drainage requirements
are essential for the proper performance of the above foundation recommendations
because ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving
or loss of strength
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the foundations
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water
infiltration into the subsurface In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away from foundations
