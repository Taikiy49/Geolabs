In accordance with our fee proposal dated January 15, 2013, we conducted a 
geotechnical engineering evaluation in support of design for the UV Disinfection at 
Hawaii Kai WWTP project, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This revision reflects 
additional foundation design parameters requested by the project structural engineer. 
This revised letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering

analyses.  The recommendations presented are intended for design of the foundations 
and site grading only.  The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
It was proposed to repair/upgrade the gaseous chlorine/sulfur dioxide effluent 
disinfection system with an ultraviolet (UV) disinfection system at the Hawaii Kai 
Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) across Sandy Beach Park, on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. Based on the information provided, we understand that the existing Hawaii Kai 
WWTP is a conventional activated sludge plant currently providing secondary level 
treatment with a sand filter. 
Based on the preliminary information provided, we understand that the planned 
improvements involve utilizing an existing channel for the UV system with pumps during 
construction of a new channel.  Geotechnical exploration program focused on the 
perimeter of the existing chemical and electrical building and adjacent areas for a new UV 
structure.  Structural details were not available at the time this revised report was 
prepared.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of 
lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as 
the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by the glacioeustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly low, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site is located near the base of the southeastern flank of Koko Crater.  
Koko Crater is a compound tuff cone, part of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
Potassium-argon dating indicated that it is about 32,000 years old. Koko Crater was 
formed by violent explosions when rising lava came in contact with sea water. 
Fragments of lava as well as fragments of older volcanic rock and coral were ejected 
and deposited as tuff. The gap at the northeast side of the crater probably resulted from 
trade wind blowing most of the ash towards the southwest. 
Following the period of eruption, the crater has been modified by processes of 
weathering and erosion to create the current landform. Volcanic fragments in the tuff 
altered to palagonite through the weathering processes. Tuff has eroded from the side 
slopes of the crater creating gullies and steep scarps. The eroded materials have been 
deposited as talus and alluvium on the interior and exterior of the crater. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings each to a depth of approximately 25 feet below the ground surface.  The 
approximate locations of our drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration generally encountered about 4 to 5 feet of surface fill and 6 to 
7 feet of alluvial soils overlying residual/saprolite soils and weathered basalt rock 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 25 feet below the ground surface.

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATION 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity that can impact the planned improvements, and seismic design 
considerations may need to be addressed. 
The geotechnical data disclosed by our limited field exploration and review of the 
geologic data of the project site were used to classify the subsurface conditions for seismic 
design considerations.  Generally, the field exploration encountered very stiff alluvium and 
residual/saprolite soils overlying weathered basalt formation.  The depths of the weathered 
basalt formation encountered are approximately 20 to 23 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Based on these subsurface site conditions, we classified the site under “Very 
Dense Soil and Soft Rock Profile” corresponding to a Site Class “C” in accordance with the 
International Building Code (Table No. 1613.5.2), 2006 Edition. 
DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we envision using 
shallow spread and/or strip footings, to support the new UV structure planned at the site.  
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used 
for the design of a shallow foundation system bearing on recompacted stiff clayey soils. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site grading 
are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Foundations 
We envision that the new UV building foundation subgrades will consist of stiff 
clayey soils.  Therefore, we recommend using shallow spread and/or strip footings to

support the proposed structure at the project site.  As an alternative, the foundation for 
support of the building may also consist of thickened-edge slab-on-grade footing. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of 
shallow foundations.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces.  The modulus of subgrade reaction of 70 kcf may be used in the foundation base 
slab design. 
In general, the foundation should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on 
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of 
the slope. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated down to stiff/dense materials or to a maximum of 2 feet deep 
in the footing excavation.  The over-excavation should be backfilled with non-expansive 
select granular fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The select granular fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials 
generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the 
occurrence of voids in the compacted mass.  The selected granular fill materials should 
be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in accordance with 
ASTM D1557.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, total settlement of foundations is estimated to be 
on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of about 
0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations.  A coefficient of 
friction of 0.3 may be used for foundations bearing on the recompacted subgrade soils.  
Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for 
foundations embedded in the compacted general backfill materials.  These values 
assume that the soils around the foundations are well compacted.  Unless covered by 
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished 
grade should be neglected.

Retaining Structures 
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the parameters presented in the “Shallow Foundations” section herein. 
Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. For sloping ground conditions, the 
footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured 
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings 
oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings. 
Below-grade retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth 
pressures due to the adjacent soil and surcharge effects. In general, the following 
guidelines may be used for preliminary design of planned retaining walls. The following 
lateral earth pressures, expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of pounds per square foot 
per foot of depth (pcf), may be used in the design of retaining structures.

We anticipate that the project site may be classified as Site Class “C” from a 
seismic analysis standpoint. Therefore, design peak ground acceleration is computed to 
be 0.20g in accordance with the International Building Code (2006 Edition). Dynamic 
lateral earth forces due to seismic loading (a = 0.20g) may be estimated by using 3.2H2 
pounds per lineal foot of wall length for level backfill conditions, where H is the height of 
the wall in feet. It should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume 
that the wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 inches in the event of an 
earthquake. The resultant force should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the 
wall. An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth 
forces are accounted for in the design of the retaining structures. 
In general, retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), placed 
directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the walls 
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated 
drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the drainage 
material. The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on-site granular 
soils or select granular fill material. Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches 
of backfill should consist of low expansion, impervious soils to reduce the potential for 
water infiltration behind the walls. 
Site Grading 
We anticipate that site grading will involve less than 1 to 2 feet of cuts and fills, to 
achieve the finished grades.  In general, a Geolabs representative should monitor the site 
preparation to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions during excavation are 
similar to those anticipated and/or encountered in our field exploration.  The following 
recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site preparation 
work. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the

potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill 
materials. 
In general, the on-site materials generated from the excavations may be re-used as 
a source of general fill and backfill materials, provided that the materials are screened of 
over-sized materials (greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension) and are free of 
deleterious materials.  Where used as fill and backfill materials, the on-site soils should be 
properly moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, moisture conditioned 
to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from 
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding.  It is also advised to install gutter 
systems around the building and that the discharge is diverted away from the foundation 
areas.  Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be 
avoided considering the slight to moderate shrink/swell potential of the on-site soils.  
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to 
reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above 
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation 
and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface soils.  The foundation 
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.  In addition, drainage swales 
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface water runoff away 
from the foundations and slabs. 
Utility Trenches 
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and 
backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities.  The excavations should comply with the 
applicable federal, state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be 
responsible for trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support under the pipes. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67

below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.
