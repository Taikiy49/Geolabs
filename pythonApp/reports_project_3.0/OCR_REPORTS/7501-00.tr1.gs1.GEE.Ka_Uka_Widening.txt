Moaniani Street to Northbound Ramp project. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate

findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau, known as Schofield Plateau.  The project site is located on the Schofield 
Plateau. 
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the 
Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed 
dense vesicular basalt rock formation with inter-bedded thin clinker layers. In-situ 
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million 
years.  The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils, which grade to 
saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock 
formation with increasing depth.

Methodology of Pavement Design 
For rigid pavement construction, roadway closures will be required to allow time 
for the concrete pavement to cure and harden. Therefore, we recommend using a 
flexible pavement section for the new pavements for the widening project to reduce the 
amount of road closures.

Design Manual dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the 
State of Hawaii, Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and 
Research Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem 
Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of 
Transportation (Caltrans).

After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated,

recommend scarifying and re-preparing the soils as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor

3.3 
Fill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches 
may be re-used as a source of general fill material.  Imported fill materials, if required, 
should consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coralline or

less.  Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

3.5 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
As mentioned previously, an existing drainage system or a drainage layer is not 
present below portion of the existing pavements at the site. Additional infrastructure 
work (such as new drain lines and inlets) would be necessary in order to incorporate a 
drainage layer into the new pavements. We believe this would not be feasible nor 
practical and that it would result in a build-up of subsurface water within the drainage 
layer if proper drainage outlets are not provided. The subsurface water likely would 
cause saturated soil conditions, which would cause the pavement subgrade soils to 
soften and result in premature pavement problems.  
Therefore, it is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately and to 
maintain positive drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface water 
may be carried off the pavement quickly for proper discharge. Surface water ponding 
should not be allowed on-site during or after construction. 
3.6 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We understand that new slabs-on-grade walkways and median slabs will be 
constructed for the project.  Based on the results of our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, the on-site clayey soils have low to moderate expansion potential when 
subjected to moisture fluctuations. Therefore, we recommend placing a minimum 6-inch 
thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill material below slabs-on-grade (such as 
the sidewalk slab or concrete median) to reduce moisture changes in the slab subgrade 
soils. Placement of the non-expansive select granular fill layer would further reduce the 
potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking 
and swelling of the on-site soils due to changes in the moisture content.  
Prior to placing the non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend scarifying 
the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioning the soils to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacting to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The underlying subgrade soils and select granular fill

3.7.3 Backfilling of Drainage Structure Excavations 
The drainage structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled. The 
excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, 
deleterious materials, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension may be used as backfill above the cushion layer up to the 
finish grades.  
Backfills using on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where 
drainage structures are located below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the drain 
box backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Traffic Signal Foundation 
We understand that new traffic signal structures will be installed for this project.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our subsurface exploration, it is 
anticipated that the traffic signals will be underlain by stiff silty clay soils.  For this

understand that the traffic signals will be similar to the typical Type I and Type II traffic 
signals presented in the Hawaii Department of Transportation (HDOT) Standard Plans. 
Based on the soil conditions encountered and the recommended foundation 
sizing presented on HDOT Standard Plan TE-33, we recommend supporting the traffic 
signal structures on shallow foundations in accordance with the recommendations for 
stiff clays as presented on HDOT Standard Plan TE-33.  
3.9 
Utility Trenches 
Based on our understanding of the project, new utility lines will be installed. In 
general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling 
of the trenches for the proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the method 
and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. The excavation should comply with the applicable local, state, and 
federal safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring 
design and installation. Trench shoring and bracing also should conform to the 
appropriate health and safety requirements. 
Granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes. Free-draining granular materials, 
such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation), should also be used for the 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support 
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the 
potential for pipe damage. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the 
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill material and placement could 
result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
eight exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8, extending to depths of 
about 11.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid stem augers. The borings were 
drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-8. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
O.D. standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented on the logs of borings have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures. The tests were 
run on relatively undisturbed and remolded samples. The test results are presented on 
Plate B-1.  
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-3. 
Two Unconfined Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the underlying 
soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
One California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a bulk soil 
sample to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-6.  
One laboratory Resistance (R) Value test (ASTM D2844) was performed by on a 
selected bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-7.
