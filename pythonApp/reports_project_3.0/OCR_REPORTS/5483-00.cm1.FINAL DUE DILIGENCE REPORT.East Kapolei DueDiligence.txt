REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows
from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau

now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank
of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway
to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials Placement of man made fills and commercial development of the area
within the last 20 years have brought the project area to its present form
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Limited field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling three borings was
conducted to supplement research and review of available in house soil and boring data
in the vicinity of the project site in order to evaluate the subsurface conditions at the
proposed development sites Based on the information obtained and our experience in
the area the native soils across the project site generally consist primarily of alluvial soils
with some residual soils in the northern most limits of the site The general geologic
classifications are described as follows
Residual Soils
Residual soils are derived from the weathering of the basalt lava flows These
soils are generally competent and consist of silty clay to clayey silt and transition
to weathered basalt rock with depth The residual soils are limited to the
northernmost areas of the site mauka of Farrington Highway near Kunia Road

Older Alluvium
This soil unit generally consists of terrigenous sediments which have been
transported by stream action from the upper reaches of the streams The
materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard brown clayey silts and silty clays
Portions of the Older Alluvium unit contain some weathered basalt boulders and
cobbles This material generally exhibits high strength with good bearing
capacity However the material often have moderate to high shrink swell
potential This is the predominant material type within the project
Recent Alluvium
This type of soil generally consists of very soft to medium stiff clayey silt and
organic silt with varying amounts of sand and infrequent pockets of gravel and
cobbles These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low
energy environment such as an estuary or bay These soils are generally
considered to be poorly suited for use as foundation loads due to their low in situ
strength characteristics and the tendency for settlement to occur in structures
overlying soils of this unit
This soil type is limited within the project site and is probably confined to areas
adjacent to Honouliuli Stream
Groundwater is generally not anticipated at shallow depths below the existing
ground surface except in the vicinity of Honouliuli Stream and other smaller
streams drainageways Water level was encountered in Boring No 1 at a depth of
about 10 8 feet below the ground surface However groundwater levels can vary
significantly depending on seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection
and other factors

Undocumented Fills and Stockpiles
Based on pervious experience agricultural areas are often underlain by
undocumented fills and disturbance from crop planting harvesting may extend to
significant depths
In general we recommend that the existing embankment or undocumented fills
disclosed during additional exploration and or site grading be over excavated where
necessary to expose the underlying firm ground The resulting over excavation should be
backfilled with compacted fill The areal extent and depth of over excavation should be
determined in the field during construction by the project geotechnical engineer The
bottom of the over excavated level should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to the placement of fill materials
The site was under cultivation for many years therefore the upper soils are highly
disturbed and probably contain significant amounts of organic material During site
clearing and grubbing the upper 18 inches of soil may need to be removed and stockpiled
for future use as topsoil or in non structural area
Foundations
We understand that the planned developments generally will consist mostly of
residential structures Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site
we believe that the proposed structures may be supported on a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings provided that several provisions are
followed
Due to the variable nature of the fill materials and potentially expansive nature of
the alluvial soils over excavation of these materials may be required during construction
Additionally house foundations may require deeper perimeter footings and reinforcing to
help resist expansive forces from the soils
The potential need for over excavation under the structure foundations should be
further evaluated based on the actual subsurface conditions at the structure locations
In addition the limits and extent of the surface fill should be delineated as much as
possible Therefore we recommend that the subsurface conditions be evaluated by
conducting a field exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling borings during
the design phase of each area when the type and distribution of the proposed structures
are better defined

Deep Shrinkage Cracks
Our experience in the area indicates that shrinkage cracks are commonly present
at the existing ground surface underlain by the alluvial soils as described previously In
general shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of
the soil moisture content as the surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry
weather conditions The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground is apparently
intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Ewa area and
the elimination of farming activities resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture
content
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations The ground cracks
could serve as conduits for water to percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of
strength of the foundation soils resulting in potential settlement of the building structure
In addition migration of the foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks
could undermine the foundation system and result in potential settlement of the building
structure Therefore remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed
structures
After clearing and grubbing we believe that the proposed building and pavement
footprints should be over excavated by about 3 feet below the existing ground surface
to remove the zone with shrinkage cracks The over excavation also should extend
laterally about 5 feet beyond the building and driveway limits If walkways are planned
around the building structures the over excavation should extend laterally by about
5 feet beyond the outside edge of the walkways
The over excavated subgrades and other on grade areas outside building and
pavement footprints should then be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum
roller for about six to eight passes to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface
voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks The exposed subgrades
should be proof rolled without moisture conditioning of the subgrade soils at the
pre existing moisture contents Loose areas disclosed during the proof rolling
operations should be backfilled with general fill material compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The proof rolling operations should be conducted
under the near continuous observation of the project geotechnical engineer
Subsequent to the proof rolling operations the subgrades should then be
scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance

Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrades we
recommend that the subgrade soil be prepared again as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill The need for over excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions
should be evaluated in the field by the project geotechnical engineer
Because the development of shrinkage cracks is largely due to fluctuations in the
moisture content of the on site expansive soils we believe that the efforts should be
directed at maintaining the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level after
preparation and compaction These long term solutions may include landscaping
installation of concrete walkways around the building structures and or placement of
non expansive fills in the yard areas
Expansive Soil Conditions
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the on site alluvial soils
may exhibit moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations
The lateral extent and thickness of the alluvial soils should be delineated during the
design phase of each individual lot development by conducting a detailed field exploration
program Furthermore the expansive potential of the alluvial soils also should be
evaluated during the design phase Where expansive soils are encountered
over excavation of the alluvial soils may be necessary under foundations slabs on grade
and pavements We estimate that over excavation depth on the order of about 24 inches
may be required in areas underlain by the expansive soils The resulting over excavation
would need to be backfilled with non expansive select granular fill
Additional Exploration
A field exploration program should be conducted for the design of the mass
grading and infrastructure installation for the development In addition a detailed field
exploration and geotechnical engineering analysis should be conducted for the design
of the fine grading and structure foundations during the individual lot development
