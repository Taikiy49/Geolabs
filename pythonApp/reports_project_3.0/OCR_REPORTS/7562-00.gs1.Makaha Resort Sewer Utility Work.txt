SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a relatively thin surface fill layer of about 0.5 to 
1 foot thick underlain by alluvial deposits to the maximum depth explored of 41.1 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The alluvium consisted of medium dense to very dense 
sandy gravel, silty sand, cobbles, and boulders; and very stiff to hard silty clay/clayey silt.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our field exploration and proposed invert elevations, we anticipate that 
the new replacement sewer line and manhole structures will bear on the dense to very 
dense alluvial deposits. A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes and manhole structures. 
We anticipate that cobbles and boulders within the alluvial deposits may be 
encountered in the excavation for the replacement sewer line and manhole structures. 
Relatively hard excavation may be encountered in these deposits during the sewer line 
and manhole installation. Hoe ramming and/or chipping may be required. 
Because of the relatively deep excavation depth and limited construction space 
available, proper shoring of the sides of the trench will be required for the replacement 
sewer line installation. The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion 
and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Makaha Resort Sewer Utility Work project in Makaha on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses. These recommendations are 
intended for the design of the new underground sewer lines for the project only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The proposed project is in Makaha Valley in the Waianae District of the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on information provided, we understand that the existing 10-inch 
sewer line will be replaced with a new 12-inch PVC sewer line.  In addition, new sewer 
manhole structures will be installed. 
The sewer replacement project extends along Jade Street and crosses the golf 
course to the southeast corner of the Makaha Valley Resort.  The sewer system will be 
about 2,000 linear feet in length.  In general, the inverts of the new sewer line will be 
about 10 feet below the existing ground surface.  However, we understand that near 
Makaha Valley Resort the sewer line invert will be up to about 30 feet below the existing 
ground surface.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas 
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials, which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series. 
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower, middle, and upper volcanic 
members. The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks 
that built the main mass of the Waianae shield volcano. The middle member consists of 
rock that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera. The upper member 
is a relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in 
its history of evolution. 
Once the Waianae shield volcano formed, a long period of deep erosion, 
sedimentation, and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred, which produced the 
large valleys of the western side of the shield volcano. These erosional valleys were 
gradually filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a 
combined result of stream erosion, base level rise (sea level rise), and subsidence of 
the island mass. Over time, and as a result of sea level fluctuation, some of the older 
alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation, 
formed relatively dense deposits. However, the current surface soil deposits above 
these older deposits are typically found to be unconsolidated. 
Makaha Valley, like all the valleys of the Waianae Range, was formed by stream 
erosion and is choked with massive amounts of alluvium and colluvium. Alluvium is

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is in the Waianae District on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
project site is along Jade Street, extends through the golf course, and ends near the 
southeast corner of the Makaha Valley Resort.  Jade Street is an asphaltic concrete 
paved  roadway with one lane of traffic in each direction. 
The replacement sewer lines are within a residential area along Jade Street.  
Overhead lines were observed along the west side of the roadway.  
The project site generally slopes down toward the south with existing ground 
surface ranging from about Elevations +58 to +145 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 1A, 2, 2A, and 3 through 5. The borings 
extended to depths of about 16.5 to 41.1 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The borings drilled generally encountered a surface fill layer about 0.5 to 1 foot 
thick underlain by alluvial deposits extending to 41.1 feet, the maximum depth explored. 
The surface fill layer consisted of dense silty gravel and very stiff silty clay/clayey silt. 
The alluvium consisted of medium dense to very dense sandy gravel, silty sand, 
cobbles and boulders; and very stiff to hard silty clay/clayey silt.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to vary with 
seasonal rainfall, time of the year, and other factors.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a relatively thin surface fill layer underlain by 
medium dense to very dense and stiff to hard alluvial deposits to the maximum depth 
explored of about 41.1 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the field exploration, we anticipate the replacement sewer line and 
manhole structures will bear on the dense to very dense alluvial deposits. A granular 
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel is recommended under the pipes 
and manhole structures. 
From the results of our field exploration, we anticipate that hoeramming and/or 
chipping will be required for the removal of the cobbles and boulders anticipated within 
the alluvial deposits during the installation of the replacement sewer line and manhole 
structures. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the replacement sewer line 
and manhole structures are further discussed in the following sections. 
3.1 
Underground Sewer Lines 
We anticipate that the installation of the replacement sewer line will be performed 
using conventional open trench method. The invert depths of the new sewer line will 
range from about 7 to 28 feet below the existing ground surface. 
3.1.1 Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes 
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench, the size of the 
pipes, the unit weight of backfill material, and the frictional resistance between the 
backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried 
sewer pipes, we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic 
foot (pcf) for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.25. Earth forces 
upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the 
width of the trench should be kept to a minimum. In addition, traffic loads on the 
buried pipes should also be considered for the portions of the pipes within the 
roadway areas.

3.1.2 Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load 
supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration. In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine 
gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the sewer line pipes. 
The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width 
of 24 inches. A typical section of the trench detail is presented on Plate 3 for 
reference. 
3.1.3 Backfill 
In general, free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to 
about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and 
to reduce the need for compaction of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of 
pipe damage. Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not be allowed. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to

the project site for the intended use. 
3.1.4 Pipe Settlement 
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the difference 
in the unit weight of the excavated original earth and the compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipeline. The net increase in loading may cause settlement 
of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our exploration and 
analyses, small primary settlements on the order of about less than 1 inch may be 
anticipated for the new sewer line alignment.

3.2 
Excavation 
In order to install the sewer lines, we anticipate that excavations up to about 
30 feet below the existing ground surface will be required for the project construction. 
Based on the planned sewer line inverts, we envision that temporary shoring of the 
excavations will be required for the utility line installation. 
3.2.1 Excavation Method 
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. Based on our 
field exploration and the available information, it is anticipated that hoe ramming 
and/or chipping will be required for the removal of the cobbles and boulders 
anticipated within the alluvial deposits during the sewer line and manhole 
installation. The excavated soils should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal 
distance equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of 
the excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement. 
3.2.2 Excavation Support 
As mentioned above, the replacement sewer line inverts range from about 7 to 28 
feet below the existing ground surface. Because the replacement sewer line will be 
along the roadway, sloping of the sides of the trenches may not be possible due to 
the limited space available. Therefore, temporary shoring of the sides of the 
trenches may be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench excavations and to 
protect personnel working in the trench. 
Because the site is underlain by dense and hard deposits, installation of driven 
sheet piles may be difficult. The excavation support and shoring system used must 
comply with all applicable safety requirements. The contractor should ret    ain a 
qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the shoring system used. 
The contractor should be made solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of 
the shoring installation. His/her representative, who should be required to be 
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work, will have the 
best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such 
as unforeseen subsurface soil conditions, unexpectedly high groundwater table,

Even if good construction procedures are followed, some movement of the shoring

3.3 
Sewer Manhole Structures 
Thirteen new sewer manhole structures are planned along the replacement 
sewer line alignments. The bottoms of the new manhole structures will be about 8 to 
30 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate 
that the manhole structures will bear on the dense to very dense alluvium. 
3.3.1 Manhole Structure 
In general, we recommend that the bottom of the manhole structure bear on 
6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) over the dense to 
very dense alluvium encountered in our drilled borings. Based on our engineering 
analyses, we believe that the net increase in bearing pressure on the underlying 
soil deposits resulting from the manhole construction would be minimal. 
Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are anticipated for the manhole 
structures.

SECTION 4.   LIMITATIONS

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 1A, 2, 2A, and 3 through 5, extending to 
depths of about 16.5 to 41.1 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our field engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil sample 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74% and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot 
core run in rock, the RQD would be 50% and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are presented on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557B) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship.  The test results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results are summarized 
on Plate B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the engineering 
characteristics of the soils. The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture 
content of the soils. Compaction of the samples was performed by dropping a 10-pound 
hammer from a height of 18 inches. The samples were compacted in five equal layers 
with each layer receiving 56 blows of energy. The samples were soaked in water prior 
to the penetration test. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.
