This technical memorandum presents our geotechnical recommendations in 
support of the micropile foundation design for the Leeward Bikeway project in the 
Waipahu area of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to replace the 
current 16.5-inch octagonal driven pile design with a micropile foundation system 
consisting of vertical and/or battered piles to support the proposed Waikele and Kapakahi 
Stream Bridge structures. 
The micropile system generally consists of a small diameter (usually less than 
12 inches), drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically 
is constructed by drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the 
borehole. Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, the installation 
of the micropile generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, adjacent soils, 
and the environment. 
We envision that a micropile system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 
7 inches may be used at the Waikele and Kapakahi Stream Bridge structures. We 
recommend that each micropile be designed based on an ultimate compressive load 
capacity of 200 kips (100 tons).  
The compressive load capacities of the micropiles were computed generally based 
on the design guidance in accordance with the AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications 
(2014). An efficiency factor of 1.0 has been applied to the extreme event load resistance for 
the pile group presented in the following table. In order to arrive at the pile load resistance 
for the strength limit state, a resistance factor of 0.55 has been applied to the extreme event 
limit state load resistance for the design of the micropile foundation.

To achieve the ultimate compressive load resistance, we recommend that the 
7-inch diameter micropile should extend to depths of about 80 and 115 feet below the 
bottom of the pile cap for the Waikele and Kapakahi Stream Bridge structures,

To facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, we 
recommend advancing the steel casing to the bottom of the micropile during the drilling 
operation. The steel casing may be withdrawn during grouting operation while a minimum 
of 5 feet of grout is maintained above the bottom of the casing at all times. To reduce the 
risk of micropile “necking” within the soft marsh/lagoonal deposits, we recommend 35 and 
70 feet of permanent steel casing to be left in-place at the top of each micropile (unbounded 
zone) for the Waikele and Kapakahi Stream Bridge structures, respectively. The 
permanent casing should have an outside diameter (OD) of about 7 inches (the same as 
the grout bulb size).

Micropiles within 5 feet of a recently completed micropile should not be drilled or 
grouted until the grout in the recently completed micropile has been allowed to cure for at 
least 24 hours to avoid disturbing or compromising the recently completed micropile. 
Downdrag Loads 
Considering the highly compressible soft soils that may be under-consolidated at the 
project site, continuous ground settlement may be present inducing ground downward 
movement relative to the pile. The downward movement will result in negative skin friction, 
generally known as downdrag forces, acting on the micropile. The magnitude of the 
downdrag force depends on the thickness of the under-consolidated compressible soil layer, 
the degree of under-consolidation, and the pile perimeter. 
Based on our foundation analyses, we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on 
the micropile due to the under-consolidated lagoonal and/or marsh deposits may be on the 
order of about 15 kips per pile at the Waikele Stream and about 30 kips per pile at the 
Kapakahi Stream. It should be noted that the ultimate compressive load capacity of the piles 
(200 kips) is a net value; therefore, the ultimate compressive load resistance has been 
reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses. 
Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads imposed on the micropile may be resisted by shear along the grout bulb 
(bonded zone) and the adjacent soils.  In general, an uplift load resistance of up to 100 kips 
per pile may be achieved for the Waikele and Kapakahi Stream Bridge structures.  The 
uplift load resistance provided is intended for transient load applications only.  Therefore, 
the uplift load resistance should not be further increased for transient load application.  For 
sustained uplift load, we recommend applying the resistance factor of 0.70 for the strength 
limit state load resistance.  The load resistance of the micropile member in tension should 
be checked by the project structural engineer. 
Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the bridge structures may be resisted by the lateral load 
resistance of the vertical and/or battered micropile group. 
The lateral load resistance of the micropile group is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the micropile, the geometry of the pile group, allowable 
deflection at the top of the micropile, and the induced moment in the micropile.  The micropile 
group consists of vertical and/or batter (in an inclination of one horizontal to twelve vertical 
[1H:12V]) micropiles. We recommend using a combination of vertical and batter micropile 
group for the Waikele Stream Bridge structure and only battered micropile group for the 
Kapakahi Stream Bridge structure.

Micropile Foundation Settlement 
Settlements of the micropiles would generally be resulted primarily from elastic 
compression of the micropile member and subgrade response. We estimate the total 
settlement of the micropile-supported foundations to be 0.75 inches or less with 
differential settlements between micropiles not exceeding about one-half of the total 
settlement. We believe these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the 
loads are applied. 
Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the micropile load capacity is highly dependent on the drilling 
procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to install the micropile.

Therefore, the micropile load capacity may vary considerably between different contractors 
and micropile foundation systems. We recommend performing one compression load test 
per bridge structure on a sacrificial vertical micropile prior to the production micropile 
installation to confirm the load-carrying capacity of the specified micropile system. The load 
tests should be performed in general accordance with the Quick Load Test Method of 
ASTM D1143 as modified herein. The load test piles should be loaded in increments of 
about 10 kips and should be held for a minimum of 4 hours at the 200-kip load increment to 
evaluate the potential for creep effect.  The compressive load test on the sacrificial micropile 
at each bridge structure is an integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system.  
Therefore, we recommend that the micropile test program be conducted under the 
observation of a representative from Geolabs. 
Micropile Construction Considerations 
A specialty subcontractor experienced in the construction of a cased micropile 
foundation system (minimum five projects) should perform the micropile installation. It 
should be noted that hard drilling conditions in the dense layers, such as basaltic 
cobbles/boulders and dense basalt rock, should be expected within the specified micropile 
depths at the project site.  
The micropile contractor should anticipate significant grout overage, especially in the 
basaltic cobbles and boulders and/or basalt rock and have a sufficient contingency fund for 
grout overage in the bid. Typical grout overage in the cobbles and boulders may vary 
significantly from 50 to 100 percent of theoretical volume or higher. 
It should be noted that artesian groundwater conditions were encountered during our 
boring exploration at the Waikele and Kapakahi Stream Bridge sites. If artesian groundwater 
conditions are encountered, additional measures will be required to maintain the integrity of 
the micropile foundation, including but not limited to specific grout mix design and grout sock, 
to prevent grout washout in the potential artesian zone.  
Due to the specialized nature of the micropile foundation construction, observation 
of the micropile foundation system should be designated as a “Special Inspection” item. 
Therefore, we recommend that a representative from Geolabs (Special Inspector) be 
present to observe the geotechnical aspects of the micropile foundation construction.
