PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The golf course development includes an access road from the future Compact Road
to the new clubhouse clubhouse building maintenance building and water reservoirs The
access road alignment traverses steep heavily vegetated undulating terrain Therefore
significant cuts and fill may be required for the grading of the road We anticipate that the site
is underlain by stiff clayey silt and sandy gravel overlying weathered rock formation
Earthwork
Based on the preliminary plans provided and the topography at the project site
earthwork for the proposed access road construction may involve excavations on the order
of about 25 feet deep and embankments on the order of up to about 10 feet thick The
majority of the embankment fill materials will be generated from the excavations along the
proposed alignment Based on the geologic conditions along the proposed alignments we
anticipate that the excavated materials would range from medium stiff to very stiff saprolite
soils to very dense volcanic breccia rock In addition the earthwork construction may also
encounter some very soft and or loose recent alluvial and lagoonal deposits
Items of earthwork that are addressed in the following subsections include the
following 1 Cut Slope Design 2 Fill Slope Design Site

1 Cut Slope Design
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated along the proposed alignment we
believe that the planned cut slopes may expose materials ranging from medium stiff to very
stiff saprolite hard volcanic breccia and tuff rock formation In general we believe that a
cut slope inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter may be used for the
design of the planned cut slopes along the proposed alignment In addition we believe that
a cut slope inclination as steep as may be used in localized areas where very
dense volcanic breccia and hard tuff rock formation are encountered
Excessive surface run off over the slope face may cause erosion of the exposed soils
thus potentially jeopardizing the long term stability and performance of the cut slopes
Therefore cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope planting or other means such
as placement of geotextile fabrics on the slope face immediately after the slope is excavated
to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed soils Detailed discussions pertaining to the
use of geotextile fabrics on the slope face are presented in the Erosion Control section of this
report
For cut slopes with vertical heights greater than 25 feet we recommend that a
minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at the mid point of the slopes to reduce the potential
for erosion due to surface run off water The slope benches may be omitted for cut slopes in
the very dense volcanic breccia rock formation encountered in localized areas Where slope
benches are provided drainage ditches should be provided on the benches to collect and
divert surface water on the slopes to appropriate discharge outlets Interceptor drainage
ditches should be considered at the tops of the cut slopes to provide proper drainage of
surface run off water
It should be noted that the planned cut slopes of 1H 1V slope ratios are steep
Therefore in the event that slight over cutting of a slope occurs or the cut slope is slightly
damaged by erosion consideration should be given to leaving the slope as is rather than
attempting to backfill the slope to the design grade with sliver fills If backfilling of an over cut
slope is necessary over steepened or unstable the cut slope should be completely over cut
and buttressed with compacted fill 9 feet wide constructed of cement treated subbase by
keying and benching the fill materials into the over cut slope
2 Fill Slope Design
The on site saprolite soils have high in situ moisture contents Therefore the
compaction criteria may be reduced from the normal 90 percent to a minimum of 85 percent
relative compaction in order to aid the earthwork construction in this high rainfall environment
using high moisture tropical soils In general permanent embankment fills constructed with
the lower compaction criteria over firm to stiff ground should be designed with a slope
inclination no steeper than 2H 1V to account for the reduced fill strength

Fills to be placed on existing slopes with an inclination steeper than 5H 1V should be

For fill slopes with a vertical height greater than 20 feet we recommend that a key be

adjacent grade In addition the base width of the key should be at least 9 feet A typical

Excessive water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting
should be provided as soon as the fill slopes are completed to reduce potential for erosion of
the finished slopes In general slope benches are not required for the planned fill slopes
except at site specific locations Where slope benches are necessary we recommend that
a minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at the mid point of the slope to reduce the potential
for erosion due to surface run off water Construction of earth berms interceptor drainage
ditches and the use of geotextile fabrics over the fill slope face should be considered to
reduce the potential for erosion thus enhancing the long term stability of the planned fill
slopes
3 Subdrainage
Seepage conditions may be present at localized areas along the proposed roadway
alignment as evident in some areas noted during our site reconnaissance Therefore we
recommend that provisions be incorporated into the construction documents to provide for
subdrains in the following areas
at all springs and seepage areas
at the bottom of all canyon fills
on the uphill side of keyways and
in other areas of the site where seepage was observed during and after
grading or as recommended by the Engineer during construction
Where needed subdrains should consist of perforated pipes with perforations placed facing
down and should be at least 4 inches in diameter All subdrains should be surrounded and
underlain by at least 4 inches of drain rock A non woven geotextile such as Mirafi 180N or
equivalent should wrap around the drain rock In general subdrain trenches should be at
least 12 inches wide and at least 24 inches deep A sketch of the Typical Subdrain Details is
presented on Plate 2 Subdrains should be daylighted into drainage ditches culverts or other
drainage structures for proper discharge

4 Site Preparation
It should be noted that the existing terrain along the majority of the alignment is heavily
vegetated Therefore substantial site preparation work involving clearing and grubbing would
be required for the subject project At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly beyond the limits of
grading to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material The
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted general fill The excavated soft
soils should be properly disposed of beyond the limits of grading
In general the over excavated subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should
be proof rolled to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
AASHTO T 180 using the moist preparation method wet to dry Optimum moisture is the
water content corresponding to the maximum dry density Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with compacted general fill
If the subgrade soils are pumping or yielding during subgrade preparation the
subgrade should be stabilized prior to fill placement and or pavement construction Subgrade
stabilization measures may include the use of additives such as cement treatment and or the
incorporation of ground stabilization fabrics
5 Fills and Backfills
In general we believe that the excavated materials may be used as a source of
general fill for embankment construction provided that the materials are properly processed
and placed in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Fill Placement and
Compaction Requirements subsection of this report Soft and or loose materials generated
from excavations into the recent alluvial mangrove swamp and lagoonal deposits should not
be used in fills and should be disposed of properly beyond the limits of grading
Fill and backfill materials within the upper 9 feet from the finished subgrades should consist
of materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension Materials generated from excavations
into the dense volcanic breccia rock formations may be used as a source of fill and backfill
materials within the upper 3 feet from the finished subgrades provided that they are screened
of over sized materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension and processed to provide
a relatively well graded material to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass

6 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area and the on site materials
generally have high in situ moisture contents ranging from about 50 to over 80 percent which
is approximately 20 to 50 percent above the optimum moisture content significant drying of
the excavated materials will be required prior to their use as fill and or backfill material in order
to meet the normal 90 percent minimum compaction requirement
As mentioned earlier substantial aeration of the in situ soils may not be feasible due
to the high rainfall environment at the project site Based on past project experience in Palau
and our experience with similar high moisture tropical soils we believe that the compaction
requirement for general fill placement for this project may be reduced from the standard of
90 percent to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D T 180 using
the moist preparation method wet to dry Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density The reduction in compaction criteria will result in
a reduction in the shear strength of the fill materials however we believe that sufficient
strength can be obtained with the lower compaction criteria for the proposed embankments
Fills and backfills up to the finished subgrade of the pavement should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts on the order
of about 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least 85 percent relative
compaction Even with the reduced 85 percent minimum compaction criteria we believe that
significant moisture reduction would still be necessary especially for materials generated from
the deep excavations where the in situ moisture contents may be in excess of 65 to
70 percent
The contractor working on this project should be made aware that aeration to lower
the soil moisture and additional compaction effort of thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified
compaction will generally reduce the rate of fill placement for this project Compaction should
be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact

7 Excavation
Our site reconnaissance disclosed that the majority of the proposed alignment is
generally underlain by saprolite soils ranging from medium stiff to very stiff It is our opinion
that excavations into these materials may be achieved by conventional heavy excavation
equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy construction equipment
Dense volcanic breccia rock formation may be encountered in the deeper cuts It is
anticipated that the dense volcanic breccia rock formation may be excavated with normal
heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers where the dense volcanic
breccia and hard tuff rock formation are near the existing ground surface However
excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoearms chipping or blasting
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with
applicable local safety requirements
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on field
data obtained from our field reconnaissance at the subject site Contractors proposing to work
on this project should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and soil rock data to make
their own interpretation
8 Boulder Disposal
Over sized materials such as cobbles greater than 6 inches in dimension and
boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than 5 feet in
largest dimension are anticipated from cuts into the dense volcanic breccia rock formation and
may be used in general fills provided that the following recommendations are followed
Boulders less than 5 feet in largest dimension may be utilized as general fills
provided that the top of the boulder is placed at least 3 feet below the finished
subgrades Boulders or portions of boulders should not encroach within 3 feet
of the finished subgrade elevation Boulders larger than 5 feet in maximum
dimension should not be used as general fill unless reduced in size
Boulders should be spread out at the bottom of deeper fills and must not be
nested together They should be placed such that compaction equipment will
be able to compact between and around them

Erosion Control
Implementation of an adequate erosion control program would be crucial to the
satisfactory performance of the planned cut slopes and fill embankments against potential
failures In general erosion of the existing slopes planned cut slopes or new fill
embankments are caused by surface water run off General guidelines such as
establishment of vegetation and provision of interceptor drainage ditches and earth berms
have been provided and discussed under the Cut Slope Design and Fill Slope Design
subsections of this report However we understand that due to the inherent properties of the
on site soils establishment of vegetation at the slope face may be difficult and may require a
long period of time Therefore other erosion control methods or applications such as the use
of flexible geosynthetic matting may be considered for this project
Flexible geosynthetic matting provides a flexible ground armor that resists erosion while
allowing natural vegetation to establish Its primary and secondary functions include the

following 1 temporary erosion control 2 mulching 3 permanent erosion control and 4

1 Temporary Erosion Control
The flexibility weight web structure and porosity features of the flexible geosynthetic
matting make it particularly effective in temporary erosion control It conforms easily to the
ground surface and remains firmly in place as a stable ground armor The mat shields soil
surface from the erosive forces of wind and rain and prevents soil seed and fertilizer from
washing away Finally it provides a rough surface to retain sediment deposits during run off
2 Mulching
The high porosity web structure of the flexible geosynthetic matting acts as a
non deteriorating mulch It holds soil seed and fertilizer in place and retains sediment run off
as a medium for root growth It also retains moisture and heat necessary for germination and
allows uninhibited growth of grass and other vegetation
3 Permanent Erosion Control
Once vegetation growth is established the flexible geosynthetic matting continues to
provide permanent erosion control as it becomes embedded in the new stem and root system
It protects new vegetation from washout and reinforces and anchors the vegetation in place
as a cohesive unit
4 Run Off Velocities Reduction
Revegetated slopes and ditches reinforced with the matting reduce run off flow
velocities which is an important advantage that cannot be achieved with concrete lining It
reduces run off flow volume and concentrations and reduces the need for energy dissipation
at the ditch outlet or slope base It also promotes water percolation and ground water
recharge

Pavement Drainage System
One of the primary distress mechanisms observed in pavement structures is pumping
due to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Based on our experience with
projects having similar soil conditions there is a strong tendency for water to accumulate at
the interface between the granular subbase course and the underlying clayey pavement
subgrade soils In addition free water voids in the pavement section repeated heavy wheel
loads and an erodible base can also cause pumping conditions Unless the pavement
subgrade soils are properly prepared and adequate drainage is provided the accumulated
water will likely soften the silty and clayey subgrade soils and cause premature failure of the
pavement Therefore paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to
carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction
In general the primary source of free water in the pavement structure is surface water
infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of infiltrated moisture
is the longitudinal pavement shoulder interface Water also enters the pavement structure
from shallow ditches To reduce moisture infiltration into the pavement structure and
underlying subgrade soils all pavement cracks should be sealed Considering the high rainfall
in this area longitudinal edge drains should be considered along alignments with relatively flat
gradients and transverse drains should be provided at low spots along the roadway alignment
and ford crossings
As mentioned earlier pavement damage is generally caused by poor surface and
subsurface drainage conditions along the roadway alignment Therefore we recommend that
longitudinal edge drains along both sides of the roadway alignment be considered where the
