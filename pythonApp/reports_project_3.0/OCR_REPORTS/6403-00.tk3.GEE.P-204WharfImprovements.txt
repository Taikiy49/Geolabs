Our field exploration at the new bulkhead alignment along Tango Wharf generally
encountered surface fills consisting of medium dense silty sands and sandy gravel on the
order of about 2 meters thick The surface fill was underlain by alluvial deposits consisting
of loose silty sands and gravel grading to loose to very loose clayey sand and clayey
gravel overlying coral and limestone formations The coral and limestone formations were
encountered between about Elevation 9 to 15 meters MLLW datum
Based on the information provided we understand that steel sheet pile bulkhead
augmented with king piles and restrained by tie rods and deadman anchors will be used
for the wharf improvement design The sheet pile bulkhead should be designed for lateral
earth pressures due to the on site soils which would include the existing fills and the
underlying loose alluvial deposits and dense coral and limestone formation
Based on our experience in the area the subsoils at the site may be considered to
be corrosive Because of the high corrosion potential of the subsoils special attention
should be given to corrosion protection of the steel sheet piles tie rods and other metallic
substructures such as ductile iron water lines in contact with the soils
The loose alluvial deposits encountered at the project site are potentially liquefiable
during the design level earthquake and will consolidate during seismic events Therefore
densification of the potentially liquefiable soils such as vibro replacement stone columns
should be implemented at the project site to mitigate the potential for soil liquefaction
during seismic events In addition a monitoring program should be implemented for the
existing sheet pile bulkhead and building structure for potential settlement and lateral
movement during the ground modification program
For the pavement design at the new wharf operation area and along Building 3169
we recommend using a CBR value of 30 and or a modulus of subgrade reaction k value
of m3 for the pavement subgrade soils to design the flexible and rigid
pavements planned for the project

Regional Geology
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island
archipelago which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming
the Mariana Trench The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches which extend from
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench Mariana Trench Yap Trench
and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces
the southern mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The island
formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic
rocks and conglomerates tuffs and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene Epochs
Lava flows and limestone formations of Miocene Epoch make up the majority of
the southern one third of the island The plateaus of the northern portion of the island
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level During the
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits Tectonic activity has lifted these
coral algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau
The central portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic
materials of the southern mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau
The project site at Apra Harbor is located in the south central portion of the island The
local geology is dominated by the volcanic materials and associated materials of the
southern mountains Due to surface drainage courses of the southern mountain terrain
the near surface soils in the area consist of Holocene age alluvium and associated
deposits such as coralline detritus alluvium and lagoonal deposits These younger
unconsolidated soil deposits generally are more susceptible to liquefaction and slope
stability issues especially around the shoreline areas A Geologic Map depicting the
local geology around the project site is provided as Plate 4

Earthquakes and Seismicity
Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms the Mariana Trench
The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately 20 earthquakes
exceeding a magnitude of 7 0 since 1902 Of these events fifteen occurred prior to
1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date Several of the larger faults and
fault zones that traverse the Island of Guam include the following Pago Bay Adelup
Point Fault River Valley Fault Zone and Tamuning Barrigada
Fault In addition to the larger faults several faults and fault zones within proximity to
the project site include the Cabras Fault southeast of site and Cocos Fault south of
site
The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration
when evaluating seismic hazards Of the substantial faults and fault zones mentioned
above the River Valley Fault Zone and the Cabras Fault lie
closest to the project site to the southwest and southeast respectively

Magnitude 6 0 and Magnitude 6 5 seismic events Based on the design peak ground
accelerations and the design magnitude earthquakes we conducted a liquefaction
potential assessment of the saturated granular soils at the Tango Wharf site
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes
In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an
increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of
the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could
equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires sufficient
mobility to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result
in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
We evaluated the liquefaction potential of the saturated granular soils at the
Tango Wharf site based on the procedures outlined by Seed and Idriss 1982 and
Seed Idriss and Arango 1983 and updated by the National Center for Earthquake
Engineering Research NCEER in 1996 and 1998 In general the field SPT N values
obtained from the ten new borings Boring Nos 301 through 310 were normalized for

Vibro Replacement Stone Columns
As mentioned previously some of the alluvial deposits consist of loose silty
sands with some cohesion which may be considered potentially liquefiable during the
design level earthquakes Seismically induced ground settlements on the order of up to
about 230 to 450 millimeters may be anticipated during seismic events resulting from
liquefaction of the alluvial deposits In order to reduce the adverse effects on the future
improvements at the site resulting from the anticipated ground settlements special
ground modification mitigation measures requiring in situ densification of the potential
liquefiable soils such as vibro replacement stone columns should be implemented at
the project site
Ground improvement by vibro replacement stone columns could be used to
densify and improve the stability characteristics of the potentially liquefiable soils
underlying the site Vibro replacement consists of densifying the soils by means of a
vibrating probe inserted into the soils filling the void created by the probe with crushed
rock and compacting the crushed rock to form a stone column The stone columns act
to reinforce the sands and can themselves carry higher loads than the surrounding
sandy soils In addition the stone columns provide a means of quickly dissipating the
increased pore water pressures that may cause liquefaction during an earthquake
Stone columns are typically 1 meter in diameter and are placed at spacing of
approximately 2 to 3 meters apart with closer spacing under more heavily loaded areas
and depending on the fines content of the in situ soils
For this project we understand the goal of soil improvement is to densify the
existing soils to achieve a corrected N value of at least 30 The bottom elevation of the
stone columns is set at about 15 meters MLLW Predrilling down to an elevation of
about 12 meters MLLW may be required at those column locations where the existing
soils are too dense for the vibro probe to advance by a combination of weight vibration

Pavement Joints
Due to the rigidity of the Portland Cement Concrete PCC pavements significant
stresses may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture
content In order to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for
significant cracking adequate joints should be provided in the PCC pavement In
addition to the jointing requirements the perimeter joint around the edge of the rigid
pavements should be doweled to reduce the potential for slab separation along the joint
due to stop and go traffic The following describes the various types of joints and
general guidelines that should be implemented in the design of concrete pavements
Crack Control Joints Contraction Joints
In general decreases in temperature or moisture content such as that during
concrete curing will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack Crack
control joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and
relieve the stresses caused by curling or warping Crack control joints such as saw
cut joints or formed grooves are provided to a depth of generally one fourth ¼ the
slab thickness These saw cut joints or formed grooves crack control joints
Final Design Submittal
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
W O 6403 00
GEOLABS INC
Page 20
Hawaii California
provide a weakened plane which likely will crack through the full depth as the
concrete shrinks during the curing process
In general the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of
6 meters Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and or life span
of the pavement The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal
and transverse directions The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent
Expansion Joints Isolation Joints
Expansion joints typically are used to relieve compressive stresses developed at
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature Expansion joints are cut to the
full depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in the
cut These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the
potential for damage to the adjoining concrete spalling of the edges of the concrete
slabs
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of
pavements with fixed structures such as the light poles mooring bollards etc
Because expansion joints generally do not provide for load transfer across the joint
placement of dowels with lubrication on one end of the dowel should be considered
for load transfer across the expansion joint where required
Construction Joints
Construction joints generally are required when two abutting slabs are placed at
different times Therefore construction joints usually form the edges of each day s
work For the relatively thick concrete slab planned for this project the construction
joints should consist of a butted joint In general construction joints should be
aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to reduce the number of
pavement joints required

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Eleven Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and
graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B 1 and B 2
Sixteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification The fines content of the sample tested percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve is indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B 3 through B 6
Fifty six fines content tests were conducted on selected soil samples to evaluate
the percent passing the No 200 sieve The tests were performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1140 The test results along with those obtained from the sieve analysis tests
are summarized on Plates B 7 through B 9
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test ASTM D 2850 was
performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the
soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied
confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil sample The test results and the
stress strain curve are presented on Plate B 10
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 11 and B 12
Corrosion tests including nine pH ASTM G 51 five Minimum Resistivity
ASTM G 57 two Chloride Content and two Sulfate Content
tests were performed by our office and TestAmerica Analytical Testing Corporation on
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test results are
summarized on Plate B 13

1 SOIL PARAMETERS AND LOADS USED FOR BULKHEAD ANALYSIS
The RFP documents indicate that the bulkhead wall of Tango Wharf Options 3
and 4 should be designed for two levels of earthquake
Level 1 An earthquake with a 50 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years
This event has a return period of 72 years The design peak ground acceleration PGA
for Level 1 is 0 21 g associated with an earthquake magnitude of 6 0
Level 2 An earthquake with a 10 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years
This event has a return period of 475 years The design peak ground acceleration
PGA for Level 2 is 0 38 g associated with an earthquake magnitude of 6 5
The proposed design would meet the intended seismic performance of the
structure under the Level 1 earthquake minor or no structural damage and temporary
or no interruptions in operations and Level 2 earthquake controlled inelastic structural
behaviour with repairable damage temporary loss of operations repairable within
months and prevention of structural collapse

2 METHODOLOGY OF BULKHEAD WALL ANALYSIS
The Prosheet software by Skyline Arcelor Mittal is utilized to aid in the design of
the anchored king sheet pile combination wall bulkhead based on the free earth support
method Both the static and seismic conditions are considered in the Prosheet analyses
To consider the variation of the soil properties in both horizontal and vertical
directions the active soil pressure diagram for both the static and seismic conditions will
be determined using the Trial Wedge Analysis Method presented in Appendix A of
Seismic Design of Waterfront Retaining Structures Ebeling and Morrison 1992 and
Final Design Submittal
12
then manually entered into Prosheet The static component of the soil pressure will
follow a triangular distribution from the top of bulkhead to the calculated bulkhead tip
elevation while the seismic component of the soil pressure will follow a trapezoidal
distribution such that the resultant force of the seismic soil pressure is located at a
distance of 0 6 H from the calculated bulkhead tip elevation where H distance from the
top of bulkhead to the calculated bulkhead tip
Reduction factors are applied to the passive soil pressure coefficients in order to
consider the possibility that the full passive soil resistance may not be mobilized due to
the displacement limits that are allowed for the soils in front of the bulkhead passive
With the consideration of the reduction factors the passive soil pressure coefficients are
then manually inputted into Prosheet
The main procedure of the bulkhead wall analysis is as follows
1 Assume a bulkhead wall tip elevation and conduct the wedge analysis to
determine the active soil pressure distribution along the height of the wall Manually
input into the Prosheet program the active soil pressure including the effect of uniform
live load surcharge as well as the hydrodynamic water pressure in front of the wall if
the analysis is for a seismic condition
2 Manually input the reduced passive soil pressure coefficients into the
Prosheet program
3 Run Prosheet
4 If the required bulkhead wall tip elevation from the Prosheet analysis is equal
to or very close to the assumed tip elevation used for the wedge analysis then the tip
elevation from the Prosheet analysis is considered to be the required wall tip elevation
Otherwise assume another wall tip elevation for the wedge analysis and repeat Steps
1 to 3 Through this trial and error process the wedge analysis and Prosheet analysis
are conducted until the calculated tip elevation from the Prosheet is equal to or very
close to the assumed tip elevation
Final Design Submittal
13
5 Determine the maximum bending moment of the bulkhead wall and the
maximum anchor tie rod force based on the Prosheet analysis

In the Trial Wedge Analysis for the seismic condition the lateral earth force due
to the seismic uniform surcharge was not separated from the force components due to
the soil self weight It was assumed the lateral force due to the seismic uniform
surcharge follows the same pressure distributions of the force components due to the
soil self weight This approach was taken for the following reasons
1 The uniform live surcharge for the seismic conditions is relatively
small There would be a minimal impact on the bulkhead wall analysis without the
separation In addition the wedge analysis can be greatly simplified
2 In the wedge analysis for the seismic condition refer to more details in
Appendix A of Ebeling and Morrison 1992 the surcharge is treated as an
equivalent soil weight in determining the seismic lateral force Hence it is not a
must to separate the force component due to the surcharge

It should be noted that in the RFP documents the allowable wall displacement

specific wall displacement criteria have been provided in the RFP documents for the
other locations of the wall Based on RFP design guideline Seismic Criteria for
California Marine Oil Terminals Page Dickenson and etc 1999 in
cases where the anchor does not experience catastrophic failure the maximum
displacements during the strong earthquake motions do not occur at the top of the
bulkhead wall but instead occur between the elevation of the anchor and the dredge
line It is our engineering judgment that the wall displacements at the top of the dense
layer on the passive side that is top of limestone gravel could reach the magnitudes of
the displacements at the top of the wall
One reason why a displacement criteria is normally only provided for the top of
the wall is because in case no catastrophic failure occurs in terms of the seismic
performance of a retaining wall supported wharf the displacement at the top of the wall
is of great concern due to the existence of the apron and other important structures for
example utility trenches crane rail and etc if there are any adjacent to the wall
