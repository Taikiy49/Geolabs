This letter report summarizes our geotechnical observation services performed 
during the installation of rock nail anchors, shotcrete slope protection, shotcrete 
pedestals, and other related items for the Atherton House & Parker Place Rockfall

In general, this letter report presents our observations of the construction 
activities performed at the Atherton House and Parker Place sites from March 16 
through March 30, 2020. Prometheus Construction was the specialty contractor 
responsible for slope scaling and rock excavation, and also the installation of rock bolt 
anchors, shotcrete slope protection, and shotcrete pedestals. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Atherton House and Parker Place Rockfall Hazard Mitigation project is 
located within the Mid-Pacific Institute (MPI) complex in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. Based on our preliminary Phase I rockfall evaluation study, potential rockfall 
hazards were identified at the Atherton House and Parker Place rock slope sites. The 
results of our Phase I study are presented in our letter report titled “Geotechnical 
Engineering Consultation Services, Atherton House & Parker Place Rockfall Evaluation, 
Mid-Pacific Institute, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii,” dated November 28, 2018. 
Our Phase II design-level study included additional field observations and 
assessment of the rock outcroppings to support the final design of the rockfall hazard

ATHERTON HOUSE – OBSERVATORY AND GARDEN OUTCROPS 
In our Phase I study, a single loose cobble-sized rock was observed on the 
Observatory Outcrop adjacent to the existing outcrop access stairway. Because the 
cobble had the potential to cause physical harm, we recommended securing the cobble 
in-place using lean cement mortar. Prometheus Construction secured the cobble-sized 
rock with lean cement mortar on March 26, 2020. 
At the Garden Outcrop, two rock outcrop undercut voids were observed during 
our Phase I study. We recommended installing shotcrete support pedestals within the 
existing undercut void areas to provide basal support for the overhanging material. The 
locations of the undercut voids are shown on Site Plan 1, Plate 3.1. 
At the onset of construction, we observed the contractor cutting and removing the 
existing vegetation within the identified undercut voids and surrounding the rock 
outcropping. In addition, loose debris, deleterious and other unsuitable or yielding 
materials were removed from the undercut voids and properly disposed of before 
commencing with the shotcrete pedestal installation work. 
Each shotcrete support pedestal generally consisted of a reinforced shotcrete 
mass that completely infilled the undercut void area. Prometheus Construction used 
6x6 – W2.9xW2.9 Welded Wire Fabric (WWF) reinforcing with a minimum shotcrete 
cover of about 8 inches. To secure the pedestal in-place within the void, 0.5-inch 
diameter stainless steel grouted anchors with centralizers were provided. The anchors 
were spaced at 24-inches on-center, each way along the top and bottom of the void 
(where practicable). Field adjustments to the anchor locations were made in the field 
depending on the geometry of the undercut void. The grouted anchors were embedded 
a minimum of 6 inches in competent basalt material and protruded a minimum of 
6 inches into the shotcrete. A drainage system consisting of permeable backfill material 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) wrapped with non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N) was

PARKER PLACE - SLOPE SITE 
At the time the Phase II report was prepared, a detailed topographic survey 
showing the elevation contours and the top-of-slope boundary for the Parker Place slope 
site was not available. Therefore, an approximate baseline was established in the field as 
a reference to the location of the recommended improvement features. Station 0+00 was 
established at the southwest corner of the grass area surrounding an existing electric utility 
pad, as shown on Site Plan 2, Plate 3.2. The baseline stationing then extends across the 
front of the four existing parking stalls and meets with the existing edge of pavement along 
the driveway. The baseline then follows the existing edge of the pavement and terminates 
at Station 1+00. 
In general, potential rockfall hazards were identified and their location mapped 
during our Phase II field reconnaissance. The potential rockfall hazard mitigation 
measures consisted of rock scaling, shotcrete slope protection, rock bolt anchors, and 
shotcrete support pedestals. Details of the rockfall hazard mitigation installation work are 
discussed in the following sections. 
Rock Scaling 
In general, rock slope scaling involved the careful removal of existing loose rocks 
and potential hazardous debris from an exposed and vegetation-free slope face. 
Prometheus Construction performed rock scaling at the areas specified in the 
project documents: between Station Nos. 0+00 and 0+27, Station No. 0+17, and 
Station No. 0+89.  
On March 16, 2020, Prometheus Construction proposed removing four additional 
boulders at approximately Station No. 0+79. The removal of these boulders would 
also eliminate the need for one rock nail anchor. Geolabs accepted the proposal. It 
should be noted that a total of five boulders were removed during the actual scaling 
work.  
The contractor conducted the rock and slope scaling work on March 16, 19, 20 
and 26, 2020.

Based on our Phase II field evaluation, we observed two soil surface slopes at the 
Parker Place slope site that were possibly susceptible to future erosion and ground 
loss. The two soil slope areas are located between approximately Sta. No. 0+00

Shotcrete Pedestals 
Eight shotcrete pedestals were recommended where rock bolts were not practical 
and/or where undercut voids posed a potential rockfall hazard. The shotcrete 
pedestals were installed in a similar manner to those constructed at the Atherton 
House site.  
Prometheus Construction worked on installing the shotcrete pedestals from 
March 18 through 20, 27 and 30, 2020.  
Rock Bolt Anchors 
Thirteen targeted rock bolt anchors were originally recommended in our Phase II 
report to provide support for boulders and/or rock blocks that presented possible 
rockfall hazards. However, as mentioned previously, one rock bolt anchor was 
eliminated due to the removal of the potential rockfall hazard (boulder) during the 
rock scaling work. The as-built locations of the 12 rock bolt anchors are 
presented on Plates 4.1 and 4.2. 
A Geolabs representative observed the drilling and grouting operations during 
construction of the rock bolt anchors. The rock bolt anchors were drilled using a 
drill head mounted on a Cat 315C excavator. The drill head was equipped with a 
down-the-hole hammer and a 1.25-inch diameter hollow threaded bar with a 
4-inch diameter B7XB HC Hardened Bit. 
The rock bolt anchor holes were drilled to depths of about 12.5 to 15.5 feet from 
the front face of the boulder or rock block at an inclination angle approximately 
perpendicular to the face of the rock. The depth of the rock bolt anchor holes 
Mid-Pacific Institute

varied to maintain the 8-foot minimum bond zone of the rock bolt anchor into the 
underlying competent rock formation.  
After each hole was drilled to the specified depth, the hollow threaded bar was 
detached from the drill head and left in the hole. To create an unbonded zone within 
the boulder or rock block, PVC pipe sleeves were cut to the approximate depth of 
the rock mass and taped to the top of the hollow threaded bar. The approximate 
size of the boulder or rock block was determined based on the drilling resistance, 
and observable decrease in drilling resistance was observed when the drill bit broke 
through the boulder. The drilling resistance then increased when the drill bit 
contacted the underlying rock formation.  
Before grouting the drilled holes, cutouts were created around the hole so that the 
anchor head assembly could be set into the face of the rock. The cutouts were 
approximately 14-inch square by 8-inch deep. However, some of the cutout 
dimensions needed to be modified based on the condition of the exposed rock 
face.  
A 1½-inch diameter high density plastic grouting tube was inserted into the drilled 
hole along the hollow threaded bar. Grouting began progressively from the 
bottom to top through the grouting tube until fresh grout appeared at the top of 
the hole. The grouting tube was removed after completion of the grouting 
operation. The grout was designed with a minimum 28-day compressive strength 
of 4,000 psi. 
After the grout cured for four days, the rock bolt anchors were tensioned and 
locked-off at a load of about 6 kips. The rock cutouts were then filled with 
shotcrete to cover the anchor head assembly. The faces of the cutouts were also 
sculpted and stained to match the color and appearance of the surrounding rock.  
The rock bolt anchors installed for the project are summarized in the Summary of 
Rock Bolt Anchor Installation, Plate 5. The individual rock bolt anchor logs, which 
include our field observations and other pertinent information recorded by our 
field representative, are provided in the Rock Anchor Installation Records, 
Plates 6.1.1 through 6.12.2. 
LIMITATIONS 
It should be noted that it is not possible to continuously observe all activities 
occurring simultaneously on a construction site; therefore, some of the work for the soil 
nail installation and testing may have occurred without our direct observation. However, 
we believe that the level of geotechnical observation provided is reasonable in reducing 
the geotechnical risks to the project.
