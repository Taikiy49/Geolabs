performed for the proposed Kauai Intermediate School site located to the southwest of
Lihue town on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site
grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed voicanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern
half of the island of Kauai
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3907 00 Page 6
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs Most of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolite
soils derived from the in situ weathering of igneous rocks
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located approximately 1 mile to the southwest of Lihue town in
the District of Lihue on the island of Kauai Hawaii The site is bounded approximately by
Kaumualii Highway to the north and Halehaka Stream to the west as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 To the south of the site exists a trash handling company that utilizes
the site as an equipment maintenance and storage yard The future school site presently
exists as undeveloped abandoned sugar cane cultivation land vegetated with tall sugar
cane
Access to the site is provided by a well established dirt road old cane haul road
which surrounds the western through southeastern portions of the site Access trails
within the project site were overgrown with sugar cane and tall grass During trail clearing
for the drilling exploration many of the pre existing trails were cleared to provide access
for surveys and our drilling equipment

The site contains a series of open ditches that traverse the site generally in a
north south direction The ditches appear to be abandoned irrigation trenches of
approximately 1 to 4 feet deep below the adjacent ground level An abandoned
underground irrigation tunnel was reported to exist along the western boundary of the
project site Evidence of the tunnel was not encountered during our field reconnaissance
and subsurface exploration In addition it was reported that concrete poles located

To the west of the project site and bordering the dirt road exists a steep slope

Based on our field observations and the site plan provided the project site is generally
level with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about 290 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL to 310 feet MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling 30 borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 30 at the proposed school site ranging in depths from about
5 feet below the existing ground surface Four bulk samples of the
near surface silty soils were collected at selected locations across the project site to
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils The approximate
locations of the borings and bulk samples are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered residual soils and
saprolite materials consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey silts and sandy silts derived from
the weathering of volcanic rocks The upper 2 feet of our test borings generally
encountered residual soils consisting of brown clayey silts and sandy silts with rootlets in
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3907 00 Page 8
a stiff to very stiff state The residual soils grade to tan and brown with weak mottling
clayey silts with some weathered gravel fragments to a depth of about 8 feet below the
existing ground surface representing the lower boundary of the residual soil layer Below
about 8 feet and extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the
ground surface the soil generally grades to saprolite materials consisting of brown and
purple mottled clayey silts with fine sands and some highly weathered gravel Some
localized areas of the site appear to contain some thin fills or alluvial soils which were
observed to be very stiff dark brown silty clays These deposits may exist as a result of
the previous sugar cane cultivation at the site
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during our field
exploration However soil moisture contents appeared to increase at about 10 feet below
the ground surface and became very moist to wet at the maximum depth explored of
about 21 5 feet below the ground level It should be noted that groundwater levels can
vary significantly depending on seasonal rainfall time of the year and other factors

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very
stiff silty soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site
we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils or engineered fills needed to achieve the
final design grades may be used for support of the proposed new facilities
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3907 00 Page 9
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface silty soils exhibit a slight
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To
reduce the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
the swelling of the near surface soils we recommend that the on site silty soils be capped
with a minimum of 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material
below all building slab areas Since the project site is located in a high rainfall
environment the 6 inch capping fill would also serve as a protective cover for the silty
subgrade soils to reduce the potential for building pad subgrade saturation as a result of
inclement weather during the project construction In general the subgrade soils should
be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should
be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of capping fill cushion fill
and or slab concrete
Due to the high rainfall environment on the island of Kauai and the relatively flat
terrain at the project site special attention should be given to site drainage to reduce the
potential for ponding and subgrade saturation during and after construction In addition
the on site soils have high in situ moisture contents Therefore special attention should
be given to the site grading and compaction requirements presented herein Our
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations site
grading and pavements are further discussed in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the current grading concept and the generally stiff subsoil conditions
encountered at the site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may
be used for support of the proposed new building construction An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of
shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted stiff silty soils or engineered fills needed
to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3907 00 Page 10
be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
For footings bearing on the stiff in situ soils or new engineered fills the bottom of
the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing
steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the
footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down
to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the in situ stiff soils or
engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should
be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 and 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent finish grades for the one and two story buildings respectively For
footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face Continuous footings
oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings
Footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be embedded deep enough
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3907 00 Page 11
to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should be
designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils or engineered fills as recommended
herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of 0 5 inches
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by
near surface silty soils with a slight expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on
grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these silty soils due to changes in the
moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and
capped with at least 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material
below the slab cushion The 6 inch capping fill will also serve as a protective cover for the
underlying silty subgrade soils to reduce the potential for building pad saturation resulting
from inclement weather due to the high rainfall environment at the project site The
capping fill should also extend beyond the building perimeter a minimum of 24 inches
GEOLABS HAWAII

In accordance with the requirements of D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61
dated a basaltic termite barrier BTB will need to be incorporated into the
slab on grade design of new facilities Therefore for the interior building slabs not
subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion
fill consisting of BTB be placed below the slabs to provide more uniform support of the
slabs and to serve as a termite barrier To reduce future moisture infiltration and
subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a
plastic membrane is recommended on top of the BTB cushion layer Two inches of BTB
may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane
and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet
vinyl should be considered since they can better mask any minor slab cracking
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or equipment operation such
as the Chiller Plant a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction is recommended in lieu of the 4 inch thick BTB cushion layer In
addition the 2 inch BTB and vapor barrier may also be omitted for these slabs The base
course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection p c i may be used
for the compacted base course Where slabs are intended to function as rigid
pavements a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design
purposes Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints
which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any
unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that concrete walkways be supported on 6 inches of non expansive select
granular fill or select borrow subbase The select granular fill or select borrow subbase
should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Construction joints

W O 3907 00 Page 13
should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at
right angle intersections
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
compacted on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the slabs and foundations
Retaining Walls

Wali Foundations

conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot set back

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site silty soils
or select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper
12 inches of backfill should consist of the on site silty soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The
excavated silty residual and saprolite soils in the cut areas may be reused as a
source of general fill material provided that they meet the requirements for general
fill presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection of this report
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
and or used in landscape areas where appropriate
Soft and wet soils may be encountered near the ground surface along the
drainage ditches that traverse the project site We recommend that these soft and
wet soils be removed where encountered to expose the underlying firm soils prior
to the placement of fill materials Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account
for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing finished subgrades and areas designated to receive
fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after
compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened
to close all cracks or prepared again as recommended above Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with

General fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed
finished subgrades may consist of the on site excavated soils provided that they
are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock fragments greater than
3 inches in maximum dimension Additional general fill needed for the site filling
may consist of imported materials with a low to moderate expansion potential
Imported general fill material should consist of materials with particle sizes of
3 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should have a laboratory
California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 8 or higher and a swell potential of not
more than 2 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1883
The 6 inch thick capping fill layer required under the building slabs should consist
of non expansive select granular fill material such as crushed coral basalt or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D1883
Aggregate base course and select borrow subbase materials should meet the
material requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in the
Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction
1994 Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended
use

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests results it appears that the
in situ soils exist in a relatively moist state with high moisture contents Therefore
drying or aeration of the excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use
as engineered fills Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort
of thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified compaction may be necessary and would
generally reduce the rate of fill placement for this project Compaction should be
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed
to compact the on site silty soils General fill materials should be moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction
The non expansive select granular fill should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture placed in 8 inch level lifts and compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction Select borrow subbase and base course materials
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

Utility Trench
It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed campus development will consist
primarily of water drainage sewer electrical and plumbing In general good
construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trench for
the proposed utilities The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The
excavation should comply with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional excavation
methods may generally be utilized for utility line trenching at the site Trench shoring
design and installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and
bracing should conform to appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material
be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site silty soils as
backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
We recommend that a minimum cover of 3 feet be provided between the top of
utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate construction of the pavement structure and
compaction of the trench backfill The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level
1 foot above the pipes to the finish subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils
or select granular material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than 92 percent relative

Drainage
Since the project site is relatively level and located in a high rainfall environment
special attention should be given to site drainage during and after construction The
finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided
as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Twenty one one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of these
tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Ten Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 4
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on
Plate B 5
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 6 through B 9
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 10 through B 13
