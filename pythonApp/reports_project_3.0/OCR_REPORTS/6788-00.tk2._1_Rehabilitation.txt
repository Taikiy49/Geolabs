REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The volcanic 
materials erupted from the Waianae Volcano is referred to as the Waianae Volcanic 
Series. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Volcanic 
materials erupted from the Koolau Volcano are referred to as the Koolau Volcanic Series. 
Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano remained active; therefore, the 
Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank was partially buried below the younger Koolau lavas that 
banked against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau 
referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu. The present day form of the Island of 
Oahu is a result of stream dissection and mass erosion of the steep valley walls. 
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu, multiple global fluctuations 
in sea-level occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of the great continental 
glaciers. These sea-level changes occurred more substantially in the Pleistocene Epoch 
and had some significant effect on the geologic evolution of the Island of Oahu. The 
changes in worldwide sea-level affected the erosional baseline of streams and caused 
local submergence and emergence of the coastal landforms with respect to the level of the 
sea. 
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by a 
long period of erosion whereby deep valleys were carved into the Koolau Mountain Range.

The valleys were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediments as the island slowly 
sank under the weight of the new landmass. 
The project site is along the southern flank of the Koolau Mountain Range. The 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are related directly to the 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis 
of Pearl Harbor and the Honolulu Coastal Plain. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted 
in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level of 
Oahu. The subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene 
age alluvial soil deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are 
intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in this area 
is Koolau Basalt. 
The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal 
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral-algal limestone and marls within the 
predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units. 
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused 
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the 
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high-energy conditions. 
During periods of insignificant sea level changes, continued stream action extended 
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments. 
About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that rise, the 
deep valleys in the project area were submerged. Within the last 10,000 years, the sea 
level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued 
to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits 
of soft harbor sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and 
drainageways.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided and our field observations, we understand that 
the right lane and shoulder area (westbound) along Interstate Route H-1 Freeway adjacent 
to Peter Buck Mini Park near Houghtailing Street exhibited pavement distresses and 
failures that are likely caused by the movement of the adjacent embankment slope, as 
evident by the tilting of guardrails and light poles in the proximity. The pavement distresses 
observed along the right lanes (both eastbound and westbound) between about 
Nuuanu Stream and Nuuanu Avenue may be attributed to the pumping of the pavement 
structure and underlying subgrade materials from subterranean seepage water, as evident 
by the presence of fines materials and water stains at the pavement surface.  Detailed 
discussions of our findings, evaluation, and recommendations are presented below.    
Shallow Slope Movement 
  A field exploration was conducted by our office to evaluate the subsurface 
conditions at the pavement distress areas observed along the right lane and shoulder area 
(westbound) adjacent to Peter Buck Mini Park near Houghtailing Street.  Based on the 
competent subsoil conditions encountered in our deep borings (Boring Nos. 1A and 1B), it 
is our opinion that the potential for global instability of the embankment slope is highly 
unlikely.  Our field exploration indicated that the upper portion of the embankment fills, 
particularly along the top edge of the embankment and sloping ground, consisted of 
soft/loose and inadequately compacted soils.  We believe shallow movement of these 
upper soils has resulted in the cracking, depression, and separation of the pavements 
observed in the area.  Based on our evaluation, we recommend reconstructing the upper 
portion of the embankment fills within the shoulder areas with Geosynthetic Reinforced 
Soil (GRS) with a Concrete Masonry Unit (CMU) facing. 
Geosynthetic Reinforced Soil (GRS) technology consists of closely spaced layers of 
geosynthetic reinforcement and compacted granular fill material.  The components of a 
GRS system generally consists of: (1) a row of blocks (the facing elements), (2) a layer of 
compacted granular fill to the height of the facing blocks, and (3) a layer of geosynthetic 
reinforcement.  A schematic detail of the proposed embankment reconstruction is 
presented on Plate 3 of this report.  Detailed recommendations of these GRS components 
are presented in the following subsections. 
GRS Facing 
The facing is not a structural element of a GRS mass. The most commonly used 
facing element for GRS walls is the concrete masonry unit (CMU) with nominal 
dimensions of 8 inches by 8 inches by 16 inches.  CMU blocks are lightweight, easy 
to place, and ensure compaction at every 8-inch lift before placement of the next 
geosynthetic layer.  The geosynthetic reinforcement extends directly beneath each 
layer of CMU blocks as a frictional connection.  The CMU should have a minimum 
compressive strength of 2,000 pounds per square inch (psi) and a water absorption

Reinforced Fill 
The reinforced fill material between the geosynthetic reinforcement should consist 
of non-expansive select granular materials, such as crushed basalt. The material 
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 2 inches in 
largest dimension. The material should contain between 6 and 12 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve. The backfill materials should have a Plasticity Index of 
less than 6 when tested in accordance with ASTM D 4318.  In addition, the material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum 
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with AASHTO T-193 
(ASTM D 1883). 
The reinforced fill material should have an angle of internal friction of at least 
38 degrees when tested by the standard direct shear test (ASTM D 3080). The 
sample to be tested should be compacted to 90 percent relative compaction at 
moisture contents above the optimum. 
The reinforced fill material should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 (ASTM D 1557). Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Geosynthetic Reinforcement 
The geosynthetic reinforcement should consist of a biaxial, woven polypropylene 
(PP) geotextile.  This geotextile was used for several reasons, including cost, ease 
of placement, and compatibility with the friction connection that is used between the 
block facing and the GRS mass.  The geosynthetic reinforcement should have a 
minimum reinforcement length of at least 5 feet and an ultimate tensile strength of 
at least 4,800 lb/ft when tested in accordance with ASTM D 4595.

Subterranean Seepage 
Based on the information provided and our field observations, the right lanes 
(both eastbound and westbound) along Interstate Route H-1 Freeway between about 
Nuuanu Stream and Nuuanu Avenue have exhibited pavement distresses and failures that 
are likely attributed to the pumping of the pavement structure and underlying subgrade 
materials from subterranean seepage water, as evident by the presence of fines materials 
and water stains at the pavement surface. 
A field exploration was conducted by our office to evaluate the subsurface 
conditions at these distressed pavement areas.  Based on our findings, we believe that the 
pavement distresses may be attributed to the presence of an existing box culvert 
(westbound) and hard basalt rock formation (eastbound) at shallow depths, impeding the 
vertical dissipation of subterranean seepage water, and resulting in water ponding, 
saturation, and pumping of the pavement structure and underlying subgrade soils.  
Therefore, we recommend reconstructing these distressed pavements with the new 
pavement design sections for the project. 
In addition, a subdrain system should be installed alongside the new pavement 
structural section to collect the subterranean seepage water and daylight for proper 
discharge. In general, subdrain trenches should be at least 18 inches wide.  Subdrains 
should consist of perforated pipes with perforations placed facing down and should be at 
least 4 to 6 inches in diameter.  All subdrains should be surrounded and underlain by at 
least 2 to 4 inches of drain rock, such as No. 3B Fine gravel or equivalent. A non-woven 
geotextile, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent, should wrap around the drain rock.  A 
schematic detail of the proposed subdrain system is presented on Plate 4.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration at the project sites consisted of drilling and sampling seven 
test borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1A through 1C, 2, and 3A through 3C, extending 
to depths of about 1.8 to 37 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist/engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected samples of 
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Five Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D 2850) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of 
the clay soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as the 
applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  The test results 
and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-3 through B-7.
