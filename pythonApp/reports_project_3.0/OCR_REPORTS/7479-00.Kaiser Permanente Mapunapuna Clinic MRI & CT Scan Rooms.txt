This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation for the 
proposed New MRI & CT Scan Exam Rooms project at the Kaiser Permanente 
Mapunapuna Clinic on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our engineering evaluation based on 
previous borings drilled at the site, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived 
from our analyses for the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of 
micropile foundation only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves the design and construction of new MRI & CT scan exam 
rooms in the existing Kaiser Permanente Medical Office Building and a new pedestrian 
walkway along the building, at the Mapunapuna Clinic facility. Based on the information 
provided, we understand that the existing medical office building is supported on deep 
foundation systems, consisting of concrete driven piles and micropiles. 
It is desired to support the new structures on an independent micropile foundation 
to prevent over-stressing the existing pile foundations. Due to limited headroom space in 
the existing building, additional borings were not planned. New micropile foundation 
design parameters will be evaluated based on the available boring information and will 
be subjected to verification during the micropile construction observation and testing. 
Based on the information provided by the structural engineer, we envision the new 
facilities and pedestrian walkway will be supported on vertical and/or batter micropiles to

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct 
shield volcanoes – Waianae and Koolau.  The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island, while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island.  It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau 
lava in Central Oahu. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as 
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world.  As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell because there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins.  Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available.  The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These

glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal and/or marine sediments in 
the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits 
and induration of the calcareous reef materials.  In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of 
the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively 
high energy conditions. 
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action extended 
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments.  
During these stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed distinct ledges of 
reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period.  
About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred.  During that rise, the 
valleys in the project area were submerged.  In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level 
has adjusted to its present stand.  Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to 
accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of 
soft and/or loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and 
drainageways. 
The project site is geologically situated on the southwest flank of the Koolau 
Volcanic Series.  The volcanic series is composed of basaltic lava flows of early 
Pleistocene Age.  The basalt formation is overlain by a complex inter-layered system of 
terrestrial and marine sedimentary deposits.  During the later Pleistocene Age, the sea 
level rose and fell several times as a result of glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulting in local 
swamp conditions, which were later superimposed by more recent deposits of terrestrial 
detritus of sands, gravel, silts and clays eroded from the older rock of the area.  In more 
recent time, the efforts of man have shaped the low-lying land to its present form by 
placement of fill in the low areas.

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition) (IBC 2006), the project 
site would be subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to 
be addressed, as well as evaluating for potential soil liquefaction. Based on the 
subsurface conditions anticipated, liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable 
settlements at the ground surface. Therefore, the effects of potential liquefaction should 
be taken into consideration in the design of the proposed project. 
Based on the previously drilled borings, the subsurface conditions at the site 
generally consist of surface fills over loose/soft lagoonal and recent alluvium deposits 
extending to depths of about 80 to 90 feet below the ground surface. Below the highly 
compressible lagoonal and recent alluvium deposits, the older alluvium deposit was 
encountered extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 147.5 feet below the ground 
surface. 
It should be noted that the lagoonal and recent alluvium deposits encountered in 
the previously drilled borings are pre-dominated by cohesive clayey and silty soils with a 
localized loose sandy soil layer about 3 to 4 feet thick. Therefore, we believe the potential 
for liquefaction is not a project consideration.

MICROPILE FOUNDATIONS 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the site, we recommend 
micropile foundation system to support the new MRI & CT scanning equipment slabs and 
the new pedestrian walkway. In accordance with Section 1810.3.10.4 of the International 
Building Code, 2012 Edition, micropiles should be considered as an alternative system 
where a structure is assigned the Seismic Design Category D. Therefore, the City County 
of Honolulu as Building Official should be consulted for the approval of the use of 
micropiles as an alternative foundation support system for this project. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, installation 
of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the 
adjacent soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that a micropile 
system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5.5 inches may be used.  We recommend 
designing each micropile based on an allowable compressive load capacity of 60 kips. 
The load supporting capacity of the micropile foundation would be derived primarily from 
skin friction between the micropile bonded zone and the dense basaltic boulders and 
cobbles anticipated at depths starting from about 85 feet below the ground surface. The 
allowable compressive load capacity for the micropiles is for supporting dead-plus-live

loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic

To achieve the allowable compressive load capacity with a factor of safety of 2, we 
estimate that each micropile would need to extend to a depth of about 95 feet below the 
bottom of pile cap.  The 95-foot depth is based on a 10-foot deep load bearing grout bulb 
(bonded zone) developed within the basaltic cobbles and boulders with an 85-foot grout 
zone (unbonded zone) within the loose or soft lagoonal and recent alluvium deposits. 
To facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, we 
recommend advancing steel casing to the bottom of the micropile, defined as “Casing 
Method”, during the drilling operation. The steel casing may be withdrawn during grouting 
operation while a minimum of 5 feet grout is maintained above the bottom of the casing 
at all times.  To reduce the risk of micropile “necking” within the loose/soft lagoonal and 
recent alluvium materials, we recommend 85 feet of permanent steel casing be left 
in-place at the top of each micropile (unbounded zone).  The permanent casing should 
have an outside diameter (OD) of about 5.5 inches, same as the grout bulb size. 
In general, the micropiles should have a minimum center-to-center spacing of 
about 24 inches to avoid reduction in vertical load capacity due to group action and to 
facilitate drilling of the micropiles. 
Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by shear along the grout 
bulb (bonded zone) and the adjacent soils.  In general, an uplift load capacity of 60 kips 
per pile may be achieved.  The uplift load capacity provided is intended for transient load 
applications only.  Therefore, the uplift load capacity should not be further increased for 
transient load applications.  For sustained uplift loads, we recommend reducing the uplift 
load capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads.  The capacity 
of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project structural engineer. 
Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the structural slabs may be resisted by the lateral load 
capacity of the micropile group to minimize the potential risk of over-stressing existing 
concrete driven piles. 
The lateral load resistance of the micropile group is a function of the stiffness of 
the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, the geometry of the pile group, allowable 
deflection at the top of the pile, and the induced moment in the pile.  The micropile groups 
consists of vertical and/or batter (in an inclination of 1H:12V) micropiles. The 
micropile layout and geometry are illustrated on the Foundation Plan provided by 
SSFM International, the structural engineer, and is included in Appendix B for reference.

Micropile Foundation Settlements 
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic 
compression of the pile member and subgrade response.  We estimate the total 
settlement of the micropile-supported foundations to be less than about 0.5 inches with 
differential settlements between the structure supported on micropiles not exceeding 
about one-half of the total settlement.  We believe that these settlements are essentially 
elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the compressive and uplift load capacities of the micropiles 
is highly dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, the compressive and uplift load capacity of 
the micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile 
foundation systems.  In order to determine whether the contractor’s methods of micropile 
installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate compressive load capacity, we 
recommend conducting a performance compressive load test on the first micropile 
installed at the site.  In general, the purpose of the performance load test on a micropile 
is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the 
contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required. 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grout injection. 
To evaluate the degree of long-term settlements of the micropile. 
In general, the performance compressive load test should be conducted in 
accordance with ASTM D1143.  Based on experience, we believe that the load test should 
be conducted at least 7 days after completion of the micropile installation to allow the 
grout adequate time to cure. 
For the compressive load test, two additional micropiles may be used for reaction 
during the compressive load testing of the performance load test micropile.  The reaction 
micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load test micropile to provide 
adequate reaction in uplift (to be determined by the contractor). 
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the 
allowable compression design load of approximately 60 kips.  In addition, we recommend 
holding the maximum test load (200 percent of the design load) for a minimum of 
1 to 2 hours depending on the recorded movements of the load test micropile. After 
successfully holding 200 percent of the design load, the performance micropile should be 
unloaded in four equal decrements. The performance load test is an integral part of the 
design of the micropile foundation system. Therefore, we recommend that a Geolabs 
representative observe the performance load test.

Micropile Construction Considerations 
A specialty subcontractor, experienced in the construction of a cased micropile 
foundation system (minimum five projects), should perform the micropile installation. It 
should be noted that hard drilling, such as cobbles, boulders, and concrete, may be 
encountered within the fill materials at the site. In addition, hard basaltic cobbles and 
boulders should also be anticipated to be encountered during drilling of the micropiles. 
The micropile contractor should anticipate significant grout overage, especially in 
the basaltic cobbles and boulders, and have sufficient contingency fund for grout overage 
in the bid. Typical grout overage in the cobbles and boulders may vary significantly from 
150 to 250 percent of theoretical volume or higher. 
Due to the specialized nature of the micropile foundation construction, observation 
of the micropile foundation installation and testing should be designated a “Special 
Inspection” item. Therefore, observation of the micropile installation operations by 
Geolabs (Special Inspector) is necessary to confirm our design assumptions and should 
be designated a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of IBC 2012.
