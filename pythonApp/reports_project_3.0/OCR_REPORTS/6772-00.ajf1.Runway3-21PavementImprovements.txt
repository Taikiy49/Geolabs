Dear Mr. Yokomizo: 
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Runway 3-21 Pavement Improvements project at the Hilo International 
Airport in the town of Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our findings with respect to the existing pavement 
structure and underlying subgrade condition based on our field exploration, and 
laboratory testing. The findings presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is at the existing Hilo International Airport (ITO) in the town of Hilo 
on the Island of Hawaii. Based on the information provided, we understand that it is 
desired to evaluate the character and thickness of the existing pavement structure at 
Runway 3-21. The following field exploration and laboratory testing program was 
conducted to accomplish this objective: 
Field coring (4-inch diameter) the existing Hot Mix Asphalt (HMA) pavement 
structure and sampling the underlying base to a depth of at least 18 inches 
from the existing finished grade at 40 locations along the runway and 
taxiway to determine the thickness of the pavement section. Samples of the 
base course were obtained for density determination and gradation. It 
should be understood that the samples were obtained by driving ring 
samplers which may affect the density readings.

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS 
As previously mentioned, a field exploration and laboratory testing program was 
undertaken to evaluate the character and thickness of the existing pavement structure 
at selected locations along Runway 3-21. Detailed discussions of our findings are 
presented below. 
Our field exploration program consisted of coring and sampling a total of 40 borings 
along the runway and taxiway. The runway borings are designated as Boring Nos. R-01 
through R-16, with the exception of Boring Nos. R-13 and R-14 which were cancelled by 
others due to their proximity to the runway being used. The taxiway borings are 
designated as Boring Nos. T-01 through T-22, T-03A, T-04A, T-15A, and T-16A. The 
borings extended to depths of about 18 to 33 inches below the existing ground surface, 
and the approximate locations of each boring are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology and graphic 
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of 
Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on the materials 
encountered below the HMA layer are presented in Appendix B. Photographs of the 
HMA cores are presented in Appendix C. 
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing, the existing pavement 
structure and underlying subgrade materials encountered along the runway and taxiway 
at the project site are summarized as follows:

Our field exploration along the runway (Boring Nos. R-1 through R-12, R-15, and 
R-16) generally encountered an existing pavement structure consisting of about 
4.5 to 11 inches of HMA, with the majority of the borings showing a HMA thickness of 
about 4.5 inches. Visual examination of the HMA cores retrieved from the field exploration 
indicated the upper 1.5 to 3 inches of HMA is in relatively good condition, while the bottom 
2 to 3 inches is in fair to poor condition with several cores showing signs of significant

deterioration. Two borings along the runway encountered HMA with thicknesses of about 
10 and 11 inches, both in relatively good condition. 
Our field exploration along the taxiway (Boring Nos. T-01 through T-22, T-03A, 
T-04A, T-15A, and T-16A) generally encountered an existing pavement structure 
consisting of about 1.5 to 12.5 inches of HMA, with the majority of the borings showing a 
HMA thickness of about 6 to 8 inches. Visual examination of the HMA cores retrieved from 
the field exploration indicated the existing pavement structure at the taxiway is highly 
variable. Eight of the twenty six borings encountered an upper 2 to 8 inches of HMA that is 
in relatively good condition, underlain by about 3 to 5 inches of HMA that is in fair to poor 
condition with several cores showing signs of significant deterioration. However, seven of 
the twenty six borings encountered an opposite condition where the upper 1.75 to 2 inches 
of HMA is in a poor condition, while the underlying 3.5 to 9.5 inches of HMA is in relatively 
good condition. It should be noted that Boring Nos. T-03A, T-04A, T-05, and T-06 were 
located in an area of the taxiway that had been cold planed to a depth of roughly 4 inches 
prior to our field exploration. In addition, five of the borings encountered an asphaltic 
leveling coat roughly 0.25 to 0.5 inches thick on the existing pavement surface. 
Based on our field observations and laboratory testing, the HMA layer is generally 
underlain by dense to very dense aggregate base course material overlying dense fill 
materials, clinker, and/or basalt formation. The fill and clinker materials generally consisted 
of sandy gravel and/or gravelly cobbles and were encountered at depths ranging from 
about 15 to 21.5 inches below the existing ground surface. In addition, basalt formation 
was encountered at depths of about 12 to 25 inches below the existing ground surface. 
Four California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests were performed on the near-surface soils 
to evaluate the strength of the in-situ materials for pavement support. The tests were 
performed in general accordance with ASTM D 1883 near the optimum moisture content. 
Based on the laboratory CBR test results, the on-site soils underlying the HMA layer 
exhibited marginal to excellent pavement support characteristics with CBR values ranging 
from about 26 to over 100. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-6 of 
Appendix B.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by coring and sampling a 
total of 40 borings along the runway and taxiway. The runway borings are designated as 
Boring Nos. R-01 through R-16, with the exception of Boring Nos. R-13 and R-14 which 
were cancelled by others due to their proximity to the runway being used. The taxiway 
borings are designated as Boring Nos. T-01 through T-22, T-03A, T-04A, T-15A, and 
T-16A. The borings extended to depths of about 18 to 33 inches below the existing ground 
surface, and the approximate locations of each boring are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 1. 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Rock 
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown 
on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-40. 
Soil samples below the HMA layer were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content and Dry Density tests were performed on selected soil samples 
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and 
correlating the layers between the borings. The moisture content tests were performed 
in general accordance with ASTM D 2216. The dry density tests were performed in 
general accordance with ASTM D 2937. The test results are presented on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 136 & ASTM D 1140) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Four California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests were performed on the near-surface soils 
to evaluate the strength of the in-situ materials for pavement support. The tests were 
performed in accordance with ASTM D 1883 near the optimum moisture content. The test 
results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-6 of Appendix B.
