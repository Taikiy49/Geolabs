An engineering study was conducted to evaluate open trench and probable
trenchless methods for installation of a new 12-inch water line across Paukauila Stream for
the project. The trenchless methods considered were horizontal directional drilling (HDD)
and microtunneling. Other trenchless methods, such as cured-in-place pipe (CIPP), slip
lining, and pipe bursting, were not considered during our evaluation because these
methods are for pipe rehabilitation purposes only. In addition, the construction of a new
bridge structure to support the new water line was evaluated. Based on our study, we
believe that water line installation by HDD is a viable option and the preferred alternative
when compared to microtunneling, open-trench methods, and construction of a new bridge
structure for installation of the water line across the stream.
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the stream crossing
generally consisted of a medium dense to dense and very stiff to hard surface fill layer
ranging in thickness from about 3 to 8 feet. Recent alluvium was encountered below the
fill materials extending to depths of about 11 to 18.5 feet below the existing ground
surface. The recent alluvium generally consisted of very soft to medium stiff silty clay and
clayey silt with sand and gravel. The recent alluvium was underlain by a lagoonal deposit
consisting of very soft and loose clays and silts with shell fragments. The very soft and
loose lagoonal deposit ranged in thickness from about 75 to 127 feet and was underlain by
stiffer lagoonal soils extending to depths of about 142 to 158.5 feet. The stiffer lagoonal
deposits were underlain by an older alluvial deposit to depths of about 153 to 224 feet
below the existing ground surface. The older alluvial deposit generally was comprised of
very stiff to hard clays and silts, dense silty sand, and cobbles and boulders. Below the
alluvial deposit, a hard to very hard basalt rock formation was encountered extending to
the maximum depths explored of about 166 to 236 feet below the existing ground surface.
Groundwater was encountered at depths varying from about 4 to 12.2 feet below the
existing ground surface. It should be noted that the groundwater level at the project site
would vary in response to the water level in the stream considering that the bridge is
located adjacent to the stream. In addition, artesian groundwater was encountered in the
borings at depths of about 82 to 105 feet.

supported by timber piles was demolished prior to construction of the existing bridge. The
wooden bridge was located on the downstream side of the existing bridge. Remnants of
the concrete abutment structures were observed at the site. To avoid these potential
obstructions during construction utilizing the different methods of installation, it is

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu, encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area,
may be divided into 4 basic geographic provinces. The provinces include: 1) Wai’anae
Mountains, 2) Ko’olau Mountains, 3) Schofield Plateau, and 4) Coastal Plain. The
Island of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the two
prominent Volcanoes, Waianae and Ko’olau. Of the two, the Wai’anae Volcano is the
older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago. The Ko’olau
Volcano rose above sea level approximately 2.7 million years ago making it the younger
of the two volcanoes.
In general, the two volcanoes were built by the long-term eruption of thinly
bedded a’a and pahoehoe lava flows, which were erupted from elongate and linear
trending volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the
volcanoes. The Ko’olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Waianae Volcano to
form the Schofield Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu. The Waianae
Mountains, which form the western third of the Island of Oahu, contain the oldest basalt
rock formations on the Island of Oahu. The Ko’olau Mountains form the eastern
two-thirds of the island and include the rock layers exposed along the Ko’olau Pali
extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku.
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of the
Waianae and Ko’olau eruptions, the island mass began to subside thousands of feet
under the tremendous weight of the rock. In addition to the subsidence of the island,
the force of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large-scale fracture and
slumping of portions of the land mass to form the topography of today. Between
850,000 to 70,000 years ago, volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the
eruption of ash, cinder, and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and
southeastern portions of the island. These most recent eruptions are collectively known
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Subsequently, erosion by streams and ocean waves

has continued to work on the exposed land surfaces to form the topography of the

The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea; however, the effects of
the early whole-island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene

development of coral reefs. The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be observed

3.1.1 Horizontal Directional Drilling Methods
Horizontal directional drilling (HDD) is a trenchless, steerable installation method
using a multi-axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the
subsurface. The pilot hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or
fluid-assisted mechanical cutting, or a combination of the two methods. Depending
on the subsurface conditions, water only or water mixed with bentonite and/or
polymer can be used as a drilling fluid in a directional drilling application. The pilot
hole is reamed, as necessary, to accommodate the pipe to be installed. The pipe is

3.1.2 Microtunneling Methods
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable, laser-guided tunnel boring machine (TBM) capable of installing
underground pipes at highly accurate lines and grades. The tunnel boring machine
can be auger, slurry, or earth-pressure balance type machines. In general, the
TBM is located below ground at the level of the pipe being installed. Jacking and
W.O. 5109-00(A) GEOLABS, INC. Page 16
Hawaii ¢ California
SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
receiving pits are required to accommodate the TBM and other microtunneling
equipment. As the TBM excavates the soils, the pipe behind the TBM is pushed
simultaneously by hydraulic jacks. The excavated material is removed during the
microtunneling process by a mixture of water and slurry, which is disposed either
through a pipe, or placed on a balanced screw auger, or placed on a screw
conveyor system. The microtunneled pipe can function as the carrier water pipe, or
it can function as the sleeve for an internally mounted water pipe.
Pipes that can be installed by microtunneling include the following: polymer
concrete pipe, fiberglass-lined polymer concrete pipe, fiber-reinforced plastic pipe,
Permalock steel casing pipe, reinforced concrete pipe, and vitrified clay pipe.
An auger-type tunneling machine is typically used in ground conditions consisting of
over-consolidated cohesive soils or in ground conditions consisting of cohesionless
soils where ground water is not encountered. To reduce the potential for surface
settlement, both slurry and earth-pressure balance (EPB) type tunneling machines
create internal pressures to counter the external pressures from the in-situ soils and
groundwater. Slurry and EPB tunneling machines are suitable in soft and wet
ground conditions. In a slurry tunneling machine, the pressure of the slurry counter
balances the in-situ soil and groundwater pressures. In an EPB tunneling machine,
the excavated soil is used to counter balance the in-situ soil and groundwater
pressures by controlling the amount of excavated soil being removed from the
tunnel shield. A slurry tunneling machine can balance groundwater pressures up to
about 100 feet below the groundwater table while an EPB tunneling machine can
balance groundwater pressures to no more than 10 feet below the groundwater
table.
Selecting the right machine cutter head for the ground condition is critical. Different
types of cutter heads are used for various types of soil and rock conditions. The
length and depth of the drive and groundwater conditions are also taken into
consideration when determining the type of cutter head used. Ground conditions
where cobbles and boulders are encountered might halt the progress of the TBM.

If the pipe alignment is located in an area consisting of different soil types, the
cutter head may encounter a mixed-face condition, which could lead to steering
difficulties, surface settlement, and/or deflection of the TBM causing an offset in the
line and grade.
Surface settlement is usually considered to be the primary source of damage to
adjacent streets, utilities, and other surface facilities during the microtunneling
operations. For the microtunneling construction method, the loss of ground is
generally associated with soil squeezing, raveling, or running in front of or into the
shield; over-cutting by the shield; shield steering adjustments; and soil movement to
fill the void between the pipe and tunnel wall created by the differential diameters
between the TBM and the pipe. In addition to surface settlement due to actual
tunneling procedures, surface settlement may be caused by the dewatering
operations within the jacking and receiving pits.
Minor ground losses during the advance of the heading are unavoidable with
normal construction techniques. The actual magnitudes of these losses are largely
dependent on the type of ground, groundwater conditions, overload factor
(in cohesive soils), size and depth of tunnel, and the skill of the operator in steering
the shield or machine. Empirical methods have been developed for estimating
surface settlement due to soft ground tunneling by study of observed settlements
on past projects. The typical settlement pattern that develops above a soft ground
tunnel is a trough-shaped depression resembling an inverted normal probability
curve with the maximum settlement occurring above the tunnel centerline. The
behavior of the ground is also influenced by the cover above the tunnel. When the
cover is greater than about two tunnel diameters, arching develops in the ground
above the tunnel reducing the potential for significant ground movement and
surface settlement.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four test borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4. The borings were
extended to depths of about 22 to 236 feet below the existing ground surface. The test
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and rotary coring tools. The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist, who monitored the drilling
operations on a near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings,
Plates A-1 through A-4.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths. .
Core samples of the rock formation and/or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 4 feet of

material is recovered from a 5-foot core run, the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 80%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any
drilling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock, based on the “Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals."
Very Poor
Excellent
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock, its
relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics. A dense rock formation with a
high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B-1.
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 1386), including two Hydrometer tests
(ASTM D 422), were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentations of the
grain size distribution are provided on Plate B-2.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. The results of the direct shear
test are presented on Plate B-3.
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D 2850) were
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of
the soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as the
applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples. The test
results and stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
