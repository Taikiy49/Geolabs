SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of surface fill overlying 
volcanic tuff extending to the maximum depth explored of about 50 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Boring No. 8 (the only location drilled in the roadway) encountered 
asphaltic concrete approximately 9 inches thick. Boring Nos. 3, 4, and 19 generally 
encountered a layer of surface fill approximately 1 to 8.5 feet thick overlying alluvium and 
weathered volcanic tuff to a depth of approximately 27 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The surface fill layer generally consisted of stiff to very stiff clayey silt/silty clay 
with gravel and medium dense to dense sandy/silty gravel and silty sand. The alluvium 
was composed of stiff to hard silty clay. The weathered tuff consisted of very stiff to hard 
silty/sandy clay and clayey silt with gravel and medium dense to dense silty/clayey gravel. 
The volcanic tuff was medium hard to very hard. We did not encounter groundwater in the 
borings at the time of our field exploration except for one boring (Boring No. 19) where the 
groundwater level was encountered at about 26.3 feet depth. 
Retaining walls constructed in fill conditions with wall heights of about 4 to 14 feet 
are planned.  Due to the presence of new utilities behind the fill walls, the use of a 
segmental retaining wall system does not appear viable due to interference between the 
utilities and the segmental wall reinforcing elements. Therefore, the use of conventional 
concrete retaining walls is recommended.  We recommend that bearing pressures of up to 
13,500 and 6,100 psf be used to evaluate the extreme event limit state and strength limit 
state of the footings, respectively. These bearing values are based on the footings bearing 
on or embedded into the volcanic tuff formation or stiff alluvium.  
Retaining walls constructed in cut conditions with wall heights up to 30 feet are 
planned. Based on our field exploration, the use of conventional concrete retaining wall 
and rock-nailed wall systems are recommended for the cut walls. Bearing pressures of up 
to 21,000 and 9,500 psf are recommended to evaluate the extreme event limit state and 
the strength limit state, respectively, of the footings bearing on weathered tuff or tuff 
formation. To minimize the footing width and the amount of footing excavation, the use of 
vertical ground anchors embedded into the tuff formation may be considered.  For a 
minimum 5-inch diameter by 30-foot long anchor, allowable compressive and uplift 
capacities of 50 kips may be used.  The rock-nailed wall system consists of a series of 
individual reinforcing bars grouted into the drilled holes and a shotcrete facing to stabilize 
the steep cut slope face.  The 8-inch diameter rock nails should have a minimum length of 
10 to 14 feet and an inclination of about 15 degrees from horizontal.  The rock nails should 
be spaced between 8 and 10 feet on-center.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age (2.7 – 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western 
one-third of the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age (2.2 – 2.5 million years ago) and forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. The end of volcanic activity associated with the 
Koolau Volcano was followed by a long period of landmass erosion, whereby deep 
stream valleys were carved into the dome-shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. 
The project site is located on the south flank of the Aliamanu vent and on the 
southwestern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  As 
the molten magma ascended through these vents, it reacted with the groundwater 
generating steam.  This steam caused explosive eruptions, which deposited low cones 
of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff, locally referred to as "mudrock.” 
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water-laid ash deposit that was laid down when the 
sea level was higher than the present level.  The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its 
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream. 
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased, sedimentary deposits accumulated 
over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase of volcanic activity began.  Then, the 
Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its 
present level.  The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from 
the older Aliamanu events. 
The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate from 
depressional springs and rainwater.  During periods of little rainfall, the shallow ponded 
water was reduced by evaporation, and over the years, the mineral salt content of the 
ponded water increased resulting in the name “Salt Lake.”  Recently, man has 
developed the crater floor by filling and grading the area for residential and golf course 
purposes.

and grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The ground anchor foundation typically is 
constructed by drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting

the borehole. Ground anchors are desirable because they can be installed readily 
in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground 
conditions. In addition, installation of the ground anchors generally causes minimal 
disturbance to adjacent structures, the adjacent soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that ground 
anchors with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5 inches may be used.  We 
recommend designing each ground anchor based on an allowable compressive 
and uplift load capacity of 50 kips. The vertical load supporting capacity of the 
ground anchor foundation would be derived primarily from skin friction between the 
anchor bonded zone and the volcanic tuff formation. The allowable compressive 
load capacity for the anchors is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces. 
A minimum embedment length of 30 feet should be provided for each anchor to 
develop the allowable compressive load capacity of the anchor. In general, the 
anchors should have a minimum center-to-center spacing of about 30 inches to 
avoid a reduction in vertical load capacity due to group action and to facilitate 
drilling of the anchors. 
To facilitate the anchor drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, advancing 
temporary steel casing to the bottom of the anchor hole during the drilling operation 
may be considered. The anchor grout should be placed from the bottom of the 
borehole up to the finished cut off elevation.  It should be noted that hard drilling 
should be anticipated within the weathered tuff and tuff formation encountered in 
the borings.

3.2.3 Vertical Ground Anchors – Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the compressive and uplift load capacities of the ground 
anchors are highly dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods 
employed by the contractor to install the anchor.  Therefore, the compressive and 
uplift load capacity of the anchor may vary considerably between different 
contractors and ground anchor foundation systems.  In order to determine whether 
the contractor’s methods of ground anchor installation are adequate and to 
determine the ultimate compressive load capacity, we recommend performing a 
pre-production compressive load test at the site.  In general, the purpose of the 
pre-production load test on a ground anchor is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the anchors to the depths required 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grout injection 
To evaluate the degree of long-term settlements of the anchor 
In general, the pre-production compressive load test should be performed in 
accordance with ASTM D1143.  Based on experience, we believe that the load test 
should be conducted at least 7 days after completion of the anchor installation to 
allow the grout adequate time to cure. 
For the compressive load test, two additional anchors may be used for reaction 
during the compressive load testing of the pre-production load test anchor.  The 
reaction anchors may be installed to depths as deep as the load test anchor to

3.2.4 Rock-Nailed Walls 
As mentioned above, a rock-nailed wall system is recommended for Walls L1 and 
L2. The rock-nailed wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the steep slope. A shotcrete facing is generally 
applied to the face of the reinforced slope. The reinforcing bars provide both 
additional tensile and shear strengths to the tuff formation and reinforce the sidewall 
of the slope. The reinforced rock mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity 
retaining wall but lacks a footing. 
The rock-nailed system will be installed within a cut slope condition.  Therefore, top-
down construction will be performed for the rock-nailed slope installation. 
Design of the rock-nailed slope systems will need to consider both the internal and 
external stability of the reinforced mass. The design of the internal stability includes 
establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted reinforcing 
bars.  The external stability includes slope stability of the reinforced mass.

3.3.1 Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, and other unsuitable materials 
should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials designated to be reused as fill and/or 
backfill. If soft or wet soils are encountered during clearing, over-excavation may be 
required to remove the soft or wet materials to expose firm and/or dense soils. The 
resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted fill material. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface should be scarified to a 
depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  For pavement

subgrades, the compaction requirement should be a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 (or ASTM D1557).  Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.

3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.  Aggregate base course and aggregate 
subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557 (AASHTO T-180) test procedures. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction

equipment. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the

3.3.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it appears that 
most of the cut slopes would expose volcanic tuff formation. In general, we believe 
that cut slopes may be excavated at an inclination of one-half horizontal to 
one vertical (0.5H:1V) or flatter. 
Fill slopes constructed of the processed on-site materials should be designed with a 
slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V 
should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new 
fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point and 
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill 
placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that over-cutting of a slope 
occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted 
away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as 
possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes. 
3.3.5 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, hard volcanic tuff formation was encountered at 
relatively shallow depths throughout the majority of the project alignment. 
Therefore, the contractor will likely encounter difficult excavation conditions during 
construction where the hard volcanic tuff formation is encountered. 
We anticipate that the surface fill and clayey alluvial soils encountered during our 
field exploration along the project alignment may be excavated readily with normal 
heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers.

3.3.6 Surcharge Fill and Settlement Monitoring 
Ground settlements are anticipated when substantial new fills are placed over the 
existing ground consisting of the clayey alluvial soils encountered approximately 
between Sta. 45+50 and Sta. 48+20 along Retaining Wall R2 to raise the site to the 
proposed finished grades. These ground settlements would affect the construction 
schedule and the earthwork quantity estimates for the project. In general, the 
anticipated ground settlements would primarily be the result of the following 
two processes: 
compression of the compacted fill material under its own weight; 
and 
consolidation and/or compression of the underlying in-situ soils 
induced by the new fill loads 
New fills up to about 14 feet thick will be placed over the underlying alluvial deposits 
along Retaining Wall R2. In order to reduce the effects of the anticipated short and 
long-term settlements on the new roadway constructed on the thick fills, a 
settlement monitoring program should be implemented. 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and the planned fill thickness, we 
estimate potential ground settlements on the order of about 1.5 to 3 inches could 
occur between Sta. 45+50 and Sta. 48+20 along the roadway widening alignment 
from the new fills. In addition, we estimate the majority of the primary settlement 
would occur in about 4 to 6 weeks after the new fills are placed. Therefore, a 
surcharge fill and settlement monitoring program should be implemented and the 
construction of the pavement and drainage should be delayed to allow for the

majority of the settlement to occur under the new load. To reduce the length of the 
settlement waiting period, a surcharge fill should be placed over the new fills. We 
recommend using a surcharge fill height of 5 feet. 
It should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the exact time 
required for the filled ground to settle because the settlement rates are affected by 
variations in the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition. For the 
soils anticipated at the project site, we believe the estimated settlement period 
could vary by as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual settlement period. 
Therefore, the actual settlement rates should be monitored, and a settlement 
monitoring program should be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of 
the estimated settlements during the settlement waiting period prior to construction 
of the roadway and improvements on the fills. In addition, provisions should be 
made for potential delays in the construction schedule if a longer settlement waiting 
period is required. 
To monitor the actual settlement rate, we recommend that settlement gauges 
(minimum of 1 gauge per 100 feet) be installed in areas where new fills are placed 
over the alluvial deposits (between Sta. 45+50 and Sta. 48+20). A typical 
settlement gauge detail is shown on the attached Plate 4.  
The settlement gauges should be read optically by an experienced professional 
surveyor, and the readings should be transmitted for review in a timely manner. We 
recommend taking two readings (minimum 24 hours apart) for each settlement 
gauges 10 days prior to any site filling to establish a baseline for the settlement 
gauges. Subsequent readings of the settlement gauges should be taken on a 
weekly basis for the entire settlement waiting period. Geolabs should review the 
settlement readings to evaluate if the settlement waiting period may be shortened 
or extended depending on the settlement readings and type of construction activity. 
Special care should be taken by the contractor to avoid damaging the settlement 
gauges.  Each damaged settlement gauge should be replaced immediately by the 
contractor.  The roadway paving and utility installation should not be performed in 
this fill area until the settlement has been completed as determined by Geolabs.

3.6.3 Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling 19 exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 19, extending to 
depths of about 25 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were 
drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers and 
PQ coring. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site 
Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-19. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used.  The blow counts may need to be factored 
to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs 
of Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Core Recovery may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Core Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as Core Recovery = 
74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

A P P E N D I X   B 
Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the samples tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) is indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Four one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under 
different surcharge loads.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-3. 
One Unconfined Compressive test (ASTM D2166) was performed on a selected 
in-situ sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site soil.  The 
test result is shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. The stress-
strain curve of the unconfined compression test is presented on Plate B-4. 
Thirty-Seven Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were 
performed on selected rock cores to evaluate their uniaxial compressive strength of the 
rock formation encountered.  Results of the uniaxial compression tests are presented on 
Plates B-5 and B-6.  
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of 
the clayey soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as 
the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  The test 
results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results 
are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Two Consolidation tests (ASTM D2435) were performed on samples of the soils 
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of 
the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B-11 and B-12.
