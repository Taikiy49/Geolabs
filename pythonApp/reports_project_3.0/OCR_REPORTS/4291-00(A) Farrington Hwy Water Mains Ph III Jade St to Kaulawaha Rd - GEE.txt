2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the west side of the Waianae Mountain Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and
reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present
form

Horizontal Directional Drilling
Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using a
multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole The pilot hole is
bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical cutting or
combinations of both The pilot hole is reamed to a diameter much larger than the
outside diameter of the pipe as necessary to accommodate the pipe Then the
pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling machine

The advantage of an HDD installation on this project would be the elimination of the
deep jacking and receiving pits needed for microtunneling installation This would
greatly reduce or eliminate the shoring and dewatering requirements However
due to limitations on the drilling angles at the entry and exit points at the ground
surface HDD would require a much longer installed length of pipe Additionally the
accuracy of HDD is less than that of microtunneling therefore care must be taken
when crossing under the existing drainage channel to provide the proper clearance
between the new pipe and existing channel
Typically HDD is well suited for clayey soils although it is possible to use HDD in
sandy soils by adding bentonite into the drilling fluid Gravel cobbles and boulders
may present difficulties in steering control and penetration of the drill head and
stability problems of the hole such as caving in of the pilot or reamed out hole
Because of the above reasons we believe that installation of the new water mains
using horizontal directional drilling may pose some difficulties and will require
additional considerations to make HDD a viable option
Microtunneling
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine Entry and exit pits will be
required These pits are generally rectangular in shape to accommodate the length
about three to four tunnel diameters or two pipe lengths and width about two
tunnel diameters of the tunnel boring machine the jacking frame reaction block
and pipe sections to be jacked into place During the microtunneling process the
excavated material is removed by using a mixture of water and slurry pumped
through a pipe balanced screw auger or a screw conveyor system
In microtunneling selecting the right machine cutter head for the ground condition
is critical Different types of cutter heads are used for various types of soil and or
rock We understand that the invert elevations for the 8 and 24 inch diameter water
mains are at about 6 to 9 feet MSL As mentioned previously medium dense
sands and gravel and medium stiff silts with the potential for the presence of

3 5 Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the medium stiff to hard clayey soils medium
dense to dense sandy and gravelly soils and the medium hard to hard coral a
minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided below the pipe For pipes
with inverts in soft and or loose soils an additional 18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the
bedding layer for uniform support A typical section of the trench is presented on
Plate 4
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth the outside pipe diameter and half way up the sides of the pipe Before the

3 6 Backfill
In general the first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at
least 12 inches above the top of the pipes or 12 inches above the groundwater level
should consist of select free draining well graded granular material to reduce the
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material
should be well graded less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less than
10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be
allowed provided the granular material is self draining such as No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet
below the finish subgrade may consist of the excavated on site clayey gravelly and
sandy soils The backfill should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift
thickness with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 The trench backfill from 2 feet below the finished
subgrade elevation up to the subgrade elevation should consist of select borrow
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction A typical section of the
trench is presented on Plate 4
It should be noted that boulders were encountered within the anticipated trench
depths in several of the borings drilled Boulders encountered in the excavated soils
should not be re used as trench backfill In addition excavated on site soils in some
areas may be wet therefore it may be necessary to aerate the excavated wet soils
prior to placement to allow compaction of the backfill material to the specified
requirements

3 7 Thrust Blocks and Earth Pressures on Pipes
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes The thrust force acting on
the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust
block Based on the available information the planned inverts are about 4 to 6 feet
below the existing ground surface Based on our field exploration stiff to very stiff silty
clays were encountered at shallow depths We recommend a lateral bearing pressure
of 1 500 pounds per square foot psf be used for the design of thrust blocks
3 8 Settlements
Primary settlement of the new water main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will
cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlement of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur if
improper trench support or dewatering procedures are used Therefore we believe that
the proposed water mains should be designed with sufficient flexibility to accommodate
possible settlements

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
12 borings extending to depths ranging from about 20 2 to 53 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 3 1 through 3 6 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The Pocket Penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the Pocket Penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of coral rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation The coral rock
material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of coral rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if
3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74
percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Eleven Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation C 136 and D 1140 Graphic
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B 1 through B 3
Three Hydrometer tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics below the No 200 sieve and to aid in
soil classification The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 422 Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution below the No
200 sieve is also provided on Plates B 1 and B 3
Two Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 4318 The results of the tests
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 4
Three Direct Shear tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics The tests were performed in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 3080 The results of the tests are presented on
Plates B 5 through B 7

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
One Percolation test was performed by using the Falling Head Method in the
drilled boring Boring No 5 The drilled boring had a diameter of about 4 inches and a
depth of about 15 feet at the time of testing The Falling Head Method was tested by
filling the boreholes with water and allowing the water to percolate into the ground The
water levels and corresponding times were recorded for the calculation of subsurface
permeability The head levels within the test borings were monitored by an electric
water level sounder during the test The results of the percolation tests conducted are
presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
