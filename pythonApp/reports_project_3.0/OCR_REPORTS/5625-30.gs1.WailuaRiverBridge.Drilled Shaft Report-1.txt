As requested, Geolabs, Inc. provided geotechnical engineering services during the 
drilled shaft foundation installation for the Wailua Cane Haul Bridge Widening project. In 
general, the purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to observe the drilled 
shaft foundation installation for general conformance with the project plans and 
specifications. This letter report summarizes our geotechnical observations performed 
during the drilled shaft installation. 
Geolabs, Inc. performed a geotechnical engineering exploration and our 
recommendations were presented in the report entitled, "Geotechnical Engineering 
Exploration, Kuhio Highway Widening Vicinity of Leho drive to Kuamoo Road, Lydgate to 
Kapaa Bike/Pedestrian Path, Project No. CMAQ-0700(49), Kapaa, Kauai, Hawaii," dated 
May 12, 2008. 
As part of the project, one trial shaft and two instrumented load test shafts were 
installed at the bridge site prior to production drilled shaft installation. The results of the 
load test program were presented in the reports by Loadtest, Inc. entitled “Report On 
Drilled Shaft Load Testing (Osterberg Method), North Test Shaft – Wailua Cane Haul 
Bridge Widening, Kapa’a, HI (LT-9498-1)” dated March 04, 2010 and “Report On Drilled 
Shaft Load Testing (Osterberg Method), South Test Shaft – Wailua Cane Haul Bridge 
Widening, Kapa’a, HI (LT-9498-2)” dated February 26, 2010.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The bridge widening project is located along Kuhio Highway at the Wailua River 
Plantation Crossing in the Kapaa area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The existing 
concrete bridge will be widened to about 31 feet to accommodate two lanes of traffic. The 
project location is shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.

As previously mentioned, the ASD method was used in determining the design 
lengths of the drilled shafts for the project. The drilled shafts were designed to derive 
vertical support primarily from skin friction. The end-bearing component of the drilled 
shafts was discounted in the design due to difficulties associated with obtaining a clean 
bottom below the groundwater level during construction. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The borings at the southern approach to the bridge generally encountered a 
surface fill layer ranging in thickness from about 2.5 to 23 feet thick. The fill layer consisted 
of medium dense to very dense silty/clayey gravel and sand, very stiff to hard silty clay, 
and hard boulders and cobbles. Below the fills, residual soil and soft to very hard basalt 
rock formation were encountered extending to the maximum depths drilled of 50.8 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The residual soil consisted of stiff to very stiff silty clay. 
The borings at the northern approach to the bridge generally encountered a surface 
fill layer ranging in thickness from about 2.5 to 16 feet thick. The fill layer consisted of 
loose to dense silty gravel, stiff silty clay, and hard cobbles and boulders. The fill layer was 
underlain by a beach sand deposit to about 6.5 to 34-foot depths. Below the beach sand 
deposit, loose to very dense and soft to very stiff lagoonal deposit was encountered 
extending to the maximum explored depth of 142.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The lagoonal deposit consisted of silty sand, sandy silt and clayey silt.  
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths ranging from about 15.8 to 
22.2 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. The 
groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to elevations ranging from about 
+3.2 to -4.2 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
DRILLED SHAFT INSTALLATION 
CMZ of Hawaii installed one trial shaft, two load test shafts, and sixteen production 
shafts for the bridge widening project. The approximate drilled shaft locations are shown 
on the Drilled Shaft Identification Plan, Plate 2. 
Trial and Load Test Shafts  
A 60-inch diameter trial shaft, designated as Trial Shaft, was installed between 
January 20 and 30, 2010 down to a tip elevation of -77.8 feet MSL on the north side of the 
bridge. The Trial Shaft was constructed using a Bauer BG40 track-mounted drill rig. The 
purpose of the Trial Shaft was to examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment 
proposed by the contractor to install the drilled shafts into the existing subsurface soil/rock 
deposits. 
Polymer slurry and a 64-inch diameter temporary steel casing were used in the 
construction of the trial shaft. Large cave-ins occurred during drilling of the Trial Shaft due

to the contractor’s inability to stabilize the shaft excavation. The contractor was able to 
remediate the problem and complete the Trial Shaft within the project specifications. 
Using the acceptable construction methods demonstrated in the trial shaft, two load 
test shafts, designated as LT-N and LT-S, were installed at the north and south 
approaches of the bridge, respectively. Load test shaft LT-N was installed from February 1 
to 8, 2010 using the same equipment that was used to install the Trial Shaft. Load test 
shaft LT-S was installed from January 27, 2010 to February 2, 2010 using a Casagrande 
180HD track-mounted drill rig. The purpose of the load test shafts was to confirm the 
design assumptions and to confirm or modify the estimated design tip elevations of the 
production drilled shafts. 
After completion of the installation of the load test shafts, LoadTest, Inc. performed 
the load tests on February 24, 2010 and February 16, 2010 for LT-N and LT-S, 
respectively. The 60-inch diameter load test shafts were loaded to approximately 
1,924 kips for LT-N and approximately 3,615 kips for LT-S. The load test results are 
presented in the reports prepared by LoadTest, Inc. referenced above. 
Based on the results of the trial shaft and load test shaft program, the drilled shaft 
subcontractor generally demonstrated that the proposed excavation methods and 
equipment were acceptable for installing the production drilled shaft foundations in general 
accordance with the project specifications. Based on the results of the instrumented load 
tests performed, the production drilled shaft lengths were determined.  
Production Drilled Shafts 
A total of sixteen 60-inch diameter production drilled shafts were installed with eight 
shafts at the northern bridge approach and eight shafts at the southern approach. The 
southern approach shafts, designated as 1A, 1B, 1C, 2A, 2B, 2C, 3A, and 3B, were 
installed at the Wailua Bridge site between April 14 and June 3, 2010. The northern 
approach shafts, designated as 4A, 4AR, 4B, 5A, 5B, 5C, 6A, 6B, and 6C, were installed 
at the Wailua Bridge site between May 13 and July 16, 2010. Due to encountering what 
appears to be an old bridge abutment and difficulties encountered during shaft installation, 
Shaft 4A was abandoned and replaced with Shaft 4AR. Shaft 4AR was drilled adjacent to 
Shaft 4A after Shaft 4A was backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM). The 
approximate drilled shaft locations are shown on the Drilled Shaft Identification Plan, 
Plate 2. A Geolabs representative observed the shaft excavation, placement of the steel 
reinforcements, and concrete placement operations during the installation of the 
production drilled shafts. 
The production shafts on the southern approach were excavated using a 
Casagrande B180 HD track-mounted drill rig equipped with 30 and 60-inch diameter 
augers; 30, 60, and 64-inch diameter core barrels; a 60-inch diameter digging bucket; and 
a 60-inch diameter cleanout bucket. The production shafts were constructed with the use 
of polymer slurry and temporary steel casing to help stabilize the shaft during the 
excavation and installation process. The production shafts were excavated to elevations

The production shafts on the northern approach were excavated using a Bauer 
BG40 track-mounted drill rig equipped with 30 and 60-inch diameter augers; 30, 60, and

cleanout bucket. The production shafts were constructed with the use of polymer slurry

-78.6 feet MSL. A summary of the northern approach shafts is shown on the Drilled Shaft 
Installation Summary, Plate 3. 
After the shaft holes were drilled to their designated tip elevations, the reinforcing 
bar (rebar) cages were placed into the drilled holes. Rebar cage spacers were used to 
provide the clearance associated with a minimum concrete cover of 3 inches between the 
sidewalls of the drilled hole and the sides of the rebar cage. Due to settlement of the rebar 
cages during concrete placement for the northern approach production shafts, a 
stabilization layer was used at the bottom of the shaft. The stabilization layer consisted of 
a minimum of 12 inches of nylon bags filled with No. 3 Fine gravel. The stabilization layer 
provided a stable base to support the free standing rebar cage during concrete placement.    
The shaft concrete was placed after placement of the rebar cage using a 10-inch ID 
steel pipe by tremie methods. During concrete placement, the bottom of the tremie pipe 
was immersed in at least 5 feet of concrete, as required in the specifications. Per project 
requirements, a minimum of 4 feet of clean laitance free concrete was over poured above 
the shaft concrete cutoff elevation, and was held at this level for a time period of 
30 minutes before being removed.  
To verify shaft integrity, the sixteen production shafts were tested using the 
nondestructive test method Crosshole Sonic Logging (CSL). Results of the CSL testing 
are presented in memorandums by Geolabs, Inc. entitled “CSL Report – South Side Shaft 
Nos. 1A, 1B, 1C, 2A, 2B, 2C, 3A, and 3B, Wailua Cane Haul Bridge Widening, Federal Aid 
Project No. BR-056-1(51), Island of Kauai, Hawaii” dated August 23, 2010 and “CSL 
Report – North Side Shaft Nos. 4A-R, 4B, 5A, 5B, 5C, 6A, 6B, and 6C, Wailua Cane Haul 
Bridge Widening, Federal Aid Project No. BR-056-1(51), Island of Kauai, Hawaii” dated 
August 23, 2010. 
Some of the rebar cages for Bent Nos. 4, 5, and 6 were out of tolerance.  We 
understand that remediation measures were performed for these shafts.  
Based on our field observations and the results of the CSL testing, we believe that 
the production drilled shafts for the Wailua Cane Haul Bridge have been installed in 
general conformance with the project plans and specifications and within the limits of 
generally accepted construction practices. The location of the top of the shafts should be 
measured using optical survey equipment to verify the shaft position is within the tolerance 
in the project specifications. Based on our observations of the drilled shaft installation, it is
