INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our field exploration performed to provide
preliminary design input for the proposed housing project in the district of Manoa on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general project area is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and presents our
preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the field
and laboratory data obtained and the preliminary project data provided to us to date
The recommendations presented herein are intended for preliminary design input and are
not intended to form the basis of preparation for final construction cost estimates or for
bidding purposes by the potential bidders

and Preliminary Design Recommendations dated

The overall project site is situated at the toe of the easterly Manoa hillside
bounded on the northwest by Woodlawn Drive and on the southeast by Kalawao Street
in the Manoa Valley on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The two 2 subject parcels to be developed are located on the opposite sides of
Lowrey Avenue The southwestern parcel is situated between Manoa Stream and Lowrey
Avenue The northeastern parcel is located between the cul de sac of Hiehie Street and
Lowrey avenue

FIELD EXPLORATION
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the two 2 separate parcels were explored by drilling
and sampling a total of fourteen 14 borings to depths ranging between 30 0 and 56 5
feet below the existing ground surface Boring Nos 1 through 9 were drilled
approximately 30 0 to 56 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the southwestern
parcel Boring Nos 10 through 14 were drilled approximately 30 8 to 51 5 feet below the
existing ground surface at the northeastern parcel
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the borings encountered a thick deposit of highly compressible marshy
subsoils below a surface crust of fill materials and alluvium in most areas of the subject
parcels It appears that the compressible layer generally decreases in thickness towards
the easterly hillside of Manoa Valley
The fill materials and alluvial deposits in the surface crust have a generally stiff to
hard consistency However the crust materials generally grade softer below groundwater
level which was measured at approximately 5 4 to 19 0 feet below the existing ground
surface at the site
In Manoa Valley and other eastern valleys on the island of Oahu the near surface
and also deeper alluvial deposits are known to exhibit high expansion potential low
strength and creep characteristics when saturated Based on our laboratory swell test
results the surface near surface fill materials and alluvium at the site exhibit similar
expansion characteristics when re wetted from dry conditions
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2784 00 Page 8
In most of the borings the marshy deposits below the surface crust are underlain
by a relatively thin layer of generally medium dense cinder sand Further below is a lower
layer of alluvial soils or colluvial cobbles and boulders generally very stiff to hard
The borings near Manoa Stream did not encounter the soft marshy deposits
indicating that the stream had removed the soft soils and redeposited alluvium in its place
However the transitional subsoils between the southwesterly area underlain by
competent colluvium and the rest of the site underlain by the highly compressible marshy
deposits can not be accurately delineated from the present amount of boring data
obtained
By interpolating between the subsoil profile encountered at the site and our
previous borings at the up slope landslide area it appears that the nearby easterly Manoa
hillside is mantled by a layer of alluvial clay deposits which has a relatively uniform
thickness It was also noted that transient perched groundwater could be encountered
approximately 10 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface on the nearby hillside

Site Stability
Although the site has a gentle terrain the two 2 parcels of land are located at the
toe of the easterly Manoa hillside which is known to have a ground movement history
Therefore it is desirable to evaluate the potential of short term and or long term instability
of the up slope hillside which may impact the proposed housing development
Based on our research of the available information including various landslide studies
performed by others there appears no evidence to date indicating that the up slope hillside
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2784 00 Page 10
movements have reached a mature stage and that the landslide movements may not
continue further down slope The available information also indicates that a toe of the
landslide may have developed along Kalawao Street on the southeasterly side of the site
However based on the subsoil conditions obtained by interpolation between the
site and the up slope hillside our slope stability analyses indicate that an imaginary slip
circle extending into the easterly portion of the parcels may have a long term safety factor
of about 1 3 and that an imaginary slip circle extending further into the site may have an
even higher long term safety factor of about 1 7
The safety factor of 1 3 is smaller than the normally accepted safety factor of 1 5
for long term safety concerns but it is higher than the safety factor of 1 0 considered to
indicate incipient slope failure Therefore we believe that this intermediate safety factor
may still be considered acceptable especially considering the normally conservative
nature of the subsoil and groundwater conditions that have to be assumed for slope
stability analyses
Based on these preliminary findings it appears that in general the likelihood of
the existing landslide affecting the project site is remote However to verify and or further
delineate the interpolated subsoil profile and groundwater conditions it is recommended
that additional field exploration in the area between the up slope and the site be
considered

Site Preparation
The expansive surface soils at the site are considered unsuitable for providing
direct building slab and or parking pavement support Therefore we recommend
that the site be specially prepared by placing a non expansive granular capping
over the on site expansive surface soils
At the onset of earthwork the project area within the contract grading limits should
be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation rubble debris rubbish
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
prior to initiation of grading The anticipated cut area may be excavated after
Clearing and grubbing
Prior to filling the cleared grade should be scarified a minimum of six 6 inches
properly moisture conditioned and recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum
dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 prior
to receiving the non expansive granular capping Any soft loose spots in the
cleared grade should be over excavated to firm ground and backfilled with select
non expansive granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum
dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 in loose level lifts not
exceeding ten 10 inches in thickness
In areas where two 2 feet or more of fills would be needed we recommend that
a minimum 2 foot thick layer of select non expansive granular material such as
coral select borrow or base course be used to form the granular capping

excavated to a minimum of 2 feet below the finished grade and backfilled with the
non expansive granular material
In cut areas or where the proposed improvements are to be built on the existing
grade we recommend that the cut cleared grade be over excavated a minimum
of 2 feet and backfilled with the select non expansive granular material
The select non expansive granular material should be compacted to a minimum

Settlement Monitoring
It should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time
required for the filled ground to settle since the settlement rate will be affected by
minute details of the subsoil structures and the history of the subsoil deposition
For a thick deposit of highly compressible subsoils such as the marshy deposits
encountered at the site we believe that the estimated waiting time could vary as
much as 50 to 100 from the actual waiting time required Therefore the actual
settlement rate should be monitored and provisions be made for potential delay in
the project construction schedule if a longer waiting period is required
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that settlement gauges as
shown on Plate 3 be installed at the future fill areas The gauges should be read
by a professional land surveyor and the readings should be transmitted
immediately to the project geotechnical engineer for review The readings should
be transmitted on a weekly basis in the first three 3 months and subsequently on
a semi monthly basis during the rest of the anticipated waiting period and or until
the geotechnical engineer determines that the settlement rate has stabilized
A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential delay
in the construction schedule and the possible extra costs that may be involved

Footing Foundation
Although most of the project site is underlain by highly compressible deposits it
appears that there is a minimum 8 foot thick surface crust of generally competent subsoils
at the site Therefore we believe that the spread and or continuous footing foundations
may be used to bear on the competent surface crust to support the lightly loaded two
story wooden structures
We recommend that an allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f be used for
design of the lightly loaded footings The footing bearing value may be increased by up
to one third 1 3 for consideration of short term loading such as seismic and or wind
loads
However the footing size should be limited to less than 4 0 feet square and the
footing embedment should be limited to less than 1 5 feet below the lowest adjacent
finished ground in order to reduce imposition of the footing bearing stresses on the
underlying compressible subsoils
Where the footings are located adjacent to the re aligned Manoa Stream or the
proposed underground box culvert the following additional design criteria for footing set
back and embedment should also be observed
1 A minimum horizontal setback of 15 feet be provided between the footings
and the re aligned stream bank
2 A minimum horizontal setback of 15 feet be provided between the footings
and the box culvert or

3 The footings be embedded below an imaginary 1 1 horizontal to vertical
plane extending upward from the bottom of the adjacent box culvert wall
A total footing settlement of up to three fourths 3 4 inch and a differential footing
settlement of up to one half 1 2 inch should be anticipated for the footing design
A passive earth pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used to calculate
the lateral resistance which may be provided by the embedded footing foundations A

of fourteen 14 borings to depths ranging between 30 0 and 56 5 feet below the existing

Core samples of boulder materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83

uses a hollow bit to cut into the hard layer formation The hard material left in the annulus
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing

quality of hard materials such as rock masses and boulders Recovery is defined as the
actual length of material recovered from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt
e g if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be
74 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
at the appropriate sample depths
Nine 9 one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 and
B 2
Six 6 Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the subsoils
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results are shown on Plates B 3 through
B 5
Four 4 sieve analyses were performed on selected samples to determine the gradation
characteristics of the subsoils The test results are shown on Plates B 4 and B 5
Six 6 consolidations tests were performed on selected undisturbed samples to
evaluate the consolidation characteristics of the subsoils The test results are presented
on plates B 6 through B 11
