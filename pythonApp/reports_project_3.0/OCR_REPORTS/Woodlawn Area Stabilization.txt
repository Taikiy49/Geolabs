EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This report summarizes collection and interpretation of data from geotechnical instruments at
the Woodlawn landslide complex in Manoa Valley. Not all of the landslide is stable, but the
drains and tiebacks that the City and County Department of Public Works installed in 1993-98
have prevented, and continue to prevent, noticeable surface deformations. Our
recommendation is to focus on reducing the water pore pressures on the geotechnical
subsurface soil by fixing 60 vertical drains.
LANDSLIDE STABILITY
In 1993 through 1997, three phases of earth movement instrumentation were installed by the
City and County to assess the rate the landslide was moving and the potential risk to affected
infrastructure and private properties. During this timeframe, mitigation measures were
undertaken to attempt to slow or stop the movement. The instrumentation shows that surface
deformations were arrested by the tiebacks and drainage wells that the City installed. Not
having any surface deformation, though, is not necessarily the same as landslide stability. The
challenge with this analysis is the steady loss of consistent instrumentation data. For example,
none of the 11 piezometers installed have been functional since 2005 and it is the most
important instrument to measure subsurface pore pressures. Reducing pore pressure by
pumping out water is the most important investment by repairing vertical drains. 21 of the 32
inclinometers installed are operational and this is the most important measurement to determine
subsurface movement. As for the tie back tensile strength to measure has been limited since
there are only 11 load cells working out of 77 installed.
The data show that tieback loads continue to creep upward in some parts of the landslide at
less than 1% per year. Several measured load cell loads have doubled and cut off the

instrumentation over the past two decades. Several high-load gauges have reported abrupt
slippage, have stopped reporting, or both. It is likely that some of the tiebacks, either
instrumented or not, have failed by overstressing. From the available data, at least two parts of
the landslide complex are still moving. The slow but persistent movement does not pose an
imminent risk of significant, damaging earth movement. Fixing vertical drains is the most
important task to implement.
Anticipating potential damage is speculative and depends on estimates of the amount of future
rainfall variables, performance of the pumped drainage system, residential development,
individual home additions, landscaping projects that may divert surface runoff, and the amount
of stress already imposed on the infrastructure by past accumulated earth movement.

LONG TERM PLAN
The active parts of the Woodlawn Landslide will slowly continue to creep downhill until further
stabilization measures are designed and installed. The instrumentation indicates continued
earth movement within parts of the landslide at varying rates. The current modeling however,
does not provide a comprehensive “picture” of the earth movement. Thus, planning and
designing supplemental stabilization measures is complicated by the intricate landslide
geometry as well as the unexpectedly persistent deformations. We postulate that the
conventional Mohr-Coulomb constitutive model that the 1989 through 1993 slope stability
models used to describe soil strength and deformation is not accurately representing the
creeping behavior of the complicated landslide failure planes. Geo mechanical modeling tools
have advanced significantly in the 25 years since the original instrumentation was proposed and
installed. More accurate time-domain finite difference software is readily available as are
several standard constitutive models that can describe low-stress deformations.
In order to develop a Long Term Plan to mitigate the earth movement, we recommend the
following approach:
1. Assess the true strength behavior of this landslide, because the collected data proves
that this landslide is not behaving according to simple linear-elastic/perfectly-plastic

models.
2. Improve the prediction of the landslide behavior so that future displacements can be
more accurately estimated.
Due to the high amount of rainfall in Manoa Valley and based on the performance of vertical
drains, there is an urgency to collect necessary supplemental information and take advantage of
improved earth movement modeling tools that will allow more lead time for landslide
stabilization measures. The plan for using these improved tools to develop a more
representative model is described in the September 2011 “Agreement for Professional Services
- Woodlawn Area Earth Stabilization Project - Phase 2.” Of the tasks identified for Phase 2, the
most critical work items are:
1. Replace 4 Inclinometers: The next task order against the contract, submitted in
October 2012 is to install 4 replacement inclinometers at locations where older
inclinometer casings failed from excessive deflection. The task order includes funds for
the specialized laboratory testing required to develop the improved creeping soil
constitutive model. Also included were funds for preparing a comprehensive summary
of the instrumentation monitoring that would improve continuity through staff changes.
2. Improved Slope Stability Modeling: After the laboratory tests develop the necessary
input parameters, improved aeromechanical models should be used to analyze the
stability of the existing landslide complex. The model should accurately describe the
creeping behavior that is being measured at some locations and should be able to
predict the effectiveness of supplemental stabilization measures.
With the completion of these tasks, specific locations within the study area can be identified to
produce a Long Term Plan of prioritized mitigation measures in a phased approach to better use

INTRODUCTION
BACKGROUND
The Woodlawn landslide complex affects a residential neighborhood on the east side of Manoa
Valley in Honolulu, Hawaii. The landslide is comprised of three subareas named (generally) for
the streets that they affect, specifically:
1. Paty-Alani Part |
2. Paty-Alani Part Il
3. Hulu-Woolsey Part III
Figure 1, at the end of this report, are the delineations between the three subareas along with
instrument locations and property lines.
The City & County of Honolulu maintains the instrumentation system and collects data to
monitor the risk to the roads, utilities, and city property in the landslide area. This annual report
summarizes data collected between October 2016 and September 2017.
The landslide complex predates development of the area and has been the subject of numerous
evaluations and repair efforts. Appendix B has a detailed description of the Woodlawn landslide
complex chronology. The most significant stabilization was made in three phases between
1993 and 1998 (named Parts |, Il, and Ill as noted above). The instruments described in this
annual report were installed, for the most part, during and soon after stabilization construction.

DISCUSSION
The following paragraphs describe the data collected during the 2015-16 monitoring period and
concluded rends.
RAINFALL
The monitoring period had above-average rainfall without a significant period of intense rain.
Figure 2 shows data from an offsite rain gauge at Lyon Arboretum near the back of Manoa
Valley. Rainfall data for the 2015-16 monitoring year is summarized as follows:
1. The total cumulative rainfall for the offsite gauge at Lyon Arboretum was approximately
137 inches.
2. The greatest three-month total at LYON Arboretum was 41 inches in April, June and
August 2017. The 3 month total is less than the 65 inches believed required to
significantly elevate pore pressures that cause landslide activity.
VERTICAL DRAINS
No water meter readings are available from the 2016-17 monitoring period. Typically, this
annual report would use data collected in September 2017. The most recent data from
Doonwood Engineering are from January 30, 2014. Reducing soil pore pressure through these
vertical drains is a critical component in stabilizing the landslides.
PIEZOMETERS
Data was last collected from the piezometers in 2005. Preliminary tests of the piezometers
conducted in December 2009 indicate that most of the equipment failed, including both the
accessible GEOKON data loggers at the surface and the pressure transducers embedded at

Piezometers would be useful for future monitoring, but there is no benefit to attempting repairs
of the failed systems because of the failed transducers. New boreholes are required for
replacement piezometers. A different piezometer design, one that facilitates periodic repair and
replacement, should be considered for any future replacement instruments.
LOAD CELLS
Data was collected from 5 of the original 69 load cells in 2016-17. The data loggers
2, and 5, which collect data from most of Parts 1 and 2, did not report data. Five load cells in
that area had been active in 2012-13 and might be responsive to manual readings.
Figure 3 shows the load cell forces recorded in 2016-17. Figure 5 summarizes load cell data
and indicates 4 instrumented tiebacks that accumulated load at a noticeable rate during the
2016-17 monitoring year. The four brown dots on Figure 1 indicate the load cells that
experienced problematic load change this past year.
These observations arise from evaluating the collected data:
1. Load cells that report to MCC-7 and MCC-9 only had data from October 2016 through
September 2017.
2. The 5 load cells that reported this year continue to report load changes consistent with
instability.
a. The upward trend in TB45a-13 and TB43-6 continues a multi-year trend of creep.
b. Decreasing forces in TB32-7 also bears monitoring, since complex surface
deformations are common in the Woodlawn Landslide.

OVERALL STABILITY
Generally, the drainage and tieback stabilization measures continue to perform effectively
against deep-seated slide movements. None of the inclinometers indicate significant or rapid
movement that would warrant an immediate response.
The data indicates that the Lanikaula Street curve area continues a shallow, slow-moving creep.
The localized unstable behavior has not progressed to the point of visible surface deformations.
Noticeable damage may occur sooner if future wet seasons have average or above-average
rainfall.
ISSUES REQUIRING ATTENTION
Lanikaula Street Curve Area
The southwestern portion of the Part Ill area is moving very slowly. Continual monitoring is
important to be able to measure any displacement that may cause future damage to City and
County infrastructure and utilities.
Vertical Drain Repairs
We recommend six repairs to the vertical drains. Figure 1 shows the repair locations and we list
the following urgent priorities:
1. Repair Pump VD-103 is part of a very effective 4-drain array near the Lanikaula Curve
area and was the 6" most productive well in the system this past year. A note from 31
July 2013 states that the pump trips at 15 amps and needs troubleshooting. It is very
important that this pump perform reliably.

Restore Pump VD-88 is in the middle of Lanikaula Street, so access requires traffic

APPENDIX B - LANDSLIDE CHRONOLOGY
HISTORY AND CHARACTERIZATION
The Woodlawn Area Earth Stabilization Project is comprised of three adjacent landslides above
Woodlawn Drive on the Diamond Head side of Manoa Valley. The landslides have a rotational-
translational displacement mode, with the large central blocks translating on slip surfaces
inclined about 8 degrees. The landslide complex affects the residential development from the
end of Paty Drive (northeast) over to the end of Woolsey Place (west), an area with 115 house
lots.
A predecessor landslide is visible in 1941 aerial photographs, with erosion around the edges
that suggest two or more decades of weathering. The exact date of the landslide is not known,
possibly because the area was largely undeveloped before the end of World War II. Residential
development began following WWII and progressed through the 1960s. Grading for roads and
house lots changed drainage patterns throughout the hillside terrain, creating flat terraces out of
the moderately sloping colluvial fans that hosted the predecessor landslide.
Landslide displacements were noticed in the 1970s, though the full extent of the landslide
complex was not recognized. Numerous uncoordinated and localized stabilization efforts were
undertaken, typically focusing on slide planes shallower than 20 feet.
The landslide progressed and expanded over more than two decades before a concerted
stabilization was undertaken, and that effort had numerous engineering and geological
contributors working parallel to each other but not necessarily in coordination with one another.
Nomenclature used to describe the various parts of the landslide complex developed through
1995 when the final stabilization measures were installed. Figure 1 indicates the boundaries
B1
between the three principal landslide areas.
The landslide characterization efforts, observations made during the stabilization design
process, and the monitoring data collected before the stabilization efforts, indicate that the
landslide complex is sensitive to pore pressures that develop during prolonged periods of
intense rainfall. Rainfall intensity on a scale of months seems to be the key factor. Broadly
distributed, yet high, annual totals do not appear to correlate with landslide activity. Short but
intense rainstorms do not seem capable of mobilizing the old landslides. As a rough guideline,
a 65-inch rainfall depth over 12 weeks or less is thought to be the threshold for reactivating the
landslide before the stabilization measures were installed.
STABILIZATION
The landslide mitigation was designed in 1989 through 1992 [2, 3] and installed in three phases
over a period of about 5 years.
1. Part! construction was completed in 1993.
2. Part Il construction was completed in 1996.
3. Part Ill construction began in 1997.
Stabilization measures were designed to address slide planes deeper than 10 feet. Shallow,
generally small, landslides were specifically excluded from the design and were left to individual
homeowners to address, if needed.
Each stabilization system included drainage, by means of wells with submersible pumps, and
rows of grouted tiebacks. Figure 1 shows the well and tieback locations. Storm drain
improvements throughout the affected areas were incorporated in the remedial construction.

INSTRUMENTATION
Instruments were installed in the landslide to monitor displacements, pore pressures, drainage
flows, and tieback forces. Table 1 in the main report summarizes the instruments and describes
the present status.
Most of the instruments installed in 1990 and 1991, during the characterization and monitoring
phase, were subsequently destroyed by the remedial construction. Replacement instruments
were installed shortly after the remedial construction was completed. Supplemental instruments
have been installed intermittently, while replacement instruments were installed as inclinometers
exceeded their displacement capacity and as electrical equipment failed from age and
corrosion.
Much of the instrumentation has suffered deterioration over the decade since it was installed,
though repairs are made when necessary and budgets permit. The following paragraphs
provide additional detail regarding the various types of instrumentation:
1. Vertical Drain Meters - Each vertical drain has an individual water meter that is read
manually. Individual well productivity varied widely as follows:
a. Some adjacent wells produce very different water volumes in a given year, and
b. Some individual wells produce widely different water volumes over time, even in
years with similar rainfall depths.
No simple correlation between rainfall, well productivity, and landslide displacement has
been demonstrated. In 2009, there was a possible correlation between wells that stopped
working and load increases in nearby instrumented tiebacks.
2. Piezometers - Six piezometer groups were installed in the landslide, each cluster having
either 3 or 4 instruments placed at different depths in a common hole. The pressure
transducers were surrounded by sand and separated by bentonite chips to isolate the
various layers penetrated by the piezometer installation boring.
The piezometers were connected to dataloggers that serve all instruments in one cluster.
Software difficulties developed after making an upgrade in 2003, and measurements were
eventually stopped because of datalogger communication difficulties. An effort to simplify
the datalogger control software started in December 2009 with the intent of restoring the
still-functioning transducers to service before the 2010-11 monitoring year. That effort
revealed that most of the pore pressure transducers are broken and all the dataloggers have
failed. A significant replacement effort is more appropriate than attempts at repair.
3. Load cells - Sixty-nine tiebacks were fitted with vibrating wire load cells connected through
multiplexers to data loggers. The recording equipment is installed in centralized motor
control cabinets and connected to the instruments by buried multi-strand cables.
A load cell is a doughnut-shaped steel cylinder with embedded vibrating wire transducers.
Compression of the steel load cell cylinder changes the transducer wire’s electrical resonant
frequency, which is measured by sending an electrical pulse to the transducer and recording
the return pulse. The return frequencies are converted into a force by averaging the
individual transducer responses and applying a calibration factor supplied with the load cell.
Load cells are installed between the tieback reaction block and the head, with the strand
passing through the annulus. Load cells cannot be replaced without de-tensioning the
strand and removing the cone. The strands were trimmed close to the cone and not enough
cable remains to attach a jack for re-tensioning, making it impossible to replace failed load

Over time the data loggers, multiplexers, connective wiring, and load cells have experienced
age-related failures ranging from simple battery case corrosion to failure of one or more
vibrating wire transducers built into the load cell. Various symptoms have been observed,
including data loggers failing to retain data, individual load cells failing to respond to the
reading pulses, and abrupt changes in load. This last symptom is particularly troubling
because it can indicate either a partial internal load cell failure (usually corrosion of one of
the transducers or connecting wire) or an abrupt change in landslide stability at the tieback.
A significant repair effort was completed in 2009 that diagnosed most of the failures and
repaired the failures that were accessible without excavation. Manual readings were made
of each vibrating wire transducer (load cells have either 3 or 4 transducers each, depending
on capacity and model year).
a. Load cells with no signal from any of the individual transducers have likely failed
along the buried multi-strand wires connecting the load cell to the motor control
cabinet.
b. Load cells with one or more failed transducers are reporting data that is potentially
misleading because the failed gauge changes the averaging algorithm away from the
conditions at calibration. It is possible, though somewhat speculative, to compute an
adjusted recalibration for these partially failed gauges. Until those computations can
be made, the gauges are listed as out-of-service.
Four load cells were excavated and tested in 2012 in an effort to further evaluate load cell
failure mechanisms. The principal observations from this effort are:

a. Cable splices between the load cell and the data logger are generally sound and are
not causing transducer or external electrical failures.
b. Termite damage to the multi-strand cable sheathing, followed by condensation and
wicking along the wire and into the load cell body, is the most likely explanation of
internal transducer failures.
c. Corrosion does not appear to be affecting the tiebacks.
At the end of September 2012, 18 load cells out of the original 69 were providing reliable
data, for a failure rate of about 74%. Two load cells failed over the course of the 2011-12
monitoring year, less than the 4 failures in the prior year but continuing the overall trend of a
few failures each year.
The load cells are an integral part of the tieback head assembly and cannot be replaced
after they fail. The tieback strands pass through the center of the cylindrical instrument, so
they can only be removed axially after disassembling the bearing ring and locking jaws.
This procedure would require de-tensioning the tieback, which can only be accomplished if
the design left enough extra strand beyond the locking ring for a jack to attach.
Unfortunately, the strands in the Woodlawn tiebacks are cut off flush with the locking ring at
the head, much too short to grasp with a jack. This head condition with flush-cut strands
was confirmed at four instrumented tiebacks as a task in the Woodlawn Paty-Alani Phase 2.

5. Rain Gauge - A rain gauge collects rainfall data in the landslide area. Originally installed
near 3102 Alani Drive, the instrument experienced intermittent problems starting in 2001.
Repairs were made in 2004, after which it functioned reliably until the 2007-08 monitoring
year. The rain gauge was not functioning through the 2008-09 monitoring year due to a
number of problems, mainly a corroded connection to the power supply.
The rain gauge was relocated to the pole next to MCC-1 in early November 2009. The
standalone data logger and at-grade GEOKON enclosure were retired and the gauge signal
was routed instead into the MCC 1 cabinet and onto the multiplexer, so that now the data
are collected in Column 12 from the MCC 1 data logger. This solution delivered three
months of high quality data before stopping in February 2010.
6. Survey Grid - A 5 x 5 survey grid approximately 80 feet square is located near 3115 Kahaloa
Place, near the upper part of the Part | landslide area. The grid was laid out in May 2000
around Inclinometer |-4b, which was indicating landslide displacement at that time. The grid
indicated no landslide movement since the 2003-04 monitoring year. Survey measurements
were stopped in July 2009 after five years with no measurable displacements.
