INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Wholehouse Revitalization Phase project located within the Camp Stover
Naval Housing facility in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site preparation and
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in Central Oahu
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The dips
of the lava beds generally range from about 3 to 5 degrees The lava flows on the plateau
have weathered to depths of 15 to 30 meters and are characterized by the red colors of the
soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are a
GEOLABS INC
W O 3752 00 C Page 5
result of this weathering process The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of
ancient alluvial soil deposits overlying the weathered rock The proposed project site is
located near the center of the Schofield Plateau
The project site is located in an area composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion
of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with
depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical
weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing
depth

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by very stiff
to hard clayey silt soils throughout the depths explored Our laboratory tests indicate that the
near surface clayey soils generally exhibit a slight to moderate shrink swell potential when
subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents Based on the generally stiff subsoil
conditions encountered at the project site we envision that slab on grade foundations with
thickened edge footings may be used to support the proposed new additions anticipated for
the subject project
In order to reduce the potential for structural distress to the new slabs on grades
resulting from swelling of the on site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive
select granular fill material be provided for a minimum depth of 300 millimeters mm below
the slab cushion The perimeter thickened edges of the new slabs should be embedded a
GEOLABS INC

minimum of 500 mm below the lowest adjacent exterior grade In addition special attention
should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade preparation of slabs and
pavements to reduce the potential shrink swell effects of the near surface expansive soils

design are presented in the following sections of this report
Slab On Grade Foundations
In general we envision that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings
will be used to support the proposed new additions anticipated for the subject project As
previously indicated the project site is generally underlain by slightly to moderately expansive
clayey silt soils Unless concrete slabs on grade supported by these expansive soils are
properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the residential structures resulting
from wetting and subsequent swelling of the expansive clayey silt soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling of the on site
expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select granular fill material be provided
for a minimum depth of 300 mm below the slab cushion The perimeter thickened edges of
the slabs should be embedded a minimum of 500 mm below the lowest adjacent exterior
grade In addition the base of the thickened edge should have a minimum width of 300 mm
Graphic illustration of the recommended foundation recommendations is presented on
Plate 4 Slab On Grade on Expansive Soil of this report
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the
design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or compacted fill
material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Lateral loads acting
on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance developed between the bottom of
the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting against the near
vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or compacted fill materials Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of

50 kilonewtons per square meter per meter of depth kN m This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 300 mm of soil should be neglected
In general the bottom of footings should bear on the very stiff to hard near surface
soils The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement
of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom
of footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over
excavation should be backfilled with the approved on site soils compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should be extended down to bear on
the underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary
to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement If foundations are designed and
constructed in accordance with our recommendations we estimate total settlements to be
less than 25 mm with differential settlements on the order of about 15 mm
Prior to placement of the non expansive select granular fill the clayey subgrades
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 200 mm moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The select granular fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil
above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of
moisture from the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction of drying and
GEOLABS INC
W O 3752 00 C Page 9
subsequent wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on
the slab caused by potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted
The select granular fill should be moisture conditioned placed in level loose lifts not
exceeding 200 mm and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
In general new interior slabs should be supported on a minimum 100 mm thick layer
of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or Basaltic
Termite Barrier BTB To reduce the potential for moisture infiltration a plastic vapor barrier
should be placed above the cushion fill A 50 mm thick moist fine sand layer may be
considered above the plastic sheet to aid in curing of the concrete Provision of the 50 mm
layer of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project
structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended that
interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate some amount of
possible ground movements
Where slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the carport slabs we
recommend that a 150 mm layer of aggregate subbase be provided in lieu of the 100 mm
cushion fill layer vapor barrier and 50 mm sand layer The aggregate subbase should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction The 300 mm select granular fill layer recommended for slab on grade
construction should also be provided for the carport slabs
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 150 mm cushion layer consisting of the select granular fill
material be provided below the concrete slab The select granular fill cushion layer should
be compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Crack control joints should
be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle
intersections

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finished grades we envision that
minimal site grading work will be required for the construction of the new improvements at
the subject site In general the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavations resulting from the demolition should be backfilled
with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed
and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill The select
granular fill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed
in 200 mm loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Utilities to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by
pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing areas beneath future new slabs should be
over excavated to provide the minimum 300 mm of non expansive select granular fill
material below the bottom of the slab cushion If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are
encountered at the over excavated subgrade level further over excavation may be required
GEOLABS INC
to expose the underlying firm material The resulting over excavation should be backfilled
with non expansive select granular fill material
The over excavated subgrade surface should be scarified to a minimum depth of
200 mm moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Slab subgrades should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks until covered by
the select granular fill slab cushion fill or concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after
compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be re scarified and prepared as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
Non expansive select granular fill material required under the slab areas should
consist of imported granular material such as select borrow crushed coral or basalt gravel
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 75 mm
in largest dimension It should also contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR
value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill material should be tested and approved
by Geolabs prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Non expansive select granular fill material required for the capping layer below the
new sidewalks and floor slabs should be moisture conditioned placed in level lifts not
exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction

Pavement Design
New asphaltic concrete pavements may be required for the proposed revitalization
project The anticipated subgrade soils for the proposed new pavements as encountered in
our borings generally consist of very stiff to hard clayey silt soils Based on our field
exploration and laboratory testing the anticipated subgrade soils generally exhibit a slight to
moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with a minimum
300 mm of aggregate subbase to reduce the potential for distress resulting from the swelling
of the potentially expansive subgrade soils Considering the light to medium traffic
anticipated for the new pavement areas we believe that the following preliminary pavement
design may be used for budgeting purposes
Flexible Pavements
Asphaltic Concrete
Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 200 mm
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction Subgrades should be thoroughly moistened and kept
moist until covered with the pavement surface course We recommend that CBR and density
tests be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during roadway construction
to confirm the adequacy of the above section The recommended section also assumes that
adequate drainage will be provided for the paved areas Weepholes should be provided in
drainage catch basin structures to relieve water which may accumulate in the subgrade and
cause pumping failure of the pavement The weepholes should be installed at the bottom
elevations of both the aggregate base course and subbase course layers A filter fabric
should be placed at the weepholes to reduce the potential for migration of the aggregate
base course and subbase materials from entering the catch basins

The aggregate base course and subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In general the paved areas
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the
site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas

Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system
be considered adjacent to paved landscape areas
Utility Trenches
We envision that new utility lines such as sewer and water lines and utility
connections will be required for the project In general granular bedding consisting of
150 mm of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the
pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 300 mm above the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material around the pipes and placement could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 mm above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular fill materials The
on site clayey soils may be used as trench backfill in landscape areas only The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
200 mm level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where
trenches are below pavement areas the upper 1 meter of the trench backfill below the
pavement finished grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction

Existing Underground Utilities
Existing underground utility lines may traverse the subject housing development
Therefore we recommend that all existing utilities within the project limits be accurately
located by the contractor prior to construction The contractor has the sole responsibility to
devise and implement an effective program to protect the utilities during construction
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with select granular fill materials The select granular fill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in 200 mm
level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to
be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided In addition planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the
subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of our
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
a total of 12 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 The borings were extended
to depths of about 3 5 meters below the existing ground surface In addition two bulk
samples of the near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 were collected at
selected locations across the project site to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the near surface soils The approximate locations of the borings drilled and bulk samples
collected are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 75 mm
OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 150 mm of a 450 mm drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Twenty four one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under different surcharge
pressures The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is
provided on Plate B 4
Two laboratory Califomia Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
