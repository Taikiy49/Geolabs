INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Daio Waikiki project on Kalakaua
on the island of Oahu Hawaii
accordance with our proposal dated May 22 1989
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2342 00 Page 2
This report summarizes our findings from the field exploration and
our geotechnical engineering recommendations were derived from our
analyses based on the data obtained during the exploration These
recommendations are intended for foundation design input and are not
intended for construction cost estimation or for construction bidding
purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided to us the proposed building will
be a 3 story structure with one full level of basement parking It is our
understanding that the finished basement floor elevation would be about 9
feet below the adjacent Kuamco and Keoniana Streets or at about elevation
4 feet
Site grading involving up to approximately 10 to 12 feet deep
excavation extending below the existing ground surface is required for the
lower level basement construction The excavation area would be about 140
feet by 120 feet It is also anticipated that construction of the
basement level would be under groundwater level
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
groundwater and subsurface soil conditions at the project site in order to
develop a generalized soil rock data set for the formulation of
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the basement
excavation excavation supports construction dewatering and building
basement wall and foundation design of the project
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2342 00 Page 3
The work was done in general accordance with our proposal to Suzuki
Kawabata Associates dated May 22 1989 The scope of work consisted of
the following
1 Drilling and sampling three 3 borings to depths of 50 0 to 52 0
feet below the existing ground surface
2 Reconnaissance of the project site and vicinity to make visual
observations of the surficial soil conditions
3 Review of available soils and geologic information pertinent to
the project site and its vicinity
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field
exploration to determine the engineering properties and
Classification of the materials encountered
5 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the
purpose of developing site grading excavation dewatering and
foundation design recommendations and
6 Preparation of this report presenting the findings and design
recommendations
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and laboratory testing
are presented in the appendices of this report

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on the proposed development it appears that the upper fill
crust would be removed for the construction of the lower basement level
and the underlying very loose lagoonal deposits are not adequate to
support the foundation loads on spread footing foundations Therefore it
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2342 00 Page 5
is our opinion that pile foundations should be used to support the
proposed structure with piles end bearing on the underlying coral
formation
For construction of the one full level basement it is anticipated
that excavation supports consisting of an adequately designed continuous
interlocking steel sheet piles penetrating sufficiently deep into the
loose lagoonal deposits with an adequately installed bracing systems would
be required We believe an adequately designed and constructed support
system should be used to facilitate construction dewatering and minimize
disturbance to the surrounding streets utilities and structures
Pile Foundations
Since coral algal formation was encountered in the borings we
believe pile foundations penetrating the upper loose lagoonal deposits and
bearing on the underlying coral formation may be utilized for the project
octagonal prestressed concrete piles may be used An allowable pile
capacity of 75 tons per pile for 12 inch 12 square piles or 100 tons
Estimated settlements are minimal for piles end bearing on the coral
formation
Based on the exploration results we estimated that pile lengths
ranging from about 30 to 35 feet below the proposed basement level at
about elevation 4 feet may be required For initial estimating
purposes an average casting pile length of about 40 feet may be utilized
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2342 00 Page 6
The piles should be driven with a hammer delivering a rated energy of
at least 27 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
level control so that driving within the loose deposits may be controlled
at a reduced energy to minimize the tensile stresses development within
the piles which could cause damage cracking to the piles
Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
The driving process should be continuous without interruption for
each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity
modified when the driving process is interrupted for more than
four 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose
sandy soil the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled
reduced energy so that excessive tensile stresses are not
induced in the pile
For 75 ton pile capacity the pile driving may be terminated when
the blow counts steadily increase with depth and reach 6 blows
per inch for the last inch or when aggregated blow counts are 55
blows for the last foot
For 100 ton pile capacity the pile driving may be terminated
when the blow counts steadily increase with depth and reach 10
blows per inch for the last inch or when aggregated blow counts
are 100 blows for the last foot
The above criteria is based on our preliminary wave equation analyses
performed for a diesel hammer delivering a maximm rated energy of
39 700 foot pounds to drive a 12 inch square concrete pile for a design
GEOLABS HAWAII

capacity The results of the wave equation analyses for pile driving are
presented in Appendix C
The purpose of the wave equation analyses was to provide the
contractor data to select pile driving hammers for production pile
driving Since the results will vary depending on the equipment the
contractor uses a new pile driving wave equation analysis should be
performed when the actual hammer data are available
An allowable lateral pile capacity of up to 8 kips per pile for the
piles may be used Ultimate uplift capacities of 16 toms per pile for
may be used
Test Pile Program
A test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following
objectives
1 To examine the drivability of piles with the use of one or more
selected hammers
2 To more accurately determine production pile order lengths and
delineate different areas for different pile lengths
3 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the driving criteria
To achieve these objectives we recommend that a minimm of 10
percent of the total piles be test driven at the site The initial test
piles should be casted 10 feet longer the anticipated pile lengths These
test piles may later be incorporated into the actual foundation
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2342 00 Page 8
Splice cans should be provided at the site during the test pile
driving period Splice cans may be fabricated with 1 4 inch thick high
grade steel and should be at least 5 feet long
It is recommended that the project geotechnical engineer be consulted
for test pile locations when the final foundation plan is available
Also the test pile program should provide the flexibility of addition or
relocation of the test piles as needed in order to suit the ground
conditions
As a part of the test pile program a minimm of one 1 pile load
test is recommended The selected test pile should be load tested to
twice the allowable load capacity in accordance with ASTM Test Method
D 1143
The test piles should be driven with the same hammer which will be
used for production pile driving The test pile program should be
conducted under close and continuous observation of the project
geotechnical engineer who will determine when piles may be stopped and to
provide guidance should additional measures be required due to unforeseen
driving conditions
The geotechnical engineer should evaluate the final pile load
carrying capacity and production pile lengths based on the results of the
test pile driving and the pile load test
Site Grading
Site grading involving excavation up to about ten 10 feet below the
existing ground surface is anticipated for the basement construction It
is our understanding that the basement level may be approximately five 5
to six 6 feet below the existing groundwater level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2342 00 Page 9
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading
limits should be cleared of vegetation rubbish demolished footings
pavements slabs and other deleterious materials These materials should
be removed and disposed of properly off site
The in situ groundwater level was encountered at about 4 0 to 6 5
feet below the existing ground surface as measured in our field borings
Considering the high in situ groundwater level and workability of pile
driving equipment we recommend that the building site be first excavated
to no lower than elevation 4 0 feet MSL during initial grading This is
to assure that the machinery and equipment for pile driving may stay above
the groundwater level After pile driving the contractor may have to
come back to install the sheet pile shoring and to complete the remaining
site excavation prior to basement retaining wall slab and pile cap
construction
The select on site upper fill material may be re used as
fill backfill material If required all imported fill material should
consist of non expansive select granular materials less than 3 inches in
maxim size with less than 20 passing the 200 sieve such as select
borrow crusher waste cinder or coral The fill material should be
tested and approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to transportation
to the site
The fill backfill should be placed in horizontal loose level lifts up
to a maximm of 9 inches thick and compacted to a minimm of 90 of its
maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 All fill or backfill
below the water level should consist of clean granular materials such as
on site coral sand with gravel upper fill material or aggregate
The site should be graded to drain water away from the foundations

The ground outside the basement excavation area should be scarified
to a depth of minimm six 6 inches moisture conditioned to above
optimum moisture content and recompacted to 90 of its maximm dry density

should be over excavated and then replaced with compacted non expansive
select materials
Basement Excavation
Based on the current information provided to us one level of
basement parking will have an area of approximately 120 feet by 140 feet
and the finished basement floor is about nine 9 feet below the adjacent
Keoniana and Kuamoo Streets or at about elevation 4 feet MSL
Therefore basement excavation up to approximately ten 10 to twelve
12 feet below the existing ground surface is anticipated It is also
anticipated that the excavation would be about five 5 to six 6 feet
below the in situ groundwater level as indicated by our water level
measurements in the field borings
Basement excavation is anticipated to he relatively easy for the
upper pavement medium dense fill layer and the underlying very loose
lagoonal deposits Since pile driving would likely be performed prior to
final basement it is anticipated that the contractor would have to
excavate in between the previously driven piles The method and equipment
used for excavation shoring and dewatering should be the choice of the
contractor within practical limits and safety considerations
In addition excavated materials should not be stockpiled within
fifteen 15 feet of the excavation boundaries in order to reduce the
loading on the shoring system and the potential of ground movement or
subsidence which could cause damages to the adjacent structures As much
as possible heavy construction equipment should also be kept away from
the top of the shored excavation

Excavation Supports
Appropriate excavation support systems such as interlocking steel
sheet piles should be utilized The selection and placement of the
excavation support system to maintain and to repair damages to the
integrity of the adjacent streets utilities and structures during the
basement construction should be the contractor s sole responsibility
For continuous steel sheet pile supports the steel sheeting should
be properly interlocked and driven with a suitable vibratory hammer to
achieve proper embedment prior to the dewatering and excavation Based on
the boring data we recommend that a minimm total sheet pile length of
forty plus 40 feet be used for the one level basement construction
The properly constructed sheet piles would reduce the potential of bottom
heaving or quick conditions by upward seepage during dewatering and
facilitate dewatering by cutting off reducing inflows
Cantilever sheet pile system may be considered if a minimm setback
of twelve 12 feet is present between the excavation boundaries and the
property limit In areas where the excavation is immediately adjacent to
the surrounding streets or the setback is less than twelve 12 feet an
adequately constructed bracing system such as rakers should be used with
the sheets
Since the excavation bottom would be under groundwater and the
subgrade soil is anticipated to be loose the previously driven concrete
piles may be used to provide additional support for the rakers An
allowable lateral pile capacity up to ten 10 kips per pile for
piles may be used for bracing rakers design before the pile caps are
constructed

For calculating earth loads in the bracing system we recommend the

Note that the enclosed pressure diagram does not include a factor of

diagram
The ground adjacent to the basement excavation should be continuously
monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of movements and

Six 6 inclinometer casings along with six 6 observations wells be

Construction Dewatering
For construction of the basement level dewatering will be necessary
since the in situ groundwater level is anticipated to be about five 5 to
Six 6 feet above the basement floor The anticipated dewatering area is
anticipated to be large about 120 feet by 140 feet
The method and equipment to be used for the dewatering operations
should be the choice of the contractor within practical limits and safety
considerations
Because of the proximity of the surrounding existing streets and
structures we believe dewatering such as wellpoint or submersible pump
system located inside the excavation is more preferable than a dewatering
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2342 00 Page 13
system which is located outside of the excavation since it may impose a
greater influence draw down of water level outside of the basement
excavation shoring to the surrounding areas
The underlying lagoonal silty sand deposits are very loose and
generally contain appreciable amounts of fines It is our opinion that
the permeability of the lagoonal deposits may be considered as moderate
To avoid piping and quick conditions the dewatering operations should
be done continuously and without pumping out soil fines pumping clear
water only
To facilitate working conditions at the bottom of the basement
excavation an above water working platform consisting of a minimum of 12
inches of clean gravel 3 Fine grave should be considered
Alternatively tremie seals or mud slab may be considered to
reduce water infiltration at the bottom of excavation and to provide firm
dry surface for the waterproofing membrane installation if pumping appears
difficult to achieve the dewatered level required to work in a dry
condition This method would involve over excavation and placing tremie
concrete under water to seal the bottom of the excavation prior to
dewatering The subgrade should be probed to verify that the required
excavation depth has been achieved To compensate for buoyancy effect a
minimum concrete thickness of three 3 feet is recommended assuming a
water head of six 6 feet outside the basement level
Note that the method of tremie seals for dewatering is relatively
expensive due to the thickness required for buoyancy effect In addition
it may be necessary to cut into the tremie concrete later for construction
of pile caps and grade beams

Basement Slab Pavement Sections
To account for buoyancy effect on the basement floor during
construction we recommend that the ultimate uplift pile capacities given
in the section of Pile Foundations be used The project structural
engineer should select an appropriate factor of safety for the above
design
It appears that the basement slab pile caps and grade beams may be
designed as one structural unit in order to mobilize the uplift pile
capacities The concrete pour for the basement slab pile caps and grade
beams could be placed monolithically
Because of high up lift water pressures below the basement slab and
around the lower basement wall it is critical that proper waterproofing
should be done for the basement construction Special attention should be
given to the details of waterproofing along the connections between the
vertical basement walls and the horizontal bearing surface of the bearing
wall foundations during construction to reduce potential leakage
It is our understanding that the basement level is for parking usage
and concrete pavement for driveway area and basement floor is anticipated
For basement level parking a minimm of 6 inches of properly
reinforced concrete above the waterproofed slab is recommended

report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made

APPENDIX
Wave Equation Analysis of Pile Driving
The wave equation analysis of pile driving was performed using Program
WEAPG which is a microcomputer adaption of the program WEAP originally
written by Goble and Associates for the Federal Highway Administration to
solve pile drivability problems
The program models hammer pile soil system by representing the pile
and driving system by a series of masses and springs The soil is modeled
by a spring and a dashpot attached to each mass The program output
provides ultimate resistance and maximum compressive and tensile stresses
in the piles as a function of hammer blowcount
The primary objectives of the analysis are
1 to analyze the driving stresses in the pile
2 to determine the suitable driving system to be used for the pile
driving and
3 to predict the ultimate pile capacity of the pile
octagonal concrete piles with a DELMAG D 22 hammer The relationships of
ultimate capacity and hammer blowcounts commonly known as the bearing
graph are presented on Plate C 1 together with input information The
maximum compressive and tensile stresses are presented on Plate C 2
