As requested, we performed Crosshole Sonic Logging (CSL) testing on
Bridge No. 3, Shaft No. 2 (South Mauka), installed for the above project. This technical
memorandum provides the results of the CSL testing and our recommendations.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project specification, Section 511 — Drilled Shafts, requires performing
CSL testing on Bridge No. 3, Shaft No. 2, installed for the project. We performed the
CSL testing on November 7, 2022 for Bridge No. 3, Shaft No. 2 installed for the above
project.
Bridge No. 3, Shaft No. 2, was drilled from October 24 to November 7, 2022,
followed by concrete placement on November 2, 2022. The approximate location of the
shaft is presented on the Site Plan, Plate 1. Four 2-inch diameter steel CSL access tubes
were installed along with the reinforcing cage. Details of the drilled shaft as-built conditions
are summarized in the following table:

CROSSHOLE SONIC LOGGING TESTING
In general, the CSL testing was conducted in accordance with ASTM D6760 using
the CHUM CSL Testing equipment manufactured by Piletest. Crosshole Sonic Logging
tests were performed between all the possible access tube combinations in the drilled
shaft (four tubes resulting in six profiles). The northernmost access tube was designated
as Tube 1, and subsequent tubes were numbered in a clockwise direction.
The steel access tubes were extended an average of approximately 6.6 feet above
the top of the drilled shaft concrete for access considerations. We understand that potable
water was used to fill the access tubes during concrete placement, and the water level
was maintained in the access tubes until CSL testing. Based on our observation in the
field, the water levels within the access tubes were above the top of the shaft concrete in
the four access tubes at the time of testing.
The CSL test results are based on the percentage decrease in velocity as
correlated to the following Concrete Condition Rating Criteria (CCRC). Deviations from the
following values are generally used for determining the Concrete Condition Rating. The
CCRC is summarized in the table below.

The average apparent sonic wave velocity can be determined from the CSL test
results by dividing the average tube distances by the average first arrival times for each
tube combination in a drilled shaft. It should be noted that the average apparent sonic
wave velocity computed in this manner is not an absolute number and should be used with
caution because the actual tube distances below the top of the drilled shaft are not known
accurately.
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
A summary of the CSL test results, including the tested concrete length, computed
apparent sonic wave velocity, and comments on the concrete condition, are presented on
Plate 2. The CSL test results for the drilled shafts tested are summarized on Plate 3. The
individual CSL test plots for each tested tube combination (profile) and a plan showing the
tube locations are presented on Plates 4.1 and 4.2. It should be noted that the depth
below the concrete is from the top of the 3.8 feet overpour height.
Based on the CSL test results, the increased first arrival times (FATs) were
generally less than 10 percent, corresponding to a calculated wave velocity reduction of
less than 10 percent, and the wave energy was relatively uniform in all the profiles for
Bridge No. 3, Shaft No. 2, except for the areas within the profile as described below. It
should be noted that changes in the relative energy can sometimes indicate defects
depending on the degree of the reduction and the associated increase in FAT.
° Profile 1-2: FAT increases up to 15.6 percent from approximately 19.5 to
21.9 feet below the top of concrete.
e Profile 1-2: FAT increases up to 15.9 percent from approximately 69 to
71.2 feet below the top of concrete.
° Profile 2-4: FAT increases up to 13.4 percent from approximately O to
2.1 feet below the top of concrete.
e Profile 3-4: FAT increases up to 23.7 percent from approximately O to
4.3 feet below the top of concrete.
The average apparent sonic wave velocities for the in-placed concrete for the test
shaft ranged from approximately 11,039 to 20,528 feet per second. Based on the results of
the CSL test and FAT increases of greater than 10%, we believe that the concrete integrity
of the Bridge No. 3 South Mauka Shaft within the testing depth and below the top of the
shaft would be classified as “Poor/Defect” based on the above CCRC.
Defect FAT increases up to 23.7 percent were measured and are located within the
overpour zone. We believe the questionable FAT increases observed near the tip of the
shaft in Profile 1-2 are due to the crookedness of Tube 2 and are not associated with the
quality of the concrete. Therefore, we believe that no significant defect/anomaly is
