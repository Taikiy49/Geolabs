INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Proposed New Minimart at Kawaihae Gas Station located adjacent to
the existing Kawaihae Gas Station at the intersection of Akoni Pule Highway and
Kawaihae Wharf Road in the District of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation and site
grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new minimart adjacent to the existing Kawaihae Gas
Station at the intersection of Akoni Pule Highway and Kawaihae Wharf Road in the
District of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii We understand that the existing
Kawaihae Gas Station will be demolished and relocated to the proposed minimart site
We also understand that the proposed project will consist of the construction of a
one story building containing the minimart and post office a parking area driveways a
pump island and two underground fuel storage tanks side by side
We understand that the finish floor elevations for the proposed new minimart will
be at approximately 15 7 feet Mean Sea Level MSL and will be constructed of concrete
slab on grade with metal framing Therefore we anticipate only minor site grading will be
needed to achieve the design grade for the proposed project
Based on our past experience in the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that
the subsoil conditions at the proposed minimart site may consist of surficial fill materials

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Kohala Mountain which is
the oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the island of Hawaii Kohala Mountain was
built during the Pleistocene Epoch by basaltic lava flows from the Polou and Hawi
Volcanic Series Typically the lava flows of the Polou Volcanic Series are composed of
tholetic olivine basaltic lava flows grading to oceanite Generally such flows are
characterized as thinly bedded with a high degree of porosity
During the later stages of the active phase of Kohala Mountain vents of the Hawi
Volcanic Series opened These events extruding lava of andesitic and trachytic
composition built cinder cones along the main rift zones of the mountain and extruded
lavas which covered most of the Polou rocks These andesitic rocks generally form
thicker and denser flows

dense gravelly soils followed by basalt rock extending to the maximum depth explored
of about 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the results of our field
exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system bearing on the near surface
medium dense to dense gravelly soils or underlying basalt rock may be used to
support the proposed new minimart We anticipate only minor site grading consisting of

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used to support the proposed minimart and pump island canopy An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on the dense
may be used for the design of footings extending through the near surface fills and
bearing on the underlying basalt rock The allowable bearing pressures recommended
are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads
such as temporary wind and or seismic forces Footings should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Deeper footing
embedments may be needed for footings designed with the higher bearing pressure to
bear directly on the basalt rock
Where located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the footings should
extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom
of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
If footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlements of footings are estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch
with differential settlements on the order of inch

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of
footings A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used between the base of the footings
and the near surface fill materials For footings bearing directly on basalt rock a
coefficient of friction of 0 7 may be used Resistance due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f for footings embedded in the near surface fill materials These values
assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly against the near surface fill
materials and or cast neat against basalt rock Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
We anticipate that the structure proposed to support the canopy for the pump
island will be subjected to uplift forces Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of
the following 1 dead weight of the structure 2 weight of backfill material above the
footing where applicable and 3 side shear between the sides of the footings and
surrounding soils Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a
unit weight of 110 p c f For side shear between the sides of the footings and the
adjacent soils a shear value of 150 p s f may be used
For footings bearing on the near surface fills the bottom of the footing excavations
should be moistened and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Any soft and or loose materials
encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with granular material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the competency of the
bearing materials and embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures such as the minimart and pump island For more uniform support we
recommend that the subgrade be properly prepared and a cushion fill layer be provided
below slabs on grade
The subgrades consisting of the near surface fill materials should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
The subgrades consisting of basalt rock should be proof rolled with a minimum
10 ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy equipment minimum of 4 passes to assist in
detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities
For more uniform support of interior slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic such as
at the minimart we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting
of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slab The
cushion fill layer would also serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture
vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion
layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be placed above the plastic membrane to
provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete The sand
should be moistened slightly prior to concrete placement Flexible floor coverings such
as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab
cracking
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3887 00 Page 9
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as at the pump
island we recommend that a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4 inch thick
cushion layer The 2 inch moist fine sand and vapor barrier may be omitted for these
slabs The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For design of slabs subjected to vehicular
traffic a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be
used for the compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load transfer
across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of
slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with the
on site granular soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential
for water ponding around the foundation
Site Grading
In general we anticipate that site grading will consist of minor cuts and fills on the
order of less than 2 feet relative to the existing ground surface to provide a level pad for
the new structures Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe whether the
exposed soil conditions during excavation are similar to those encountered in our
exploration The following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the
design of site grading work
At the onset of earthwork all area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and properly disposed of off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3887 00 Page 10
In areas to receive fill the exposed gravelly subgrade soils should be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For subgrades
of improvements subjected to vehicular traffic such as the asphalt pavement and pump
island slabs the compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If the exposed subgrade consists of basalt rock the subgrade should be
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory roller or similar heavy equipment minimum
of 4passes to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities
Soft loose weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during site grading should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The material resulting from the over excavation should be removed and properly
disposed of off site
In general the on site soils generated from the excavation may be reused as a
source of fill and backfill material provided that no particles are greater than 6 inches in
largest dimension and the material is free of deleterious materials Fills and backfills
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level
lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement of the fill and backfill
material below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be increased to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
If imported fill materials are required the imported fill and or backfill material should
consist of well graded select granular material less than 6 inches in maximum dimension

The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by
Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for its intended use
It should be noted that basalt rock was encountered at about 1 5 to 3 5 feet below
the existing ground surface Excavations such as for the underground storage tanks

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all

2 inches into the subgrade below the aggregate base course to reduce migration of
landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we recommend a_ subdrain system be constructed adjacent to
paved landscape areas
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines would be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining
backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or the
on site soils The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slabs and footing
foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings to a depth of about 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with hollow stem augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 5 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 70 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 70
The Reck Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
