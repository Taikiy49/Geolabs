INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Million Gallon MG Reservoir in the Hilo area
on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the proposed new reservoir project These recommendations are intended
for the design of foundations site preparation and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located off Kawailani Street in the Hilo area on the Island of
Hawaii Based on the information provided we understand that it is proposed to construct
a new reservoir at the project site The general layout of the new reservoir is
shown on the Site Pian Plate 2

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa The lava
formation encountered at the site appears to consist of pahoehoe flow which spread
and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities

are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is
still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities voids and
inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten
lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend
for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally
smailer in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities
and along clinker layers The permeability of the fractures and cavities is high
therefore they serve as the major water transmission features
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located off Kawailani Street in the Hilo area on the Island of
Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The site is generally bounded by
residential housing to the north and east Kawailani Street to the south and heavily
vegetated vacant land to the west The site is approximately rectangular in shape
encompassing approximately 28 500 square feet in area and bounded by a chain link
fence around its perimeter
Based on the site plan provided the proposed reservoir site will be situated to the
east of the existing 500 000 gallon steel tank Our field observations indicate that the
existing steel tank is heavily corroded with some small concrete pads scattered around the
south side of the tank structure Based on our field observations and the site plan
provided the project site is relatively level with existing ground surface elevations at about
455 to 456 feet MSL

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by medium
dense to very dense granular fills overlying weathered clinkers and hard basalt rock
formation at greater depths Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project
site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of a perimeter ring footing bearing on
12 inches of compacted base course over the recompacted in situ granular fill soils may
be used for support of the proposed new reservoir
W O 4197 00 Page 7
Due to the potential for variation in the quality of the surficial fill soils we
recommend that the surficial fill soils be capped with a minimum of 12 inches of
compacted base course below the reservoir slab areas This serves to provide more
uniform support for the tank slab and to reduce the potential for distress to the future
slab on grade
As previously indicated cavities are commonly encountered in the basaltic lava
flows in the vicinity Therefore we recommend that a program of probing and grouting the
reservoir foundation locations be implemented during construction A representative of
Geolabs Hawaii should observe the probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent
of cavities if encountered Special attention should be given to the Foundation Probing
section of this report
In general the subgrade soils should be proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer
or similar heavy equipment such as a 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of
six passes to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities Soft loose
weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during site preparation operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with structural fill Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the
design of foundations site preparation and pavements are further discussed in the
following sections of this report
Reservoir Foundations
Based on the existing topography and the generally dense subsoil conditions
encountered at the site we believe that shallow foundations may be used for support of
the proposed new reservoir An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of a perimeter ring footing bearing on
12 inches of compacted base course over the recompacted surficial fill soils anticipated at
the project site This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4197 00 Page 8
Due to the potential for presence of cavities in the basaltic lava flows in the vicinity
we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the foundation locations be
implemented during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe the
probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Probing
and grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of this
report
For footings bearing on the compacted base course over the recompacted in situ
dense soils the bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface
prior to placement of the base course material If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation may be backfilled with on site
granular soils compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing
bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted base course over
the in situ dense soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using
an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility tench or as deep as the inverts of the
utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement

We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the compacted base course over in situ dense soils as recommended herein to
be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of 0 5 inches
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration and previous experience in the vicinity of the site
cavities and or voids may be present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project
site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the
cavities below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting
at the foundation locations be conducted during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and at 10 feet intervals along the perimeter wall foundation The
probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet
below the planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be
drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity
and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout
should have a slump of about 6 to 9 inches
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be
required in the construction schedule
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4197 00 Page 10
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed
Slab On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by surficial fill
soils consisting of medium dense to very dense silty sands and sandy basalt gravel Due
to the potential for variation in the quality of the near surface fill soils we recommend that
the materials below the bottom of the tank floor be over excavated and replaced with at
least 12 inches of compacted base course for more uniform bearing support The
12 inches of compacted base course should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond
the limits of the tank structure The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of
structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per
inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted base course The area adjacent to
the reservoir slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with compacted on site soils
and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding
around the slab and foundation
Site Coefficient
Basalt rock formation was encountered at relatively shallow depths from about 8 to
15 feet at the site Based on this information the project site may be classified from a
seismic analysis standpoint as being a stiff soil or rock site corresponding to a Type Soil
Profile A seismic site coefficient of 1 0 may be used for the project site for seismic
analysis of the project structure Table No 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1994
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the design finish floor elevation of the new
reservoir we anticipate that minor site grading consisting of cuts and fills of less than
3 feet thick will be required for the project construction

At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill Tne excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath future slabs and foundations should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick compacted base
course layer as recommended earlier Prior to placement of the base course layer the
subgrade soils should be proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
equipment such as a 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist in
detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities Soft loose weak or yielding
areas or cavities disclosed during site preparation operations should be over excavated to
expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with structural fill
material well graded 6 inch minus granular materials compacted to a minimum
95 percent relative compaction The material resulting from the over excavation should be
removed and properly disposed of off site The proof rolling operations should be
conducted under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii
Aggregate base course material should meet the material requirements for
Aggregate for Untreated Base Section 703 06 as specified in the Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Imported fill
material if required should consist of non expansive imported granular material such as
crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than
1 percent Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use

Base course and imported granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

Water Lines
We envision that new water lines will be required for this project In general for
support of the water lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes The initial
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills
such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finish grade may consist of the on site soils or select granular
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction
For utility lines located below the footprint of the reservoir tank the utility trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the base course
layer The base course layer recommended below the tank floor should be placed above
the concrete to provide more uniform support for the tank slab The concrete around the
utility lines below the reservoir tank structure should be placed neat against the sides of
the excavation
Drainage
The finished grades outside the reservoir structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be

properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to a depth of about 30 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected soil samples
of the near surface fill soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid
in soil classification The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the
near surface soils to evaluate the moisture density relationship Results of the test are
presented on Plate B 3
