SECTION 1 GENERAL
This report summarizes the findings of our geotechnical site reconnaissance
performed to evaluate the Math Science MS building and the existing retaining wall at
the Leeward Community College in the Pearl City area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
This report also presents our recommendations based on our geotechnical analysis
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted
at the end of this report
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map

Project Considerations
The project involves a building and a retaining wall within Leeward Community
College at 94 045 Ala Ike Street in Pearl City on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
MS building is at the western side of the College Cracks were observed on the first and
second floor We observed an uneven floor surface at the joint between the MS building
and the adjacent Biological Science BS building The retaining wall is south of the
Campus Center building Signs of distress were recently observed in the wall and
adjacent structure
A reconnaissance of the project site was performed on May 12 2011 to observe
and evaluate the possible cause s of the distresses Due to the limited nature of the
project study we did not include a field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling
borings Available subsurface data from our archive was used as reference during the
project study A subsurface exploration consisting of borings should be conducted for
future detailed design

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the early Pleistocene
Epoch Dips of the lava beds are near horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees from
horizontal The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering to depths
of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil Deep narrow
canyons such as Waikakalua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are a result of this weathering
The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits
overlying the weathered rock The project site is on the southern side of the Schofield
Plateau and north of the Pearl City Peninsula which is between Middle and East Lochs

Subsurface Conditions
No new exploratory borings were drilled for the MS building and the retaining
wall Based on a review of the general geology older alluvium is likely present below
the site Based on available information provided by and on our
previous exploration for the Education and Innovation Instructional Facility at the
Leeward Community College campus completed in April 2011 the anticipated
subsurface conditions are presented below
Based on available boring logs from the February 1976 construction plans
alluvium deposits of sandy silt and clayey silt with traces of gravel cobbles and
decomposed rocks were encountered underneath the MS building
Based on our previous project clayey alluvium overlying residual soils were
encountered in the Library area northeast of the distressed wall section Surface fills
consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays were encountered in some of the borings to a
depth of about 4 5 feet below the ground surface The alluvium encountered generally
consisted of very stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts The alluvium was generally
underlain by residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard clayey silts extending to the
maximum depth explored of approximately 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on readily available boring information alluvium deposits of sandy silt and
clayey silt with gravel cobbles and boulders may be present underneath the
MS building Based on our previous project very stiff to hard clayey alluvium overlying
very stiff to hard residual soils may be encountered below the Library area east of the
distressed wall section Surface fills consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays were
encountered in some borings to depths of about 4 5 feet below the ground surface This
fill exhibits a slight expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
The MS building distresses consist of a number of concrete slab cracks in the
floor including some cracks around the columns The cracks were more severe on the
second floor and relatively minor on the first floor This condition is consistent with other
nearby buildings The MS building was built on a cut area in about 1976 Up to
approximately 15 feet of the original ground soil was cut
Based on the survey data for the MS building an elevation difference of
zero 0 0 to one half 0 5 inch is present at the column locations and about 1 to
2 inches of deflection occurred at locations other than the column positions This
indicates that the cracks are more of a structural deflection issue than a foundation
settlement issue This conclusion is also supported by the fact that no significant
cracking was observed on the exterior building walls and that the building is built on a
cut area with relatively competent subsurface strata
The retaining wall distresses consist of localized leaning out of about 1½ to
3 inches and about 2 to 3 inches of separations along the joints of adjacent panels In
total four sections of the retaining wall in front of the Campus Center are distressed as
indicated on the Site Plan Plate 2 The retaining wall was built on fill in about 1969 The
original ground elevations before constructing the wall varied from approximately 42 5 to
71 0 feet MSL The finished elevation of the top of the retaining wall was 72 5 feet MSL
and the finished ground elevation in front of the wall was 69 0 feet MSL According to
the construction plans the fill thicknesses in front of the retaining wall in the distressed
