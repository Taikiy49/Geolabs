INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed H 3 North Halawa Highway Unit 1 Phase 1B in Halawa
Valley on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the highway alignment
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based on the
obtained data These recommendations are intended for design input and are not
intended for construction bidding or estimation purposes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the site in order to develop a generalized soil rock data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the site
preparation and grading design of this section of the proposed H 3 highway
The work was performed in general accordance with our proposals to ParEn
Inc dated and May 3 1989 The scope of work consisted of the
following
1 Drilling and sampling eighteen 18 borings to depths of 40 5 to 57 0
feet below the existing ground surface
2 Reconnaissance of the project site and vicinity to make visual
observations of the surface soil conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 3
3 Review of available soils and geologic information pertinent to the
project site and its vicinity
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field
exploration to determine the pertinent engineering properties and
classification of the materials encountered
5 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the purpose
of developing site preparation grading and bridge foundation design
recommendations
6 Preparation of this report presenting the design recommendations
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2
through 7 Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and laboratory testing are
presented in the appendices of this report A separate report will be issued for the
foundation evaluation of the two 2 Bridges Nos 3 and 4 included in this section

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
General
The project site is located on the island of Oahu the third largest of the eight
major islands of the Hawaiian Archipelago Oahu was formed by the volcanic activity
of two large shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau North Halawa Valley is an
erosional feature which has been deeply incised into the Koolau dome
Koolau was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene by the
accumulation of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows extruded from vents along a fissure
line over thirty miles in length which roughly paralleled the trend of what is now the
Koolau Range The main shield building activity ended in the early Pleistocene
approximately 2 5 million years ago
After the end of the main shield building activity the forces of erosion began
to cut deep valleys into the volcanic dome During the middle to late Pleistocene the
Ice Age sea levels fluctuated eustatically in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
ocean basins so sea leveis fell to stands below present sea level When the glaciers
melted and retreated an excess of water became available so sea levels rose to
elevations above present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 6
The processes of erosion and deposition were effected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below present sea level
When sea level was high the base level was raised so that sediments accumulated
at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated
by mass transfer i e masses of soil and or rock become detached from the steep
valley walls and are transported downslope toward the axis of the valley primarily by
gravity as colluvium Once this material reaches the stream in the central portion of
the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported
and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy i e the flows
transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of a stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sand and fines When base levels
change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place long enough chemical processes begin to alter
the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material and
Causing an induration or cementation of the coarse portion of the sediment into a
poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion
continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in the headwaters This
continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope or downstream
covering the older alluvial deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 7
Depending upon the local base level and the rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geological time In addition their
consistency and density is generally less than that of the older partially consolidated
deposits
units
In general our exploration encountered several distinct types of geological
The following are descriptions of the material types
Koolau Basalt
Koolau Basalts consist of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows with an average
flow unit thickness of 10 feet or less The flows occur both in the a a and
pahoehoe forms with transitions between the two forms The lavas are
generally vesicular with jointing resulting from contraction during cooling and
solidification of the molten rock
The Koolau Basalts grade with depth from extremely weathered saprolites to
relatively unweathered The basalts generally have good strength
characteristics and may be considered an excellent bearing formation
Residual Materials
Residual materials are actually a subset of Koolau Basalts since they are soil
materials derived from the in situ chemical weathering of the parent basaltic
rocks These soils range from structureless residual clayey silts or silty clays
to saprolites extremely weathered basalt which are soils that retain the relict
structure of the parent rock e g joints vesicles and or flow structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 8
These soils generally grade through the range with depth and continue to
grade to the underlying basalt The residual materials generally exhibit good
strength characteristics and are good bearing materials for lightly to
moderately loaded structures
Older Sediments
These materials were laid down during the later Pleistocene and are poorly
sorted boulderly alluvium and colluvium In situ weathering and induration
have altered the materials into a highly weathered poorly consolidated
conglomerate Core samples of these older sediments are typified as highly
weathered cobbies and gravel in a matrix of cemented sandy silt
These materials generally have good to excellent strength characteristics and
provide a good bearing layer for foundation loads
Recent Sediments
These materials are differentiated into two units Alluvium and Colluvium The
alluvium is composed of terrestrial sediments deposited by the movement of
water and is mostly silts and clays with gravel cobbles and boulders The
deposits are poorly sorted highly variable in consistency density and are
encountered in and adjacent to active stream channels
Colluvium is detrital rock and soil which has been transported from its original
position by gravity This material is generally bouldery Adobe clays and
typically have poor stability and foundation support characteristics

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that most of the highway alignment along the
valley floor is underlain by recent alluvial deposits consisting of cobbles and boulders
in a matrix of river gravel and silty sand The alluvial deposits appear to be adequate
to support the proposed high embankment fills The sidehill areas are underlain by
stiff colluvial soils over dense basalt rock formation
Site Grading
The proposed site grading involves cuts up to approximately 40 feet and
extensive fills up to 35 feet high
Ground Preparation
The site should be cleared of all ground surface vegetation debris and
existing structures
Any local soft spots and unsuitable soft materials encountered within the
construction area should be removed and replaced with compacted fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 11
Following the clearing and grubbing all areas designated to receive fill should
be scarified to a depth of six 6 inches and recompacted to a minimum of
90 of its maximum dry density per AASHTO Test Method T 180 The
moisture content of the soil should be near the optimum moisture content
Fill Materials
Most of the potential embankment fill material will be generated from the
hillside cuts into the colluvium This material is predominantly clay with
moderate to high swelling potential and a significant percentage of boulders
The in situ material generally has a moisture content in excess of 40 which
is about 10 above the optimum moisture content Therefore although use
of the natural on site material would result in a significant savings on material
costs there are definite limitations in the use of these materials as described
below
1 The excavated soil would need to be dried out to near optimum
moisture content prior to placement in the fill In light of the relatively
high rainfall normally experienced in North Halawa Valley the drying of
the soil would not be feasible during a significant portion of the year
thereby slowing construction of the embankments
2 The boulders would need to be screened and separated from the finer
portions of the fill to exclude them from the embankments This
additional processing of the soil would slow the embankment operations
and increase costs
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 12
3 Due to the expansive potential of the clayey soil the on site material
should not be used within the top three 3 feet of the embankment fill
In light of the above limitations consideration should be given to the use of
imported granular material for the embankment fills
Imported borrow should consist of non expansive granular material generally
less than three inches in size with less than 20 percent passing a No 200
sieve The imported borrow material should be tested and approved by the
geotechnical engineer prior to transportation to the site
A limited amount of excavated basalt rock is anticipated Generally the
excavated basalt rock material may be used for the embankment fills if the
particles larger than 6 inches in size are first screened out
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
After the above site preparation the fill should be moisture conditioned to near
optimum moisture content placed in maximum 10 inch thick loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density established by
the AASHTO Test Method T 180
The upper three feet of fill below pavements should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density
Where the existing ground is steeper than five horizontal to one vertical
5H 1V adequately prepared benching and keying are required to properly
bond the new fill to the existing slope surface The embankment fill operation
GEOLABS HAWAI

should start at the lowest point and continue up in level compacted layers in
accordance with the above fill requirements For benching and keying
construction details refer to Plate 9
The field exploration indicated that the proposed cuts into the hillsides would
be in colluvial deposits over basalt rock formation Cut slope ratios of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V are recommended for permanent cut slopes
in the colluvium
A steeper cut slope ratio of three fourths horizontal to one vertical 3 4 H 1V
may be used for permanent cuts in rock formation
Slope benches are generally recommended for slope heights in excess of
about 30 feet in colluvium Slope benches may be eliminated for cuts in hard
basalt rock however in order to reduce the hazard of rock fall damage we
recommend that the toe of the steep rock slope be set back a minimum of 20
feet from the edge of the pavement
Two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V fill slopes are recommended for fills
constructed from the on site soils or imported borrow
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling past the design fill siope and
then cutting back to the finish slope ratio
Water should be diverted away from the top of slopes and slope planting
utilized as soon as practicable to reduce erosion

Mechanical Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls MSERW
Deep cuts into the hillside up to about 40 feet deep are required at several
locations along the highway The subsurface conditions in these areas generally
consist of a variable thickness of colluvium or residual soil over basalt rock formation
The existing ground surface in these areas is generally steep with slope ratios
of about three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V or steeper Therefore permanent
cut slopes at two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V could not daylight within a
reasonable distance in most of the areas
Reinforced concrete retaining walls were considered but due to the depth of
cuts the size and corresponding costs of the walls were very high
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 15
In light of the construction difficulties and high costs Mechanically Stabilized
Earth Retaining Walls MSERWs were evaluated In order to improve the aesthetics
of the walls a system of terraced walls in lieu of one very high wall was selected
MSERWS are composite soil reinforcing systems which utilize layers of welded
wire mesh or steel strips within the backfill to improve the shear and compressive
strength of the soil The reinforcing strips are placed in horizontal layers as the fill is
placed and are connected to the precast wall facing members
The external stability of the structure is analyzed as a gravity retaining wall for
the following modes of failure 1 bearing capacity 2 base sliding 3 overturning
4 deep seated slips Our analysis indicates that these external stability criteria may
be satisfied by the proposed MSERW structures
The internal stability analysis primarily determines the length spacing and type
of reinforcing members used
Select granular material is required in order to develop the required frictional
resistance between the backfill and reinforcing members The minimum requirements
for the backfill material are as follows
a Not more than 15 by weight finer than a No 200 sieve
b not more than 25 by weight coarser than 3 inches
C a maximum particle size of less than 6 inches and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 A Page 16
d the corrosion potential must also be limited to reduce deterioration of
the metal reinforcing members
The walls should be embedded a minimum of about one tenth the height of the
wall above the concrete level pad or about 4 feet
The remaining internal design details of the wall system will depend on the
particular system used and therefore must be determined by the specialty contractor
selected for the construction
Steep temporary cuts would be required during construction in order to allow
installation of the MSERW It appears that the lower half of the deep wall excavation
would encounter basalt rock or conglomerate which may be cut at a slope ratio of
one fourth horizontal to one vertical on a short term basis The upper half
of the excavation would be into stiff soil Ideally these soils should be sloped at
about one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter for temporary slopes however
due to the steep existing ground surface very large cuts would result and would
require a tremendous amount of backfill In light of the above it appears that steep
temporary slope ratios of one half horizontal to one vertical would be
required for construction of the MSERWs Some sloughing of the soil slopes may
occur depending on the weather conditions and length of time that the cuts are
exposed Additionally temporary shoring of the steep cuts in soil may be required

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based upon
information obtained from the borings Variations of conditions between the borings
may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident
until construction If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate
the recommendations given in this report
The locations of the borings were determined by taping from structures
indicated on the topographic survey Elevations of the borings were approximately
determined by interpolation from elevation points on the topographic survey map
The physical location and elevation of the borings should be considered accurate only
to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the boring points
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a
gradual transition The strata lines shown on profiles or cross sections are based
upon interpolation between borings and may not represent actual subsurface
conditions
