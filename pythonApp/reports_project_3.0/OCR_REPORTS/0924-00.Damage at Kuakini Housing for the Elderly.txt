DESCRIPTION OF BUILDING DAMAGED AREAS
The Kuakini Housing for the Elderly is a 4 story U shaped
concrete masonry building with a central courtyard area Each
leg of the building is approximately 31 by 211 feet in plan
Based on a review of the structural plans for the building
the concrete block bearing walls forthe structure are supported
by wall footings bearing on the near surface soils The footings
were designed with a 2 foot embedment below the finish grade
The bearing walls are generally about 25 feet apart with in
termediate non bearing walls
W O 924 00
P 5
The ground floor has a slab on grade while the upper floors
consist of precast planks supported by the bearing walls
The remainder of the exterior and interior non bearing walls
are constructed of concrete masonry blocks
The damaged area is concentrated between the first three
bearing walls in the northern corner of the building near
Kuakini Street Large cracks were noted in the northern
exterior block wall about midway between column lines 1 and
2 and 2 and 3 at the location of the interior non bearing
wall Generally the cracks are larger at the ground floor
level and decrease in size at the upper floor levels Visual
observations of the cracking pattern on the wall indicate that
the damage may be caused by differential settlement between
the bearing walls and non bearing walls at this corner of the
building
It appears that the bearing walls have settled and dragged
down the upper concrete floor slabs Due to the lighter loads
on the non bearing walls between the bearing walls they have
not settled as much as the bearing walls and subsequent
differential settlement has caused the wall cracking
Photographs of the damage to the exterior block wall are
attached on Plate 3
W O 924 00
P 6
The remainder of the building appears to be stable as major
damage was not noted
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The subject building was constructed about 10 years ago
Discussions with the tenant who lives in the ground fooor
end unit next to Kuakini indicates that most of the ob
served cracking has occurred within about the last four years
It appears that the cracking may be slowing down however
this has not been verified by survey readings
Based on the three borings drilled it appears that the
building site is underlain by alluvial deposits consisting
mostly of clays and silts with some gravel and boulders
The borings disclosed that the upper 5 to 10 feet of the de
posit is soft and wet near the damaged portion of the building
while stiff subsoils were encountered near the undamaged por
tion of the building
It appears that the foundations for the building bear on this
upper alluvial layer
The cracking pattern visible on the northern exterior block
wall indicates that the bearing wall footings along column
lines 1 2 and 3 may have settled several inches while the
W
P 7
non bearing walls between the bearing walls have not
settled as much This total settlement and differential
settlement between the bearing and non bearing walls appears
to be the cause of the damage noted The footing settle
ments appear to be largest at Column Line 1 near Kuakini
Street and decreases at Column Lines 2 and 3
Although some of the observed footing settlements could be
attributed to the anticipated normal consolidation of these
soft near surface soils encountered near the damaged portion
of the building the amount of total settlements observed
and the extended total time for these settlements to occur
indicate that other factors may have also influenced the per
formance of the footings and subsoils
Field observations at the site disclosed that surface rain
and landscape water from the upper planting area adjacent
to Kuakini Street and the planting strip along the northern
portion of the building near the driveway entrance from
Kuakini Street to the parking area drains toward the damaged
portion of the building It is our understanding that a
sprinkler system was previously utilized to water the plants
however the sprinkler System has been out of order for some
time and the plants are now watered manually Large amounts

of water could have been introduced to the subsoils due to
the sprinkler system Additionally high perched groundwater
conditions were observed in Boring No 3 These two sources
of water infiltration could cause saturation and additional
softening of the already moist subgrade soils thereby in
creasing settlements
Broken water lines were not noted during our exploration
however this could be another source of subsurface water
which could cause additional settlement

Due to the presence of underground utility lines close to
the damaged portion of the structure and the limited access
available the scope of our investigation was very limited
Therefore our work has not been comprehensive We feel that
the data obtained during this investigation is not adequate
for the complete development of the definitive answers to all
of the existing structure damage problems
Based on our field investigation and laboratory test results
several alternatives which could reduce the future damage of
the structure were presented to you during a meeting with
the representatives of the Hawaiian Housing Authority on

Alternate 1 Reconstruction of the Damaged Portion of the Structure
The most simple alternative would be to completely reconstruct
the damaged portion of the structure between column lines 1
and 3 However due to the enormous cost involved to recon
struct the damaged portion this alternative is considered
economically unfeasible at this time
Alternate 2 Underpinning
The method of underpinning the continuous shear wall under the
damaged portion of the structure footings could reduce the future
settlement of this structure This would require providing
additional footing upport and transmitting the structure loading
from the near surface soft materials to the competent subsurface
materials below Pre drilled Cast in place cassions could
be used to transmit the structural loads to the clayey silt
decomposed basalt The cassions length is estimated to be
about 15 feet below the existing ground surface
The cassions could be designed to provide additional support
to the footings using an allowable bearing pressure of
The above procedure of underpinning the foundations is considered

be adequate near the damaged area Therefore the method
of underpinning does not appear to be a feasible alternative
However if this alternative of underpinning is selected
more comprehensive study would be required to develop appro
priate design recommendations
Alternate 3 Drainage Improvement Lime Stabilization and
Patching of Cracks
The third alternative presented below is based on the assump
tion that the seepage of the surface water near the damaged
structure may have contributed to the settlement problems
Drainage Improvement Prior to any cosmetic repair of the
damaged structure we recommend that the surface water drainage
should be diverted away from the structure area Sub drains
should be provided adjacent to the damaged portion of the
structure to drain the surface water away from the structure
and carry this water to the street A typical retaining wall
drainage detail is presented on Plate 2 The planting strip
along the outside wall on the northwestern side of the struc
ture should be covered with asphalt concrete pavement after
the lime stabilization operations are completed This pavement
section should shed the surface water away from the structure
W O 924 00
P 11
Lime Stabilization During the excavation for the retaining
wall drainage installation lime solution could be pressure
injected through a series of 3 inch diameter holes to create
a curtain wall of lime holes to minimize future water infil
tration The lime can also react Chemically with the subsoils
to reduce its plasticity and water absorption capacity The
holes should be drilled at least 6 feet deep around the
damaged structure area at about 4 feet intervals This tech
nique of lime stabilization has been used in the past to
strengthen soft and wet clayey subsoils
The effectiveness of this method depends on the chemicals
present in the clayey soils to be stabilized and the structure
of the soils As the lime slurry is pumped into the clayey
soils under pressure it follows the path of least resistance
moving along fractures bedding planes root lines sand
lenses or seams and fractures created by the setting lime
Slurry The lime is deposited in a network of horizontal
sheet like seams which serve as individual moisture barriers
and help stabilize the moisture content of the soil Although
the effectiveness of lime stabilization is generally variable
we feel that this method has a relatively good chance of
helping to stabilize the moisture content and settlement of
the upper soils

Future Settlement Monitoring We recommend that the future
settlement of the distressed area should be monitored before
and after the completion of the above repair work The settle
ment monitoring should consist of establishing a series of
settlement markers and survey readings taken at a periodic
interval of not more than two weeks The settlement readings
should be plotted and analyzed to study the present behavior
of the distressed structure and to predict the future settle
ment of the structure presently under distress
Based on the discussions with you during the meeting it is
our understanding that the third alternate presented above is
considered the most feasible from an economic standpoint
However if future structural problems should continue at
this affected area after the cosmetic repairs are made a
more comprehensive study would be required prior to determining
other alternatives
