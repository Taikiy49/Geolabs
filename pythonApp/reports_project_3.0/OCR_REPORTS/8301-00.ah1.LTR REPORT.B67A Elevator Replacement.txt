PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located near the northeast corner of the existing Building 67A 
at JBPHH on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Building 67A is generally at the northern 
portion of the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard (PHNSY) near the intersection of Port Royal 
Street and Seventh Street. 
We understand the existing hydraulic elevator was constructed over 40 years 
ago and have since found hydraulic leakage prompting its replacement. The 
construction of the elevator will require the removal and replacement of the piston and 
the casing. We anticipate the excavation for replacement of the piston casing will 
require drilling equipment and tools like that of drilled shaft construction.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex 
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level 
changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following 
the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were 
deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and 
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level 
during the late Pleistocene era, the eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, 
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff (locally known as “mudrock”) layers. 
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of volcanic tuff.  
During periods of insignificant sea level changes, continued stream action extended 
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments. 
During stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed distinct ledges of reef 
limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period. About 
15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. 
During the recent epoch, as sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits. These deposits were 
subsequently covered with man-made fills placed during the middle portion of this 
century for land reclamation projects during the construction of the existing Pearl Harbor 
Naval Shipyard.  The project site generally is located at the northern portion of the Pearl 
Harbor Naval Shipyard (PHNSY) about 400 feet from the Southeast Loch. The 
subsurface conditions of the project site generally consist of fill materials overlying 
coralline deposits and alluvium.

Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling one exploratory boring in 
support of the design and construction of the elevator replacement at Building 67A. The 
boring, designated as Boring B-1, was drilled and sampled to a depth of about 53 feet 
below the ground surface to develop a generalized soil data set beneath the project site.

Based on the results obtained from our field exploration, the project site generally is 
underlain by surface fill and coralline detritus materials with intermittent layers of alluvium. 
The fills generally consisted of stiff clayey silt and dense silty sand extending to a depth of 
about 8.5 feet below the ground surface. Beneath the fills, our boring encountered 
coralline detritus materials consisting of loose to medium dense sands and gravel with 
varying silt content and stiff gravelly clay extending to a depth of about 51 feet below the 
ground surface. Based on the geology in the area, we anticipate the coralline detritus 
encountered within the depth explored is weakly cemented as part of coralline reef 
formation, which was disturbed from the drilling and sampling process. 
Alluvium consisting of loose clayey sand and medium stiff silty clay was 
encountered intermittently within and below the coralline detritus extending to the 
maximum depth explored of 53 feet below the existing pavement surface. It should be 
noted that lenses of volcanic tuff rock were also encountered within the coralline detritus at 
about 27 feet below the ground surface.  
We encountered groundwater in the drilled boring at a depth of about 8.3 feet below 
the ground surface. Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, 
groundwater levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations. In addition, groundwater 
levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, storm surge, and 
other factors. 
SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site will be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for the 
potential for soil liquefaction. Based on the project plans, we understand seismic design 
considerations shall be based on the International Building Code, 2018 Edition (IBC 2018), 
which references the American Society of Civil Engineers 7-15, Minimum Design Loads 
and Associated Criteria for Buildings and Other Structures, 2015 Edition (ASCE 7-15). 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water 
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. Soils most 
susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and loose silts 
with little cohesion. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, the 
geology in the area, the cementation anticipated in the coralline materials, and our 
engineering analyses, the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site is non-existent 
due to the presence of mostly medium dense/stiff materials encountered within the depth 
explored. Therefore, the potential for liquefaction is not a design consideration at this 
project site.

Piston Shaft Excavation Considerations 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the weight of the elevator 
and piston will be supported by flanges spanning the piston shaft and bearing onto the 
elevator pit slab. Therefore, the piston shaft PVC casing will not support the structural 
loading from the new elevator. 
As mentioned above, loose to medium dense sands and gravel were encountered 
in the boring drilled. Therefore, loose soil conditions may be encountered within the depth 
of the piston shaft excavation. In order to provide stable access and reduce the risk of 
caving-in in the drilled holes, steel casing may be required for the piston shaft excavation. 
The annulus space between the piston shaft PVC casing and steel casing may be 
backfilled with neat cement grout via a tremie method. 
Based on our field exploration and experience in the area, the project site is 
underlain by the coralline reef formation with intermitter hard coral and/or tuff formations at 
relatively shallow depths. Therefore, coring into hard coral and/or tuff formations should be 
anticipated during the piston shaft construction. Appropriate drilling equipment (and coring 
tools) should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to install the piston shaft to the depth 
and dimension specified in accordance with RFP documents and design drawings. 
It is imperative for a Geolabs representative to be present during construction to 
observe the excavation of the piston shaft. Critical items include, but not limit, to verify the 
soil/rock cutting, record the piston shaft excavation depth and dimension, and document 
the cleanness of the excavation prior to near cement grout placement. Therefore, Geolabs 
observation of the piston shaft excavation is necessary to confirm the assumed subsurface 
conditions and should be designated a “Special Inspection” item. 
Corrosion Potential 
Chemical analyses, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride contents, and 
sulfate contents, were performed on selected soil samples obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. The 
test results are summarized and presented on Plate B-4 of Appendix B. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as fuel lines or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Resistivity 
is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics regarding the 
corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the resistivity, the 
greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. Conversely, the higher 
the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of metallic objects.

Based on the test results presented on Plate B-4 of Appendix B, the subsurface 
soils at the project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of about 1,900 ohm-cm, 
indicating that the subsurface soils are moderately to severely corrosive (Corrosion 
Category A) based on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board 
of Water Supply. Therefore, we recommend properly designing metallic substructures 
(such as piping) for protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the 
soils. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate content tested 
on the in-situ materials, we believe that the subsurface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used 
for the concrete in contact with the ground, provided that the appropriate minimum 
concrete cover over the reinforcing steel is maintained. It may be appropriate to consult 
with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
one exploratory boring, designated as Boring B-1, extending to a depth of about 53 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The boring was drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers and coring tools. The approximate boring 
location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our logger classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Log of Boring, Plates A-1.1 and A-1.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Log of Boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Log of 
Boring at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided 
on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-3. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained from 
our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4.
