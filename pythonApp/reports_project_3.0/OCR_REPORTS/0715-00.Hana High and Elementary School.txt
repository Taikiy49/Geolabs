SITE DESCRIPTION
The Hana High and Elementary School is located in Hana
Maui approximately 1 5 miles east from the Hana Airport
and on the makai northeast side of the Hana Highway
The proposed classroom buildings are about 100 feet north
west of the existing Hana High and Elementary School s
Food Service Center building
The project site is bounded on the northeast and southwest
by an open area covered with grass and brush Hana Highway
is located to the southwest beyond the open area It is
bounded on the northwest by an adjacent pasture land and
on the southeast by the adjacent school yard beyond which
lies the Food Service Center building
The overall area slopes down in the makai or northeast
direction at about 5 percent gradients In localized
areas the lava rock is exposed on the surface particularly
in the area of Borings 7 and 8 The surface drainage is
in the makai direction but no visible signs of surface
drainage patterns or drainage ways were present There
is a slight depression in the middle of the project area
running in a southwest to northeast direction

PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
The proposed construction consists of two single story
classroom buildings A timber and Glulam roof system

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
After the major formation of East Maui by the Honomanu
Volcanic Series and the Kula Volcanic Series a long
quiescent period followed during which canyons and large
valleys were carved in the East Maui volcanic shield
GEOLABS HAWAII
HANA HIGH ELEMENTARY SCHOOL P 4
Volcanic eruptions then resumed creating new lava flows
cinder cones and ash deposits which represented a new
series called the Hana Volcanic Series The cones built
by the Hana Volcanic Series eruptions ranged from small
spatter cones a few feet high to big cinder cones more
than a mile across at the base and 600 feet high The
resulting lavas poured down the major valleys such as
and Waihoi and along the slopes
of the older volcanic shield burying a large portion of
the eroded topography on the southern and eastern ends
of East Maui Although the Hana lavas were generally
more fluid and the lava flows tended to be thinner and
less massive than the preceeding Kula lavas thick accu
mulations of lava did occur in the valleys and valley
heads These eruptions were often separated by intervals
long enough to allow weathering and erosion of the lava
surface before it was buried by the next flow thus creating
weathered layers between successive flows
The lava rock from these Hana eruptions comprises the rock
encountered at the project site beneath a relatively thin
layer of soil This rock probably originated from the Hana
lava flows that came down the older volcanic slope surface
and built its thickness gradually by the hardening of suc
cessive lava flows adding a relatively thin lava layer each
time a flow occurred in contrast to the build up of lava in
the older valley areas by ponding

Soil Conditions
The soil profiles of the project site are generally the same
throughout the investigated area with the exception of the
varying thickness of the soil layer at any one boring location
The soil layer consists of a material grading between a brown
clayey silt and sand and a brown silty sand primarily depen
ding upon the percentage of sand present within each sample
tested Many cobbles and small boulders were present within
the layer of soil which ranged from 1 foot to approximately
6 feet in depth These cobbles and small boulders are better
described as being lava rock fragments ranging in size from
several inches to about 1 foot in diameter that probably
originated from the underlying fractured lava rock formation
GEOLABS HAWAII

Beneath the brown soil layer a fractured partly vesi
cular lava rock formation was encountered beginning at
various depths and through the bottom of each boring
with the shallowest being in Boring 1 at about 1 foot
and the deepest being in Boring 6 at about 5 5 feet
Most of the Yock that was recovered was of a fractured
nature with brown sections indicating that a degree of
weathering has occurred within the fractured zones
A poorly graded localized cobble layer was encountered
in Boring 2 approximately between 2 to 4 feet in depth
This cobble layer was probably a shallow fill or pushed
aside material from previous excavations as suspected
from its loose nature and lack of finer material between
the cobble size aggregates as observed during the investi
gation
Groundwater was not noted in the bore holes at the time of
our field exploration

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our investigation the proposed structures may
be supported on spread and continuous footings bearing
on the gray lava rock layer
Foundation
During the subsurface exploration a competent rock layer
was encountered from 0 to 5 feet below the existing surface
Generally the surface soils were dense silty sand and
stiff clayey silt with rock fragments However near the
rock layer loose cobbles were encountered in Boring 2
and loose silty sand was encountered in Boring 6
To minimize the differential settlement caused by foundation
bearing on different types of soil and rock formations a1
the footings for the proposed classroom building should bear
upon the in situ lava rock layer An allowable bearing pre
ssure up to 5 000 pounds per square foot may be used For
footings bearing on rock settlement will be minimum
Generally the bottoms of the exterior foundations should
be a minimum of 18 inches below the finish adjacent grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
HANA HIGH ELEMENTARY SCHOOL P 8
Slab On Grade
Based on the laboratory test results the on site soil
have low expansion potential therefore no special pro
visions for expansive soils are required for slab on
grade construction
To reduce differential settlements where the slab rests
partially on rock and partially on existing soil or fill
the rock should be over excavated 12 inches below the floor
slab and replaced with compacted granular material
Slab subgrades close to the existing grade should be free
of topsoil The subgrade soil should be kept moist and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum density
established by ASTM D 1557 A 4 inch layer of base course
should be provided above the subgrade and compacted to 95
of its maximum dry density
To protect against moisture damage of the flooring material
a polyethylene moisture barrier should be placed below the
concrete slab
Earthwork and Compaction Requirements
Footing excavation backfills should be placed in 8 inch
thick loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum
dry density within the slab areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
HANA HIGH ELEMENTARY SCHOOL P 9
All areas to receive fill should be undercut to remove
the surface vegetation and topsoil Prior to placement
of any new select backfill and fill the ground surface
should be brought back to near optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 90 of maximum dry density
Additional fill should be non expansive select fill placed
in 8 inch thick loose lifts and compacted to the above speci
fied density
The on site silty and sandy soil can be used as fill material
provided that no rocks in excess of 6 inch in diameter are
incorporated into the fills
Construction Observation
To a degree the performance of any structure is dependent
upon construction procedures and quality Hence observa
tions of site clearing fill placement and inspection of
foundation excavations should be carried out by a soils
engineer to permit correlation between the preliminary
soil data obtained during this investigation and the actual
soil conditions encountered during construction in order
to minimize misunderstanding of the field forces by both
the letter and spirit of our report as well as to note any
subsurface conditions different from those forming the
basis of our recommendations

APPENDIX A
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling nine 9
borings utilizing a portable equipment with rock coring attachments
The depths of all the borings terminated at 3 3 to 15 0 feet below the
existing ground surface The locations of the borings are shown on the
enclosed Site Plan Plate 1
The soils and rock were classified by visual and textural examination
in the field by our geologist who continuously supervised the drilling
operations The classifications webe verified by visual inspection
and testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in accordance
with the Unified Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of
the soils and rock encountered is presented on the Boring Logs Plates
A 1 through A 9
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D ring sampler
or a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling a
distance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last
12 inches are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depth
Rock samples were recovered by diamond coring with NX size core barrel
The percentage of recovery and rock quality designation RQD are shown
on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depth The percentage
of RQD is determinated by considering only pieces of hard and sound core
that are at least 4 inches long
