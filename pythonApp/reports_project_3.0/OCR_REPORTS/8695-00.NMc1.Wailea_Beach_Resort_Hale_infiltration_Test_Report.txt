Dear Mr. Mow: 
In accordance with the scope of services outlined in our revised fee proposal dated 
March 10, 2023, we performed infiltration testing services in support of the Wailea Beach 
Resort New Hale project in Wailea on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. This letter report 
summarizes the work performed and presents our findings and results for the field infiltration 
testing performed at the project site. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located within the existing Wailea Beach Resort employee parking 
lot in the Wailea area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The parking lot is located east of the 
existing Wailea Beach Resort, west of Wailea Alanui Drive, and north of the resort's 
driveway.  Based on the information provided, we understand it is desired to evaluate the 
infiltration characteristics at the proposed future new hale site. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The purpose of our field exploration was to evaluate the infiltration characteristics 
of the underlying subsurface materials at the project site by performing three Low Impact 
Development (LID) tests, designated as L-1, L-2, and L-3, at the approximate locations 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. These tests were performed in general accordance with 
the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other states’ procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard.

Field Exploration 
The borings were drilled using a CME 45 truck-mounted drill rig equipped with a 
4-inch continuous flight solid-stem auger. Our geologist classified the materials encountered 
in the borings by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with 
ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored 
the drilling operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering 
Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate 3.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate 3.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered in the drilled borings are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates 4 through 6. 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling three borings, designated as Boring 
Nos. L-1, L-2, and L-3, each extending to a depth of approximately 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a surface fill consisting of loose to dense silty 
sand, silty gravel, and clayey sand, extending to depths of approximately 1.5 to 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The fill was generally underlain by weathered basalt formation 
consisting of medium dense to very dense silty sands (broken down due to the augering 
drilling method). The weathered basalt extended to the maximum depth explored of 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on rainfall, temperature, 
surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
Field Infiltration Testing 
The field infiltration tests were performed by auguring three borings, designated as 
L-1, L-2, and L-3, to the selected test depth at the infiltration test locations, shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. Upon reaching the test depth and cleaning out the borehole of soil 
cuttings, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow 
infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the boring. The annular space 
between the borehole and the outside of the PVC casing was filled with bentonite to ensure 
a seal between the borehole and the PVC casing. The cased boreholes were then pre-
soaked prior to performing the infiltration test. 
Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the average infiltration 
rates of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials of filling the 
casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a 1-hour trial period. The 
infiltration rates are then calculated based on the results of the fourth and last trial for each

Limitations 
The geotechnical field data submitted in this letter report are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and infiltration tests. Variations of the subsurface 
conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident 
until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the findings provided in this letter report. 
The field boring and infiltration test locations and elevations indicated herein are 
approximate, having been estimated using Google Earth™. The physical locations and 
elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the 
methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. However, it must be noted 
that fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall, temperature, and other factors.
