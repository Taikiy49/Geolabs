Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
two extinct Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of
the island including the project site The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active therefore
Waianae s eastern flank was partially buried below the westerly flowing Koolau lavas
These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad upland plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau
Based on our review of available geological maps the majority of the project site
is underlain by weathered basaltic rock and derived residual and saprolitic soils
belonging to the Tertiary Waianae Basalt Twb geological mapping unit The basaltic
rock materials consist of sequentially layered lava flows which dip gently toward the
south and southeasterly direction Based on our site reconnaissance the near surface
lava flows appear to represent the a a lava flow type A a lava rock is typically
characterized as a dense volcanic rock within the lava flow core bounded by rubbly
clinker lava materials which were extruded as slow moving relatively thick and viscous
lava flows
During the later stage of eruptive activity the chemical composition of the
Waianae lavas shifted towards andesitic and trachytic alkalic basalts During a late
period of rejuvenated Waianae volcanism a series of events along the principal
southern rift zone formed several relatively small composite cinder and lava cones
including Puu Makakilo and Puu Palailai These later eruptions spread some lobes of
fresh alkalic lavas over the southeastern flank of the old shield interrupting the
erosional dissection of the volcano As a result less weathered basaltic rock materials
may be encountered overlying deeper and more strongly weathered basaltic rock
materials It should be noted that lavas from the Puu Makakilo and Puu Palailai cones
are typically very dense with a platy outcrop structure resulting from strong horizontal

In situ chemical weathering of the surficial cinder and lava materials formed a

Farrington Highway The project site encompasses exposed upland terrain subject to

Seismic Refraction Field Exploration
Seismic refraction geophysical surveys are often conducted to supplement and
enhance traditional subsurface geotechnical exploration such as test borings The
refraction surveys may provide quick and affordable general subsurface
characterization of the strata thickness and lateral continuity and an estimation of
the rock rippability for excavation Seismic refraction survey was proposed for this
project due to the relatively remote and difficult access condition for traditional
subsurface exploration such as drilled borings Seismic refraction methods are
among the least intrusive and most cost effective methods of subsurface
exploration in consideration of the remote access and potential for encountering
existing sensitive archaeological features in the field However the seismic
refraction methods do rely on some simplified assumptions that may limit its
application in some settings
In general the seismic refraction geophysical exploration method involves using a
signal enhancement seismograph and seismic data processing software to record
and analyze an induced seismic energy source expressed as compressional
P waves which travel through and refract on layered subsurface geologic
mediums consisting of varying density and hardness Seismic energy travels with a
compressional velocity that is characteristic of the density porosity structure and

water content of each distinguishable geologic layer encountered The primary
constraint of the P wave refraction method assumes the subsurface geologic
layers increase in material density generation of higher P wave velocities with
increasing depth The refraction method is not effective where the materials
P wave velocity decreases with increasing depth a condition known as a velocity
inversion Normally over 80 percent of natural geological settings conform to
increasing material density corresponding with increasing depth of burial In general
velocity values are greater for rock material that is dense less fractured and less
unweathered Highly fractured rock such as clinker generally results in lower
velocity values
However because of the sporadic late eruptive stage lava emissions from volcanic
vents in the project vicinity the potential occurrence of seismic wave velocity
inversion at portions of the project site should be anticipated This is due to the
potential occurrence of near surface hard and dense younger basaltic rock
materials overlying more weathered older and less dense basaltic rock materials
In addition during analysis the wave velocities in the profile may be averaged as
a result of the interbedded nature of the variable basaltic rock materials ranging
from very hard to soft clinker Therefore the subsurface information provided by the
geophysical surveys should be interpreted with caution to account for the wide
variability in the subsurface conditions that may exist

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The existing soils and weathered rock formation in this area are competent and
should be able to support the high fill with proper keying and benching and compaction
effort However special provisions should be given for compaction requirements and
special foundation systems to reduce differential settlements of the future structures
Some boulders will be generated from the earthwork operations A limited
amount of boulders can be incorporated into the fill It should be noted that many of the

Based on our exploration and our understanding of the subsurface conditions in the

along soil rock interfaces during periods of heavy precipitation Therefore we recommend
installing subdrains in the base of the fill to drain at the toe

settlements will affect the construction schedule and the earthwork quantity estimates

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the subdivision contract grading limits
should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious
material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of
properly off site or stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for
contamination of the excavated materials Boulders and cobbles should also be
removed and disposed properly off site or stockpiled in a designated area to reduce
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill material
Prior to filling the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction However the minimum

Ground Settlement
Based on the preliminary plans provided significant fills are anticipated for the
proposed development Ground settlements are anticipated when new fills in excess of
about 20 feet high are placed over the existing ground to raise the site to the finished
elevations These settlements will affect the construction schedule and the earthwork
quantity estimates Based on the results of our exploration the anticipated ground
settlements are primarily the result of the following two processes
Compression of the fill material under its own weight
Consolidation of the in situ soils induced by the new fill loads
The settlement induced from compression of the fill material under its own weight
is a function of the relative percentage of compaction and time period Our analyses
indicated the magnitude of the fill settlements could be reduced by increasing the
compaction for portions of the fills from 90 to 95 percent Additionally it is our opinion
most of the settlements would occur within about 2 months from the completion of the
filling operations Some long term settlements may continue for high fill greater than
40 feet areas therefore special foundation systems are recommended for these areas

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings extending to depths of approximately 40 to 52 feet below the existing ground
surface The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using both truck mounted and caterpillar track drill rigs
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers and HQ rock coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core

REPORT FOR SEISMIC REFRACTION FOR MAKAIWA HILLS
DEVELOPMENT PROJECT MAKAKILO OAHU HI
This letter report presents the results of the geophysical surveys performed by Global Geophysics
The seismic refraction survey was carried out six locations in December 2009 and February
2010 for the residential development project in Makakilo Oahu Hawaii The objective of the
investigation is to determine the depth to bedrock and rock rippability
GEOPHYSICAL METHODS AND FIELD PROCEDURES
Seismic refraction was used to determine the depth to bedrock and rock rippability The following
paragraphs describe the methods and field procedures
Seismic refraction
Seismic refraction is the traditional method for determining the rock velocity for rock rippability
using a controlled energy source hammer blank shotgun shells or chemical explosives to
generate a seismic signal The seismic signals are received by a series of geophones 24 for
example that are connected to a seismic cable laid on the ground surface in a linear manner The
geophones evenly spaced along the geophone cable are placed on the ground surface The
seismic energy source is discharged at several places along the array and off both ends
The seismic wavelets travel through the earth to the geophones that convert the acoustic energy in
the ground to an electric signal in the geophone cable The seismograph detects the arriving
electric signals with respect to time and stores the records for future data processing The seismic
data is processed to determine the seismic velocity of the earth material through which the energy
has traveled and to model the subsurface geology This geophysical model depicts the earth in
cross section showing the velocity and thickness of the subsurface layers below the seismic line
The seismic refraction survey was conducted using a Geometrics Geode 24 channel digital
seismograph The sensors were Mark Products vertical geophones and the seismic energy
sources were a 20 lbs sledge hammer The weight drop method appeared to provide sufficient
energy and it was subsequently used for the survey The typical field procedure consisted of
laying out the cables and planting the geophones at 10 ft intervals The sledge hammer was

CLOSURE
Global Geophysics services are conducted in a manner consistent with the level of care and skill
ordinarily exercised by other members of the geophysical community currently practicing under
similar conditions subject to the time limits and financial and physical constraints applicable to
the services However seismic refraction is a remote sensing geophysical method that may not
detect all subsurface conditions such as velocity and thickness variations Furthermore the faster
velocity layers i e lava flow layers result in velocity inversion and hidden layers below which
are beyond the limitations of seismic refraction In general the errors in the interpreted depths
and velocities dependent on the resolution of the technique are estimated to be approximately
15 of the true depths and velocities
