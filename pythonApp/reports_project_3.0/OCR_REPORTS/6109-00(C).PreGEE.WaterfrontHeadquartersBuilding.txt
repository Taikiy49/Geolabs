REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Marianas Island
Archipelago which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming the
Marianas Trench The Marianas Trench is part of a belt of trenches which extend from
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench Marianas Trench Yap Trench
and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces
the southern mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The formation
of the island started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic rocks
and conglomerates tuffs and limestones of Eocene and Oligocene in age Lava flows and
limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority of the southern one third
of the island The plateaus of the northern portion of the island were formed while this part
of the island was still beneath the sea level During the Oligocene and Miocene Epochs a
complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an elongated platform of limestones and
related deposits
Weathering and solution working of the limestone has partially re crystallized the
calcium carbonate of the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and poorly
to well cemented detrital limestones in others Typically the detrital limestones are highly
variable in strength characteristics and are frequently karstic or cavernous in nature
In addition the limestone formations are generally porous Except during heavy storms
the majority of the precipitation naturally percolates through the formation producing little
or no surface water runoff Within the last century development of the military facility has
shaped the existing land by grading and filling

ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on the available soil boring data mentioned in the afore mentioned reports
we envision that the project site may be underlain by a surface layer of fill material on the
order of about 1 5 to 3 meters thick consisting of medium dense to dense gravelly sand
and sandy gravel The surface fill crust will likely be underlain by very loose clayey gravel
deposits with silts and sands extending to depths of approximately 8 to 11 meters below
the existing ground surface We anticipate the compressible deposits will be underlain by
sandy limestone gravels with varying densities ranging from medium dense to very dense
extending to depths of over 30 meters below the existing ground surface
Groundwater is anticipated at depths of approximately 3 meters below the existing
ground surface These depths generally correspond to elevations of approximately
0 to 1 meter Mean Sea Level MSL Due to the proximity of the project site to Apra
Harbor and the Pacific Ocean groundwater levels are expected to vary with tidal
fluctuations It should be noted that groundwater levels also may vary with seasonal
precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors
SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is in the relatively high seismic area of the Marianas Islands
Therefore the project site would be subjected to high seismic activity and seismic design
considerations will need to be addressed for the project The following sections provide
discussions on the seismicity of Guam the soil profile for seismic design at the site and
the potential for liquefaction at the site
Earthquakes and Seismic Hazards
Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms the Marianas Trench
The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately 20 earthquakes
exceeding Magnitude 7 0 since 1902 Of these events fifteen occurred prior to 1950 and
five have occurred subsequent to that date Several large faults and fault zones that
traverse across the Island of Guam include the following Pago Bay Adelup Point Fault
River Valley Fault Zone Tamuning Barrigada Fault Pugua Fault
and Mount Santa Rosa horst block
The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration
when evaluating geologic and seismic hazards Of the major faults and fault zones
mentioned above the trace of the Pugua Fault lies closest to the project site at
approximately 9 kilometers km to the west of the project site The Pugua Fault stretches
from off shore near Uruno Point and extends south to about Taguac Several minor faults
about south of the project site are also present
Based on our evaluation of the project site we do not believe that significant
geologic and seismic hazards would impact the proposed Waterfront Headquarters

Liquefaction Potential
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore water
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the
build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is

reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the
overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to
behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit
both horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant
deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
The available soil boring data suggested that the project site may be underlain by
loose saturated sandy materials up to approximately 8 meters thick below the surface fill
crust Based on our evaluation liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable
settlements to the ground surface on the order of about 180 millimeters Therefore the
effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into account in the design of the project
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our review of the available soil boring data we envision that the project
site may be underlain by thick compressible deposits consisting of very loose clayey gravel
materials extending to depths of about 4 5 to 8 meters below the surface fill crust
Because of the moderate to high structural loads anticipated for this project a shallow
foundation system used to support the proposed building would be subjected to
appreciable total and differential settlements As a result we recommend using a
foundation system consisting of friction and or end bearing piles to support the proposed
waterfront headquarters building
As previously mentioned we anticipate that liquefaction of the loose sandy soils
would occur when subjected to earthquake induced vibrations Ground settlements on the
order of about 180 millimeters may occur resulting in appreciable cracking of the on grade
building slab As a result we recommend that the floor slab for the proposed building be
structurally supported on pile foundations to reduce the potential for differential settlements
between a floor slab supported on grade and the columns supported on piles

Pile Foundations
Because of the highly compressible deposits anticipated at shallow depths we
recommend that pile foundations deriving support from skin friction and or end bearing in
the sandy limestone gravel deposits be used to support the proposed structure Based on
the available soil boring data and laboratory test results piles driven through the upper fill
crust and compressible deposits to a depth of approximately 21 meters below the surface
will be required to develop the recommended capacity to support the proposed structure
Vertical Pile Capacity
We recommend using 420 mm octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles with
an allowable compressive load capacity of per pile to support
the proposed building structure The allowable compressible load capacity will
need to be reduced by an estimated downdrag load of 133 KN per pile to support
building loads This is further discussed in the following subsection The piles will
derive load supporting capacity from frictional resistance and or end bearing in the
sandy limestone gravel deposits Due to the potentially compressible nature of the
very loose clayey sand gravel deposits the frictional supporting capacity of the piles
within these loose layers have been discounted To develop the recommended pile
capacity of 623 kNs a pile embedment of approximately 21 meters below the
existing surface will be required A minimum pile spacing of 1 2 meters center to
center is recommended to avoid reduction in pile capacity due to group action
An allowable uplift capacity of 133 kN per pile may be used in design The
recommended pile capacities are for sustained loading conditions For transient
loads such as wind or seismic forces the compressive and uplift load capacities
may be increased by one third
The above pile length is developed for preliminary pile foundation cost estimation
purposes based on available soil boring data only The preliminary pile length
should be modified and or confirmed after completion of a detailed geotechnical
engineering exploration program
Downdrag
Where regional artesian head is lowered or fill is placed over a compressible soil
the latter consolidates and moves downward relative to the pile The downward
movement results in negative skin friction forces generally known as downdrag

forces on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the
thickness of the compressible layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the
pile perimeter area
With a structurally supported floor slab the amount of potential ground settlements
would be induced only by the 0 3 to 0 6 meter of new fills needed to raise the
existing ground surface to the bottom elevation of the structural slab We estimate
these potential fill ground settlements would be on the order of 6 to 19 millimeters
As a result we do not believe that significant downdrag forces would be imposed
on the piles as a result of this small settlement
However as previously discussed in the Seismic Design Considerations section
of this report liquefaction of the loose clayey sand gravel deposits would occur in
the event of an earthquake The earthquake induced liquefaction would result in
significant settlements and would impose downdrag loads on the pile foundations
driven through these deposits We estimate that downdrag loads on the order of
133 KN per pile may be anticipated during a moderate to large earthquake
The allowable pile capacity of 623 kN will need to be reduced by the estimated
downdrag load of 133 kN resulting in a net allowable pile capacity of 490 KN for
support of building loads However the pile should be designed to support a
structural load of 623 KN
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
resistance of the pile soil system to deflect and passive resistance on the front side
of pile caps and grade beams Due to the possible future settlement of the ground
surface sliding friction between the bottom of the slab and the supporting subgrade
should be neglected due to uncertainty regarding long term soil contact
The lateral load resisting capacity of the 420 mm octagonal pile is estimated to be
on the order of 35 KN per pile This assumes a lateral deflection of 13 mm at the
top of the pile for a free head condition For passive soil resistance an equivalent
fluid pressure of 47 kN per square meter per meter of depth may be used
It should be noted that passive soil resistance would not be available for the
liquefiable soils during seismic events This is because the surface fill crust is
assumed to be relatively thin 1 5 to 3 meters and may be subject to sand boils
during liquefaction Therefore we recommend ignoring the passive soil
resistance in the upper liquefiable soils during seismic events from a
conservative design standpoint

Predrilling
Based on the available soil boring information and our experience in the area it is
our opinion that the piles should be able to penetrate through the surface fill crust
without the need for predrilling However this upper fill crust will contribute
downdrag load on the pile foundation system during seismic excitations Therefore
predrilling through the upper fill crust is recommended to reduce the contribution of
downdrag loads from this upper fill layer and unit prices for predrilling should be
obtained during the bidding process
Floor Slabs
Because of the presence of the loose and soft compressible soils at the site we
anticipate that future settlement of the ground surface would occur due to the liquefaction
of the loose sandy soils during moderate to strong earthquakes As a result to reduce the
potential differential settlements between the structure supported on piles and the floor
slab supported on grade we recommend that the floor slabs be structurally supported on
pile foundations Floor slabs should be designed to span between pile foundations
For interior building slabs not subjected to machinery vibration we recommend
placing a minimum 100 mm thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill would
provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a capillary moisture break To
reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent
damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier should be provided on top of
the No 3B Fine gravel cushion fill layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet
vinyl should be considered because they can better mask minor slab cracking Interior
walls should be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount
of possible ground movements
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend providing a minimum 100 mm thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel below
the slabs Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
