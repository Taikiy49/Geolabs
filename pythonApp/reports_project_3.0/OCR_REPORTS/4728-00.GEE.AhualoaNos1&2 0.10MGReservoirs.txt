2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an area
of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
materials These lava flows are believed to be late Pleistocene in age and belong to the
Hamakua Volcanic Series Cavities are commonly encountered in these lava flows
Cavities are formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which
may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter
flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
2 2 Earthquakes and Seismicity
Based on our review of available geologic information the project site is not located
in the immediate proximity of mapped geologic fault structures It appears that there are
no known faults within about 5 miles of the project site Due to the proximity of the project
site to active volcanoes and mapped fault zones the project site is considered to be
located in a seismic zone of potential recurring activity Earthquake activity associated
with fault movement and underground magma movement should be anticipated
However the potential for fault rupture at the ground surface of the project site is unlikely
It should be noted that the Pahala area about 50 miles to the south has
experienced several notable earthquakes in recorded history Pahala is located where the
Kaoiki and Hilea Fault Zones of the southeastern flank of the Mauna Loa shield intersect

3 1 Reservoir Tank Foundations
Based on the existing ground surface elevations and the proposed finished floor
elevations the reservoir tank structures will straddle a cut and fill transition To reduce the
potential for significant differential settlements due to differing subgrade materials and to
provide a firm base for support we recommend that the reservoir tank structure subgrades
be over excavated by a minimum of 36 inches below the bottom of the tank floor The
over excavation should also extend a minimum of 24 inches beyond the perimeter of the
reservoir tank structures
The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials
consisting of well graded granular materials with particles less than 6 inches in maximum
dimension The structural fill material should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of the soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Prior to placement of the structural fill
material the exposed subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory
drum roller a minimum of six passes to provide a firm and unyielding subgrade due to the
high moisture soils at the site
Based on the over excavation of the volcanic ash and replacement with
well compacted structural fill materials we believe that the reservoir tank structures may
be supported on a shallow foundation system supported on 36 inches of compacted
structural fill materials An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for design of foundations bearing on the compacted structural fill

We understand that retaining walls are planned at the two reservoir sites to retain
the soil and or rock in a cut condition The following general guidelines may be used for
design of conventional retaining structures planned at the site
Retaining Wall Foundations
In general we believe that the retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Reservoir Tank
Foundations section of this report In lieu of providing the minimum 36 inch thick
structural fill layer as recommended below the reservoir tank structures we
recommend that the soils encountered at the bottom of the wall foundation be
over excavated by a minimum of 12 inches below the bottom of footing elevation

compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a
minimum 6 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of
the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of
the slope should be constructed in stepped footings

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Because the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii
Seismic Zone 4 in accordance with the 1997 Uniform Building Code forces due to
dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining
walls The force caused by dynamic lateral earth pressures may be
estimated using 16H pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions
where H is the height of the wall in feet
For a maximum 3H 1V sloping backfill the force due to dynamic lateral earth
pressures may be estimated using 28H pounds per lineal foot of wall It should be
noted that the forces caused by dynamic lateral earth pressures provided assume
that the wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in the
event of an earthquake If this amount of lateral earth movement is not acceptable
the retaining wall should be designed with higher forces due to dynamic lateral
earth pressures for a semi restrained wall condition For a semi restrained wall
condition the force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be estimated using
28H pounds per lineal foot of wall for level backfill conditions
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the
mid height of the wall The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures An appropriately reduced
factor of safety may be used when forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures
are accounted for in the design of the retaining walls

and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course for the
pavements should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of
cobbles and or rock fragments Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should

be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number
of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a
large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons traveling at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at
least eight times for structural fill
Excavation
Based on our site reconnaissance the project sites consist of soft to medium stiff
clayey silts underlain by soft to medium hard highly to extremely weathered basalt
formation and very dense basaltic cobbles and boulders in a soil matrix It is
anticipated that the highly to extremely weathered basalt formation and the basaltic
cobbles and boulders may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as backhoes and ripping with large bulldozers However some excavations
may encounter localized hard basalt rock and may require the use of hoerams
chipping or blasting to remove the hard materials in localized areas The above
discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on our
visual observations of the existing basalt formation at the site We recommend that
the contractors be encouraged to examine the site conditions to make their own
interpretation
Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration we
believe that the planned cut slopes will expose the soft to medium stiff clayey silts
the extremely to highly weathered basalt formations and the colluvial soil deposits
at the site In general cut slopes for the project planned at the site may be
designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter where the vertical height of the
slopes are 10 feet or less Slopes greater than 10 feet in vertical height should be
designed with a slope inclination of 3H 1V or flatter

benched into firm and stable materials to provide stability of the new embankment

3 4 Water Line Trenches
It is anticipated that most of the trenches for the new water lines will be excavated
in the compacted fills clayey silts and the highly weathered basalt formation observed
and encountered at the project site Therefore we envision that the majority of the trench
excavations may be made with normal heavy excavation equipment such as backhoes
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the pipes for
uniform support Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and
improper placement of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to
the pipes
As an alternative the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the top of the pipes may consist of 1 inch minus well graded granular
materials aggregate base course Where the 1 inch minus well graded granular
materials are used the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be
moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well graded granular materials
or the excavated on site materials with a maximum particle size of 6 inches The backfill

should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about 8 inch
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In areas subjected to vehicular traffic the top 3 feet below the pavement grades should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
It should be noted that the on site soils have high in situ moisture contents some of

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project sites were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring 201 and 202 extending to
depths of about 21 3 to 26 6 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD value would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
