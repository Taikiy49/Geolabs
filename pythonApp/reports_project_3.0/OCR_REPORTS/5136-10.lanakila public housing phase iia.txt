2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield
Volcano which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows
and pyroclastic materials The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist
of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the
ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava These cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and they
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving
a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along
the flow Blisters and pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the primary water transmission features

2 3 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that only sensitive instruments can detect the small tremors However
some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to
moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes located
on the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as
embankment fill materials We wish to point out that caution should be exercised
by the contractor while clearing and grading near the collapsed lava tube features
and or cavities which may represent potential lava tubes or cavities that may
further collapse under the load of heavy equipment
We recommend that the areas to be filled and finished subgrades in cut areas be
proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight
passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids The
vibratory drum roller should also be operated at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet
per minute
In general we understand that one to two story single family dwellings are planned
at the residential parcels It should be noted that excavation for building footings
and utility line installation within the building footprint may encounter difficulty in the
cut areas where the subgrade materials consist of hard basalt formation
Therefore in order to reduce the potential for significant rock excavation conditions
during the building construction we believe that consideration should be given to
over excavating the cut subgrades by a depth of about 2 feet and backfilling with
compacted structural fill within the building footprint areas We believe that it may
be more cost effective if excavation of the hard basalt formation were conducted
during the site grading phase of the project in lieu of the building construction phase
of the project

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend that the backfill and structural fill materials consist of
well graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension
with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In
general the excavated materials a a lava and pahoehoe basalt may be re used
as a source of structural fill materials provided that the materials are processed to
meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements presented above
The maximum particle size of the fills and backfills should be limited to 3 inches for
fills and backfills to be placed in confined locations Confined locations are defined
as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less than 2 tons in
static weight
Imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
greater than 6 inches or 3 inches depending on the intended use in largest
dimension Imported materials should be observed and or tested by Geolabs prior
to being transported to the site for the intended use

optimum moisture and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Fill
materials consisting of 3 inch minus or smaller materials should be placed in level
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on available subsurface information it appears that most of the cut slopes
would expose the loose a a and porous pahoehoe basalt formation Therefore cut
slopes exposing the loose a a and porous pahoehoe basalt formation should be
designed based on a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter Large cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face should be
backfilled and or grouted if necessary A representative from Geolabs should
observe and map the cut slopes during construction to evaluate whether the
exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our assumptions
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the structural fill materials recommended
herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter In addition fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be benched The fill slope face should
be finished to a relatively smooth and well compacted surface The fill slope face
should be free of voids which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur
In addition slope planting or other means of slope protection should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished
slopes
3 2 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we
recommend that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support the new
structures planned for the Lanakila Public Housing project As an alternative the

dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces

Based on the lightly loaded structures one and two story buildings planned for
the project we believe that foundation probing and grouting normally recommended for
larger and heavier structures may be omitted for this project The intent of the probing
and grouting operations at the foundation locations is to provide the owners with a
higher degree of assurance that cavities and or voids commonly encountered in the
basalt formations likely are not encountered directly below the foundations
In general we believe that the subgrade preparation recommendations
proof rolling the subgrades with a vibratory drum roller outlined in the Site Grading
section of this report would assist in detecting cavities and or voids in the shallow
subsurface The intent of the proof rolling operations is to reduce the potential for
encountering large cavities and or voids in the shallow subsurface within the footprint of
the buildings However if the owners of the structures desire a higher level of
assurance that the likelihood of encountering cavities and or voids below each individual
footing is further reduced consideration may be given to implementing a program of
probing and grouting at each foundation location Geolabs may be contacted to provide
supplemental recommendations for probing and grouting the foundation locations if
desired
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent exterior grade For footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on
slopes the footings should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance
measured from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the face of the slope is
not less than 6 feet
Foundations located next to other foundations utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or the footings should be extended to a depth as deep
as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used throughout the proposed
residential subdivision project For the interior building slabs which will not be
subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of
cushion fill be provided below the slabs for uniform support The cushion fill should
consist of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation and also would serve as
a capillary moisture break
To reduce the potential for appreciable moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of
the gravel cushion layer A 2 inch thick moist fine sand layer may be considered above
the moisture barrier to aid in the curing of the concrete Provision of the 2 inches of
moist fine sand above the moisture barrier is optional and will be left up to the discretion
of the project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings should be considered above
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking
We envision that minor fills on the order of less than 1 foot may be required
below the cushion fill material below the building slabs and the finished pad elevations
In general the fill layer should consist of select granular materials such as base course
or subbase course 3 inch minus compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557
In addition we envision that exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork
likely will be required for the proposed project In general we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation be provided below the exterior concrete slabs As an alternative the exterior
concrete slab may be constructed directly on the compacted structural fill subgrade
provided that the subgrade is properly compacted To reduce the potential for the

3 4 Garage Slabs and Concrete Driveways
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the garage slabs and
driveways For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the
garage slabs and the driveways we recommend that a 6 inch layer of subbase course
3 inch minus granular materials be provided below the slabs in lieu of the cushion fill
layer for uniform support The moisture barrier and the 2 inch moist fine sand layer also
may be omitted for the slabs subjected to vehicular traffic The subbase course should
consist of well graded crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
In general the slab subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction to provide a firm and unyielding base The
6 inch thick subbase course where required should be placed as soon as possible
after preparation of the slab subgrade
In general concrete slabs should be designed for the intended loads For design
of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per
inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted subbase course In addition
provisions should be made for proper load transfer across slab joints that will be
subjected to vehicular traffic
