SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered a 3.5 to 7 feet thick 
surface fill layer consisting of 4 to 7 inches of asphaltic concrete, recycled asphalt 
material, stiff clayey silt, and medium dense sandy gravel with some cobbles. Below the 
fill layer, a colluvial deposit consisting of stiff clayey silt and medium dense silty gravel 
with cobbles was encountered to about 9.5 to 12 feet depth. The colluvium was underlain 
by basalt formation and clinker extending to the maximum depth explored of 25.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The basalt formation was medium hard to very hard.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels may vary with tides, 
rainfall, seepage, time of year, surface runoff, and other factors. 
The use of a soil nailed slope remedial measure is recommended to mitigate 
further failure and erosion of the existing makai slope. The soil nailed slope system 
consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the 
slope. A shotcrete facing is applied to the face of the reinforced slope. The shotcrete 
should have a minimum compressive strength at 28 days of 5,000 psi. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a 5.5-inch diameter hole with an 
inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The soil nail bars should consist 
of ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter of 1.375 inches. Nails 
should be 15 and 25 feet in length and be spaced 5 feet both vertically and horizontally. 
The 25-foot soil nail length is recommended from Sta. 0+30 to 1+40 and the 15-foot soil 
nail length is recommended in the other locations. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Emergency Makai Slope Repairs at Alelele (Hana Highway MP 38.9), 
Job No. 24-40 project on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the soil nail slope 
design for the project. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located along Hana Highway at Milepost 38.9 on the 
southeastern portion of the Island of Maui, Hawaii. Subsequent to two storm events on 
January 16 and 23, 2024, a portion of the existing makai slope along the narrow roadway 
failed and slid down the slope towards the ocean. The existing concrete barriers at the 
top of the makai slope along portions of the makai side of the roadway also slid down the 
slope. Due to the precarious conditions for motorists, we understand that the immediate 
repair of the failed makai slope area and surrounding slope areas is desired. The slope 
remedial area extends for a distance of about 190 linear feet.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located along Hana Highway at Milepost 38.9 on the 
southeastern portion of the Island of Maui, Hawaii. In this area, Hana Highway is about 
8 feet in width with a steep mountain side on the north and the ocean on the south. 
The makai slope ranged in height from about 25 to 30 feet. At the slope failure 
location, the concrete traffic barriers at the top of the slope slid onto the slope face. The 
makai slope face on the western portion of the site was covered with grouted riprap. An 
overhead line was observed along the top of the makai slope.  
An 18-inch concrete reinforced drain pipe was observed near the top of the slope 
in the failed slope area. 
Based on topographic survey information, the existing roadway elevation ranged 
from about +33 to +35 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The bottom of the makai slope ranged 
from about +4 to +10 feet MSL. 
2.2 
Subsurface Conditions  
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, to depths of about 15.5 to 25.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our borings generally encountered a 3.5 to 7 feet thick surface fill layer consisting 
of 4 to 7 inches of asphaltic concrete, recycled asphalt material, stiff clayey silt, and 
medium dense sandy gravel with some cobbles. Below the fill layer, a colluvial deposit 
consisting of stiff clayey silt and medium dense silty gravel with cobbles was encountered 
to about 9.5 to 12 feet in depth. The colluvium was underlain by basalt formation and 
clinker extending to the maximum depth explored of 25.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  The basalt formation was medium hard to very hard.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
To mitigate further erosion to the existing slope and distress to the existing 
roadway, we recommend stabilizing and protecting the existing makai slope by using a 
soil nailed slope system. Detailed discussions and recommendations for these items and 
other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Soil Nailed Slope 
We recommend using a soil nail slope system to mitigate further slope distress and 
erosion. Design of the soil nailed slope system considers both the internal and external 
stability of the reinforced mass. The design of the internal stability includes establishing 
the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted reinforcing bars. The external 
stability includes slope stability of the reinforced mass. 
Items pertaining to the soil nailed slope system are addressed in the subsequent 
subsections and include the following:

Construction of a permanent soil nailed slope system should be performed by a

Due to the specialized nature of the construction, a Geolabs representative should be

of the soil nails. 
3.1.1 Slope Preparation 
At the on-set of construction, surface vegetation, loose material, and debris should 
be removed from the faces of the scoured slopes. Care should be taken when 
removing material so that the stability of the existing slope is not compromised.

3.1.2 Soil Nailed Slope 
A soil nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted 
into drilled holes to stabilize the eroded slope. A shotcrete facing is generally 
applied to the face of the reinforced slope. The reinforcing bars provide additional 
tensile and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the slope. The 
reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but 
lacks a footing. The soil nailed slope system will have slope heights up to about 
30 feet. A typical soil nailed slope system is presented on Plate 3. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole with 
an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The soil nail bar should 
consist of ASTM A615 Grade 75 solid threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter 
of 1.375 inches. We anticipate that the subsoil may be very corrosive. Therefore, 
we recommend using a triple corrosion protection system for the nails. The epoxy 
coated bars should be surrounded by neat cement grout or sand-cement mixture 
with a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 pounds per square inch 
(psi). In addition, corrugated plastic sheathing should be used to provide an 
additional layer of corrosion protection. Centralizers should be used to center the 
soil nail within the borehole.  
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using design nail lengths of 15 and 
25 feet extending into the stiff and medium dense colluvium and medium hard to 
very hard basalt formation encountered in our field exploration. The 25-foot long 
soil nails are recommended from Sta. 0+30 to 1+40 and the 15-foot long soil nails 
are recommended in the other locations. The soil nails should be spaced at 5 feet 
on-center horizontally and vertically in a grid pattern. The first row of nails should 
be installed about 2 to 3 feet below the top of the slope. 
Based on the above design parameters, the soil nailed slope system should have a 
factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external stability for 
the static loading condition considering a surcharge load of 250 pounds per square 
foot (psf) applied on the roadway travel lanes.

3.1.3 Soil Nail Installation 
Construction of the permanent soil nails should be performed by a specialty 
contractor who is experienced in the construction of soil nails. Due to the 
specialized nature of the soil nail construction, a Geolabs representative should be 
present to observe the geotechnical aspects of the soil nail construction. 
Potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation of 
the soil nails due to the presence of cobbles and boulders within the colluvium and 
clinker materials anticipated at the project site. Utilizing a temporary casing may 
be required to maintain an open hole for the soil nail installation. In addition, due 
to potential variations of basalt rock within the subsurface, hard drilling conditions 
should be anticipated. The contractor should fully investigate the project site and 
make his/her own interpretation as to the character of the materials to be 
encountered. It is the contractor’s responsibility to bid the project accordingly with 
sufficient contingency taking into account potential caving-in, hard drilling, and 
difficult access conditions. 
3.1.4 Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nailed retaining 
slope, we recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification 
test on a sacrificial soil nail at the project site. In addition, proof tests should be 
performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to 
confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent 
of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment should be held 
for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes. The 
proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the 
design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). The 
proof test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nail slope, provided 
they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are 
integral components of the design for the soil nail slope system. Therefore, we

3.1.5 Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of 
shotcrete be constructed for inspection. Top and bottom keys should be provided 
for the shotcrete facing. 
The shotcrete should have a minimum compressive strength at 28 days of 
5,000 psi. In addition, the shotcrete should contain either 7.5 pounds of 
Strux 85/50 synthetic structural fiber per cubic yard, Forta Mac Matrix or equal. 
3.1.6 Drainage 
The soil nailed slope should be well-drained to reduce the potential for the build-up 
of hydrostatic pressures. Drainage elements consisting of 2-foot wide strips of a 
prefabricated drainage composite product may be used on the face of the slope 
before the application of a shotcrete flash coat used for temporary stabilization. 
The prefabricated drainage composite product should extend from the top of the 
slope to the base of the soil nailed slope and hydraulically connected to a weephole 
near the base of the soil nailed slope.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions between 
and beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations 
may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident,

of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods 
used.  
The stratification breaks represented on the Logs of Borings show the approximate 
boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition. The 
data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. We did 
not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it 
must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in tides, seasonal rainfall and 
other factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, to depths of about 15.5 to 25.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring locations

Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."
