INTRODUCTION
The geotechnical engineering exploration was performed for the proposed general
purpose berthing wharf Y 3B in the Naval Submarine Base of Pearl Harbor on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of the exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the exploration These recommendations are primarily intended for design input

The project site is located at the end of Kuahua Island at the entrance to Magazine
Loch in the Naval Submarine Base in Pearl Harbor on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
existing Quaywaill Y 3 is located south of Building 1636 along Laffey Street and extends
approximately 1000 feet along the coast line of Southeast Loch between the existing
Quaywall Y 2 and Pier K 1
Based on as built drawings the existing quaywall was constructed with 60 foot long
M Z32 sheetpiles in 1942

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Following the cessation of
activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial material were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine
environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising
about 95 feet above the present sea level
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during late Pleistocene era eruption of the Salt
Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with
thick deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called
mudrock layers Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of tuff
During the recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits Those deposits were subsequently
covered by man made fill placed during the middle portion of this century for land
reclamation projects during the construction of the existing Naval Submarine Base

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on the project design criteria various framing schemes had been proposed
and evaluated for the new wharf construction Five 5 framing schemes namely B C
D E and F were considered
These framing schemes ranged from driving piles to support a single 60 foot wide
structural deck for the entire new wharf to installing a new sheetpile wall in front of the
corroded quaywall to eliminate the need for a pile supported deck behind the quaywall
Other variations to these alternatives were also considered
More detailed discussion and geotechnical design recommendations for these
framing schemes are presented in the following sections
Framing Schemes
The general design concepts behind Schemes B C D E and F are portrayed on
the sketches on Plates 5 through 9 Each of these schemes is be discussed in more
detail below

and the sunken areas behind the wall will be backfilled to enable the use of a
will be a structural deck supported on pile foundations

Pile Foundations
Due to the heavy wharf loadings it is apparent that high capacity piles are
preferred for use in order to reduce construction cost Based on our foundation
analyses two 2 sizes of precast and prestressed concrete piles inch octagonal
piles and 20 inch square piles may be used to support the structural wharf deck
In general we recommend that the octagonal piles be considered for use on land
since the smaller tip and shaft areas of the piles may facilitate pile driving into thicker stiff
subsoil deposits behind the existing or the new anchored bulkhead The larger square
piles may be used off shore since the piles have thicker cover to steel reinforcement
which may provide additional corrosion resistance to the piles The boring did not
encounter a hard formation capable of supporting high capacity end bearing piles
However the stiff alluvial soils can provide significant frictional resistance

Pile Settlement
Since large pile groups are not anticipated to support the proposed wharf we
believe that the settlement of a single pile may be applicable for the project
foundation design
In general pile settlements consist of two basic components pile settlement due
to loading transfer to the surrounding and underlying subsoils and the elastic
deformation of the pile itself Due to the hardness and the high over consolidation
of the underlying clay layers we estimate that less than half of an inch pile
settlement may be developed due to compression or consolidation of the subsoils
under the long term design vertical load of 150 tons
Up to 0 25 to 0 56 inch elastic deformation may also develop in the square and
the octagonal piles respectively under the 150 ton design loading As a result
a maximum total pile settlement of up to an inch may be anticipated
Pile Driving
During pile driving the friction piles should be driven to their full design lengths to
derive the required compression or tension capacities
The piles may be stopped earlier during driving if the following driving criteria are
met which depend on the design pile capacities The criteria may be used as a
GEOLABS HAWAI

guide for test pile driving and should be verified with static pile load tests for use
in production pile driving
1 150 ton compression capacity the pile should be driven to a minimum
resistance of 15 blows per inch for the last 6 inches or 180 blows per foot
for the last foot of pile penetration
2 100 ton compression tension capacity the pile should be driven to a
minimum resistance of 6 blows per inch for the last 6 inches or 68 blows
per foot for the last foot of pile penetration
For design pile capacities between 100 and 150 tons per pile the bearing graph
on Plate 13 may be used to estimate the corresponding driving criteria
Due to the underlying stiff to hard clay subsoils hard and slow driving conditions
are anticipated especially for driving of the larger 20 inch square piles Therefore
a heavy pile hammer capable to deliver a minimum of about 54 000 foot pounds

Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be

Predrilling
The existing quaywall backfill appears to be uncontrolled fill of sandy and gravelly
materials placed randomly above the in situ recent alluvium and coral detritus
subsoils during the harbor construction several decades ago Our previous work
experience in Pear Harbor indicates that miscellaneous materials such as boulders
are often contained in similar fill materials and may pose potential obstruction to
pile driving
Therefore we recommend that the on land pile locations near the existing quaywall
be predrilled prior to pile driving to reduce the potential of encountering
obstructions during pile driving Based on the existing fill crust thickness we
believe that a minimum predrilling depth of 15 feet per pile may be used for the
on land piles
If the quaywall backfill is flattened to a slope as proposed in Scheme D predrilling
may be omitted where the depth of slope cut below the original grade is more than
15 feet
Our experience also indicates that pile driving through the existing mudline in front
of the quaywall may be obstructed with localized boulders or other objects which
may have been deposited during the last several decades In addition localized
very dense layers were encountered in the subsoils below mudline in Boring No
GEOLABS HAWAII

2 between the approximate depths of 35 and 50 feet below the Mean Lower Low

from the 600 p s f live loads may be eliminated or reduced along the upper
lengths of the new sheetpiles and thus the anchor pull may also be reduced
Based on our analyses it appears that the use of a minimum 14 foot wide
pressure relieving platform behind the new bulkhead may reduce the anchor pull
to about 20 kips per foot We recommend that the lateral earth pressure diagram
on Plate 17 be used for the anchored bulkhead design with the use of a 14 foot
wide pressure relieving platform
Deadman Anchor Tieback System
Buried deadman anchors may be required for the sheetpile bulkhead
For deadman anchor design we recommend design earth pressures of 35 pcf for
active and 350 pcf for passive We believe that the face of the deadman should
be at least 55 feet away from the sheetpile for the preferred Scheme F Generally
this horizontal distance will place the passive earth pressure wedge of the
deadman anchor outside the active wedge zone of the sheetpile
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1977 00 Q Page 23
We recommend that the top of the deadman be embedded at least 2 feet below
the finished grade Some provisions should be made to limit any future trenching
operation in front of the deadman anchor to avoid loss of passive resisting earth
pressures
It is anticipated that the bottom of the deadman anchor excavation will be below
the prevailing groundwater level and that dewatering may be necessary In
addition any soft or loose soil encountered at the bottom of the excavations
should be removed to a minimum depth of 18 inches and the over excavation
backfilled with coarse crushed rock such as No 3B Coarse material
Slope
Based on the subsoil conditions and the assumption that minimal prop wash will
be generated during berthing of the submarines we recommend that a 2H 1V
Horizontal Vertical slope ratio be used for the dredged slopes in front of the existing
quaywall or new bulkhead
Fender Pile
Fender piles are required to support LANTDIV camels for the docking of
submarines at the new wharf Based on the available fender pile data for the adjacent
Whart Y 3A from the Navy it is our understanding that 20 inch square precast and
prestressed concrete fender piles may be used and spaced about 4 feet center to center
behind the camels Berthing loads on the fender piles will be limited to avoid cracking of
the concrete pile section by limiting the design moment capacity of the fender piles to
about 169 kips foot
Based on the design moment capacity of the fender piles and the configuration of
the camel our lateral pile analyses indicate that the new fender piles may support up to

are driven to a minimum depth of 70 feet below the top of the new wharf deck
Site Grading
Except for the backfilling of the sunken areas or the flattening of slopes required
in some of the framing schemes minor site grading is contemplated for the proposed
construction due to the existing flat grades at the wharf site
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the project area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation debris rubbish
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
in order to prevent contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing areas at grade or designated to receive fill should be scarified to a
depth of at least 6 inches and recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum
dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 New slab subgrades should be
scarified and recompacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density per
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Loose or soft spots encountered at the slab subgrade level should be removed
and replaced with select on site material compacted to a minimum of 95
maximum dry density

W O 1977 00 Q Page 25
Fill and Backfill
The excavated on site soils except clays and organic materials may be re used
as a source of fill and backfill materials Clays and organic materials should be
disposed of properly off site to prevent contamination of the wharf site where future
construction may occur
If required new imported materials should consist of non expansive select granular
materials less than 3 inches in maximum size with less than 20 passing the 200
sieve
Fill and backfill materials above water level should be placed in maximum 10 inch
thick loose horizontal lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 of the ASTM
D 1557 78 maximum dry density
Self compacting materials such as 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gravel
should be used for fill and backfill below water level and within 2 feet of the
sheetpile wall No mechanical compaction is required for the placement of the
self compacting material

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
The moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these test are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Two 2 Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
their liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Four 4 sieve analyses were performed on selected soil samples to determine the
gradation characteristics of the soils The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 and
B 2
Two 2 Proctor tests ASTM d 1557 78 were performed on selected near surface
samples of the subsoils to evaluate their compaction characteristics The test results are
presented on Plate B 2
Four 4 CBR tests were also performed on selected near surface samples of the
subsoils to evaluate their subgrade support characteristics The test results are also
presented on Plate B 2
One 1 direct shear test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate its
shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 3
Nine 9 unconsolidated undrained UU triaxial tests were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate their undrained shear strength characteristics The test results
are presented on Plates B 4 through B 12
Two 2 consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
their compressibility The test results are plotted on Plates B 13 and B 14
