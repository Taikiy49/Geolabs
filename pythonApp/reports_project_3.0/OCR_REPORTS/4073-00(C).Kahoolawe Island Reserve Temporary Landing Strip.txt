Based on our geotechnical field reconnaissance performed for

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of the Island of
Maui and about 27 kilometers to the southeast of the Island of Lana i as shown on the
General Site Plan Plate is approximately 117 square kilometers in land area
with a maximum elevation of about 450 meters above sea level at the Lua Makika summit
on the eastern portion of the island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layered thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the rift Zones and caldera at Lua Makika The primary eruptive rift zones of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 C Page 7
the former volcano extend westerly from the summit and to a lesser degree to the north and
east from the summit Two cinder cone remnants can be seen located at Puu Moiwi and
Puu Kamama The area ait Lua Kealialalo is believed to represent the weathered remains
of a collapsed crater at the crest of the broad lava shield Some of the more recent aged
rocks composing the upper caldera filling layers of the island have been dated at about
1 million years old
Intermittent stream flow has incised gullies and some deep gulches into the residual
soil and weathered rock surface of the island The climate is generaily arid due to the
island s leeward location with respect to the Island of Maui Most rainfall occurs during large
Kona storm events and as light misting showers carried by the trade winds The
geomorphology of the island is most significantly affected by wind erosion which has created
a generally barren hard pan soil surface The lower slopes of the island show exposure of
bare rock with thin soil cover suggesting that some ocean wave erosion had occurred during
higher stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch It is believed that much of the soil
cover existing below about Elevation 240 meters Mean Sea Level MSL was historically
swept into the ocean by a large tsunami Similar large scale soil erosion and coral fragment
deposition have been found on the Islands of Maui and Lana

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field reconnaissance and laboratory test results we believe that the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for construction of the proposed Seagull
Road and temporary landing strip include the following
e The near surface soils are relatively dry and will require a significant amount
of water to moisture condition the soils for site preparation grading and
soil cement mixing
e Excavation of the near surface soils may encounter soil clumps cobbles and
boulders which would affect the mixing of the on site soils to form a
soil cement pavement structure especially in cut subgrades
e Proper backfilling around the excavated areas and grading to shed water away
from and around the pavements
As previously mentioned the on site near surface soils exist in a relatively dry
condition of about 12 to 16 percent below the optimum moisture content Therefore in order
to prepare the on site soils from their existing moisture content to near the optimum moisture
content for site preparation grading and soil cement mixing a large amount of water will be
needed at the site to facilitate the site grading work Based on our estimates we believe that
approximately 170 to 230 liters of water with an average of 200 liters would be needed for
each cubic meter of controlled fill for the grading operations Therefore an adequate water
supply and number of water trucks should be available at the site to moisture condition the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 C Page 12
on site soils to the specified moisture contents to prepare the subgrades and work the on site
soils as controlled fills
It should be noted that the soil cement pavement mixture is quite sensitive to the
moisture content of the on site soils and during the mixing procedures The contractor
should avoid over watering of the soil cement pavement mixture which would reduce the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil cement product Conversely if too little water
is used in the mixing of the soil cement the specified unconfined compressive strength may
not be achieved Therefore care should be exercised by the contractor to work with the
on site materials in a relatively small range of moisture contents for the soil cement
pavement structure Based on our soil cement testing performed for the project we believe
that the range of moisture contents for soil cement mixing should be between about
5 percent below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content based on ASTM
Test Designation D 698

Cracking in Soil Cement
Cracking in soil cement products pavements is primarily the result of shrinkage
caused by drying Cracks are natural characteristics of soil cement and are evidence that
the cement mixture is producing a hardened material with significant strength properties The
shrinkage in the soil cement is due to a loss of moisture and will occur soon after
construction similar to a concrete pavement The shrinkage in the soil cement pavement
will be restrained by friction between the soil cement and the subgrade Therefore when the
tensile strength of the soil cement is exceeded cracks will occur It should be noted that the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 C Page 15
load carrying capability and performance of the soil cement are generally not significantly
reduced by shrinkage cracking
Because cracking in the soil cement is primarily the result of drying and shrinkage the
least amount of cracking should occur in soil cements having the lowest moisture content
and highest density at the time of compaction It should be noted that the optimum moisture
contents of the soils at the project site are relatively high about 30 percent Consequenily
the moisture contents of the soil cement pavements at the time of compaction will also be
relatively high Therefore considering the higher moisture contents of the soil cement
pavements required at the time of compaction more shrinkage cracking in the soil cement
pavements than usual should be anticipated
Based on consultation with our soil cement expert we anticipate that the amount of
transverse shrinkage cracking in the soil cement pavements constructed with the on site
fine grained soils to be on the order of about 4 to 6 meters on center based on well cured
soil cement Longitudinal shrinkage cracking of the soil cement pavements is anticipated to
be generally along the middle of the pavement The typical crack widths could be on the
order of about 1 to wide The above discussions regarding crack spacing and crack
widths are based on previous studies performed on soil cement by the Portland Cement
Association
In order to reduce the potential for significant shrinkage cracking of the soil cement
pavement consiructed with the on site fine grained soils we recommend that special
attention be given to the curing of the soil cement pavements One of the most critical
aspects of proper curing of the soil cement pavement is early and effective retention of
moisture within the soil cement pavement The soil cement pavement should not be allowed
to dry out for the first 7 days after compaction This may be accomplished by keeping pieces
of burlap continuously moist or by frequent applications of water from a water truck As an
alternative to water curing consideration may be given to sealing the soil cement pavement
surface with asphalt emulsion if the use of asphalt emulsion is permissible on the island

Use of Seawater
We understand that consideration is being given to the use of seawater for the
mixing of the soil cement and grading operations since the source of large volumes of fresh
water is not readily available at the site In general it is possible to use seawater in lieu
of fresh water to mix and produce a soil cement pavement Presence of chlorides in the
seawater will likely promote high early strengths of the soil cement which is generally not
detrimental to the performance of the soil cement pavement
Use of seawater for the soil cement mixing is not necessarily detrimental to the
soil cement pavement mixture especially since the soil cement pavements have no
metallic structures embedded in the pavements such as reinforcing bars However it
should be noted that use of seawater to mix the soil cement would make the soil cement
more corrosive to metallic structures embedded in the soil cement or even reinforced
concrete structures in contact with the soil cement Appropriate design of the metallic
and or reinforced concrete structures would be necessary in these conditions
We also believe that the grading operations may use seawater in lieu of fresh water
for moisture conditioning the soils for the earthworks Use of seawater for the earthworks
would be reasonable provided that the compacted soils will be covered by paving or other
improvements However it should be noted that the soils would have a higher salt content
which contributes to corrosion making buried metallic structures more susceptible to
corrosion In addition the soils may also be more corrosive to buried and or on grade
reinforced concrete structures
lt should also be noted that the use of seawater for the grading operations would
also make vegetative growth on the soils with higher salt content more difficult An
appropriate soil conditioning expert should be consulted on this issue of vegetation growth
on soils with high salt content due to seawater
Several test sections of the soil cement pavements were conducted in the Kunia
area on the Island of Oahu in April 1999 to evaluate the construction of soil cement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4073 00 C Page 17
pavements One of the objectives of the soil cement test sections was to evaluate the
effect of seawater used in the mixing of the soil cement pavements Based on the limited
test sections conducted it appears that there were no significant differences in the
appearance strength and performance of the soil cement pavements constructed using
seawater over the soil cement pavements constructed using fresh water Therefore it
appears that significant detrimental effects in the soil cement pavements constructed using
seawater were not evident in the test sections

General fill and or controlled fill materials should be moisture conditioned to between
5 percent below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 698 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Boulders should be spread out only one layer at a time and should not be nested
together They should be placed such that compaction equipment will be able to
compact the materials between and around the boulders The materials between and

Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut and fill slopes on the order of up to 5 5 meters high will be constructed at the site
for the proposed construction We envision that the cut slopes would generally
expose the residual and saprolitic soils described in the Surface Soil Conditions
section of this report In general we believe that cut slopes exposing the very stiff
residual and saprolitic soils may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal
to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter As previously indicated graded surfaces exposing
the on site materials are highly susceptible to erosion by wind and surface water
run off Therefore freshly graded cut slopes should be protected by appropriate
slope planting or other means to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed
materials as soon as possible after the slope faces are exposed
Permanent fill slopes constructed using compacted general fill or controlied fill
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed
on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start
at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented herein In general
fill slopes should be constructed by overiilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In addition slope planting should be

provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished
slopes
Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the soil cement pavements grade for the
landing strip taxiway parking apron and access roads should be sloped to shed water away
from the pavements to reduce the potential for ponding Surface water ponding should not
be allowed on the site during or after construction These drainage requirements are
essential for the proper performance of the soil cement pavements since ponded water could
cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength of the
underlying subgrade soils The edges of the soil cement pavements should be properly
backfilled to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should
be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface waiter run off
away from the soil cement pavements
