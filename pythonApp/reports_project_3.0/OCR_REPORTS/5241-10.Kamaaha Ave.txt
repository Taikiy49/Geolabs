EARTHWORK OPERATION
Based on the information provided, it is proposed to extend the existing
Kamaaha Avenue from Manawai Street to Fort Barrette Road in the Kapolei area on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed roadway extension alignment will run between the
Kapolei Regional Park and Luana Koa development. In addition, the new roadway
construction will also connect to the existing paved portion of Manawai Street as shown on
the Site Plan, Plate 1.
The new roadway extension for Kamaaha Avenue, measuring approximately
1,680 feet long, will consist of two 30-foot wide travelways with an 8-foot wide sidewalk on
both sides separated by a 20-foot wide median. The new roadway extension for Manawai
Street, measuring approximately 740 feet long, will consist of two 22-foot wide travelways
with an 8-foot wide sidewalk on both sides. Based on our field observations and the plan
and profile drawings provided, we anticipate that cuts and fills of less than about 3 feet
thick will be required to achieve the finished grades for the project construction. In addition,
new utilities, such as water, drain, and sewer lines, are planned as part of the roadway
construction project.

Delta Construction Company performed roadway and utility construction work. The
earthwork operation consisted of clearing and grubbing of the existing ground,
over-excavation of the surface expansive clays, preparation of pavement subgrades,
placement and compaction of select granular material, select borrow, and base course,
and utility trench excavation and backfill.
Following the clearing and grubbing operation, the existing ground was generally
over-excavated to approximately 27.5 inches (Manawai Street) and 30 inches
(Kamaaha Avenue) below the finished pavement grades to provide the recommended
pavement structural sections. However, the pavement subgrade soils have become
saturated as a result of the heavy rains during the project construction. In order to facilitate
the pavement construction work, we recommended removing the soft and wet soils
underneath the pavement structural sections to a depth of about 24 inches. The 24-inch
over-excavation below the subbase course layer was subsequently reduced to 18 inches
with the implementation of the following measures:
» The bottom of the over-excavation was cement treated at a rate of about
one sack of cement per 25 square feet of subgrade area.
« A layer of geotextile fabric was placed over the cement treated subgrade prior
to placing the select granular fill material. In general, woven geotextile fabric
(Propex 2006) was used in areas that require substantial stabilization after
cement treatment. Non-woven geotextile fabric (Propex 4553) was used in
areas that require little additional stabilization after cement treatment.
The over-excavation was backfilled with select granular fill material, placed in 8-inch
loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The geotextile
fabric serves to provide separation between the underlying soft soils and the upper select
granular material, thus allowing the placement and compaction of the select granular fill
material. It should be noted that the geotextile fabric is not part of the pavement structural
section. Therefore, subsequent damage to the geotextile fabric resulting from trenching
activities will not affect the performance of the pavement structural section.
In order to reduce the potential for saturating the pavement structural sections
and the underlying subgrade soils, a subdrain system was installed along the mauka
side of Kamaaha Avenue to collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation,
especially from the adjacent park site. The subdrain system generally consists of a
4-inch PVC perforated pipe (with perforations facing down) surrounded by No. 3B Fine
gravel wrapped around by a layer of non-woven geotextile fabric. The subdrains
generally daylighted into appropriate drainage structures, such as catch basins, for
proper discharge.
Utility trench backfills were generally placed in near-level lifts, brought to about
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the
maximum dry density determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557 (Modified Proctor).

During the earthwork operation, samples of the on-site and imported materials

The maximum dry densities and optimum moisture contents were established in our

Periodic field density tests were performed during the fill and backfill operations to
confirm our field observations as variations in construction procedures and soil conditions
were noted. A total of 400 field density tests, including retests of failed areas, were
performed using the nuclear probe method in accordance with ASTM D 2922. The results
of the compaction tests performed are summarized on Plates 3.1 through 3.37 of this
report. These tests were performed on the prepared ground surfaces and on constructed
engineered fills and backfills. Test locations and elevations were estimated by pacing and
interpolating from on-site survey stakes and construction plans. These locations and
elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
Areas assessed to be unsatisfactory were reworked until the specified minimum
compaction was obtained and/or approved by our field representative based on visual
observation and hand probing.

specifications and our geotechnical engineering recommendations presented in the
