INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration for the
design of the mass grading of the proposed Maui Lani Development Phase The
residential and golf course development site is located about miles to the south of
Kahului on the island of Maui Hawaii The general location of the project site and its
vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work on the project was performed in general accordance with our revised
proposal to Maui Lani Development c o Herbert K Horita Realty dated September 11
1990 This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Maui Lani development consists of a planned residential community
with recreational facilities and commercial areas The residential portions of the
development will be primarily single family residential subdivisions with some multi family
areas The major recreational facility will be an 18 hole golf course The project will be
developed in several phases
Phase is situated in the eastern portion of the Maui Lani project area and consists
of about 200 acres of former sugar cane land and vacant land to the northwest of
Kuihelani Highway Preliminary mass grading plans indicate cuts ranging up to as much
as 40 feet below the existing ground surface and fills of up to about 25 feet deep The
golf course area particularly the water features will be used as a source of borrow for
the mass grading of the residential areas
Previously we performed a preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration for
what is currently the eastern half of the Phase development area Subsequently the
GEOLABS HAWAI

grading and development plans were modified and the Phase portion of the project was
expanded
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain soil and rock data for the formulation
of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design and construction
of the mass grading and infrastructure installation for the proposed Phase residential and
golf course development including roadway grading and liners for proposed water
features
The scope of our exploration consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and operators
to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of twenty 20 borings at the project site to depths

data

and logging of the borings by a Geologist from our firm
5 Laboratory testing of the selected samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid to classifying the materials encountered and
determining their engineering properties

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated between Wailuku and Kahului in the Wailuku area of the
island of Maui Hawaii The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
The easterly half of the Phase area site is fallow rural land that has previously
been used for the cultivation of sugar cane Much of the westerly portion is raw vacant
land consisting of sand dunes with a dense cover of vegetation Most of the site is
densely vegetated by volunteer sugar cane haole koa and other semitropical trees
shrubs and grasses
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2413 10 Page 5
In general the topography is nearly level to gently rolling with locally steep relief
where drainageways cross the site and between individual dune ridges Portions of the
dune areas were previously used for mining of the sand as shown by the remaining open
sand pits The overall slope and drainage is towards the northeast
Elevations at the Phase portion of the site vary from about 85 to about 180 feet
above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Maui is composed of two shield volcanoes West Maui and Haleakala
built up by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the Pleistocene Epoch The Maui Lani
project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two voicanic domes
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts
of the two volcanoes During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fell to stands lower than
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand
dunes
In some areas these dunes have been mantled by man made fill which was
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar
cane

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on the data obtained during both of our explorations and the analyses
based on these data it is our opinion that development of Phase of the proposed
residential and golf course project is feasible from a geotechnical engineering
point of view The dune sand and alluvial soils encountered at the site are generally
competent and should provide adequate bearing for the site improvements of the
proposed development The excavated materials should provide a suitable source of fill
material Some hard excavation and or ripping may be encountered in localized
cemented areas or where bouldery horizons are encountered

Grading
In general based on the results of our explorations it appears that conventional
grading methods may be used for the mass grading of the Phase portion of the Maui
Lani development project
The following recommendations are general guidelines for the design of the mass
grading and for the development of general grading specifications
1 Prior to commencement of grading areas within the proposed grading limits
should be cleared of vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting
grubbed and spoil material should either be disposed of properly off site
or else the material snould be shredded chipped and stockpiled for use in
landscaping
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below
areas designated to receive fill should be overexcavated to firm natural
material and the resulting depression backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill
2 Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning
of fill material will be necessary Because of silty and fine sandy soil
conditions it is also advisable to institute a dust control program during
grading operations
3 In general the excavated soil materials should be suitable as fill material
Boulders or cobbles larger than twelve 12 inches in maximum dimension
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 10 Page 9
should be removed from the excavated material and either disposed of
off site or be placed in the deeper fills as discussed below
The material should be adequate as a source of trench and structural
backfill if needed Trench and structural backfill should not contain rock or
lumps larger than six 6 inches in maximum dimension To densify the
trench backfill water tamping could be utilized selectively for the porous
sandy and silty on site soils
4 Imported material if required should be non expansive with no particles
greater than six 6 inches in size and no more than fifteen 15 percent
passing the 200 sieve It is recommended that if additional off site borrow
is required it should be tested by the project geotechnical engineer to
determine if it is suitable for its intended use prior to its delivery to the
jobsite
5 Large boulders or rock fragments should be buried in the deepest portions
of the fills provided that they are not nested and an adequate amount of
compacted finer grained soil fills the space between the boulders to reduce
the potential of loss of fines from the upper portion of the fill Care must be
exercised to avoid placement of any boulders in locations which would
conflict with future utility lines In general boulder or rock fragments larger
than twelve 12 inches in size should be placed at depths greater that four
4 feet below finish subgrade level
6 Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve
12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and

7 Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal

to lessen potential instability of the new fill see Plate 3 The filling

Excavation
Based on the results of our preliminary and current explorations it appears that
most of the materials encountered in the Phase portion of the Maui Lani development
area may be conventionally excavated Some hard excavation and or ripping may be
encountered in localized cemented areas or when bouldery horizons are encountered
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor within practical
limits and safety considerations However care should be exercised to avoid overcutting
which would necessitate backfilling of the overcut area This is particularly critical in slope
areas to avoid the placement of sliver fills on the disturbed sandy silty slope faces in an
attempt to repair the overcut area
Slopes
Provided that on site materials are utilized design cut and fill slopes of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter are recommended Fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio Care should be
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2413 10 Page 11
exercised to avoid overcutting on cut slopes in order to avoid the placement of sliver fills
on the slope faces in an attempt to repair the overcut area
Sandy and silty soils such as those encountered at the project site are susceptible
to erosion when exposed particularly in slope areas Water should be diverted away
from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be utilized to reduce potential drainage
run off erosion and or destabilization of the finished slopes For slopes steeper than
three horizontal to one vertical 8H 1V the use of an erosion control mat such as
Miramat or similar may be considered to reduce the potential of slope erosion until such
time as the vegetative cover is well established
In general midpoint siope benches should be provided for slopes steeper than
three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V and having vertical heights greater than 20 feet
Modification to the above could be made on an individual basis by the project
geotechnical engineer based on the actual site soil conditions and other design
considerations
Infrastructure Installation
Itis anticipated that underground utilities such as water sewer drain and irrigation
lines will be installed as part of the development work Based on the results of our field
exploration it appears that relatively conventional excavation methods may be utilized for
utility line trenching However bouldery horizons may be encountered in the deeper
excavations
In general the subsurface materials encountered in the easterly portion of the site
are relatively dry and competent and would require minimal trench shoring for shallow
trenches However areas of loose sand or gravel may be encountered particularly in the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 10 Page 12
thicker sand deposits encountered during the current exploration in the westerly portion
of the site
Trench shoring design and installation should be left to the contractor and should
conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements If sufficient space is available
the trench walls may be sloped in lieu of shoring Generally the temporary trench walls
should be sloped at ratios of about one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter
The trenches should be excavated and backfilled in accordance with Section 11
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction 1986 Trench Excavation and
Backfill
Pipes should be bedded and cushioned in a granular material such as 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation Proper backfill placement and compaction is
Critical for soil supported pipes such as corrugated metal pipes for drainlines Improper
backfilling could result in structural distress and deflection of the arch of the pipe causing
settlements of the soil above the pipe It is our opinion that only granular material should
be used for backfill from the cushion level to one 1 foot above the top of the pipe This
opinion holds for all types of pipe
Trench backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density per ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78
Mechanical compaction equipment is highly suited to the materials encountered
at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used as a means to
attempt to compact the on site soils

Sand Utilization
Our field explorations encountered poorly graded fine sand Dune Sand at shallow
depths throughout the Phase area The thickness of this sand varied a great deal at
various locations ranging from about two 2 to in excess of twenty six 26 feet
Based on our laboratory testing this sand material should provide a good source
of fill and backfill material The material could be exploited as export material if there is
a surplus in the excavation vs embankment balance
As far as using the material for concrete sand the gradation of the on site dune
sand is finer than the requirements of ASTM C 33 specifications for concrete fine
aggregate However it is our understanding that it has been the practice for several
years on the island of Maui to blend the dune sand with crushed basalt sand Man Sand
to create fine aggregate According to Maui concrete suppliers this blended aggregate
has performed well in terms of workability and strength
Laboratory testing performed during our previous exploration indicates that the
sand is acceptable for concrete aggregate in terms of organic content ASTM Test
Method C 40
Ponds and Water Features
It is our understanding that the proposed golf course development will involve
ponds and water features The field percolation tests conducted in selected borings
during the current exploration indicate that the subsoils underlying the site have rapid
infiltration characteristics
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 10 Page 14
Due to the dry site conditions and the permeable nature of the materials at the site
we recommend that the ponds be lined with synthetic flexible membranes to reduce the
loss of water from the ponds by infiltration into the soils
To reduce the potential of punctures in the flexible membranes a cushion layer of
the on site sand is recommended for the bottoms and sides of the ponds and water
features and should be installed prior to the placement of the flexible membrane The
cushion should be a minimum of six 6 inches thick
Although the flexible membranes are considered to be impervious there is a
possibility of seepage due to punctures in the liner or breaks in the seams Leakage
through the membrane could potentially cause piping losses or weakening of the
subgrade soils Piping losses could result in loss of subgrade support leading to liner
failure Weakening of the subgrade soils could cause loss of stability in impoundment
slopes potentially leading to slope failure and or liner failure Therefore we recommend
that a subdrain system be installed below the sand cushion
Our experience with similar liner installations indicates that there is sometimes a
tendency for flexible liners to become detached at the top of the water feature or
impoundment It appears that this is may be the result of inadequate anchoring of the
top of the liner and insufficient flexibility in the liner It is our recommendation that the liner
manufacturer be consulted regarding proper installation with particular attention to proper
anchoring of the liner to reduce the potential of liner failure
Foundation Support
Our laboratory testing indicates that the on site soils generally have low to
moderate expansion potential when saturated from a dry and compacted condition It is
Our opinion that shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to support
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 10 Page 15
structures Alternately thickened edge slabs on grade may be ideal to support the lightly
loaded single family residential structures
For the design of the foundations of single family residential structures an
allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f may be used for footings bearing either on the
in situ soils or on properly compacted fill consisting of on site materials The bottom of
footing foundations should be embedded a minimum of eighteen 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade
The thickened edges of slabs on grade should extend a minimum of twelve 12
inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade The slab should be a minimum of four
4 inches thick and should be supported by a four 4 inch thick cushion of 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 67 gradation A moisture barrier membrane should be incorporated
between the slab and cushion if necessary to protect the interior floor covering from
moisture infiltration
Site specific exploration to provide data for foundation design recommendations
should be conducted for the multi family commercial or golf course structures when their
final location and configuration have been established

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of twenty 20 borings to depths ranging between 13 5 and 46 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing hollow stem continuous flight
augers or rotary core drilling tools as appropriate
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a guide to the relative quality of rock
masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material that is
in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative rock quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to determine the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in
the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering
properties The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 10
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data are used
in various engineering calculations The results of the direct shear tests are presented
on Plates B 11 through B 13

Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate B 14

Fill Materials
Material for fill shall consist of on site soils or approved import material Fill

Representative samples of imported materials to be used as compacted fill shall

jobsite delivery
Placement and Compaction of Fill
A _ The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers not exceeding eight inches 8 in
loose thickness Each layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed to
attain uniformity of material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer which has not been
properly compacted Subsequent removal of any unacceptable layer shall
be done at the Contractor s expense
