Regional Geology 
Extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the Waianae and Koolau Shield 
Volcanoes built the Island of Oahu. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the 
Island of Oahu. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene (latest Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the eastern two-thirds of the 
Island of Oahu.   
Following the coalescence of the two volcanoes and after a long period of 
volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the 
Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder 
and tuff cone formations. These late series of eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series, which are confined to scattered areas of the Koolau Volcano footprint. 
The Honolulu Volcanic Series, which began approximately 700,000 years ago 
(MacDonald and Abbott, 1970), produced several prominent cinder and tuff cone 
structures with some basaltic lava flows that became inter-layered with the developing 
Southern Oahu coastal plain deposits.

The project site is on the lower southern flank of the Koolau Volcano. As a result, 
the geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the area are directly related to 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the 
genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions in the Moiliili area 
include various marsh land and alluvial soil deposits overlying fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal limestone reef and related marine/lagoonal deposits laid during higher stands 
of the sea. These deeper marine deposits are intercalated with other terrigenous 
derived sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
Most of the Honolulu Coastal Plain developed during the Pleistocene Epoch 
when the sea level experienced significant fluctuations related to the continental glacial 
stages. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic 
basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers 
melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose 
to above the present sea level. The processes of landform erosion, sediment 
deposition, and reef development were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level 
fluctuations. 
When the sea level was relatively lower, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower and stream valleys were carved deeper into the island basaltic 
rock, the fringing coastal sediments, and the offshore reef deposits. Also, during periods 
of relatively lower sea level, the sub-aerial exposure of calcareous marine sediments 
caused consolidation and cementation of the deposits to form hardened calcareous 
deposits. In contrast, when the sea level was relatively higher, the erosional base level 
was raised such that marine sediments and coral reef formations filled the pre-existing 
depressions and accumulated as reef platforms at correspondingly higher elevations. 
The higher sea levels also caused the formation of deltas and alluvial fans composed of 
terrigenous sediment in the head region of old bays. 
Finally, the placement of near-surface man-made fills associated with the 
development of the urban area within the last 80 years has brought the Honolulu 
Coastal Plain to its present form.

Field Infiltration Testing 
The infiltration tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures 
in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with 
other states’ procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed in Boring Nos. 1 through 6 and Boring 
No. 8 by augering boreholes to the selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing 
was set to the bottom of each drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the soil 
exposed at the bottom of the boring. A hand auger was used to clean out the soil 
cuttings at the bottom of the hole, and the PVC solid casing was reset to the bottom of 
the hole if necessary. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the 
infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials

Permeable Pavement Design Considerations 
We understand that permeable pavements are being considered for stormwater 
management and infiltration. Due to the high shrink/swell potential of the on-site clay 
soils, the installation of an impervious membrane above the clayey subgrade soils 
should be considered. The impervious membrane should be at least 40 mil in thickness. 
In addition, a drainage system to carry the water percolating from the permeable 
pavement and away from the clayey subgrade should be provided. Furthermore, we 
recommend that the finished subgrade should be sloped towards the drainage pipe or 
inlet to reduce the potential for ponding of the subsurface water. 
The permeable pavement for the bike path will be adjacent to the existing 
roadway and sidewalk. Subsurface water from the permeable pavement may move 
laterally below the pavement and sidewalk and soften the subgrade soils, which can 
cause distress to the pavement and sidewalk structure. To reduce the potential for the 
above, we recommend that the impervious membrane extend up the sides of the trench 
for the permeable pavement system.
