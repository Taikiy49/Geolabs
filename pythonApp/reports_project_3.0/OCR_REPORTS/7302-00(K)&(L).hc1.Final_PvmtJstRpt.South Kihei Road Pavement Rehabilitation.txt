SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway rehabilitation project encountered a 
pavement section consisting of approximately 6 to 10 inches of AC underlain by a fill layer 
of 0.5 to 4 feet thick overlying beach deposits and/or alluvium.  The fill layer encountered 
generally consisted of medium dense silty sands and sandy gravel and stiff sandy silts.   
Beach deposits consisting of loose to medium dense silty sands were encountered below 
the fill layer in the majority of the drilled borings.  Alluvium consisting of soft sandy silts, 
medium stiff sandy clays or very dense boulders was encountered in Boring Nos. 5, 6, and 
10.  Very dense silty gravel (weathered basalt) was encountered in Boring Nos. 10 through 
12.   
We encountered groundwater at depths of about 1.7 to 5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface in Boring Nos. 1 through 6 at the time of our field exploration.  The 
groundwater levels measured generally correspond to about Elevations +2.1 to +3 feet 
MSL.  It should be noted that water level may vary with tidal fluctuations, seasonal rainfall, 
time of the year, and other factors. 
Our field survey indicated that the existing pavements of South Kihei Road from 
Lipoa Street to Auhana Road and from Keonekai Road to Kilohana Drive under 
consideration for this project appear to exist in fair to poor conditions. Based on our 
engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we believe that the areas in fair 
condition may be rehabilitated with a regular AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 
inches of the existing pavements and replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
We observed pavement failures along various portions of South Kihei Road within 
the project limits. These distressed pavements should be reconstructed with a new 
pavement structural section designed based on a 50-year or 30-year pavement life, 
depending on the design traffic volume, in accordance with the guidelines presented in the 
pavement design manual prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, 
Highways Division, Material Testing and Research Branch. In general, the existing 
distressed pavements from (i) Lipoa Street to Auhana Road may be reconstructed with a 
new pavement structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC on 5.0 inches of ACB over 
6 inches of AB, and from (ii) Keonekai Road to Kilohana Drive may be reconstructed with 
a new pavement structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC on 3.5 inches of ACB 
over 6 inches of AB.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the South Kihei Road Pavement Rehabilitation, 
Lipoa Street to Auhana Road and Keonekai Road to Kilohana Drive project in the Kihei 
area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project is along South Kihei Road between Lipoa 
Street to Auhana Road and between Keonekai Road to Kilohana Drive for a total 
distance of approximately 2 miles in the Kihei area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The 
approximate limits of the roadway rehabilitation project are shown on the General Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity will be 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an asphaltic concrete (AC) overlay. Reconstruction 
of the existing pavements will be performed for those areas where the observed 
distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or 
subgrade materials.  We understand that the proposed construction will be a federally 
funded project. Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to the 
requirements and guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will 
be required. The new pavements for the project will be designed based on the 
methodology described in the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 prepared by 
the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials 
Testing and Research Branch.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located 
between these two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting 
the two volcanic domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing South Kihei Road within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway except for the additional turn lanes at the intersection areas and 
shoulder parking lanes. The approximate project limits of the roadway are shown on the 
General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of paved and 
unpaved road shoulders, short-cut grass, sidewalks, traffic signs and utility posts. The 
surface soils exposed along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist 
of tan coral sands with gravel in various mixtures.  The existing South Kihei Road 
roadway alignment gently slopes down from north to south, within the limits of the 
project.   
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in May and June 2020 to collect 
general information for the existing pavement conditions along the portions of the 
roadway under consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in 
the following subsections. It should be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the existing both lanes of South Kihei Road  (i) 
from approximately 530 feet south of the Lipoa Street intersection to approximately 
125 feet south of Waiohuli Street, (ii) from approximately 200 feet south of the 
Kaala Place intersection to Auhana Road, (iii)  from Keonekai Road to 
approximately 680 feet south, and (iv) from the Kihei Boat Ramp entrance to 
Kilohana Drive within the existing pavements under consideration fall into this 
category and generally appear to exhibit minor to moderate signs of structural 
defects. Overall pavement deterioration is restricted to longitudinal cracking of low 
to medium severity along the paving joints and tire lanes. The ride characteristic of 
the pavement was generally fair.  
2.2.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
In general, the majority of South Kihei Road, except for those areas noted above, 
falls under this category and was observed to be in poor condition with moderately 
to highly distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to 
be widespread and generally moderate to high in severity. The poor areas consist 
of elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions 
of 0.5 to 2 inches, rutting and some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes. 
Potholes patched with AC material were observed at various locations along the 
alignment. The ride characteristic of the roadway was observed to be generally 
poor to irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor pavements.  The approximate 
limits of these poor areas requiring pavement reconstruction are shown on the Site 
Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.3.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed. Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  The procedures used in 
determining the new pavement section were generally determined based on the 
methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated 
March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii - 
Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans).

3.4 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our evaluation, it 
is our opinion that pavement areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an 
AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of the existing pavements and 
replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up 
by the cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not

3.5 
Design Pavement Section 
As discussed in the “Existing Site/Pavement Conditions” section herein, some of 
the observed distresses along the roadway are likely caused by failures in the 
underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. In general, these areas should be 
reconstructed with a new pavement section in lieu of an AC overlay section. In addition, 
these distressed areas should be repaired prior to the AC overlay work for the entire 
roadway under consideration. 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that some portion of the 
roadway alignment under consideration for this project is generally underlain by sandy 
soils with little or no cohesion.  As a result, the subgrade may not be able to compact 
into a dense mass to receive placement and compaction of the aggregate subbase 
(ASB) layer of the recommended pavement reconstruction section.  Based on our 
evaluation and these construction considerations, we recommend providing a layer of 
reinforcing geogrids and permeable separator between the upper ASB layer and the 
underlying subgrade soils. To maximize the benefits of the triaxial geogrid, we 
understand that AB with a nominal maximum size of 1.5 inches, in lieu of ASB, should 
be used. In general, the triaxial geogrid will interlock with the aggregate base course, 
resulting in two benefits during initial construction and for the life of the project: 1) lateral 
confinement – increasing the modulus of the aggregate base course, and 2) subgrade 
bearing capacity enhancement.

Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform 
to Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.6 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain, and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling 
and sampling 12 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12, extending to depths 
of about 3.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.3. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-12. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
