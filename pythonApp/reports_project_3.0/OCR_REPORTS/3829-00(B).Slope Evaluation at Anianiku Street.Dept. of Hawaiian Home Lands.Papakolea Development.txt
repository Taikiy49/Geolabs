SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Field observations indicate that some of the residential lots
located along Kanaha Stream have experienced varying
degrees of slope erosion related to surface drainage
discharge over the top of the slope and stream flow scour at
the base of the slopes
To aid in the protection of the top of the slope from erosion
and gradual retreat the installation of a drainage swale
parallel to the top of the slope and including appropriate
lateral drainages flowing down the slope to discharge to the
stream is recommended
Also the accumulations of greenwaste and rubbish located
along the top of the slope should be removed to reduce the
loading of the slope and to reduce the potential for
landsliding of the loose overcast materials The natural
vegetation should be allowed to establish itself on the slope to
aid in slowing the potential down slope creep movements
To assist in the protection of the base of the slope from
stream scour and erosion installation of an ungrouted boulder
rip rap revetment throughout the stream channel alignment
may be considered
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed
discussions and specific design recommendations

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the slope evaluation project at Anianiku Street within the Department of
Hawaiian Home Lands Papakolea Development in Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field reconnaissance and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended to assist in the identification of the existing slope
damage and to present a conceptual plan to stabilize the slope The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Anianiku Street project site is located within the existing Department of
Hawaiian Home Lands Papakolea subdivision development in the Papakolea Punchbowl
area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project limits involve 22 residential
properties located along Anianiku and luakea Streets within the Papakolea subdivision
The subject properties reside on the eastern side of Anianiku and luakea Streets and
border the Kanaha Stream alignment
Several of the parcels bordering Kanaha Stream have experienced varying
degrees of erosion and some slope creep The problems include a gradual westward
retreat of the top of the slope and localized scouring of the bottom of the slope at the
stream channel The problems appear to be occurring throughout the length of the
project site but were observed as being more significant at particular portions of the site

special attention to protect buildings which may be damaged by continued slope erosion

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned to the island of Oahu with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations The most recent volcanic series
are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series The Punchbowl crater located to the
west of the project site is one such extinct tuff and cinder cone eruptive center
in the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
mass wasting which is essentially the detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley
walls and transportation downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity and
deposited as colluvium Once these colluvial materials reach a stream typically in the
central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3829 00 B Page 5
The general project area is located near the mouth of a valley extending down the
southern flank of the Koolau mountains The site is bordered to the west by the eastern
flank of the Punchbowl crater which is composed primarily of volcanic tuff and cinder
materials The land area comprising the project site is believed to be underlain by alluvial
soils derived from the weathering of the Koolau mountain and Punchbowl crater Some
fills may have been placed and graded at the site during the initial development of the
subdivision The Kanaha Stream which flows perennially from the Koolau mountains is
located at the toe of the alluvial fill slope of the project site This slope is the subject of
our evaluation and the reported erosion and landslide occurrences at the project site
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site exists as a row of residential properties situated along and atop
the slope which parallels the Kanaha Stream alignment A total of 22 parcels were
observed for signs of slope failure and erosion in the backyard area of the lots In
general the lots are rectangular in shape and slope gently then moderately from the
street elevation along Anianiku Street down to Kanaha Stream Most of the homes
were constructed on the generally flatter topography closer to the street however
some of the homes extend out to the moderately sloping portion of the lot
Based on our understanding some of the residents have reported landslides
that have occurred at the backyard of their lots Some of these landslides have
reportedly claimed storage structures once residing at the top of the slope in their
backyards Based on our communication with some of the residents there appears to
be several distinct problem areas that tend to experience more erosion than others In
addition some of the residents reported that flooding of their backyards typically occurs
during heavy stream flow

General Slope Conditions
Based on the available General Topographic Plan of the vicinity regional
elevations at the site range from about 130 feet Mean Sea Level at the southern limit
of the project site to about 260 feet MSL at the northern limit Based on our
observations of the ground surface the slope appears to be comprised of dark brown
silty clay and clayey silt soils It is unknown whether the soils observed represent fill
material or alluvial soils however it is anticipated that some fills may have been placed
to level portions of the lots
The slopes were observed to be generally covered with shrubs tall grass
scattered trees and in places a dense mantling growth of vine vegetation Other
portions of the slope generally beneath the canopy of large trees were observed as
barren soil It appears as though the dense vine growth has covered some areas so
completely as to queil the growth of other shrubs and smail trees
The slopes contained within the subject land parcels range in height with the
tallest slopes generally existing at the southern through the middle portions of the site
Based on the undated Topographic Plan the vertical slope height appears to be on the
order of about 20 to 30 feet from the stream to the upper portion of the parcels at
42 Parcel Nos 37 through 49 Progressing upstream the slope height
reduces to about 5 to 15 feet as observed at 42 Parcel Nos 36 and 35
Nos 31 and 32 the entire lots contain moderately sloping ground with elevation
changes across the lots ranging between about 5 and 30 feet in vertical height As a
result of the varying slope topography and the varying location of the stream channel
with respect to the property boundaries and structures located within some of the
parcels along the stream appear to be at greater risk for erosion than other parcels
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3829 00 B Page 7
As mentioned previously most of the homes have been constructed away from
the steep portions of the lots and appear to be relatively safe from slope erosion
however active slope erosion appears to be threatening the foundation of the
residence located at 40 44 The resident reported that the slope was slowly
retreating back towards the house and was threatening to undermine the foundation at
the rear of the building Our observations confirmed active erosion of the surface soils
which may impact the stability of the building foundation
Furrows and other small scale surface erosional features were observed along
the top of the slope The furrows are believed to represent the erosional effects of
surface water flowing over the top and down the slope from locations within the various
properties In addition ground surface cracks located parallel to the top of the slope at
the location of 42 43 indicate active down slope movement such as soil
creep Greenwaste and rubbie debris such as concrete blocks and rocks have been
placed by residents at the top of the slope with limited success in efforts to reduce the
slope retreat
Kanaha Stream
In general the Kanaha Stream channel appears to be composed of silty and
clayey soils with some boulder accumulations lining the banks and stream bottom in
places The occurrence of boulder deposits appears to be more concentrated at the
northern portion of the stream alignment In situ rock formation was observed in the
channel bottom only at the extreme southern and northern portions of the stream
channel alignment Exposed rock formation was not observed elsewhere on the slope
or within the stream channel at the site
The general stream channel location is controlled by the axis of the valley and
the adjacent slopes however the meandering channel alignment was poorly defined
and clogged with potential obstructions such as trash vegetation and other discarded
debris During heavy stream flow conditions it appears that the stream water may
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3829 00 B Page 8
swell to overflow the existing poorly defined stream banks In particular such a
condition appears to occur at the portion of the stream fronting parcel Nos 38 through
50 Here the stream banks appear poorly defined and a broad gentle slope exists on
the eastern side of the stream that appears to be frequently covered by water during
heavy stream flow
Other parcels located along the northern reach of the stream where the slope
bordering the stream gradually merges with the stream channel appear to be subject to
flood water overtopping the relatively low poorly defined stream bank During our site
visit the resident located at 40 44 reported that during heavy stream flow
portions of his her lot adjacent to the stream are inundated with turbulent floodwater
The resident reported that the stream flow often overtops the low bank located at the
bend in the stream fronting the property forcing the water to flow through the property
and beneath the home The resident has placed rubble fill along the stream boundary
in an effort to divert the flood waters with some success However the grade difference
between the relatively low lying level lot and the adjacent stream channel is slight
therefore the rocky fill that was placed frequently washed away Furthermore the
resident reported that the neighbor located at 42 29 had also experienced
flooding of his her lot by the same circumstance in the past
Communication with several area residents located along the southern reach of
the stream revealed that the stream channel flows vigorously during heavy runoff often
carrying debris such as large automobile parts and other rubbish downstream
Occasionally the debris entangles in the vegetation growing within the lower more
gently sloping reach of the stream thus creating blockages that force the stream to
broaden and flow outside of the normal channel The turbulent flows are believed to
produce areas of increased scour and erosion located at the base of the slope

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our findings and recommendations are based on visual observations of the
exposed slope surfaces at accessible locations of the site and the observed condition of
the stream channel located at the base of the slopes Subsurface data was not available
for our analyses but may be necessary to further evaluate the stability of the steeper
slopes at the southern portion of the site In addition due to the size and complexity of
the variable slope conditions at the site it is difficult to make generalizations for the entire
site For these reasons we are providing our recommendations for consideration of
possible methods to stabilize the slope on a very general level
To summarize our field observations ongoing erosion of the ground surface soils
was observed in places along the top of the slope bordering the stream We believe the
erosion is primarily a result of the direction of storm water including surface and roofline
drainage waiter flowing over the top of the siope and down towards the stream below in
addition the erosion may be augmented by the occurrence of soil creep acting on the
slope Furthermore the base of the slope bordering the stream has experienced varying
degrees of scour and erosion from the stream flow
Although we believe that erosion and slope creep have occurred throughout the
project site particular areas have experienced more erosion and should have higher
priority during the planning of the repair work These areas are discussed in the following
section of the report
Areas of Significant Erosion
Several areas of the site have experienced erosion and scour that may adversely
affect property or structures located thereon The slope located within
42 Parcel Nos 42 through 44 has eroded to an extent that structures located
within the properties may be subject to damage In particular the residential structure at
Parcel No 44 is situated precariously close to the edge of the slope Furthermore some
storage structures located on Parcel No 43 are located in close proximity to ground
surface cracks at the edge of the slope Although Parcel No 42 was not accessible at
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3829 00 B Page 10
the time of our site visit based on the available topographic map we believe that the
potential exists for significant erosion at that site and closer observation may be
warranted
Observation of the building located at Parcel No 37 indicates that the rear of the
structure is sagging in the down slope direction The structure appears to have been
constructed out over the slope area extending very close to the stream We believe the
structure sag is due to slope movement
The rear of the parcels bordering the stream at 42 29 and
40 44 and 32 show some signs of stream scouring and periodic flooding of the
backyard areas The reason appears to be the existing low and poorly defined stream
bank at these locations
Finally the parcel located at 40 31 was observed to be affected by
stream scouring The resident has placed a near continuous loosely stacked wall
consisting of cobbles and boulders and other concrete rubble along the base of the slope
bordering the stream in an effort to reduce the erosion The effort appears to be
producing some positive results however the wall is in need of rehabilitation
Surface Erosion and Soil Creep
Based on our observations we believe that excess surface water may be flowing
from the roofs and ground surface of the properties over the top of the slope creating an
erosion problem for the surface clayey soils Our observations indicate that some
concentrated flow from roof lines and developed portions of the properties flow over the
top of the slope accelerating the erosional loss of the surface soils In addition the soils
comprising the moderately steep vegetated slope may be undergoing some slow creep
movement under the unrestrained influence of gravity Creep is a gradual movement of
surface soil materials down slope under the influence of gravity and is a relatively
common occurrence on steeper slopes

Stream Scour
Based on our observations we believe the stream flow at the toe of the slope
causes scour and erosion at the base of the slope during periods of heavy stream flow
The scour and erosion at the base of the slope may trigger small scale landslides or other
erosion reflected in the retreat of the slope face Furthermore the meandering and poorly
defined stream channel character appears to significantly contribute to the erosive action
of the flood waters
Slope Stabilization
It is our opinion that long term stabilization of the slopes at the site would likely
involve the construction of a concrete drainage channel and or retaining walls in addition
to providing proper surface drainage at the top of the slope The methods would serve to
improve the surface drainage at the top of the slope and control the stream flow and
migration of the channel over time However the construction would involve substantial
costs An alternative would be to provide smaller scale drainage and slope protection
structures such as
e installation of a drainage ditch at the top of the slope to intercept runoff and
e installation of boulder rip rap lining to reduce stream scour
Furthermore it may be advantageous to have the lessees individually begin to
undertake temporary measures to protect the slope and control the flow of surface storm
water flow over the top of the slope Measures may include the installation of roof gutters
and downspouts with piping to discharge storm water away from the slope or further
down the slope face Additional protective measures would include the cessation of the
dumping of greenwaste and loose rubble fill at the top of the slope These measures
would serve to reduce storm water flows down the slope and reduce the loading of the
top of the slope with loose debris or organic waste Additionally it is advisable to promote
the growth of rooted shrubs and small trees on the slope to aid in the retention of surficial
soils and reduce the effects of down slope creep movement

Drainage Ditch
To reduce the effects of surface water flowing over the top of the slope it may be
feasible to install a drainage ditch along the top of the steep slope in the vicinity of
42 Parcel Nos 37 through 45 at the project site The drainage ditch
should include lateral ditches that drain down the slope to the stream below Other
areas may not require the ditch installation since the slopes are flatter and or
shorter in height The lateral ditch drains may be installed at selected intervals and
at locations where existing obstructions such as buildings presently exist A
schematic sketch of the drainage swale system is provided as Plate 3 of this
report
The ditches may consist of earth swales stabilized with erosion resistant geotextile
This type of flexible system may be preferable to a rigid concrete ditch since slope
creep movements may continue
Rip Rap Lining
To reduce the effects of stream scour and erosion along the base of the slopes
and at the rear property boundaries it may be feasible to install an ungrouted
boulder rip rap revetment along the banks and bottom of the stream
The rip rap should extend up the stream banks to a distance equal to or higher
than the projected flood stage of the stream as appropriate due to the varied
existing slope height and slope inclination conditions The beginning and
termination points for the rip rap revetment should be grouted and keyed back into
the existing slope to protect against erosion and scour The revetment should be
installed throughout the project limits to aid in the establishment of stream flow
equilibrium and provide for better performance of the protective revetment A
schematic sketch of the rip rap revetment is provided as Plate 4 of this report

LIMITATIONS
The recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon information
obtained from field observations and communication with residents at the site Variations
of conditions between and beyond our field observations may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until additional exploration is
underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate the
recommendations presented in this report
