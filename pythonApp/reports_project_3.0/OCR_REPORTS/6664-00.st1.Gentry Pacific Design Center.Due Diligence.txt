Project Considerations 
The Gentry Pacific Design Center is located along Nimitz Highway in Honolulu on 
the Island of Oahu. We understand it is desired to proceed with a due diligence study to 
assess the conditions of the existing building and evaluate the feasibility of future 
improvements. Details of the proposed improvements were not available at the time this 
letter report was prepared. 
Due to the stringent time constraints, due diligence study consisting only of site 
reconnaissance and literature research was conducted. In general, maps, reports and 
other records provided were used for this due diligence study. 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be

middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava 
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of the 
Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived 
from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below 
the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water 
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea. 
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava flows 
to be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils. During the Kaena stand of the sea 
(at approximately +90 feet MSL) of the Pleistocene Epoch, a delta of silt, sand, and gravel 
built out into the embayment near the project site. Therefore, the project site is generally 
underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments and lagoonal deposits. 
With the urbanization of the area, man-made fills were placed to reclaim marshy 
areas and lagoons. The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands 
with coralline fragments. The lower clayey material was deposited during periodic influxes 
of non-marine alluvial deposits that apparently smothered the coral reef development. 
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the 
area to its present form. 
Site Description 
The Gentry Pacific Design Center is located along Nimitz Highway between the 
excising City Mill home improvement store and K Mart store in Honolulu on the Island of 
Oahu.  The project site encompasses an area of about 5 acres. 
The existing two to three-story masonry building occupies a footprint of about 
90,140 square feet.  Based on our observations, the majority of the existing structure 
appeared to be in a good condition.  However, a diagonal crack on the masonry wall was 
observed inside of the electrical room on the eastern side of the existing structure.  The 
exposed ground floor slabs and tiles were also generally in a good condition with 
occasional cracks at localized areas.  We understand minor cracking of the tiles along the 
Iwilei Road side building entrance was observed after the Kiholo Bay/Hawi earthquake in 
October 2006.

Subsurface Conditions 
As mentioned previously, subsurface conditions at the project site were evaluated 
based on literature research of in-house and provided reports, plans, maps and other 
information.  Based on the geologic setting of the project site and our experience in the 
vicinity, we anticipate that the subsurface materials generally would consist of fill materials 
underlain by lagoonal deposits and coralline detritus. 
We envision the fill materials generally would extend to about 3 to 6 feet below the 
ground surface.  The consistency and composition of the fill materials are likely to vary 
significantly across the project site.  Below the fill materials, we anticipate that lagoonal 
deposits consisting of soft silty clays and loose silty sands may be present at the site.  The 
thickness of the lagoonal deposits may range from about 10 to over 40 feet. Coralline 
detritus materials consisting of medium dense to dense sands and gravel are generally 
anticipated below the lagoonal deposits.  It should be noted that localized layers of beach 
deposits, cinder sands, and alluvium may be present within the lagoonal deposits and 
coralline detritus. 
Groundwater should be anticipated near the ground surface at elevations ranging 
from about -2 to +3 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  Due to the proximity of the project site to 
Honolulu Harbor and its connection to the Pacific Ocean, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are expected to change with tidal fluctuations. Seasonal precipitation, 
storm surge condition, surface water runoff, and other factors may also influence the 
groundwater levels at the project site.

Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code, the project site may be subjected to 
seismic activity and should be evaluated for soil liquefaction potential. Soil liquefaction is a 
condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a 
substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting 
from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes.  Soils most susceptible to 
liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and loose silts with little 
cohesion. 
Based on the information available, the site may be underlain by soft and/or loose 
lagoonal deposits overlying coralline detritus at greater depths. The lagoonal deposits 
consisting of loose sands and gravel with varying amounts of silt with little cohesion may 
be potentially liquefiable during a seismic event.  Therefore, liquefaction potential should 
be evaluated for the proposed project after completion of a design-level field exploration 
program. 
It should be noted that the older building codes by which this building would have 
been design based on did not require liquefaction assessment and consideration for the 
design. However, additions to the existing building and/or new structures on the site will 
require an assessment of the liquefaction potential and design consideration. 
Foundations 
Details of the proposed improvements were not available at the time this letter 
report was prepared.  As mentioned previously, the site may be underlain by soft and/or 
loose lagoonal deposits.  Lagoonal deposits in the vicinity of the project site are 
compressible under moderate to heavy structural loads. 
In general, we envision that minor structures (up two to or three stories) planned 
may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous 
strip footings.  Shallow foundation may require over-excavation and provision of a 
stabilization layer consisting of crushed rock wrapped in filter fabric.  The requirement to 
provide a stabilization layer generally would be based on the type of structure planned, 
bottom of footing elevations, and subsurface conditions. 
In addition, depending on the consistencies and extent of loose and/or soft lagoonal 
deposits, a deep foundation system may be required to support new structures at the site. 
A deep foundation system may consist of micropiles, drilled shafts, micropiles, or similar 
systems.  The size and depth of the foundations would need to be determined based on 
the type of new structures planned and the result of a design-level field exploration 
program.

Contaminated Soils 
Our research indicates that contaminated soils may be present at the project site. 
Based on our experience in the project vicinity, petroleum hydrocarbons and related 
contaminants may be present, especially between depths of about 4 and 6 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  The depths where contaminated soils are commonly 
encountered generally reflect elevations of about -2 to +2 feet MSL.  Therefore, special 
treatment likely will be required for the handling and disposal of excavated contaminated 
soils and water during the project construction.  An environmental consultant should be 
retained and consulted with the specific requirements related to contaminated soils for this 
site. 
Additional Services 
The presence and extent of loose and/or soft lagoonal deposits should be further 
evaluated based on the actual subsurface conditions at the new structure locations and 
the extent of the structures proposed. Therefore, we recommend evaluating the 
subsurface conditions by conducting a design-level field exploration program consisting of 
drilling and sampling borings during the design phase when details of proposed 
improvements are established. 
Limitations 
It should be noted that it is not possible to observe all the site conditions based on a 
review of literature only. Variations of subsurface conditions from those described in this 
letter report may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become 
evident until further geotechnical engineering exploration is conducted at the site or 
construction is underway. A design-level geotechnical engineering exploration should be 
conducted to confirm and/or modify the preliminary recommendations provided herein. Our 
services on this project were performed in accordance with generally accepted standards 
of geotechnical engineering practice; no warranty is expressed or implied. 
Our geotechnical consultation services provided for this project was not intended to 
investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials existing at the site. It should be 
noted that the personnel used to conduct a geo-environmental exploration differ 
substantially from those applied in geotechnical engineering.  
This letter report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the owner in 
the evaluation of potential geotechnical risks of the project only. Therefore, this report may 
not contain sufficient data, or the proper information, to serve as the basis for detailed 
design and preparation of construction drawings. A design-level geotechnical exploration 
should be conducted for detailed design of the project.
