SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed East Lipoa Street Pavement Rehabilitation 
project generally encountered medium dense to very dense silty sands and gravel and 
hard sandy silts extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 5.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface. We encountered groundwater at depths of about 4 and 
2.8 feet below the existing pavement surface in Boring Nos. 3 and 4 drilled towards South 
Kihei Road at the time of our field exploration. 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed. Existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity are generally 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements is usually performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Our pavement condition survey at the project site indicated that the pavements 
between Liloa Drive and Piilani Highway under consideration for this project appear to 
exist in fair to good condition. Based on our engineering evaluation and construction 
considerations, we recommend rehabilitating these areas with a regular AC overlay 
consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of the existing pavements and replacing with 
2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
Since the areas requiring reconstruction due to structural distress amount to more 
than 15 percent of the total pavement area proposed for rehabilitation, the pavement 
reconstruction should be designed based on a pavement life of 30 years in accordance 
with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual prepared by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. In general, the distressed pavements may be reconstructed with a new pavement 
structural section consisting of 3 inches of AC on 6 inches of ACB over 6 inches of AB. It 
should be noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the 
actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design 
section is adequate. 
A layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 or equivalent) on a 
layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the 
new pavement structural section. The reinforcing geogrid would serve to enhance the 
R-value of the subgrade materials by providing stabilization and reinforcement to the 
underlying base/subbase/subgrade materials.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the East Lipoa Street Pavement Rehabilitation 
(South Kihei Road to Piilani Highway) project in the Kihei area on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project is along East Lipoa Street between 
South Kihei Road and Piilani Highway for a total distance of approximately 0.6 miles in 
the Kihei area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The approximate limits of the roadway 
rehabilitation project are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity will be 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements will be performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials.  We 
understand that the proposed construction will be a federally funded project. Therefore, 
a pavement justification report conforming to the requirements and guidelines of the 
State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will be required. The new pavements for 
the project will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement 
Design Manual dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located 
between these two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting 
the two volcanic domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing East Lipoa Street within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway between Piilani Highway and South Kihei Road except for the 
additional turn lanes at the intersection areas and shoulder parking lanes. The 
approximate project limits of the roadway are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short-cut 
grass, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils along the shoulder areas below 
the vegetation generally consist of brown silty sands and sandy silts with gravel patches 
is some areas. The existing East Lipoa Street roadway alignment gently slopes down 
from east to west within the limits of the project.  
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in June 2016 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under 
consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following 
subsections. It should be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to areas where 
the pavement deterioration is believed to be caused by failures in the underlying base,

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the existing pavements for the portion of East 
Lipoa Street between Piilani Highway and Liloa Drive falls into this category and 
appear to be generally fair to good with only a few minor signs of structural defects 
observed. Overall pavement deterioration is restricted to light longitudinal cracking 
of low severity along a paving joint. The ride characteristic of the pavement was 
generally fair.  
2.2.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
In general, the existing pavements for the portion of East Lipoa Street between 
Liloa Drive and South Kihei Road falls under this category and was observed to be 
in poor condition with moderately to highly distressed areas. Alligator and alligator 
block cracking were observed to be widespread and generally moderate to high in 
severity. The poor areas consist of elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator 
block cracking with depressions of ¼ to 1 inch, rutting and some shoving of the 
pavement within the tire lanes. Potholes patched with AC material were observed at 
various locations along the alignment. The ride characteristic of the roadway was 
observed to be generally fair to irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor 
pavements. The approximate limits of these poor areas requiring pavement 
reconstruction are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2.

3.4 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our evaluation, it 
is our opinion that pavement areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an 
AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of the existing pavements and 
replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up 
by the cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not 
preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the contractor 
should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing begins.  Finally, 
vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed immediately prior 
to paving.

3.5 
Design Pavement Section 
As discussed in the “Existing Site/Pavement Conditions” section herein, some of 
the observed distresses along the roadway are likely caused by failures in the 
underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. In general, these areas should be 
reconstructed with a new pavement section in lieu of an AC overlay section. In addition, 
these distressed areas should be repaired prior to the AC overlay work for the entire 
roadway under consideration. 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that some portion of the 
roadway alignment under consideration for this project is generally underlain by sandy 
soils with little or no cohesion.  As a result, the subgrade may not be able to compact 
into a dense mass to receive placement of the asphalt concrete base (ACB) layer.  In 
addition, other areas of the roadway alignment may be underlain by sandy silts with a 
significant amount of fines content.  We understand that placement of ACB directly on 
these clayey subgrade is not recommended.  Based on our evaluation and these 
construction considerations, we recommend providing a layer of aggregate subbase 
between the upper ACB layer and the underlying subgrade soils.  The aggregate 
subbase will serve to provide confinement of the sandy soils as well as a granular 
surface for the placement of the ACB. 
Based on the assumptions and the construction considerations described above, 
we have determined the following flexible pavement design section for the proposed 
East Lipoa Street Pavement Rehabilitation project.  Detailed calculations and analyses 
of the pavement design are presented on Plate C-3.1 in Appendix C. 
East Lipoa Street (South Kihei Road to Piilani Highway) 
  3.5 Inches Asphaltic Concrete 
  5.5 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity)

22.0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade

The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes. The 
vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute.

Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform 
to Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent

Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt

require reconstruction with new pavement sections. In addition, a contingency fund 
should be allocated in the construction budget for these possible extra costs. 
3.6 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling 
and sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths 
of about 5 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. The rock material left in the 
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock 
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
