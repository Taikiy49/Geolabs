Introduction
In accordance with the scope of service outlined in our fee proposal dated
March 3 2008 we researched existing available subsurface information along the
pipeline alignment This report summarizes the findings from our research performed
for the Cold Seawater Distribution Pipeline System project in the Downtown Honolulu
area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii and presents generalized subsurface profiles along
the alignment including generalized soil properties The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This information is to assist in the planning of the final pipeline alignment to take
advantage of potential better ground conditions and also provide a more realistic cost
estimate The findings presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The seawater air conditioning project will utilize cold seawater pumped from a
depth of 1 600 feet below the sea through a pipeline distribution system to buildings in
the Downtown Honolulu area The chilled water will provide air conditioning service to
the buildings The pipeline distribution system will start from the chiller station in the
Kakaako area and extend mauka through existing downtown streets to the various
buildings that will be served The pipe will be up to 42 inches in diameter and will be
installed in a cut and cover trench with a minimum 3 foot cover The system will require
equivalent sized return pipes however it is not known if they will be co located or
placed in separate trenches
The geology in the downtown area is complex with a mixture of soft loose harbor
deposits hard coral formation volcanic tuff and rock and overlying alluvium
Groundwater levels in the southern portion are shallow which will require dewatering for
the pipe installation In addition the corridor is congested with numerous existing
utilities

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of weathered remnants of the extinct
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active
and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The proposed corridor is located along the southern flank of Koolau and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of Pearl Harbor
and the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions along the majority of the
alignments are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and
related deposits laid during high stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch which
are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this
area is Koolau Basalt

The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene
Epoch and the early Pleistocene Epoch by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main
shield building activities ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence
from drilled wells indicates that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet
since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across the mouth of the bays The
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments
accumulated in the lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both
higher and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quit waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted
in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal
sedimentary unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the

volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
post erosional events i e those following the cessation of shield building were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene Epoch sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder These deposits of
pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock
One of these eruptions Aliamanu caused diversion of Halawa Stream to the north of its
original location into the area of what are now the Southeast and Magazine Lochs of
Pearl Harbor during the Kaena 95 feet high stand of the sea
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the deep valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so
sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in
thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in the areas which were formerly
valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have
brought the Honolulu area to its present form A great many of those projects were
originally constructed for commercial residential and or government development
Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural
loads

Subsurface Conditions
The soils and rocks within the project area may be grouped into several major
stratigraphic units The engineering characteristics of these units appear to be
reasonably consistent These soil units were used on the maps in the body of this
report and are identified as follows

Based on our review of the available data and previous experience in the areas
of the proposed alignment the following discussions summarize the general
characteristics of the materials that may be encountered along the route
Coral Algal Deposits Qcrs
This unit consists of fossil remnants or coral algal reefs and their related detrital
debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant portion of
any coral rock assemblage
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral debris consisting of
gravel sand and silt sized clastic fragments Some of the coral fragments were
dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented and or uncemented fine
sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal deposits and the degree
of cementation frequently present this formation generally has high in situ strength
characteristics Structurally the coral algal reef deposits are capable of supporting
heavy foundation loads Difficult excavation can be encountered in areas with a
high degree of cementation

The competent nature of the material may reduce the need for elaborate shoring
systems but poorly cemented areas and lagoonal deposits may be less stable
Again due to the granular nature of the coralline materials they are generally
highly permeable and capable of transmitting large volumes of water In general
any excavation encountering groundwater will require dewatering systems capable
of handling large volumes of water
In this report and the generalized subsurface profiles presented on Plates 3 1
through 3 5 the following subunits of coral algal deposits are used
This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations
The soils are generally medium dense to dense sandy gravels and
gravelly sands Silts and clays are only present in minor or trace amounts
Detritus may also be weakly cemented
This formation is composed of corals and calcareous algae in their original
growth positions or detrital deposits which have become moderately to
well cemented Excavation through massive reef deposits may be difficult
c Lagoonal Reef Formation
This subunit consists of dendritic or branching corals in their original
growth forms The interstices between the branches of coral have been
filled with a fine sandy organic silt Conventional excavation methods may
be used in lagoonal deposit however shoring may be required due to the
weak structure of the formation
d Filled Cavity
This term is used to describe what appears to be a cavity in a reef
formation which has been filled by loose sand and soft silts

Older Alluvium QTao
The Older Alluvium is assigned the position of being the second oldest unit in the
stratigraphic column of the project area even though some of the coral algal reef
deposits may be intercalated with the alluvial deposits
This unit generally overlies the Koolau Basalt and consists of terrigenous sediments
which have been transported by stream action from the upper reaches of the
streams The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard brown clayey silts and
silty clays Gravel lenses representing buried stream channels are sometimes
encountered Portions of the Older Alluvium unit contain some weathered basalt
boulders and cobbles These coarse grained parts of the unit may also contain
artesian water from the basal fresh water system or as a sub aquifer
The old lagoonal deposits of the Older Alluvium unit consist of marine or lagoonal
sediments which have been desiccated and consolidated into very stiff to hard gray
clayey silts
The Older Alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength which leads to a
relatively high bearing capacity Frequently these materials show a moderate to
high shrink well potential however this is not considered a constraint for this
project Conventional excavation methods may generally applied to these
materials
The materials are generally competent with respect to excavation wall stability The
contractor should be responsible for shoring systems and applicable safety
requirements

The clayey nature of the Older Alluvium materials gives them low permeability and
generally restricts their ability to transmit groundwater Dewatering in these
materials is generally able to be handled with simple pumping systems unless
granular areas such as old stream courses are encountered
Cinder Deposit Qov
This mapping unit represents deposits of scoriaceous sand fragments These
sands are generally composed of medium to coarse grained fragments derived
from the formation of Cinder cones The consistency of the sand is generally on
the order of medium dense to dense Some areas may be weakly to moderately
cemented
Cinder deposits can typically be excavated by conventional methods however
difficulty may be encountered with cemented horizons The relatively competent
nature of the material may reduce the need for elaborate shoring systems but
poorly cemented areas may be less stable
Due to the granular nature of the cinder materials they are generally moderately to
highly permeable and capable of transmitting significant volumes of water In
general excavations encountering groundwater may require dewatering systems
capable of handling relatively large volumes of water
Recent Alluvium Qa
The Recent Alluvium consists mainly of very soft to medium stiff brown to dark
gray clayey silt and organic silt with varying amounts of sand and infrequent
pockets of gravel and cobbles
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low energy
environment such as an estuary or bay Frequently the soils assigned to this unit
accumulated very rapidly under water and have never been exposed Therefore
there is a tendency to encounter underconsolidated sediments which settle under
the weight of a minimum overburden or structural loading

Included in this unit are subunits of localized thin layers about 10 feet of lagoonal
marine sediments consisting mostly of very soft to medium stiff gray clayey silts
These gray silts are identified as lagoonal facies subunits of the young alluvial unit
The soils assigned to this unit are generally considered to be poorly to very poorly
suited for use as support for foundation loads due to their low in situ strength
characteristics and the tendency for settlement to occur in structures overlying soils
of this unit However excavation is typically easy but extensive shoring may be
required
Since the Recent Alluvium materials are encountered at low elevations dewatering
is almost always required for infrastructure work in these materials Caution is
required for dewatering work in the recent alluvium since the lowering of the water
table will increase the effective overburden pressure and can result in significant
ground settlements in the adjacent areas

Man Made Fill Qf
This mapping unit represents low areas which were filled during urbanization The
majority of these low areas is underlain at relatively shallow depths by Recent
Alluvium and was originally marshy areas that were filled to create fast land A
small part of the filled lands are underlain by coral
The quality and composition of the fill materials are variable These fills were
frequently placed without control Therefore it is difficult to make any general
statement regarding their engineering properties or construction in these areas
Groundwater in the fill areas is generally expected to occur as a basal water table
near sea level However higher perched water tables may be encountered
The capability of these man made fills to support structural loads is partly
dependent upon the design load and the quality of the fill and its placement and is
partly dependent upon the materials underlying the fills The actual fill materials
may be of suspect quality Excavation is typically easy but extensive shoring may
be required
Since the Fill materials are encountered at low elevations dewatering is almost
always required for infrastructure work in these materials Caution is required for
