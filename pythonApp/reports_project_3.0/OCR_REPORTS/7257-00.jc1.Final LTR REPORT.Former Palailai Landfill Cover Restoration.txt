This letter report presents our evaluation of the former Palailai landfill cover 
restoration project in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering recommendations 
resulting from our engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for the 
slope stability and site grading only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at the former Palailai Quarry. After closure, the quarry 
site was used as a municipal sanitary landfill, which was eventually terminated around 
1988. Based on the information provided, we understand that the landfill site was capped 
with a 20 mil PVC liner covered with coralline materials and topsoil for a total of about 
3 feet of final cover. The surface was initially graded to direct drainage to a concrete lined 
ditch on the eastern side of the landfill, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Since the closure of the landfill, the materials within the landfill have settled, 
resulting in an alteration of the drainage pattern resulting in storm water run-off ponding in 
low-lying areas of the site. It is proposed to import additional fill materials to re-grade the 
landfill cover and to restore the original drainage pattern towards the concrete lined 
drainage channel on the east. Based on the information provided, we understand that the 
proposed restoration earthwork will consist of 3 to 5 feet of new fills to achieve the design 
finished grade. Due to the presence of highly compressive landfill materials, it is desired to 
evaluate the slope stability at the site and to estimate the amount of future settlement 
induced by the new fill planned.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger Koolau 
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms 
the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while Koolau 
was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking 
against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred 
to as the Schofield Plateau.

Puu Makakilo is a cinder and spatter cone that was built by one or more eruptive 
events, which produced large quantities of pyroclastic material such as cinder, spatter, and 
ash. In some areas, welding and/or hypothermal action has fused or cemented the 
pyroclastic material into weak and strong rock, (e.g., tuff). 
In addition to the pyroclastic material, the eruptions produced lava flows of very 
dense basalt, which are interbedded with the pyroclastic materials. Structurally, 
Puu Makakilo is a heterogenous mixture of interbedded cinder, tuff, and dense basalt. The 
units are discontinuous and difficult to differentiate on an areal basis in the field. 
In-situ chemical weathering of the pyroclastic material and basalts has produced a 
surface layer of residual soil, which mantles most of the project site. This residual soil 
grades rapidly to the underlying basalt formation.

survey data, as summarized in Table 1 for reference. In general, the slope stability model 
was established and back-calculated by simulating the settlements under the fill 
surcharge.

formation. A typical cross-section was established from the topographic survey data, to 
depict the soil profile, as shown on Plate 3. Based on the magnitude of the ground 
settlement estimated by the topographic survey maps, it appears that the majority of the 
ground settlement occurred between 1988 and 2003.

Additional fills of 5 to 15 feet added to a near-constant gradient at approximately 6

Based on the results of the slope stability and settlement analyses, we believe that 
the main concern of restoring this former landfill site is likely the potential for continuous 
ground deformation, which could again alter the run-off water drainage pattern in the 
future. The ground settlement is induced from highly compressible landfill waste materials 
under the fill surcharge. Based on our engineering analyses, up to about 6 feet of ground 
settlement was estimated due to the restoration operation, which will involve about 3 to 
5 feet of new fills to restore the ground surface drainage pattern. 
In general, the computed factors of safety for the slope stability are greater than 2.0 
for both global and local critical slip circle rotational slope failure mechanisms. Typical 
slope rotation critical slip circles are illustrated on Plates 5 through 8.  It should be noted 
that localized critical slip circle near the toe of the slope may have a factor of safety of 
about 1.5 due to shallow depth of rotation failure. A minimum factor of safety of 1.5 is 
generally used in geotechnical design. 
Based on the above discussions, we believe that the proposed filling restoration 
operation would not likely result in slope instability at the former Palailai landfill. As a 
precautionary measure, the filling area and temporary stockpiles should be kept away from 
the slope toe, to reduce the risk of localized slope failure/raveling. In addition, provisions 
should be given for periodic grade restoration, to maintain the ground surface drainage

SITE PREPARATION 
In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and grubbed 
thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense materials, 
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. As an 
alternative, a layer of geogrid, such as Tensile TX140 or equivalent, may be placed on the 
existing ground where soft and yielding areas occur, to provide a relative stable  ground 
condition and minimize over-excavation to expose the PVC liner. 
Prior to filling, the existing ground exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches (where possible), moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where shrinkage cracks 
are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend thoroughly moistening the 
soil to close the cracks or preparing the subgrade again. Saturation and subsequent 
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require 
over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fills. The 
excavated soft soils may be re-used as fill, provided that they meet fill material 
requirements. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. 
FILL PLACEMENT AND FILL SLOPES 
Imported general fill materials needed to fill the site may consist of materials with a 
low to moderate expansion potential, such as the soils generated from Kapolei 
Interchange cut areas.  Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with 
particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension and should be free of deleterious 
materials.  The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 
8 or higher and a swell potential of not more than 2 percent when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. 
As an alternative, recycled asphalt material may be utilized as imported general fill. 
Large sizes of the recycle asphalt concrete material may be crushed or broken down by 
the grading construction equipment to no more than 6 inches in maximum dimension. 
General fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of

the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during site grading to assist 
the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture 
content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be 
applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the specified 
compaction. 
Fill slopes constructed of the processed imported general fill materials should be 
designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. For fill 
slopes greater than 30 feet in vertical height, we recommend providing slope benches at 
maximum 30-foot vertical height intervals. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V 
should be keyed and benched into the existing benches to provide stability of the new fill 
against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in 
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. 
Fill slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should be constructed by overfilling and 
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling 
of slopes in excess of 5 feet in height is not acceptable. In the event that over-cutting of a 
slope occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted away 
from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to 
reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes. 
We also recommend placing hydro-mulching on the finished grade, to promote 
vegetation growth and minimize potential erosion from run-off surface water.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the available topographic maps and as-built information.
