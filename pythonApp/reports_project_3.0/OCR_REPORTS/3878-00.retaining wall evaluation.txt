INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Retaining Wall Evaluation at 01 2 located in the Lanikai
area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration a video tape of the water line repair
provided by Pacific Acquisition laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of retaining wall site grading and concrete
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located south of the intersection of Aalapapa Drive and Mokulua
Drive in the Lanikai area of Kailua on the island of Oahu Hawaii The existing concrete
driveway is supported by a concrete retaining wall up to approximately 8 feet high
During our initial site visit a lateral separation of 1 to 3 inches was noted between
the concrete retaining wall and driveway In addition the ground adjacent to the bottom
of the retaining wall appeared to slump downward away from the retaining wall These
observations indicate that the retaining wall has experienced lateral movements and may
continue to move both laterally and vertically in the future
Based on discussions with the property owner we understand that a water line
owned by Bellows Air Force Base runs near the subject retaining wall We also
understand that a break in this water line was discovered on around July of 1994 The
repair work performed for the break in this water line was videotaped on and
forwarded to our office by Pacific Acquisition on for review It should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3878 00 Page 3
be noted that we received this new information subsequent to our field exploration Since
the repair of the water line we understand that the water line has been abandoned by the
Air Force
It was suspected that the break in the water line and or the work involved in repair
of the water line may have caused the distress in the existing retaining wall An
evaluation of the existing retaining wall condition and possible causes to the distress and
recommendations pertaining to possible stabilization or reconstruction of the wall was
desired

surface

an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their engineering
properties

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their original shield
outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering
process has formed a mantle of saprolite In general saprolite is composed mainly of
silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite
grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth Land developments in recent
years have brought the area to its present form

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located in the Lanikai area of Kailua on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The retaining wall is located along the northern side of the property as shown on
the Schematic Site Plan Plate 2
At the time of our field exploration a separation of up to 3 inches between the
concrete retaining wall and driveway was observed The separation was patched with
cement in some areas Horizontal cracks on the face of the retaining wall were also
noted In addition the ground adjacent to the down slope side of the wall showed signs
of slumping away from the retaining wall A vertical movement of up to about 2 5 feet was
measured at the time of our field exploration The crack in the ground up to about 12
inches wide caused by the slumping extended down to about 3 feet below the ground
surface Although slumping of the ground was observed along nearly the entire length of
the retaining wall the most severe movements were found at the eastern end of the wall
The existing house is built on a hillside and the area slopes relatively steeply
down northward The surrounding area is heavily vegetated with grasses shrubs and
small trees Information regarding elevations at the project site were not provided at the
time this report was prepared
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths ranging from
about 6 5 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Schematic Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered fill of up to about 13 feet below the
existing ground surface consisting of various materials In Boring No 1 fills consisting of
loose to medium dense gravelly sand and clayey sand extended to 6 5 feet below the
GEOLABS HAWAII

existing concrete driveway The boring encountered the top of the retaining wall footing
at 6 5 feet below the ground surface In Boring No 2 the fill extended down to
approximately 10 feet below the existing ground surface The fill consisted of medium
stiff sandy silt and medium dense basalt gravel Below the fill layer very stiff saprolite
consisting of clayey silt was encountered to the maximum depth explored of 15 feet below
the existing ground surface In Boring No 3 the fill material generally varied from
medium stiff sandy silt to medium dense silty sand and gravelly sand The fill extended to
the maximum depth explored of 13 feet below the ground surface
Subsequent to our field exploration a videotape of the water line repair at the
northeast corner of the site was provided to us by Pacific Acquisition The videotape
showed that a trench was opened along the northern side of the retaining wall near
Boring Nos 2 and 3 Upon review and evaluation of the videotape we believe the fill
materials encountered in Boring Nos 2 and 3 may be backfill materials placed during the
water line repair work In addition the face of the open trench appeared to consist of
natural saprolite material based on the videotape
Groundwater was not encountered during our field exploration However it should
be noted that water levels at the project site may be influenced by seasonal precipitation
and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration encountered fill of up to about 13 feet below the
existing ground surface The fill materials generally consisted of medium stiff silt and
loose to medium dense sands and gravels In Boring No 2 saprolite consisting of very
stiff clayey silt was encountered below the fill layer at about 10 feet and extended to the
maximum depth explored of 15 feet below the existing ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3878 00 Page 7
Subsequent to our field exploration a videotape of the water line repair at the
northeast corner of the site was provided to us by Pacific Acquisition The videotape
showed that a trench was opened along the northern side of the retaining wall near
Boring Nos 2 and 3 The videotape indicated that the open trench was located less than
about two feet from the northern face of the wall Based on the video tape the depth of
the trench appeared to extend down to about 5 to 7 feet below the original ground
surface Ponded water was also observed at the bottom of the trench in the videotape In
addition about 1 foot of soil against the northern face of the retaining wall appeared to
have been removed
Based on this new information and our field exploration we believe that the
breakage of the water line near the retaining wall and or the work involved in the repair of
the water line were a major cause of the significant distress to the existing retaining wall
at the site Since sufficient details of the wall design and construction were not
available the feasibility of strengthening the existing wall could not be evaluated
Therefore we recommend that the existing retaining wall be demolished and replaced
with a new retaining wall founded on the stiff natural soil below the fill The
reconstruction would also allow recompaction and proper placement of fill behind the
wall
Detailed discussions and recommendations for the new retaining wall design site
grading pavements and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the
following sections of this report

Based on the existing subsurface conditions we recommend that the existing fill
material at the wall location be removed to expose the natural saprolite material The
new retaining wall footing should bear on the saprolite found at greater depths Site
grading involving excavation and backfilling of about 10 to 15 feet may be anticipated
for the reconstruction of the retaining wall Items of grading that are addressed in the
following subsections include the following 1 Site Preparation 2 Fill Material and
Placement and 3 Excavations

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Any unsuitable materials such as old wall
footings concrete pavement vegetation and deleterious materials should be
_ removed and disposed of properly off site
The original ground should then be scarified to a minimum depth of 6 inches
moisture conditioned and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 Any
loose and or soft spots encountered at the subgrade level within the fill area
should be removed and replaced with compacted non expansive select material
Fill Material and Placement
It is anticipated that off site borrow will be required for the fill material Imported
fill material should consist of non expansive select granular materials less than
3 inches in maximum size with less than 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve
The material should be tested and approved by the geotechnical engineer prior
to transportation to the site
The fill should be placed in maximum 10 inch thick loose level lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91

Based on the existing topography and the subsurface conditions encountered in

New Retaining Wall
Based on the condition of the existing retaining wall we recommend that a new
retaining wall bearing on saprolite found at depths of about 10 feet below the existing
footing be constructed The new retaining wall of up to about 18 feet in height may be
required to replace the existing retaining wall Items of new retaining wall design that
are addressed in the following subsections include the following 1 Wall Foundations
2 Lateral Earth Pressures and 3 Subdrainage
Wall Foundations
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for design of wall footings bearing on the saprolite soils encountered at
greater depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3878 00 Page 10
The existing fill materials found at shallow depths should be removed to expose
the underlying saprolite soils We recommend that footings be embedded to a
minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent saprolite soils In
addition where retaining walls are located adjacent to or on slopes the walls
should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of at least 8 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the
slope
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for wall footing settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f for level
areas This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted or
undisturbed Unless covered by pavements the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected The passive resistance should be
reduced to account for sloping ground conditions A coefficient of friction of
0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the saprolite soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footings bear on the natural saprolite If
soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The
over excavation should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3878 00 Page 11
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should be
extended down to underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with procedures described in
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
competency of the foundation bearing materials and the required embedment
depths

Subdrainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall

a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to paved landscape areas
Drainage
The finished grades surrounding the new retaining wall should be sloped to shed
water away from wall foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the retaining wall should also be avoided Planters next to the
retaining wall should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water
infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above retaining wall recommendations since ponded water may cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the subgrade soils The wall footing
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 6 5 to 15 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Schematic Site
Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted and a portable drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
