SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the site encountered a 9-inch thick Portland cement 
concrete pavement underlain by very dense gravel and sand fill material over medium 
dense beach deposits and very loose lagoonal deposits that extended to depths of about 
24 to 25 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The very loose lagoonal deposits are 
susceptible to liquefaction during a moderate earthquake event.  The borings then 
encountered medium hard to hard coral formation and medium dense coralline detritus 
extending to the maximum depth explored.  We encountered groundwater in both of the 
borings at depths varying from approximately 6.1 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  
We understand that in-place soil improvement consisting of vibro-replacement 
stone columns is being considered as an option to mitigate liquefaction potential at the 
site.  Since vibration is used to install the stone columns, vibration during the installation in 
addition to further densification of the existing soils below and around the existing building 
structure from the vibration could cause significant structural damage to the existing 
building structure.  It is our opinion that in-place soil improvement using vibro-replacement 
stone columns at the site is not a viable option due to the potential structural damage that 
could occur during the installation operations.  Therefore, we recommend supporting the 
building addition on a deep foundation system consisting of cased micropiles. 
The cased micropile foundation system should have a permanent casing and grout 
bulb diameters of at least 9.625 inches and 8.5 inches, respectively.  The permanent 
casing (unbonded zone) should extend to a depth of about 25 feet below the existing 
pavement surface where the top of coral formation is anticipated.  We recommend utilizing 
three classes of micropiles that are differentiated by their allowable compressive load 
capacities.  The three micropile classes are as follows:   
Type I with an allowable compressive load capacity of 50 kips and bonded 
zone length of 11 feet. 
Type II with an allowable compressive load capacity of 90 kips and 
bonded zone length of 18 feet. 
Type III micropile with an allowable compressive load capacity of 120 kips 
and bonded zone length of 23 feet.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, 
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch.  The Koolau Volcanic 
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows. 
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials 
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor.  
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising 
about 100 feet above the present sea level.   
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits.  Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have 
added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration.

2.4.1 Earthquake and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+).

2.4.2 Liquefaction 
Based on the 2012 International Building Code, the project site may be subject to 
seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to 
be evaluated.  
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the site below the 9-inch thick Portland cement 
concrete pavement consists of very dense gravel and sand fill material over medium 
dense beach deposits and very loose lagoonal deposits that extended to depths of 
about 24 to 25 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Medium hard to hard coral 
formation and medium dense coralline detritus was encountered below the lagoonal 
deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of about 63 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. We encountered groundwater in both of the borings at depths 
varying from approximately 6.1 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
As mentioned above, the very loose lagoonal deposits are susceptible to 
liquefaction during a moderate earthquake event.  We understand that in-place soil 
improvement consisting of vibro-replacement stone columns is being considered for the 
project to mitigate liquefaction potential at the site.  Since vibration is used to install the 
stone columns, vibration during the installation of the vibro-replacement stone columns 
could potentially cause structural damage to the existing building structure since the 
new addition adjoins the existing building structure.  In addition, the vibration during the 
installation process could further densify the existing soils below and around the existing 
building structure resulting in foundation settlement that could cause significant 
structural damage to the existing building structure.  Based on the above discussion, we 
believe that in-place soil improvement using vibro-replacement stone columns at the 
site is not a viable option.  Therefore, a deep foundation system consisting of cased 
micropiles with a casing outside diameter (OD) of about 9.625 inches and bond lengths 
of 11 to 23 feet should be used to support the building addition. 
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for 
the design of building and minor equipment pad foundations, site grading, pavements, 
and underground utilities for the project are presented in the following sections.

allowing the use of shallow foundations.  However, since vibration is used to install the 
stone columns, vibration during the installation of the vibro-replacement stone columns 
could potentially cause structural damage to the existing building structure since the 
new addition adjoins the existing building structure.  In addition, the vibration during the 
installation process could further densify the existing soils below and around the existing 
building structure resulting in foundation settlement that could cause significant 
structural damage to the existing building structure.  Therefore, we believe that in-place 
soil improvement using vibro-replacement stone columns at the site is not a viable 
option.

addition on a deep foundation system consisting of cased micropiles. Detailed

3.2

In general, the cased micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter 
(usually less than 12 inches), drilled and grouted, pile with steel casing and reinforcing. 
The micropile foundation is typically constructed by drilling a hole (with casing), placing 
reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the hole.  Micropiles are desirable because 
they can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil 
types and ground conditions.  In addition, the micropile installation generally causes 
minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the adjacent soils, and the environment. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend utilizing a cased micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of 8.5 inches 
to support the proposed addition. The permanent casing for the cased micropile system 
should have an outside diameter (OD) of about 9.625 inches.  The permanent casing 
(unbonded zone) should extend to the top of the coral formation encountered at an 
average depth of about 25 feet below the existing ground surface.  The minimum 
thickness of the permanent casing should be about 0.545 inches. 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, we 
recommend utilizing three classes of micropiles differentiated by their allowable 
compressive load capacities.  Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the 
project site, we recommend utilizing a Type I micropile with an allowable compressive 
load capacity of 50 kips (25 tons), a Type II micropile with an allowable compressive 
load capacity of up to 90 kips (45 tons), and a Type III micropile with an allowable

allowable compressive load capacities of the micropiles are for supporting

3.1.3 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the new structure may be resisted by the lateral load 
capacity of the micropiles.  The lateral load resistance of micropiles is a function of 
the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, allowable deflection at 
the top of the pile, and the induced moment in the pile.  We understand the lateral 
load analyses will be performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which 
is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded pile program 
originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin.  The soil parameters to be 
used for lateral load analyses of the micropiles are presented on Plates 3.1 and 3.2.   
Lateral loads imposed on the new structure also may be resisted by passive earth 
pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade beams and 
by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams. 
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade 
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 330 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf) above the groundwater level and 140 pcf below 
the groundwater level.  The passive pressure resistance should be fully mobilized 
with a deflection of 0.5 inch provided that the bottom of the pile caps and grade 
beams are embedded a maximum of 24 inches below the finished grades. 
Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by shear resistance between 
the sides of the pile caps and grade beams and the adjacent soils.  A side shear 
resistance of up to 150 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for transient 
loads such as wind or seismic forces only. 
3.1.4 Micropile Foundation Settlements 
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic 
compression of the pile member and subgrade response.  We estimate the total 
settlement of the micropile-supported foundations to be less than 0.75 inch with 
differential settlements between columns supported on micropiles not exceeding 
about one-half of the total settlement.  We believe that these settlements are 
essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.

3.1.5 Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the compressive and uplift load capacities of the micropiles 
is highly dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed 
by the contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, the compressive and uplift load 
capacity of the micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and 
micropile foundation systems.  In order to determine whether the contractor’s 
methods of micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate 
compressive load capacity, we recommend performing a pre-production 
compressive load test at the site.  In general, the purpose of the pre-production 
load test on a micropile is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grout injection 
To evaluate the degree of long-term settlements of the micropile 
In general, the pre-production compressive load test should be performed in 
accordance with ASTM D1143.  Based on experience, we believe that the load test 
should be conducted at least 7 days after completion of the micropile installation to 
allow the grout adequate time to cure.   
For the compressive load test, a minimum of four additional micropiles may be used 
for reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre-production load test 
micropile.  The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load 
test micropile to provide adequate reaction in uplift (to be determined by the 
contractor). 
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the 
allowable compression design load of approximately 120 kips.  In addition, we 
recommend holding the micropile for a minimum of 4 hours at 200 percent of the 
allowable design load depending on the recorded movements of the load test 
micropile.  Please note that the pre-production load test is an integral part of the 
design of the micropile foundation system.

TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. tested selected soil samples to determine the 
chloride and sulfate contents of the in-situ soils.  The chloride/sulfate contents were 
determined in general accordance with the EPA 300.0 test method. Results of the soil 
analyses conducted on selected soil samples are summarized in Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion to metallic objects.  Based on the resistivity value of the in-situ 
soil encountered, the near-surface soil may be classified as moderately corrosive for 
buried metallic structures.  Due to the moderately corrosive nature of the soils, we 
recommend deducting a minimum of about 0.10 inch from the thickness of the steel 
casing.   
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil.  In 
general, soils with a chloride content of less than 300 ppm, sulfate content of less than 
2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered “non-corrosive” to 
underground concrete pipelines and structures.  Based on the test results, the in-situ 
soils generally may be considered “non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines 
and structures; therefore, we recommend using either Type I or II cement. 
It should be noted that because some of the underground utility lines and 
structures may be embedded within the imported fill material, we recommend 
conducting additional testing on the imported fill material to evaluate the corrosivity of 
the imported fills.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2. The borings extended to a depth of about 
63 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.   Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.   
The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for 
the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society 
for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the 
Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative 
quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks 
and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plates B-1 and B-2. 
One Consolidation test (ASTM D2435) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of 
the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-3. 
Four Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength 
of the coral formation encountered. Uniaxial compressive strength test results are 
presented on Plate B-4. 
Two pH (ASTM G51) and two Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57) tests were 
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-5. 
One Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and one Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests 
were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil sample obtained 
from our field exploration.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-5.
