INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the wash rack facilities at Buildings 2620 and 2640 of Schofield Barracks
located in the district of Wahiawa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project sites are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for foundation design input and may
not be appropriate to form a basis in the preparation of construction cost estimates nor
for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project sites are located in the district of Wahiawa on the island of Oahu
Hawaii It is proposed to construct new wash rack facilities at Buildings 2620 and 2640
within Schofield Barracks
Based on the current design concepts each wash rack facility will consist of a
concrete vehicle wash slab an oil water separator within a sedimentation basin and new
asphaltic concrete pavement It is our understanding that the wash racks will be
subjected to vehicle loads contributed by Humvees tractor trailers forklifts and five ton
trucks
Based on the current design concepts 35 by 50 feet concrete wash slabs are
planned at both wash rack facilities Sidewalks with widths of approximately 10 feet are
also planned along the entire length of the wash slab as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The sedimentation basin will occupy an area of approximately 10 by 62 feet with depths
extending to 9 feet We understand that the concrete wash slabs and pavement areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3107 00 Page 3
will be constructed near the existing grades therefore with the exception of the
excavation for the sedimentation basins we anticipate that site grading for both projects
will be minimal

The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the subsurface conditions at the approximate locations of the wash rack facilities The
subsurface information obtained was used to develop an idealized soil data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of the
wash rack facilities

11 feet below the existing ground surface

Engineer of our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration to evaluate the classification and engineering properties of the
materials encountered

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 4 borings to depths of
approximately 111 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of
the 4 borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
On the basis of our exploration the subsurface conditions for the wash rack facility
at Building 2640 consist of about 2 inches of asphalt concrete pavement overlying about
15 inches of medium dense sandy gravel fill Underlying the fill material residual soils

consisting of clayey silt extending to a depth of approximately 7 feet Below the residual
soil very stiff sandy silt was encountered to a depth of approximately 111 feet the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results the near surface clayey
silt soils encountered in our borings were generally very stiff and are suitable for
foundation support of the wash rack facilities
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3107 00 Page 6
Based on our laboratory test results the on site clayey silt soils generally
encountered at shallow depths have a relatively high in situ moisture content The high
moisture content of the near surface soils may pose difficulties for compaction during
construction Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation
For the design of vehicle wash slab we recommend that 6 inches of Portland
Cement Concrete overlying 6 inches of base course be used
For the design of new pavements we recommend that flexible pavements
consisting of 2 inches of asphalt concrete over 6 inches of base course be used
Shallow foundations may be used for the retaining walls for the sedimentation
basin An allowable bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be
used for the foundation design For surcharge loading contributed by vehicular traffic we
recommend that additional vertical surcharge pressure be added to the lateral earth
pressure
Recommendations addressing the above geotechnical considerations and other
geotechnical aspects of the wash rack facilities are discussed in the following sections of
this report
Wash Rack Concrete Vehicle Slab
In consideration of the generally very stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the
site we believe that a 6 inch concrete slab bearing on 6 inch base course material may
be used for the design of the concrete vehicle slab We also recommend that the slabs
be structurally reinforced The structural engineer should be consulted for the design of
slab reinforcement A modulus of subgrade reaction Kv of 200 pounds per square inch
per inch of deflection p c i may be used for structural design of the slab Unless
GEOLABS HAWAII

abutted by pavement the base course material should extend a minimum of 18 inches
beyond the perimeter of the vehicle slab
Where no vehicular traffic is anticipated sidewalks may consist of 4 inch thick
concrete slab on four inch base course
Subgrade Preparation
The in situ moisture contents of the near surface soils are relatively high
Therefore prior to placement of base course if the moisture content of the
subgrade soils is at least five percent 5 above the optimum moisture content
the underlying subgrade should be proof rolled to provide a firm and unyielding
surface Otherwise the subgrades should be scarified to a depth of at least eight
inches and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the
subgrade level they should be over excavated to expose competent in situ
material and replaced with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95
percent relative compaction The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Subgrade soils should be kept moist and should not be allowed to dry prior to
placement of base course material and slab concrete If shrinkage cracks appear
in the subgrade the subgrade should be scarified moisture conditioned to above
optimum moisture content and recompacted to 95 percent relative compaction
Base course should be placed as soon as possible after the subgrade soils have
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3107 00 Page 8
been prepared as recommended The backfill against the slabs should be
adequately compacted and graded to shed water away from the slab perimeter
Resistance to Lateral Loads and Settlements
Lateral loads may be resisted by a combination of passive earth pressures exerted
against near vertical foundation faces and soil friction between the base of
foundations slabs and the supporting subgrade Passive earth pressure equal to
an equivalent fluid weighing 350 pounds per cubic foot p c f and a base friction
factor of 0 35 may be used for design Unless covered by slabs or pavements
passive soil resistance in the upper 18 inches should be neglected
If foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we estimate
total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order
of inch

permeable material immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe
perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to a discharge

backfill should consist of non expansive impervious material compacted to

Asphaltic Concrete Pavements
We understand that asphaltic concrete pavement is planned for roads around the
wash rack facilities We anticipate the vehicle loading for this project will consist of
Humvees tractor trailers forklifts and 5 ton trucks
Our preliminary pavement design assumed the pavement subgrade soil will be
similar to the clayey silt near surface soils described in the boring logs If site grading
exposes soil other than those assumed we should perform additional tests to confirm or
revise the recommended pavement sections for actual field conditions If imported fills
are used for the proposed roadways the subgrade on which the pavement structural
section is to be placed should have a minimum California Bearing Ratio CBR of 12
Based on the above assumptions the following pavement sections may be used for
preliminary design purposes

The subgrade soils for the pavement section should be scarified to a depth of at
least 8 inches and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The
compacted subgrade should be in a non yielding condition
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during grading or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a
minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the baserock to reduce migration of
landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation
Site Grading
Based on the current design concepts only minor fills and cuts up to 10 feet are
anticipated during the construction The following recommendations are presented to
provide general guidelines for the site grading work
1 The area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed All vegetation and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site
2 In areas to receive fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
3 The subgrade for areas to receive slabs and pavements should be scarified
to a depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

Excavation
Generally the on site clayey silt soils may be reused as fill material provided
they are properly moisture conditioned and compacted as recommended
herein All fill and structural fill materials should be placed in level loose lifts
not exceeding 8 inches in thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction
If needed imported fill material should consist of non expansive select
granular material less than 3 inches in maximum size with less than 20
percent passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill material should be
tested and approved for its intended use by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery
to the project site
Measures should be incorporated into the grading of the project site to
reduce the potential for erosion and to prevent concentration of water
around structures The ground surface around structures should be sloped
away from structures
Based on the current design the bottom elevations of the sedimentation basins
may require excavations on the order of up to about 10 feet An open cut excavation
may be desirable provided that the sides of the excavation may be set back away from
existing on grade and below grade structures If the open cut excavation method is
adopted by the contractor we recommend the excavation of the sedimentation basins be
performed prior to the construction of concrete vehicle slab
Excavation Method
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on our
GEOLABS HAWAII

borings excavation in the subsoils utilizing conventional mechanical earth moving
equipment should be possible The excavated soils should be stockpiled at least
10 feet away from any underground utilities to reduce the potential of ground
movement or subsidence which may damage the underlying utility lines
Excavation Support
Where an open cut excavation is not possible the excavation for the construction
of the sedimentation basin will need to be adequately shored to reduce the
potential of adjacent ground movement
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the
best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during construction
such as unforseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate
construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability

Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The dry unit weight and moisture content expressed as a
percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample are shown at the appropriate sample
depths on the Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of the soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining
to the performance or suitability of a soil
California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883 test was performed on a bulk sample as
an aid to evaluating their pavement support characteristics The test results are presented
on Plate B 1
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 2
