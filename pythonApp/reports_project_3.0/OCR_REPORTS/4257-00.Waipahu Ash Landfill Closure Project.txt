REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
GEOLABS INC
W O 4257 00 Page 7
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago As volcanic activity in
Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a
broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments that
were transported to the coast These alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with
marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in
thickness from zero feet in the area of Interstate Route H 1 to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach
This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the
artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from
earlier geologic time through the present
The project site is located on the northern half of the Waipio Peninsula and is
composed of a composite of alluvial sediments interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations as described above and of man made fills Man made fills were
generally placed in marshy areas to raise the grade and extend the limits of useable land
These man made fills generally consisted of silty sand to sandy silt material obtained from
dredging or excavations from nearby areas overlying rubbish and incinerator ash from the
nearby incinerator plant The lower marshy soils generally consisted of highly organic muck
or peat material that accumulated from organic marine sediments that deposited near coasts
due to the low energy environment

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we estimate primary consolidation settlement
of the underlying alluvial material to be on the order of 1 to 1 5 feet during a period of about
45 years due to the additional fill surcharge We also estimate primary consolidation
settlement of the incinerator ash and Municipal Solid Waste MSW material to be on the order
of 0 5 to 0 8 feet during a period of about 6 months due to the additional fill surcharge
We estimate settlement to be on the order of 3 to 4 feet during a period of about 25
years due to the on going primary consolidation settlement of the underlying alluvial material
due to the existing surcharge
We believe that the existing incinerator ash and Municipal Solid Waste MSW material
has undergone its compression settlement due to the existing surcharge However due to its
high organic content we anticipate that some secondary compression of the materials may
occur due to decomposition Although difficult to predict we anticipate secondary
compression settlement to be on the order of 0 5 to 1 foot during the consolidation period of
45 years
In summary we anticipate settlement to be on the order of 0 5 feet to 1 foot for the first
6 months following placement of the additional fill Subsequent to the first 6 months we
anticipate settlement to be on the order of 0 1 to 0 3 feet per year for a period of 25 to 45
years
We believe that the differential settlement following the first 6 months after placement
of the additional fill will be less than the total settlement This is because we believe that the
fill incinerator ash and Municipal Solid Waste MSW materials will behave as a composite
mat as the underlying alluvial material consolidates We estimate the differential settlement
following the first 6 months to be about 1 inch per year

Site Preparation
Since the vegetation present on site will be placed into the landfill we recommend that
the plateau area not be cleared and grubbed We believe that the vegetation growth
should be left in place to perform similar to a geotextile mat We believe that this
should help in achieving the recommended compaction of fill over the plateau area It
should also help in reducing the amount of pumping normally experienced in
soft loose poorly compacted soils However the small mounds of soft loose soils
should be leveled and recompacted to a minimum 85 relative compaction ASTM D
1557 91 In addition large trees and bushes should be cut down
In lieu of scarification and compaction prior to filling we recommend proof rolling the
surface to an unyielding surface Because proof rolling of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork observations should be performed by Geolabs Inc during site
preparation to assist the contractor in obtaining the required unyielding subgrade
surface
Fills and Backfills
It is also our understanding that incinerator ash and debris from the slope flattening
process and from nearby locations may be collected and placed within the landfill
These fill incinerator ash and Municipal Solid Waste MSW materials to be overlain
by the geomembrane and geotextile layers should consist of well graded materials

6 inches of fill also referred to as the foundation layer Import material should also

The capping fill material protective soil layer above the geomembrane and geotextile

The fill material should also be free of vegetation incinerator ash and deleterious

Base course and select borrow subbase material should consist of crushed basalt

Select granular material should consist of non expansive material such as crushed

Surge rock is defined as material with a maximum particle size of 8 inches with less

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to a minimum of 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Fills placed at below water grades should consist of surge rock If fill is placed over the
surge rock a geotextile fabric AMOCO 4551 or equivalent should be placed over the
surge rock prior to fill placement to act as a filter and separation layer
Base course select borrow subbase and select granular fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the soils are critical elements of
earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by Geolabs Inc
during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of
compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than required
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as
necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that the moisture
requirement of the fills a minimum of 2 percent above the optimum moisture is an
important requirement for the use of the on site clayey soils to achieve a lower
GEOLABS INC
W O 4257 00 Page 15
permeability and to reduce the potential for future settlement of the fills caused by
collapse from water infiltration

Structures and Slabs
Our laboratory testing of the on site clayey materials indicated a high expansion
potential and a moderate shrinkage potential To reduce the potential for structural
distress resulting from the shrinking and swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend
that the subgrade soils be kept at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
prior to final placement of concrete in addition future sidewaiks and siab on grade
areas should be designed with a minimum of 18 to 24 inches of select granular fill
beneath the slab To reduce the amount of select granular fill needed below the
slab the upper 18 to 24 inches of subgrade material should consist of material with
a lower expansion potential Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal
GEOLABS INC
W O 4257 00 Page 17
to the width of the walkways or sidewalks with expansion joints at right angle
intersections
Due to the anticipated continual settlement of the landfill area we recommend that
future structures be limited to small structures such as comfort stations These
structures should be constructed on structural mat foundations to reduce the potential
for damage due to differential settlement
The area adjacent to the slabs and foundations should be backfilled tightly against the
slab and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential of water
ponding around the foundation and slabs

We recommend that flexible pipes be utilized for utilities traversing the landfill area
These pipes should also have flexible connections especially at the interfaces between
the landfill and non fill ground conditions
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist
of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining
granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use
of on site soils as backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
For utility excavations that encounter soft dark gray soils we recommend that a 24 inch
thick layer of 3B Fine gravel bedding wrapped with a geotextile fabric AMOCO 4551
or equivalent be provided below the 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel recommended
above

minimum of 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper
2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two one inch ring shrinkage tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate
the shrinkage potential of the proposed fill and capping fill materials Results of these
shrinkage tests are presented on Plate B 1
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the proposed fill and capping fill materials under surcharge loads These
tests were run on air dried samples Results of these swell tests are presented on Plate B 2
Fifteen organic content tests ASTM D 2974 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the organic content of the materials Results of these organic content tests are
presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
Six Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of these
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 5
Eight sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 and C 117 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil coarser than the
No 200 Sieve and to aid in soil classification Results of these tests are presented on
Plates B 6 and B 7
One hydrometer test ASTM D 422 was performed on a sample of the alluvial soil
material to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil finer than the No 200 Sieve 075
mm and to aid in soil classification Results of these tests are presented on Plate B 7
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate their shear strength characteristics The test results and shear stress versus
normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 8 through B 10
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing Continued
Five consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on site soils The test results and
consolidation curves are presented on Plates B 11 through B 15
Two hydraulic conductivity tests ASTM D 5084 were performed on selected soil
samples in flexible wall permeameters to evaluate the hydraulic conductivity of the underlying
alluvial material under surcharge loads similar to field conditions The test results are
presented on Plates B 16 and B 17
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
proposed fill and capping fill materials Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 18 and
B 19
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of proposed fill and capping fill materials to evaluate the strength characteristics for
pavement subgrade support Results of the CBR tests are presented on Plates B 20 and B 21
