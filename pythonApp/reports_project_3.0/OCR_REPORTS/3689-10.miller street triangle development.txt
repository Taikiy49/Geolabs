INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Miller Street Development project for the Queen s Medical
Center in the Punchbowl area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design
of foundations basement walls underground tunnel and excavation and backfill only and
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is on the southern flank of Punchbowl Hill The Punchbowl Hill
Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of the
Koolau Range The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with scattered
fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3689 10 Page 6
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field explorations generally encountered stiff to very stiff silty and clayey soils
overlying dense to very dense cinder sand deposits at greater depths Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered we believe that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the new parking garage
structure and underground tunnel planned for the proposed development Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered at the anticipated foundation subgrade levels about
6 feet MSL it is our opinion that an allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds
per square foot psf may be used for design of foundations bearing on the upper stiff to
very stiff silty and clayey soils In areas where a higher bearing value is desired an
allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used provided that the foundations
are extended through the upper silty and clayey soils and bear on the underlying dense to
very dense cinder sand deposits
Based on our laboratory test results the upper clayey soils exhibit a low to
moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the
potential for distress to future slabs on grade resulting from swelling of the upper clayey
soils we recommend that concrete slabs on grade underlain by the slightly to moderately
expansive clayey soils be supported on at least 12 inches of non expansive select
granular fill materials
Considering the relatively deep excavation and proximity of the new parking
garage structure to travelways we believe that temporary shoring of the excavation will
be necessary to reduce the potential for undermining the existing pavements utilities and
to facilitate construction of the underground portion of the parking garage structure and

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used to support the proposed new parking garage structure Based on discussions with
the project structural engineer we understand that the foundation subgrade levels will
generally be at about 6 feet MSL which is near the interface between the upper stiff silty
and clayey soils and the underlying dense cinder sand deposits Because of the
proximity of the groundwater table 1 2 to 6 8 feet MSL encountered to the bottom of
footing elevations it is our opinion that the building foundations should be designed
based on bearing on the upper silty and clayey soils instead of the underlying cinder
sands This serves to reduce the potential need for over excavation to bear on the cinder
sands and or the need for dewatering In addition over excavation extending below the
groundwater table may be required in localized areas to reach the underlying cinder sand
deposits which may result in potential pumping subgrade conditions during footing
preparation Based on these considerations we recommend that the footings be
designed with an allowable bearing pressure of up to bearing on the stiff to very
stiff upper silty and clayey soils
In areas where a higher bearing value is desired an allowable bearing pressure of
up to may be used provided that the foundations extend through the upper silty
and clayey soils and bear on the underlying dense to very dense cinder sand deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3689 10 Page 10
The bearing pressures above are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finish grade New footings to be located adjacent to the
planned basement and underground tunnel walls should be lowered to the same depths
as the wall footings to avoid surcharging the wall foundations If foundations are located
next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded below a one horizontal to
one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
For footings bearing on the upper silty and clayey soils the bottom of footing
excavations should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete For footings bearing on the underlying cinder sand deposits the bottom of
footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with on site cinder sands or imported select granular
materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In lieu of
backfilling the over excavation with compacted granular fill the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential
settlements on the order of 0 5 inches

Slabs On Grade
The project site is generally underlain by silty and clayey soils extending to depths
of about 16 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface Our laboratory tests indicate
that the upper silty and clayey soils are considered to be slightly to moderately expansive
when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless concrete slabs supported by these soils
are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from the shrinking and swelling of these soils due to changes in
moisture content
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
upper silty and clayey soils we recommend that concrete slabs on grade constructed
near the existing ground surface underlain by the expansive clayey soils be supported on
at least 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill material The capping fill should
also extend beyond the edges of the slabs a minimum of 2 feet The 12 inch capping fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3689 10 Page 12
recommended above may be omitted for slabs on grade underlain by the cinder sand
deposits encountered at greater depths
The non expansive select capping fill where needed should be placed as soon as
possible after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The compacted capping fill and
the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of
concrete The select capping fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil
above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of
moisture from the subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and
wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab
Where concrete slabs on grade will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the
garage basement level tunnel floor and entrance driveway we recommend that the
slabs be supported on a 6 inch layer of aggregate base course in addition to the
12 inch capping fill The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction We recommend that the
concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should
be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate some amount of differential
movement
For design of structural floor slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of
300 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
base course A minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design
purposes Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints
which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to
unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finish
grade

hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot

connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall

below grade portion of the parking garage structure and underground tunnel we
anticipate that the majority of the site preparation work for the proposed project will

Prior to placement of the non expansive select granular capping fill the subgrade
soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The subgrades should be kept moist until covered by the non expansive select
granular capping fill Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the
subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
The on site clayey soils generated from the excavations should not be used as fill
materials below the building areas They should be disposed of properly off site or used
in landscaping areas if appropriate Select granular fill materials needed for the 12 inch
capping layer may consist of the on site cinder sands or imported non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well
graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less

Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use Granular
fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction with the exception of retaining wall backfill

Excavation
Based on the design finish grades of the basement level of the parking garage
structure we anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 25 feet below the existing
ground surface will be required for the project construction Based on our field
exploration results the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff silty and
clayey soils within the anticipated depth of the excavation Therefore we believe that
conventional excavation equipment may be used for the proposed excavation
Excavation Method
In general the method and equipment used for excavation should be determined
by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on
our field exploration results we believe that the in situ stiff to very stiff silty clays
and clayey silts encountered in our borings may be excavated by a bulldozer or
other heavy excavation equipment

Excavation Support
Since part of the parking garage structure will abut the adjacent Vineyard
Boulevard and Miller Street the planned excavations should be adequately shored
to reduce the potential for damage to the surrounding utility lines streets and
buildings caused by earth movements toward the open excavation The
excavated materials should not be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation
within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation to reduce the
potential for ground movement The excavation support and shoring system used
is the responsibility of the contractor and must comply with all applicable safety
requirements
Based on the very stiff and dense subsurface conditions encountered at the
project site we believe that the use of steel sheet pile shoring may encounter
difficulties due to the potential for penetration refusal during the sheet pile
installation operations In lieu of steel sheet piling we believe that a soldier pile
and lagging with tie backs or soil nail wall may be used for shoring support of the
proposed excavations The use of horizontal bracing or struts inside the
excavation for the parking structure does not appear practical due to the width of
the excavation Consideration may be given to the use of horizontal bracing or
struts for the tunnel excavation traversing Miller Street
Lateral Earth Pressures for Shoring
For shoring heights less than about 12 to 15 feet high we believe that a cantilever
soldier pile and lagging system which mobilizes lateral load resistance through the
use of passive pressure on the portion of the soldier pile that extends below the
bottom of the excavation may be used The soldier piles should consist of
wide flange steel beams and heavy timbers should be used for lagging The
lateral earth pressures presented in the Basement Walls and Underground
Tunnel section of this report may be considered in the design of a cantilever
shoring system

Tied Back Shoring System
For shoring exceeding about 15 feet in height it is our opinion that a restrained
shoring system consisting of soldier pile and lagging with temporary tie backs may
be considered for shoring purposes A tied back wall is a structural system that
uses an anchor embedded in the ground to secure a tendon which applies a
lateral force on a retaining structure Lateral load resistance can be mobilized
through the post tensioned tie backs and through the use of passive pressures on
members extending below the bottom of the excavation
Tie backs are advantageous since they are external to the excavation and
permanent construction may proceed without hindrance from internal bracing
Tie backs have been employed primarily for temporary excavations generally
utilizing soldier piles with lagging for soil retention Since tie backs do encroach
underground of the adjacent properties permission for encroachment from the
owners of the adjacent properties should be obtained prior to the tie back
design installation For a restrained shoring system with braced or tied back
conditions the apparent pressure diagram presented on Plate 3 may be used for
preliminary design purposes A typical cross section of the tied back wall system
is presented on Plate 4 Appropriate loads due to surcharge building
foundations equipment traffic or sloping ground conditions adjacent to the
excavations should be added to the recommended earth pressures for design
purposes An appropriate safety factor should be included by the shoring
designer
Soil Nail Retaining Wall System
As an alternative to the tied back wall system a soil nail retaining wall system may
be considered for the project A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series
of individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or
slope A shotcrete facing is generally applied to the face of the reinforced
excavation to provide the appearance of a conventional concrete retaining wall

W O 3689 10 Page 21
The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil
and reinforce the sidewall of the excavation The reinforced soil mass behaves in
a similar manner to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing A typical cross
section of the soil nail retaining wall system is presented on Plate 5
Installation Considerations
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during
construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring
stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent ground should be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
with instruments
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation In general we believe that the majority
of the planned excavations will be above the static groundwater table at the project site

Utility Trench
New utility connections will be required for the proposed new development
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches
above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the
pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill
and damage to the pipes

W O 3689 10 Page 23
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finish grade may consist of the on site soils or select material
The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finish grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Drainage
The finish grades outside the new parking garage structure should be sloped to
shed water away from slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is
also advised that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge
be diverted away from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the
slabs and foundations should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slabs and
foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of these tests are
summarized on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Five sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic representation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 3
Three strain controlled consolidated drained direct shear tests ASTM D 3080
were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of
the on site soils The test results are presented on Plates B 4 through B 6
