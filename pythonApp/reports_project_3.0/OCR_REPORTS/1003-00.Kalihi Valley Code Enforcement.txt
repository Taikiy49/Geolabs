Subgrade Preparation
The in situ moisture contents of the subgrade soils are rela
tively high During construction some areas may be encoun
tered where 95 compaction of the subgrade soils may not be
feasible due to the high natural moisture content and addi
tional moisture due to runoff seepage or leaking utilities
Conventional drying of the subgrade soils is not practical
due to the necessity to remove and replace the pavement within
the working day to minimize traffic interruption Therefore
additional subgrade stabilization utilizing filter fabric or
additional layers of select borrow may be required in some
areas We recommend that a contingency item for this addi
tional stabilization be included in the construction costs

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this soil engineering investigation was to
explore the shallow subsurface soil conditions below the
existing roads in order to provide soil data for the design
of the roadway pavement and related structures for the pro
posed road improvements
Retaining wall design parameters will be presented at a
later date when the actual locations of the walls are deter
mined Additional field exploration and engineering analysis
may be required during the final design phase of the district
code enforcement

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in the lower central portion
of Kalihi Valley along the southeastern valley side slopes
The project improvement area is generally bounded by Kalihi
Street Akone Place Kamanaiki Stream and Kilohana Street
The site is divided into two areas by a small ridge of the
Koolau Range
Kalihi Street extends along the northwestern side of the
ridge while Kamanaiki Street runs along the southeastern
side
Kalihi and Kamanaiki Streets are generally gently sloping
while most of the side streets are relatively steep Wahine
and Anuu Place have existing concrete pavement while the
remaining streets have asphaltic concrete pavements Most of

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed improvements consist of the following
1
Improvement of Kalihi Street and the other City and
County streets in the area according to City and

Improvement of existing private streets up to City and

New retaining wall construction where required for
grade adjustments along existing roads The actual wall
locations are not definite at this time and their design

Relocation or improvement of existing utility lines under
roadway areas

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on the boring data it appears that the near surface
soils at the site can be generalized into two basic types
1 Residual soil stiff residual soil consisting of red
dish brown silty clay to clay was encountered in the
southeastern portion of the site mostly in the vicinity
of Kamanaiki Street and adjoining small access streets
These soils can generally be classified as CH MH soils
according to the Unified Soil Classification System
Laboratory soil tests indicate that these soils when
remolded and compacted have moderate to high swelling
potential
2 Alluvium stiff alluvial sOils consisting of mottled
dark brown to brown clayey silt to silty clay were
encountered in the northwestern portion of the site
along Kalihi Street These soils are generally geologi
cally mapped as older alluvium

P 5
These soils are generally classified as MH to CH MH
according to the Unified Soil Classification System
Laboratory CBR tests also indicate that these soils
have moderate swelling potential
Groundwater was encountered in some of the deeper borings
along Kalihi Street at 8 to 14 foot depths
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration encountered stiff residual and alluvial
soils below the existing roadway pavements
The near surface soils have moderate to high swelling poten
tial and therefore somewhat thicker than existing pavement
sections are recommended
In general hard excavation is not anticipated for utility
trenches
The existing roadway pavements have deteriorated and in gen
eral are in poor condition In order to provide uniform
Support for the new pavement sections the existing pavements
should be removed down to the new pavement section level and
reconstructed
The broken up asphaltic concrete could be recycled or stock
piled at the site if space is available for reuse as borrow

the pavement section
Relatively thin asphaltic concrete and base course sections

Pavement Design
During our field exploration representative bulk samples of
the near surface soils were obtained from the auger cuttings
or hand dug holes These samples were tested in the labora
tory to evaluate their subgrade supporting capacity Seven
California Bearing Ratio tests CBR were performed on the
bulk samples in accordance with the American Society for Test
ing and Materials Test Designation D 1883 The test results
indicate CBR values of 7 7 to 18 5 for the residual reddish
brown silty clay and 5 7 to 11 5 for the dark brown Silty clay
alluvial soils The CBR test results are presented on Plates
B 1 and B 2 of the appendix

APPENDIX B
LABORATORY TESTING
Moisture content determinations and Atterberg Limits
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classi
fication and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1
through A 42
Seven laboratory CBR tests were performed on samples of
the typical near surface soil along the existing roads in
order to evaluate its suitability as pavement support The
test results are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
Two l inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate its swelling
potential The test results are summarized on Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed
samples of the near surface soils in order to evaluate their
shear strength parameters The test results are shown on
Plates B 4 and B 5S
