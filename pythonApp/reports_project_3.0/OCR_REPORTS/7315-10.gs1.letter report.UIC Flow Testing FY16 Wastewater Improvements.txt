In accordance with our revised budgetary fee proposal dated December 9, 2015, 
this letter report presents the results of Underground Injection Control (UIC) Well Flow 
Testing performed for the FY16 Wastewater Improvements project within Kilauea Military 
Camp on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the results of the UIC Well Flow Testing performed on 
UIC Well #7 for the proposed project. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is situated within the Kilauea Military Camp between 
Mamalahoa Highway and Crater Rim Drive on the Island of Hawaii. There are a total of 
13 UIC wells located in Kilauea Military Camp. The flow testing was performed on 
UIC Well #7 located east of Building No. 26 as shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2 and 
Site Plan, Plate 3. 
It is planned to make improvements to the existing wastewater system at Kilauea 
Military Camp. Included in these improvements may be an increase in the volume of 
wastewater disposed of through existing UIC Well #7.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY & ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five shield 
volcanoes including the following: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Loa (some activity during 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be dormant), and 
Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both are still active). The project site is situated north of the 
Kilauea caldera and is located within the basaltic lavas of the Puna Volcanic Series. 
We anticipate that the site is underlain by a thin layer of fill followed by basalt rock 
formation. 
UIC FLOW TESTING 
Prior to performing the flow test, UIC Well #7 was cleaned by Underground 
Services, Inc. Following well cleaning, the flow test was conducted by placing 
approximately 2,400 gallons of clean fresh water into the well at a rate of about 10 gallons 
per minute for about 4 hours. The water level in the well was recorded at about 5-minute 
intervals during the rising head portion, steady state portion and falling head portion of the 
test. 
Due to difficulties in providing a low steady flow of 10 gallons per minute with the 
fire hydrant and flowmeter, two hoses connected to two water spigots were used to 
provide the required flow rate into the well. The water spigots were adjusted until a 
constant water flow of about 10 gallons per minute was achieved. The flow rate was 
measured using a 5-gallon bucket. The rate of water flow was measured several times 
prior to the test and also after the test to verify the constant rate of water flow. 
A water level detector, watch and drop pipe for the water sensor were used in the 
flow test. A schematic diagram of the set-up is shown on Plate 4. 
Since the actual geometry of the well is not available and the actual characteristics 
of the receiving aquifer are not known, the performance test data were not subjected to 
rigorous analysis to estimate hydraulic conductivity or maximum capacity. The test data 
was reviewed on a subjective basis to evaluate well performance. Based on the test 
results, a constant head condition was achieved at a depth of about 15.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface with a flow rate of about 10 gallons per minute. We believe that 
UIC Well #7 should have satisfactory well performance with a flow rate of about 10 gallons 
per minute. The results of the flow test are presented in Appendix A.
