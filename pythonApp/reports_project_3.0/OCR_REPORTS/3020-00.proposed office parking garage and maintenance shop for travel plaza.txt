INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed office parking garage and maintenance shop for the Travel
Plaza located in the district of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from the field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for foundation design input and may not
be appropriate to form a basis for the preparation of construction cost estimates or for
construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Koolau Basalt
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3020 00 Page 6
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 5 million
years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has subsided as
much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and
Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may
have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly
lagoonal sedimentary unit
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3020 00 Page 7
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action extended the
alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
post erosional events following the cessation of shield building were contemporaneous
with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder Some of the eruptions
produced lava flows which filled the valleys and moved seaward inundating coral reefs
and alluvial deposits
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the deep valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years the sea
level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in
thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly
valleys and drainageways
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3020 00 Page 8
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form A great many of those projects were
originally constructed for agricultural residential and or military development Many of
the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting structural loads

General

medium stiff to very stiff older alluvium A medium hard basalt stratum underlie the older
alluvium The basaltic rock stratum was encountered at depths of approximately 60 feet
below the surface
Based on the heavy structural loads 500 kips per column anticipated we believe
that appreciable total and differential settlements would occur if shallow foundations are
used for support of the proposed office parking garage building Therefore we
recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of driven piles bearing on the
basalt formation be used to support the office parking garage building
For the maintenance shop addition we believe that the recompacted near surface
fills could provide adequate support for the light structural loads 10 kips per column
Therefore shallow spread footing foundations bearing in compacted fills may be used to
support the addition

Pile Foundation
Pile foundations should be driven through the loose and soft compressible recent
alluvial deposits and develop its supporting capacity from end bearing on the basalt
formation at approximately 60 to 65 feet We considered using 12 inch square piles for
support of the proposed office parking garage building below the surface however
because of the significant contrast in strength between the alluvial deposits and the basalt
formation tensile stresses may develop in the 12 inch pile resulting in potential damage
to the piles As a result we recommend that 14 inch and or 15 inch square precast
prestressed concretes piles be used for support of the proposed structures An allowable
compressive load capacity of 100 tons and 120 tons may be used for the 14 inch and 15
inch square piles respectively bearing on the basalt formation The allowable
compressive load capacity will need to be reduced by the estimated downdrag loads
which are discussed in the following subsection When piles are driven in groups they
should have a minimum center to center spacing of three feet 3 to avoid capacity
reduction due to group action

Downdrag Loads
Where fill is placed over a compressible soil the latter consolidates and moves
downward relative to the pile The downward movement results in negative skin
friction forces generally known as DOWNDRAG forces on the pile shaft The
magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the compressible
layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the pile perimeter area Because
potential future settlements due to placement of new fills are anticipated we
recommend that the pile be designed for downdrag loads The downdrag load on
the pile shaft is estimated to be about 35 kips for the 14 inch square pile and 40
kips for the 15 inch square pile Therefore the allowable compressive load
capacities of the piles will need to be reduced by the expected downdrag loads
Higher pile capacities therefore are recommended for the combined downdrag
and building loads
To reduce the downdrag forces coating of the portion of the pile shaft within the
compressible layer with bitumen coating may be considered The bitumen coating
should only be applied to the portion of the pile which will be embedded in the
negative skin friction zone or compressible soil layer However due to the
anticipated hot temperature and high humidity depending on the time of the year
placement of the bitumen coating may be difficult during pile installation and is not
recommended

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of 45 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
level control so that driving within the loose compressible soil may be controlled
at a reduced energy to reduce the tensile stress development within the piles
which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data form to Geolabs
Hawaii prior to bidding for evaluation We will review the submittal and assess the
driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer Due to possible variation
in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained during bidding for add
tions shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four 4 hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving such as when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop in the pile
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This can be accomplished by using a driving
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3020 00 Page 14
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the
more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the whole pile
length is driven down to the finished elevations or the blow counts steadily
increase with depth and reach five 5 blows per inch for the last six inches 6 of
final pile penetration or when aggregated blow counts are 60 blows for the last
foot
Appropriate driving criteria will be developed after selection of the pile driving
equipment

Pre drilling and Backfilling
Due to the possibility of encountering obstructions in the existing fill and to reduce
downdrag forces contributed by the upper ten feet 10 of fill the pile locations
should be pre drilled In addition pre drilling will help reduce pile driving induced
vibration which may result in potential damage to shallow foundations of the
existing buildings The pre drilling should penetrate through the dense fill and
extend to approximately ten feet 10 below the existing ground surface After
installation of piles in the pre drilled holes each pile location should be backfilled
with pea gravels
Foundation Settlements
Settlements of pile foundations will result from the elastic compression of the pile
member and the subgrade response of the end bearing on the basalt rock
stratum We estimate that the total settlement of the pile foundations would be on
the order of inch with differential settlements between piles not exceeding about
one half that amount We believe these settlements will be essentially elastic and
should occur as the loads are applied

Excavation
Based on our field exploration excavation depths on the order of four feet 4 or
more below the existing ground surface may encounter groundwater and or loose soil
conditions The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined
by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained
by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used The adequacy and
safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor
His representative who should be required to be continuously present on site during
excavation and construction work will have the opportunity to promptly observe any
changing conditions during construction such as unforseen subsoils unexpectedly high
groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or techniques that may affect
shoring stability
However it must be noted that some movement of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation Due
to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to predict accurately the magnitude

quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed and on the subsoil
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practicable if necessary and that the adjacent ground should
be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with
instruments We strongly recommend that the contractor retain a competent structural
engineering consultant to design and to coordinate the implementation of the excavation
support system
Construction Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations for the utility trench will be necessary where the existing
groundwater level is above the excavation bottom Therefore we recommend that
dewatering provisions be included in the contract documents for the installation
and relocation of utility lines
Dewatering for construction should be made the sole responsibility of the
contractor The selection of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left
up to the contractor and he should be aware that modifications to the dewatering
system may be required during construction depending on _ conditions
encountered The dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the
groundwater level surrounding the proposed excavation
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents be
written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit construction of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3020 00 Page 26
proposed utility pipes under safe and dry conditions These works or systems
should include but not be limited to pumping grouting cut off walls or other
possible methods
Dewatering operations should be performed so that only clear water is pumped
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may cause subsoils to be
susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottom Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done without
pumping out soils fines bumping clear water only and should be coordinated
with shoring installation so that the excavation stability is not jeopardized
Extra caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering of utility trenches that are
connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in
the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause areal settlements and
damage to the utility lines and or other existing adjacent structures

contractor prior to construction The contractor has the sole responsibility to devise and
implement an effective program to protect the utility lines during construction
