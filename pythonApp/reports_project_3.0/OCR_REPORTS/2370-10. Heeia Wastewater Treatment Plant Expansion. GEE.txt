INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed expansion of the existing Heeia Wastewater Treatment Plant
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2370 10 Page 2
in Keauhou North Kona on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
of the data obtained from the field exploration and laboratory testing of selected samples
Where applicable our analyses also considered the information contained in the
report entitled Geotechnical Engineering Exploration Heeia Wastewater Treatment Plant
Expansion Increment Keauhou North Kona Hawaii dated prepared
by Geolabs Hawaii pertaining to the existing treatment plant
The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input and may not
be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates nor for construction
bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to upgrade the existing Heeia Wastewater Treatment Plant by
constructing a new Operations Building and a future Scrubber The proposed new
facilities will be located in the eastern part of the existing treatment plant Previous
geotechnical explorations in the vicinity of the project site indicate that the basalt formation
in this area consists of aa and clinker zones
Based on the information provided to us the new Operations Building would be
a two story concrete structure of approximately 50 by 85 feet in plan dimension The
finish floor elevation of the proposed building will be at approximately 27 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL We understand that the proposed building site is presently occupied by an
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2370 10 Page 3
underground tank structure the Rapid Bloc Aerobic Digestors Therefore the project
construction would involve the removal of the existing underground tank structure and
backfilling of the excavation We anticipate that backfills on the order of about 12 to 18
feet would be needed to backfill the excavation to the proposed building pad elevation
Based on the information provided to us by the structural engineer maximum column
loads for the proposed Operations Building are estimated to be on the order of 240 kips
per column with wall loads of approximately 17 kips per lineal feet We also understand
that the proposed ground floor slab will be subjected to vehicular and forklift traffic
We understand that the new Scrubber will be a lightly loaded on grade slab with
a floor area of about 500 square feet Minimal grading is anticipated for the construction
of this structure
Since the project site is located in a zone with potential seismic activity special
attention should be given to the dynamic settlement characteristics of the existing and
backfill materials under the anticipated seismic excitation

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration and the information provided by R M Towill
Corporation the proposed Operations Building site is currently occupied by an existing
underground tank structure We anticipate that grading for the project construction will
involve the removal of the existing tank structure and the backfilling of the excavation
Structural backfills on the order of about 12 to 18 feet thick will be required to fill the
excavation to the proposed building pad elevation of 26 feet MSL Special attention
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2370 10 Page 7
should be given to the type of backfill material used due to the potential for dynamic
compaction of the backfill material at the project site under seismic excitations
Conventional shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings
bearing on the compacted structural backfill may be used for support of the Operations
Building Slab on grade foundation bearing on the recompacted existing fill may be used
to support the future Scrubber Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations are
presented in the following subsections of this report
Site Grading
Site preparation and grading should be performed in accordance with the following
guidelines Additional earthwork recommendations are presented in related sections of
this report Because of the possible variation in the quantities of the excavation and
backfilling contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
additional over excavation and backfilling with structural fill
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing the existing underground tank structure and all other
buildings structures designated to be demolished should be completely removed
and disposed of properly off site Previously placed backfill material consisting of
sandy basalt gravel encountered at the bottom and at the sides of the existing tank
excavation should be completely removed to expose the underlying in situ basaltic
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2370 10 Page 8
rock formation To reduce additional loading on the walls of the excavation
stockpiling of the excavated material adjacent to the excavation should be strictly
avoided Based on the information from our borings the depths of excavation
would be approximately 12 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface
Fill and Backfill
Structural fill material needed to backfill the excavation and to raise the ground
surface to the proposed finish grades should consist of imported granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than three inches 3 in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of less than one percent 1 when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill material should
contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
The sandy basalt gravel encountered at the bottom and at the sides of the existing
tank excavation placed previously during the construction of the existing facilities
may be stockpiled and reused as a source of structural fill material provided that
they meet the above requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding ten inches 10 in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 test procedures

Excavation
Based on the information provided by R M Towill Corporation we anticipate
excavations on the order of about 12 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface will be
required to remove the existing underground tank structure for the project construction
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations Generally the existing
underground tank structure with its surrounding backfill could be demolished and
removed by a large bulldozer or other heavy equipment It is our opinion that excavation
of the in situ basalt rock formation if needed may require hard ripping or even blasting
Therefore allowances should be made for the additional time and expenses which may
be required by the hard basalt rock excavation
Based on our field exploration the vicinity of the project site is generally underlain
by competent basalt rock formation It is our opinion that excavations in competent
basalt rock formation may be able to stand unsupported at relatively steep angles for
temporary construction purposes Due to the presence of the near surface fill layer
sloping back the sides of the excavation at the upper five feet 5 may be required to
provide stability during the project construction
The excavation must comply with all applicable safety requirements It is
recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to
observe the excavation operations
The adequacy and safety of the excavation should be made the sole responsibility
of the contractor His representative who should be required to be present on site during
excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to promptly observe any
changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2370 10 Page 10
groundwater table inappropriate construction sequences or techniques etc which may
affect the stability of the excavation
However it must be noted that some movement of the adjacent ground may still
occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation The magnitude of movement
is difficult to accurately predict due to the complexity of the stress changes The
magnitude depends greatly upon workmanship and also on the subsoil conditions the
size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation operation should be properly
performed and that the adjacent ground should be continuously monitored for cracks
dips and or other indications of movements with instruments such as settlement markers
and inclinometers Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing
facilities and utilities
Shallow Foundations
Provided that the existing structures and the previously placed backfill are
completely removed and the excavation is properly backfilled in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading and Excavation sections of this report
we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous
footings bearing on structural fill may be used to support the proposed Operations
Building
An allowable bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be
used for the design of footings bearing on the compacted structural fill material These
bearing values are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Footings should have
a minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2370 10 Page 11
the lowest adjacent finish grade All footings should bear entirely on the compacted
structural fill and should not straddle across the rock formation to reduce the potential for
differential settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated with an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soils around
footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of friction of
0 35 may be used for footings bearing on compacted fill
We estimate the foundation settlements under the anticipated design loads bearing
on compacted imported fill material to be on the order of one inch 1 total with
differential settlements on the order of 2 inch
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
Slab on grade foundation bearing on the existing surficial fill layer may be used to
support the future Scrubber The bearing subgrade should be properly prepared by
scarifying to a depth of eight inches 8 and compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction A six inch 6 thick base course cushion layer compacted to the same
requirement should be provided beneath the concrete pad

Seismic Considerations
Since the site is located in a region with potential seismic activities and may be
subjected to relatively strong earthquakes during the life of the project proposed
structures at the site should be designed in accordance with the applicable seismic
provisions of the building codes
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated granular soils undergo a substantial
loss of strength and deformation due to pore pressure increase resulting from cyclic
stress application induced by earthquakes In the process the soil acquires a mobility
sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements if the soil mass is not confined
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose clean uniformly graded silt fine sand
and gravel deposits In the absence of a groundwater table loose silts fine sand and
gravel deposits are not susceptible to liquefaction However they may be susceptible to
dynamic compaction which will densify when subjected to seismic excitations Therefore
we recommend that self compacting material such as No 3B Fine gravel or similar type
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2370 10 Page 16
of material not be used as a source of structural backfill material due to its potential for
seismically induced dynamic compaction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of five
5 borings to depths ranging between 2 5 and 20 0 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are
presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30
inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
