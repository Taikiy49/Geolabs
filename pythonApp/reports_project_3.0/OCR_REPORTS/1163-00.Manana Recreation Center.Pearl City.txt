INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soil engineering investigation
for the Manana Housing Recreation Center Courts at Camp H M Smith
Pearl City Oahu Hawaii The general location of the site and its
vicinity is shown on Plate 1 Project Location Map
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the subsurface investigation was to explore the soil
conditions at the site to provide data to evaluate possible causes of
the existing asphaltic concrete pavement cracking and possible
remedial measures
The scope of our investigation included field reconnaissance the
drilling of six exploratory borings laboratory testing of selected
samples engineering analysis and report preparation
Results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which were used
in the engineering analysis that formulated our conclusions and recom
mendations are presented in the appendices of this report

SITE DESCRIPTION
The site is located about 500 feet north of Acacia Road which is the
entrance to Manana Housing in Camp H M Smith located in Pearl City
on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general project location is shown
on Plate 1
The site is bounded from the west to the north by an embankment which
slopes down toward the site at an approximate grade of 2 1 H V
Bordering the site to the east is a private apartment complex and to
the south is Acacia Road
The area under investigation is currently used as a recreational
facility which includes a combined tennis basketball and volleyball
court The layout of the courts is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The existing asphaltic concrete courts show quite an extensive amount
of pavement cracking and wear There appears to be several utility
trench excavations under the pavement as evidenced by linear pavement
cracking along the trench edges
The tennis court which is enclosed by a 12 foot high chain link fence
has recently been resurfaced with a thin leveling course and paint
coating however pavement cracking has reappeared in this pavement
The basketball and volleyball courts have not been resurfaced

A drainage swale has been cut along the northern edge of the courts to
divert surface runoff from the nearby embankment However during our
investigation it was noted that standing water was ponding inside the
drainage swale caused by insufficient grading of the swale
The asphalt pavement ground surface is relatively flat with elevations
ranging from 105 2 to 105 8
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Field Exploration
A total of six 6 exploratory borings were drilled through the
asphaltic concrete court pavements to depths ranging from 5 0 to 6 5
feet to investigate the subsurface conditions The approximate boring
locations are presented on the Site Plan Plate 2
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and the log of borings
together with a brief description of the laboratory testing and
summary of the test results are presented in the appendices to this
report
Soil Conditions
The six borings encountered a surface layer of 1 5 to 2 inches of
asphaltic concrete and 4 inches of coral base course Directly below
the base course layer is a stiff reddish brown silty clay with traces
of coral sand and gravel This clay layer varies from one to two feet
thick and appears to be fill material placed during the tennis court
construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
Below the fill layer soft to medium stiff light brown clayey silts
were encountered at depths of about 2 0 feet and extending down to 5 0
feet These soils were saturated Directly beneath this layer is a
stiff brown mottled clay to the depth of our investigation
Groundwater was encountered in and 5 ranging from 3 3 to
6 4 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater was not
encountered in the other borings
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The existing pavements have deteriorated and are in a relatively poor
condition Some of the deteriorations can be attributed to normal
wear due the age approximately 20 years of the pavement However
we believe that to a significant degree other conditions may have
contributed to the present pavement deterioration
Our investigation disclosed several probable causes of the observed
pavement and resurfacing cracking
1 The field exploration disclosed relatively poor soil
conditions below the playing courts Soft and wet soils
were encountered from 2 to 5 feet below the existing
grade The soft soil condition may have been primarily a
result of localized high water level caused by poor drain
age around the courts The water appears to travel in the
silty rather porous layer encountered at 2 to 5 foot depth
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1163 00 Page 5
Water level was encountered at 3 8 to 6 4 foot depth in
half of the total borings drilled during our field
exploration which was conducted during a moderately dry
season of the year
It appears likely that the water level could rise sub
stantially higher during periods of heavy rainfall
It is our opinion that the wavy surface of the courts could
probably be due to previous up and down movements of the
subgrade soils caused by water level fluctuations and
subsequent soil volume changes during wetting and drying
cycles
2 The existing pavement next to the tennis net posts and
around some of the utility posts indicate that some settle
ment of the ground surface have occurred This might
probably be due to consolidation of the soft silty soils
when the weight of the upper fill was applied
3 Some of the cracks in the pavement are a result of settle
ment due to poor utility trench backfill Linear cracks
were noted in the tennis courts parallel to the net and
near some of the light poles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1163 00 Page 6
4 Many cracks were noted in the resurfaced and painted
tennis court In a few of the cracked areas the
surface paint coats were removed to expose the
underlying asphaltic concrete surface Relatively large
cracks approximately 1 8 inch were noted in the
asphaltic concrete below the cracks in the surface
coats It appears that the leveling course thickness
was not sufficient to provide a smooth surface for the
surface coats thus resulting in reflection cracks in
the surface
Our field observations and testing indicate that the playing
courts are probably affected by several conditions Therefore
remedial work should be attempted to correct or minimize these
conditions
For the remedial work we recommend the following
Improve site drainage The swale on the northern side of
the courts should be regraded to provide rapid drainage of
surface runoff to prevent ponding Considerations should
also be given to a concrete lined drainage swale
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1163 00 Page 7
2 Improve Subsurface Drainage A deep approximately 4 feet
subdrain is recommended around the northern and western
perimeter of the courts to intercept possible subsurface
seepage in the porous silty soil layer see Plate 3
5 Recompact utility trench backfill Where cracks are noted
along the utility trenches the existing asphaltic concrete
surfacing and loose wet backfill should be removed and
replaced with a compacted granular backfill material such
as 3 Fine gravel After placement of a 4 inch base course
layer above the backfill the trench pavement should be
repaved
4 Overlay existing pavement A minimum 1 5 inches thick
overlay of asphaltic concrete is recommended to provide
additional pavement strength and a smooth level surface for
the finishing paint coats
It is our opinion that with all of the above remedial measures a
functional playing court surface could be obtained at a substantial
lower cost than to construct a new facility
With the installation of an improved drainage system to lower the
moisture contents of the near surface soils a small amount of areal
ground settlements may occur as a result of soil shrinkage However
we believe that these settlements would be small and would not be
considered critical

Design Review
We recommend that plans and specifications for grading resurfacing
and subdrain construction be forwarded to the soils engineer for
review and written comments prior to construction to see that the
intent of our recommendations is included in the project plans and
specifications
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the filling subdrain construction and trench
backfilling operations be observed by a soils engineer during
construction This is to see that adequate compaction has been
obtained and the subsurface conditions are consistent with the
findings of our subsurface investigation The recommendations given
in this report are contingent upon the above inspections

in accordance with generally accepted soil and foundation engineering
principles and practices This warranty is in lieu of all other
warranties either expressed or implied The analysis and recom
mendations submitted in this report are based on our site recon
naissance soil information from boring data and laboratory tests
obtained from this soil investigation

Although the subsurface conditions interpreted from the borings are
generally correct at the boring locations some variation may be

Unanticipated soil conditions are commonly encountered and cannot be

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by
drilling 0 to 6 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 Hand carried portable drilling equipment was used to
drill the borings Three bulk samples were recovered from the
auger cuttings two samples of the existing subgrade at depths
of 0 5 to 3 5 feet below the existing ground and one sample
ofthe existing base course
The material encountered in the borings were classified
by visual and textural examination in the field by our engineer
who continously monitored the drilling operations The
classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered is
presented on the Log of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Soil samples were obtained from the boring by driving
a standard penetration sampler or a 2 4 inch I D split barrel
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the
log of borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were
performed on selected samples to aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are
presented on the log of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Eight Atterberg Limit tests were performed as a
further aid in the classification of the On site soils The
test results are shown on Plate B 4
One laboratory CBR test was performed on a bulk sample
of the near surface material obtained from the subgrade
elevation to evaluate its swelling potential and subgrade
Support capacity The test result is summarized on Plate B 2
One ASTM D 1557 test modified proctor was performed
on a bulk sample of the near surface existing material to
evaluate its compaction characteristics The test result is
summarized on Plate B l
Two sieve analyses were performed to determine the
gradation characteristics of the site materials The test
results are summarized on Plate B 1 3
Five one inch ring swell tests were performed on un
disturbed samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the
existing surface soil The test result is summarized on
Plate B 5
