INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Home Depot Pearl City Junction project located in the
Pearl City area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures site grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
A retail facility for Home Depot is planned in Pearl City near the intersection of
Kamehameha Highway and Acacia Street on the island of Oahu Hawaii We understand
that the retail center facility will consist of a retail building with a planned area of
approximately 130 140 square feet a garden center of approximately 15 000 square feet
a parking area with about 535 spaces and other ancillary structures
We anticipate that the proposed new building will primarily be of tilt up concrete or
masonry construction with a sheet metal roof Based on the information provided we
understand that it is proposed to level out the ground surface by cutting the eastern side
of the building location and filling the western side Therefore we anticipate that site
grading consisting of about 5 feet of cuts and 10 feet of fills would be needed to achieve
the design grades A retaining wall is planned for the western side of the proposed
building to support the fill material In addition short retaining walls of up to about 5 feet
in height are planned for the north and south sides of the site along the property lines

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their
original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth
During the erosion process of the Koolau and Waianae shield numerous tributary
streams were developed off of larger streams which actively carried and deposited
sediments A portion of the project site may be underlain by an old stream bed which
may have been a tributary to the existing Waiawa Stream Typically old stream beds are
characterized by presence of soft organic and or inorganic alluvial deposits With the
urbanization of the area man made fills may have been placed to reclaim the project site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Competent soil conditions were encountered at the eastern portion of the site
Normal grading and foundation design may be used at this portion of the site
However relatively thick compressible soft soils as much as 25 feet thick were
encountered at the western end of the site Due to the presence of the soft alluvium
and anticipated fill heights of up to about 10 feet ground settlements may be expected
Areal ground settlements on the order of 2 to 6 inches may result from the approximately
10 feet of new fills
In the area underlain by soft alluvium we recommend that a surcharge program be
implemented prior to building construction to reduce the building settlements The
surcharge program should consist of placing an additional 5 feet of temporary fill
W O 3787 00 Page 7
over finished grades of the proposed fill area at the western portion of the site prior to
building construction Based on our engineering analyses we believe that a settlement
period of about 2 months would be adequate to achieve approximately 80 to 90 percent
consolidation To assess the rate of the fill settlement implementation of a
settlement monitoring program is recommended
There is an existing 42 inch water line within this soft alluvium area In order to
reduce the possibility of damage to the existing 42 inch water line we recommend that
new fills within 20 feet of the water easement be limited to less than 2 feet high
Detailed discussions and recommendations for building foundation and retaining

Surcharge Program
To aid in the acceleration of the anticipated ground settlements we recommend
that a surcharge program be implemented prior to building construction The program
should consist of the placement of an additional 5 feet of temporary fill over the finished
grades of the proposed fill area in the western portion of the site prior to building
construction Fill materials required to raise the existing grade to finish grade should
conform to the recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report
Since soft compressible soils were found only at the western portion of the site surcharge
and settlement monitoring programs may be restricted to this area An approximate
extent of the area where surcharge and settlement monitoring program should be
implemented is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
A slope ratio of up to one and one half horizontal to one vertical may
be used for the temporary surcharge material The surcharge fill should be placed in
level lifts of 12 inches in height The first layer of the surcharge fill should be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3787 00 Page 8
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Adequate precautions
must be taken to preclude erosion of the surcharge fill slopes during construction and
the subsequent surcharge period to attain the required consolidation
In general the surcharge material should be similar to the construction fill material
Ideally if the surcharge material is the same as the construction fill material it may be
used on other fill areas at the project site after completion of the surcharge program
Based on our engineering analyses we believe that a settlement period of about
2 months would be adequate to achieve approximately 80 to 90 percent consolidation
However it should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time
required for the filled ground to settle since the settlement rate will be affected by
variations of the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition
For the compressible soils encountered at the site we believe that the estimated
settlement period could vary as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual settlement
period required Therefore the actual settlement rate should be monitored and
provisions should be made for potential delays in the construction schedule if a longer
surcharge period is required
Settlement Monitoring
It should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact
amount of time required for the filled ground to settle because of variable localized soil
conditions Therefore the actual settlement rate should be monitored during construction
after the surcharge program is complete
To monitor the actual settlement rate during the surcharge program we
recommend that a minimum of four settlement gauges be installed in the fill areas A
typical settlement gauge is shown on Plate 3 The elevation of the gauges should be

A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential for

Fill Material and Placement
Cuts of about 3 to 5 feet are planned at the eastern end of the proposed building
and the parking lot The borings in this area indicate that the cut material may
consist primarily of silty clay saprolite soils with some remnant boulders and
weathered rock The saprolite soils at the site exhibit moderate expansive
potential therefore it should only be used in selected areas To reduce the
expansive pressures from these soils they should be kept a minimum of 3 feet
below the concrete floor slabs and 5 feet laterally behind retaining walls
The saprolite soil fills should be moistened to 3 to 5 percent above optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 10 inch lifts and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557 91
It is anticipated that off site borrow will be required for the fill material Imported
fill material should consist of non expansive select granular materials less than
3 inches in maximum size with between 10 and 30 percent passing the
No 200 sieve The material should be tested and approved by the geotechnical
engineer prior to transportation to the project site
The fill should be placed in maximum 10 inch thick loose level lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91
The upper 6 inches of subgrade should be compacted to 95 percent of its
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91

Fill Settlements
Due to soft subsoil conditions below the western portion of the proposed
building the estimated general ground settlements induced by the weight of the
new fill are on the order of 2 to 6 inches It is estimated that 90 percent of the fill
settlements may occur in about 2 months Therefore site filling should start as
early as practicable and the building construction activity should be delayed until
the settlements have reduced to tolerate levels
It is further recommended that construction of slab on grade and rigid pavements
in this area be delayed towards the end of the project in order to allow maximum
ground settlement to occur
Slopes

Building Foundations
It is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous
footings bearing on the compacted fill and or saprolite soils encountered at shallow
depths may be used to support the proposed building Based on the subsoil conditions
encountered and the structural loads anticipated for the proposed development footing
settlements on the order of less than 1 inch may be anticipated for the column footings
with differential settlement of approximately one half the amount
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for design of footing foundations bearing on the fills and or saprolite soils
encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces We recommend that the footing foundations be structurally connected together
with tie beams to improve the rigidity of the foundation thus reducing the anticipated
differential settlement
W O 3787 00 Page 12
We recommend that footings be embedded a minimum depth of about 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade If foundations are located next to utility
trenches or easements they should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soil around footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
near surface soils
It is recommended that the bottom of footings bear on the recompacted
near surface soils or compacted fill If soft and or loose materials are encountered at
the bottom of the footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with granular material compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should be
extended down to the underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
Structures Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we
anticipate that about 5 feet of cuts and 10 feet of fills will be required to achieve the
proposed finished subgrade Because of the soft deposits underlying portions of the site
some amount of ground settlement may be anticipated under the new fill loads
Therefore construction of the slabs on grade should be delayed as long as practicable in
order to permit maximum ground settlement to occur
The near surface soils encountered at the proposed site consist of clayey silt and
silty clay which exhibit a moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to
moisture fluctuations Therefore to reduce the potential for structural distress to lightly
loaded slabs we recommend that a minimum of 24 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material be provided below the concrete slab The subgrades for
slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

An existing 20 foot wide water easement crosses the western portion of the site
The 42 inch pipe within the easement is the major water source for the U S Navy The

Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most common
cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This process may
cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not confined can result in
significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with
little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally liquefaction is not likely to
occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface The greater
overburden pressure can be sufficient to prevent liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is mostly underlain by very stiff to
hard cohesive soils Therefore we believe that the potential for liquefaction at this project
site is minimal
Drainage
The finish grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain all surface run off away from the
slabs and footing foundations

Percolation Testing
Four percolation tests were performed in Boring 13 and 14 in the
proposed parking area to evaluate the subsurface permeability conditions at the site The
falling head method was used for the percolation testing After the boring was drilled
water was injected into the borings To obtain a steady state percolation rate the soil
was allowed to soak in the water for at least four hours After the initial soaking period
water was readjusted and drop of the water level in the boring was measured Results of
the falling head percolation tests are presented in Appendix C
The test indicated that the subsurface silty soils have coefficient of permeability k
in the range of 6 7 x 10 to 1 5 x 10 centimeters per second which corresponds to about
0 014 to 0 032 gallons per square foot per day

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Twelve one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized on
Plates B 1 and B 2
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 3
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil Classification
Results of the test are presented on Plate B 4
Four direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear tests are
presented on Plates B 5 thru B 8
Four consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate compressibility characteristics of the on site soils Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plates B 9 thru B 12
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on the near surface soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soil Results of the tests are presented on Plate B 13 and B 14
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
near surface soils Results of the tests are presented on Plate B 15 and B 16
Two minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 and tests were performed to
evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils Results of the tests are summarized on
Plate B 17
