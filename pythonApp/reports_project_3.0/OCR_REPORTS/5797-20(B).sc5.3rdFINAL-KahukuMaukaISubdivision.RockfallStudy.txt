REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
The project site straddles the eroded north facing foothill of the Koolau Range and
is within the Hoolapa Gulch drainage basin as shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 The drainage basin contains an intermittent stream channel which passes
through the project site The project site interior contains some hills bordering
Hoolapa Gulch which are generally composed of scattered low relief basaltic rock
outcroppings mixed with residual soil materials Other taller slopes containing frequent
massive basalt rock outcroppings reside outside the project limits at the northwestern
corner of the site Based on our reconnaissance the hilly terrain within the project site and
vicinity is underlain by weathered volcanic rock and derived residual soils of the
Tertiary age Koolau Volcanic Series

towards the north and the existing Charlie Road along the eastern perimeter Charlie Road

and the adjacent private Kahuku Training Facility at Lot 1190 A Other military and

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Site reconnaissance was performed to assist in the evaluation of the existing
project site conditions with respect to natural hazards such as rockfall potential slope
stability and debris flow potential In addition to our site reconnaissance we performed
computer simulation and statistical analysis of potential rockfall activity using the Colorado
Rockfall Simulation Program Version 4 CRSP
Based on our evaluation of the existing project site conditions with respect to
potential natural hazards such as rockfall slope instability and debris flow it is our opinion
that the site is suitable for agricultural subdivision development and is feasible from a
geotechnical judgment provided that the rockfall mitigation recommendations provided
herein are implemented
Existing Slope and Drainage Conditions
Based on our site reconnaissance evidence of previous slope instability related to
debris flow and landslide activity was not observed Some shallow near surface erosion
features comprising the residual and saprolitic soils were encountered The surface soil
erosion conditions were observed mainly confined to limited areas of steeper slopes at the
northern corner of Lot 3 and locally on the lower easterly facing slope within Lot 3 westerly
of Hoolapa Gulch Evidence of other deep seated earth movement was not encountered
Based on our site reconnaissance and review of available topographic maps and
aerial photos the primary established drainage feature that passes through the project site
is the intermittent stream channel within Hoolapa Gulch Based on our observation of
Hoolapa Gulch evidence of previous hazardous debris flow activity such as anomalous
earth debris deposits excessive channel scour and slope toe undermining were not
observed
Rockfall Simulation Analysis CRSP
As previously discussed we performed a site reconnaissance to visually identify
and locate potential rockfall hazards such as loose surface boulders and unstable rock
outcroppings residing within the project site and the adjacent up slopes The site
reconnaissance also permitted us to evaluate probable rolling trajectories in relation to the
existing topography and proposed development boundaries Following the site
reconnaissance selected information recorded in the field was input to the CRSP to
validate the field observations
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input
parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project
were assigned based on the observations and measurements collected in the field The
program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on hill slope
topographic profiles and other specific input information such as rock size shape and

Our rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable interception and
catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the computer rockfall
simulation analysis CRSP The 90 percent catchment criteria is a target that is commonly
used in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified by a statistical
and probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences such as rockfall activity
Since the analyses are based on statistics and probability a 100 percent criteria is
impractical to achieve
Based on our CRSP analyses portions of Lots 2 and 3 could be affected by falling
rock emanating from varying hillside sources located both inside and outside of the
proposed development boundary Our analyses indicate that the vegetated lower gradient
slopes downhill from the mapped potential rockfall source areas could be used as a
natural rockfall hazard buffer that effectively dissipates falling rock energy Thus because
there is an abundance of open space associated with the subdivision of the agricultural
lots an effective and low maintenance control for falling rock encroachment appears to be
the establishment of dwelling structure setbacks away from the potential rockfall source
areas

control The developer has expressed interest in adopting a low maintenance and
non structural rockfall mitigation program for this agricultural subdivision development

Based on our study we recommend a mitigation program consisting of the
establishment of dwelling structure location restrictions and setbacks for Lots 2 and 3
Through the use of development setbacks a significant area of land is restricted to
permanent dwelling structure construction however it appears there is adequate
remaining land area for dwelling structure development outside of the established dwelling
setback areas to make the rockfall mitigation concept feasible to implement The proposed
dwelling restriction area is comprehensive and takes into account the widespread and
varied slope exposures of the potential rockfall source areas and the proposed flood and
building setback area encompassing Hoolapa Gulch
The construction of other rockfall control and containment structures such as
rockfall impact barrier fencing net drapery systems and catchment ditches are less
desirable due to the high construction and probable replacement costs the long term
maintenance requirements and access issues
Based on our study it is our professional opinion that an effective and low
maintenance rockfall mitigation program for this agricultural subdivision project should
consist of the establishment of dwelling structure location restrictions
Dwelling Structure Location Restrictions
The establishment of permanent dwelling location restrictions for rockfall protection
is appropriate where the site conditions terrain and available land area are
favorable for the natural dissipation of rolling rock kinetic energy within a designated
no build buffer land area Dwellings such as permanent residential structures
should not be permitted for construction within the designated rockfall buffer area
The proposed subdivision development plan and our rockfall mitigation program
