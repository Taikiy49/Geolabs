Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the pedestrian walkway stabilization planned at the Kilauea Point National
Wildlife Refuge KIPO in the Kilauea area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our preliminary geotechnical
engineering recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the preliminary design
of pedestrian walkway stabilization measures only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
KIPO is part of the U S Fish Wildlife Service s FWS Kauai National Wildlife
Refuge Complex on the north shore of the County and Island of Kauai The refuge is
about 23 miles north of Lihue and about 2 miles north of the town of Kilauea The
203 acre refuge was established in 1985 to protect migratory seabirds and endangered
nene populations and to preserve the integrity of the historic Kilauea lighthouse and
associated facilities
The project generally consisted of evaluating the deteriorating condition of the
existing pedestrian walkway which is supported by a retaining wall constructed on a
steep slope Based on the deteriorating condition of the existing pedestrian walkway
preliminary engineering study was conducted to formulate several alternatives to repair
and or stabilize the pedestrian walkway We understand that the proposed project will
be awarded as a design build contract Therefore this report contains preliminary
geotechnical engineering recommendations only The design build contractor should
conduct his own design level engineering analysis and provide appropriate
recommendations

Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series Following the cessation of
this main shield building phase renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands and today
encompasses approximately 555 square miles with a maximum elevation of about
5 170 feet above Mean Sea Level at Mount Waialeale near the center of the island It is
believed that during the Tertiary Period about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene
Epoch basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly
circular island volcano from the ocean floor which is estimated to have been about
20 000 feet deep at the time Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch this main shield
building volcanic phase came to a halt
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence volcanism was
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch about 1 5 million years ago with the
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island This
post erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Series
Because of the long period of erosion that occurred between the eruption of the
Waimea Volcanic Series and the Koloa Volcanic Series the deposits of the Koloa
Volcanic Series typically filled the erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that
time Consequently the basaltic rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found
to overlie thick residual soil and alluvial deposits
The dominant geologic processes of weathering landform erosion and sediment
re deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs Combined with
the erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations which

Pedestrian Walkway Stabilization Measures
As mentioned previously a portion of the existing pedestrian walkway leading to
the lighthouse is supported by a retaining wall on the eastern side Based on our
observations it appears that portions of the shotcrete may be shifting downslope In
addition the downslope edge of the shotcrete apron has been undermined in many areas
Based on our observations erosion appears to be the significant cause of the
undermining of the shotcrete apron The slope faces the direction of incoming
prevailing winds In addition rainfall and resulting runoff from the pedestrian walkway
and shotcrete apron may be eroding the unprotected slope face We understand that
birds may also be burrowing into the ground to nest contributing to the undermining of
the shotcrete apron
Based on the existing deteriorating condition of the walkway retaining wall and
shotcrete apron it was desired to evaluate various stabilization measures for the
pedestrian walkway The different stabilization measures will involve varying degrees of
impact to the Refuge and its wildlife Therefore selection of stabilization measure for
the project will need to consider the environmental factors as well as technical issues
The following subsections summarize the various stabilization measures that may be
considered and implemented at the project
Relocation of Walkway
The existing walkway is located along the east face of the slope The west side of
the walkway slopes up to a small hill By relocating the walkway to the west the
new walkway would be further away from the face of the hill and would
significantly reduce the potential for future stability issues resulting from erosion
of the slope and undermining of the walkway However relocation of the walkway
would require some grading of the hill and or construction of a new retaining wall
In addition construction of a relocated walkway may be considered a significant
disturbance to the wildlife habitat The preliminary design of conventional
retaining walls may follow the guidelines provided in the Retaining Structures
section of this report

New Tieback Anchor System
Due to the deteriorating condition of the existing retaining wall a permanent
tieback anchor system may be utilized to stabilize the existing retaining wall A
permanent tieback anchor system would consist of installing tieback anchors
through the existing CMU retaining wall to provide the lateral support for a new
shotcrete retaining wall in front of the existing CMU retaining wall
In general the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a
borehole through the existing CMU retaining wall installing a steel tendon or
reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the borehole We envision the
permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally from the bond
between the grout and the very stiff to hard saprolite anticipated at the project
site
In order to reduce the potential for future undermining of the bottom face
of the retaining wall the retaining wall will have to be embedded into the existing
slope Therefore construction of the tieback anchor system would include
removal of the existing shotcrete apron and excavation of the existing slope to
embed the wall footing Significant disturbance to the wildlife habitat should be
anticipated with this alternative In addition construction BMPs would need to
address the potential for excavation debris falling into the ocean below The
preliminary design of tieback anchor system may follow the guidelines provided
in the Tieback Anchor Wall section of this report
New Walkway Supported on Deep Foundations
In lieu of stabilizing the existing slope a consideration may be given to stabilizing
the walkway by constructing a new walkway supported on a deep foundation
system The new walkway may follow the same grades and alignment as the
existing walkway The walkway may consist of a reinforced concrete deck or
other acceptable materials supported on micropile foundations The new
pedestrian walkway would be designed so the micropiles would support the
walkway even if undermining of the walkway areas occur in the future

Retaining Structures
The following general guidelines are provided for preliminary design of
conventional retaining structures at the project site
Retaining Structure Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend using shallow
continuous strip footings to support the new retaining walls An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used to design the
footings bearing on the re compacted on site materials or compacted fills This
bearing value is for sizing footings based on dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
In general the retaining structure footings should have a minimum width of
18 inches In addition wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded
a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade A qualified
geotechnical engineer should observe the footing excavations during construction
to evaluate the exposed conditions

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as open graded gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation
placed immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base
of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weep holes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product also should be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on site
materials or free draining compacted fills Unless covered by concrete slabs the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious
materials to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration behind the walls

competent subsurface conditions the use of a permanent tieback anchor system to
provide the lateral support for a new reinforced shotcrete facing in front of the existing
CRM wall may be considered for the project In general the permanent tieback anchor
system may be designed to provide resistance to the lateral earth pressures and lateral

In general the tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a borehole installing
a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the borehole The basic
components of a grouted tieback anchor consist of the anchorage and the bonded and
unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar In general the unbonded length
of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that does not
have direct contact with the grout allowing the tendon or reinforcing bar to elongate
elastically transferring the resisting force from the structure to the bonded length The
bonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar
that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the applied load into the ground
The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of the
permanent tieback system for this project
Tieback Anchor Design
In general we envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support
principally from the bond between the grout and the stiff clayey soils encountered
in our borings The bond stress which is the pullout resistance per unit area of
the grout soil interface contact is variable depending on the type of soil and
grout the overburden stress and the construction procedures used in installing
the permanent tieback anchors For preliminary design purposes an average
ultimate bond stress of 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the
stiff silty soils encountered in the borings The ultimate bond stress used in the
design will need to be confirmed during construction by load testing

may be supported on a deep foundation system consisting of cased micropiles In
general the cased micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually
less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile
foundation typically is constructed by drilling a hole placing reinforcing steel in the hole
and grouting the hole Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in
access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In
addition the micropile installation generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent
structures the adjacent soils and the environment
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend using a cased micropile system with an outside casing diameter of at least
7 5 inches to support the new structures The micropiles should be drilled and grouted
with a temporary casing extending down for the full depth of the micropile to the tip
elevation In addition the unbonded zone of the micropiles should have a permanent
casing with an outside diameter OD of at least 7 5 inches The permanent casing
unbonded zone should extend at least 5 feet below the elevation where the anticipated
future erosion may occur
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we
recommend utilizing a micropile with an allowable compressive load capacity of up to
about 60 to 80 kips to support the pedestrian bridge The allowable compressive load

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 101 through 103 to depths of about 40 to 65 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We
used a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools to
advance the borings
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by laboratory testing Soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
