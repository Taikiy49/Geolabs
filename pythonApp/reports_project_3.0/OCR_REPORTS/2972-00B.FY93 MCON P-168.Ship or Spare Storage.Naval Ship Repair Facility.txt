PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a storage facility to provide additional indoor storage
space for the materials required for voyage repairs by the Ship Repair Facility within the
U S Naval Station at Orote Point on the island of Guam in the Mariana Islands
The new storage facility would consist of a one story structure with concrete frame
and masonry unit walls providing approximately 26 300 square feet of floor space We
understand that the proposed concrete floor slab will be subjected to forklift traffic for
handling of storage materials Supporting facilities will include paved parking areas
walkways and site utilities
Based on the information provided to us by the structural engineer the maximum
column loads are estimated to be on the order of 500 kips for the proposed building The
finish grade of the proposed building will be at approximately 11 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL We anticipate that minor fills on the order of one 1 to two feet 2 will be needed
to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed building pad elevation
Previous explorations by others in the vicinity of the project site indicate that the
area may be underlain by compressible deposits at shallow depths Therefore we
anticipate that pile foundations may be required to support the proposed storage facility
under the anticipated heavy loadings

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the western portion of the island of Guam the largest
and southernmost of the Mariana Islands The approximate location of the project site
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The foundation of the island is composed of volcanic rocks including pyroclastic
materials which were extruded during early to mid Tertiary time Volcanic activity ceased
during the Oligocene epoch Following the cessation of the volcanic activity extensive
coral algal reef deposits were laid down on the island from the Miocene through
Pleistocene epochs
Tectonic uplift has raised these relatively old reef deposits Subsequent marine
activity created lagoonal environments where fine grained sediments accumulated Within
the last century development of the military facility has shaped the existing land by
grading anc filling

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration of the site thick compressible deposits consisting
of soft clayey silt and loose sandy gravel materials were encountered below a surficial fill
crust to a depth of about 70 feet below the surface Because of the significant structural
loads anticipated for this project a shallow foundation system used to support the
proposed storage facility would be subjected to appreciable total and differential
settlements As a result we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of
friction piles be used to support the proposed storage facility structure An end bearing
Stratum was not encountered within the maximum depths of our exploration of up to 135
feet below the surface
We understand that fills on the order of up to two feet 2 are planned within the
limits of the proposed structure We also understand that significant structural loads will
be imposed on the floor slab of the structure Because of these additional loads which
would be imposed on the significant thickness of the compressible deposits future
settlements of the ground surface are anticipated As a result we recommend that the
floor slab for the proposed storage facility be structurally supported on pile foundations
to reduce the potential for differential settlements between a floor slab supported on grade
and the columns supported on piles
Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to foundation
design site grading and pavement design are further discussed in the following sections
of this report

Pile Foundations
Because of the anticipated high column loads for the proposed storage facility we
recommend that deep pile foundations deriving support principally from skin friction
between the pile shaft and the sandy limestone gravel be used to support the proposed
structure Based on the data obtained from our field exploration and laboratory testing
piles driven through the upper fill crust and compressible deposits to a depth of
approximately 100 feet below the surface is recommended to develop the recommended
Capacity to support the proposed structure
Vertical Pile Capacities
We recommend that 16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles with
an allowable compressible load capacity of 70 tons per pile be used to support the
proposed storage facility structure The piles will derive support primarily from the
frictional resistance between the pile shafts and the sandy limestone gravels Due
to the compressible nature of the loose and soft clayey silt deposits the frictional
supporting capacity of the piles within these loose and soft layers have been
discounted To develop the recommended pile capacity a pile embedment of
approximately 100 feet below the existing surface will be required A minimum pile
spacing of four feet 4 center to center is recommended to avoid reduction in pile
capacity due to group action
An allowable uplift capacity of 40 kips per pile may be used in design The
recommended pile capacities are for sustained loading conditions For transient
loads such as wind or seismic forces the compressive and uplift load capacities
may be increased by one third

To facilitate handling and pile driving we believe that the long piles may need to
be casted in two 2 sections to be connected by adequately designed splice cans
if splicing is needed we recommend that the upper sections be a minimum of 60
feet long to develop the required uplift and lateral load capacities if a simple non
mechanical type of splice is used Otherwise we recommend that special
mechanical splice connections capable of transmitting bending moments be used
to connect the pile sections
The above pile lengths are for initial cost estimation purposes The actual
production pile lengths should be estimated after completion of a test pile program
Foundation Settlements
Pile settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than one inch 1 with
differential settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that amount
Settlements should be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Predrilling
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered during our field exploration it is our
opinion that the piles should be able to penetrate through the surficial fill crust
without predrilling This assumption should be confirmed during the test pile
program proposed for the project Therefore we recommend that unit prices for
predrilling be obtained during the bidding process If predrilling is found to be
necessary during the test pile program we recommend the predrilling be included
in the production pile driving operations Recommendations pertaining to
predrilling would be presented after completion of the test pile program

Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
at least 50 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the loose and soft deposits may be performed
at a lower energy level to reduce the development of tensile stresses within the
piles which could cause damage cracking to the piles
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
during driving This should be accomplished by using a driving resistance
procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the more
sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity The
driving criteria will be developed after the selection of the pile driving equipment for
production pile driving Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose sandy soil the
hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with reduced energy levels so
that excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven
This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of
the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs Hawaii should review the pile

Floor Slabs
We understand that significant live loading of the floor slabs are planned for the
concrete floors of the storage facility Because of the presence of the loose and soft
compressible soils at the site we anticipate that future settlement of the ground surface
would occur due to the loading imposed on the compressible soils by the new fills floor
slab and the anticipated loading on the slab As a result to reduce the potential
differential settlements between the structure supported on piles and the floor slab
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2972 00 B Page 14
supported on grade we recommend that the floor slabs be structurally supported on pile
foundations Floor slabs should be designed to span between pile foundations
A base course layer beneath the structurally supported floor slab will not be
necessary However the floor slab should be supported on a prepared subgrade The
subgrade soils beneath slabs should be scarified to a depth of eight inches 8 and
compacted to at least 95 percent of the maximum dry density determined in accordance
with ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
resistance of the pile soil system to deflect and passive resistance on the front side of pile
caps and grade beams Due to the possible future settlement of the ground surface
sliding friction between the bottom of the slab and the supporting subgrade should be
neglected
The lateral load resisting capacity of the 161 inch octagonal pile is estimated to
be on the order of 25 kips per pile This assumes a lateral deflection of 4 inch at the top
of the pile for a free head condition For passive soil resistance an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth may be used
Site Grading
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or designated to receive fill should be
scarified to a depth of at least six inches 6 moisture conditioned to above the optimum
