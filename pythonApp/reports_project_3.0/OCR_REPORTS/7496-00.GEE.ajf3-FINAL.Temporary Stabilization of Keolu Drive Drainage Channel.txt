SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We anticipate the subsurface conditions behind the collapsed drainage channel wall 
section will consist of medium stiff to stiff silty clay fill material with varying amounts of sand, 
gravel, cobbles, and boulders of up to about 2 feet in diameter.  
Groundwater is not anticipated within the exposed channel wall section. However, 
seepage water was observed in a hand hole (Hand Boring No. 1) at a depth of about 
6 inches below the existing ground surface. It should be noted that Hand Boring No. 1 was 
located about 6 inches away from an existing irrigation line. We believe this seepage may 
be attributed to a leak in this irrigation line. In addition, localized seepage water was also 
observed seeping through the existing gravel material at the bottom of the exposed slope 
face at the time of our field exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject 
to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
We recommend re-grading the existing near-vertical slope to an inclination of 1H:1V 
or flatter as a temporary mitigation. To reduce the risk of slope erosion from surface 
runoff/channel flow and shallow/raveling slides with a slope inclination of 1H:1V, we 
recommend providing a shotcrete channel lining extended to at least the height of the 
existing channel wall. The remainder of the slope should be covered with a Turf 
Reinforcement Mat (TRM). In addition, we recommend keying the shotcrete into the cut 
slope at the top and bottom of the shotcrete lining to reduce the potential for significant 
erosion and undermining of the temporary channel lining. This shotcrete key should be at 
least 12 inches wide by 12 inches deep and should be installed at the slope toe adjacent to 
the bottom concrete slab of the existing channel and at the top of the shotcrete lining.  
To reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures behind the temporary channel lining, 
we recommend installing a drainage system consisting of 2-foot wide geocomposite drain 
strips extending from the top of the lining to the toe of the slope and hydraulically connected 
to a weephole near the toe of the slope. In addition, we recommend repairing the leaking 
irrigation line at the top of the existing slope to reduce the potential for significant water 
infiltration into the underlying soils.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Temporary Stabilization of Keolu Drive Drainage Channel project in 
Kailua on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
temporary stabilization measures for the collapsed drainage channel wall. The findings 
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of 
this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project consists of providing temporary stabilization measures for roughly 
90 linear feet of drainage channel on the southern side of Keolu Drive in Kailua on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand the eastern side of the existing drainage channel 
wall adjacent to the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints property collapsed on 
January 19, 2017. A layout of the project site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the information provided, the existing concrete-lined drainage channel is 
about 20 feet wide at the collapsed wall section, with 7.5-foot high walls on both sides of 
the channel. In addition, an approximate 5 to 6-foot high grassed slope was observed 
above the collapsed channel wall section with a slope inclination of about two horizontal 
to one vertical (2H:1V).  
We understand it is desired to only provide temporary stabilization measures to 
this section of collapsed drainage channel wall at this time, and the permanent channel 
wall repair/replacement will be included in a separate design contract at a later date.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is largely composed of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that the Waianae 
Volcano became extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is 
partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central Oahu. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell because less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
filled pre-existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and at the heads of valleys, erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock materials from the steep valley 
walls and transportation down slope toward a valley axis primarily by gravity as colluvial 
earth materials. Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, 
stream flow processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and 
deposited as alluvial earth materials.

In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream 
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short duration. Because of this, transport 
of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly 
sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional base 
levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical processes begin 
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material. 
Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the coarse-grained portion 
of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock, or conglomerate. 
Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in 
headwaters.  This continued erosion generates material, which is transported downslope, 
covering the older alluvial deposits. 
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, these newer sediments 
are generally transient in terms of geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density 
are generally less than those of the older, partially consolidated deposits. Land 
development and reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project 
site to its present form.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions behind the collapsed 
drainage channel wall section generally consisted of medium stiff to stiff silty clay fill 
material with varying amounts of sand, gravel, cobbles, and boulders up to about 2 feet 
in diameter. We did not encounter groundwater in the boring at the time of our field 
exploration. However, seepage water should be expected at the site. It should be noted 
that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
In general, we believe that the primary geotechnical considerations for the 
temporary stabilization of this section of collapsed drainage channel wall include the 
following: 
Cutting back the near-vertical slope face to an inclination that provides 
short-term stability and reduces encroachment into adjacent property 
Adequate protective lining of the slope face to reduce potential for 
significant erosion 
Repair of the leaking irrigation line observed at the top of slope 
Based on the results of our field exploration and our slope stability analyses, we 
recommend cutting back the existing near-vertical slope to an inclination of 1H:1V or 
flatter to provide sufficient short-term slope stability. We recommend providing a shotcrete 
channel lining extended to at least the height of the existing channel wall. The remainder 
of the slope should be covered with a Turf Reinforcement Mat (TRM).  
In addition, we recommend keying the shotcrete into the cut slope at the top and 
bottom of the shotcrete lining to reduce the potential for significant erosion and 
undermining of the temporary channel lining. This shotcrete key should be at least 
12 inches wide by 12 inches deep and should be installed at the slope toe adjacent to the 
bottom concrete slab of the existing channel and at the top of the shotcrete lining. 
As mentioned above, seepage water was observed through the existing gravel 
material at the bottom of the exposed slope face at the time of our field exploration. In 
addition, seepage water was observed in Hand Boring No. 1 at a depth of about 6 inches 
below the existing ground surface, possibly from a leaking irrigation line near the

3.1 
Global Slope Stability 
We recommend cutting back the existing slope with near-vertical faces to a slope 
inclination of 1H:1V or flatter. Global slope stability of the proposed 1H:1V cut slope was 
evaluated using the computer program Slide from Rocscience Inc. Bishop’s simplified 
method was used to evaluate the balance of forces and moments acting within the slope to 
determine the factors of safety for the slope stability based on the generalized subsurface 
profile and shear strength parameters. The Bishop’s simplified method of slope stability 
analysis assumes a circular slip surface, which is divided into slices with finite mass. The 
following parameters were used in our global slope stability analyses to evaluate the 
rotational slope failure mechanism:  
1. 
Slope geometry 
2. 
Density of the existing fill materials from laboratory tests 
3. 
Shear strength of the existing fill materials from laboratory tests and DCP test 
results 
Based on the results of the global slope stability analyses, it is our opinion that the 
above slope grading should provide sufficient short-term global slope stability. To reduce 
the risk of slope erosion and shallow/raveling slides, we recommend installing a protective 
lining of the slope face consisting of a combination of shotcrete and TRM. 
3.2

Based on our site observations and the results of our field exploration, we 
recommend placing a protective lining along the new 1H:1V cut slope face to reduce the 
potential for significant erosion and shallow/raveling slides. This protective lining may consist

3.3.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, loose surface soils, debris, 
deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
disposed of properly off-site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials. 
3.3.2 Slope Cut Back 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing slope behind the collapsed drainage 
channel wall section with near-vertical faces should be cut back to a slope 
inclination of 1H:1V or flatter. We recommend grading the slope to a relatively 
smooth surface, removing any rills and gullies. These grading requirements are 
intended to provide a good contact between the firm ground surface and the 
proposed protective lining of the slope. 
3.3.3 Excavations 
Based on the anticipated grading and subsurface conditions, excavation for this 
project will generally consist of cutting back the slope face to a 1H:1V inclination or 
flatter and excavations for anchor trenches for the proposed protective lining. Some 
of the excavations may encounter cobbles and boulders. It is anticipated that most 
of the material may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
However, excavations encountering large boulders may require the use of hoerams. 
Care should be taken when removing large boulders from the slope face to avoid 
over-steepening of the slope resulting in new slope instability. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our visual observation of the exposed slope face behind the collapsed 
drainage channel wall and limited field exploration at the site. Contractors should be 
encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make their 
own reasonable and prudent interpretation.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one hand hole, designated as Hand Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 6 feet below 
the existing ground surface. In addition, collection of one bulk sample, designated as Bulk-1, 
of the soils exposed from the collapsed drainage channel wall section for laboratory testing. 
The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
boring was drilled using portable hand augering equipment.  
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the hand augering operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 20-pound hammer falling 18 inches. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the first and second 6 inches of a 12-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample 
depth. The penetration resistance shown on the Log of Boring indicates the number of 
blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be 
factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
A Pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test result is 
summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were also explored using Dynamic 
Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing at two locations, designated as DCP-1 and DCP-2, 
extending to depths of about 7.8 and 8.4 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate DCP locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) was used for probing by driving an 
8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height.

converted to standard penetration resistance (SPT, ASTM D1586) using correlation 
between Penetration Index (PI) and SPT, developed by Sowers and Hedges and later 
Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs of DCP at the 
appropriate depths, presented on the Logs of DCP, Plates B-1 and B-2.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate C-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate C-3. It should be noted that a portion of this laboratory test could not 
be conducted because the sample contained an excessive amount of gravel.
