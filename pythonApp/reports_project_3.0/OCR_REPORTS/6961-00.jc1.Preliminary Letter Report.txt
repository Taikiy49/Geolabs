GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EVALUATION 
LANAI ADVENTURE PARK 
ISLAND OF LANAI, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Eguchi: 
This letter report presents our preliminary assessment of foundations and site 
grading at the Lanai Adventure Park project site on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Site Plan, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering recommendations 
resulting from our site reconnaissance and preliminary field exploration. These 
recommendations are intended for the foundations and site grading only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project involves design and construction of an adventure park by Synergo, who 
is based in Portland, Oregon. We understand that the proposed adventure park consists of 
eight ziplines, an adventure center, and mountain bike trails with other adventure activity 
courses. Based on the information provided, we understand that the ziplines are planned 
to be installed through the mountain terrain, supported by power poles (Class 1 and 
Class 2) about 12 to 14 feet and/or 40 to 50 feet tall. Three of eleven pole locations do not 
have vehicle access and are planned to be constructed via helicopter lifting for equipment 
and material. 
The proposed adventure center consists of a one-story building with shop, locker 
room and restroom. Detailed locations of the poles for the zipline tour were surveyed by 
others. Structural details of the poles and the building were not available at the time this 
report was prepared. To expedite the adventure park design, we proposed performing 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing, to examine the subsurface bearing 
characteristics. We also proposed drilling and sampling test borings for the foundation 
design parameters, to validate the DCP testing data.

Purpose and Scope 
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and

Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is the 
result of volcanic extrusion on the southwest rift zone. The rift zones intersect at the island 
summit. The summit of the shield volcano collapsed to form a caldera from which a 
shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends south-southeast toward 
Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff indicate that this Manele Graben lies 
along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. 
The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe 
tholeiitic basaltic flows that are generally inclined at about 6 to 15 degrees from horizontal 
(where not disturbed by faulting). Volcanic rocks on the Island of Lanai are grouped as the 
Lanai Volcanic Series. The near-surface soils generally consist of well-drained, 
fine-textured and moderately fine-textured soils derived from volcanic ash and the in-situ 
weathering of the igneous rocks. The formation of the Island of Lanai was also the result of 
large changes in sea-level that caused submergence and re-emergence of the land mass 
through geologic time. Faulting and stream erosion in conjunction with changes in 
sea-level are also responsible for the high sea cliffs along the southern and western 
coastlines. 
The project site is northerly from the Palawai Basin and traverse across Kapano 
Gulch in the mountain range. Most of the soils in the project area are residual/saprolitic 
soils, derived from the in-situ weathering of volcanic rock. In general, the residual and 
saprolitic soils grade to basaltic rock formation with depth.

Subsurface Condition 
Our field exploration program consisted of probing at the proposed zipline pole 
locations and adventure center building, using a dynamic cone penetrometer (DCP). A 
total of twelve DCP probes, designated as P-1.1 through P-9.2, were conducted, including 
ten at the zipline pole locations and two at the adventure center, extending to the 
maximum depth of about 11 feet below the ground surface. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consisted of 3 to 4 feet of medium 
stiff to very stiff clayey soils with SPT resistance of about 10 to 30 blows per foot (bpf), 
overlying hard saprolitic soils with SPT resistance of about 30 to 50 bpf. It should be noted 
that the above SPT resistance was estimated by the empirical relationship to DCP 
penetration index in millimeter per blow. 
It should be noted that the upper 2 feet of the clayey soils may have soft to medium 
stiff consistency due to softening from high precipitation in the mountain range. Localized 
deeper soft clay zones may occur. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the probes at the time of our field exploration. 
However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, surface water 
runoff, and other factors. 
Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we envision using 
shallow continuous footings to support the new one-story adventure center building 
planned at the site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot 
(psf) may be used for the design of shallow foundation system. 
We recommend installing the proposed cross-plates within the very stiff in-situ 
clayey soils to develop resistance to support the guy wires of the poles. The cross-plates 
should be embedded at least 2 feet below the ground surface or deeper. Each cross-plate 
should be tested for pull-out resistance, to verity the design load capacity. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site grading 
are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Foundations 
We envision that the proposed one-story adventure center building subgrades 
will consist of stiff in-situ clayey soils.  Therefore, we recommend using shallow spread 
and/or continuous footings to support the proposed structure at the project site.  As an 
alternative, the foundations for support of the buildings may also consist of 
thickened-edge slab footings. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of 
shallow foundations. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be

Where soft clayey soils are encountered at the foundation subgrade, we 
recommend over-excavating the soft clayey soils below the building footing footprint to 
expose the underlying stiff in-situ clayey soils or over-excavating to a maximum depth of 
24 inches below the subbase layer.  The subgrade over-excavation should extend at 
least 3 feet beyond the edge of the footings.  The over-excavation should be backfilled 
with compacted structural fill materials. 
In general, the building footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent exterior grade.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or 
on sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of 
the slope. 
In order to reduce the potential for appreciable differential settlement, the new 
building structure should be situated entirely over cuts and should not straddle across 
cut and fill transitions.  If the building structure is to straddle a cut and fill transition, 
Geolabs should be contacted to provide supplemental recommendations to reduce the 
potential for appreciable differential settlements due to a cut and fill transition condition. 
The structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials 
generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the 
occurrence of voids in the compacted mass.  The subbase materials recommended 
below the water tanks should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates (3-inch minus 
granular materials).  The structural fill and subbase materials should be compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, total settlement of foundations is estimated to be 
on the order of 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of about 0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance 
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth 
pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of 
foundations.  A coefficient of friction of 0.3 may be used for foundations bearing on the 
compacted subgrade.  Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be 
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 330 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf) for foundations embedded in the compacted structural fill materials.  These 
values assume that the soils around the foundations are well compacted.  Unless covered

Slabs-On-Grade 
Concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the floors of the new building.  For the 
interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic, we recommend providing a 
minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support.  The cushion 
fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) and would also 
serve as a capillary moisture break.  Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or 
sustained vibrations, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted aggregate base course should 
be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The aggregate 
base course layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion layer or compacted aggregate base course layer.  Flexible floor coverings should 
be considered above the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
We envision concrete pads and exterior fuel storage would be constructed at the 
project site.  In general, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer 
consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) or select borrow subbase 
below exterior concrete slabs.  Select borrow subbase should be compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction.  To reduce the potential for significant slab cracking due to 
concrete shrinkage, crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width 
of the concrete pads with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor slab-on-grade operations to observe the 
operation to evaluate the compaction quality of the fill material. 
Ground Anchors 
We understand that the cross-plate anchors were proposed to support the zipline 
poles via guy wires. Based on the preliminary field exploration, the near-surface in-situ 
clayey soils may exhibit soft consistency due to softening by high precipitation and natural 
weathering process. We recommend embedding the cross-plate anchors at least 2 feet 
below the ground surface. In addition, the cross-plate anchors should be embedded deep 
enough to maintain a ratio of the embedment depth (H) and height of the anchorage (h) no 
less than 1.5.

Retaining Structures 
Retaining wall foundations may be designed in accordance with the parameters 
presented in the “Shallow Foundations” section herein. Wall foundations on relatively flat 
areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade. For sloping ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to obtain a 
minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the 
footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope 
should be constructed in stepped footings.

Over-compaction of the retaining structure backfill should be avoided. In general, an active 
condition may be used for gravity walls and walls that are free to deflect by as much as

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, traffic loads, line loads, and point 
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in 
the design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a 
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 33 percent of the vertical surcharge 
pressure acting on the entire height of the wall, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be 
used in design. For walls that are restrained, a rectangular distribution equal to 50 percent 
of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may be used for 
design. Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge 
effects of point loads and line loads. 
In general, retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), placed 
directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the walls 
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated 
drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the drainage 
material. The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on-site granular 
soils or select granular fill material. Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches 
of backfill should consist of low expansion, impervious soils to reduce the potential for 
water infiltration behind the walls.

Site Preparation 
We anticipate that site grading will involve cuts and fills, to achieve the finished 
elevation of the adventure park facility. In general, a Geolabs representative should 
monitor the site preparation to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions during 
excavation are similar to those anticipated and/or encountered in our field exploration.  
The following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site 
preparation work. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill 
materials. 
In general, the on-site materials generated from the excavations may be re-used as 
a source of general fill and backfill materials, provided that the materials are screened of 
over-sized materials (greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension) and are free of 
deleterious materials.  Where used as fill and backfill materials, the on-site soils should be 
properly moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
Excavation 
Based on our site reconnaissance and preliminary field exploration, the project site 
is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff residual and saprolitic soils overlying weathered 
basalt rock at greater depth.  It is anticipated that the near-surface soil deposits may be 
readily excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators and 
ripping with large bulldozers.  However, some of the deeper excavations may require the 
use of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based 
on our visual observation of the existing ground conditions and field data from the DCP 
penetration testing performed in the project site.  We recommend encouraging contractors 
to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make their own interpretation. 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
We believe that the cut slopes planned at the site may be designed with a slope 
inclination of one and one-half horizontal to one vertical (1.5H:1V) or flatter.  In areas 
where soft clayey soils, unstable, and/or friable materials are exposed at the cut slope 
faces, the cut slopes may need to be flattened.  Therefore, provisions to flatten the cut 
slopes and/or slope stabilization alternatives should be considered in the design of the 
project.  A Geolabs representative should observe and map the cut slopes during

Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and structural fill materials 
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  In

Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, moisture 
conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Utility Trenches 
We envision that the utilities for the new adventure park facility may primarily 
consist of water, sewer, and drain lines. In general, good construction practices should be 
utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities. In addition, 
the excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety 
requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) for support under the pipes. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of 
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill 
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on-site soils (with a maximum 
particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in 
maximum 8-inch loose level lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent 
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. 
Where trenches are located below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill 
below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.

Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our site reconnaissance and preliminary field exploration in the 
project site. Variations of subsoil conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway.  If variations then 
appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented herein.
