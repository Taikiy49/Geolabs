REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
The project site straddles the north facing foothills of the Koolau Range and the
Kahuku coastal plain as indicated by the topography shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 The Hoolapa Gulch drainage basin encompasses the eastern portion of the
project site The drainage basin contains an intermittent stream channel which passes
through the project site The project site contains some hills encompassing above ground
weathered basaltic rock outcrops mixed with residual soils at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

Rockfall Simulation Analysis CRSP
As previously discussed we performed a site reconnaissance to visually identify
and locate potential rockfall hazards such as loose surface boulders and unstable rock
outcroppings residing within the project site and the adjacent up slopes The site
reconnaissance also permitted us to evaluate probable rolling trajectories in relation to the
existing topography and development boundaries Following the site reconnaissance
selected information recorded in the field was input to the CRSP to validate the field
observations
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input
parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project
were assigned based on the observations and measurements collected in the field The
program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on hill slope
topographic profiles and other specific input information such as rock size shape and
parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions Information obtained
from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity bouncing height
kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is useful to assist in the
site specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall impact barriers
rock catchment ditches or other rockfall containment systems
Topographic information input to the CRSP was obtained from the available project
site topographic map which is derived from an aerial survey The air photo contour map

rockfall activity Since the analyses are based on statistics and probability a 100 percent
criteria is impractical to achieve

Establishment of Dwelling Location Restriction
The establishment of a dwelling location restriction for rockfall protection is
appropriate where the site conditions terrain and available land area are favorable
for the natural dissipation of rolling rock kinetic energy within a designated
no build buffer land area Future dwelling sites would be restricted to locations
down slope from the limits of the buffer area as determined from computer rockfall
simulation analysis of probable rockfall deposition Dwellings such as permanent
residential structures should not be permitted for construction within the rockfall
buffer area The dwelling restriction boundary could be defined by ground surface
elevation or by distance from the potential rockfall hazard source s
Construction of Rockfall Catchment Ditch
A rockfall catchment ditch could be used effectively to provide rockfall
protection interception provided the ditch is constructed on suitable terrain and is of
adequate dimension to capture most potential rockfall activity modeled by computer
simulation The rockfall catchment ditch essentially consists of a below grade
excavation oriented perpendicular to the hill slope with drainage consideration in
mind for rolling rock interception and catchment purpose We believe catchment
ditches may be feasibly constructed on existing hill slopes flatter than about
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V Steeper hill slopes typically require greater
areal slope disturbance and grading limits Catchment ditches are favorable
because of the typical lower maintenance needs as opposed to rockfall protection
systems such as rockfall impact barriers and cable net drapery which are
constructed of steel components requiring maintenance and replacement In
addition the rock catchment ditch could also be used as a hillside drainage
interception system
Construction of Rockfall Impact Barrier
Rockfall impact barriers could be used as an effective rockfall protection system
provided the system is carefully designed and constructed with respect to the
anticipated kinetic energy interception criteria Rockfall impact barriers are
commonly constructed on hillsides where the protection of down slope areas from
varied and widespread sources of falling rock is necessary Rockfall impact barriers
are considered to be a viable rockfall protection measure where a specific and
limited source of potential rockfall cannot be readily identified and stabilized or
where the source area may encompass disturbance sensitive land that makes
other mitigation measures impractical to implement

Rockfall impact barriers are specially designed fences consisting of steel support
beams linked by wire rope cable or steel wire ring nets which are designed to flex
and absorb rockfall impact energy The nets and beams are supported by drilled
and grouted ground anchors at specified intervals The barrier height is typically
constructed 8 to 12 feet above ground level and black in coloration Rockfall barrier
fences require periodic inspection and maintenance to remove accumulated
boulder debris and replace worn steel components Portions of the barrier may
require repair or replacement following a very large impact where severe
deformation of the system is experienced Ultimately the rockfall barrier will require
replacement of components affected by natural environmental degradation such as
corrosion
We roughly estimate the effective life of the barrier system in the near coastal
setting such as Kahuku as being about 25 to 40 years at which time replacement
of components such as nets ground anchors and support beams may be required
to maintain the desired level of protection Stainless steel components and special
surface coatings are available to increase resistance to corrosion but at a significant
extra cost
Site Grading Flattening of Existing Slopes
Site grading consisting of cut and or fill earthwork using heavy construction
machinery could be employed to reduce the risk of rockfall hazards by altering the
existing terrain Slopes containing potential rockfall hazards could be entirely
removed or flattened to reduce the number of potential rockfall hazards and
achieve more stable slope inclinations In some cases engineered fills could be
placed to develop a protective berm raise the down slope grades or to bury
existing surface rockfall hazards Due to the widespread occurrence of large block
source rock material high on the slopes controlled excavation and complete
removal of potential rockfall hazards may not be practical at portions of the project
site
Removal of Hillside Rock Outcroppings and Boulders Slope Excavation and or
Scaling
Slope scaling is the selective removal of rockfall hazards from steep slopes using
manual labor and hand tools to pry loose rock from the slope surface and move the
material to the slope bottom for collection and disposal Specially trained scaling
crews use rappelling equipment to remove loose rocks in a methodical controlled
manner working from top to bottom on the slope Slope scaling is often employed
where access is limited and the use of heavy construction equipment is not feasible
due to the terrain The use of hydraulic rock splitters pneumatic tools and small
blasting charges may be used to remove larger rocks from the slope Due to the
widespread occurrence of large block source rock material high on the slopes

Construction of Cable Net Slope Drapery
This alternative consists of the installation of anchored cable net drapery over the
existing rock outcrops and surface boulders residing on the hillside The purpose is
to reduce the potential for rock movement initiation and to reduce momentum and
control the fall of rock material under the netting Due to the large block size and
widespread area of coverage heavy duty steel cable nets would be required as
opposed to lighter duty wire mesh drapery The net system and anchors would
require periodic inspection and maintenance to maintain the desired level of safety
and effectiveness Eventual replacement of the net system may be required due to
environmental corrosion of the galvanized steel components Stainless steel
components are available at extra cost

the potential rockfall hazards is not considered practical due to the difficult hillside access
substantial rock layer thickness and widespread distribution of the potentially hazardous
outcrops In addition some of the potential rockfall source areas are beyond the
development boundary Therefore we consider our recommended rockfall mitigation
program to be a more viable rockfall mitigation scheme because the long term
maintenance cost is lower the improvements are considered to be permanent and easier
to maintain and the catchment ditches could serve to augment hillside drainage
interception and control
In general our recommended rockfall mitigation program consists of the
construction of positively drained rockfall catchment ditches on the hillside below the
rockfall source regions and above the possible dwelling sites as depicted on the
Conceptual Rockfall Mitigation Plan Plate 4 Based on our preliminary CRSP analysis it

Based on our preliminary analysis the rockfall catchment ditch should have a floor

analysis such as the simulated modeling of potential rockfall activity

Rockfall Hazard Mitigation Design
Our professional opinion is that the recommended rockfall mitigation program offers
a comprehensive cost effective and lower maintenance mitigation system capable of
providing a high level of rockfall protection for development down slope of the existing
potential rockfall hazards identified by this study
Following the acceptance of the recommended rockfall mitigation program by the
owner design of the rockfall mitigation program may proceed For the dwelling location
restrictions some refinement of the restriction designations may be possible based on
additional input from the design team A topographic ground survey by a licensed surveyor
should be performed encompassing the location of the probable ditch and cut slope
construction sites to obtain detailed topographic information necessary for the design of
the improvements Test boring or test pit exploration may be desired to investigate and
characterize the general subsurface conditions at the proposed rockfall mitigation
construction sites
Based on our site reconnaissance the catchment ditch excavations may encounter
both alluvial colluvial clay soils with embedded boulders and hard rock formation
depending on the hillside location
We envision that some catchment ditch sections may require crossings for
driveway access at selected locations In addition because the ditch will collect surface
water runoff the ditch should be designed for positive drainage to appropriate discharge
points The design and construction of ditch crossings if desired within the individual lots
could remain the responsibility of the future lot owners
If the catchment ditch is dedicated to a community association for maintenance
responsibility a maintenance easement across the lots encompassing the ditch would
need to be established At this time we envision that the individual lot owners would be
responsible for future maintenance and repair of the rockfall protection system
