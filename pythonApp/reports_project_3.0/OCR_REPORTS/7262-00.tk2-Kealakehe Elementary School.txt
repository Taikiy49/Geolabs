SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer, less than about 
5 feet thick, overlying basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in 
the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the conceptual plans provided, the new classroom building will be 
supported on level grounds behind a new retaining wall system. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 4,000 psf may be used for design of foundations bearing on the basalt 
formation and/or compacted structural fill materials. Cavities and/or voids are commonly 
encountered within the basalt formations in this portion of the Island of Hawaii.  To reduce 
the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities and/or 
voids below foundations underlain by the basalt formation, we recommend implementing a 
program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations during construction.  
Based on field permeability test results, we believe a disposal capacity of about 
5 cfs may be used for the preliminary design of the planned drywells at the project site.  It 
should be noted that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation at the site 
can be highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the basalt 
formation or the presence of embedded clinker seams, which could greatly affect the 
disposal capacity of a drywell.  Therefore, the drywell installation and testing program 
should be conducted under the observation of a Geolabs representative. This would allow 
our firm to monitor the subsurface conditions, to evaluate the stormwater disposal 
capacity, and to allow for additional recommendations to be made. 
Due to the rapid infiltration rate measured at the proposed leach field area, we 
recommend the use of imported bedding material containing the appropriate amount of 
fines content to reduce the infiltration rate to an acceptable level in accordance with the 
Department of Health (DOH) standards. We recommend replacing the in-situ materials 
within 2 feet at the bottom of the leach field with less permeable material such as select 
borrow or structural fill materials. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and geotechnical 
engineering recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed New Eight Classroom Building project at the 
Kealakeha Elementary School campus in the Honokahau area of North Kona on the 
Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity of the project site are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations 
resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the 
project. These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design 
of foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, pavements, 
stormwater runoff and on-site sewage disposal systems only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the construction of a new classroom building for the existing 
Kealakehe Elementary School located in the Honokahau area of North Kona on the 
Island of Hawaii.  The new classroom building will be a two-story structure with a total 
floor area of approximately 9,480 square feet. 
Due to the sloping terrain at the project site, we understand that new retaining 
walls, on the order of about 13 feet high, are planned to create a level pad for the 
construction of the new classroom building.  According to the project structural 
engineer, the proposed retaining wall system would be initially braced laterally during 
the retaining wall backfilling and building slab construction.  In the final stage of 
construction, the building slab would then be relied upon to brace the top of the 
retaining wall against lateral movement. 
Based on the information provided, drywells for stormwater runoff disposal are 
planned for the new classroom building project.  The drywells will be typically 5 feet in 
diameter, 20 to 30 feet deep, and will be located on the downhill (makai) side of the new 
classroom building.  Two field permeability tests were performed in the deep borings

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago with an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five shield 
volcanoes. These are Kohala, which is long extinct; Mauna Kea, which has had activity 
during recent geologic times; Hualalai, which last erupted in 1801; and Mauna Loa and 
Kilauea, which are currently active. 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain, which has 
formed the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the site 
appears to be of a'a flow type, which spread downslope from vents further up on the 
mountain flank. a'a lavas are typically characterized by a porous, rough and irregular 
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and 
boulders. Typically, a more dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the 
lava flow core. 
Cavities, such as lava tubes or other void space, are commonly encountered 
buried within a'a lavas. Many of these cavities were formed when the still molten lava 
created the structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava. The cavities represent 
both lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) and inter-flow cavities, such as blisters and pockets. 
Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow 
tube-like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow. 
Inter-flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
The basalt lava rock encountered at the site is generally considered to be 
relatively permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both the horizontal and 
vertical directions. Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix, along joints, 
fractures, inter-flow contacts, cavities, and along embedded clinker layers. The 
permeability of the clinker and cavities is high; therefore, they serve as the major water 
transmission features.

2.2 
Site Description 
The Kealakehe Elementary and Intermediate School Complex is located to the 
west of Kealakaa Street in the Honokahau area of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. 
The school complex is bounded by Kealakaa Street to the east, residential subdivisions 
to the north and west, and open undeveloped land to the south. 
Based on the site plan provided, the new classroom building project will be 
located on sloping ground between the existing Cafetorium Building B and Building G at 
the Kealakehe Elementary School campus.  At the time of our field exploration, the site 
is occupied by a single-story, wood-framed, post-and-pier portable building 
(Building P-4) situated a few feet east of Building G. Other existing features include a 
paved walkway/ramp with a metal railing that zigzags under the canopy of several 
medium-sized trees that grow along the slope. The majority of the site is generally 
covered with landscape grass. 
The existing concrete walkway that runs sub-parallel to the slope provides 
benches that break the slope at several levels.  No exposures or rock outcrops were 
observed during our field exploration. The existing ground elevations at the project site 
generally range from about +764 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) near the Cafetorium 
Building B to about +747 feet MSL near Building G with a grade difference of about 
17 feet.

Seismic Design Considerations 
We understand the project will be designed in accordance with the requirements 
of the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the current code adopted 
by the County of Hawaii. Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition, the 
project site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will 
need to be addressed. The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity of 
the Island of Hawaii, the soil profile type for seismic design, and the potential for soil 
liquefaction. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when a loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations.

3.1 
Building Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new classroom 
building planned.  We recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 
4,000 psf for design of footings bearing on the hard basalt formation and/or compacted 
structural fill materials. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and 
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on 
slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured 
from the outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is no less than 
6 feet. 
In addition, foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, easements or 
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane 
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the footings 
should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated until competent materials are exposed in the footing 
excavation. The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may 
be extended down to bear directly on the underlying competent material.

At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be

3.5.2 Fills and Backfills 
Fill materials required for the project, including the backfill materials behind the 
retaining walls at the new classroom building, should consist of structural fill 
materials.  Structural fill materials should consist of non-expansive select granular 
materials, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The 
material also should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or 
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. The excavated on-site granular soils and basalt 
rocks may be reused as a source of structural fill materials provided that they are 
processed to meet the above requirements. Imported materials should be observed 
and/or tested by Geolabs for its suitability prior to being transported to the site. 
3.5.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheep foot rollers, non-vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
3.5.4 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by surface fills over fresh 
to slightly weathered, medium hard to very hard basaltic rock with varying degrees 
of fracturing ranging from severe to massive. The basalt near the existing ground 
surface is generally porous and friable. Ripping the basalt rock near the existing 
ground surface with a large bulldozer is not recommended due to the proximity of 
the new building site to existing structures. Therefore, we recommend evaluating 
the volcanic rock with less invasive methods such as chipping or with the use of 
hoerams for a more controlled rock breaking operation.

3.5.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing the basalt 
formation may be designed with a slope inclination of 1H:1V or flatter. In areas

considered in the project design where 1H:1V cut slopes are planned. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the structural fill materials recommended 
herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. In addition, fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be benched. The fill slope face should 
be finished to a relatively smooth and well-compacted surface. The fill slope face 
should be free of large boulders (greater than 18 inches in largest dimension) and 
should be free of voids, which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur. 
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back 
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. The fill slope face 
should be finished to a relatively smooth and well-compacted surface. The fill slope 
face should be free of voids, which would allow erosion and migration of fines to 
occur. In addition, slope planting or other means of slope protection should be 
provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the 
finished slopes.

Stormwater Runoff Disposal System 
Based on the information provided, we understand drywells for stormwater runoff 
disposal are planned for the project. Constant head permeability tests were 
conducted in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 to provide data for the preliminary design of the 
proposed drywells. The results of our field permeability tests are presented in

Based on the field permeability test results, we believe a preliminary drywell 
disposal capacity of about 5 cubic feet per second (cfs) may be used for the design 
of drywells with a diameter of 5 feet and a depth of 20 feet.  It should be noted that 
the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation at the site can be highly 
variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the basalt formation 
or the presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal 
capacity of a drywell.  In general, the greater the number of clinker seams and/or

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this 
report. 
The locations of the test borings are approximate, having been estimated by 
taping from reference points and visible features shown on the "Preliminary Leach Field 
Location Plan" transmitted by Urban Works, Inc. on June 27, 2016. Elevations of the 
borings were also estimated based on the spot elevations and contour lines shown on 
the same plan. The physical locations and elevations of the test borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. We did not encounter groundwater in our drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration.  However, it must be noted that groundwater fluctuation may occur due 
to variation in rainfall, temperature and other factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, to depths of about 5 to 31.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and 
coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, 
by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. 
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings 
indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow 
counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow 
counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).
