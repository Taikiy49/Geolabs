SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway pavement rehabilitation project 
indicated that about 50 percent of the existing pavement structure explored is generally 
underlain by a thin layer of stiff to very stiff alluvial clay over weathered tuff formation. 
The other half of the pavement explored rests directly on tuff and gravelly fill materials. 
The near surface clay encountered at the project site is classified by the 
USDA Soil Conservation Service as “Makalapa Series”. Our laboratory tests indicate 
that these clay soils are highly plastic with high shrink/swell potential when subjected to 
related changes in moisture content. Special attention is required in the subgrade 
preparation during construction to limit the effects of ground movement on the new 
pavement structure. We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at 
the time of our field exploration. Recommended pavement repair sections following the 
Types 1A, 2A and 4A from the RFP were used in our analyses. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement 
surface should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface 
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
pavement design recommendations for the new pavements.

SECTION 1. GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
analyses performed for the following roads and road segments at the Joint Base 
Pearl Harbor-Hickam at Pearl Harbor Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Bougainville Drive from Pearl Harbor Elementary School to Radford Drive, 
Radford Drive from Bougainville Drive to Salt Lake Boulevard, and  
DeHaven Street between Bougainville Drive and Warden Avenue. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses and 
design of pavement sections in accordance with the traffic data provided. The findings 
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of 
this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves repair and repaving Radford Drive, Bougainville Drive, and 
DeHaven Street at Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
Planned improvements include removal and replacement of pavement in indicated 
areas, restriping with thermoplastic pavement markings, install raised pavement 
markers, provide drainage improvements, remove existing crosswalk sign and install 
solar powered signs, and provide accessible ramp at the crosswalk.

conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration and drilled 35 borings (approximately 
250 feet spacing) to depths of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface 
along the aforementioned road segments. Approximately half of the borings were drilled 
to depths of 5 feet while the remaining borings were drilled to a depth of about 2.5 feet. 
Core samples of the surface asphalt and sampling of the base course and subgrade 
were obtained. Drilling was performed using a truck-mounted drill rig. Soil samples were 
collected using split spoon samplers. Auger cuttings to a depth of 5 feet collected from

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. A long period of volcanic inactivity followed during 
which time erosion formed deep gullies in the Koolau shield. Volcanic activity resumed 
with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formation known as the 
Honolulu volcanic series. 
The project site lies near the southwestern flank of the Aliamanu vent and 
Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. As the molten magma rose 
through these vents, it reacted with the groundwater generating steam that developed 
into explosive eruptions depositing low cones of volcanic ash which consolidated to form 
tuff, locally referred to as “mudrock”. 
The “Aliamanu tuff” is an older water-laid ash deposit that was laid when the 
seawater level was higher than the present level. The Aliamanu eruptive event 
deposited its material in alluvium that originated from Halawa Stream. 
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased, sedimentary deposits accumulated 
over the Aliamanu tuff before the resumption of the next phase of volcanic activity. The 
Salt Lake eruptions occurred when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its 
present level. The Salt Lake eruptive events deposited its materials over earlier tuffs 
from the older Aliamanu events.

2.3 
Existing Pavement Conditions 
2.3.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, majority of the roads within the project limits 
mentioned above fall under this category and appear to be generally fair with 
minor signs of structural defects. Overall pavement deterioration is restricted to 
light alligator cracking of low to moderate severity. The ride characteristic of the 
pavement was generally fair.  
2.3.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
Some of the existing asphaltic concrete pavements in localized areas along the 
roadway alignment within the project limits fall under this category and were 
observed to be in poor condition with moderate to highly distressed areas. 
Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to be widespread and 
generally moderate to high in severity. The poor areas consist of elongated, 
severely distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of 1 to 
2 inches, rutting and some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes. 
Potholes and subsurface utility trenches patched with AC material were observed 
at various locations along the alignment. The ride characteristics of the roadway 
within these areas were observed to be generally fair to irregular and bumpy in 
the areas of poor pavements.  
Pavements falling under this condition include portions of Bougainville Drive on 
both north and south-bound lanes and localized areas of Radford Drive more 
prominently at the west bound lane near the NEX.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
Although Bougainville Drive is both a State and Federal roadway, our understanding 
is that the portion within the project limits is contained wholly on Federal property. The road 
structural section was determined based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the 
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Pavement Design 
Manual dated March 2002 in accordance with UFC 3-201-01 Paragraph 4.1. The Pavement 
Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii -Department of Transportation, 
Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. The pavement design 
methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the 
California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). For continuity and uniformity of design 
of the pavements at Radford Drive, DeHaven Street and non-State owned portion of 
Bougainville Drive, we have used the same design method for all streets. 
The program first tries to use a thicker AC layer, as this will result in a thinner overall 
pavement section. This is appropriate for new pavements. However, when evaluating 
existing pavements, this may not be practical. Therefore, the existing AC and base course 
thicknesses were inserted in the analyses to evaluate the strength of the existing pavement 
section. Examples of the calculations are included in Appendix C.

Our field exploration indicates that about half of the roadway explored for this project 
is directly underlain by tuff at relatively shallow depths. As a result, the base/subbase 
designed under these sections are  anticipated to rest directly on the tuff and will therefore 
vary in thickness depending on the depth to tuff from the design finish grade at that 
particular pavement section. On the other hand, a layer of clay or previous fill is anticipated 
between the tuff and the pavement on the other parts of the roads. We recommend placing 
a suitable geotextile such as Mirafi 180 N over the clayey subgrade for separation and 
added stability prior to placement of the base or subbase course.  
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to 
those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D 1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density. 
Asphaltic concrete (AC) material should be constructed in accordance with 
Section 401 Asphalt Concrete Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for 
Road and Bridge Construction (2005) and its amendments.

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement 
surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry 
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water ponding 
should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good shoulder drainage 
along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly 
recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of the 
pavements.

specifications, or recommendations and to expedite suggestions for design changes that

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling 35 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 19, 21, 22, 24, 25, 27 through 34 
and 43 through 46. The borings extended to depths of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with a core barrel and continuous 
flight augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic representations of 
the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-35. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California 
sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained 
from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-
Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same 
hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches 
of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates 
the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to 
be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Twenty-three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution curves are 
provided on Plate B-1 through B-5. 
Ten Atterberg limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the results are 
provided on Plate B-6. 
One Proctor test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a combined soil sample to 
evaluate the moisture and density relationship of the subgrade materials. This test 
measures the optimum moisture content and the maximum dry density of the 
representative subgrade material. A graphic representation of the test results are 
provided on Plate B-7. 
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1833) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils by combining auger cuttings of 
similar soils from contiguous borings to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-8 through B-12.
