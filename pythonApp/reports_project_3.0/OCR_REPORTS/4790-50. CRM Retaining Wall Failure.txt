SITE CONDITIONS
Building 24 at the Kalihi Valley Homes public housing facility is located in the upper
portion of the development The building is currently under renovation as part of the Phase
4 improvements contract The building is situated above the access road and parking area
below The grade difference between the building and access road is maintained by a
CRM retaining wall approximately 10 feet high and 280 feet long The retaining wall is
about 20 feet away from the building edge
OBSERVATIONS

building failed The approximate limits of the failed wall section are shown on the Site Plan

between the top of the wall and the building at the time of failure

PRELIMINARY FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It appears that the existing CRM retaining wall is under designed The current
retaining wall may have just enough strength to support the existing soils but does not
have sufficient factor of safety to accommodate additional loads such as the weight of the
excavator The weight of the excavator was sufficient to cause wall failure There are
two conditions that require evaluation First the failed wall section must be restored to
prevent loss of support for the existing building Secondly the remaining portions of the
retaining wall although currently intact do not meet current design standards
Repair replacement of the failed wall is complicated by the proximity of the existing
building Normally a 10 foot high retaining wall supporting clay soils would require a
footing width on the order of about 7 to 8 feet Therefore the excavation required to
construct a new retaining wall would extend very close to the building increasing the
potential for settlement or damage to the building
In order to eliminate additional excavation into the hillside we recommend the use
of tie back anchors for the restoration of the failed wall section and to increase the stability
of the intact portion The design concept is illustrated on the attached Plate 2
It appears that a permanent tieback anchor system consisting of two levels of the
tieback could be used to provide the lateral support for a new concrete retaining wall in
front of the existing CRM wall The new concrete retaining wall could be constructed by
either cast in place or shotcrete In general the permanent tieback anchor system will
be designed to provide resistance to the lateral earth pressures
In general the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a
borehole installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the
borehole The basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the
anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar
In general the unbonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or
reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with the grout allowing the tendon or
reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transfer the resisting force from the structure to
the bonded length The bonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel
tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer the
applied load into the ground
We envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally
from the bond between the grout and the clay and cobbles boulder colluvium deposits
