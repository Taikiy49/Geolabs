SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our boring at the project site generally encountered a 4-foot thick surface fill layer 
underlain by a thick deposit of recent alluvium to a depth of about 48 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  Below the recent alluvium, residual and saprolitic soils underlain 
by basalt rock formation were encountered extending to the maximum drill depth of 
100.5 feet.  The compressible recent alluvium consisted of very soft to stiff clayey silt and 
sandy silt with sand, and loose silty sand with some gravel.  Groundwater was 
encountered in the boring at a depth of about 3.2 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The groundwater level corresponds to an elevation of about +4.1 feet Mean Sea Level. 
The planned junction box repair depth is about 15 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Therefore, shoring of the sides of the excavation will be necessary during the 
repair work.  Because of the existing pipe lines going into and out of the junction box, a 
combination of different shoring systems may be considered.  The shoring systems may 
consist of interlocking steel sheet piling and jet-grouted columns.  Design soil parameters 
for the shoring design are provided herein. 
To reduce the amount of dewatering during construction, a jet grout stabilized soil 
zone may be constructed in the areas extending beyond the bottom slab of the junction 
box.  The jet grout stabilized soil may also provide resistance to the hydrostatic pressure at 
the bottom of the excavation. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for design of the project.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Pearl City Wastewater Pump Station (WWPS) Junction Box Repair 
project located in Pearl City on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and 
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended to provide design soil 
parameters for the temporary shoring system only.  The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located on the south side of the existing bike path, west of 
Lehua Avenue, in the Pearl City area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  We understand 
that the project consists of repairing the existing junction box on the eastern side of the 
facility. The project location and vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The junction box is a buried concrete structure consisting of a 4-foot by 8.5-foot 
riser section over an L-shaped lower section, approximately 8.5-foot by 18-foot in plan. 
The bottom of the structure is about 22 feet below the existing ground surface and is 
supported on 12-inch square prestressed precast concrete piles.  The junction box 
combines the flow from a 24-inch and 42-inch pipe into a 48-inch pipe that discharges 
into the Pearl City WWPS.  Based on available drawings, the 42-inch and 48-inch pipes 
are supported by a concrete cradle and 12-inch timber piles.  We understand that the 
riser and the top slab of the junction box are deteriorated and must be replaced. 
Based on the topographic survey provided, the existing grades near the junction 
box vary from about +7.5 to +8.3 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The new junction box will 
be below the groundwater table, therefore, shoring and dewatering will be required for 
the repair work for the junction box structure.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became 
extinct while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank forming a broad plateau, now known as 
the Schofield Plateau. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and 
fans of accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef 
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine 
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused 
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the 
sediments and calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and 
induration of the calcareous reef materials. 
Based on geologic maps, the project site is generally underlain by 
unconsolidated non-calcareous deposits.  The unconsolidated non-calcareous deposits 
are mainly composed of recent alluvial deposits.  This deposit generally consists of 
compressible material.  Below the alluvium, basalt rock formation of the Koolau Volcanic 
Series is anticipated.  The upper portion of the basalt rock formation may be weathered 
and consists of residual and saprolitic soils.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling one test boring, designated as Boring No. 1.  The boring was extended to a 
depth of about 100.5 feet below the ground surface.  The approximate location of the 
boring drilled is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The boring generally encountered a 4-foot thick surface fill layer underlain by a 
thick deposit of recent alluvium to a depth of about 48 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Below the recent alluvium, residual and saprolitic soils underlain by basalt rock 
formation were encountered extending to maximum drill depth of 100.5 feet. 
The fill layer generally consisted of asphaltic concrete, loose silty sand with some 
gravel, and medium stiff silty clay with some sand.  The recent alluvium was comprised 
of very soft to stiff clayey silt and sandy silt with some sand, and loose silty sand with 
some gravel.  The residual and saprolitic soils consisted of medium dense 
gravelly/clayey sands with some silt.  The basalt rock formation was medium hard. 
Groundwater was encountered in the boring at a depth of about 3.2 feet below 
the ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The groundwater level measured 
corresponds approximately to Elevation +4.1 feet MSL.  It appears that artesian water 
condition may have been encountered in the boring.  It should be noted that 
groundwater levels are expected to vary with seasonal rainfall, tidal fluctuations, and 
time of the year.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our boring generally encountered a relatively thin surface fill layer underlain by 
very soft/loose alluvial deposits at the project site.  Below the alluvium, residual and 
saprolitic soils were encountered followed by basalt rock formation.  Groundwater was 
encountered in the boring at a depth of 3.2 feet below the existing ground surface at the 
time of our field exploration. 
Based on the planned repair of the existing junction box structure and the 
subsurface conditions encountered in the boring, we believe that shoring of the sides of 
the excavation will be necessary during the junction box repair work.  Detailed 
discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations for the 
design soil parameters are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Excavation 
In order to repair the junction box structure, we anticipate that excavation below 
the existing ground surface and groundwater level will be required for the project 
construction.  Based on the planned repair level and very soft and/or loose subsurface 
soil conditions encountered in our boring, we envision that temporary shoring of the 
excavations will be required for the junction box repair work. 
Our field exploration at the project site encountered a surface fill layer underlain 
by very soft to stiff and loose alluvial deposits from the ground surface to near the repair 
level at the top slab of the junction box structure.  Therefore, we anticipate that 
conventional excavation techniques using backhoe equipment may be considered for 
the planned excavation.  Because of the existing sewer pipes coming into and out of the 
junction box, we believe that a combination of different shoring systems may need to be 
used.  Shoring systems consisting of interlocking steel sheet piling and jet-grouted 
columns may be considered for temporary shoring purposes.  Care should be used 
during shoring installation to avoid damaging existing underground lines.  Since the 
excavation will be relatively deep (about 15 feet from the existing ground surface), 
interlocking steel sheet piling with waler or horizontal bracing may be required in order 
to reduce the potential for significant adjacent ground movement.  Design soil

the potential for bottom heave and design the shoring system accordingly. 
It is important that adequate shoring be installed prior to the excavation and 
maintained tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation.

3.2 
Jet Grouting 
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with 
radial horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil-cement columns.  The jet-grouting 
technique is a process that produces soil-cement columns by pumping neat cement 
grout slurry through horizontal jets injected at high pressures.  The horizontal jets of 
cement grout slurry cuts and mixes the surrounding in-situ materials with the neat 
cement slurry grout as the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil-cement 
column. 
To reduce the amount of dewatering during construction, a jet grout stabilized 
soil zone may be constructed in the areas extending beyond the bottom slab of the 
junction box.  The jet grout stabilized soil may also provide resistance to the hydrostatic 
pressure at the bottom of the excavation.  The stabilized soil zone should be

3.3 
Dewatering 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater 
level is above the bottom of the proposed excavation.  Groundwater was encountered at 
a depth of about 3.2 feet below the existing ground surface in the boring drilled during 
our field exploration.  Based on the groundwater level anticipated, dewatering will be 
required at the planned excavations of the project construction.  Therefore, dewatering 
provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for the proposed 
construction.  Since the excavation may involve discharge of groundwater, a National 
Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit may be required for this 
discharge.  The contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest 
regulations and information pertaining to the permit application. 
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavation 
is generally not recommended.  The resultant areal depression of the natural 
groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible subsurface soils 
resulting in potential ground settlements, which could affect the existing structures. Prior 
to the dewater, the contractor must carefully evaluate the potential impact of the 
dewatering system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table.

To reduce the amount of dewatering required during the junction box repair, 
construction of a jet grout stabilized soil zone below the areas beyond the junction box 
bottom slab may be considered.  Cut-off wall systems, such as interlocking steel sheet 
piles and jet-grouted columns may be used to aid in dewatering the excavation.  
However, sumps will be needed to collect water that percolates up into the base of the 
excavation or infiltrates through the sheet piles and jet-grouted columns.   
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor.  The selection 
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she 
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during 
construction depending on the conditions encountered.  The dewatering method 
selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the 
proposed excavation.  The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the 
shoring support such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized.  The 
operations should be carried-out without softening the bottom of the excavations. 
It is our opinion that the definition of "Dewatering" in the contract documents 
should be written to include works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater 
table and/or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed 
structures under safe and dry conditions.  These works or systems may include, but are 
not limited to, pumping, jet grout columns and sheetpiles. 
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a 
suitable method of dewatering: 
a. 
The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or 
damage to existing buildings, roads, and environment. 
b. 
The dewatering method should maintain stability of, and also 
provide safe and dry working conditions in, the excavation. 
c. 
The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow 
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions. 
Prior to dewatering, the contractor must carefully evaluate the potential impact of 
the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table. It is

use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level 
should not be allowed. 
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches 
connected to the excavations.  If this occurs, the granular bedding and/or backfill in the 
utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater 
drawdown.  Significant areal groundwater drawdown would result in ground settlements 
and potential damage to utility lines and/or other existing adjacent structures.

upon information obtained from the field boring.  Variations of subsurface conditions 
beyond the boring may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it will

plan.  The physical location and elevation of the boring should be considered accurate 
only to the degree implied by the methods used.

the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition.  Water level data from the boring was measured at the time shown

have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report.  
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides, rainfall, 
temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition at the project site was explored by drilling and 
sampling one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a maximum depth of about 
100.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The boring was drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with auger and coring equipment. The approximate boring 
location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Boring, Plates A-1.1 through A-1.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. Relatively “undisturbed” samples of the soft 
soil formation were obtained by pushing a 3-inch OD thin-walled steel Shelby tube into the 
soil formation in general accordance with ASTM D1587, Standard Practice for Thin-Walled 
Tube Sampling of Soils for Geotechnical Purposes. The range of hydraulic pressures 
needed to advance the sampler through a 24-inch push is shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests conducted are presented on 
the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling-induced 
fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the 
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Boring as RQD = 50%.  
Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, based on the 
"Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are presented on the 
Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation test results are 
provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D2166) was performed on a selected 
in-situ cohesive soil sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the 
soils. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample 
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-3. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of 
the soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as the 
applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  The test 
results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength parameters.  The test results are presented on Plate B-6.
