SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and literature research the
existing gravity sewerline alignments are generally underlain
by highly variable subsoil conditions ranging from soft marsh
and organic deposits at the GTE Baseyard and Moanalua
Gardens premises to stiff dense alluvial soils at the Fort
Shafter SPS and to hard tuff and basalt rock formation at the
TAMC complex Relatively shallow groundwater levels were
encountered along the downstream side of the sewerline
alignments at depths of about 5 4 to 14 2 feet below the
existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Because of the highly compressible marsh deposits
underlying the GTE Baseyard site we recommend that the
new sewerline segment within the baseyard premises be
supported by driven pile foundations For preliminary design
and cost estimation purposes we believe that 12 inch square
precast prestressed concrete piles with a compressive load
capacity of up to 30 tons per pile may be used An estimated
pile tip elevation on the order of about 120 feet MSL is
recommended for the driven piles
Soft organic deposits are anticipated at the invert elevations
of the new sewerline pipes and manhole structure within the
Moanalua Gardens premises Therefore we recommend that
a stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No 2 Rock
wrapped in a filter fabric be provided under the bedding layer
for more uniform support Special precautionary measures
such as sewing the filter fabric for underwater placement and
use of light compaction equipment should be implemented
during construction to reduce the potential for significant
pipeline settlements

W O 3898 00 Page 2
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered at
the sewage pump station site we recommend that shallow
foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings
bearing on the surficial weathered tuff rock be used to support
the proposed new generator building extension For
preliminary evaluation and cost estimation of the new overflow
dry well we believe that a mat foundation bearing on the
medium dense alluvial soils underlying the site at greater
depths may be used for the design of the underground
structure
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may
be used for temporary shoring for excavations in materials
other than the hard tuff or basalt rock formation especially
where dewatering of the excavations will be necessary Due
to the potential for presence of cobbles and boulders we
believe that predrilling through these obstructions would be
necessary in order to facilitate the sheet pile installation
operations For shallow excavations and excavations in open
areas we believe that a cantilever type of sheet piling may be
considered For deeper excavations and excavations located
adjacent to buildings utilities and pavements we
recommend that interlocking steel sheet piling with horizontal
bracing be used for excavation support in order to reduce the
potential for adjacent ground movement

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava _ flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of
Oahu has subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbot 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Page 7
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under
relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Page 8
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly
valleys and drainageways
The TAMC side of the existing sewerline alignment is geologically situated on the
northeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series As the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the
groundwater generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited
low cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as
mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than its present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream After this phase of volcanic
activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the next
phase began Then the Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea was at about 40 to
60 feet below its present level The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over
earlier tuffs from the older Aliamanu events The water that accumulated in the crater
was believed to originate from depressional springs and rainwater During periods of little
rainfall the shallow ponded water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the
mineral salt content of the ponded water increased resulting in the name Salt Lake
The Fort Shafter side of the existing sewerline alignment is geologically situated on
the southwest flank of the Koolau Volcanics These volcanics are composed of basaltic
lava flows of early Pleistocene Age The basalt is overlain by a complex interlayered
system of terrestrial and marine sedimentary deposits During the later Pleistocene Age
the sea level rose and fell several times as a result of glacioeustatic fluctuations resulting
in local swamp conditions which were later superimposed by more recent deposits of
terrestrial detritus of sand gravel silt and clay eroded from the older rocks of the area In
GEOLABS HAWAII

more recent time the efforts of man have shaped the low lying land to its present form by
placement of fill in low areas

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our literature research and field exploration the existing gravity
sewerline appears to be underlain by highly variable subsoil conditions ranging from soft
marsh deposits at the Baseyard to stiff dense alluvial soils at the Fort
Shafter SPS and to hard tuff and basalt rock formation at the TAMC complex In
addition shallow groundwater levels were encountered along the downstream side of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Page 13
alignment at depths of about 5 4 to 14 2 feet below the existing ground surface at the time
of our field exploration
For the installation of the new sewerline pipes and manhole structure underlain by
soft lioose subsoils within the Moanalua Gardens premises we recommend that a
stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No 2 Rock wrapped in a filter fabric be
provided under the bedding layer for more uniform support see Plate 6 Special
precautionary measures such as sewing the filter fabric in lieu of overlapping for
underwater placement and use of light compaction equipment should be implemented
during installation of the sewerline pipes and associated manhole structure due to the
presence of soft loose soil deposits
Based on our field exploration at the Baseyard thick
compressible soft marsh deposits were encountered to a depth of about 90 feet below the
existing pavement surface Because of the highly compressible marsh deposits
encountered and past problems associated with settlement at the site we believe that
new sewerlines installed at the Baseyard premises could be subjected
to appreciable settlements resulting from consolidation of the soft soil deposits due to
previously placed fill loads As a result we recommend that driven piles with a concrete
cradle be used for support of the new sewerline to be installed at the baseyard premises
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we recommend that
12 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles embedding in the stiff dense alluvial

Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the existing gravity sewer is generally underlain
by highly variable subsoil conditions ranging from soft marsh deposits at the GTE
to stiff dense alluvial soils at the Fort Shafter SPS and to hard
tuff and basalt rock formation at the TAMC complex In addition shallow groundwater
levels were encountered along the downstream side of the alignments at depths of about
5 4 to 14 2 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Therefore we envision that temporary shoring of the sewerline and manhole excavations
would be required for the project construction except in the hard tuff and basalt rock
formation
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may be used for
temporary shoring purposes especially where dewatering of the excavations will be
necessary For shallow excavations and excavations in open areas we believe that
cantilever type sheet piling may be considered For deeper excavations and excavations
located adjacent to buildings utilities and pavements we recommend that interlocking
steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing be used for excavation support in order to reduce
the potential for adjacent ground movement
A monitoring program should be developed and implemented by the contractor to
detect ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the sewerline and manhole
excavations especially in soft soil deposits which may result in damage to nearby
structures and pavements It should be noted that during and after installation of the
sheet piles minor settlements may occur Therefore it is recommended that a qualified
geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring
system used
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the sewer trench and manhole excavations based on the subsurface conditions
encountered However it should be noted that hard tuff and basalt rock formation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Page 37
may exist at shallow depths in the vicinity of the TAMC complex and in other
localized areas that would require heavy equipment for excavation or removal In
addition cobbles and boulders should be anticipated in the excavations at the
stream crossings Moanalua Gardens premises and Baseyard
premises The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in
order to reduce the potential for ground movement In addition the excavated
soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground utilities to
reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence which may damage the
underground utility lines
Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 20 to 30 feet may be required in
localized areas along the gravity sewerline alignment Based on the subsoil
conditions encountered in our borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the
excavations will be necessary to protect personnel working in the excavations
The requirement for shoring in hard tuff and basalt rock formation should be
evaluated on a site specific basis
Temporary shoring of the excavations using sheet pile shoring especially where
dewatering of the excavations will be necessary may be considered However it
should be noted that the existing gravity sewerline alignment may likely traverse
other utility lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating the
existing utility lines if sheet piles are to be used as shoring If sheet pile shoring is
used it would also serve as a cut off wall for the dewatering operations
Therefore the sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient
depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the
amount of dewatering within the excavations due to the high permeability of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Page 38
subsoils It should be noted that the excavations at Moanalua Gardens and
Baseyard will likely encounter soft loose soil deposits at the
bottom of the excavation There is a strong potential for bottom heave in these
soft loose soil conditions Therefore the contractor should carefully evaluate the
potential for bottom heave and design the shoring system accordingly A minimum
factor of safety of 1 5 should be provided in evaluating shoring stability and bottom
heave of the excavation
It is important that adequate sheeting be installed prior to excavation and
maintained tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during
excavation The properly braced sheeting is essential to reduce lateral
movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in
potential settlements or distress to adjacent structures or other improvements
such as roads or utilities
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction
such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate
construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation In addition it should be noted that settlement of the new pipeline may
occur as a result of the vibrations generated during the extraction of the sheet pile
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Page 39
shoring Therefore special attention should be given by the contractor during the
sheet pile removal process to reduce the potential for pipeline settlement
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements with instruments
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation Groundwater was encountered at
depths ranging from about 5 4 to 14 2 feet below the existing ground surface in the
borings drilled during our field exploration Based on the groundwater levels anticipated
and the nature of the soils to be encountered during the excavations dewatering will be
required at the excavations along the downstream side of the gravity sewerline alignment
Therefore dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for
the proposed construction Since the excavation will involve discharge of groundwater a
National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary
The contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations and
information pertaining to the permit application
Based on the anticipated depths of the sewerline pipes and manhole structures
and the relatively shallow groundwater table dewatering with cut off walls such as
interlocking sheet piles and or tremie concrete plugs may be considered However
sumps will be needed to collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation
or infiltrates through the sheet piles The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth
to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of
dewatering within the excavations in areas underlain by granular subsoils with generally
high permeability Use of an interlocking sheet pile shoring support system with
dewatering with tremie concrete plug is relatively watertight which should allow the
groundwater levels outside the excavations to be maintained close to the original
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Page 40
pre construction levels Therefore some type of groundwater control requirement should
be specified in the contract documents
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring
support such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations
should be carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of
the proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may
include but are not limited to pumping
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing buildings roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
GEOLABS HAWAII

dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained
by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and
to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial
measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering
operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be
performed without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should
be coordinated with the shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a
blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the
use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater
level should not be allowed
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches
connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in
the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater
drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or
other existing adjacent structures

examination in the field by our engineer geologist who observed the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 4 2

ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
