Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed in support of the design of the Inter Island Cargo Terminal Facility Phase 2
Pier 4 project in Hilo Harbor on the Island of Hawaii The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended only for the design of the new pier The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the imitations noted at the end of this
report

Project Considerations
The main objectives of the project are to improve safety and efficiency by
separating the cargo and passenger operations and to create additional berth space to
accommodate the growing cruise industry and the needs of the general maritime
industries These objectives will be accomplished by the construction of a new pier
Pier 4 west of the existing Pier 3 Young Brothers will be allowed to conduct operation
solely on the new Pier 4 The existing Pier 2 which is currently being used by both the
cruise industry and Young Brothers will be used by the cruise industry only The project
has been separated into the following phases
Phase 1 Dredging
Phase 2 Construction of Pier 4
Phase 3 Cargo Yard Improvements
This report pertains to the Phase 2 portion of the project only The Phase 2
portion of the project consists of constructing Pier 4 which would be used by Young
Brothers Based on the available information Pier 4 will be located in front of the
Jungle area which is located west of Pier 2 The new pier will be approximately
50 feet wide by 661 feet long The proposed finished deck elevation is set at
approximately 9 0 feet Mean Lower Low Water MLLW

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 4 030 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
both still active
The project site is within the Hilo Harbor area Hilo Harbor lies at the intersecting
slopes of the Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea Volcanoes The lava flows of the Mauna Loa
Volcano are relatively young in terms of geologic time and may be on the order of only
750 to 1 500 years old Below the basaltic lava flows of the Mauna Loa Volcano the
underlying lava flows of the Mauna Kea Volcano are believed to be Pliocene to
Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The land surrounding the Hilo Harbor area was reclaimed from the bay by the
placement of fill over the lagoonal deposits and or coralline detritus The project site is
generally underlain by lagoonal deposits and deposits of calcareous sediments also
known as coralline detritus Alluvial deposits associated with the Wailuku River underlie
the lagoonal deposits and calcareous sediments Basalt formation from the recent
Mauna Loa lava flows may be encountered at greater depths below the alluvial
deposits

Earthquakes and Seismicity
Earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
Earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the Island of
Hawaii Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island
of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent are
related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the active volcanoes in the southern
portion of the Island of Hawaii

Liquefaction
Based on the International Building Code the project site may be subjected to
seismic activity and should be evaluated for soil liquefaction potential Soil
liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration
such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly
enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress
internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During
liquefaction the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and
vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations

Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the borings indicate that the
site is underlain by loose to medium dense sand and soft silt with little cohesion
Based on our analyses the loose to medium dense sand and soft silt are potentially
liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6 with an associated peak ground
acceleration of 0 36g Based on our analyses the loose to medium dense sand and
soft silt below the groundwater level have a factor of safety of 0 3 to 1 0 against
liquefaction The effects of potential liquefaction at the site should be taken into
consideration when designing the proposed new pier structure

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we recommend
supporting the new pier on driven concrete piles consisting of 20 inch octagonal
precast prestressed concrete piles An allowable compressive load capacity of up to
120 tons may be used for foundation design of the recommended piles We believe that
the piles would derive support from mainly end bearing on the basalt rock formation
anticipated at about Elevation 110 to 115 feet MLLW Based on these estimated pile
tip elevations we believe that the foundation piles will need to be about 110 to 117 feet
long Considering the long unsupported pile length of about 30 to 40 feet pile splicing
should be avoided in order to utilize the bending and uplift capacities of the precast
concrete piles Therefore we recommend manufacturing the proposed foundation piles
in one piece or a mechanical splice should be used for pile installation
A concrete bulkhead with tie rods and deadman anchors is planned along the
proposed pier to retain the new fills for the container storage yard The concrete
bulkhead should be designed for lateral earth pressures due to the in situ soils which
would include the new and existing fills The tie rods are planned at 0 MLLW therefore
the deadman will extend below the groundwater level which will require significant
dewatering during the construction of the deadman The deadman planned above
0 MLLW will be located in the existing and new fills The deadman planned below

Pile Foundations
We understand that the pier will be supported on 20 inch octagonal precast
prestressed concrete piles along Gridlines B C and D An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 120 tons per pile may be used for the foundation design of the pier
The allowable compressive load capacity for the pile is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces We
recommend extending the 20 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles to
elevations of about 110 to 115 feet MLLW to achieve an allowable compressive load
capacity of 120 tons Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices
should be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
A factor of safety of 2 0 was used in arriving at the allowable compressive load
capacity provided above In general the 20 inch octagonal piles should be spaced a
minimum of 4 feet center to center to avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group
action and to facilitate pile driving
Considering the long unsupported pile length of about 30 to 40 feet pile splicing
should be avoided in order to utilize the bending and uplift capacities of the precast
concrete piles Therefore we recommend manufacturing the full length of the pile in
one piece or a mechanical splice should be used for installation

Downdrag Loads
The loose to medium dense granular soils may be susceptible to liquefaction in the
case of a seismic event producing a peak horizontal ground acceleration of about
0 36g at the site In the event of seismically induced liquefaction settlement of the
loose to medium dense granular soils will occur at the site When the loose to
medium dense granular soils move downward relative to the pile the downward
movement will result in negative skin friction forces generally known as downdrag
forces acting on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on
the thickness of the liquefiable layer relative density of the potentially liquefiable
deposits and the pile perimeter
The allowable compressive load capacity of the piles has been reduced by the
expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses Based on our foundation
analyses we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the vertical piles due to
the potential for liquefaction of the loose to medium dense granular soils may be on
the order of about 85 kips per pile
Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads imposed on the driven pile foundations may be resisted by a
combination of the dead weight of the driven pile and shear along the pile surface
and the adjacent soils In general an uplift load capacity of up to 90 kips per pile
may be achieved with the pile embedded in the coralline detritus and lagoonal
deposits The uplift load capacity provided above includes the weight of the pile
and is intended for transient load applications Therefore the uplift load capacity
should not be further increased for transient load applications For sustained uplift

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the piles will result primarily from elastic compression of the pile

pile supported foundations to be less than 0 5 inch with differential settlements
between adjacent piles not exceeding about one half of the estimated total
settlement We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should
occur as the loads are applied
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 60 000 foot pounds of energy per blow The hammer should be equipped
with an energy control level such that driving within the loose soft soils may be
controlled at a lower energy to reduce the potential for tensile stress development
within the piles which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Consideration should be given to the matching of the pile hammer with the weight
of the piles to be driven when determining driving equipment to be used This will
result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the
pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles Geolabs should review the
pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of
installing the piles to the recommended capacity

dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557

the maximum dry density

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
ten borings extending to depths ranging from about 26 5 to 138 5 feet below the existing
ground surface and 83 to 109 3 feet below the existing mudline The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The land borings were drilled using truck
mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
and the off shore borings were drilled with a skid mounted drill rig placed on a fabricated
floating platform raft equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates
A 1 through A 10
Samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the coralline materials encountered at the project site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The rocky material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock
Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Nineteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on
selected samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil
classification The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designations
C 117 and C 136 Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plates B 1 through B 4
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The results of the
direct shear test are presented on Plate B 5
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 7012 were performed on
selected rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock
encountered Unconfined compression test are presented on Plate B 6
Two pH ASTM G 51 tests and one Resistivity ASTM G 57 test were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B 7
