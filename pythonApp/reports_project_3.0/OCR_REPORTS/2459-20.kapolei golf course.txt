SUMMARY OF FINDINGS
Our field observation of the drywell installation generally
indicated that a surficial layer consisting of reddish brown
clayey silt was encountered to depths ranging from 1 to 30
feet below the existing ground surface Below the surficial
clayey layer permeable coralline deposits were encountered
to the maximum depths drilled of approximately 30 to 34 feet
below the existing ground surface in the golf course area
Groundwater was not encountered in the drywells at the time
the drywells were drilled
Following the installation of the drywells injection tests were
performed in all of the constructed drywells The injection test
results indicated that the percolation rates of the drywells vary
significantly over the golf course area and the estimated water
disposal capacity is less than the design capacity Installation
of additional drywells may be necessary to meet the design
water disposal capacity in this area
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed
discussions

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated within the Villages of Kapolei development project in the
Ewa district on the island of Oahu Hawaii The Villages of Kapolei development consists
of a planned residential community with related facilities and the Kapolei Golf Course
As part of the overall drainage system of the development the golf course will
serve as both a detention and infiltration basin Storm water run off generated from
Kapolei Village 3 Makakilo Gulch and Makalapa Gulch will discharge into the golf course
area through concrete drainage pipes and box culverts After passing through the golf
course the remaining flow will discharge into the Kapolei Lower Channel located to the
south of the Kapolei Golf Course and northerly adjacent to Franklin D Roosevelt Road
in Barbers Point Naval Air Station Therefore surface and subsurface infiltration and
drainage through the golf course is very important to this project To supplement the
natural infiltration capacity of the golf course area a series of 21 drywells were designed
for use within the golf course
Under the State of Hawaii Department of Health Administrative Rules drywells for
the disposal of surface run off are considered injection wells and permits for the
construction and operation of the drywells must be obtained under the Underground
Injection Control UIC program DOH Administrative Rules Chapter 11 23 requires that
an approval be obtained prior to constructing new injection wells and that a permit be
obtained to operate the wells These are accomplished by submittal to the Department
GEOLABS HAWAII

of Health of UIC permit application and an engineering report documenting the
construction and testing of the wells
An application was submitted to the State of Hawaii Department of Health for
approval to construct the 21 new drywells A preliminary approval letter for construction
was received under UIC permit No UO 1756 from the Department of Health on February
5 1993

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The site is located in the Ewa district on the island of Oahu Hawaii The climate
of the Ewa district is mild with an average annual temperature of about 78 F and an
average annual rainfall of about 40 inches per year In general the topography of the
project site can be characterized as gently sloping terrain with an overall slope and
drainage in a southerly direction
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the
bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 20 Page 6
of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the Ewa Coastal Plain which abuts the southern flank
of Waianae and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pearl
Harbor and the Ewa Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the majority of the
coastal plain are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during
high stands of the sea which intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Waianae Basalt
As volcanic activity on the island of Oahu drew to a close the island began to
subside slowly While the island was sinking fringing coral algal reefs began to grow on
the seaward margins of the island
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell in response to
the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew the global sea level fell below the
existing level and as the glaciers melted and retreated the sea level rose to heights above
the current stand of the sea The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable during
these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 20 Page 7
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the reef
materials and lagoonal sediments
Agricultural land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form
Subsurface Conditions
Our field work consisted of observing and logging the installation of 20 drywells
which were drilled to depths of about 30 to 34 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the drywells are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the drilled drywells encountered a surficial layer of clayey silt which is
approximately 11 to 30 feet deep underlain by coralline deposits which include coral reef
formation as well as unconsolidated coral sands and gravels to the drilled depths of
about 30 to 34 feet below the existing ground surface at elevation 50 feet Mean Sea
Level Detailed descriptions of the subsurface conditions encountered are presented on
Drywell No 9 was drilled to about 30 feet below the ground surface A thick layer
of clayey silt was primarily encountered throughout the depth of the drywell Due to the
anicipated low percolation rate of the clayey silt layer the drilled well was abandoned and
backfilled The abandonment process has not yet been completed at the time of
preparation of this report To date no additional drywell s has have been constructed

to replace the abandoned Drywell No 9 Therefore a total of 20 drywells have actually
been installed in the golf course area
Groundwater was not encountered in any of the drywells at the time the drywells
were drilled
Earthquake Conditions
The City and County of Honolulu formally adopted the 1988 Uniform Building Code
UBC with amendments which assigned Oahu to Seismic Zone 2A Since no new
structures will be associated directly with the drywells seismic conditions are not
considered to be significant
Land Use
The site is an area which is planned for residential community use and includes
related facilities The site is currently under development for these purposes and the
drywells together with the golf course are parts of the proposed infrastructure system of
the whole development which will be used to partially intercept runoff originating within
and above the development area Therefore construction of the drywells at the site is
consistent with this use
SYSTEM OPERATION
Kapolei Golf Course will form a part of the drainage system for the development
of the Villages of Kapolei Based on the current design concepts the storm water run off
from Kapolei Village 3 Makakilo Gulch and Makalapa Gulch will flow into Kapolei Golf
Course through concrete drainage pipes and box culverts The golf course will function
as a detention and infiltration basin to allow some of the run off to infiltrate through
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 20 Page 9
surface percolation After passing through the golf course the run off will discharge into
the Kapolei Lower Channel
Part of the run off entering the golf course will be disposed of by infiltration through
the surface of the golf course In order to increase the natural water infiltration capacity
of the golf course 21 drywells had been designed for the golf course area of which 20
drywells have actually been installed to date Based on the information provided to our
office the golf course was designed to limit the 100 year 24 hour outflow rate to 1 200
cubic feet and also dispose 200 acre feet of runoff on site
The drywells were constructed in general conformance with the specifications of
the UIC Application UO 1756 In general the drywells were drilled to approximately 30
to 34 feet and installed using 12 inch diameter perforated casings The sketch details of
the system are presented on Plate 3
GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The ground surface elevations at the drywell locations at the time of installation
and testing were approximately 50 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Groundwater was not
encountered during the drywell installations The groundwater beneath the project site
may consist of brackish water in an unconfined coralline formation aquifer Based on
area topography and the subsurface conditions encountered the down gradient direction
for groundwater movement appears to be toward the southly direction
The logs of the drilled drywells are presented in Appendix A Generally the
drywells encountered silty clay and clayey silt overlying coralline deposits

PERMEABILITY TESTING
After the drywells had been drilled and cased to their intended depths six drywells
including 13 and 18 were tested by the injection of clear water
to confirm the subsurface permeability conditions at the site as required by the UIC
permit
As indicated previously a thick layer of clayey silt was primarily encountered
throughout the depth of Drywell No 9 Due to the anicipated low percolation rate of the
clayey silt layer the drilled well was abandoned and backfilled Therefore it would be
necessary to evaluate the actual overall run off water disposal capacity of the remaining
20 drywells in the golf course area and compare it with the overall design capacity in
order to determine if an additional drywell will be needed to replace Drywell No 9
Subsequently the remaining 14 drywells were also injection tested At the time of
injection testing at Drywell No 21 it was noted that the head level was constantly near
the level of the existing ground surface indicating that the drywell had only a very small
percolation rate The source of the injected water was from the adjacent golf course lake
A paddle wheel flow sensor was used to monitor the flow rate Flow rates for the
injection tests were typically about 10 to 301 gallons per minute GPM Each test ranged
about 114 to 2 hours in duration During the injection of water the changes in head level
in the drywells were recorded with respect to time and the rate of injection by an electric
water level sensing device
The estimated coefficient of permeability of the subsurface soils at the project site
ranged from 7 00x10 to 2 17x10 feet per minute The results of the injection testing
program indicate that the water disposal capacities of the drywells vary significantly over
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 20 Page 11
the golf course area and the estimated overall water disposal capacity is less than the
design capacity Detailed results of these tests are presented in Appendix B
LIMITATIONS
The findings and analyses submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as the drilled wells and injection tests
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until actual operation of the drywells
If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the findings presented
in this report
The stratifications shown on the drywell logs depict the approximate boundaries
between soil types and as such may denote a gradual transition
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the times shown on the
logs and or in the text and appendices of this report These data have been reviewed
and interpretations have been made in the formulation of this report however it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
