1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Bulkhead Repairs at Piers 24 29 project located within Honolulu Harbor
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the Bulkhead Repairs at
Piers 24 29 project The recommendations are intended for the design of the new sheet
pile bulkhead only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu The coastal
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of
alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated
deposits
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains were developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell
below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess
of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea When
the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
The project site is generally underlain by deposits of calcareous sediments and
alluvium The deposition of soils at the site is likely associated with the nearby Nuuanu
Stream lagoonal deposits from Honolulu Harbor and coralline deposits at greater

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3 1 Sheet Pile Bulkhead
Based on the available information we envision that new sheet piles will be used
for the bulkhead repair In general the new sheet pile bulkheads along the piers will
serve two purposes 1 to provide lateral support for the existing and new fills and 2 to
maintain stability of the soil mass Design of the new sheet pile bulkhead will need to
consider both overturning stability and overall slope stability Geotechnical design
parameters to evaluate these stability conditions are presented in the following
subsections of this report The overturning stability will need to be evaluated by the
design engineer We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the proposed sheet pile
bulkhead and the results are presented in a subsequent subsection of the report
Lateral Earth Pressures
Due to the relatively shallow retained depth of the new sheet pile bulkhead we
envision that the new bulkhead will likely consist of a cantilevered sheet pile
bulkhead system In general the bulkhead should be designed to resist the lateral
earth pressures due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects Therefore we
recommend that the new sheet pile bulkhead be designed for lateral earth
pressures due to the in situ soils which include the new fill the existing fills harbor
deposits cinder sands and coral formation The recommended lateral earth
pressures for design of the new sheet pile bulkhead are provided on the Apparent
Pressure Diagram presented on Plate 3
As mentioned previously the coral ledge was encountered at varying depths at the
project site For the purposes of sheet pile bulkhead design we believe that the
presence of coral ledge may be estimated at Elevation 5 feet MLLW at Piers 24
and 29 The coral ledge may be estimated at Elevation 14 feet MLLW at Piers 27
and 28 Based on our engineering judgment the approximate transition line where
the coral ledge is present at greater depths is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 for
estimating purposes
We understand that a row of concrete piles currently provides lateral support to the
existing concrete bulkhead Due to the uncertainty regarding the lengths and

Unbalanced Hydraulic Pressures
For waterfront structures such as sheet pile supported piers the maximum lateral
pressures may be expected when the tide is at its lowest level A receding tide
may cause higher water levels behind the sheet pile than in front of it depending
on the type of soils behind the sheet piles The high water level behind the sheet
pile wall will introduce additional seepage pressure due to hydrostatic load For
soils encountered at the project site a hydrostatic pressure estimated based on
the maximum tide difference conservative may be used for the sheet pile design

Corrosion Protection
Due to exposure to salt water and the marine environment we envision that the
new sheet pile bulkheads would be subjected to a highly corrosive environment
Therefore we recommend that metallic substructures be designed for protection
against potential corrosion Consideration may be given to the use of other types
of sheet piles such as vinyl composite or other types of non corrosive materials
in lieu of the conventional steel sheet piles If a steel sheet pile system is elected
for the project a corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed
recommendations pertaining to corrosion protection of the steel sheet pile
bulkhead

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Vegetation old pavements debris deleterious material and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Yielding areas loose areas or voids disclosed during the clearing and
scarification operations should be backfilled with well compacted fill materials
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Fills and Backfills
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source
of general fill or backfill material at the site provided that the materials are free of
vegetation deleterious materials and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than
3 inches in maximum dimension Due to the relatively shallow groundwater level
at the site the excavated on site soils may require some aeration to reduce the
moisture content of the soils prior to being re used as backfill materials
Where imported material is required as fill or backfill the material should consist
of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral basaltic gravel or
cinder sand Imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The imported fill material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use
Where backfilling below the groundwater level is required we recommend that the
backfill consist of free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent The free draining granular backfill should extend to at least 12 inches
above the groundwater level Backfill above the free draining granular backfill may
consist of compacted general fills or select granular materials
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight

3 3 Pavements
We understand that portions of the existing concrete pavement behind the
bulkhead may be demolished prior to installation of the new sheet pile bulkhead system
After the installation of the new bulkhead and backfilling a new asphaltic concrete

Asphaltic Concrete Pavement

6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six test borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about 25 to
30 feet below the existing ground surface The test borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the cemented coralline materials encountered at the site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the cemented coralline formation
The cemented coralline material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered
for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
