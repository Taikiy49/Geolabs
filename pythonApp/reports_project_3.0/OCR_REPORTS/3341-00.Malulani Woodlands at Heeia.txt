REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The eastern part of the island of Oahu was built by
the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the rift Zones of the Koolau shield volcano which
extended from Kahuku to Makapuu The project site is located roughly at the northern
boundary of the central caldera complex of Koolau near its junction with the northeast
rift zone
In situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1
to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which
grade with depth to saprolite The saprolite grades with depth to basaltic rock
formation In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty and clayey materials with
cobbles and boulders partially weathered basalt and is typical of the tropical
weathering of volcanic rocks

rock formation with increasing depth Laboratory tests performed on the near surface
clayey residual soils indicate that the materials generally range from moderately to
highly expansive Therefore the near surface soils have moderate to high shrink swell
potential when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content Some of the saprolite

Expansive Soils
Based on our laboratory test results the near surface clayey residual soils
generally have moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations
in the moisture content Therefore we recommend that proposed slabs on grade be
supported on a minimum of 24 inches of non expansive capping fill to reduce the
potential for distress resulting from shrinking and swelling of the near surface expansive
soils when subjected to moisture fluctuations
If post and beam construction is elected for support of structures we recommend
that the post footings be supported on shallow drilled piers Since minor grading is
anticipated we envision that the near surface soils below the proposed residential units
will consist of expansive clayey soils We anticipate that the potentially expansive
subgrades for the proposed buildings supported on a post and beam foundation system
will be exposed to the environment such as winds and drying resulting in soil
shrinkage in the future due to loss of soil moisture Therefore the drilled piers
supporting the post footings should be embedded to a depth below the seasonal
moisture variation approximately 3 feet below finish grade In general frictional
support of the drilled pier from the upper 2 feet should be discounted in foundation
design due to potential soil shrinkage around the pier shaft near the surface

Cut slopes exposing the expansive residual soils at the slope face should be
protected from drying to reduce the potential for development of shrinkage cracks
Development of shrinkage cracks may lead to slope instability of the cut slope In
addition the potential for soil erosion would be significantly higher if shrinkage cracks
were to develop in the cut slope face
Fill slopes constructed of the recompacted on site clayey soils would behave
similarly Therefore the faces of cut and fill slopes should be protected from drying
and development of shrinkage cracks In addition adequate slope protection should
be provided to reduce the potential for erosion of the slope faces Alternatively cut
slopes may be buttressed with fill slopes constructed of non expansive or low
expansive fills

Slabs On Grade
Slabs on grade for the proposed project may consist of concrete floor slabs and
exterior flatwork To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling
of the on site expansive soils we recommend that a minimum of 24 inches of non
expansive capping fill material be provided below slabs on grade The non expansive
capping fill should extend at least 2 feet beyond the limits of the proposed structure
The non expansive capping fill may be reduced to 6 inches for sidewalks and to a
minimum of 12 inches for driveways and garage slabs
The non expansive fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil
above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of
moisture from the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential
drying and wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the distress on the slab due to
potential heaving of the underiying expansive clayey soils when wetted
In general non expansive capping fill should consist of granular materials such
as crushed coral basalt crusher waste or cinder The non expansive capping fill
should be well graded with no particles greater than 3 inches in size and should have
a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR swell value not exceeding 1 percent when
prepared above the optimum moisture The non expansive capping fill should not
contain organic or other deleterious materials
In general we recommend that the non expansive capping fill be moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90
percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

As previously indicated the site is heavily vegetated with shrubs and tress At
the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to

After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a

optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be
filled with engineered fill moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the

Fills and Backfills
a a ni a i a
Based on our exploration and laboratory test results the soils in the project site
generally have high natural moisture contents due to the wet climatic conditions
inthe area The moisture contents are usually significantly in excess of optimum
moisture content This high natural moisture content could result in increased
handling of the soils since they are too wet to be properly compacted at their
natural moisture contents and would have to be aerated by blading with a bull
dozer until they have dried sufficiently for site fill placement
In general the on site clayey materials rnay be used as fill materials provided
that they are placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Due to the expansive
potential of the clayey materials the clayey materials should not be used as
backfill for retaining structures Any bouiders or cobbles larger than 6 inches in
maximum dimension should be separated from the excavated material and
disposed of off site

Import fill materials should consist of granular materials such as crushed coral
basalt crusher waste or cinder and should not contain organic or other

Drainage
The on site surficial soils are highly susceptible to erosion As a result water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and adequate slope planting should
be done as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion Concrete V ditches
may be provided at the top of slopes to reduce the potential for erosion Drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possibie and maintained to drain all surface run
off away from the slab footing foundations and the edges of slopes
The finish grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the founda

avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the
subsoils

soil saturation and subsequent loss of strength or heaving of the near surface soils

Trench Backfill
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3341 00 Page 28
design consideration For pipes bearing on the stiff silty soils a minimum of 6 inches
of compacted granular bedding material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No
67 gradation or equivalent should be provided below the pipe
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or equivalent should be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
compaction of the backfill thus reducing the potential for damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finish grade should consist of on site soils or select
granular material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in maximum 8 inch loose level lifts and mechanically compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavements and other improvements such as
sidewalks the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should
be compacted to 95 percent relative compaction
Box Culvert
During our field exploration an existing box culvert was noted near the entrance
to the project site near the location of Boring No 11 Therefore we envision that
construction of a new box culvert would be needed to connect the proposed drainage
system to the existing box culvert
In general we recommend that an allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 000 p s f be used for the design of the box culvert bearing on the stiff native soils
The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third for transient loadings such as wind or seismic forces
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3341 00 Page 29
We also recommend that the box culvert be supported on a crushed rock
cushion consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped in a filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent to reduce the potential for
migration of fines into the cushion fill
Generally the walls of the box culvert are restrained at the top from deflection
Therefore the wall should be designed for the at rest condition The recommended at
rest lateral earth pressure for design of the box culvert walls expressed in equivalent
fluid pressure is 55 p c f This value assumes that the on site soils may be used as
backfill materials around and on top of the box culvert structure The recommended
lateral earth pressures above do not include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused
by groundwater trapped behind the walls Therefore box culvert walls should be well
drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures Typical drainage systems for
the box culvert walls are presented in the Drainage subsection of this report
For lateral load resistance a passive earth pressure of 250 and 150 p s f per
foot of depth may be used above and below groundwater levels respectively A
frictional coefficient of 0 35 may be used to evaluate the sliding resistance developed
between the base of the box culvert structure and the underlying cushion fill

Based on the current design concept ndividual Wastewater System IWS will
be installed at each lot for disposal of wastewater The treated effluent would be
disposed of in seepage pits In general accordance with the State of Hawaii
Department of Health requirement percolating tests were performed in 14 drilled

The subsurface percolation rates were tested by the falling head method Water
was added from the bottom of the borings up to approximately 6 inches above the
gravel layer before testing The cnanges in water level in the borings were recorded
with respect to time for about 4 hours In general percolation tests were performed at
two depths within the boring to evaluate the near surface and deeper level soil
percolation rates A summary of the percoiation test results is presented on Plate C 1
The Site Evaluation Percolation Test data are presented on Plates C 2 through C 29
In general the percolation test results generally indicated that the near surface
soils have a higher permeability rate than the deeper soils Based on the near surface
percolation test results we believe that it is prudent to use an average percolation rate
of 45 minutes per inch for depths from the existing ground surface to about 15 feet
below the ground The deeper soils appear to be less permeable with percolation rates
on the order of 100 minutes per inch Therefore we recommend that the shaliower
seepage pits on the order of 15 feet or less be used in lieu of deeper seepage pits
To provide the required capacity multiple shallow seepage pits should be considered

Moisture content unit weight determinations and unconfined compression tests
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation
of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on selected bulk
samples collected for pavement design The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Five Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 2
Four one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in the natural
state Seven one inch Ring Swell tests were also performed on remolded and
compacted samples The results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 3
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 were performed to
estimate the angle of internal friction and cohesion of the soil sample tested The test
results are graphically presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two Compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the moisture content and dry density relationship
The test results are summarized on Plate B 1 Graphic presentation of the test results
are provided on Plates B 6 and B 7

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testings
Twenty eight percolation tests were performed in the drilled borings in general
accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health guidelines After the borings
were drilled to the desired test depths about one inch of No 3B Fine gravel was
placed on the bottom of the 4 inch diameter drilled holes Water was then added into
the borings to presoak the soils at the bottom portion of the borings for at least 4 hours
The subsurface percolation rates were tested by the falling head method Water
was added from the bottom of the borings up to approximately 6 inches above the
gravel layer before testing The changes in water level in the borings were recorded
with respect to time for about 4 hours or less where appropriate A summary of the
results of the percolation tests is provided on Plate C 1 The Site Evalua
tion Percolation Test data is presented on Plates C 2 through C 29
