Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the subsurface conditions at the site to develop a subsurface data set to formulate
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of generator building. Our work
was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated November 30, 2006.
Our scope of work included the following tasks and work efforts:
1.
Mobilization and demobilization of truck-mounted drill equipment and
two operators to and from the project site.
Drilling and sampling of one boring to a depth of approximately 27.5 feet
below the existing ground surface.
Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the boring by our
geologist.
Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their engineering
properties.
Analyses of field and laboratory data to formulate geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the design of the generator building.
Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations.
Coordination of our overall work by our project engineer.
Quality assurance and client/design team consultation by our principal
engineer.
Miscellaneous work efforts, such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling one boring,
designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth approximately 27.5 feet below the
existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operation on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate 3. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate 4.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the boring in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.
One pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer test
are summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth.
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consisted of fill materials that
extended to a depth of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill materials
consisted of very stiff silt and medium dense gravel. Medium dense cinder sand was
encountered below the fill materials and extended to the maximum depth explored of
about 27.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
At the time of our field exploration, we encountered groundwater in the boring at a
depth of about 11.8 feet below the existing ground surface. It should be noted that
groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate with variation in rainfall, surface water
runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

Laboratory Testing
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained
from our field exploration.
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations

Discussion and Recommendations
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the site consisted of fills
underlain by cinder deposits. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we
believe that a concrete slab-on-grade with thickened edge footings may be used to
support the generator building.
Due to the location of the proposed generator building, the foundations will be
underlain by an existing sewer line. The generator building foundations would likely
surcharge the existing sewer line below the generator building with additional structural
load. Since the existing sewer line was not designed to support additional structural
loads, the existing sewer line may be damaged. To avoid surcharging the existing
sewer line below the generator building, we recommend supporting the generator
building (in the areas where the sewer line traverses below the generator building) on a
micropile foundation system. The micropiles would straddle the sewer line and transfer
the additional load from the generator building below the sewer line.
Detailed discussions and recommendations for building foundations, retaining
walls, and site preparation are presented in the following sections herein.
Generator Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend
using concrete slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge footings to support the proposed
generator building. Because the concrete slab with thickened edge footing will be
subjected to vibration, we recommend placing a 6-inch layer of compacted aggregate
subbase course below the slabs and footings. The 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase
course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
the design of footings bearing on the compacted aggregate subbase course. This bearing

value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade. Where foundations are located next to utility trenches, and/or
easements, they should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility tench or embedded as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement.
Soft/loose materials encountered at the slab or footing subgrade level should be
over-excavated to expose the underlying dense/firm materials and replaced with
engineered fill material.
The soils encountered at the slab and footing subgrade level (below the aggregate
subbase course) should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with procedures
described in ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations, we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the
compacted aggregate subbase course may be on the order of about 1 inch. We
estimate that differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on the
aggregate subbase course to be on the order of about 0.5 inch.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of
0.45 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted aggregate subbase course.
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the
soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the
passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. It should
be emphasized that surface water should be diverted away from the foundations.
A Geolabs representative should observe the foundation excavations prior to
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and
the embedment depths.

Micropile Foundations
Due to the location of the proposed generator building, the foundations will be
underlain by an existing sewer line. The generator building foundations would likely
surcharge the existing sewer line with additional structural load. Since the existing sewer
line was not designed to support additional structural loads, the existing sewer line may be
damaged. To avoid surcharging the existing sewer line below the generator building, we
recommend supporting the generator building (in the areas where the sewer line traverses
below the generator building) on a micropile foundation system. The micropiles would
straddle the sewer line and transfer the additional load from the generator building below
the sewer line. Micropiles are desirable because they can be readily installed in access
restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition,
installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures,
the adjacent soils, and the environment.
In general, the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter (usually
less than 12 inches) pile, drilled and grouted, with steel reinforcing. The micropile
foundation is typically constructed by drilling a borehole with casing, placing reinforcing
steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. Based on the subsurface conditions
anticipated at the project site, we estimate that a micropile system with a grout bulb
diameter of 7 inches may be used to support the proposed generator building in the areas
where the existing sewer line traverses below the foundations. We recommend an
allowable compressive load capacity of 15 kips per micropile. The allowable compressive
load capacity of the micropile is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased
by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces.
The micropile should extend a minimum of 20 feet (bonded zone) below the pile
cap to achieve the recommended allowable compressive load capacity. The micropiles
would derive its vertical support primarily from skin friction between the grout bulb and the
surrounding fills and cinder deposits.

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall.
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weephole should
consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the retaining wall
backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the retaining wall unless covered by concrete
slabs at the surface.
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography, we envision that minor site grading work
would be required. At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits
should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Debris, deleterious materials, and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed off-site. Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future
improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or organic
soils should be properly disposed off-site. Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account
for variations in the over-excavation quantities.
The materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of general
fill material, provided that they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and fragments
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. If required, import fill should consist of
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed basalt or coral. The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200
sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent. Geolabs should test imported fill material for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use.

Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness,
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Compaction by water tamping, jetting,
or ponding should not be allowed for the on-site soils.

Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from the field boring. Variations of subsoil conditions may occur, and
the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is
underway. If variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate
the recommendations presented herein.

features shown on the Topographic Map provided by Ronald N.S. Ho & Associates, Inc.
on May 8, 2007. Elevation of the field boring was interpolated from the elevation points
and contour lines shown on the same map. The location and elevation of the field boring
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the boring depict
the approximate boundaries between the soil and rock types, and as such, may denote a
gradual transition. Water level data from the boring was measured at the times shown on
the graphic representations and/or in the text of this report. These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, temperatures, and other
factors.
