INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of Sverdrup Corporation we have conducted a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the proposed Fish and Wildlife Refuge
Maintenance Facility and residence project located in the Hanalei area of the island of

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Hanalei Fish and Wildlife Refuge in Hanalei Valley
on the island of Kauai Hawaii It is proposed to develop the site as a maintenance facility
for the refuge and as a residence for refuge workers
In the initial stage of development it is proposed to grade the site to create level
building pads and yard area This site grading work will require up to about 19 feet of cut
and the placement of up to about 8 feet of fill
After the site grading a new residence a vehicle storage building and a shop
building will be built at the site It is anticipated that the maintenance and shop buildings
will be reinforced masonry buildings with slab on grade floors The vehicle yard will be
gravel surfaced
The proposed residence will probably be single story and of wood frame
construction Either post and beam footings or slab on grade could be used
The site is situated in an unsewered area Therefore on site treatment and
disposal of sanitary wastes will be required It is anticipated that a septic tank system will
be used

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in Hanalei Valley to the southeast of the town of Hanalei
on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The site is located near the base of a ridge in a relatively undeveloped rural area
The site generally slopes down in an easterly direction and is partially terraced The site
is currently being used as a residence and maintenance facility for the Hanalei Fish and
Wildlife Refuge
The existing ground surface elevations of the site vary from approximately 18 to
about 44 feet above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
more than 2 3 million years before present Following the cessation of this main
shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of the
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
Our exploration of the site indicates that the site is most likely underlain by alluvial
soils These alluvial soils appear to be recent alluvium overlying Koloa Basalts

Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four 4 borings to depths ranging between 6 5 and 16 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the borings encountered a thin surface layer of dark brown highly
expansive silty clay Adobe with gravel and cobbles overlying very stiff brown silty clay
soils to depths of about five 5 to ten feet 10 Below these surface layers the soils
graded to very stiff mottled clayey silts which graded with traces of sand and gravel with
increasing depth In Boring Ha 2 loose silty gravel was encountered at a depth of about
13 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered during our exploration in Boring Ha 2 at a depth of
about 13 feet below the existing ground surface It is believed that groundwater was
encountered at a depth of about 13 feet in Boring Ha 4 however the boring collapsed
after the last sample at 15 foot depth Therefore the groundwater level could not be
measured
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that development of the proposed project site is feasible provided
that our recommendations are followed We recommend that spread or strip footings
bear on the underlying very stiff alluvial soils encountered at the site or on fill placed and
compacted in accordance with recommendations presented herein
The surface silty clay with gravel and cobbles Adobe appears to be a highly
expansive soil This soil should not be used to support fill embankment building
foundations slabs or pavements

The bottom of all footing excavations should be observed by the geotechnical
engineer prior to the placement of the reinforcement to evaluate if adequate bearing has
been obtained and that loose soils are removed prior to concrete placement
Site Grading
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The surface layer of highly
expansive dark brown silty clay with gravel and cobbles should be stripped from the site
and disposed of properly at an off site location
Areas designated to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches
moisture conditioned to at least two 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to 90 percent of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 91 Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing or
recompacting operations should be over excavated to firm ground and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Generally the on site soils with the exception of the near surface adobe soils
should be suitable for use as engineered fill If additional imported material is required
the material should conform to the requirements stipulated in Section 30 of the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction Select Borrow for Subbase Course dated
September 1986
All fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight inches 8 in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry density
established by ASTM Test Method D should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding six inches 6 in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
of the maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 The last two
GEOLABS HAWAII

feet 2 of backfill below subgrade level in paved areas should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent of the maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Method
D 1557 91
The on site soils are silty clay soils therefore mechanical compactive effort is
necessary to achieve the proper degree of compaction for structural fill Sneepsfoot type
compactors are highly suited to the on site soils Water tamping or flooding will not
provide sufficient compaction
Due to the silty nature of the on site soils we recommend that dust control

Cut areas underlying structures or pavements should be excavated to rough finish

Existing Cesspools
There is an existing cesspool on the site servicing the existing residence It is our
understanding that this cesspool will probably be abandoned when a new septic
system is installed
There may be other abandoned cesspools within the project site The available
county and state records should be researched as well as conducting interviews
with former residents to determine their locations
When the existing cesspool is abandoned it should be opened cleaned of
loose soft materials and backfilled with granular material such as No 3 B Fine
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation up to two feet 2 below finished subgrade
Above this level the cesspool excavation should be filled with on site soils and
compacted as specified above
Any older abandoned cesspools should be similarly opened cleaned and
backfilled
Foundation Support
Spread or strip footings should bear on the very stiff alluvial soils encountered at
the site or on fill placed and compacted in accordance with the recommendations
presented herein
An allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f may be utilized for the design of
foundations bearing on the very stiff alluvial soils An allowable bearing capacity of 2 000
p s f may be utilized for the design of foundations bearing on fill placed and compacted
in accordance with the recommendations presented herein These values may be

increased by one third when temporary wind and seismic loads are considered The
footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Deeper embedment depths would not significantly increase the allowable pressure

they should be remedied by overexcavation of the soft and or weak materials to very stiff
soils and backfilling of the resultant void with on site soil placed and compacted as

We estimate that for footings designed and constructed in accordance with the

Slabs On Grade
It is anticipated that the floors of the proposed maintenance building and other
structures on the project site will be concrete slabs on grade Concrete slabs subject to
vehicular traffic should be considered rigid pavements and are discussed in a subsequent
section
Building slabs and their cushions may be supported by the upper alluvial soil or
by fill provided the subgrade is first scarified to a depth of ten inches 10 moisture
conditioned to at least two 2 percent above optimum moisture content and recompacted
to 90 percent relative dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 A six
inch 6 layer of No 3 B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided
below the slabs as cushion fill
To reduce future subgrade soils from heaving due to subsequent saturation we
recommend that the subgrade soils be kept continuously moist by water sprinkling prior
to concrete placement However care must be exercised to avoid over saturation and
softening of the slab subgrade soils Such over saturation and softening could result in
a loss of bearing support for the slab
Slab perimeter thickened edges should extend a minimum of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent exterior grade

Percolation Testing
Falling head percolation tests were conducted in two 2 areas of the project site
i e Borings Ha 1 and Ha 2 The test results are summarized in Appendix C
The test in Boring Ha 1 indicated that the soil absorption rates for the upper on site
soils are in excess of 60 minutes per inch This rate is too slow for use of subsurface
disposal Based on our observation of the soils encountered in Boring Ha 2 it was
decided to conduct a test in this deeper boring to obtain data for the possible use of a
leachpit for disposal of sanitary wastes
It is our opinion that the absorption rate of the upper soils is too low for the
practical use of soil absorption systems such as leach fields or seepage pits for the
disposal of sanitary wastes The deeper gravelly soils appear to have sufficient
absorption capacity for subsurface disposal
Based on the methods given in Recommended Standard for Individual Sewage
Systems as referenced in Section d of Hawaii Department of Health Administra
tive Rules Wastewater Systems for the percolation rates obtained from our tests the
effluent loading from a septic tank should be about 1 0 gallon per day per square foot of
effective seepage pit wall area Based on the materials encountered in our exploration
the effective area should be based only on the sidewall penetrating the gravel formation
encountered in Boring Ha 2 at a depth of 13 0 feet from the existing ground surface

Generally septic systems are designed on the basis of a minimum of 200 gallons
per day per bedroom For a three 3 bedroom residence a minimum net effective area
of at least 600 square feet would be required for the observed percolation rate It may
be necessary to distribute the effluent loading between multiple seepage pits

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data are used
in various engineering calculations The results of the direct shear tests are presented
on Plate B 1
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate B 2
Modified proctor tests ASTM Test Method D 1557 are used to determine the
maximum dry density and optimum moisture content for soils when compacted
California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883 are used to determine the pavement support
characteristics of soils These test results are shown on Plates B 3 and B 4

Fill Materials
Fill materials shall consist of on site or import materials containing less than 5

A General Site Fill
General fills shall consist of materials with particles not exceeding six 6

B Structural Fill
Structural fills shall consist of well graded materials with particles not
exceeding 3 in maximum dimension and should be sufficiently well graded
to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass
C Retaining Wall Backfill
Backfill behind retaining walls shall consist of free draining well graded
crushed rock with a maximum particle size of one inch 1 The final two
feet of backfill should consist of materials sufficiently well graded to create a
dense choking layer upon compaction

V Placement and Compaction of Fill
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers not exceeding eight inches 8
feet in loose thickness in general fill areas and six inches 6 in loose
thickness in structural fill areas Each layer shall be spread uniformly and
blade mixed to attain uniformity of material and water content within each
layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer which has not been
properly compacted Subsequent removal of any unacceptable layer shall
be done at the Contractor s expense
Structural fills should be used in the upper one foot below footing slab and
pavement subgrades
Utility alignments should be kept free of materials in excess of six inches 6
in maximum dimension to prevent difficulty in the excavation for utility
trenches
All fill shall be compacted to a minimum of 80 percent of the maximum dry
density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 unless otherwise
specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s Report
Backfill behind retaining walls should be placed in lifts not exceeding six
inches 6 in loose thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction of the maximum dry density determined by ASTM D 1557 91
