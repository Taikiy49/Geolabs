Project Considerations 
The planned Kukui’ula Resort Community development encompasses about 
1,045 acres in Koloa on the south shore of Kauai and includes hotel, vacation ownership, 
commercial park, golf course and residential uses. The project is a part of the 
improvements for Kukui’ula Resort Community.

The general area was formerly used for sugar cane cultivation. The U/AA 
Subdivision site was previously used to produce crushed rock material from the boulders 
generated by the site grading operations. The crusher machinery has been removed;

plan calls for grading and installation of related infrastructure, such as roads, drainage, 
sewer and other utilities. It is anticipated that the residential structures will be one to 
two-stories high.  The site grading plans indicate that the grading for the development will 
involve placement of fills up to about 25 feet high and cuts up to about 12 feet deep. We 
understand that required import fill will be generated from the Manuhonuhonu borrow site. 
Previous experience indicates that this borrow material has variable quality (expansive 
potential) and high natural moisture content, which may affect the embankment fill 
progress.

Field Exploration 
Five test pits were excavated by Earthworks Pacific using a track excavator. The 
test pits were concentrated in the southeastern portion of the site, where the existing 
boulder fill is thickest. 
The three test pits in this southeastern area were excavated to depths of 9 to 
11 feet below the existing ground surface. The material encountered consisted of cobbles, 
boulders, and gravel in a silty clay matrix. The excavator could not penetrate below about 
11 feet due to the abundance of boulders encountered. Only minimal amounts of organic 
material were noted in the test pits.  
Groundwater was not encountered in the test pits. 
Findings and Recommendations 
Treatment of Boulder Stockpiles 
The test pits confirmed that a significant portion of the development site is underlain 
by stockpiled material from the crusher activity. The thickness of the stockpiled material 
appears to be more than 11 feet in the southeastern portion of the site. The stockpiled 
material primarily consists of cobbles and gravel in a silty clay matrix with boulders ranging 
from 1 to 5 feet. Large or extensive voids were not observed in the test pits. 
Ideally, the stockpiled material should be removed down to the original ground, 
processed to remove material larger than 12 inches, moisture-conditioned and placed as 
structural or general fill. Boulders larger than 12 inches should be crushed to meet fill 
specifications. Alternatively, boulders larger than 12 inches but less than 36 inches may be 
used in boulder fills. A schematic sketch illustrating a boulder fill is attached. 
In order to reduce grading costs, an alternate scheme to allow some of the 
stockpiled material to remain in place was evaluated. This was deemed feasible due to the 
following reasons. 
The stockpiled material is mostly granular (i.e. cobbles, boulders, and gravel) 
and were found to be in a moderately dense state. Therefore, long term 
settlements are not anticipated. 
Minimal boulder-to-boulder contact and corresponding voids were observed 
in the test pits. 
Significant amount of fills in excess of 10 feet are planned over most of this 
area. This amount of fill can generally span over irregularities and form a 
“mat” for support of new structures.

Based on the above, it is our opinion that an alternate grading scheme may be as 
follows. 
1. 
Remove the existing stockpile material to at least 15 feet below finished 
grade.  
2. 
Proofroll and flood surface of remaining stockpile to fill voids and seal ground 
surface. 
3. 
Place geotextile (Mirafi 180 or equivalent) if boulder to boulder contact with 
voids noted in the stockpiled material. 
4. 
Place new fill (minimum 15 feet thick) over prepared stockpiled material 
surface. 
5. 
Place settlement gauges (minimum 10) at finished grade and monitor for at 
least 3 months. 
6. 
After settlement gauge readings confirm that there are no ongoing 
settlements, construction of infrastructure may begin. 
Fill Material 
A significant amount of fill is required to achieve the desired finished lot grades. Two 
sources of fill are available; the onsite boulder stockpile and soil from the Manuhonuhonu 
borrow site.  
The on-site boulder stockpile material contains a significant percentage of oversize 
material (i.e. larger than one foot size). Therefore, crushing of the oversize material would 
be required prior to use as fill. Once crushed, this material would be suitable as structural 
fill for use below future houses. 
The borrow soil has been used previously on various portions of the development. 
Past experience shows that the borrow material has variable quality, depending on where 
in the borrow pit it comes from. Therefore, the structure foundations placed on this soil may 
require special foundation design depending on the actual swelling potential of the soil near 
finished grade. This material is more adversely affected by weather conditions (rain) than 
crushed rock. Therefore, although the borrow soil is less costly than crushing the boulders 
to produce structural fill, additional expense may occur in foundation and pavement costs.
