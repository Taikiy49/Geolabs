DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our geotechnical due diligence survey methodology, test results and geotechnical 
recommendations for future renovations and upgrades at the existing Macy’s Store in 
downtown Honolulu is described in the following sections.  
Site Description 
The existing Macy’s building site is in Downtown Honolulu just north of Pier 14 of 
Honolulu Harbor. The site is bounded by Bethel Street to the west, Hotel Street to the 
north, Fort Street Mall to the east and South King Street to the south. The current ground 
elevation at the ground floor level is about 17 feet.  
The existing building consists of two levels of retail space with four levels of parking 
above. The building structure is generally of reinforced concrete with the upper floor slabs 
supported on Tri-Tee type structural system.  
Regional Geology 
The downtown Macy’s building is situated over the Honolulu coastal plain which 
consists of a series of thick marine and non-marine sediments capping the Koolau Basalt. 
The near subsurface soil structure at the site consists of a broad elevated coral reef 
partially covered by non-marine alluvium and fill. These conditions are perceived to be the 
result of a complex series of Pleistocene sea level changes. The present reef platform is 
probably a wave cut terrace formed by wave erosion of an older reef which originally stood 
about 25 feet above sea level. This widespread reef structure is known locally as the 
Waimanalo Reef.  
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration at the ground floor of the structure consisted of drilling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of about 2.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
In general, the subsurface conditions encountered under the concrete slab at the 
two boring locations revealed a moisture barrier consisting of a plastic geo membrane 
immediately under the slab followed by a cushion layer of gray gravelly sand of about 
4 and 6 inches thick in Borings Nos. 1 and 2, respectively. The cushion fill is underlain with 
select borrow subbase consisting of dense, brown sandy gravel with silt to the maximum 
depth our hand-augered borings could be advanced, at 2.5 feet. The thicknesses of the 
concrete slabs were measured at 3.75 and 4.5 inches in Borings Nos. 1 and 2 
respectively. The cores also revealed that the slab is reinforced at the bottom with 
galvanized steel wire mesh of about 1/8 inch in diameter.

Soil Strength Characterization 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing (ASTM D 6951) was performed in the 
two boring locations to obtain indicative subsoil strength characteristics such as bearing 
ratio and bearing value. The tests were performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic 
Cone Penetrometer from the top of the base course or bottom of the concrete slab through 
the subgrade to a depth of at least 5 feet. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the lower 
rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The upper rod 
holds a 4.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the 
cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone is just below the 
testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts 
and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the strength of the soil at the test 
location. Following each sequence of hammer drops, a penetration reading is taken. The 
process is continued until the desired depth of testing is reached or the full length of the 
lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as millimeters 
per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR) and bearing value 
as shown in the attached DCP Data Sheets (Appendix B). 
Based on the above tests and correlations, the in-situ layer of soil immediately 
under the floor slab (cushion fill) has an estimated bearing value of about 2,400 pounds 
per square foot (psf) in both DCP test locations. The indicative bearing values of the 
subbase material from 1 to 5 feet below ground surface are shown in the following table.

A total of 10 non-destructive rebound hammer (Schmidt Hammer) tests (ASTM 
C805/805M) using a spring driven steel hammer were performed at the site. Two of these 
tests were performed on the concrete slab-on-grade in the boring locations and the 
remaining were performed on the exposed slabs at the parking structure to assess the 
in-place uniformity of the concrete, to delineate regions in the structure of poor quality or 
deteriorated concrete and to estimate in-place strength.

Rebound Number of Hardened Concrete 
For this test, a spring loaded steel hammer is released with a pre-determined 
amount of energy to strike a steel plunger that is in contact with the surface of the 
concrete. The impact causes the steel hammer to rebound. The rebound distance of 
the steel hammer from the steel plunger is measured on a linear scale attached to 
the frame of the instrument indicating the rebound number of the concrete being 
tested. Relationship between rebound number and concrete strength provided by 
the manufacturer was used in the determination of the indicative relative concrete 
strength in different locations in the structure as shown on the Site Plan (Plate 2) 
and upper level parking plans (Plates C-1 to C-5). The following table presents a 
summary of the results of the rebound hammer tests performed on different parts of 
the structure.  
The rebound hammer test results indicate a concrete strength of 4,300 pounds 
per square inch (psi) and 3,250 psi at the ground floor slab in the vicinity of 
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, respectively. The same tests performed on the exposed 
concrete floor slabs at the parking structure measured concrete strengths 
ranging from 3,900 to 6,600 psi. The strength value of 1,600 psi obtained at the 
roof ramp of the parking structure described as Test No. T9 may not represent 
the true strength of the underlying slab due to the sandy and rough anti-slip finish 
that was placed over the ramp that affected the rebound readings. The other 
tests were performed on relatively smooth concrete slab surfaces.

Earth Retaining Structures 
Based on our discussions with the structural consultants for this project, Wallace 
Engineering, it is our understanding that earth retaining structures including new utility 
trenches of about 3 feet deep are planned as part of the future upgrades for the building. 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, the following general guidelines may be 
used for design of the retaining structures planned for the project. 
Retaining Wall Foundations 
Retaining wall foundations should be at least 18 inches wide and should be 
embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. For 
sloping ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 
6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing 
to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope 
should be constructed in stepped footing. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure 
developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations. 
A coefficient of friction of 0.4 may be used for footings bearing directly on the

Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weep holes.  As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or free-draining compacted fills, where specified by the designer. Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of 
low-expansion, relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration behind the walls. 
Slabs-On-Grade 
As part of the building upgrade and renovations work, we envision that some 
sections of the existing slabs-on-grade will be demolished to accommodate 
modifications for building improvement and replaced with new slabs bearing on the 
on-site sandy soils and/or select granular fill materials. We believe that a 
conventional design similar to the existing slab-on-grade structure may be utilized. 
However, if loose soils are encountered at the subgrade level, these soils should be 
over-excavated and replaced with select granular fill materials compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), 
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The 
open-graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and 
would serve as a capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future 
moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an 
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion fill layer. 
Where the slabs will be subjected to equipment vibration and/or vehicular traffic, we 
recommend placing the floor slab over 6 inches of aggregate subbase. The 
aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. For the design of structural slabs 
supported on the aggregate subbase, a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 
250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for the 
compacted aggregate subbase. Where slabs are intended to function as rigid 
pavements, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary 
design purposes. Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the 
slab joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic. The thickened edges of slabs

Utility Trench and Backfill         
We anticipate the underground utilities for the building renovations will primarily 
consist of water, electrical, and sewer line connections. In general, good construction 
practices should be implemented in the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the 
new utilities. The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for 
trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the 
excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety 
requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation where deemed necessary.  
In general, we recommend  providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes or groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is 
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for the formation of voids 
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or 
groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils 
(with a maximum particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill 
should be placed in 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench 
backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 
and 2, extending to depths of about 2.5 feet below the existing ground surface below the 
existing floor slab at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We drilled 
the borings through the concrete slab using a concrete coring machine and with a hand 
auger for the underlying base and subbase soils.  
Our field engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the concrete coring and hand augering 
operations. Two 4-inch-diameter concrete cores including base and subbase soil samples 
were brought to Geolabs laboratory for further examination and relevant testing. The Logs 
of the borings are shown as Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Two dynamic cone penetrometer tests were performed within the two boring 
locations to obtain indicative subsoil strength characteristics such as bearing ratio and 
bearing value down to at least 5 feet below the soil surface. The results of these tests are 
shown as Plates B-1 and B-2. 
A total of 10 non-destructive rebound hammer tests using a spring driven steel 
hammer were performed at the site. Two of these were performed on the concrete 
slab-on-grade in the boring locations and the rest on the exposed slabs at the parking 
structure to assess the in-place uniformity of the concrete, to delineate regions in the 
structure of poorer quality or deteriorated concrete and to estimate in-place strength. The 
results of these tests are shown in the text of this report.
