Dear Mr. Barrett: 
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Barrett Residence, 4152 Koko Drive project in the Honolulu area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and project vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the proposed project. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at 4152 Koko Drive in the Kaimuki area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that subsequent to heavy rain events, distress was noted on 
top of the driveway and garage concrete slabs. In addition, we understand that rainwater 
was observed flowing down from the neighbor’s wall and into the driveway and garage area 
prior to constructing a curb in front of the neighbor’s driveway. 
Numerous cracks were noted on top of the existing driveway concrete slab. Some of 
the cracks in the slab were repaired with cement grout. In addition, differential settlement 
was observed at the crack locations (see Photograph Nos. 1 through 3, Plates 2 and 3). 
One of the cracks observed on the driveway extended into the garage slab area. See 
Photograph No. 1, on Plate 2). 
The driveway and garage structures are located on a sloping hillside. The south and 
west end of the driveway and garage structures are bearing on Concrete Masonry Unit

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of about 20.5 and 2 feet below 
the existing ground surface, respectively. Boring No. 1 was drilled on the existing driveway 
area and Boring No. 2 was drilled within the garage area. 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface concrete slab, about 6 and 
4.5 inches thick, underlain by fill to about 5 feet deep.  Below the fill, colluvial deposits were 
encountered extending to the maximum explored depth of 20.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The driveway concrete slab thickness was 6 inches and the garage 
concrete slab thickness was 4 inches. 
The fill layer generally consisted of medium dense to very dense sandy 
gravel/cobbles. The colluvial deposits consisted of very stiff clay, dense to very dense 
gravelly cobbles, silty sand, and sandy gravel with cobbles. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may fluctuate 
depending on seasonal precipitation, and other factors. Detailed descriptions of the 
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and 
A-2 in Appendix A.  Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples are 
presented in Appendix B. Photograph of the recovered core samples are presented in 
Appendix C. 
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our exploration, the project site is generally underlain by 
dense to very dense, and very stiff fill and colluvium. Groundwater was not encountered in 
the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our observations during drilling, the gravelly cobbles encountered at about 
6.5 feet depth appeared to be loose and unstable. In addition, voids were noted between 
the cobbles in the deposit. The moisture content of the silty sand deposit right below the 
gravelly cobbles layer was high at a measured moisture content of 73 percent. 
Based on the above, we suspect that during the heavy rain events, the rainwater 
flowed down through the upper fill and clayey colluvium, and through the gravelly cobbles 
and into the underlying silty sand layer. The fines from the upper fill and clayey colluvium 
may have been transported into the more open graded gravelly cobbles layer. It appears 
that the distresses observed on the driveway and garage concrete slabs may be due to the 
loss of fines in the upper fill and colluvium which may cause settlement of the subsoil.

To reduce the potential for future loss of fines and settlement, we recommend that a 
drilling and grouting program be performed in the driveway and garage areas. Due to the 
low headroom within the existing garage area, drilling and grouting utilizing a hollow bar 
system would need to be utilized. We recommend drilling probe holes at 5-foot on centers 
in a grid pattern. The probe holes should be about 3 inches in diameter and should extend 
to a depth of at least 18 feet below the top of the concrete slab. Geolabs should review the 
proposed probing layout to evaluate whether the above requirements are met. 
The probe holes should be backfilled with cement grout with the lowest slump that 
can be pumped. The cement grout should be injected at low to moderate pressures. The 
probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a Geolabs 
representative. This would allow our firm to monitor the presence voids and to allow 
additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and/or changed conditions are 
observed.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 20.5 and 2 feet below the 
existing ground surface, respectively. The borings were drilled using a portable drill rig 
equipped with auger and coring equipment and concrete core drill. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our engineer, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM  D3550 and D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch O.D. Modified California sampler and a 2-inch O.D. standard penetration sampler 
with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24-inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the cobbly materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses 
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit 
is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual length 
of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For 
example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
