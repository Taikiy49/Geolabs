SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the project site 
generally consist of a surface layer of volcanic ash soils overlying medium dense to dense 
clinker materials and/or hard basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 11 feet below the existing ground surface. In general, the near-surface 
a’a clinker deposits consisted of weakly cemented to cemented sandy gravel and cobbles 
with some boulders, and varied from medium dense to dense. The exception being 
Test Pit No. 3 in which an apparent 8.5-foot thick layer of uncontrolled loose fill was 
encountered overlying dense a’a clinker.  
The near-surface ash materials, which are relatively soft and will compress when 
subjected to structural and vehicular loads, should be removed at the commencement of 
earthwork to expose the underlying dense clinkers and/or basalt rock formation. The 
stripped volcanic ash soils should be disposed of properly off-site or re-used in 
landscaping areas. After removal of the surface volcanic ash soils, we recommend 
proof-rolling the cut subgrades with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller 
for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or 
voids. 
Uncontrolled loose fill containing wood log fragments was encountered at Test Pit 
No. 3 to a depth of 9 feet below the existing ground surface. In our opinion, this 
uncontrolled fill is susceptible to irregular and unpredictable future settlements. For this 
reason, the fill is not considered suitable material to build upon and should be removed 
and replaced with compacted structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist 
of well-graded granular materials generally less than 3 inches in maximum dimension. In 
general, the excavated on-site materials may be re-used as a source of structural fill 
materials, provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum particle size 
and gradation requirements presented above. 
Based on the structural loads anticipated and the subsurface conditions 
encountered during our field exploration at the site, we believe a shallow foundation 
system consisting of individual spread footings and grade beams may be used to support 
the proposed storage facility. The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades. We recommend using an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) to size the footings bearing on 
compacted structural fill materials, dense clinkers, and/or basalt rock formation. This 
bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of 
five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent geologic 
time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be dormant), and Mauna Loa 
and Kilauea (both are still active). 
The project site is on the southeastern flank of Mauna Loa, which has been built 
up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic materials. The site 
is near the contact between the eastern flank of Mauna Loa and the northern flank of 
Kilauea. The near-surface lava flows are from Mauna Loa and overlie the older 
Kilauea Shield. The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to be Holocene in 
age and belong to the Kau Basalt Series. Near-surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa 
Shield may be as recent as a few years but are generally on the order of about 200 to 
3,000 years in age. 
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of both a'a and 
pahoehoe type flows originating from the northeast rift of Mauna Loa. A’a lava is 
typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a 
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of 
vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe lava. 
Cavities form when lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes 
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when 
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may 
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are 
generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water 
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is about 4 miles northwest of Volcano town on the Island of 
Hawaii and is accessed from the existing Piimauna Drive roadway adjacent to the 
Volcano Golf Course. The site is unused vacant land located on the southwest side of 
Pu’u O’o Volcano Trail at the base of a Pali, which appears to be an approximate 40 to 
50-foot thick a’a flow.  
At the time of our field exploration, the project site was generally covered with 
wild grass roughly 2 feet in height. Based on our field observations and the topographic 
map provided, the site gently slopes down to the southeast with existing ground 
elevations on the order of about +4,098 to +4,107 feet MSL. We understand this site 
was previously used as an animal corral site and may have been graded with small cuts 
and fills to achieve a safe usable pasture. 
During our site reconnaissance, a fissure (ground crack) was observed through 
the tall grass covering the site, approximately 70 feet west of Pu’u O’o Volcano Trail 
near the existing fence. The visible portion of the ground fissure was roughly 8 feet in 
length, 1.5 to 2 feet in width, and 4 feet deep. Note that ground cracks/fissures are 
known to exist throughout this regional area and could possibly be a fault rupture.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by excavating and 
sampling six test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 6 extending to depths 
ranging from 3 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface layer of volcanic ash soils 
overlying medium dense to dense clinker materials and/or hard basalt rock formation 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 11 feet below the existing ground 
surface. In general, the near-surface a’a clinker deposits consisted of weakly cemented 
to cemented sandy gravel and cobbles with some boulders, and varied from medium 
dense to dense. The test pits were terminated on hard basalt rock formation which 
could not be penetrated with the excavator. 
The exception was Test Pit No. 3 in which an apparent 8.5-foot thick layer of 
uncontrolled loose fill was encountered overlying dense a’a clinker. This fill consisted of 
brown silty gravel and cobbles with sand, occasional boulders, and wood log fragments. 
The surface volcanic ash soils, locally referred to as Pahala Ash, generally 
consist of soft/loose friable silty fine sands extending to depths of about 0.5 to 2 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The ash is characterized as having very high in-situ 
moisture contents and is usually highly compressible. The ash also frequently exhibits 
thixotropic properties (i.e., the material loses strength when disturbed by remolding or 
when subjected to earthquake vibrations). 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors.

on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration are presented in

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006) as amended, 
the project site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will 
need to be addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity 
and soil profile type for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by 
shifts in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked 
primarily to volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally 
occurs before or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result 
from the underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but 
does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each 
year, but most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive 
instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and 
a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa 
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in 
Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic 
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from 
the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has 
experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6.0. Based on 
information obtained from the United States Geological Survey (USGS) Bulletin 
2006, the following is a list of destructive earthquakes that occurred on the Island 
of Hawaii since 1868.

3.2 
Foundation Probing 
Based on our field exploration and experience in the project area, cavities and/or 
voids are commonly encountered in the pahoehoe lava flows that characterize the 
project site. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the 
collapse of cavities below foundations, a cavity probing and grouting program is usually 
specified for new structure foundations during construction. However, due to light loads 
anticipated and budget constraints, it is our opinion that the probing and grouting 
program may be omitted if some risk is acceptable to the owner. 
If a higher level of assurance is desired to reduce the risk of settlement due to 
collapse of cavities, then a probing and grouting program should be implemented. 
For estimating purposes, we recommend drilling probe holes at each isolated 
spread footing location (one probe per 50 square feet). The probe holes should be at

3.4.1 Site Preparation 
As previously mentioned, the project site is generally underlain by basalt lava 
flows covered with volcanic ash soils at the ground surface. The volcanic ash 
soils, which are relatively soft/loose and will compress when subjected to 
structural and vehicular loads, generally have poor structural characteristics with 
respect to foundation and pavement support. In addition, ash soils have 
thixotropic properties (i.e., they temporarily lose strength when remolded or are 
subjected to vibrations, such as seismic events). Therefore, to the extent

practicable, site grading should be designed to remove the surface ash soils from 
structural or pavement areas.  
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. As previously mentioned, we recommend 
removing the surface volcanic ash soils to expose the underlying dense clinkers 
and/or basalt rock formation. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. The 
contractor should exercise caution while clearing and grading near-collapsed lava 
tube features and/or cavities that may further collapse under the heavy construction 
equipment loads. 
After removal of the surface volcanic ash soils, we recommend proof-rolling the cut 
subgrades with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a 
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or 
voids. The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during 
clearing and proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with 
compacted fill materials. Contract documents should include additive and deductive 
unit prices for over-excavation of the collapsed lava tube features (and other 
features) and backfilling with compacted fill to account for variations in the 
over-excavation and backfill quantities. 
The uncontrolled loose fill containing wood log fragments encountered at Test Pit 
No. 3 to a depth of 9 feet below the existing ground surface should be removed 
and replaced with compacted structural fill materials. Structural fill materials 
should consist of well-graded granular materials generally less than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension. In general, the excavated on-site materials may be re-used 
as a source of structural fill materials, provided that the materials are processed 
to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements presented above. 
The structural fill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts of about 12 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.

3.4.2 Volcanic Ash Soils 
As previously mentioned, the surface volcanic ash soils at the site, locally referred 
to as Pahala Ash, consist of soft/loose friable silty fine sands generally extending to 
depths about 0.5 to 2 feet below the existing ground surface. The volcanic ash is 
characterized as having very high in-situ moisture contents and is highly 
compressible. Due to the soft consistency and high moisture contents, our 
experience in the past with similar material suggests that the contractor will have a 
difficult time when working with these volcanic ash soils during the earthwork 
operation. 
Volcanic ash soils in the existing ground may be left in place, provided that there 
are no structures, pavements, slabs, and other improvements on top.  Volcanic ash 
soils under building should be completely removed. Since the volcanic ash soils 
also exhibit thixotropic properties (i.e., the material loses strength when disturbed 
by remolding (during earthwork operation) or when subjected to earthquake 
vibrations (which can be expected on the Big Island) or dynamic loads 
(from vehicular traffic), we recommend removing volcanic ash soils within the 
pavement areas.  
In other areas where the volcanic ash deposit is thick and it is not practical to 
completely remove the volcanic ash soils, consideration may be given to removing 
the top 2 to 3 feet of ash soils and replacing with a layer of woven geotextile fabric 
capped with 2 to 3 feet of structural fill material.  Our field exploration at this site 
suggests that the volcanic ash soils are generally on the order of about 0.5 to 2 feet 
thick.  Therefore, in cut areas and in areas where the design finish grades are close 
to the existing grades, we believe that the amount of volcanic ash soils remaining 
after the clearing and grubbing operation would be minor. As a result, we 
recommend completely removing the surface ash soils within the pavement areas 
under such conditions.   
As indicated earlier, we believe that the volcanic ash soils may be stockpiled and 
reused as a source of general fill materials in landscape areas.  However, we would 
like to point out that additional handling may be required due to the soft consistency

ranging from about 50 to over 100 percent.   
3.4.3 Fill and Backfill Materials 
In general, we recommend replacing the uncontrolled loose fill encountered in 
Test Pit No. 3 and the stripped volcanic ash soils with compacted structural fill 
materials. Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials 
generally less than 3 inches in maximum dimension. In addition, the material should 
have a laboratory CBR value of 25 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. The excavated 
on-site materials may be re-used as a source of structural fill materials, provided 
that the materials are processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation 
requirements presented above.  
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials required for the project 
should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Sections 30 
and 31 of the County of Hawaii Standard Specifications (September, 1986). 
Geolabs should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations. 
Imported fill materials, if required, should be free of organics and deleterious 
materials and should be suitable for the intended use based on the 
recommendations for structural fill materials provided herein. Geolabs should test 
the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
3.4.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base course

3.4.5 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by medium 
dense to dense clinkers and basalt rock formation near the existing ground surface. 
We anticipate the dense clinkers and vugular (porous) basalt formation may be 
excavated readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators 
and ripped with large bulldozers. However, some of the deeper excavations into the 
dense lava may require the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based 
on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and field and laboratory 
data from the excavated test pits. Contractors should be encouraged to examine 
the site conditions and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and 
prudent interpretation. 
3.4.6 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the excavated test pits, it appears that most of the cut slopes would 
expose medium dense to dense clinkers and/or basalt formations. In general, we 
believe that the cut slopes planned at the site exposing basalt formation may be 
designed with a slope inclination of one and one-half horizontal to one vertical 
(1.5H:1V) or flatter. However, due to the presence of clinker layers, the individual 
cut slopes should be re-evaluated during construction. In areas where clinker 
layers, unstable, and/or friable materials are exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut 
slopes may need to be flattened to inclinations of 2H:1V or flatter. Therefore, 
provisions to flatten the cut slopes and/or construct retaining walls should be 
considered in the design. A Geolabs representative should observe the cut slopes

consistent with our assumptions. 
Permanent fill slopes, if required, may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V 
or flatter. This assumes that the fill slopes will be constructed using well-graded 
granular materials (structural and general fills). In addition, fills placed on slopes 
steeper than 5H:1V should be benched. The filling operations should start at the 
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed 
by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted 
slope face. The fill slope face should be free of voids, which would allow erosion 
and migration of fines to occur. Surface water should be diverted away from the 
tops of slopes. In addition, slope planting or other means of slope protection should 
be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the 
finished slopes.

bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
