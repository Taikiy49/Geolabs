SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be required for Segments A through C of the Aiea Access
Road Reconstruction project in the Aiea area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Because the
project will involve less than 4 lane miles of new pavements a detailed economic
comparison involving a rigid pavement section and flexible pavement section will not be
required for the access road reconstruction project
Based on our evaluation of the existing site conditions such as sloping travelway
and the proposed limited pavement reconstruction work it is our opinion that inclusion of
an untreated permeable base UTPB layer for subdrainage purposes would not be
practical nor necessary for the subject project In addition we believe that the majority of
the pavement distresses observed along Segment B are a result of the high stresses
imposed by the wheel loads during the vehicular turn around the loop ramp Therefore
we recommend that rigid pavements be used for Segment B of this project Finally in an
attempt to reduce the potential for prolonged disturbance to vehicular traffic asphalt
concrete base ACB is generally used as the base material to facilitate the pavement
construction work to be performed in a timely manner Therefore the use of aggregate
base course untreated was not considered in our pavement design analyses
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and the traffic data
provided for our pavement analyses we recommend that new pavements for Segment A
consist of 3 5 inches of AC on 14 inches of ACB For the rigid pavements along Segment
B the pavement section may consist of 9 5 inches of PCC on 6 inches of ASB in order to
resist the high stresses imposed by the wheel loads around the loop ramp Finally a
pavement section consisting of 3 inches of AC on 12 inches of ACB may be used for the
new pavements along Segment C
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific
recommendations for pavement design

analyses performed for the design of new roadway pavements for the Aiea Access

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield
reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range and is
composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava from the Koolau Volcanic
Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular
basalt interbedded with thin clinker layers The lava flows have weathered in place and
are characterized by the red colors of the soil The brown soils are generally ancient
alluvial deposited over the surface During the construction of the existing roadways
man made fills were placed in localized areas to raise the site to the existing grades

those described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 The

3 6 Fill Materials and Compaction Requirements
Imported fill materials below the asphalt concrete base or aggregate subbase
layer if required should consist of non expansive select granular material such as
crushed coral or basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The material should have a
laboratory CBR value Kentucky method of 4 or more or a R value of 20 or more with
a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
AASHTO T 193 or ASTM Test Designation D 1883 test procedures
In addition the select granular fill material should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve Imported materials should be brought
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction Surfaces should be wetted finished to create relatively smooth
unyielding subgrades and kept moist until covered by pavements or slabs Imported

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3 7 Pavement Joints
As previously mentioned rigid pavements is recommended for Segment B of the
proposed roadway reconstruction project In general joints should be provided in rigid
pavements for the following reasons
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused by
temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction
Paragraph Pavement Joints in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design
Manual prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation should be
referred to for pavement joint details and construction requirements
3 8 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction

examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
