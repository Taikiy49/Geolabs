Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally blanketed by a thin
surface fill layer consisting of low to moderately expansive clayey soils about 0 to 4.5 feet
thick, underlain by a residual soil horizon consisting of very stiff to hard silty clay about 3 to
8 feet thick. This horizon was, in turn, underlain by a deeper residual soil consisting of very

grades.

slab-on-grade design. To reduce the potential for distress to the future slabs-on-grade
resulting from swelling of the clayey soils, we recommend providing a minimum of
12 inches of non-expansive, select granular fill material below the building slab cushion.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Haliimaile Village Center project in the Hali imaile area on
the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, and pavements only. The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report.

may be used to provide grade separation at various locations within the development.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older West Maui
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene
Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these two
volcanoes. The project site is near the northern end of the island and on the lower
northern slope of the East Maui Volcano (Haleakala).
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows. The
lower unit, the Honomanu Volcanic Series (late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch)
consists of thinly-bedded, basaltic pahoehoe and a’a lavas. These lavas tend to be very
permeable and carry water for the basal aquifer. Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic
Series (early Pleistocene Epoch), primarily composed of thicker alkalic a’a flows, which
may contain many inter-stratified, thin ash-soil layers. A long quiescent period followed,
during which time, canyons were carved in the volcano, although a few eruptions may
have occurred during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes
(higher and lower stands of the sea level). In-situ chemical weathering of the Kula lava
flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years. The weathering process has
formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite. In general, saprolite is mainly composed
of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite
grades to basaltic rock formation with depth.
The project site is in an area that has been mapped as being underlain by soils of
the Haliimaile Series (HhHB and HhC). This series consists of well-drained soils on
uplands on the Island of Maui. These soils developed in material weathered from basic
igneous rock. They are gently to strongly sloping. Elevations range from 500 to
2,000 feet. The annual rainfall amounts to 30 to 50 inches. The mean annual soil
temperature is 71° F. Haliimaile soils are geographically associated with
Hamakuapoko, Keahua, Paia, and Pane soils.

3.1. Building Foundations
Based on the existing site topography, we anticipate that shallow cuts and fills
will be used to create pads for the future buildings.
Based on the conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe that
shallow spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support the new building
structures at the site. An allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per square foot
(psf) may be used for the design of foundations bearing on compacted fill or prepared
subgrade.
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing
over-excavations should be further over-excavated to expose underlying firm materials
and then backfilled with non-expansive select granular fill material compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of
the slope. Footings adjacent to planned retaining walls should be embedded deep
enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the planned retaining
walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads.
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is

necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent below-grade utility lines or structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement.
Lateral loads acting on the building structure may be resisted by friction
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A
coefficient of friction of 0.30 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff on-site soils or

A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavation and completed

embedment depths.
3.2. Slabs-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for buildings and other
structures in the proposed mixed-use development. The near-surface soils encountered
at the site consist of clayey silt and silty clay, which exhibit a low to moderate expansion
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. Therefore, to reduce the potential for
structural distress to lightly loaded slabs, we recommend providing a minimum of
12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill material below the concrete slab. The
subgrades for slabs-on-grades should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches,
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted
to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

The non-expansive select granular fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral, basalt gravel or cinder
sand. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger
than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a swell of 1 percent or less when tested
in accordance with ASTM D 1883. It should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. All select granular fill materials should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction. The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered
by base course.
For interior building slabs, we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer
of cushion fill consisting of No. 3 Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the
slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill would serve as a capillary moisture break
and would provide for uniform support of the slabs. Where slabs will be subject to
vehicular traffic, such as trucks and/or forklifts, the slabs should be considered as rigid
pavements and should be designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the pavement design section herein.
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled
tightly against the edges of the slabs with low expansion, relatively impervious soils.
These areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce
the potential for water ponding around the slabs and foundations.

3.3.3 Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls
A mechanically stabilized earth retaining wall (MSERW) is a composite wall system,
which utilizes high density polyethylene, or other reinforcing elements to reinforce
the backfill zone and improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone. This
composite system (e.g., Keystone or Anchor Block walls) essentially forms a gravity
wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and differential settlements.
In addition, MSERW are also desirable due to the flexibility of the wall, ease of
construction, high load carrying capacity, and economy.
Hawaii * California
SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Design of the MSERW system will need to take into consideration both the external
and internal stability of the structure. In evaluating external stability, the retaining
wall must satisfy four stability conditions: (1) bearing failure, (2) translational
sliding, (3) overturning stability, and (4) overall slope stability. Geotechnical design
parameters to evaluate these stability conditions are presented in the following
subsections. Some of the geotechnical parameters necessary in evaluating the
internal stability of the retaining wall are presented in the "Reinforced Backfill
Materials" subsection.
3.3.3.a MSERW Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we
recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf for
design of the retaining wall bearing on the on-site soils. This bearing value
is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In
general, the retaining wall should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. In addition, the footing should
be extended deeper to obtain a minimum 8-foot setback distance
measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of
the slope for sloping ground conditions.

3.8 Drainage
As previously indicated, proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations and slabs constructed on moderately expansive soil conditions. Therefore,
the finished grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water away from the
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also advised to install gutter
systems around the building and to divert the discharge away from the foundation and
pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the
subsoils.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations
should be backfilled properly against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration. In addition, drainage swales
should be provided as. soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from foundations and slabs.

We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
a total of eleven borings. The approximate locations of the borings drilled and bulk

truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a

testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1

Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified

samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with

needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are

sample depths.
Pocket electrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in

summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B-1.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on
bulk samples of in-situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3.
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on a bulk
sample of the near-surface soils and potential borrow material to evaluate the dry
density and moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-4.
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D 2166) was performed on a selected
sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the material tested. The test
results are summarized on Plate B-5.
Seven one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under
different surcharge loads. The test results are summarized on Plate B-6.
Four sets of corrosion tests, including pH (AASHTO T 289), and one minimum
resistivity test (ASTM G 57), were performed by E.L. Pacific on selected soil samples
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-7.
