SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The anticipated subsurface conditions at the northern portion of the site would likely 
consist of alluvial deposits about 1 to 2 feet thick over medium dense calcareous 
sediments and hard coralline limestone.  The anticipated subsurface conditions at the 
southern portion of the site would likely consist of a thin surficial layer of soil over medium 
dense calcareous sediments and medium hard to hard coralline limestone.  Groundwater 
is generally not anticipated to be a consideration for this project. 
Based on the grading plan provided, fill on the order of about 6 to 11 feet high will 
be required for the majority of the site.  The fill will be placed at the central and southern 
half of the site.  Minimal cuts are planned along the northern side of the site.   
We envision that imported general fill material will be used as fill for the site.  The 
imported general fill material should be less than 6 inches in maximum dimension and be 
free of vegetation and deleterious materials. The material should have a laboratory CBR 
value of 5 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 3 percent when tested 
in accordance with ASTM D1883. Imported general fill material meeting the above 
requirements should be placed at least 3 feet below the proposed finished mass grades.   
Areas filled with the imported general fill materials should be capped with at least 
3 feet of structural fill material.  Structural fill material may consist of the excavated on-site 
calcareous sediments and reef formation materials provided that the material is well 
graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  Imported 
structural fill used for the capping layer should meet the above requirements. In addition, 
the imported structural fill should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent.  The text of this report should be referred to 
for detailed discussions and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was mainly built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western portion of the island.  
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the northern and eastern portions of the island.  
As the older Waianae volcanic activity ceased, younger lava flows from Koolau 
continued to bank against Waianae’s east facing slopes forming a broad upland, known 
as the Schofield Plateau. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main 
shield-building phase approximately 2 million years ago.   
Chemical weathering and physical erosion of the volcanic uplands generated 
copious terrestrial sediments, which were transported down slope toward the coastline. 
The terrestrial and embedded marine sediments accumulated to comprise the low 
topographic gradient coastal plain known as the Ewa Plain.  In the project vicinity 
(Ko Olina) and further towards the east and northeast (East Kapolei), the terrestrial 
sediments accumulated and became interbedded with other marine sediments and 
hardened coral-algal and coral limestone reef formations to form a thick sedimentary 
wedge of material fronting the mountain foothills.  The thickness of the collective 
sedimentary wedge ranges from nearly absent in the area underlain by the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at the shoreline near Ewa Beach.  This wedge of 
sedimentary and calcareous reef materials comprising the Ewa Plain serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu.  
Deposition of recent or Holocene age sediments has continued from earlier geologic 
time through the present.   
During the Pleistocene Epoch, the global sea level fluctuated in response to 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.

The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.  These changes in sea level, over periods of geologic 
time, was a major driving force in both the deposition and erosion of the terrestrial 
alluvial/colluvial deposits and coralline reef materials underlying the project site.  In 
addition, previous agricultural development conducted within the last 100 years or so 
have aided in the transformation of the relatively undeveloped project site to its present 
landform. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is at the south side of the Kapolei West Development in the 
Kapolei area in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The site is located 
north of the Oahu Railway and Land (OR&L) railroad tracks and south of Pump Road.  
The location of the project site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The site is undeveloped and appears to have been used previously for 
agriculture crops and livestock.   In general, the site is vegetated with dryland grasses, 
Haole Koa brush and scattered Kiawe trees.  We observed some abandoned concrete 
slabs and construction materials.  The project site is generally level with existing ground 
surface elevations ranging between about +60 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) and +63 feet 
MSL.   
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
A field exploration was not conducted at the project site.  Instead, a review of our 
previous overview study of the Kapolei West Development was performed in order to 
evaluate the subsurface conditions at the site.  The subsurface information is contained 
in our report entitled “Preliminary Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, Overview 
Study, Kapolei West Development, Kapolei, Oahu, Hawaii” dated January 22, 2013.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The anticipated subsurface conditions at the southern portion of the site consist 
of a thin surficial layer of soil over medium dense calcareous sediments and medium 
hard to hard coralline limestone.  The northern portion of the site would likely consist of 
alluvial deposits about 1 to 2 feet thick over medium dense calcareous sediments and 
hard coralline limestone. Based on the existing ground surface elevations, groundwater 
is generally not anticipated to be a consideration for this project. 
The grading plan indicates that the majority of the site will be filled on the order of 
about 6 to 11 feet high.  Minimal cuts are planned along the northern portion of the site.  
In general, the majority of the fill materials required to fill the site will be imported to the 
site.  Imported general fill material should consist of materials less than 6 inches in 
maximum dimension and be free of vegetation and deleterious materials. The material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 5 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
less than 3 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Imported general fill 
material should be placed at least 3 feet below the proposed finished mass grades.  
Where imported, general fill materials are used within the fill areas, these areas should 
be capped with at least 3 feet of structural fill material.  Our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations for the design of site grading, are further discussed in the following 
sections.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site.  Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills.  The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used 
in landscape areas, where appropriate.   
Slabs and foundations of existing structures to be demolished should be 
completely removed. Over-excavations resulting from the demolition operations 
should be backfilled with compacted on-site soils or structural fill material. 
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting 
excavation should be properly backfilled with on-site soils or structural fill material 
placed in 8-inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
Cavities and/or voids are encountered commonly in coralline limestone. In areas 
where coralline limestone are anticipated to be near the finished subgrade in cut 
areas or areas to receive fill, special attention should be given to the subgrade 
preparation to reduce the potential for distress to the future improvements. 
The finished subgrade in cut areas or areas to receive fill should be proof-rolled 
with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller to detect the potential presence of

near-surface cavities, sinkholes, or loose zones. The vibratory drum roller should 
be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. A Geolabs representative 
should monitor the proof-rolling operations. If the proof-rolling operations detect a 
near-surface cavity or loose material zones, the area should be ripped open, 
cleaned of the debris, and then backfilled with compacted fill materials or lean 
concrete. 
Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, subgrades exposing soil should then be 
scarified, where possible, to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the 
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density 
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is 
the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the majority of the excavated on-site soils will consist of the clayey 
alluvial deposits.  We believe that the excavated clayey alluvial deposits may be 
used as a source of general fill materials provided that the materials are properly 
processed and placed in accordance with the “Fill Placement and Compaction 
Requirements” subsection herein.   
Calcareous sediments and coralline limestone encountered during excavation may 
be used as fills provided that these materials are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension.  If 
large sized cobbles and boulders are generated during the excavation into the 
coralline limestone, the large sized cobbles and boulders should not be used as fill 
material unless these cobbles and boulders are processed into particles that are no 
greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension. 
Imported general fill material should consist of materials less than 6 inches in 
maximum dimension and be free of vegetation and deleterious materials. The

Where imported general fill materials and the on-site clayey soils are used within

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Structural fill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment.

3.1.4 Excavation 
Based on the grading plan, minimal cuts are planned for the project.  Excavation at 
the site will likely be in the clayey alluvial deposits.  It is anticipated that the clayey 
soils may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as a large backhoe 
excavator.  
If medium hard to hard coralline limestone and medium dense calcareous 
sediments are encountered during excavation, we anticipate that the calcareous 
sediments may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as a large 
backhoe excavator. However, the coralline limestone would likely require hoe-ram 
or chipping.   
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
experience in the area. We recommend encouraging the contractor to examine the 
site conditions and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent 
interpretation. 
3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe that cut and fill slopes may be made at an inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.  If the existing ground surface is 
steeper than 5H:1V, fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to 
provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling operations should start at 
the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be 
allowed.  In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching

Construction Monitoring 
Geolabs should be retained to provide geotechnical engineering services during 
construction of the proposed project. The critical items of construction monitoring that 
require "Special Inspection" include observation of excavations, subgrade preparation, 
fill placement and compaction, and trench backfill construction. A Geolabs

actual soil conditions encountered during construction are different from those assumed

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from site reconnaissance, literature research, and past project 
experience in the vicinity of the proposed development. It should be noted that it is not 
possible to observe and/or anticipate all the site conditions based on a brief 
reconnaissance of the project site and review of literature. Variations of the subsurface 
conditions from those described in this report may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations 
then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented herein. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of R.M. Towill Corporation 
and other project subconsultants for specific application to the proposed Kapolei West 
Mass Grading – Phase 3 project in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical 
engineering principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in the design of site grading only. Therefore, this report may not contain 
sufficient data, or the proper information, to serve as the basis for preparation of 
construction cost estimates nor for bidding purposes. A contractor wishing to bid on this 
project should retain a competent geotechnical engineer to assist in the interpretation of 
this report and/or in the performance of additional site-specific exploration for bid 
estimating purposes. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are 
commonly encountered. Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft 
deposits, hard layers, or cavities, may occur in localized areas and may require 
additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to 
attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is 
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs.
