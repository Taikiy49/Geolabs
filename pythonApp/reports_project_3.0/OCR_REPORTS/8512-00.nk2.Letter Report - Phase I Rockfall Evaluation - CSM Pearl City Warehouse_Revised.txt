REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity at Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from 
Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming the Schofield Plateau. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene Epoch and early 
Pleistocene Epoch by the extrusion of successive thin-bedded lava flows. The main 
shield-building stage ceased approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from historic 
drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet 
since the cessation of the early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). During 
the period of island subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow along the southern 
coast of Oahu forming embayments protected by barrier reefs. A series of lagoons 
formed behind the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments 
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the oceans fell because there was less 
water available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded (melted), 
global sea levels rose because the volume of water increased. The land mass 
comprising the Island of Oahu remained essentially stable during these water level 
changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations 
resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea 
level on the Island of Oahu.  
The higher sea level stands caused landform changes, including the 
accumulation of deltas and alluvial fans composed of terrigenous sediments in the 
heads of the old bays, the accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher 
elevations, and the accumulation of lagoonal and/or marine sediments in the quiet 
lagoonal waters protected by barrier reefs. The concurrent growth of reefs and the 
accumulation of lagoonal sediments also resulted in the deposition of coral-algal 
limestone and marl materials within the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary unit. 
The lower sea level stands caused streams to carve drainageways into the 
coastal plain platforms composed of sedimentary and coral reef deposits. In addition, 
subaerial exposure of the calcareous sediments caused the consolidation of the soft 
deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and the induration of calcareous reef materials.

During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued meandering stream

The project site is within the lowland coastal alluvial plains along the southern 
distal flank of the Koolau Mountain Range.  The coastal plain surfaces are relatively flat 
to gently undulating in the easterly to westerly direction.  Two primary stream channels 
discharge at the Middle Loch of Pearl Harbor. The meandering streams have 
contributed significantly to the erosional evolution and deposition of the coastal alluvial 
plains by incising into the underlying basaltic rock (Koolau Basalt) comprising the 
basement rock formation of this portion of the Island of Oahu. 
On the Pearl City/Aiea coastal plains and vicinity, the sedimentary wedge of 
alluvial materials is referred to as caprock overlying the basal aquifer. The capping 
material keeps the basal aquifer under a confining water pressure; therefore, artesian 
flow conditions may be encountered when the basal aquifer with sufficient groundwater 
head is pierced, and a conduit is provided, extending toward the ground surface. 
In summary, the project site is located on the Southern Oahu Coastal Plain near 
the shoreline of Middle Loch of Pearl Harbor and adjacent to Waiawa Stream. In 
general, the subsurface materials consist of alluvial soils, residual soils, and basalt rock 
formation.

Rockfall Mechanisms 
Based on our site reconnaissance at the project site, it appears that there are two 
main mechanisms generating rockfall hazards at the site: raveling failure and differential 
erosion of alternating volcanic rock layers. 
Raveling of one or several rocks from the slope may occur due to a number of 
causes. The hazard develops when the raveling rock falls and has sufficient velocity 
and volume to impact and damage the adjacent building. Raveling may be caused by 
water pressure behind the rock, differential weathering or erosion along faults or shear 
zones, weakening of rock due to temperature and moisture cyclic fluctuations, and 
loosening by animals or trees and wind. Due to the weathered condition of the basalt 
rock slope surfaces in the project area, raveling is an important process that continually 
works to shed weathered and broken rock materials from the slope. 
One of the most frequent causes of raveling is by invasive tree roots prying on 
the existing cracks located on the rock slope surface. Tree roots (especially of the larger 
trees) frequently develop and grow larger in the discontinuities of rock materials. The 
added force of wind action on tree limbs and branches may create leveraged forces that 
are transferred to the roots lodged within the discontinuity. Over time, the repetitive 
leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock and cause it to fall. Because most of the 
existing slope is covered by small to medium-sized trees, we believe that tree root pry is 
a significant mechanism for creating rock slope instability because of the hardy growth 
of trees and vegetation on the slope. In addition, the large trees near the top-of-slope 
outside of the property boundary may also generate rockfall hazards that will affect the 
future warehouse structure. 
In addition to raveling processes, inter-layered rock with variable strength and 
rates of weathering commonly exhibits accelerated erosion and rockfall. The weaker 
layers tend to erode and undercut the more weathering resistant rock located above. 
This may lead to the raveling of the harder layers or failure of undercut blocks of rock as 
support from the underlying weaker layers is removed. As mentioned previously, an 
overhang was observed to be forming on the northern side of the slope by a relatively 
large rock block underlain by an eroding clinker layer. In addition, another large rock 
block on the southern side of the slope was also observed to be underlain by a clinker

Rockfall Simulation Analysis 
As previously discussed, site reconnaissance was performed to visually identify 
and locate potential rockfall hazards, such as loose surface boulders and unstable rock 
outcroppings, within the project study area. The site reconnaissance also permitted us 
to evaluate probable rolling trajectories in relation to the existing topographic features 
encountered in the field. Following the site reconnaissance, selected information 
recorded in the field was input to the CRSP computer program to validate the field 
evaluation.  
The CRSP computer program is a widely accepted engineering tool used by 
experienced personnel to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall 
activity based on input parameters that are assigned on a site-specific basis. The input 
parameters for this project were based on the topographic survey map provided and 
observations and measurements collected in the field. The program provides a 
statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on the typical hillslope 
topographic profile and other input information, such as rock size, shape, and 
parameters used to describe ground surface irregularities. Information obtained from the 
CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity, bouncing height, kinetic 
energy, and roll-out distance. The output information is useful to assist in the 
site-specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes, such as rockfall impact 
barriers or other rockfall containment systems.  
The slope profiles used in our CRSP analyses were generated from the 
topographic survey map provided. In addition, the slope inclination of the upper slope 
area outside of the property boundaries (and therefore outside the limits of the 
topographic survey map) was estimated based on our field observations. Rockfall 
hazards generated from this upper slope area were also modeled to analyze the effects 
of falling rocks coming from the neighboring properties. Sections were cut in areas 
where potential rockfall sources were observed and where evidence of past rockfall 
activity was noted in the field.  
Based on our preliminary CRSP analysis, we estimate that under the existing site 
conditions, more than 90 percent of the simulated spherical boulders from 1 to 6 feet in 
diameter may impact the existing building. Therefore, we believe a rockfall mitigation 
system is necessary for the project site.

slope. Effective rockfall hazard mitigation systems will need to account for rocks falling 
into the project site from the overlying properties. Due to this, rockfall hazard mitigation 
systems such as anchored wire mesh, draped wire mesh, and draped ring nets would 
not be effective for the project. These systems could only be installed on the portion of 
the slope within the project limits and would not be able to capture rockfall hazards 
originating from beyond the property boundary. Therefore, we analyzed the 
performance of two rockfall hazard mitigation systems that would address this issue: a 
rockfall impact barrier (RIB) and a rockfall catchment ditch.  
Rockfall impact barriers are specially designed fences consisting of steel support 
beams linked by wire rope (cable) or steel wire ring nets, which are designed to flex and 
absorb rockfall impact energy. This system is used worldwide as an effective rockfall 
mitigation protection system where a specific and limited source of potential rockfall 
cannot be readily identified and stabilized or where the source area may encompass 
disturbance-sensitive land that makes other mitigation measures impractical to 
implement. Due to Hawaii’s corrosive environment, the steel components of the RIB 
should be galvanized (as a minimum). The useful life of the rockfall impact barrier is 
estimated to be about 10 to 15 years, depending on the quality of the barrier 
components, hardware, and workmanship employed during the impact barrier 
installation. Maintenance is also required to remove accumulated rockfall debris 
captured by the barrier on a regular basis or after significant rockfall events. 
Rockfall catchment ditches are designed to capture and retain cobbles and 
boulders at the base of the slope. The side slopes of the ditch are designed to absorb 
the impact forces of the fallen material and redirect the rocks away from the adjacent 
building. Because the construction of the rockfall catchment ditch only requires site 
grading at the base of the slope, the cost of installing the catchment ditch will be less 
than a RIB system. In addition, clearing accumulated rockfall material from the ditch will 
be easier and cheaper than clearing material captured by the RIB. The rockfall 
catchment ditch should have adequate drainage to prevent the accumulation of 
standing water within the ditch.  
Both rockfall hazard mitigation systems were analyzed using CRSP. The RIB 
was placed at Elevation +120 feet MSL due to the topography of the slope and for 
constructability considerations. Based on our analyses, a 3-meter-tall, 1,000kJ rockfall 
impact barrier would be required to retain at least 90 percent of the simulated spherical 
boulders falling within the property boundaries. To account for rocks falling from above 
the property boundary, the rockfall impact barrier would need to be a minimum of 
4 meters tall and rated for 2,000kJ. On the other hand, the rockfall catchment ditch was 
able to retain 90 percent or more of the simulated spherical boulders falling from within 
and from above the property boundary. Due to the effectiveness and cost efficiency of 
the rockfall catchment ditch over the RIB, we recommend installing the rockfall 
catchment ditch for the CSM Pearl City Warehouse – New project.

Slope Vegetation 
Based on our observations, the existing slope is generally densely vegetated, 
with small to medium-sized trees across most of the slope face, large trees 
above the property boundary, and dense California grass and haole koa trees at 
the base of the slope.  
The existing vegetation should be removed from the slope face and from the 
base of the slope. All existing woody stem shrubbery and trees on the slope face 
should be cut flush with the finished ground surface and poisoned to reduce the 
potential for undesired re-growth on the slope face. In addition, existing ground 
cover vegetation should be removed to completely expose the slope surface in 
preparation for slope scaling. 
The existing large trees near the top-of-slope (above the property boundary) 
should be cut and poisoned close to the existing ground surface. Subsurface tree 
trunk remnants should not be excavated (i.e., left in-place).
