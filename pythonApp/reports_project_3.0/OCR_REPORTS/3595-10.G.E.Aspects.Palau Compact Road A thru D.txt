1 Grading
1 1 High Moisture Soils
Because of the high moisture content of the in situ soils it may be necessary to adjust
the lift thickness to thinner lifts in order to compact the fill materials to the specified degree of
compaction The fill test section required in the specifications is intended to test the fill
construction methods proposed by the contractor to compact the high moisture soils The test
section should consider the use of different types of compactors including smaller compactors
along with varying lift thickness to evaluate the appropriate combination of compactors lift
thickness and moisture content of the soils that would produce the specified compaction
requirements
The fill test section should be constructed using soils with relatively high moisture
contents such as 40 to 80 moisture contents Field density tests and moisture contents
should be conducted on the fill test section to evaluate the fill compaction Special attention
should be given to observe the fill test section on a continuous basis by personnel responsible
for the Quality Control contractor and Quality Assurance Government of the fill embankment
construction The field density test results should be analyzed to evaluate the combination of
compaction equipment soil moisture content and lift thickness that would be needed to
achieve the specified compaction requirements

The contractor should be reminded to always exercise good construction practices
such as sealing the previously prepared ground surface and grading the prepared surfaces to
provide positive drainage at the end of each work day in order to reduce the potential for
ponding and saturation Due to the high moisture content of the soils pumping subgrade
conditions may be easily induced by the vibratory action of a compactor Therefore the
vibratory action of the compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid inducing a pumping
subgrade condition due to the high moisture soils when they are compacted as fill If pumping
subgrade conditions are encountered during the compaction of fill lifts or subgrades the
pumping subgrade may be stabilized as discussed in the Subgrade Stabilization section of
the GER
1 2 Shrinkage Cracks
The in situ soils are considered to be expansive if compacted dry of the optimum
moisture content Considering the high moisture content of the in situ soils expansion of the
soils is generally not likely a factor However if the subgrade soils for the pavement structure
swales approach slabs etc are allowed to sit for an extended period and allowed to dry out
the on site soils may be considered to be potentially expansive Therefore the subgrade soils
should be checked for the moisture content to ensure that the subgrade soils are wet of the
optimum moisture content Subgrade soils exhibiting shrinkage cracks due to drying should
be re prepared prior to final placement of the pavement section swales and slab concrete etc
1 3 Fill Compaction Requirements
Due to the tropical nature of the in situ soils exhibiting high moisture contents it must
be emphasized to the Quality Control and Quality Assurance personnel that the proctor tests
ASTM D 1557 should be performed from wet to dry instead of the normal dry to wet fashion
The maximum dry density and the optimum moisture content of the saprolite soils can be quite
different when the sample is prepared from wet to dry versus dry to wet This phenomenon is
common in tropical soils where there may be irreversible changes to the soil mineralogy when
the sample is prepared dry to wet
Compaction requirements for subgrade preparation and fill placement has been
reduced from the normal 90 to 85 relative compaction to account for the high moisture
content of the in situ soils Even with the reduced compaction criteria some moisture
reduction would still be necessary especially for materials generated from the deep
excavations where the in situ moisture contents may be in excess of 65 to 70 The lower
compaction requirement is based on past project experience in Palau and experience with
similar high moisture tropical soils Therefore the fill test section is an important field testing
exercise to evaluate the compaction criteria and equipment as discussed in the High Moisture
Soils section above Personnel with geotechnical engineering background from the Quality
Control staff and Quality Assurance staff should be present to observe the fill test section

1 4 Keyway Construction
For fill slopes with a vertical height greater than 6 meters a key should be constructed
at the toe of the fill slope to provide stability of the new fill embankment against sliding Refer
to Typical Keying and Benching Detail in GER and Sheet DR 18 of the Standard Details
Due to the high moisture content of the on site soils stiff supgrades exposed at the bottom of
the keyway may be proof rolled in lieu of scarifying and recompacting the keyway subgrade
to reduce the potential for inducing pumping subgrade conditions The prepared subgrades
should be firm and unyielding The Contracting Officer should document the conditions of the
keyway subgrades prior to the placement of embankment fill
For fill slopes greater than 10 meters in height the keyway subgrade soils should be
further evaluated to determine if the consistency of the exposed soils at the bottom of the
keyway is capable of supporting the high fill slope If needed the keyway should be extended
deeper to bear on stiffer materials
Keyway construction will not be necessary for fill slopes with the toe of the fill slope
bearing on the soft lagoonal deposits and mangrove swamps When constructing
embankments over soft soils and coastal mangrove areas details of construction should be
referred to the Embankment Over Soft Soils and Typical Sections in the construction
drawings for the various design packages
Benching of the fills into the existing slopes as shown on Sheet DR 18 of the Standard
Details should be consistently practiced by the contractor during fill placement The new fills
should be benched into stiff materials The benching requirement is especially important in the
saprolite soils since the soils are very slippery slick when wet and fill slopes placed on
improperly prepared surfaces will likely slip along this interface
1 5 Undocumented Fills
Undocumented fills were placed for the construction of the power pole access roads
and maintenance of the existing Babeldaob Road which intersect portions of the Palau
Compact Road alignments Car chassis were also reported in localized areas along the
Package A alignment These undocumented fills where encountered within the grading limits
of the proposed roadway alignment should be removed to expose firm materials and
recompacted to the requirements of general fill The keyway for fill slopes should not be
founded in the undocumented fills

1 7 Settlement Waiting Period
A settlement waiting period is required of all fill segments for embankments constructed
over stiff soils no soft soils The settlement waiting period after completion of the fill
placement is critical to reduce the effects of the anticipated settlements on the future
performance of the pavement structure and other improvements constructed on fills The
settlement waiting period may range from a minimum of 1 month to about 5 months depending
on the fill thickness as indicated on Sheet DG 3 of the Standard Details It is possible that the
settlement may have occurred during the fill placement and the readings from the settlement
markers and gauges may show no settlement
It should be noted that the number of settlement markers and or gauges recommended
is the minimum requirement Additional settlement markers and or gauges may be installed
to facilitate the settlement monitoring program and possibly reducing the settlement waiting
period The contractor should establish a means of informing the Contracting Officer of when
the settlement waiting period for each fill segment starts and stops
If the settlements and rates of settlement have not decreased to an acceptable rate as
indicated by the survey readings the settlement waiting period should be extended for that fill
segment Additional survey readings should be taken to evaluate the settlement rates The
acceptable rate will vary with each fill area and will depend on the amount of settlement
measured the estimated settlements and the duration the fill has been placed An accurate
log of the construction activities such as when the fills were placed and rate of fill placement
should be maintained by the contractor

Settlement gauges should be installed along the soft soil areas prior to the fill placement
operations Vibrating wire piezometers should be installed in the soft soil areas where fill
embankments will be constructed by stage construction Settlement markers should be
installed near the finished grades after completion of embankment construction or each stage

Awritten plan should be submitted by the contractor detailing the actual number and
location of the settlement gauges settlement markers and vibrating wire piezometers for the
soft soil areas The plan should be evaluated by the Contracting Officer prior to installation of
the instrumentation at the project site For complete soft soil removal the restriction on fill
rates and requirement for vibrating wire piezometer installation would generally not be
required However the requirements for settlement gauges and settlement markers would still
apply for the remove and replace alternative It is critical that the consistency of the bottom
of the excavation be evaluated by the Contracting Officer to determine if the subgrade material
is capable of supporting the fill slope to be constructed
It should be noted that the number of settlement markers gauges and piezometers
recommended are a minimum requirement Additional instrumentation may be installed to
facilitate the settlement monitoring program and possibly reduce the settlement waiting period
It should be emphasized that two readings minimum 24 hours apart for each
settlement gauge and vibrating wire piezometer be taken 10 days prior to any site filling to
establish a baseline Vibrating wire piezometers may take some time to come to equilibrium
with the installed environment due to disturbance caused by the installation process Readings
for the vibrating wire piezometers should be taken using dataloggers on a near continuous
basis and downloaded on a weekly basis Dataloggers are capable of storing data for up to
3 to 4 weeks or more However the data should be downloaded on a weekly basis plotted
and interpreted to evaluate the settlement patterns
2 3 Settlement Estimates
The planned embankments should be over filled by some amount and the finished
subgrades should be adjusted during construction to account for the anticipated fill ground
settlements It should be noted that a portion of the estimated soft ground settlements would
occur as the fill loads are applied during the earthwork operations The estimated settlements
are generally conservative or upper limit estimates of the settlement The actual settlement
measured from the settlement gauges will likely be less than those shown on the construction
drawings

2 4 Settlement Waiting Periods
It is difficult to accurately predict the exact time required for the filled ground to settle
therefore the actual settlement rates should be monitored In addition a settlement monitoring
program will be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the estimated settlements
during construction prior to construction of the pavement structure and other improvements on
the fills If the settlement of the embankment has not decreased to an acceptable rate as

segment Additional survey readings should be taken to evaluate the settlement rates In
addition an accurate log of the construction activities such as when the fills were placed and

construction in the soft soil areas
2 5 Other Alternatives
In order to reduce the settlement waiting period and subsequent long term settlements
a temporary surcharge fill program may be considered for embankment fills over the soft soil
areas Use of temporary surcharge fills to reduce the settlement waiting period should be left
up to the contractor as an option However it should be noted that potential slope instability
of the fill embankment may be induced as a result of improperly designed temporary surcharge
fills Therefore details of the temporary surcharge fill program should be submitted by the
contractor for review and evaluation by the Contracting Officer prior to implementation
2 6 Slope Stability of Fills
For fills greater than 6 meters over stiff ground conditions or fills greater than 5 meters
over soft soils special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation keyway
construction or soft soil stabilization fill placement and compaction and settlement
monitoring These items are critical to the proper construction of the fill embankment thus
reducing the potential for slope failure In addition the ground surface of the fill embankment
should be observed for signs of distress dips and cracks It should be noted that some cracks
may be observed in the fill embankment due to settlement of the fills and or underlying soft soil
deposits

4 Reinforced Soil Slopes
A field inspection checklist is attached to this document for the use of field inspection
personnel during construction of the reinforced soil slopes
4 1 Primary and Secondary Reinforcements
Primary and secondary geosynthetic reinforcements should consist of geogrid or
geotextile satisfying the requirements in Specification Section 02165 Reinforcing elements
should arrive at the project site securely bundled or packaged to avoid damage
Reinforcements must be protected from sunlight during storage The Contracting Officer
should verify that the material is properly identified and check the specified designation
AASHTO ASTM or agency specifications Material verification is especially important for
geotextiles and geogrids where many product types look similar but have different properties
Mesh reinforcement should be checked for gross area and length width and spacing
of transverse members It is important for the inspector to recognize that use of the wrong
material or its incorrect placement can result in significant slope distress At no time should
any construction equipment be in direct contact with the reinforcements because protective
coatings and reinforcements can be damaged
4 2 Erosion Control Mat Placement
Erosion control mat should be installed in accordance with the manufacturer s
instructions The erosion control mat should be placed and anchored on a smooth graded firm
surface Anchoring terminal ends of the erosion control mat may be accomplished through use
of key trenches The erosion control mat in the trenches should be anchored to the soil at a
maximum of 0 5 meters on center Erosion control mat should be anchored and overlapped
to ensure performance until vegetation is well established Pins should be as designated by
the manufacturer s instructions with a minimum recommended length of 300 mm and should
be spaced in accordance with the manufacturer s instructions The recommended maximum
depth of the pins should be limited to 1 25 meters

4 3 Review and Approval of Product Submittals
To reduce the cost of the reinforced soil slopes high strength geosynthetic
reinforcements were used in the design Our reinforced soil slope designs are based on
specific damage and creep reduction factors for the reinforcing elements Since different
manufacturers suppliers will claim different reduction factors for their specific products
representative samples should be retrieved and tested to confirm their stated specifications

strength requirements In addition the damage and creep reduction factors claimed by the
manufacturers will need to be validated
4 4 Keyway Construction
A keyway should be constructed at the toe of the reinforced fill slopes to provide stability
of the new fill embankment against sliding The bottom of the keyway should be embedded
a minimum depth of 0 5 meters below the lowest adjacent finished grades The base width of
the keyway should be a minimum of 3 meters or one fifth of the fill height whichever is greater
As indicated previously the keyway subgrade soils should be evaluated to determine if the
consistency of the exposed soils at the bottom of the keyway is capable of supporting the fill
slope If needed the keyway should be extended deeper to bear on stiffer or more competent
materials
5 Bridge Foundations
The seven bridges for this project are generally supported on a deep foundation
system consisting of precast concrete driven piles and cast in place concrete drilled
shafts The selection of the two types of deep foundations was based on the subsurface
conditions at each of the sites and the relative difficulty in installing the foundation
elements In general the capacities of the drilled shafts and driven piles are high in an
effort to reduce foundation costs However the high capacities of the drilled shafts and
driven piles make the installation process of these foundation elements critical The
Ngaraard Causeway Bridge is supported on a composite foundation system consisting of
shallow foundations and driven piles In addition the north abutment of Bridge No 2 is
also supported by a shallow footing

5 2 Driven Pile Foundations
Due to the lengths of the driven piles for Bridge Nos 4 and 5 we envision that one
splice will be required for each pile to connect the upper and lower pile segments to be
installed The splice detail used for this project is a non mechanical splice splice can
Therefore the final location of the splice can will be critical to the performance of the pile
foundation In general the final location of the splice can should be embedded a minimum
of about 15 meters below the below the bottom of footing elevation to provide the
necessary uplift load resistance
At the location of the Ngaraard Causeway Bridge the very dense volcanic breccia
underlying the causeway alignment is sloping down towards the center of the bay from the
north and south sides of the causeway In order to facilitate the pile driving operations into the
sloping rock formation and other potential obstructions predrilling at the proposed pile
locations for the north abutment structure will be required prior to pile installation The
predrilling should extend a minimum of 2 meters into the hard rock formation or to about
Elevation 12 meters MSL for the north abutment structure whichever is deeper In addition
the driven piles should be installed in one piece Non mechanical splices should not be
allowed for this bridge location
The required predrilling depth should be verified and or modified by Contracting Officer
during the test pile program depending on the conditions encountered and pile driving
equipment used Therefore observation of the predrilling operations for all the piles by the
Contracting Officer will be necessary to determine the top of the rock elevation at the various
pile locations We anticipate that the rock layer will vary in depth across the footing therefore
the predrilling depth will also vary accordingly Socketing the driven pile into the volcanic
breccia is a critical item since the driven pile may slip laterally along the relatively steep rock
surface during pile driving if not socketed
We wish to emphasize that the diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited to less
than about 800 mm to facilitate the pile driving operations to socket the pile into the dense
volcanic breccia The annular space between the piles and predrilled holes should be
backfilled with drain rock to reduce the potential for migration of soils into the predrilled holes
The upper 5 meters of the predrilled holes below the bottom of pile cap elevation should be
grouted with tremie concrete after pile installation to provide the lateral load capacity
It should be noted that over driving of the piles should be avoided Over driving of the
piles may damage the piles by cracking the piles Where hard driving of the piles are
encountered during the pile installation process the top of the piles should be observed and
checked for cracks during the footing construction process to verify that the top of the pile does
not exhibit distress and significant amount of cracking

performance of the hammer used
5 3 Test Pile Program
The equipment proposed by the contractor to install the driven piles for the three
causeway bridges should be evaluated for suitability to install the piles to the estimated
tip elevations Wave equation analyses should be performed by the Contracting Officer
to determine the driveability of the selected equipment for pile driving at each of the three
bridge locations requiring driven piles for support In addition the wave equation analyses
should be used to establish preliminary driving criteria for the test piles
The test pile program includes the performance of dynamic load tests during the test
pile driving operations and performance of static load tests on a selected test pile for each
bridge The Contracting Officer should observe the performance of the hammer during test
pile driving and the dynamic load tests The production pile driving criteria will be
determined based on the results of the dynamic load test report CAPWAP analyses and
refined wave equation analyses and the static load test report
We recognize that the static load tests on the piles will likely be difficult to perform
considering the relatively small area to work within at the edge of the causeway fills
Therefore special attention should be given to the methods and load test set up proposed
by the contractor to perform these static load tests The reference bench mark used to
determine the settlement of the test pile during the static load test should be checked
against a more stable bench mark since bench marks set on the causeway fills are likely
to settle
5 4 Drilled Shaft Foundations
Installation of the drilled shaft is a critical item of construction considering the high
Capacities of the drilled shafts used for this project In addition the U shaped footing was
used to minimize the number of drilled shafts needed to support each of the abutments
It should be emphasized that if a drilled shaft was to be rejected due to installation
problems or other reasons it will probably require two additional drilled shafts as a
minimum to replace the one defective drilled shaft It will also be very difficult to place the
two additional drilled shafts for the U shaped footings Therefore significant re design
of the foundation to evaluate the vertical and lateral load resistance of the foundation due
to the eccentric loading and stiffer foundation member will be required in the event of a
defective drilled shaft The contractor should be cautioned of the implications of a
defective drilled shaft not meeting the specifications For this reason trial shafts are not
incorporated into the foundation system due to the high potential for a shaft not meeting
the specifications

The Contracting Officer should observe the entire drilled shaft installation process
to confirm that the design intent and the capacities of the drilled shafts may be achieved
The ultimate capacities of the drilled shafts for each bridge supported by drilled shafts are
shown on the construction drawings The Contracting Officer representative responsible

Based on the soft soil conditions at the bridge locations temporary casing of the
drilled holes will likely be required during drilled shaft installation to reduce the potential for
caving in of the drilled holes Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary
casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 2 meter
head of concrete above the bottom of the casing or adequate concrete head to counter
hydrostatic pressures should be maintained during removal of the casing Consequently it is
critical that the concrete volume be monitored during placement into the drilled shaft and
compared to theoretical to determine if collapse of the drilled hole or necking of the drilled shaft
occurred during installation This is especially critical in drilled shafts with temporary casing
and or high groundwater levels
Some of the drilled shafts are designed to be embedded into the volcanic breccia rock
formation encountered at depth Therefore coring into the dense volcanic breccia rock
formation will be required In addition there is potential for encountering boulders at the
stream locations of the some of the bridges requiring the use of coring tools Special
precaution should be taken when boulders are encountered to avoid dislodging any boulders
into the drilled shaft hole during the drilling and shaft installation process
Due to the high structural capacities of the drilled shafts the sidewalls of the drilled
shaft holes will need to be rifled with grooves to provide the necessary contact and side shaft
resistance assumed in our analyses The grooves should be at least 50 mm deep by 75 mm
wide and should run spirally along the shaft circumference at a pitch of about 300 mm The
rifling procedure may be omitted in the hard rock formation and in the soft recent alluvial
deposits The rifling procedure will be required in the saprolite soils and weathered rock
formation The high groundwater levels may pose construction difficulties since proper
observation of the sides and bottoms of the drilled shaft may not be possible Therefore
continuous observation of the drilling procedures to confirm that the pitch is maintained and
that the grooves are made in the appropriate layers of subsurface materials will be required
The Contracting Officer should observe these items and make records of the drilled shaft
installation Refer to the Drilled Shaft Record in the Attachments
Rifling of the drilled shaft sidewalls is critical in the saprolite and weathered rock
materials because the drilling operations and temporary casing would normally smear the

sidewalls of the drilled hole If the grooves are not made in the materials the drilled shaft will
likely slip along the drilled hole and concrete contact surface resulting in low capacities This
is especially true because the soils are very slippery slick when wet Therefore the grooves
in the drilled shafts help increase the adhesion of the drilled shaft with the adjacent soils by
forcing the drilled shaft to fail in the soil not slip along the interface between concrete and soil
Due to the high groundwater table at the bridge locations drilling of the shafts will be
performed in wet conditions The water in the drilled holes will mix with the drill cuttings to form
a slurry Therefore the slurry should be checked for viscosity density and sand content prior
to placement of concrete to evaluate if the slurry is within an acceptable range of values
Otherwise the solids in the slurry may settle out during the concrete placement process
resulting in poor quality concrete and soil pockets in the drilled shaft The slurry should be
checked for viscosity density and sand content on every drilled shaft about 30 minutes prior
to concrete placement
Concrete placement should follow shortly after the drilled hole is completed with the
rifling grooves The drilled holes with the rifling grooves should not be allowed remain
underwater overnight prior to concrete placement In general the excavated drilled shaft holes
should not be allowed to remain unattended underwater for more than 8 hours This will allow
the groundwater to soften and expand the sidewalls of the drilled holes with rifling grooves
resulting in poor soil adhesion at the drilled shaft sidewalls Therefore rifling of the drilled hole
should not commence unless concrete placement is planned for that shaft on the same day
If the sidewalls are allowed to soften and or expand into the open excavation the drilled hole
should be over reamed by some amount to remove the soft soils in the sidewalls to expose stiff
sidewall materials The rifling procedure will also need to be performed again
In addition drilling for the shafts adjacent to the hole being drilled may not commence
until the drilled shaft has been completed by placement of concrete to the top of shaft elevation
and allowed to cure for about 24 hours Otherwise if caving in of the drilled hole occurs the
integrity of the adjacent drilled shaft with wet concrete may also be compromised
Recognizing the costs and time implications of a defective drilled shaft not meeting
the specifications it may be prudent for the contractor to give special attention to the
drilled shaft installation procedures especially the concrete placement procedures
Concrete placement is normally the area where defective drilled shafts are encountered
If a defective drilled shaft is suspected as a result of the concrete placement it may be
prudent to remove all the concrete placed and the rebar cage and clean the drilled hole
A new rebar cage should then be placed in the cleaned hole and place the concrete again
Otherwise if the concrete is hardened and the drilled shaft is rejected the remedial work
to correct the deficiency will likely be substantial and costly

5 9 Trial Shaft Program and Bi directional Axial Load Tests
The trial shaft program is intended to allow the contractor to demonstrate that their
procedures for installing the drilled shafts will produce the results consistent with the design
assumptions In addition all the proper procedures required to install the drilled shafts are to
be demonstrated by the contractor Therefore all personnel involved in the observation of the
production drilled shafts should observe the trial shaft program on a near continuous basis All
the requirements for the production drilled shafts as discussed in the previous section should
be performed by the contractor in the trial shaft installation The trial shaft program provides
the contractor with an opportunity to work out the details of the drilled shaft installation
The bi directional axial load test is an expensive load test program designed to yield
results consistent with the methods the contractor will use to install the drilled shafts In an
effort to reduce the cost of the trial shaft program the trial shaft may be converted to the load
test shaft if the trial shaft is installed properly consistent with the requirements Therefore if
the trial shaft was constructed with deficiencies the rebar cage along with the load cell should
be recovered from the defective trial shaft immediately when concrete is still wet Then
another drilled shaft should be constructed to conduct the bi directional axial load test
Care should be exercised to protect the embedment strain gages from being damaged
during the trial shaft installation and placement of concrete Therefore it is critical that a
qualified geotechnical engineer experienced in the installation of the instrumentation be
present at the site during the trial shaft program Otherwise the trial shaft program may not
yield the desired results

formation will be required The drilled shaft contractor will need to demonstrate that the
proposed drilling equipment and coring tools will be able to install the drilled shafts to the
recommended depths and dimensions in the trial shaft program
2 6 Bridge Foundation Construction Sequence
The approaches to the seven bridges planned for this project are generally in a fill
condition In addition the bridge locations are generally located in designated soft soil areas
with the exception of two bridges where the rock surface is near the existing or finished grades
For the two bridge abutments and center pier supported on a shallow foundation system
construction of the shallow footings may commence soon after placement of the fills without
a long settlement waiting period
For the bridges supported by a driven pile or drilled shaft foundation system the bridge
foundations may not commence until the fills for the approaches are placed and allowed to
settle during the settlement waiting period Therefore there will be some lag time between the
completion of the fills and construction of the deep foundations The lag time may be as long

6 Construction of Pavement Structure
Soil Compaction
The subgrade soils below the pavement structural section should be re prepared
at the time when the pavement structural section is to be constructed The subgrade soils
should be scarified to a depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 relative compaction
for cohesive soils For cohesionless soils the pavement subgrade should be scarified to
a depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 95 relative compaction
The compaction requirement for the pavement subgrade has been increased to
facilitate compaction of the aggregate subbase and or cement treated base above the
subgrade soils Otherwise it would be difficult to obtain the 95 relative compaction on
the aggregate subbase and or cement treated base placed on subgrades compacted to
only 85 relative compaction We envision that there would be some time between
completion of the fill areas and pavement construction such that the subgrade soils may
be aerated and or allowed to dry to the degree needed to achieve the 90 relative
compaction in the cohesive soils

6 3 Cement Treated Bases CTB
It should be noted that if CTB will be used for the pavement section the CTB should
be allowed to properly cure for a period of 3 to 4 weeks prior to placement of the asphalt
concrete pavement on the CTB This recommendation is to reduce the potential for
shrinkage cracking of the CTB during the curing process to reflect through the asphalt
concrete pavement above if the asphalt concrete is placed soon after the CTB is placed
In addition during the curing process the CTB should be protected from contamination
by the adjacent and underlying clayey soils prior to placement of the asphalt concrete
Due to the wet and clayey subgrades mixing of the CTB in place directly above the
clayey subgrade soils is not recommended and should be avoided Based on the clayey
subgrade soils the potential for contamination of the CTB would be high for the mixed in
place method of mixing the CTB The potential for contamination of the CTB would be
moderate to high for the traveling plant method of CTB mixing and placement If the
mixed in place method or the traveling plant method of mixing the CTB is elected by the
contractor it may be prudent to place an additional 50 mm of the aggregate above the
clayey subgrade soils for the mixing to reduce the potential for contamination of the CTB
by the underlying clayey subgrade soils The preferred method of CTB mixing is with the
use of the central plant method The central plant method results in the lowest potential
for contamination of the CTB with the underlying clayey subgrade soils
6 4 Cement Treated Subbases CTSB
Similar to the CTB it is preferred that the CTSB mixing be performed with the use
of a central plant It is possible to mix the CTSB in place or use the traveling plant method
provided that special precautions are taken to avoid contamination of the CTSB with the
underlying clayey subgrade soils during the mixing process which would reduce the
strength of the CTSB Special attention should be given to the submittals of the CTSB mix
design mixing and placement methods and curing procedures for the CTSB to evaluate
if the CTSB thickness and strength can be readily achieved with the proposed methods
