INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of Mr Allan L Parker we have conducted a field
observation of the standby injection wells for the Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant
Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal to Kuilima
Development Company dated March 11 1992
This report summarizes our findings from the site observation
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for disposal
of secondary treated effluent generated in the Turtle Bay resort development The
majority of the effluent treated at the plant is used for irrigation of the new golf course in
the Turtle Bay complex Therefore the wells are used for injection of treated effluent only
on a standby basis
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit issued for the
facility UIC Permit No UO 1532 it is required that periodic monitoring of the wells be
made by a professional engineer or geologist familiar with injection well operation This
observation is intended to monitor that the wells remain operational

GENERAL INFORMATION
The facility has been granted a permit to operate injection wells under the
Underground Injection Control program In this permit the facility is identified by Facility
ID No and the wells are identified as Well Nos A B and C

The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant is a privately owned treatment works
and is used for the treatment of sanitary wastes generated in the Turtle Bay resort
development The plant is generally of the aeration lagoon type and has a capacity of
about 1 3 million gallons per day At present the plant is handling about 150 000 gallons
per day of domestic wastewater
The plant is located mauka or southerly of Kamehameha Highway Effluent is
piped to the golf course area which is makai or northerly of the highway for disposal
The wells are used only on a standby basis The primary method of disposal is
by irrigation of the new golf course area Golf Course G 2 If necessary it is also possible
to divert effluent for the irrigation of the existing Turtle Bay Golf Course G 1 The current
irrigation needs of Golf Course G 2 average about 300 000 gallons per day This exceeds
that amount of treated effluent presently available
DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
Well Site Conditions
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of Golf
Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and the moat
around Punahoolapa Marsh See Photo No 1
The well heads are enclosed in below ground vaults approximately four 4 feet by
four 4 feet in plan dimension The vaults are covered with steel plate doors See Photo
Nos 2 and 3

Inside of the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with plates bolted to their
tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed See Photo No 3
These plates were removed for the inspection
Well Inspection
The general conditions of the wells were observed by sounding of the open well
and by bailing of water from the wells to allow visual observation of the amount of
turbidity The following are the results of those observations
According to Mr Parker this well has never been used for the injection of effluent
At 0845 hours on May 7 1992 the water level in the well was measured at a depth
of 4 8 feet below the top of the well head Sounding of the well with a weighted
tape measure indicated that the well was open to a depth of 42 4 feet below the
top of the well head
A bailer sample of water taken from a depth of about ten 10 feet below the top
of the well head showed that the water was relatively clear with some suspended
particles of what appear to be rust
A bailer sample of water taken from the bottom of the well showed that the water
was relatively clear with some suspended particles of what appear to be rust In
addition the bailer retrieved some fine to medium grained basalt sand which
appears to be part of the well s gravel pack

Injection System
The injection system is regulated by valves which can be used to isolate the wells
so that any one of the wells or any combination of wells may be used for injection At the
time of the site visit only the valve to Well C was open
The system is also equipped with a totalizing meter See Photo No 4 This meter
measures any flow to the well field rather than to individual wells Flows to individual wells
may be determined by records of which valves are open during injection periods
At the time of our site visit the totalizing meter had a cumulative reading of 410 000
gallons According to Mr Parker the original reading on the meter when the well field
was placed in service was 400 000 gallons
Records of previous meter readings are shown in Appendix A
Recommendations
To facilitate future inspections of the wells we recommend that inspection ports
be placed in the plates on the tops of the well heads This would eliminate the need to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2884 00 Page 7
remove the top plates The inspection ports should be a minimum of two 2 inches in
diameter i e two inch pipe to allow the use of a bailer inside the wells
To attempt to maintain the injection capacity of the wells we recommend that an
Operation and maintenance program be instituted There is a tendency for injection wells
to clog over time as a result of suspended solids in the effluent and biological action in
the wells In general to reduce the level of biological activity the use of the wells should
be rotated so that the wells are allowed to rest between extended periods of injection
To partially restore capacity lost by the introduction of suspended solids and gas
accumulation due to biological activity we recommend that the wells be redeveloped on
a periodic basis
