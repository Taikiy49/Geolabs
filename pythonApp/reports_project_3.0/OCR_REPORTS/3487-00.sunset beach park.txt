Summary Report
Field Percolation Testing
Sunset Beach Park
Sunset Beach Oahu Hawaii
This letter report presents the results of our field percolation testing performed
at the above referenced project The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Project Considerations
The project site is located at the Sunset Beach Park in the District of Koolauloa
on the island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to construct a support park for the
existing Sunset Beach Park Based on the information provided we understand that
a restroom facility is planned for the support park We also understand that wastewater
effluent from the restroom facility would be directed to a leach field which will be
located in an open space adjacent to the proposed parking areas
Our previous experience in the area suggests that the project site is underlain
by beach sand deposits over alluvial soil deposits and weathered basalt formation
Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our field exploration program was to characterize the
hydrogeologic conditions at the proposed project site and conduct percolation testing
of the subsurface soils Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee

Subsurface Conditions
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 to depths of approximately 5 to
26 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered medium dense sands to a depth of
about 9 feet below the existing ground surface Very stiff silty clays were encountered
below the medium dense sands in Boring and 5 The silty clays were
underlain by gravelly sands and weathered basalt to the maximum depth drilled of
approximately 26 feet below the ground surface

Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were conducted at the site in general accordance with State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State Standards to evaluate the permeability of
the subsurface materials for wastewater disposal In Boring Nos 2 and 4 sandy soils
were encountered from the ground surface to the maximum depths drilled of about 5
to 6 feet below the surface Approximately one inch of gravel No 3B Fine was
placed on the bottom of the 4 inch drilled holes Then twelve inches of water was
added to the bottom of the borings and the times to seep away were recorded
Boring Nos 3 and 5 were drilled to a depth of 11 feet below the ground
surface Approximately one inch of gravel No 3B Fine gravel was placed on the
bottom of the 6 inch drilled holes Due to the potential for caving in of the sandy soils
PVC pipes surrounded by gravels were placed in Boring Nos 3 and 5 Since clayey
soils were encountered at the testing depths of Boring Nos 3 and 5 water was added
into the drilled holes to presoak the clayey soils as required The clayey soils were
presoaked for at least 4 hours and allowed to swell for about 18 hours before
commencing the percolation tests
The subsurface percolation rates at the sites were tested by the falling head
method Water was added from the bottom of the borings up to approximately 6 inches
above the gravel layer before testing The changes in water level in the borings were
recorded with respect to time The results of these tests are presented on Plates 3 1
through 3 4
In Boring Nos 2 and 4 due to the granular nature of the subsoils encountered
percolation testing was performed immediately after preliminary test runs indicated that
the subsoils are permeable Based on the percolation test results a percolation rate
on the order of 0 25 minutes per inch was obtained for the sandy soil formation at a
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface in Boring Nos 2 and 4

Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based in
part upon information obtained from field data points Variations of conditions between
field data points may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will
be necessary to reevaluate the findings presented in this report
