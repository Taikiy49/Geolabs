Project Considerations 
Based on available information, the current Veterinary Treatment Facility (VTF) is 
in a deteriorating, wood-framed, World War II era building (Number 1864H) constructed 
in 1945 and fails to meet minimum standards established by the Public Health Command 
(PHC). The replacement facility is estimated at 12,300 square feet at an approximate cost 
of $18 million. The new facility includes a surgical suite that meets or exceeds American 
Veterinary Medicine Association (AVMA) standards, radiology, laboratory, pharmacy, and 
examination rooms. 
The replacement VTF site is located along Kuntz Avenue and Halehaka Street on 
the Hickam side of the base. The approximate location of project is shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. The project site had been developed previously, and several 
building pads occupy the site.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two 
shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as 
the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. The Koolau Volcanic 
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows. 
Following cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were 
deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor. The sea 
level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 100 feet 
above the present sea level. 
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Epoch, eruption of 
the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older 
alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, which consolidated to form volcanic tuff 
(locally called “mudrock”) layers. Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those 
mantles of tuff. Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years 
have added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration.

The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers and PQ coring tools. Our geologist classified the materials encountered in 
the borings by visual and textural examination in the field and observed the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate 3). 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate 4. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates 6.1 through 12. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. In general, when the blow counts exceed 
about 50 blows per 6 inches or when the sampler does not advance with repeated 
hammer blows, driving is discontinued and the blow counts are shown on the logs with 
the distance penetrated.  
Please note that the efficiencies of the automatic trip, safety hammers used for this 
project were measured in May 2015, and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown 
on the Logs of Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs 
have been adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate 6. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Laboratory Tests 
Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil and rock core samples obtained 
during the field exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their 
engineering properties. The tests performed are described below. 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate 13.

Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the near-surface soils and to aid in 
soil classification. The fines content of the samples tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) are indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates 14 and 15. 
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
materials. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and stress-
strain curves are presented on Plates 16 through 18. 
One Uniaxial Compressive Strength test (ASTM D7012, Method C) was performed 
on a rock core sample to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the underlying 
basalt formation. The test results are presented on Plate 19. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the quality of the materials tested for 
use as fills. The test results are presented on Plates 20 and 21. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557, Method C) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates 22 and 23. 
Infiltration Testing 
One infiltration test, designated as P-1, was conducted to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials. The test was performed in general 
accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland Department of 
the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These 
procedures are consistent with the other states’ procedures and generally may be 
considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration test was performed by augering a boring to a selected test 
depth of about 5.2 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, 
a 4-inch inside diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow 
infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the boring. A falling head 
infiltration test was performed to determine the average infiltration rate of the underlying 
subsurface materials. The test consists of four trials of filling the casing with about 
24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a 1-hour trial period or until the hole 
drains completely. The infiltration rates are then calculated based on the results of the 
fourth and last trial. The calculated infiltration rates are summarized in the following table, 
and the detailed test results are presented on Plate 27.

Limitations 
The geotechnical field and laboratory data submitted herein is based upon 
information obtained from the borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions between 
and beyond the exploration points may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until additional exploration and/or construction is 
underway. 
The boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been taped from 
existing visible features at the project site. The physical locations of the borings should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used.  
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data presented in the text herein were based on 
measurements recorded in the borings at the times shown on the graphic representations 
of the borings. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in 
tide, rainfall, temperature, and other factors.
