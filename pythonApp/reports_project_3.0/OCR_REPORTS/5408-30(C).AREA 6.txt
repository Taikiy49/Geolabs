Project Considerations
The Sea Country Subdivision Area 6 project site is located in the Lualualei Waianae
area on the western side of the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1 The Area 6 subdivision development consists of 127 new housing units
located on the valley floor adjacent to a hillside containing potential rockfall hazards As a
result of a hill slope assessment and rockfall hazard evaluation which was required by the
City and County of Honolulu rockfall protection improvements have been constructed at
the project site to reduce the potential for rockfall activity to affect the subdivision
development
In general the rockfall protection project consisted of the construction of
2 750 linear feet of rockfall impact barrier across the hill slope on the upslope side of the
existing concrete drainage ditch The approximate locations of the rockfall impact barrier
and wire cable ground anchors installed for the project are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 1 through 2 5
This report describes our field observations of the rockfall impact barrier
construction and provides a summary of the wire cable ground anchor installations In
addition the wire cable anchor proof test data which was performed by the contractor is
presented in Appendix A
Rockfall Protection Construction
We observed construction of the rockfall protection improvements on a part time
basis between May 24 2007 and March 28 2008 Hi Tech Rockfall Construction Inc
performed the rockfall impact barrier installation contract work Based on our observations
the completed 2 750 linear feet of rockfall impact barrier consists of approximately
225 linear feet of Type Barrier 74 foot ton capacity approximately 1 600 linear feet of
Type II Barrier 184 foot ton capacity and approximately 925 linear feet of Type III Barrier
295 foot ton capacity as indicated on the construction plans A Geolabs representative
observed the following major work tasks
Construction of the barrier post footings part time observation
Construction of the wire cable ground anchors part time observation
e Ground anchor proof testing part time observation
Barrier Post Footings
The Type post footings were excavated using a truck mounted rotary drill rig
equipped with an 18 inch diameter auger The Type II and Type Ill post footings were
excavated using a truck mounted rotary drill rig equipped with a 36 inch diameter auger

and a 36 inch diameter core barrel The core barrel was used on foundations where the
auger could not penetrate due to buried hard boulders The subsurface conditions

Island Ready Mix Concrete supplied the approved concrete mix for the post

Wire Cable Ground Anchors
The contract documents specified a minimum 6 inch diameter borehole having a
minimum borehole depth of 14 feet below the ground surface for the installation of the
1 0 inch diameter wire cable ground anchors The contractor was unable to provide the
specified borehole due to their drilling equipment limitations Therefore an exception was
granted allowing the contractor to install the wire cable anchors in a 4 inch diameter hole
having a minimum depth of 15 feet below the ground surface The exception was granted
provided that the installed anchors meet the criteria for acceptable load capacity
performance as verified by the required anchor proof testing Therefore each upslope and
lateral anchor installed consists of a 1 0 inch diameter galvanized wire cable with a tendon
length of 16 feet Based on our observations the 1 0 inch diameter wire cable anchors
were typically completed grouted to a depth of about 15 feet below the ground surface
within the 16 foot deep borehole
During the beginning of the anchor proof testing effort approximately 30 percent of
the tested anchor installations did not meet the criteria for acceptable load capacity
performance Therefore at the contractor s request we granted approval for installation of
supplemental 0 75 inch diameter wire cable anchor s alongside the failed primary
1 0 inch diameter anchor to act as one unit The purpose for the supplemental anchor s is
to augment the load capacity of the primary anchor such that the grouped anchors meet
the criteria for acceptable anchor load performance The supplemental anchor tendons are
10 feet in length Based on our observations the 0 75 inch diameter supplemental wire
cable anchors were typically completed grouted to a depth of about 9 feet below the
ground surface within a 10 foot deep borehole
The wire cable anchors were installed in boreholes drilled using an air track drill
using a 4 inch diameter drill bit The anchors were grouted with Edoco 621 Multipurpose
Grout

Anchor Proof Testing
The contractor performed the anchor proof testing work which consisted of testing
100 percent of the lateral anchors and at least 50 percent of the upslope restraining
anchors in general accordance with the contract documents The testing was performed
under the observation of our engineer geologist with one exception The exception is that
the contractor tested 27 anchors without our on site observation over a period of
three consecutive days As a result to verify the contractors test results additional testing
of a portion of the 27 anchors was performed under our observation
The anchors that failed to meet the criteria for acceptable anchor performance
during proof testing were replaced or supplemented with additional anchor installations at
the contractor s expense The replacement and supplemental anchors were retested to
confirm acceptable anchor performance The records of the anchor proof testing are
presented in Appendix A

Barrier Maintenance Considerations
Rockfall impact barriers require routine non technical visual inspection to ensure
the desired performance of the system In addition periodic maintenance work to repair or
replace broken or worn barrier system components following large rock impacts may be
necessary The routine visual inspection should be performed about once per year
minimum or following known rockfall impact s The visual inspection serves to identify
potential rock debris accumulations that may require removal and to inspect for possible
changes in the system condition or effects of environmental corrosion that may reduce the
barrier performance
Barrier maintenance such as the repair or replacement of worn broken
components i e the braking elements may be required following a larger rockfall impact
The frequency of barrier maintenance typically depends on the size and frequency of
rockfall events Rockfall impact barriers are designed to require minimal routine
maintenance Where a low incidence of rockfall activity is experienced more thorough
inspection of the system components by qualified persons should be conducted at
scheduled intervals of about 5 to 10 years In corrosive environments and where rockfall
activity is relatively moderate qualified persons should perform the thorough inspections at
intervals of about 5 years
Due to the often difficult access condition of some barrier installations owners
typically establish a contract with a local rockfall specialty contractor to perform the routine
inspections large volume rock debris removal and other system maintenance when
needed In some cases where the barrier is clearly visible from readily accessible
locations such as a roadway or the base of a low height slope the owner may elect to
perform the routine visual inspections themselves
Furthermore where barriers are located at readily accessible locations the owner
may perform the removal of rock debris using construction machinery However a rockfall
specialty contractor who maintains the proper equipment and trained personnel to conduct
work on difficult access slopes should be contracted to perform the removal of large
rockfall debris and to perform barrier system repair The rockfall mitigation specialty
contractor typically utilizes portable equipment which is transported onto the slope to
perform the potentially hazardous work Furthermore the rockfall specialty contractor
would typically employ temporary safety controls to protect down slope persons and
property during the performance of the work where needed
It is important to note the need for maintenance on rockfall impact barriers should
be minimal because the barriers are designed to withstand multiple rock impacts
Therefore it is not necessary to clean the barrier if there is only a light accumulation of
rocks in the bottom of the nets The ring nets could be temporarily detached to provide
access to the rocks from the front of the barrier

Finally we suggest that the tall wild grass and brush surrounding the rockfall impact
barrier be trimmed periodically to reduce the potential for damage resulting from possible
brush fires on the hillside Fires have occurred in the past at the site although mainly
during the dry summer months High temperatures from a brush fire adjacent to the barrier
could affect the PVC coating on the wire mesh panels resulting in melting and a loss of
coating for the wire mesh Other steel components of the rockfall barrier may be adversely
affected by fire depending on the temperature

good engineering practice The degree of rockfall protection also may be diminished by
failure to use manufacturer approved replacement parts or by environmental corrosion of
the various components
