INTRODUCTION

for the proposed Manana Community Park and Youth Center project located in the Pearl City
area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site preparation and
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
GEOLABS INC
W O 4321 00 Page 5
eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their original shield
outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas
of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of
the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering
process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general saprolite is
composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks
The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 to depths ranging from about 11 to 21 5 feet

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff
clayey soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted in situ stiff soils or engineered fill materials may be used to support the
proposed new buildings
GEOLABS INC
W O 4321 00 Page 7
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit slight to low
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To reduce
the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from the swelling
of the near surface soils we recommend that the on site clayey soils be capped with a
minimum of 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below all slab
areas In general the subgrade soils should be scarified moisture conditioned and
recompacted to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement
of fill material The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior

recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations site preparation and pavements

Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new
construction An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted stiff clayey soils
or engineered fills needed to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm
and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or
loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation may be
backfilled with on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or
the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent materials For
footings bearing on new compacted fills the bottom of the footing excavations should also
be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete
GEOLABS INC
W O 4321 00 Page 8
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the in situ stiff soils or engineered fills
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil
around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations
located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face Footings
located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to avoid
surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should be designed to resist
the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils or engineered fills as recommended herein to
be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of 0 5 inches
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of Geolabs
prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with slight to low expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 6 inches
of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below the slab cushion The
capping fill should also extend beyond the new slab on grade footprint a minimum of
24 inches
The non expansive select granular capping fill should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below the slabs to serve as a
capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce the potential for
future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture
vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the No 3B Fine gravel
or BTB cushion layer Two inches of moist sand or BTB may be considered above the
plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since
they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommend that interior walls be
designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible ground
movements

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately
around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated
drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site clayey silt or
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches
of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls

foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings to depths ranging from about 11 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
