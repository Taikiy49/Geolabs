SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering consultation 
performed for the Retaining Wall Distresses at Krauss Street and Tantalus Drive in the 
Papakolea area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This 
report 
summarizes 
our 
findings 
and 
geotechnical 
engineering 
recommendations derived from our field observations and engineering analyses. These 
recommendations are intended for the design of remedial repairs to the retaining wall 
only.  The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is near the intersection of Krauss Street and Tantalus Drive in 
the Papakolea area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Anecdotal information 
indicates that the area was developed in the 1940’s.  The wall is situated on the 
downslope side of Krauss Street and is constructed of stone masonry consisting of 
basalt stone.  The wall is approximately 10 to 12 feet in height and shows signs of 
considerable distress and movement of the exposed face of the wall. 
The signs of distress include a lateral separation from the sidewalk along Krauss 
Street and a bulging face with at least three lateral displacements towards the 
downslope side.  It is believed that the base of the wall may have shifted downslope.  
The distresses appear to have been ongoing for many years as revealed by mortar 
patches applied over some of the displacements. 
1.3

of Action” documents.  To accomplish this, we have provided a program consisting of 
the following tasks and work efforts:

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Existing Site Conditions 
As previously discussed, the project site is near the intersection of Krauss Street 
and Tantulus Drive in the Papakolea area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
Anecdotal information indicates that the area was developed in the 1940’s.  There are 
no record drawings available of the wall construction, nor for Krauss Street.  Local 
residents believe the wall was constructed as a community effort during the early 
development of the neighborhood. 
The wall is situated on the downslope side of Krauss Street and is constructed of 
stone masonry consisting of quarried basalt stone.  The wall is approximately 10 to 
12 feet in height and shows signs of considerable distress and movement of the 
exposed face of the wall.  The impacted portion of the wall is about 75 feet in length and 
extends from the easterly end of the wall to underneath the existing driveway bridge 
near the intersection. 
The signs of distress include a lateral separation from the sidewalk along Krauss 
Street and a bulging face with at least three lateral displacements towards the 
downslope side.  It is believed that the base of the wall may have shifted downslope. 
The distresses appear to have been ongoing for many years as revealed by mortar 
patches applied over some of the displacements.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field observations at the project site generally indicated that the existing wall 
is failing.  Alternate methods of repair and rehabilitation were considered.  Most of these 
methods have relatively significant constraints that limit their applicability to the project.  
Individual discussions for the various methods are presented in the following 
subsections. 
3.1 
Replacement 
This alternative would involve demolition of the existing wall and replacement 
with a new wall.  This method was not selected due to safety concerns, such as loss of 
support for Krauss Street and the adjacent sidewalk and the proximity of occupied 
residences. 
Demolition of the existing wall would require shoring of the excavation; however, 
the existing overhead lines and probably shallow depth to rock formation would greatly 
hinder installation of adequate shoring systems. 
3.2 
Retaining Wall with Reverse Foundation 
Consideration was given to the installation of a cast-in-place cantilever retaining 
wall with a reverse foundation (i.e., the foundation on the downslope side of the wall). 
However, this alternative would require excavation at the toe of the existing wall which 
would remove passive resistance that is supporting the wall.  Therefore, this method 
was also not selected. 
3.3 
Retaining Wall with Caissons 
For this alternative, a new retaining wall would be constructed in front of the 
failing wall. The new retaining wall would be supported by caissons (drilled shafts) to 
provide vertical and lateral support. 
Site constraints greatly reduce the feasibility of this alternative. There is 
insufficient space at the bottom of the wall for a caisson drill rig access. Placing the 
heavy drill rig at the top of the wall (at street level) would surcharge the wall and

3.4 
Shotcrete Facing with Drilled Anchors 
This alternative would require the installation of drilled anchors through the 
existing wall and installation of a reinforced shotcrete facing over the face of the failing 
wall. This appears to be the most feasible of all the alternatives evaluated. 
For estimating purposes, the anchors should extend a minimum of about 20 feet 
beyond the existing wall face surface, or 5 feet into competent basalt rock formation, 
whichever is less.  A schematic of this repair alternative is shown on Plate 2.  The 
anchors would be placed in pairs on about 5-foot centers and grouted in place.  A 
reinforced shotcrete facing would be installed over the face of the existing wall.  
Preliminary discussions with the structural engineer indicate that the reinforcing would 
most likely consist of a double layer of #4 bars on 10-inch centers both ways.  
Weepholes should be provided at about 5-foot centers to relieve hydrostatic 
pressure behind the walls.  This would require drilling weepholes through the existing 
wall.  It is our opinion that the drilling should be done after installation of the new facing 
to minimize the potential of destabilizing the existing wall. 
A Cost Estimate for this alternative is presented in Appendix A. 
3.5 
Future Investigation 
This consultation was based on visual observations only.  On that basis, certain 
assumptions have been made, such as an assumed distance to competent rock 
formation of about 10 feet or less.  At present, there is no information pertaining to the 
existing wall and actual subsurface conditions at the site. Therefore, we strongly 
recommend conducting a formal geotechnical engineering exploration in order to obtain 
sufficient information for the actual design of the work if the remedial work is to move 
forward.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field observations and data review.  Variations of 
conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become 
evident until a formal exploration is conducted or remedial construction is underway.  If 
variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented herein. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of AECOM, their client, 
Department of Hawaiian Home Lands and other project consultants for specific 
application to the Retaining Wall Distresses at Krauss Street and Tantalus Drive in 
accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices.  
No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of developing a plan of 
action for the proposed remedial work.  Therefore, this report may not contain sufficient 
data, or the proper information, for actual design nor to serve as the basis for 
preparation of construction cost estimates nor for bidding purposes.  A contractor 
wishing to bid on this project should retain a competent geotechnical engineer to assist 
in the interpretation of this report and/or in the performance of additional site specific 
exploration for bid estimating purposes. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are 
commonly encountered.  Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft 
deposits, hard layers, or cavities, may occur in localized areas and may require 
additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to 
attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is 
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs. 
This geotechnical engineering consultation was not intended to investigate the 
potential presence of hazardous materials existing at the site.  It should be noted that
