REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion cut
valleys deep into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
The MCBH site at Kaneohe Bay is located on Mokapu Peninsula which was built
by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series These vents are believed to be
among the oldest of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from vents
formed salients permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of
the island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age as a result of the lowering of the sea level
in response to continental glaciation sands from emerged coral reefs were blown inland
by the prevailing winds to form sand dunes along the northern portion of the airfield
GEOLABS HAWAII

An extensive deposit of unconsolidated calcareous marine sediments primarily
consisting of beach sand made up of water worn coral shells and organisms covers the
western tip of Mokapu Peninsula Man made fills consisting of marine sediments
dredged from the ocean floor were used to build up the area around the southern
and western shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula

ACTI Tl
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough due to a large areal
extent of the soil deposit to dissipate the build up of pore pressure the effective stress
internal strength of the soils is reduced Under sustained vibration the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil
shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements and if not confined would result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and
loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction would not
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2922 10 B Page 8
occur if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground level In deeper deposits the
greater overburden pressure is sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The
major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit include the
following
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine grained and uniform sands and silts with little
cohesion are more susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded
sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose fine sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Saturation The deposit has to be saturated in order to be susceptible to
liquefaction
4 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
Our field exploration conducted at Boring Nos 1 through 3 indicated that over
10 feet of loose silty sands are present at the project site It is our opinion that
liquefaction of these loose granular soils could occur when subjected to earthquake
induced vibrations generated by an earthquake of magnitude 6 0 with a peak horizontal
ground acceleration of 0 1g or stronger in the event of a moderate to large earthquake
there is potential for liquefaction of the loose granular soil resulting in ground
deformations at the site Based on our engineering analyses ground settlements on the
order of about 4 to 6 inches may occur at the subject site due to liquefaction of the loose
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2922 10 B Page 9
granular soils The estimated liquefaction related settlements would result in cracking of
the pavement structure
in general the proposed pavement structure may be constructed and performed
as intended under normal use conditions provided that the recommendations presented
herein are implemented during construction However liquefaction induced settlements
and damage to the proposed pavement structures may be expected in the event of a
moderate to large earthquake producing peak horizontal ground accelerations on the
order of 0 1g or greater The risk of liquefaction at the site may be reduced by the
incorporation of relatively costly site improvement techniques It should be noted that
many structures in the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay are constructed over similar type of
subsoils without mitigating the liquefaction potential

Subgrade Preparation
Because of the presence of a shallow groundwater table we recommend that the
subgrade be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton smooth drum roller a minimum of
8 passes to obtain a firm and unyielding subgrade To reduce the potential for subgrade
pumping heavy or rubber tired equipment should not be operated on the excavated
subgrade level In addition vibratory action of the compaction equipment should not be
used to reduce potential pumping of the subgrade If soft clayey spots are encountered
at the subgrade level during proof rolling the soft clayey materials should be removed to
a maximum depth of 18 inches A reinforcing fabric such as Mirafi 600X or equivalent
should be placed over the resulting over excavation and backfilled with free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The reinforcing
fabric should also be placed above the backfilled over excavation and should extend a
minimum of 3 feet beyond the edges of the over excavation Therefore we recommend
that unit prices for placement of the reinforcing fabric and free draining material be
obtained during construction bidding In addition we also recommend that a budget be
allocated for these items in the cost estimates Guide specifications for the reinforcing
fabric are presented on Plate C 1
Import fill material that may be required for the project construction should consist
of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches

Base Preparation
The new rigid pavement section planned for the helicopter landing pad will require
aggregate base course In general the aggregate base course should conform to the
general requirements of base course as specified in Section 6 of the Navy Design Manual
DM 21 3 August 1978 which has been modified for local conditions and presented on
Plate C 2 The requirements for the crushed basalt base course have been reduced from
a minimum CBR value of 100 to 80 to account for local conditions The aggregate base
course should be placed in lifts not exceeding 8 inches in thickness and compacted to not
less than 100 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91
Pavement Joints
Joints should be provided in rigid pavements for the following reasons
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused
by temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete

3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction
revision Joints Reinforcements and Mooring Eyes in Concrete Pavement should be

Joint sealants should be used to provide a seal to reduce potential infiltration of
water and to provide a compressible joint Joint sealing compounds used should be jet
fuel resistant and blast resistant and should follow the recommendations of Federal
Specification SS S 200 Type M Two Compound Cold Applied Sealing Compound
Detailed joint sealant design are contained in NFGS 02561

from the pavement areas and to reduce ponding of water adjacent to the pavement
areas
