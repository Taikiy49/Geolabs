PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
In general, the Kapiolani Residence project involves the construction of a new 
45-story high-rise building with about 485 residential units. We understand the project also 
will include a nine-level parking garage with 705 parking spaces on the makai side of the 
residential tower. The site is generally bounded by Kapiolani Boulevard to the north, Kona 
Street to the south, commercial buildings to the east, and a mixed-used building to the 
west. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
Based on the geotechnical engineering report referenced above, we recommended 
supporting the high-rise and parking structures on a deep foundation system consisting of 
18 and 24-inch diameter ACIP piles with allowable compressive load capacities of 500 and 
1,200 kips (250 and 600 tons) per pile, respectively. The ACIP piles would extend through 
the near-surface fills and lagoonal deposits and derive support primarily from skin friction 
and end-bearing within coral ledges and/or coralline detritus materials encountered below 
the lagoonal deposits with design pile lengths of about 65 and 110 feet for the 18 and 
24-inch diameter ACIP piles, respectively.  
OSTERBERG METHOD LOAD TEST 
In order to evaluate the adequacy of the recommended design length of the 24-inch 
diameter ACIP pile foundation, a bi-directional pile load test was conducted on a sacrificial 
ACIP pile, designated as TP-1, on January 9, 2017.  
The pile load test was conducted on a 24-inch diameter ACIP pile, designated as 
TP-1, with a length of approximately 112 feet below the existing ground surface. To better 
understand the load transfer mechanism at the pile-soil interface, the load test pile was 
instrumented with multiple levels of sister bar vibrating wire strain gages installed at various 
depths for the load test pile. The location of the Osterberg Method load test pile (TP-1) is 
shown on the ACIP Pile Identification Plan, Plate 2.2. 
A Manitowoc 4100W Crawler Crane equipped with a Bauer/Berminghammer ACIP 
BG46 drill, equipped with automatic monitoring equipment and a continuous flight 
hollow-stem auger was used to install the load test pile. A trailer-mounted hydraulic concrete 
pump with an average stroke volume of about 0.069 cubic yards per stroke was used to 
pump the grout into the pile system. The load test pile was loaded in general accordance 
with ASTM D1143, Quick Load Test Method. The maximum applied test load on test pile 
TP-1 was 2,513 kips. 
The bi-directional load test results were summarized in the following report by 
LOADTEST, a division of Fugro USA Land, Inc. 
 “Report on CFA Pile, Load Testing (Osterberg Method), TP-1 – 
1631 Kapiolani Blvd., Honolulu, HI (LT-1544-1),” dated January 16, 2017.

Based on our field observations and the results of the Osterberg Method load test, 
it is our opinion that the 24-inch diameter ACIP pile load test is capable of supporting the 
specified maximum test load without failure, meeting the project specification.  Based on 
the favorable load test results, we have recommended that the ACIP pile length be 
reduced from 110 feet to 105 feet for the production piles. 
STATIC PILE LOAD TEST 
In order to evaluate the adequacy of the recommended design length of the 18-inch 
diameter ACIP pile foundation, a static pile load test was conducted on a dedicated ACIP 
load test pile, designated as TP-2. To better understand the load transfer mechanism at 
the pile-soil interface, the load test pile was instrumented with vibrating wire embedment 
strain gages installed at various depths along the length of the load test pile. The 18-inch 
diameter load test pile was installed to a depth of about 78.4 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The location of the static load test pile (TP-2) is shown on the ACIP Pile 
Identification Plan, Plate 2.1. 
A Bauer BG-28 drill rig equipped with automatic monitoring equipment and a 
continuous flight hollow-stem auger was used to install the load test pile. A truck-mounted 
hydraulic concrete pump with an average stroke volume of about 0.152 cubic yards per 
stroke was used to pump the grout into the pile system. The load test pile was loaded in 
general accordance with ASTM D1143, Quick Load Test Method. The static pile load test 
results are summarized in the following report by Geolabs, Inc. 
“18-Inch Diameter ACIP Pile Load Test Report, Kapiolani Residence, 
1631 Kapiolani Boulevard, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii,” dated April 14, 2017 
Based on the static pile load test results, the 18-inch diameter load test pile was 
loaded to a maximum load of 1,006 kips. Based on our field observations and the results 
of the static pile load test, it is our opinion that the 18-inch diameter ACIP piles drilled to 
design tip elevation would be sufficient to achieve a minimum design compressive load 
capacity of 500 kips per pile with a factor of safety of at least 2.0.  
PRODUCTION PILE INSTALLATION 
The general contractor for the project and the ACIP pile installation subcontractor 
was Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company, Inc. (HDCC). The production ACIP pile 
installation program, consisting of installing 137 18-inch and 183 24-inch diameter ACIP 
piles, was conducted from December 30, 2016 through June 14, 2017 under the 
near-continuous (full-time) observations by our field engineers. A Manitowoc 4100W 
Crawler Crane equipped with a Bauer/Berminghammer ACIP BG46 drill and a continuous 
flight hollow-stem auger and a Bauer BG-28 drill rig equipped with hollow-stem auger were 
used to install the production piles. Automatic monitoring equipment was mounted on both 
drilling/pumping equipment with the intent of providing auger depth, grout volume, grout 
pressure, etc., on a near-continuous basis.

During the pile installation operation, logs were maintained for each ACIP pile,

encountered, it is our professional opinion that the production 18 and 24-inch diameter

load capacities of 500 kips per pile for the 18-inch diameter ACIP piles and 1,200 kips per 
pile for the 24-inch diameter ACIP piles, with a minimum safety factor of 2.0.
