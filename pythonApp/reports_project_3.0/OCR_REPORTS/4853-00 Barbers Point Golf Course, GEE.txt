2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from
Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as
the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south
these alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero
feet in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This
wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the
artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued
from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and

2 2 Site Description

3 1 Water Tank Foundations
Based on the existing topography and the subsurface conditions encountered at
the site we believe that concrete slab on grade with a perimeter ring footing may be used
to support the new water tank An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of the water tank foundations bearing on
6 inches of compacted base course over the recompacted in situ coralline materials or
new compacted fills placed to achieve the design finish grades This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces
Due to the potential for presence of cavities in the coral formation in the vicinity we
recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the foundation locations be
implemented during construction A representative from Geolabs should observe the
probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Probing
and grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of this
report
The aggregate base course below the footings should be compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction In addition the bottom of the footing excavations below
the aggregate base course layer should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction where possible to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior
to placement of the aggregate base course material If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation may be backfilled with on site
granular soils compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing
bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent materials Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted base course over
recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected
In general the perimeter ring footing should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finish grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches
or easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration and experience in the vicinity of the site cavities
and or voids may be present in the coral deposits encountered at the project site To
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities
below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations be conducted during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at the center of the
water tank slab and at 10 foot intervals along the perimeter wall foundation The probe
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below
the planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer
spacings to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The
probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected
at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about
6 to 9 inches
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time
between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

3 3 Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration and the design finish floor elevation for the project
we anticipate that the proposed new water tank may straddle across the new compacted
granular fills and the in situ coralline materials To reduce the potential for cracking of the
slabs due to differential settlement resulting from differing subgrade materials we
recommend that the bottom of the tank floor be underlain with at least 6 inches of
compacted base course for uniform bearing support The 6 inches of compacted base
course should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the limits of the tank structure
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for
the compacted base course The area adjacent to the water tank floor slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slab and
foundation
3 4 Site Preparation

water tank we anticipate that minor site grading work consisting of fills of up to about
3 feet thick will be required for the project construction

thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to
receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if
appropriate
After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or designated to receive fills should be
scarified to a minimum depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the

3 7 Drainage
Based on the generally permeable subsurface conditions encountered and the
relatively dry conditions at the project site we believe that a perimeter drain would not be
required around the new water tank structure for surface run off disposal However the
finished grades outside the water tank structure should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and slab and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slab should also be avoided These drainage
requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above foundation
recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled
against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the
potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slab and
foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the new water tank site were explored by drilling and
sampling one boring designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of about 35 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate location shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The boring was drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
A relatively undisturbed soil sample was obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the
appropriate sample depths
