REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3596 00 Page 6
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in
sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms
of supporting heavy structural loads

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site was explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 to depths ranging from about 92 to
102 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

lagoonal deposits to depths of about 30 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface The
loose lagoonal deposits were underlain by a hard coral ledge varying from about 5 to
18 feet in thickness Below the hard coral ledge loose to medium dense coralline
materials interbedded with dense cemented sand layers were generally encountered to

layers graded to sandstone in some of the borings Hard basalt rock formation was then
encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 102 feet below the
existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the time of our field exploration Due to the proximity of the project site to the
Ala Wai Canal it is likely that the water levels measured in the borings during our field
exploration were affected by tidal influences In addition the water levels at the project
site may be influenced by seasonal precipitation and storm surge conditions

Pile Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated heavy
structural loads we recommend that driven pile foundations be used to support the
proposed 26 story apartment building We believe that 16 inch octagonal precast
prestressed concrete piles may be used to support the proposed apartment building The
piles would derive support principally from end bearing on the hard basalt rock formation
underlying the site at greater depths as encountered in our borings drilled for the subject
project An allowable compressive load capacity of about 150 tons may be used for the
recommended pile foundation design The allowable compressive load capacity for the
piles is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3596 00 Page 10
such as wind or seismic forces Piles should be spaced a minimum of 4 feet center to
center to avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group action and to facilitate pile
driving
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site the anticipated
lengths of the piles would be approximately 85 to 95 feet below the existing ground
surface The above estimated pile lengths are for initial cost estimation purposes The
actual production pile lengths should be estimated after completion of a test pile program
Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained during
bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Due to the relatively long preliminary pile lengths of about 85 to 95 feet we
anticipate that splicing of two shorter pile sections would be required in order to facilitate
the handling and pile driving operations It is our opinion that the upper pile section
should consist of a minimum 45 foot section in order to provide the recommended uplift
load capacity Splice cans should be made available at the site during the test pile driving
program Splice cans may be fabricated with inch thick high grade steel and should be
at least 5 feet long Where piles will be subjected to high tensile forces consideration
should be given to mechanical splicing of the piles to take advantage of the bending
capacity and the full uplift load capacity of the pile
Predrilling
Our borings indicated that the project site is underlain by upper coral ledges and
lower cemented sand layers These hard layers range from about 2 to 18 feet in

high rise building it is our opinion that the recommended pile foundation should

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the driven pile
and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils Because of the
estimated length of the piles it is anticipated that the pile would be spliced
Therefore an uplift load capacity of 15 kips based on a 45 foot long pile section
upper section may be used in design of the pile foundation The uplift load
capacity already includes the weight of the pile In areas where high uplift load
capacity is required we recommend that mechanical splicing be considered to
connect the upper and lower pile sections for higher uplift load resistance Where
mechanical splicing is used an uplift load capacity of 50 kips may be used

Settlements of pile foundations will result primarily from the elastic compression of
the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the

between columns supported on piles not exceeding about one half that amount

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum energy
of 60 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control such that driving within the loose soft soil may be controlled at
a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which could
damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs Hawaii
should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is
capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity The contractor
should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs Hawaii prior to
construction for evaluation Geolabs Hawaii should review the submittal and
assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design purposes pile driving may be terminated when aggregated
blow counts are at least 120 blows per foot for the last foot or 12 blows per inch for
the last 3 inches This preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed after the
selection of the pile driving equipment and during the test pile driving to
accommodate variations of subsurface conditions in the field
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3596 00 Page 15
In general each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may
be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is
interrupted for more than four hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required
depth and or meeting the pile driving criteria During soft driving i e when the pile
tip is within soft loose soils the hammer stroke or energy should be carefully
controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop within the pile
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field by a
representative from our office during driving It should be noted that the pile
design was developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration data A
test pile program should be carried out to confirm our preliminary pile design and
to establish pile driving criteria The production pile driving criteria may be revised
based on the test pile program

Site Preparation
Based on the current design concept and existing topography we anticipate that
minimal site grading work will be required for the project construction The weight of any
new fills planned for the project will initiate ground settlement due to compression of the
loose soft subsoils Therefore site filling should be minimized to reduce the potential for
ground settlements If filling on the order of 2 to 3 feet is necessary the filling should be
initiated as soon as possible to allow the existing ground to adjust to the new fill loads
Site grading and earthwork operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office be present to
observe the site preparation to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered
during the excavation and scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock
conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration

the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 to depths ranging from about 92 to
102 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary tools as necessary
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Five 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test results
is provided on Plate B 2
Five sieve analyses ASTM D 422 tests were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on
Plate B 3
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR ASTM D 1883 tests were performed on bulk
samples collected for pavement design Results of the CBR tests are presented on
Plates B 4 and B 5
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
