INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Varona Village Infrastructure Improvements project at the
Ewa Villages Revitalization development on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of pavement structures site
grading and utility excavations only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 30 Page 5
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Agricultural land development and
reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present
form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results the existing pavement
structures are underlain by very stiff to hard silty and clayey soils over coral sand and
gravel or coral formation at the relatively shallow depths Based on our experience with
projects in the Ewa area and with projects having similar soil conditions there is a
strong tendency for water to accumulate at the interface between the granular subbase
material and the underlying clayey pavement subgrade soils Unless the pavement
subgrade soils are properly prepared and adequate drainage is provided the
accumulated water will likely soften the silty and clayey subgrade soils and cause
premature failure of the new pavement The potential sources of water infiltration
generally include landscape irrigation and seepage To provide adequate subsurface
drainage in order to reduce the potential for saturation of the clayey pavement
subgrade we recommend that a longitudinal edge drain be provided at the edge of the
new pavement structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 30 Page 7
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface silty and clayey
soils encountered at the subgrade level exhibit a slight swelling potential when
subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore design of the proposed improvements
should take into consideration the slightly expansive soils at the subgrade level
Special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements for
pavement structures and other improvements
We anticipate that site grading will consist of removal of the existing pavement
structures preparation of subgrades placement and compaction of the subbase and
base courses and construction of sidewalks In addition excavations on the order of
up to about 14 feet relative to the existing ground surface and subsequent backfilling
are anticipated to install the new utilities

Longitudinal Edge Drain
As previously indicated based on our experience with projects in the Ewa area
there is a strong tendency for water to accumulate at the interface between the granular
subbase material and the underlying clayey pavement subgrade soils Therefore
unless the pavement subgrade is properly prepared and adequate drainage is provided
the accumulated water at the interface will likely saturate and soften the silty and clayey
subgrade soils and cause premature failure of the new pavement structure
To collect and divert free water from the pavement section we recommend that
an edge drain system with outlet pipes on the sides of the roads and streets be
provided The outlets must discharge into appropriate drainage outlet structures such
as drainage ditches culverts or catch basins The typical edge drain would consist of
a 4 inch PVC perforated pipe with the perforations facing down surrounded by a
permeable backfill material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation A typical edge drain section is presented on Plate 3 The bottom of the
edge drain trench should be at least 6 inches below the bottom of the subbase layer
A filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be placed around the
permeable backfill material to reduce the potential for migration of fines into the
drainage material Care should be exercised when placing the permeable base and or
construction of the surface course to avoid damaging the edge drain collector pipe As
an alternative a geocomposite fin drain may be used in lieu of a PVC collector pipe as
shown on Plate 3

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed The existing pavement sections should be
removed and the asphalt concrete should be disposed of off site All vegetation
debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of properly off site to reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 30 Page 12
All subgrades and fill areas should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
compaction requirement for pavement subgrades should be increased to at least
95 percent relative compaction The pavement subgrade should be kept moist
until covered by the subbase course Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing and demolition
operations should be over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting
excavations should be backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the over
excavated subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Fills and Backfills
Based on the proposed construction we envision that the on site silty and clayey
soils and granular materials generated from the excavation may be reused as
a source of general fill and backfill material General fill and backfill materials
should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content placed in 8 inch loose level lifts and compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction

All permanent fill slopes should have slope inclinations of two horizontal to
one vertical 2H 1V or flatter All fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling
past the design fill slope and then cutting back Surface water run off should be
diverted away from the top of slopes and slope planting should be utilized to
reduce the potential for erosion
If imported fill materials are required as general fill materials the imported fill
and or backfill material should consist of well graded granular materials of less

Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain between 10 and 30 percent
passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs
Hawaii for conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for its intended use Imported materials should be brought to above the
optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch thick loose lifts and compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction The final lift of fill below the
pavement section bottom of subbase course should be compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction
Aggregate base course and subbase course materials should meet the material

Box Culvert and Manhole Structures
We understand that a box culvert and manhole structures are planned for the
subject project In general we believe that the bottom of the box culvert and manhole
structures will likely bear on the medium dense to dense coral sands and gravels or
dense coral formation as encountered in our borings If precast concrete construction
is planned for the box culvert and manhole structures we recommend that the box
culvert and manhole structures be supported on a 6 inch layer of cushion fill consisting
of free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
for more uniform support for the structures If cast in place concrete construction is
planned the 6 inch cushion layer below the box culvert and manhole structures may
be eliminated and the concrete for the structures may be cast directly on the
recompacted coral sand and gravel or coral formation As a minimum the subgrade
for the box culvert and manhole structures should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of the cushion layer and or concrete
For the foundation design of the box culvert and manhole structures an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design
Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are estimated for these structures
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground box culvert and
manhole structures will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and
the compactive effort on the backfill material around the structures In general we

Utility Trench
It is anticipated that the utilities for the roadway construction will consist primarily
of water drainage and sewer We anticipate that the drainage and sewer lines will be
installed at depths of about 10 to 14 feet below the road finish grade and the water
lines will be installed at depths of about 3 to 5 feet below the road finish grade
in general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and
backfilling of the trench for the proposed utilities The method and equipment to be
used for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits
and safety considerations The excavation should comply with all applicable local
state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should
be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should conform to
the appropriate health and safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility line trenching at the site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 30 Page 16
The near surface clayey and silty soils encountered at the site are generally stiff to
hard However deeper excavation may encounter medium dense to dense coral sand
and gravel or dense coral formation as described in the logs of borings
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free
draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material
be used to reduce the potential of formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site clayey and
silty soils as backfill around utility pipes is not recommended
We recommend that a minimum cover of 3 feet be provided between the top of
utility pipes and the finished grade to facilitate construction of the pavement structure
and compaction of the trench backfill The upper portion of the trench backfill from the
level 1 foot above the pipes to 2 feet below the finish subgrade should consist of the
approved on site soils or select granular material The backfill material should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8
inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of
the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials at
the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to compact the
backfill material

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings to depths of about 10 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts need
ed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was formulated to aid in evaluating pertinent
engineering properties of the soil and materials sampled
Moisture content ASTM Test Method D 2216 is used as an aid to soil classifica
tion and to aid in evaluating certain properties such as relative saturation The
moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample
and unit weight are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limit tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 were performed to
evaluate the plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the
material is used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The
results of these tests are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Logs of
Borings and are summarized on Plate B 2
Four California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM Test Method D 1883 were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the soil strength for
preliminary pavement design The test results are summarized on Plate B 1 Graphic
presentation of the CBR tests are presented on Plates B 3 through B 6
