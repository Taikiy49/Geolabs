PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
The project site is at the northeast corner of Auahi and Kamakee Streets on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. In general, the Block K, Anaha Tower project involves 
construction of a new residential high-rise building with a parking structure. The general 
project vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the geotechnical engineering report referenced above for the project, we 
recommended supporting the new condominium and parking structure on a deep 
foundation system consisting of 18 and 24-inch ACIP piles with allowable compressive 
load capacities of 600 and 1,200 kips, respectively, per pile. Most of the ACIP piles would 
extend through the soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits (near the surface) and derive 
support primarily from skin friction and end-bearing within coral ledges and/or coralline 
detritus materials encountered below the lagoonal deposit. The typical ACIP pile lengths 
would be about 80 and 100 feet for the 600 and 1,200-kip capacity piles, respectively. The 
general contractor for the project was Albert C. Kobayashi, Inc. (ACK), and the ACIP pile 
installation subcontractor was Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company, Inc. (HDCC). 
STATIC PILE LOAD TEST 
As part of the project specifications, one sacrificial 24-inch diameter load test pile 
was installed at the approximate location indicated on the ACIP Pile Identification Plan, 
Plate 2. The load test pile was installed by HDCC on December 19, 2014 and contained 
an Osterberg Load Cell and embedment strain gage instrumentation.  
A Manitowoc 12000 series crane equipped with automatic monitoring equipment 
and a crane-mounted hollow stem auger powered by an APE 75C Hydraulic pump were 
utilized to install the ACIP test pile. A hydraulic concrete pump with an average stroke 
volume of about 0.099 cubic yard per stroke was used to pump the grout into the pile 
system. The 24-inch diameter load test pile was extended to a depth of about 102.3 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  
After the load test pile was installed and cured for about ten days, Loadtest USA 
performed the load test on December 29, 2014. The 24-inch diameter load test pile was 
loaded in general accordance with ASTM D1143, Quick Load Test Method to a maximum 
sustained bi-directional load of approximately 4,200 kips. Complete details of the load test 
program are presented in the Report on CFA Pile Load Testing (Osterberg Method) dated 
January 7, 2015 submitted by Loadtest USA. 
Based on the results of the load test conducted, we recommend revising the ACIP 
pile lengths for the project as shown in the following table to support the allowable 
compressive loads (Design Capacity). Changes to the ACIP Pile Schedule are highlighted 
in bold in the table.

PRODUCTION PILE INSTALLATION 
The production ACIP pile installation program, consisting of installing 322 ACIP 
piles, was conducted from January 9, 2015 through April 13, 2015 under the 
near-continuous (full-time) observations by a field engineer from our office. HDCC utilized 
a Manitowoc 12000 series crane with a crane-mounted hollow stem auger powered by an 
APE75C Hydraulic pump to install the production piles. Automatic monitoring equipment 
was mounted on the drilling/pumping equipment with the intent of providing auger depth, 
grout flow rate, grout pressure, etc., on a near-continuous basis.  
During the pile installation operation, logs were maintained for each ACIP pile, 
which included information pertaining to pile number, date installed, depth of pile, grout 
volume used, grout return depth, etc. The locations of the production ACIP piles installed 
are shown on the ACIP Pile Identification Plan, Plate 2. The production ACIP pile 
installation summary is presented by pile number on Plates 3.1 through 3.14 of this letter 
report. 
As with most foundation installation projects, some problems did arise during 
construction.  The associated problems and the corresponding resolutions are presented 
in the “Remarks” column of the ACIP Pile Installation Summary. 
Based on our observation of the equipment performance and subsurface conditions 
encountered, it is our professional opinion that the production ACIP piles have been 
installed to sufficient depths and/or resistance to provide the allowable design load 
capacities.
