SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on a review of available information and our field reconnaissance, we 
believe that the proposed project is generally feasible from a geotechnical engineering 
point-of-view. Our field reconnaissance generally encountered residual soils at the ground 
surface consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey silt and silty clay. Based on available soil and 
boring data in the area, we anticipate the project site to be underlain by residual soils that 
grade with depth to saprolite and weathered basalt formation.  
We envision that the proposed development for the project will generally consist of 
one to two-story structures. Based on the anticipated structural loads and subsurface 
conditions at the project site, we believe a shallow foundation system consisting of spread 
and/or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed structures.   
Laboratory results from surface samples obtained at the project site indicate the 
on-site surface soils have a low expansion potential when subjected to moisture 
fluctuations. However, we recommend placing a minimum 12-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill material below the concrete slab-on-grade to reduce 
moisture changes in the slab subgrade soils. Placement of the non-expansive select 
granular fill layer would further reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded 
slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the on-site soils due to changes in 
the moisture content. 
Additional field exploration and geotechnical engineering analysis should be 
conducted for the final design of the site grading and structure foundations. We 
recommend exploring the subsurface conditions by conducting a field exploration program 
consisting of drilling and sampling borings to confirm whether the geotechnical 
recommendations presented herein are adequate. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Following the cessation of 
this main shield building phase, there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion 
of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional Kōloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent 
deposition of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands and today 
encompasses approximately 555 square miles with a maximum elevation of about 
5,170 feet above Mean Sea Level at Mount Waialeale near the center of the island. It is 
believed that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene 
Epoch), basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly 
circular island volcano from the ocean floor, which is estimated to have been about 
20,000 feet deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this main shield 
building volcanic phase came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was 
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the 
eruption of the Kōloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This 
post-erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic 
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Series. Because of the long 
period of erosion that occurred between the eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series 
and the Kōloa Volcanic Series, the deposits of the Kōloa Volcanic Series typically filled 
the erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that time. Consequently, the 
basaltic rocks of the Kōloa Volcanic Series are commonly found to overlie thick residual 
soil and alluvial deposits. 
The dominant geologic processes of weathering, landform erosion, and sediment 
re-deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs. Combined with 
the erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea-level fluctuations, which

The project site is situated at the southern portion of the island. Based on a

Groundwater seepage and high-level perched groundwater are commonly 
encountered in the subsurface materials. These groundwater conditions contribute to 
the formation of spring discharge at lower elevations and near-continual stream 
discharge, which occurs in the larger drainage ravines.

soft, weathered basic igneous rock. The slope range of this soil is 0 to 6 percent. 
Permeability is moderate, runoff is slow, and the erosion hazard is slight. This soil has a

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is generally between Hanapepe River and Wahiawa Stream in 
the Ele'ele area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. Kaumualii Highway borders the western 
portion of the site, and Halewili Road is located to the south. Kapa Reservoir is located 
just north of the site and consists of an earth dam roughly 12 to 15 feet in height. The 
proposed development for the project is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The project site is within a portion of an active coffee plantation consisting of 
rows of coffee trees roughly spaced at 12 feet on center. Farm roads traverse through 
the rows of coffee trees at various locations throughout the site. At the time of our field 
reconnaissance, these farm roads consisted of residual soil in a firm/hard condition with 
minimal signs of erosion.  Some cobbles and boulders were visible on the exposed

A concrete drainage ditch, roughly 1.5 to 2 feet deep, generally traverses the site

was being discharged into the ditch near the eastern portion of the site and flowing 
outside of the project towards Wahiawa Stream. 
In general, the topography of the site gently slopes towards the southwest with 
some isolated areas sloping towards the south and southeast. A topographic map of the 
project site was not available at the time this report was prepared; however, ground 
surface elevations are expected to range from about 160 to 270 feet Mean Sea Level

Subsurface Conditions 
It should be noted that a field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling of 
borings was not conducted for this preliminary study. Instead, research and review of 
available soil and boring data in the vicinity of the project site was conducted in order to 
evaluate the subsurface conditions at the project site. In addition, samples of the 
surface soils were obtained from the project site, designated as Bulk Samples 1 through 
3, and laboratory testing was performed to aid in classifying the materials and 
evaluating their engineering properties. The approximate sample locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples are presented in Appendix A. 
Our field reconnaissance generally encountered residual soils at the ground 
surface consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey silt and silty clay. Based on available soil 
and boring data in the area, we anticipate the project site to be underlain by residual 
soils that grade with depth to saprolite and weathered basalt formation.  
Note that rock outcrops were observed roughly 1,500 feet east of the project site 
at shallow depths along the Wahiawa Stream bank. Based on this observation, hard 
basalt formation could be encountered at relatively shallow depths below the existing 
ground surface. However, this can only be confirmed by conducting a more detailed 
geotechnical exploration involving drilling of boreholes.  
We did not anticipate groundwater at shallow depths below the existing ground 
surface. However, it should be noted that groundwater may occur or fluctuate 
depending on seasonal precipitation and other factors. In addition, subterranean 
seepage may be encountered during construction due to high rainfall in the area, 
sloping terrain, and relict structure in saprolite and highly weathered basalt.

2.4.1 Earthquake and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+).

3.1 
Building Foundation  
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, 
we recommend using shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous 
footings bearing on the in-situ soils for support of the proposed buildings. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used to design 
shallow foundations bearing on the in-situ soils. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused 
by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the 
lowest adjacent finished grades. Foundations next to utility trenches or easements 
should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on a review of available information and our field reconnaissance, the 
project site is anticipated to be underlain by residual soils consisting of stiff silty 
clay/clayey silt. Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface on-site soils have a 
low expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To provide a protective 
layer to reduce the potential for changes in the moisture content of the slab subgrade 
soils, we recommend capping the slab subgrade with a 12-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill. The non-expansive select granular fill should be 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Prior to placing the non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend scarifying 
the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioning the soils to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacting to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The underlying subgrade soils and select granular fill 
should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete. Where 
shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend 
preparing the soils again as recommended. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the 
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require 
over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with engineered fill. 
For interior building slabs, we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of 
cushion fill consisting of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the

3.3 
Site Preparation 
Based on the existing topography, we anticipate minor grading at the project site. 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared 
and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered 
during clearing and grubbing should be over-excavated to expose firm material. The 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or 
organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.  
After clearing and grubbing, areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers

Compaction should be accomplished with sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the on-site clayey soils. 
3.4 
Excavation 
It is anticipated that the soils may be excavated with normal heavy excavation 
equipment. However, areas of hard excavation may be encountered where hard basalt 
rock formation occurs at shallow depths or in deep utility trenches and require chipping 
and/or the use of hoerams. If excavation into the underlying rock formation is required 
for site grading, the work will generate boulders and over-sized material. A small 
amount of boulders and over-sized material may be incorporated into deeper fill areas. 
Otherwise, crushing the boulders for general fill and roadway base is recommended, or 
hauling boulders off-site may be necessary.  
3.5 
Pavement Design 
We envision flexible pavements will be used for the proposed subdivision. The 
design traffic for the proposed roadways was not available at the time this report was 
prepared. However, we anticipate that vehicle loading for the subdivision driveways and 
parking areas will primarily consist of passenger vehicles and light pick-up trucks. For 
the main entry drive and service roads, we anticipate that vehicle loading will consist of 
heavier 3-axle trucks such as fire trucks, buses, delivery trucks, and garbage trucks. 
We have assumed the pavement subgrade soils will be similar to the 
near-surface soils observed during our field reconnaissance. On this basis, we 
recommend utilizing the following preliminary pavement designs for the project:
