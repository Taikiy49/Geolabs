This report presents the results of our soils investigation
conducted for the proposed addition to the commissary at Hickam Air
Force Base Oahu Hawaii The approximate location of the site and
its general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
It is proposed to construct an approximately ft
single story addition to the north of the existing commissary
consisting of checkout area queuing area cashier office and
equipment rooms and entry exit vestibules Also included is a
ft single story warehouse addition to the south of the
commissary for non perishable food storage Included in the warehouse
addition is a ft mini store with a small cold food storage
area In general the finished floor slabs will be at the same grade
as that of the existing commissary
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1293 00 Page 2
The foundation loadings are anticipated to range from 30 to 110
kips per column and about 1 kip per linear foot for wall footings
For the proposed warehouse addition 3000 1b capacity forklifts
are scheduled There will be a depressed dock height loading ramp
with levelling mechanism The height of the dock will be 50 inches
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF OUR SERVICES
The purpose of our investigation was to explore the subsurface
conditions and to provide geotechnical design recommendations for the
proposed construction
For this investigation 5 borings were drilled to depths ranging
from 10 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Subsoil
samples obtained were examined in our laboratory and selected for a
series of testing Engineering analyses were made and our
recommendations and conclusions were developed based on the field and
laboratory findings
Detailed description of the materials encountered in the borings
along with the laboratory test data are presented in the appendices of
this report
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations

SITE CONDITIONS
General
The site for the proposed warehouse addition consists of a large
asphalt concrete paved area with concrete slab and a wooden structure
Building T 2085 which will be demolished The proposed site for the
checkout area consists of landscaped area with roadways and
sidewalks Both sites are relatively flat with several utility lines
of various sizes buried underneath
Subsurf ace Conditions
Borings 1 through 3 drilled at the proposed warehouse area
disclosed that the subsurface conditions consisted of about 3 inches
of asphalt concrete surface course 2 feet of dense sand and or gravel
fill underlain by volcanic tuff at a depth of 0 5 to 4 5 feet below
the existing grades In Boring 1 there was about 1 foot of grayish
brown stiff silty clay under the gravelly fill but over the volcanic
tuff It appears that this silty clay is moderately expansive
Borings 4 and 5 drilled at the proposed checkout area disclosed
that this site is underlain by a more complex fill consisting of about
0 5 foot of reddish brown stiff clayey silt 0 5 to 2 feet of white
dense coral sand and or gravel which is underlain by about 1 foot of
grayish brown stiff silty clay overlying dense volcanic tuff
encountered at a depth of about 3 5 feet below the existing grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 4
It appears that the volcanic tuff layer under the proposed
addition areas is quite extensive and probably extends to a
significant depth The top of the tuff varied from about Elevations
10 to 12 feet Note The existing commissary floor is assumed to be
at Elevation 15 feet However the tuff may be locally depressed or
excavated during utility installations
Water levels were encountered and measured in our borings as shown
on the boring logs It is believed that the shallow water levels
noted are mostly due to the drilling water retained in the boring and
may not truly reflect the actual groundwater level situation It
appears that the static groundwater level is at 7 to 8 feet below the
existing grade and may fluctuate due to seasonal and tidal influences
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
From a soils engineering point of view boths sites are suitable
for the proposed additions Spread footings founded on the dense
volcanic tuff are recommended On grade slabs may be constructed over
the existing pavement for the warehouse and checkout additions
However the moderately expansive silty clay and clayey silt
underneath the proposed checkout area should be kept from drying out
during construction to minimize future heaving potential The
existing utilities under the project area may need to be removed In
such a case the trench should be backfilled with compacted material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1293 00 Page 5
If the utility lines are left in place the upper portion of the
existing utility trench backfill should be excavated and the
subsequent backfill recompacted to minimize future settlement of slab
subgrade Detailed recommendations are discussed and presented in the
following sections
Site Grading
For the new warehouse addition it is anticipated that 1 to 2 feet
of fill will be required to raise the grade Some clearing of trees
and vegetation will be required in the new checkout area and some
excavation work for the depressed loading ramp construction
Site Preparation Warehouse and Parking Area
To prepare the site for the proposed warehouse the existing
wooden building T 2085 would have to be demolished The
existing concrete slabs should be removed along with portions of
the existing asphalt concrete pavement located within 8 inches
below the finished floor or pavements The existing pavement from
8 to 18 inches below the finished grade should be broken up and
left in place The existing A C pavement more than 18 inches
below the finished grade may remain However the pavement within
the proposed cold storage area plus 3 feet beyond its perimeter
should be removed
Any vegetation organic or other deleterious materials should be
removed

Our borings drilled outside the utility trenches disclosed that
this site is underlain by 0 5 to 1 5 feet of dense sand and
moderately expansive silty clay about 1 foot thick just over the
volcanic tuff This underlying clay layer will be located within
2 to 3 feet below the finished floor and thus special care must
be exercised during the construction to minimize the potential for
slab heaving which usually occurs when the clay becomes dry
especially before slab construction and reabsorbs moisture
afterwards Therefore it is important to maintain the subsoils
adequately moist throughout the site grading work to minimize the
swelling potential of the underlying clay
Since the moistening of the underlying clay is related more
closely to on grade slab construction more detailed discussions
and recommended procedures are presented under ON GRADE SLAB
section
Fill Material
Both sites will require some imported fill to raise the grade or
for the backfill of trenches and over excavation Although some
coral sand and gravel materials may be generated from the
depressed loading ramp excavation it is anticipated that the
quantity of reusable materials will be relatively small In
addition the quality of such reusable materials should be
carefully controlled to avoid contamination by undesirable
materials such as the underlying expansive Clay soils
GEOLABS HAWAII

All clean on site granular materials should be inspected tested
and approved by a qualified soils engineer before being stockpiled
for reuse as fill under the slabs Other materials such as
debris concrete blocks clay silt and contaminated granular
materials should not be used as fill under the slab or paved areas
The imported fill materials should be non expansive soil free of
organic matter and should form a dense stable mass when
compacted to the densities required In general the imported
fill materials should meet the following requirements specified in
the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction City
and County of Honolulu May 1975 except where modified below
a Select Borrow for Subbase Course Section 30 of the
Specifications The material passing the 200 mesh may be
as much as 20 if coral material is used The select borrow
material should be used as subgrade material within the top
24 inches beneath the finished grade in paved and slab areas
b Borrow Section 16 This material may be used in all other
areas

Spread Footings
It is recommended that all foundations should bear at least 6
inches into the volcanic tuff at about 1 to 4 feet below the existing
grade In any case the footing bottom should be at least 2 feet
below the lowest adjacent grade
An allowable soil bearing value up to 12 000 p s f may be used
for footings bearing on the volcanic tuff A one third increase may
be allowed for soil bearing under wind or seismic loads All footings
should have a minimum dimension of 16 inches
The footing excavations should be inspected by a soils engineer to
verify that they are located in the volcanic tuff
Footings adjacent to any utility trench should be deepened so that
their bottom would fall below a plane rising 45 degrees from the
nearest edge of the trench bottom
Any footing to be situated over an existing utility line which
will be abandoned for the project construction should be extended to
the trench bottoms and embedded into volcanic tuff
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1293 00 Page 11
For footing located over an existing utility line that is to
remain on the site it should be designed to bridge over the line and
reach the volcanic tuff at appropriate depths recommended above
If the footings are constructed according to the above recommended
criteria then the total and differential settlements are anticipated
to be negligible
Non load bearing walls may be placed on new compacted fill or
existing granular fill The wall footings should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade and be designed
for an allowable soil bearing pressure of 2000 p s f
Retaining Walls
A low retaining feature would be required at the depressed loading
ramp In addition the new warehouse floor will be raised 1 5 to 2
feet above the surrounding existing parking areas and low retaining
walls would be required along the 3 sides of the warehouse addition
For the design of the retaining walls we recommend that the following
design criteria be used
1 Active earth pressures for restrained top retaining walls should
be 55 p s f per foot of depth The surcharge load if applied
should be converted to equivalent fill height at a rate of 1 foot
of fill height for every 120 p s f of surcharge load For
unrestrained walls the active earth pressure may be reduced to 35
p s f per foot of depth
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1293 00 Page 12
2
The wall footings should bear directly on undisturbed volcanic
tuff with the maximum allowable bearing of 12 000 p s f The
minimum footing bottom embedment should be 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade
Passive earth pressure of 350 p s f per foot of depth may be used
to resist the lateral load In addition a base friction factor
of 0 45 may be used
All backfill material behind the retaining wall should be
free draining granular material compacted to a dry density of at
least 90 of the maximum ASTM D 1557 78 density The backfill
within 2 feet below the finished floor should be compacted to 95
of the maximun
Subdrains or weepholes should be provided to prevent build up of
hydrostatic pressure behind the wall

The warehouse slabs will be subject to 3000 1b Capacity forklift

The mini store area will generally be raised about 1 to 2 feet

Except for the cold storage area the thickness of the slab may be
a more conventional thickness of 4 inches A minimum 4 inches of
granular cushion layer should be provided under the slabs
Moisture proofing membrane should be considered below the slab to
protect floor covering from moisture infiltration

Our Boring 3 drilled near the proposed cold food storage area
disclosed a frost heave susceptible silty sand layer below the
existing A C pavement It is therefore recommended that the
existing asphaltic concrete pavement be removed from the cold
storage area and 3 feet beyond its perimeter The underlying
silty sand should be removed down to a depth of at least 12 inches

coarse aggregate rock such as No 3 Coarse material with less

Such a class of rock should be used to minimize capillary rise
through the rock particles
The rock cushion should be compacted by at least 4 passes of a
hand operated vibratory compactor
New Checkout Area
Our borings disclosed that most of the new checkout area is
underlain by about 1 foot thick moderately expansive clay under
1 5 to 2 feet of fill consisting of non expansive silt sand and
gravel
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1293 00 Page 15
To control potential slab heaving from the swelling of expansive
clays after slab placement the clay soils should be maintained in
a moist condition at the time of slab placement We believe that
this condition could be achieved by adequately wetting the
subgrade surface prior to the placement of moisture proof membrane
or slab concrete to prevent drying of the underlying clay layer
The amount of watering will depend on the weather and site
condition A relatively heavy wetting may be made at the end of
each day during this construction period Excessive wettings that
tend to muddy up the site should be avoided
The moistening work may be done concurrently with other
construction activities and it should be monitored by a soils
engineer It should be done sufficiently in advance of covering
the slab area
The on grade slab should be structurally separated from columns or
bearing walls
It is recommended that the on grade slab be supported on a 4 inch
compacted base course layer

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored
by drilling 5 borings at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted drilling rig was
used to drill the borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classi
fied by visual and textural examination in the field by our
engineer who continuously monitored the drilling operations
The classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection
and testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered
is presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 5
In the borings soil samples were obtained by
driving a 2 inch standard split spoon sampler or a 2 4 inch
I D split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts used to drive the samplers for
12 inches are shown on the log of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Rock core samples were obtained by using a rotary
wash NWD4 series double tube core barrel The core recovery
and rock quality designation RQD are presented on the logs
of borings
