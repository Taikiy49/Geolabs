INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our additional geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed large cut slope area of the H 3 North Halawa
Valley Highway in Halawa Valley on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 60 Page 2
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations based on the data obtained during the
exploration These recommendations are intended for design input only and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding
purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Unit 1 portion of the North Halawa Valley Highway extends from Station
374 25 northwestern side of Halawa Quarry to Station 480 00 approximately two 2
miles into the Valley The Phase 1A portion of the project approximately one mile long
includes Station 374 25 through Station 415 00
An approximately 100 foot high cut slope is planned near the western end of the
Phase 1A portion of the North Halawa Valley Highway project approximately between
Station 378 00 and Station 382 00 A Hawaiian Electric Company HECO access road
is planned near the toe of the proposed cut slope Preliminary plans depicted the subject
slope at an inclination of horizontal to 1 vertical 4H 1V Steep cut slopes such as
the planned 4H 1V cut slope are often used for cut slopes exposing bedrock material
Since rock outcrops were not evident in the area of the subject cut slope concern was
expressed that weaker soil materials could exist in this area that may not have adequate
long term stability if exposed on a steep cut slope

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed cut slope is located approximately between Station 378 00 and
Station 382 00 on the southeast side of North Halawa Stream in the area of an existing
northwest facing slope The existing Halawa Valley access road is located at the bottom
of the slope An unpaved access road associated with the adjacent quarry operations
is located at the top of the slope The existing site conditions and the proposed cut slope
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The existing slope is thickly vegetated with subtropical trees and shrubs Based
on the available topographic map the existing slope has a gradient ranging from about
2H 1V to as steep as 1H 1V Our observations at the site indicate that localized rock
outcrops are nearly vertical on some portions of the slope Existing ground surface
elevations range from about 160 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the toe of the slope to
about 270 feet MSL on the unpaved access road at the top of the slope
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 60 Page 5
An existing fill berm is evident along the top of the slope at the edge of the
unpaved access road Some fill material appears to have been pushed over the slope
although the horizontal and vertical extent of this fill material is uncertain Therefore it
should be expected that overcast fill exists on some portions of the existing slope
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The project site is underlain by basaltic rock of the Koolau volcanic series Where
exposed in outcrops the basaltic rock varies from vesicular to dense and appeared to
be slightly weathered Bedding in the rock was observed to be roughly horizontal with
fracture spacings of about six inches 6 to four feet 4
Bedrock over most of the site is covered by a heterogeneous group of soil types
including existing fill residual soils colluvial soils and talus These soil materials in
general appear to consist of gravel cobbles and boulders with or without matrix
materials Where matrix materials were observed they appeared to vary from silty clay
to sandy silt
As noted above subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling 4
and 1B were horizontal borings and were drilled to approximately
71 and 55 feet respectively from the face of the slope Borings 1C and 1D were vertical
borings drilled at the top of the cut slope to depths of about 40 feet below the ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Detailed boring logs are presented in Appendix A and photographs of the cores
recovered are included in Appendix C The conditions encountered in each of the
borings are briefly summarized as follows

In Boring 1A approximately 5 feet of colluvium consisting of silty clay with basalt
gravel and cobbles was encountered Vesicular Koolau basalt was encountered from 5
to 71 feet The basalt encountered was slightly to moderately weathered and slightly to
moderately fractured Basalt clinker zones were encountered from about 29 to 341 feet
and from about 52 to 55 feet
In Boring 1B approximately 19 feet of colluvium consisting of basalt gravel cobbles
and boulders in a matrix of silty clay was encountered Koolau basalt was encountered
between about 19 and 55 feet with similar characteristics as noted in Boring 1A
Alternating zones of dense basalt and clinker typically between 3 and 7 feet thick were
encountered
About 13 feet of colluvium and or fill consisting of sandy silt with basalt gravel and
cobbles was encountered in Boring 1C drilled at the top of the slope Koolau basalt was
encountered between about 13 feet to the maximum depth explored of about 40 feet
below the ground surface The characteristics of the Koolau basalt encountered were
similar to those described in Boring 1B
In Boring 1D approximately 10 feet of colluvium and or fill consisting of silt with
basalt gravel and cobbles was encountered From depths of about 10 to 40 feet below
the surface Koolau basalt was encountered The basalt encountered in Boring 1D was
slightly to moderately weathered and moderately to slightly fractured Clinker zones were
encountered from about 14 to 17 feet and from about 32 to 35 feet below the surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and observations
from our site reconnaissance we believe that the proposed 100 foot high cut slope
should be flattened It appears that the colluvium and or fill soil layer encountered at the
top of the slope may be as thick as 13 feet or more on the existing slope Therefore a
large portion of the currently proposed 4H 1V cut slope at the higher elevations of the
cut slope would expose the colluvium and or fill soils which were encountered in the
borings As a result we believe that it may be prudent to flatten the portion of the
proposed cut slope where colluvium and or fill soils will be exposed in the proposed
excavation
Flattening of the proposed cut slope will encroach into the unpaved access road
located at the top of the cut slope Therefore additional State Right of Way and
relocation of a portion of the unpaved access road will be required We understand that
the additional Right of Way and relocation of the access road are acceptable and are in
progress Based on meetings and discussions with Parsons Brinkerhoff and ParEn Inc
the following general guidelines are provided for design of the proposed cut slope
1 The cut slope may be designed with a slope inclination of 1H 1V or flatter
beginning from the toe of the proposed cut slope to an elevation of
approximately 15 feet below top of slope
2 The upper portion of the cut slope from an elevation of approximately 15
feet below top of slope to the top of slope should be designed with a slope
inclination of 1172H 1V or flatter
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 60 Page 8
Flattening of the slopes as described above is intended to reduce the slope
gradient of the portion of the cut slope exposing colluvium and or fill soil material
Therefore we recommend that an engineering geologist from our firm be retained during
construction to map the face of the cut slope to confirm that conditions exposed on the
slope are consistent with the recommendations provided above
In addition run off from areas upslope of the proposed cut slope should not be
directed over the face of the slope Run off should be collected in swales or ditches and
directed to appropriate discharge locations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 2
vertical borings to depths of about 40 feet below the existing ground surface and 2
horizontal borings to depths of 55 and 71 feet from the face of the slope The borings
were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The vertical
borings were drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig using continuous flight augers and
HQ wireline diamond coring methods The horizontal borings were drilled by a modular
skid mounted drill rig using HQ diamond coring methods
Soil samples were obtained from the vertical borings in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test by driving a standard split spoon
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the boring logs at the
appropriate sample depths
Samples of the rock encountered in the vertical and horizontal borings were
obtained by HQ diamond coring methods Photographs of the core samples are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 7 of Appendix C
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2488 84 Description and Identification of Soils
Visual Manual Procedure by an engineer or geologist from our firm The logs of the
Core recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet
of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run
the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration to aid in classification of the materials and to evaluate the pertinent
engineering properties of the soil materials sampled
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to evaluate the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Liquid Limit and Plasticity Index of the
material are used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The
results of these tests are shown on Plate B 1
