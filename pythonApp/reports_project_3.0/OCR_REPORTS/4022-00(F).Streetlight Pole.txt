REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava thus
forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered
the basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes
consisting of detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered

the natural condition of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the
central portion of the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older
alluviul colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time
Except for the Fort Weaver Road project site the project sites are generally
underlain by these older and more recent alluvial deposits or by the soil like weathered
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban development has since altered the natural
formation of the area
In the vicinity of the Fort Weaver Road project site the site is generally underlain
by alluvial sediments from the Schofield Plateau These alluvial sediments accumulated
and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a
sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from Zero feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over
1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining
formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of
these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the materials encountered in nearby previous field explorations geologic
maps and soil survey maps we believe that the proposed streetlight poles may be
supported on drilled shaft type foundations to resist the generally high ground line and
torsional moments induced by loading of the poles
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated loading
conditions axial loads lateral loads ground line overturning moment and torsional
moment we recommend that 24 inch diameter drilled shafts be used for support of each
streetlight pole structure The recommended lengths of the drilled shaft range from 5 to
12 feet below the ground surface
Based on traffic safety concerns some of the streetlight poles proposed for sloped
areas above Kamehameha Highway will be constructed 8 feet up the slope However for
sloped areas above Mokapu Saddle Road we recommend that streetlight poles not be
constructed up the slope We believe that it is not practical to install drilled shafts in hard
rock on the very steep slopes up to 4H 1V We recommend that the streetlight poles be
constructed at the toe of the slope if possible Guardrails should be installed to protect
motorists from hitting the poles
The performance of the shaft foundations will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction construction procedures and workmanship The
recommendations and general guidelines presented in this report should be followed
closely by the contractor during foundation construction work
Foundation analyses were conducted based on a combination of axial and lateral
loads in addition to ground line overturning moments using the computer software
program LPILE Plus for Windows In addition the shaft foundations were also checked
for the torsional moments induced by the poles The analyses were based on a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4022 00 F Page 10
free head condition and a maximum lateral deflection of not more than inch at the top
of the shaft
Torsional moments of up to about 6 3 kip feet were used in the design of the
drilled shafts In addition foundation analyses for the drilled shafts were performed with
lateral loads of up to about 1 2 kips along with ground line moments of up to about
31 9 kip feet
Difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during installation of the drilled
shafts due to the presence of shallow basalt rock formations along Mokapu Saddle Road

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the loading conditions provided and the subsurface conditions
encountered we recommend that the poles for the streetlights be supported by drilled
shaft foundations Based on the bottom diameter of the steel poles up to 15 inches at
the base the available auger core barrel sizes from the local drilling companies and the
shaft lengths anticipated drilled shaft diameters of 24 inches were considered in our
foundation analyses The major considerations in our foundation analyses included the
lateral loads and ground line overturning moments induced in the drilled shaft
foundations
In general lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the shaft
and induced moment in the shaft Based on a maximum horizontal deflection of inch at
the top of the drilled shaft subjected to ground line overturning moments and lateral
forces we have evaluated the required drilled shaft lengths using the computer software
program LPILE Plus for Windows This program is a microcomputer adaptation of a
finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the University of
Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a drilled
shaft under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use
of internally generated non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4022 00 F Page 16
deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths
The analyses were performed for a laterally loaded 24 inch diameter cast in place
concrete drilled shaft The results of our engineering analyses are summarized in the
following table
The recommended shaft lengths were based on the depth measured from the
lowest adjacent grade This is especially important for sloping ground Also in the case
of Likelike Highway the recommended foundation depth can be assumed to be the

Uplift Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the pole
structure drilled shaft foundation and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent
soils The combined dead load of the pole structure and drilled shaft foundation were
determined to range from 3 to 6 kips The combined dead load of the pole structure and
drilled shaft foundation alone should be able to resist the uplift loads without significant
movements
Torsion Resistance
Torsion may be resisted by the side shear along the shaft surface and adjacent
soils In our foundation analyses we have evaluated the shafts subjected to the torsional
moments Based on our analyses we believe that the 24 inch diameter drilled shafts
extending to the lengths recommended should be able to resist the torsional moments

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the Contractor s
method of construction construction procedures and workmanship As a result we
recommend that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the
installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may have a
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated drilling through basalt rock
formations will be required on Mokapu Saddle Road near the cut area through the
mountain west of the H 3 Interstate Highway We also anticipate that drilling through
basalt rock formations will be required on Likelike Highway between the State of Hawaii
DOT Materials Testing Lab and the Wilson Tunnel We anticipate that drilling through
coral formations will be required on the southern half of Fort Weaver Road The drilled
shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and or coring tools to drill
through the boulders and rock formation Appropriate measures by the contractor will
also be needed to avoid dislodging any boulders into the drilled shaft excavation prior to
placement of concrete
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated drilling through soft loose pockets
of soil may be required during construction of the drilled shaft foundations To reduce the
potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled holes may be
needed during construction Installation of the casing may be by vibration driving or
twisting to advance the casing In addition care should be exercised during removal of
the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore

a minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained above the bottom of the casing
and or groundwater level during removal of the casing
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all drilled shafts should be
cleaned of disturbed material prior to constructing the foundation The bottom should
also be fairly level The concrete should be poured neatly against the excavation side
walls Backfill against the shaft foundations should not be allowed
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids slurry is not recommended
Groundwater conditions may be encountered therefore placement of concrete by wet
construction methods using a tremie pipe may be required in some locations A
low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the
concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from the bottom of the drilled shaft
Placement of concrete using a tremie pipe will likely cause some groundwater and
soil to discharge out of the top of the drilled shaft The Contractor should be made aware
that this fluid should be properly disposed of Since the water will be turbid from soil
particles direct disposal into storm drains will not be acceptable The Contractor
should also be made aware that a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination
System NPDES permit may be necessary depending on the proposed
method of water disposal The Contractor should consult their independent consultant or
the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest regulations and information
pertaining to the permit application

Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should
comply with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring
may be necessary due to the presence of near surface soft loose soils Trench shoring
design and installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and
bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes or up to 2 feet
above the water table whichever results in a greater thickness should consist of
free draining backfill material such as No 3B Fine gravel This is to reduce the potential
for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining
granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of
on site soils as backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
Soft or loose soils encountered at the bottom of trenches should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and backfilled with select borrow material
compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction If groundwater is encountered
the excavation may be filled with No 3B Fine gravel encapsulated by a geotextile fabric
AMOCO 4545 or similar This material should be considered as additional to the
recommended 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel bedding
The upper portion of the trench backfill above the free draining backfill to the finish
subgrade below the existing pavement section or sidewalk section should consist of the
on site soils or select borrow material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4022 00 F Page 22
The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement or sidewalk finish
subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
The pavement section material and sidewalk section material should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be
used to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be used to compact the backfill material
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of
backfilling observations and soil density tests should be performed by Geolabs Hawaii
during excavation and backfilling operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the
required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift
Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be applied
with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

It is recommended that Geolabs Hawaii be retained to provide geotechnical
engineering services during construction of the proposed streetlight poles and utilities as
part of the Special Maintenance Projects of Various Streets The items of construction
monitoring that are critical which require Special Inspection include observation of drilled
shaft foundation drilling utility excavation and backfilling and placement of concrete

from Geolabs Hawaii This is to observe compliance with the intent of the design
concepts specifications or recommendations and to expedite suggestions for design
changes that may be required in the event that subsurface conditions differ from those
considered in this report The recommendations provided in this report are contingent

different from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications
to the design should be made

may not become evident until construction is underway f variations then appear evident
it will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report
