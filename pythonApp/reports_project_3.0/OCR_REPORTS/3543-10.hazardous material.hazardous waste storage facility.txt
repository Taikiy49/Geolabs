INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hazardous Material Hazardous Waste Storage Facility
project located within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH in the Kaneohe Bay area on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion cut valleys deep into
the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder
and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay is located on Mokapu Peninsula which was
built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series These vents are believed to
be among the oldest of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from vents
formed salients permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of
the island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age the sea level fell below the present sea
level in response to continental glaciation Subsequently sands from emerged coral
reefs were blown inland by the prevailing winds to form sand dunes along the northern
portion of the airfield area An extensive deposit of unconsolidated calcareous marine
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3543 10 Page 6
sediments primarily consisting of beach sand made up of water worn coral shells and
organisms covers the western tip of Mokapu Peninsula Man made fills consisting of
marine sediments dredged from the ocean floor were used to build up the area around
the southern and western shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located approximately at the intersection of Mokapu and Reed
Roads within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The project site is bounded by existing buildings to the west and south by
Reed Road to the east and by vacant lands to the north
The project site is presently vacant and heavily vegetated with bushes and
trees A drainage ditch of about 3 to 4 feet deep was noted traversing the site in a
northwest southeast direction at the time of our field exploration as shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 In addition a trail traversing the site from the west to the east was also
noted The ground surface at the site appears to consist of reddish brown clayey silts
with sands Based on our field observations and a review of the topographic survey map
the site is relatively level with existing ground surface elevations of approximately 118 to
128 feet The project site slopes down gently from the east to the west

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to hard clayey silts and silty clays
throughout the depths of the borings drilled at the subject site Based on the relatively
competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the stiff
in situ soils or new engineered fills may be used for support of the proposed structures
Laboratory test results indicate that the near surface soils exhibit moderate to high
swelling and shrinking potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we
recommend that siabs on grade be supported on at least 12 inches of select granular fill

Building Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed structures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used
for the design of foundations bearing on the stiff in situ soils or new engineered fills
needed to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below
the lowest adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near top of slopes or on
sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum
horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to
the slope face
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement

We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the stiff in situ soils or new engineered fills as recommended herein to
be less than one inch total with differential settlements on the order of inch
For footings bearing on the in situ soils or new engineered fills the bottom of the
footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures The near surface soils encountered at the site exhibit moderate to high
expansion potential when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content Therefore to
reduce the potential of structural distress to lightly loaded slab on grade construction
resulting from swelling of the on site expansive soils we recommend that the subgrade
soils be properly prepared and a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular
fill material be provided below slabs on grade The non expansive select fill should
extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the limits of the structures
The subgrade to receive the non expansive select granular fill should be scarified
to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The 12 inch non expansive select fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 to
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve All select granular fill materials should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3543 10 Page 11
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by the cushion layer
base course or concrete slabs
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slab above the 12 inch thick select
granular fill to provide more uniform support of the slab To reduce future moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor
barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer
Two inches of moist fine sand should be placed above the plastic membrane to provide
protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask any minor slab cracking
We envision that some of the slabs will be subjected to vehicular and or forklift
traffic Therefore we recommend that a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction be provided below the slabs subjected to
vehicular traffic in lieu of the 4 inch thick cushion layer The 2 inch moist fine sand and
vapor barrier may be omitted for these slabs The base course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For design
of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per cubic inch
p c i may be used for the compacted base course A minimum slab thickness of
6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes Provisions should be made for
proper load transfer across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The
thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade

Retaining Walls
We understand that a partial basement is planned for the proposed hazardous
material minimization building The basement wall should be designed as restrained
walls In addition retaining walls may also be needed for grade separation Foundations
for retaining walls should be designed in general accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Foundations section of this report The footing embedment for
retaining walls should be a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade We recommend that wall footings have a minimum width of 18 inches
Where wall footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or
utilities the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane
projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations

saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings to depths of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drilling rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
