Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating
sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and further south
these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef
formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from 0 feet in the area of
H 1 freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and
serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of
southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time
through the present
Much of the sediments and coral formations were laid down during the Kaena
high stand of the sea 95 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The surface was then planed
down by wave action during the later Waimanalo high stand 25 feet MSL During
World War II the area was developed for military use as what appeared to be munitions
storage After the war the site was slowly converted to plantation use for the cultivation
of sugar cane
Based on our field exploration the site is covered with a thin mantle of brownish
red silty clay containing coralline gravel and sand This mantle generally about 1 foot in
thickness is either alluvium or fill material brought in by the plantation to increase the
arability of the land Under the surface mantle the site is underlain by thinly bedded
coralline sandstone which appears to be a littoral or beach deposit

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is an open field area that is relatively flat with a very gentle slope
down towards the south Based on the available topographic plans the ground surface
elevation is about 23 feet MSL
At the time of our field exploration the southern half of the site was covered with
sparse dry low grass and some brush The northern half of the site appeared to have
been covered with some fill materials This northern area is being used as a contractor s
staging yard and has had some uncontrolled fill about 6 inches placed over the original
ground surface Some small stockpiles of soil were observed in the yard area Loose
soils were noted on the slope face adjacent to existing house structures at the western
end of the site

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by excavating and
sampling of seven test pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 7 to depths ranging
between about 1 5 to 3 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate test
pit locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

very stiff dry friable brownish red silty clay with sand and coralline gravel that was
identified as Alluvium or Fill that had been disturbed by agricultural activity This surface
layer was underlain by coralline deposits consisting of medium hard to hard cemented
sand sandstone identified as Beachrock formation to the maximum depth explored of
3 5 feet below the existing ground surface The test pits were terminated when the
Caterpillar 420E encountered refusal in the coralline deposits
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits excavated at the project site

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with low to moderate swelling potential In addition we
anticipate that the on site clayey soils in the development area may be used as general
fill to raise the grade of the site Unless floor slabs are properly designed and
constructed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of these soils due to changes in the moisture
content To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the shrinking and
swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly
prepared and capped with at least 24 inches of non expansive structural fill material
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and
graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding
around the foundation
Concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new pool structure We recommend
over excavating the area below the pool slab a minimum of 12 inches and backfilling
with compacted select fill material to provide uniform support and reduce the potential of
differential settlement
The structural fill should be placed immediately after moisture conditioning and
compaction of the clayey subgrade soils The prepared structural fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete
Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Site Grading section of this report Compaction requirements for the subgrade
soils and select granular fill are also discussed in the same section of this report
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed below
the slab The cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB should provide more uniform support of the

The subgrade soil under the pavement area should be moisture conditioned to
about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 95 percent
relative compaction The subgrade should be kept moist prior to placement of the
aggregate base course Base course materials should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and density
tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction
to confirm the adequacy of the above sections
Aggregate base course material should meet the material requirements for Base

Construction Department of Public Works City and County of Honolulu
September 1986 Imported fill material should be tested for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site

Utilities
It is anticipated that utilities will be installed for the proposed project The
contractor should be determine the methods and equipment to be used for excavation
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with
all applicable local state and federal safety requirements The contractor should be
responsible for trench shoring design and installation Trench shoring and bracing
should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend using a granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for pipe
damage from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as
backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the finish subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or select granular
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the
materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to
compact the backfill material
