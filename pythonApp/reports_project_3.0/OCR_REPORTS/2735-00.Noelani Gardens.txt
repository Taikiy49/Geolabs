INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Noelani Gardens townhouse development in the district of
South Hilo on the island of Hawaii Hawaii The general project area is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
The recommendations presented herein are intended for civil and structural design input
and are not intended to form the basis of preparation for final construction cost estimates
or for bidding purposes
This report supplements our design memorandum dated August 26 1991 which
presented our preliminary recommendations regarding the design concepts for the
project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a townhouse development at the project site Based
on the information provided to us the development will consist of four 4 three story
buildings It is anticipated that the buildings will be of masonry and wood construction
The new buildings will be constructed on cut building pads along the riverfront
perimeter of the project site It is anticipated that up to about twelve 12 feet of cut will
be required to create the building pads

areas will be constructed essentially at existing grade with some minor cut and fill areas
between the buildings and the entrance to the development Asphaltic concrete
pavement is anticipated for the parking areas and driveways
The parking areas will be connected to the second floor levels of the new buildings
by short span footbridges To create grade separation between the parking areas and
the buildings retaining walls up to approximately twelve 12 foot high will be required

It is planned to utilize dry wells to provide on site disposal of storm water runoff

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated at the top of a steep slope on the northerly bank of the
Wailuku River in the district of South Hilo on the island of Hawaii The site is bounded by
Wailuku River on the south and east Puueo Street on the west and Kou Lane to the
north
The site is vacant land which was formerly used for residential purposes
Based on the available topographic Survey it appears that the existing ground
surface slopes gently from approximately 41 feet elevation above Mean Sea Level MSL
at the west corner down to approximately 35 feet above MSL in the central and northern
portions of the site The ground then slopes more steeply towards the river bank which
are almost vertical cliffs dropping from approximately 12 to 26 feet above MSL at the river
edge The original topography of the site was Slightly altered during the clearing which
was required to provide access for our field exploration equipment
A small beach lies at the toe of the cliff along the northerly bank of Wailuku River
There are several features on the beach indicating previous development such as a short
concrete rubble masonry CRM wall remnants of a pavement and a small fireplace The
condition of these features indicates that the area has been subjected to erosion
Based on our visual observations it appears that the river banks are underlain with
a weathered basalt formation located slightly above the river level The weathered basalt
is overlain by volcanic ash and saprolite soils

During our field exploration it was noted that the southerly facing slope of the river
bank at the site and the top of the bank at the easterly end of the site were covered with
thick vegetation including large trees and dense underbrush
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of
eight 8 borings to depths ranging between about 24 to 40 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site is underlain by a thick deposit of soft clayey silt soils
i volcanic ash and saprolite which is derived from the in situ weathering of volcanic
rock formation with occasional horizons of silty sand saprolite Underlying the surface
soils our exploration encountered weathered basaltic rock formation to the maximum
depths explored
In our borings approximately eighteen 18 to twenty eight 28 feet of soft clayey
silts were encountered above the weathered rock In localized horizons within the soil
layers the consistency of the soils increased slightly to medium stiff The thickness of
the soft soil deposit decreases towards the river
Based on our laboratory testing the volcanic ash soil has very high in situ moisture
contents exceeding 100 percent This is typical for ash deposits which are locally
referred to as Pahala Ash It is believed that this surface ash is an airfall deposits from
explosive eruptive events which may have occurred on either Mauna Loa or Kilauea
Generally Pahala Ash soils generally have relatively low strength and if the in situ
moisture contents are high enough they are thixotropic i e they lose strength when
remolded under construction or traffic loads or when disturbed by seismic vibrations

W O 2735 00 Page 7
However if a gravel cushion is provided above the ash we believe that the soft
ash deposit will be sufficiently protected from disturbance to retain its ability to support
pavements for light to medium vehicular traffic
Groundwater Conditions
Groundwater levels were observed in the borings at depths ranging from
approximately 13 to 23 5 feet below the existing ground surface or at approximately 2 to
22 feet above MSL The higher groundwater elevations measured were much higher than
anticipated for the geologic and hydrologic conditions at the site and in its vicinity We
suspect that due to the low permeability of the materials in the subsurface the high water
levels are the result of surface runoff which drained into the borings during overnight
rainfalls and did not dissipate The water levels measured are probably transient perched
water levels and therefore do not reflect the static groundwater conditions
Percolation Tests
Two 2 percolation tests were performed in selected borings to determine the in
situ permeability characteristics of the underlying weathered basalt formation to obtain
data for design of the proposed dry wells The percolation test results are presented in
Appendix C
Based on the test results it appears that the underlying weathered basalt formation
has low permeability within the maximum depths explored by our borings Permeable
Clinker zones which are sometimes present in similar geologic formations were not
encountered in our borings which extended to as deep as 40 feet below the existing
ground surface These clinker zones are generally much more favorable for the disposal
for water

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Our exploration indicates that the project site is underlain by soft soil conditions
along the steep banks of Wailuku River Based on the anticipated structural loads which
will be generated by the proposed townhouse buildings it is our opinion that the subsoils
will not provide adequate bearing support for these residential structures In addition as
previously discussed the soil materials are thixotropic volcanic ash which has a significant
potential for loss of stability and possible liquefaction during earthquake conditions
Therefore we recommend that the townhouse buildings be supported by drilled
pier foundations developing their load carrying capacity from end bearing on the
underlying rock formation It is our further recommendation that the ground floors of the
buildings be designed as structural slabs capable of spanning between the foundations
to minimize the potential impacts of loss of ground support below the slabs
Where new pavements and a stable working platforms to support equipment are
required the subgrades resulting from either clearing or excavation in the soft volcanic
ash should be capped with a two 2 foot compacted gravel cushion to improve the
stability of the material
The soft volcanic ash should be removed from the active wedge area behind the
new retaining walls Select granular backfill should be used to reduce lateral earth loading
on the walls under both static and seismic conditions The walls should also be
embedded sufficiently since the soft ash deposit has relatively high erosion potential
For disposal of storm runoff our percolation tests performed indicate that the
underlying basalt formation has relatively low permeability characteristics to the maximum
GEOLABS HAWAII

Based on the information provided to us the river banks along the boundaries of

Drilled Pier Construction
The loading capacities of the drilled piers depend to a large extent on the contact
between the pier shafts and the surrounding soil and rock Therefore proper
construction techniques are important We recommend that the drilled pier
construction be observed by the project geotechnical engineer or his designated
representative
The contractor should exercise the utmost care in drilling the pier holes and
placing the proper concrete mix into the drilled holes
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered we believe that the pier holes should
be advanced by auger drilling first and rock bit chopping if needed Drilling fluids
which may saturate and weaken the surrounding soil and rock should be avoided
because the fluids could prevent adequate bonding between the pier shafts and
the surrounding soil and rock
A non shrinkage concrete mix should be specified to promote close contact
between the pier shafts and the surrounding soil and rock The tremie concrete
should be placed as promptly as possible in the predrilled holes to reduce the
potential of hole cave in The concrete should also be placed in a suitable manner
to avoid segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2735 00 Page 11
Complete filling of a pier hole should be checked by comparing the volume of
concrete placed with the computed volume of the hole A lesser volume of
concrete would indicate necking down of the hole or arching of the concrete either
of which would reduce the bearing capacity of the pier
Site Grading
Based on the available grading information it is anticipated that up to 15 feet of cut
may be required for the building and retaining wall construction Some minor cut and fill
may also be required for the parking areas
At the on set of earthwork the project area within the contract grading limits should
be thoroughly cleared and grubbed The use of heavy construction equipment should
be avoided to reduce disturbance to the near surface soils during the clearing and
grubbing operations and the subsequent construction period All existing vegetation
rubble debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of properly off site prior to initiation of grading After grubbing the cleared and or cut
grades in the future paved areas should not be further disturbed by recompaction efforts
Where either new pavements or stable working platforms for equipment are
required over the volcanic ash a gravel cushion capping that is a minimum of two 2 feet
thick should be provided
Due to its sensitive nature the volcanic ash material resulting from excavation
should not be re used for general site and structural fill For the above gravel cushions
or structural site filling imported select granular materials such as select borrow or well
graded clinker may be used For backfilling behind retaining walls we recommend that
Clean granular material such as No 3B Fine ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel be used

Retaining Wall
Retaining walls will be required to create grade separations within the townhouse
development It is our understanding that an inexpensive block type retaining wall was
originally considered for use to reduce costs Our preliminary report indicated however
that conventional retaining walls such CRM or concrete walls would be needed Further
analysis by the project structural engineer indicates that all major retaining walls will be
constructed of reinforced concrete
In general the retaining walls will be constructed in cut areas Based on the
anticipated excavation depths it appears that most of the underlying ash subsoils will be
removed along the high retaining walls behind the proposed buildings Therefore we
believe that footing foundations bearing directly on a compacted gravel cushion may be
used to support the proposed retaining walls
Due to the sensitive nature of the on site volcanic ash soil we recommend that
imported select granular material such as No 3B Fine ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel be
used as backfill behind the walls to reduce the compactive effort required and the lateral
earth loads on the walls

Design Limitations
Our recommendations for the foundations of the retaining walls are based on the
use of spread footings founded on the on site ash subsoils It must be noted that
in the event of major earthquakes some distresses such as minor cracking and
shifting of the walls may result from soil movement due to the thixotropic nature
of these subsoils To reduce these potential damages to the walls a foundation
system such as the use of drilled piers bearing on the underlying basalt formation
may be considered However it is believed that the use of this system for th
retaining walls would greatly increase the project costs
Dry Wells
The percolation test results indicate that the underlying basalt formation has low
permeability within the maximum depths of our borings
Based on the calculated permeabilities of the rock formation encountered it
appears that a five 5 foot diameter dry well drilled to a minimum depth of 50 feet below
the existing ground at the site may be able to dissipate a maximum flow rate of 0 1 cubic
feet per second Casing should be utilized within the upper portion of the dry wells in
ash and saprolite soils to reduce the potential of eroding the soft subsoils and causing
ground surface collapse around the dry wells
Alternatively additional and deeper borings may be performed to determine if more
permeable clinker layer s may be located at greater depths in the underlying basalt
formation

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of eight 8 borings to depths ranging between 20 and 40 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary core
drilling tools as appropriate
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D
2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the
Boring Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative
quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed for appropriate tests to aid in
determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture contents expressed as a percentage of the dry
weight of the tested samples are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring
Logs
An Atterberg Limits test ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 is used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of a selected soil sample The Plasticity Index of the
material is used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The
results of this test are shown on Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Two 2 laboratory CBR tests are performed to evaluate the subgrade support
characteristics of selected near surface soil samples These results are also summarized
on Plate B 1
Two 2 expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of near
surface soils under structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use
as foundation support and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate B 2
Two 2 Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine
the angles of internal friction and cohesion values of selected soil samples The results
of the direct shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
Two 2 permeability tests were performed in selected borings during our field
exploration in order to evaluate the in situ permeability of the subsurface material
Constant head test method was utilized
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until
the water surface reached and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The
rate of pumping water and the water level were then measured
The test data are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
