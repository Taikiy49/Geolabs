PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is situated along Kapolei Parkway in the Kapolei area on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project site encompasses an area of about 10.2 acres.  We 
understand that an affordable housing complex is planned at the site.   
Based on the preliminary site information provided, a total of about 11, three-story 
residential buildings are planned at the site.  Three two-story parking structures are also 
planned.  In addition, single-story recreation/laundry buildings are planned at three locations. 
The new buildings will have a design finished first floor elevations of about +58.8 to 
+61 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). Based on the finished floor elevations and the site 
topography, we envision site grading consisting of cuts and fills of up to about 7 feet thick 
will be required to achieve the design grades.

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, existing 
structures to be demolished, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for contaminating the excavated 
materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be reused as fill materials and 
should be properly disposed of off-site.  
As mentioned above, previously placed fills and coralline boulder stockpiles are 
present at the site. Fill materials previously placed at the site and coralline boulder 
stockpiles should be considered as undocumented fills and should be removed prior to 
placement of additional fills and/or construction of new improvements. 
After clearing, grubbing, and over-excavation of undocumented fills, areas to receive 
fills should be scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
In general, a Geolabs representative should monitor the site preparation 
operations to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the 
scarification and over-excavation processes and to confirm whether the exposed soil 
conditions are similar to those encountered in the field exploration. 
Fills and Backfills 
We recommend capping the project site with a minimum 3-foot thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill materials. In general, the excavated on-site granular 
materials, including previously placed fill and coralline boulder stockpiles, may be reused 
as a source of select granular fill materials, provided that they are processed to meet the 
maximum particle size and gradation requirements presented below.  Excavated clayey 
alluvium materials may be used as general fill below the minimum 3-foot thick layer of 
select granular fill materials. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a

Boulder Disposal 
Boulders (i.e., coral or rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but 
less than 3 feet in largest dimension), such as the coralline boulder stockpiles at the site, 
may be used in rock fills, provided that the following recommendations are followed: 
1. 
Boulders less than 3 feet in largest dimension may be utilized as rock fills, 
provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 4 feet below the 
bottom of footings. Boulders larger than 3 feet in maximum dimension should 
not be used as rock fill unless reduced in size. 
2. 
Filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be placed on top of the boulder 
fills to reduce the potential for long-term fine material migration into the 
boulder fill. 
3. 
Placement of boulders should be avoided in the proposed utility line alignment 
(roadways) and within the depths of the proposed utility lines to reduce the 
potential for encountering the boulders during excavation for the utility 
trenches. 
It should be noted that disposal of boulders will require the entire fill mass to be 
compacted and properly “choked-off” at the surface of rock fill to reduce the potential for 
migration of fines through the voids between boulders. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The general fill and non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in 
level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction (or 95 percent relative 
compaction for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic). 
"Rock" fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the 
majority of the rocks or 3 feet, whichever is less. Placement should be by haul-trucks 
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift. 
Spreading of the "rock" fill should be by a large bulldozer to facilitate "seating" of the 
rocks. The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing 
in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during placement.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and earthwork recommendations submitted in this letter report are 
based in part upon information obtained from the field borings and site reconnaissance.  
Variations of the subsurface conditions between and beyond the field data points may occur, 
and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented in this letter report.

herein, in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and 
practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. This letter report has been prepared solely 
for the purpose of assisting the design engineers in support of the earthwork

The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated subsurface conditions are 
commonly encountered. Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as perched 
groundwater, soft deposits, hard layers, or loose fills, may occur in localized areas and 
may require additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction 
delays) to attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund 
is recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs.
