2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation The project site is on the southern flank of the
shield volcano
Rock formation of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick
flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south
trends at various locales Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava flows are
some deposits of pyroclastic materials volcanic ash and cinders which usually form
low cinder cones surrounding the vent Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series
cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch many sea level changes occurred as a result of
widespread continental glaciation As the great continental glaciers accumulated the
ocean level fell since less water available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the
glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because more water was available
The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes and the
fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in
stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level of
the Island of Kauai Most of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolitic soils
derived from the in situ weathering of igneous rocks and alluvial soils deposited from
streams
The project site is generally underlain by fills overlying residual soils and
saprolite and basalt rock at greater depth

that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the new building
structure An allowable bearing pressure of about may be used to design
foundations for support of the building structure bearing on the re compacted on site

Soft and or loose materials that may be encountered at the bottom of the footing

over excavation may be backfilled with the fill material compacted to the same relative

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback

45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench

3 4 Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not
confined can result in significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally
liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the
ground surface The greater overburden pressure can be sufficient to prevent
liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is mostly underlain by medium stiff to
very hard cohesive soils Therefore we believe that the potential for liquefaction at this
project site is minimal

the City and County of Honolulu Board of Water Supply Therefore we recommend
properly designing all metallic substructures such as ductile iron water lines in contact
with the soils to be protected against potential corrosion Because some of the utility lines
may be embedded within the upper fill materials placed to raise the existing ground to the
design finished grades we recommend conducting additional testing on the proposed
imported fill materials to evaluate the corrosivity of the imported fills
3 9 Underground Utility Lines
We anticipate that the underground utilities for the proposed development will
primarily consist of water drainage and sewer In general good construction practices
should be utilized to install and backfill the trenches for the proposed utilities The
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used to excavate
trenches subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the trench
excavation should comply with the applicable local state and federal safety
requirements The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and
W O INC Page 16
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
installation Trench shoring and bracing should also conform to the appropriate health
and safety requirements
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the pipes to support the utility
lines The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining granular backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining
granular material as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes We do not recommend using clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around
utility pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 1 foot above the pipes to
the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill
materials with a maximum particle size of 6 inches or less The backfill material should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper
3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less
than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be
used to compact the backfill materials at the project site Compaction efforts by water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed considering the expansive soil
conditions at the site
3 10 Drainage
As previously indicated proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations and slabs constructed on expansive soil conditions Therefore the finished
grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water away from the foundations
and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised to install gutter systems
around the building and to divert discharge away from the foundation and pavement
areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
W O INC Page 17
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be backfilled properly against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from foundations and slabs

Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 66

Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations

soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Six one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under
different surcharge loads The test results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 2
One Sieve Analysis test was performed on one selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The test was
performed in accordance with ASTM C 117 and C 136 Graphic presentation of the
grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 3
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test
are presented on Plate B 4
Five tests and four minimum resistivity tests
were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test
results are summarized on Plate B 5
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture content The
test results are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7

EXISTING WAL MART PAVEMENT CONDITIONS
Based on new laboratory test results a CBR value of 15 is used to evaluate the
existing pavement sections Based on the traffic data provided by Wal Mart we
performed pavement analyses using AASHTO design method Based on engineering
analyses and field observation we believe that the existing pavement sections could be
sufficient for a total of 32 years of service life which will allow the existing pavements to
continue serving 20 more years However Wal Mart s new design criteria require a
minimum AC thickness of 3 inches for standard pavement and 4 inches for heavy duty
pavement Therefore a one inch asphalt concrete overlay is recommended on the
existing pavement sections in order to improve pavement performance through its
service life
Based on the field observation the existing pavement is generally in good
condition However we observed longitudinal and alligator cracks of moderate to high
severity at the middle of the parking row at the south end of the parking lot We also
observed some localized distresses of low severity throughout the parking lot In
addition we also observed pavement bulging obviously caused by tree roots at the
areas surrounding some of the existing planters in the parking lot
The low severity cracks should be sealed prior to overlay The more serious
cracks may require partial removal and repair prior to overlay The existing planters
may require tree wells or barriers to block the tree roots
