This technical memorandum provides our preliminary recommendations of the MBR 
Treatment Plant project located within the existing Kahului Airport Pump Station in Kahului 
on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The final report should be consulted when it becomes 
available. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located within the existing Kahului Airport Pump Station in Kahului 
on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  Based on the information provided, the project site is about 
110 feet long by about 38 feet wide and will involve the construction of new membrane 
bioreactor (MBR) treatment package plant, 20,000-gallon storage tank and shed structure. 
The new MBR treatment package plant and the new storage tank are planned to be on a 
19-foot by 59-foot foundation footprint and on a circular 19.5-foot diameter foundation 
footprint, respectively.  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions within the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 26.5 and 
35.5 feet below the existing ground surface, respectively.  
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally covered with surficial 
fills underlain by recent alluvium, beach sand deposit and lagoonal deposits with residual 
soil at greater depths. In general, our drilled borings encountered surficial fills consisting of 
asphaltic concrete, medium dense sandy gravel, and medium stiff to stiff sandy clay 
extending to a depth of about 2 feet below the existing ground surface. The surficial fill was 
generally underlain by recent alluvium consisting of very soft to soft clayey silt/silty clay 
extending to depths of about 6.5 and 7.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The recent 
alluvium was generally underlain by a beach sand deposit consisting of very loose to 
medium dense sand extending to depths of about 14 and 17.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Underlying the beach sand deposit, lagoonal deposit consisting of very loose to 
medium dense clayey sand was encountered extending to depths of about 26.5 and 
27.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Beneath the lagoonal deposit in Boring No. 2,

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition) (IBC 2006), the project 
site would be subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to 
be addressed, as well as evaluating for potential soil liquefaction. 
Our field exploration generally encountered surficial fills underlain by recent alluvium, 
beach sand deposit and lagoonal deposit with residual soil at greater depths extending to 
the maximum depth explored of about 35.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In Boring 
No. 2, the very loose beach sand and lagoonal deposits have an aggregate thickness of 
about 20 feet. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered and our engineering analyses, the 
project site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils. Therefore, the project site would need 
to be classified as Site Class F based on IBC 2006 Section 1613.5.1, that may require a 
site-specific response analysis to determine the spectral accelerations due to the potential 
liquefiable soils. Based on the assumption that the proposed structures are relatively short, 
we assumed that the fundamental period of vibration of the structures is less than 
0.5 seconds. The fundamental period of vibration of the proposed structures should be 
checked by the project structural engineer. In accordance with Chapter 20, Site 
Classification Procedure for Seismic Design contained in ASCE 7-10 (Minimum Design 
Loads for Buildings and Other Structures), a site-specific response analysis will not be 
required. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site and the geologic 
setting of the area, we believe the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis 
standpoint as a “Soft Soil Profile”. Therefore, we believe that seismic design may be 
based on a Site Class E soil profile in accordance with the International Building Code 
(Table No. 1613.5.2), 2006 Edition.

Based on our analysis, it appears that the very loose beach sand and lagoonal 
deposits at the project site have factors of safety of less than 1.0 against liquefaction 
based on the design earthquake. Therefore, the beach sand and lagoonal deposits are 
susceptible to soil liquefaction during the design earthquake event. The project site could 
be subjected to some seismically induced ground settlements (on the order of about 
7 inches) in the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+) based on the 
intensity of the design earthquake ground motions. However, we believe that lateral 
spreading associated with liquefaction would not be a significant design consideration 
based on the relatively flat topography of the project. 
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Foundation Design 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our seismic analysis, the very 
loose beach sand and lagoonal deposits have factors of safety of less than 1.0 against 
liquefaction for the design earthquake. Due to the potentially liquefiable soil at the project 
site, we recommend supporting the MBR Treatment Plant on a deep foundation system 
consisting of micropiles. A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 
12 inches) drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically 
is constructed by drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the 
borehole. Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, installation of

Based on the availability of equipment, we envision a micropile system with a 
minimum grout bulb diameter of 5.5 inches should be used. The load supporting capacity of

bonded zone and the underlying residual soil. We recommend designing each micropile 
based on an allowable compressive load capacity of 10 kips. The allowable compressive 
load capacity for the micropiles is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one 
third (1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces. 
We recommend that the micropiles extend through the very loose/soft alluvial, beach 
sand and lagoonal deposits and derive load bearing support from the underlying hard 
residual soil encountered at greater depths, as a bonded zone. The micropile length is based 
on a load bearing grout bulb (bonded zone) developed within the residual soil with an 
unbonded zone within the alluvial, beach sand and lagoonal deposits. 
To achieve the allowable compressive load capacity with a minimum factor of safety 
of 2.0, we estimate that each micropile would need to extend to a depth of about 32 feet 
below bottom of pile cap.  The micropile length is based on a minimum 5-foot long load 
bearing grout bulb (bonded zone) developed within the residual soil with 27 feet of 
permanent casing (unbonded zone) within the alluvial, beach sand and lagoonal deposits. 
To reduce risk of micropile “necking” within the very loose beach sand and lagoonal 
materials, we recommend about 27 feet of permanent steel casing be left in-place at the top 
of each micropile (unbounded zone).  The permanent casing should have a minimum 
outside diameter (OD) of about 5.5 inches (same as the grout bulb size), and the permanent 
casing should extend 1 foot into the residual soil. The center reinforcing steel bar should 
consist of a No. 9 (Grade 75) reinforcing bar with a cross sectional area of 1 square inch.

Downdrag Loads 
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction, settlement of the compressible 
deposits will occur at the project site. When the loose soil deposits consolidate, or 
move downward relative to the micropiles, the consolidation or downward movement 
will create negative skin friction forces, generally known as downdrag forces, acting 
on the micropile shafts. The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the 
thickness of the liquefiable layer, relative density of the potentially liquefiable 
deposits, and the micropile size. 
The allowable compressive load capacity of the micropiles has been reduced by the 
expected downdrag loads in the micropile foundation analyses. Based on our 
foundation analyses, we estimate the downdrag load imposed on the micropiles due

Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by shear along the grout bulb 
(bonded zone) and the adjacent soils.  In general, an uplift load capacity of up to 
5 kips per pile may be achieved with a bonded zone extending a minimum of 5 feet 
below the bottom of casing.  The uplift load capacity provided is intended for transient 
load applications only.  Therefore, the uplift load capacity should not be further 
increased for transient load applications.  For sustained uplift loads, we recommend 
reducing the uplift load capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist sustained 
uplift loads.  The capacity of the pile member in tension should be checked by the 
project structural engineer. 
Lateral Load Resistance 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile.  Therefore, the lateral 
load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected.  Lateral 
loads imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily by passive 
earth pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade beams 
and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams. 
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade 
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf) above the groundwater level.  Additional resistance 
to lateral loads may be provided by shear resistance between the sides of the pile 
caps and grade beams and the adjacent soils.  A side shear resistance of up to 
150 pounds per square feet (psf) may be used for transient loads such as wind or 
seismic forces only. 
Micropile Foundation Settlement 
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression 
of the pile member and subgrade response.  We estimate the total settlement of the 
micropile foundations to be less than 0.5 inches.  We believe that these settlements 
are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.

Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the micropile load capacity is highly dependent on the drilling 
procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to install the 
micropile.  Therefore, the micropile load capacity may vary considerably between 
different contractors and micropile foundation systems.  In order to determine 
whether the contractor’s methods of micropile installation are adequate and to 
determine the ultimate load capacity, we recommend performing one pre-production 
load test. In general, the purpose of the pre-production load test on a sacrificial 
micropile is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grout injection 
To develop criteria for determination of the bonded zone and evaluate 
the ultimate bonded stress 
We recommend that the pre-production load test micropile should consists of 5-foot 
bonded zone (grout bulb) and 27-foot unbonded zone (non-grout) with steel casing. 
Upon completion of the load testing, the steel casing may be removed after the 
unbonded zone is fully grouted. 
In addition to the pre-production load test, we also recommend performing pullout 
tests (proof tests) on selected production micropiles during construction to confirm 
the load carrying capacity of the installed micropiles. We recommend testing a 
minimum 10 percent of the total number of production micropiles or minimum of 
two micropiles, whichever is greater, for pullout.  
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate that noncritical structures, such as the planned 12-foot by 12-foot shed, 
will be supported on a reinforced concrete slab-on-grade.  Based on the existing topography 
and the anticipated finished floor elevation, we envision that the slabs-on-grade for the 
noncritical structures will be supported either on recompacted subgrade (fill) materials. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to about 1,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for design of the slabs-on-grade foundations bearing on recompacted subgrade 
soils or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations presented herein. This 
bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.

The subgrade beneath the slabs-on-grade should be re-compacted to at least

finished subgrade should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials.  The 
foundation over-excavation may be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to

Lateral loads acting on the slabs-on-grade may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the slab and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the slab. A coefficient of friction of 0.3 may be used for 
slabs bearing on the recompacted subgrade. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may 
be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pcf. This assumes that the soils 
around slabs are well compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. 
As mentioned previously, the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground 
settlements in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event (M6+).  
Seismically induced ground settlements on the order of about 7 inches may be anticipated, 
and the slabs-on-grade may sustain some damage.  Therefore, there exists the potential for 
damage and distress to the slabs-on-grade (not structurally supported).  However, we 
believe that the potential for damage may be significantly reduced by increasing the slab 
thickness and by incorporating steel reinforcement into the concrete slab. 
As discussed above, concrete slabs-on-grade may be subjected to ground 
settlements and distress in the event of seismically induced soil liquefaction.  As an 
alternative to slabs-on-grade, consideration may be given to structurally supporting the slabs 
on the micropile foundations to reduce the potential for damage to the slabs in the event of 
seismically induced ground settlements. 
Based on our field exploration, we anticipate low to moderate shrink/swell potential 
of the on-site, near-surface clayey soils. Therefore, we recommend providing a minimum 
12-inch thick capping fill layer consisting of non-expansive, select granular materials or 
select borrow subbase below the slabs. The non-expansive, select granular materials or 
select borrow subbase should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to 
at least 90 percent relative compaction. To reduce the potential for significant slab cracking 
due to concrete shrinkage, crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the 
width of the walkways with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled 
tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils.  These areas should 
also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the potential for water 
ponding around the slabs.

Underground Utility Lines 
It is anticipated that the installation of underground utilities will be needed for the 
project. In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and 
backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the 
method and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. The excavation should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal 
safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation. Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety 
requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open 
graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The initial trench 
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills, such 
as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of 
the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce the potential for 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement of 
the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes.  Where 
groundwater is encountered, the bedding should be wrapped on all sides by non-woven 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent). 
We envision that soft recent alluvium and/or loose beach sand deposits may be 
encountered at or near the invert elevations along some of the utility lines planned for the 
project.  Therefore, we recommend that a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of 
24 inches of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C 33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support.  The 
stabilization layer should also extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of one 
fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or 12 inches, whichever is greater. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill material. 
The select granular fill and/or on-site backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the 
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench 
backfill below any slab or pavement grades should be compacted to no less than 95 percent 
relative compaction.
