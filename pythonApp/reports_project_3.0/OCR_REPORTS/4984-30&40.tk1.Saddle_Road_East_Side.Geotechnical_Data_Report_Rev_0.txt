Regional Geology
The Hawaiian Archipelago consists of an elongated island chain comprising the
eight major islands of the State of Hawaii and hundreds of small islets and atolls
extending for more than 1 000 miles to the northwest of the Hawaiian Island of Niihau
The age of the islands in the Hawaiian Archipelago increases progressing from the
southeast toward the northwest direction
The Hawaiian Archipelago is believed to have formed as a result of plate tectonic
movement working in combination with a relatively deep stationary focal point of
volcanic heat and magma lava generation The Pacific Plate containing the upper
earth s crust underlying the Central Pacific Ocean continues to slowly move in a
northwesterly direction while the nearly stationary hot spot of magma generation
continues to produce eruptive emissions beneath the plate As a result a continuous
chain of volcanic islands formed and has slowly moved off in the northwesterly direction
carried away from the active center of volcanism by plate movement The Island of
Hawaii is presently located adjacent to the deep seated hot spot region The most
distant volcanic island remnants located far to the northwest of Hawaii have been
reduced to submerged seamounts as a result of a long history of land erosion and
subsidence
The Island of Hawaii the largest and youngest island in the Hawaiian
Archipelago covers an area of approximately 4 030 square miles The Island of Hawaii
was formed by the coalescence of five primary shield volcano mountains The five
primary shield volcanoes in decreasing geologic age include the following
Kohala located at the northern tip of the island which is long extinct
Mauna Kea located at the northern central portion of the island which
has had some activity during recent geologic time

Submarine volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii is believed to have
commenced in the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch about 1 to 2 million years
ago and continues today with eruptive activity at Kilauea Volcano It is believed that the
Island of Hawaii breached the ocean surface approximately 500 000 years ago Due to
the coalescence of the flanks of the volcanoes composing the Island of Hawaii the
older mountain flanks have been overlapped and buried by recent volcanic eruptions
therefore the evolution of the broad dome shape of the Island of Hawaii is visible today
The summit elevation of Mauna Kea Volcano resides at about 13 796 feet Mean
Sea Level MSL and the summit elevation of Mauna Loa Volcano resides at about
13 679 feet MSL The broad high elevation region of the Humuula Saddle is located
between the summits of the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes The elevation of
the Humuula Saddle region is on the order of approximately 6 500 feet MSL
As an illustration of the recent volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii over the
past 150 years or so Mauna Loa Volcano has averaged a caldera eruption
approximately every few decades Kilauea Volcano continues an on going active
eruption cycle which has spanned continuously since about 1983 Therefore the
southern and eastern portions of the Island of Hawaii remain volcanically active
whereas toward the north volcanism appears to be in its waning stages
A sixth volcano Loihi which is presently an active submarine volcano lies
offshore of the southeast coastline of the Island of Hawaii and may emerge some
hundreds of thousands of years in the future Due to the presence of ongoing volcanic
activity at and Loihi the Island of Hawaii is located in a zone of
active seismic influence The Island of Hawaii experiences frequent light to moderate
earthquakes associated with the movement of underground magma and shifting of the

Local Site Geology
The Saddle Road East Side Alignment between Milepost 11 and Milepost 5 6 is
on the broad easterly facing mountain slopes formed by the coalescence of lava flows
which erupted from the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes In the vicinity of the
proposed Saddle Road East Side Alignment the older weathered lava flows associated
with Mauna Kea Volcano are buried by younger lava flows that were erupted from
Mauna Loa Volcano in historic time and in the late Holocene Epoch These younger
lava flows generally extend from the summit region of Mauna Loa Volcano toward the
north and east directions The northerly flowing lavas banked against the southerly
facing flank of Mauna Kea Volcano and were diverted further downslope toward the
easterly direction where some of the historic flows approach the outskirts of Hilo The
exposed lava flows mapped at the ground surface along the project alignment are
shown on the Geology Map Plate 4
In sporadic locations where the youngest surface lava flows from Mauna Loa
Volcano are thin in coverage the older slightly more weathered lava rock formations
erupted from Mauna Loa may be exposed locally at the ground surface The existing
Saddle Road alignment between Milepost 11 and Milepost 5 6 generally was
constructed following the natural ground surface topography resulting from these recent
lava flows which extend east northeasterly down slope toward Hilo
Based on our geological literature review and geotechnical field exploration for
this project the Saddle Road East Side Alignment generally traverses surface basaltic
rock lava flows consisting mainly of pahoehoe type lava with some localized a a type
lava with very minimal surface soil exposure between Milepost 11 and Milepost 8 In the
Kaumana area at the far eastern end of the project from about MP 8 to the
alignment traverses ground that appears to be underlain by thick deposits of volcanic
ash materials that are located at the ground surface and potentially buried in the
shallow subsurface beneath some lava rock cover

and Kilauea Volcanoes has partially buried the older ash deposits leaving only isolated
pockets of surface exposure of the volcanic ash soils

In contrast pahoehoe lava flows typically encompass thinner less viscous lavas
that flow more rapidly as successive layers typically averaging about 3 to 10 feet in
thickness with only limited inter stratified clinker materials The pahoehoe lava flows
may be thicker where underlying topographic depressions were filled as a result of
ponded lava The pahoehoe lava flows also may be very thin where the lava streamed
faster over steeper underlying terrain Pahoehoe lava flows typically comprise lower
relief terrain generally consisting of low height hummocky ground containing frequent
open surface cracks and crevices derived from the cooling of the rock layers The
pahoehoe flow surface is generally hard with a limited mantle of clinker materials
Pahoehoe lavas generally are less porous than the surface clinker of a a lavas
however the multitude of open surface cracks readily permits water percolation into the
subsurface
Pahoehoe lavas typically contain internal flow cavities and or void spaces derived
from the transfer and cooling of molten rock within the flow core The cavities may occur
as localized pockets or as elongated lava tubes that may extend for significant
distances miles along the lava flow lobe Lava tubes represent the drained conduit that
supplied the advancing flow front with molten rock Lava tubes on the Island of Hawaii
may range in size from several feet to over 40 feet in diameter and may consist of tube
alignments that branch converge plunge and breach the ground surface The
supporting roof over the buried tube structure may be as thin as several feet and poses
as a potential geotechnical hazard for tube structure collapse and overlying ground
settlement
Based on our field exploration surface pahoehoe lavas were encountered at the
western end of the project site between about existing Milepost 11 and Milepost 8

Climate
Over the ocean near Hawaii rainfall averages between 25 and 30 inches a year
The islands receive as much as 15 times that amount in some places and less than
one third of it in others This phenomenon is caused mainly by orographic or mountain
induced rains which form within the moist trade wind air as it moves from the sea over
the high terrain of the islands Precipitation amounts typically are greatest over the
mid mountain slopes and hill crests and are least in the leeward lowlands On the higher
mountain slopes the belt of maximum trade wind induced rainfall typically lies between
about Elevations 2 000 and 3 000 feet MSL and precipitation amounts usually
decrease rapidly with higher elevations except during cyclonic storm conditions
The project site spans between about Elevation 2 850 feet MSL near
Milepost 11 and about Elevation 1 340 feet MSL near Milepost 5 6 Based on a review
of the rainfall distribution map presented in the Atlas of Hawaii Third Edition the mean
annual rainfall in the vicinity of the project site is relatively high to very high and ranges
from about 160 to over 240 inches of rainfall per year depending on the elevation of the
east facing mountain slopes The relatively high annual rainfall is the result of the
project site location with respect to a belt of maximum orographic precipitation just west
of Hilo

Lower precipitation often is experienced during the summer months of June and

Based on a review of Koppen climate information pertaining to the project site
and vicinity the project site spans a climatic transition zone between a coastal Humid
Tropical Af Climate and an inland upslope Temperate Climate Cfb Based on the
available maps the climate transition zone appears to be near the 3 000 to
4 000 foot MSL Elevation zone along the existing Saddle Road The Af climate regime
may be characterized as tropical continuously wet and encompasses the coastline and
lower mountain slopes westerly of Hilo including the majority of the project site The Cfb
climate regime may be characterized as continuously wet warm and temperate and
extends further upslope toward the Humuula Saddle area between the peaks of the
Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes
Based on our observations typical rainfall generally was observed to be light to
moderate in intensity consisting of fog drip and misty rainfall at elevations greater than
about 3 000 feet MSL Below approximately 3 000 feet MSL rainfall is typically of
greater intensity with frequent moderate to heavy precipitation Due to the close
proximity of the volcano summits and upland slopes afternoon heating of the landmass
may produce cumulus cloud buildups which result in locally heavier downpours that
generally terminate in the cooler early evening hours At elevations greater than about
5 000 feet MSL evaporation of moisture was observed to be appreciably greater
The average daytime air temperatures in degrees Fahrenheit range between
the 70s and 80s at the project site with abrupt temperature changes occurring with the
formation of afternoon clouds and rain showers Night time temperatures average in the
60s to 70s It should be noted that low cloud and fog formation is a common occurrence
especially between about Elevations 3 000 and 6 000 feet MSL

Geologic Material Type No 1
The Pahoehoe Basalt Rock Geologic Material Type No 1 typically consists of
more fluid lava that flowed more rapidly as successive flow layers averaging
about 3 to 10 feet in thickness with only limited inter stratified clinker seams The
lava flows may be thicker where underlying depressions were filled as a result of
ponded lava or very thin where the lava streams flowed readily down steeper
underlying terrain Pahoehoe lava flows typically comprise flatter terrain
containing lower topographic relief Pahoehoe lava typically forms hummocky
ground surfaces with frequent ground pits and surface cracks and crevices
derived from the deposition and cooling of the molten rock layers The pahoehoe
flow surface generally is hard with a limited mantle of clinker material Pahoehoe
lava flows generally are less porous than the surface clinker comprising a a
lavas however the multitude of open surface cracks readily permits water
percolation into the subsurface

Pahoehoe lavas typically contain internal flow cavities and or void spaces derived
from the transfer and cooling of molten rock within the flow core The cavities
may occur as localized pockets or as elongated lava tubes that may extend for
significant distances miles along the lava flow lobe Lava tubes represent the
drained conduit that supplied the advancing flow front with molten rock Lava
tubes may range in size from several feet to over 40 feet in diameter and may
consist of tube alignments that branch converge plunge and breach the ground
surface The supporting roof over the buried tube structure may be as thin as
several feet and poses as a potential geotechnical hazard for tube collapse and
overlying ground settlement
Geologic Material Type No 2
The A a Basalt Rock Geologic Material Type No 2 typically consists of thick
rubbly and irregular clinker surfaces consisting of loosely indurated welded
basalt rock fragments cobble to small boulder size with layer thickness
generally on the order of about 5 to 20 feet Below the clinker rubble surface
very dense and often massive basalt rock locally termed as blue rock may be
encountered The dense rock contains the core of the a a lava flow which may
range to several tens of feet in thickness The flows advanced down slope at a
slower pace due to the thickened viscous character of the lava therefore there
typically is greater surface topographic relief associated with the deposition of a a
lava flows The surface relief takes the form of rolling terrain encompassed by
steep sided elevated mounds of piled rocks located adjacent to topographic
depressions The surface of the a a lava flows generally is very porous due to the
rubbly clinker character which encompasses considerable void space between
the piled rock fragments

Based on our evaluation the individual pahoehoe lava flows may range in

and or voids associated with the movement of magma and cooling of the more
fluid lavas The voids may consist of large continuous lava tube features or
isolated blister pockets
Excavation of pahoehoe generally involves hard rock excavation methods which
may include blasting and the materials produced typically consist of boulders
and larger blocks of rock In general the hardness of pahoehoe basalt may be
estimated by the degree of inter connecting vesicularity small shallow textural
pits derived from exsolving gases during the cooling of magma with the softer
rock usually being less dense more porous as a result of the higher degree of
vesicle density A highly vesicular pahoehoe rock is usually less hard than
slightly vesicular rock The term scoriaceous is defined as highly vesicular
texture to the point of almost being pumice like in character Scoriaceous rock is
often representative of clinker materials that may be associated with the upper
and lower boundaries of individual pahoehoe lava flows
A a basalt typically consists of thicker individual lava flow units composed of a
substantial surface layer of clinker rubble overlying a dense core of massive
basalt material The clinker rubble generally consists of a loose to moderately
consolidated accumulation of basalt rock fragments ranging from gravel and
cobble size materials to some large blocks and plates of rubble on the order of
about 5 to 10 feet in diameter The clinker typically is very porous and may be
consolidated to a lower volume by traversing the clinker surface with heavy
construction equipment such as a heavy bulldozer The clinker materials

Test Boring Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions along the segment extending from about
Milepost 11 to Milepost 5 6 of the Hawaii State Route 200 Saddle Road by drilling and
sampling 47 borings designated as Boring Nos 201 through 247 The borings
extended to depths ranging from about 15 to 39 feet below the existing
ground pavement surface using truck and skid mounted drill rigs equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools The field exploration work for Boring
Nos 201 through 225 was conducted between August 12 and 21 2009 while the field
exploration work for Boring Nos 226 through 247 was conducted between February 1
and are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 11
Soil borings were advanced with a combination of 4 inch OD continuous flight
solid stem augers and continued with HQ size wireline coring methods to advance
through the rock formations After the borings were advanced to the maximum depth of
the boreholes the bored holes outside the pavement areas generally were backfilled
with the soil and or rock cuttings Borings within the pavement areas were backfilled
with the soil cuttings and grouted with Quikcrete concrete mix in the upper 12 inches
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are
described in the Soil Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 47
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D3550 Standard Practice for Thick Wall Ring Lined
Spilt Barrel Drive Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD modified California sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained
from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586 Standard Test
Method for Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts Relatively
undisturbed samples of the soft soil formation were obtained by pushing a 3 inch OD

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques consisting of an HQ wireline coring system
with double tube core barrel core size of 2 5 inches in general accordance with
ASTM D2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as
shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 3 The Rock Description System is based on
the publication Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in
Rock Masses by the International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977
Recovery REC may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses where appropriate Recovery is defined as the actual
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt
For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is
sound rock material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling mechanical and handling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5
feet of sound rock material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run in rock the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally
the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals by Robert S Carmichael

ReMi Data
The ReMi method uses the same instrumentation and field layout as a standard seismic refraction survey
However with this seismic method there are no predefined source points or any need for timed or triggered
seismic shots ReMi is classified as a passive surface wave technique That is the ReMi method uses ambient
noise or vibrational energy that exists at a site without the use of input energy from hammers or explosives like
those used on refraction and reflection seismic surveys or even multi channel analysis of surface wave MASW
seismic surveys Ambient energy can be anything from foot traffic to vehicles construction activities tidal
energy and microtremor earthquakes Either by active noise ambient energy and other cultural disturbances
these small strain vibrations cause surface wave energy to propagate up and down the line in both directions
These vibrations are the seismic energy that is recorded measured by the ReMi method Many of these noise
sources were not sufficiently present along the Saddle Road project site so active off end energy sources were
used to help create more coherent surface wave energy The ReMi sounding data were recorded utilizing 10 and
20 pound sledge hammers for a passive non triggered energy source These random sources were located

Data Processing
1D ReMi The ReMi method calculates the shear wave velocity Vs of layers and the respective depths to
interfaces beneath the seismic line as described by Louie 2001 The ReMi method is primarily used to calculate
shear velocity versus depth at a point 1D in order to differentiate between different soil and rock strata The
method is a surface wave technique that correlates the Rayleigh wave velocity with the subsurface shear wave
velocity
Noise records collected at this site were processed using the SeisOpt ReMi software
Louie 2001 The ReMi technique is based on two fundamental concepts The first is that seismic refraction
recording equipment can effectively record surface waves at frequencies as low as 2 Hz with the use of low
frequency geophones The second idea is that a simple two dimensional slowness frequency p f transform of a
microtremor record can separate Rayleigh surface waves from other seismic arrivals This separation allows
recognition of the true phase velocity and discriminates against energy propagating in other modes without
dispersion
There are four processing steps to derive the 2D ReMi Vs profiles
Step 1 Create a velocity spectrum or slowness p versus frequency f curve i e a p f plot from the noise
records The distinctive slope of dispersive surface waves is an advantage of the p f analysis body waves and
airwaves cannot have such a slope Even if most of the energy in a seismic record is in a phase other than
Rayleigh waves the p f analysis will identify the dispersion of the surface waves
Step 2 Pick the Rayleigh wave dispersion Picking is done along a lowest p f envelope bounding the dispersed
energy appearing on the p f image The picks thus discriminate against higher apparent velocities present when
along an envelope of the lowest phase velocities at each frequency has a further desirable effect Because higher
mode Rayleigh waves have phase velocities above those of the fundamental mode the refraction microtremor
technique preferentially yields the fundamental mode surface wave phase velocities

This algorithm produces results identical to those of the forward modeling computer codes
used by Iwata et al 1998 and by Xia et al 1999 within their inversion procedure The modeling iterates on
phase velocity at each period frequency The analysis approach and the propagation properties of surface
waves allow velocity reversals low Vs layers at depth to be modeled successfully
Step 4 Generate 2D shear wave profiles For this project 48 active channels were used for each setup during
data acquisition but processing allows for the selection of any 24 channels or other number of channels of each
original 48 to process to generate a given sounding Here the selection of 24 channels is incremented along the
seismic line by 4 channels 40 feet for each sounding achieving the same approach as one would using a roll
box in the field selecting 24 channels and only recording 24 channels at a time Once a series of 1D Vs
soundings have been modeled the data are entered into a contouring or smoothing algorithm that produces a 2D
smooth model from the 1D inverted models soundings The 2D profile represents a cross section of the
subsurface with soundings spaced at 40 feet for this project Initially an unsmoothed profile is generated
which only plots the 1D sounding without any smoothing operation applied then the smoothing is applied
appropriately for the anticipated geologic setting A concerted effort is taken during Step 4 to ensure that any
geologic or geotechnical data available are analyzed and integrated with the final processing step Soil stiffness
e g relative density from blow counts and rock competency from drill core RQD and depth that bedrock
was encountered are important parameters for modeling 1D Step 3 and generating 2D smooth ReMi profiles

Results from the 2D ReMi analysis for Lines 1 through 4 are presented on Figures 3 through 6 These figures
were generated in a manner that allows comparison from line to line that is each figure uses the same color

figure presents up to 1100 line feet of ReMi cross section Depth of investigation was focused on the upper
most 50ft Cross section stationing and elevations as shown on each figure were taken from the Track

feet
Where geotechnical borings were located along or near the 2D ReMi lines their locations and correlative
geologic sequences have been posted on the figures for integration of material types with the ReMi shear wave
velocity results These boring data were provided by Geolabs Two velocity contours have been placed on the
cross sections 1 a 600 feet per second ft s contour shown as a dashed line which represents an interpreted
transition from soft loose to stiff dense soil deposits and 2 a s contour shown as a solid line that is

boring log information provided by Geolabs indicate that basalt bedrock shear wave velocities range between
1 000 and s at this project site Fracturing and weathering significantly decreases the compressional and

Conclusions
The acquisition of 2D seismic data along portions of the proposed Saddle Road alignment provided imaging of
the overburden soils and the depth to rock layers bedrock The ReMi method was mostly selected because of its
ability to image velocity inversions low velocity layers beneath fast velocity layers and to distinguish between
soft soils stiffer soils and very hard sediments rock based on their shear wave velocity and correlations with
borehole data regardless of water saturation within soils The ReMi passive surface wave method is not affected
by the presence of a shallow water table which was important as much of the survey area receives rain on a
regular basis
This geophysical survey met the primary project objective delineating areas of soft soils This is corroborated by
geotechnical boring data Correlation with geologic logs for soft soil zones and depth to basaltic rock was very
good The ReMi data indicate material with shear wave velocities less than 600 ft s interpreted to be soft soils
Thee velocities are shown to be no more than 25 feet thick along the alignment segments tested There was
general agreement between the borehole data and geophysical data to reasonably state that the secondary survey
objective image the soil hard sediment basaltic rock contact was achieved Some variability of the shear wave
velocity is expected to exist in the transition zone from soils to weathered bedrock to competent bedrock The
