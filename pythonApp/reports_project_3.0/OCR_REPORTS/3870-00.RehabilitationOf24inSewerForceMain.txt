INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Rehabilitation of 24 Inch Sewer Force Main project in
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations are intended for the design of foundations shoring and
dewatering and site preparation only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided the existing 24 inch cast iron force main starts
at the Fort Shafter Sewage Pump Station SPS Building 1605 at Fort Shafter Flats and
connects to the City and County of Honolulu s Sand Island Wastewater Treatment Plant
WWTP on the island of Oahu Hawaii The force main rehabilitation project is
approximately 14 000 feet 2 6 miles long between the sewage pump station at Fort
Shafter and the Hart Street junction box at the Sand Island WWTP The approximate
location of the force main alignment is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The 24 inch force main was originally constructed in 1945 by the U S Army and
was subsequently modified by relocation and or replacement of the original line due to
line breakage Due to the deteriorated condition as the force main approaches the end
of its useful life expectancy studies were conducted in 1975 1984 and 1996 to evaluate
GEOLABS HAWAII

the existing condition of the force main and to recommend various remedial alternatives
for implementation
Because of the various construction constraints associated with open trench
construction methods such as subsurface utility conflicts relocation surface disturbance
to affected property owners and traveled roadways extensive shoring support
dewatering requirements and handling of potentially contaminated soils etc we
understand that two trenchless alternatives are being considered for the rehabilitation of
the existing force main including Cured In Place Pipe CIPP and Insertion Sliplining
method
Based on discussions and the recommendations presented by Wilson Okamoto
Associates Inc WOA in their Basis of Design for the subject project we understand that
the Insertion Sliplining method has more disadvantages than the CIPP method
Therefore Cured In Place Pipe is being proposed as the construction method to
rehabilitate the existing 24 inch sewer force main The CIPP method will require the
construction of insertion points or pressure manholes at selected locations along the force
main alignment Subsurface information will be needed at the insertion points to provide
data pertaining to the design of the foundation excavation and shoring system for the
pressure manhole structures
Because of the relatively shallow water table anticipated along the force main
alignment dewatering would be required during construction of the pressure manholes
Therefore three of the drilled borings were converted to monitoring wells for water
sampling pertinent to the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES
permit application In addition contaminated soils encountered during manhole
excavation if present will require special handling Therefore it is desired to perform
environmental laboratory testing on selected samples to evaluate the potential for subsoil
contamination

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of
Oahu has subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbot 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3870 00 Page 7
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under
relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly
valleys and drainageways

The Fort Shafter side of the existing force main and the new bypass alignment are
geologically situated on the southwest flank of the Koolau Volcanics These volcanics are
composed of basaltic lava flows of early Pleistocene Age The basalt is overlain by a
complex interlayered system of terrestrial and marine sedimentary deposits During the
later Pleistocene Age the sea level rose and fell several times as a result of
glacioeustatic fluctuations resulting in local swamp conditions which were later
superimposed by more recent deposits of terrestrial detritus of sand gravel silt and clay
eroded from the older rock of the area In more recent time the efforts of man have
shaped the low lying land to its present form by placement of fill in low areas
The existing sewer force main alignment in the vicinity of Nimitz Highway is located
on the seaward fringe of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The coastal plain is composed of
interbedded alluvial marine and post erosional volcanic deposits There is evidence that
a basaltic flow of the Honolulu Volcanic Series possibly originating from the Makuku vent
in Nuuanu or from Punchbowl reached the eastern portion of what is now Sand Island
During the Waipio stand of the sea at elevation 60 feet MSL this layer of basalt
would have provided a resistant surface which could have diverted the flows of Nuuanu
and Kapalama Streams to the west The diversion of these two streams would have
resulted in deposition of extensive subaerial sediments consisting of alluvial clays and
silts These exposed alluvial deposits would have become consolidated due to seasonal
moisture changes
Subsequently as the sea level rose to the Waimanalo stand at elevation
25 feet MSL and then fell to its present level the stream laid alluvium was buried
beneath backreef deposits of sands as a fringing coral reef grew and emerged on the
seaward margin of the island Land reclamation projects during this century have built
Sand Island to its current topography

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site traverses the Mapunupuna Fort Shafter Flats Kalihi Kai and
Sand Island areas of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project limits extend
from Moanalua Stream at Mokumoa Street in Mapunapuna to the Fort Shafter SPS
and continuing to the Sand Island WWTP as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
distance from the Fort Shafter SPS to the Sand Island WWTP is approximately
14 000 feet in length
From the Moanalua Stream crossing to the SPS at Fort Shafter Flats the
proposed alignment traverses generally flat undeveloped land with ground surface
elevations on the order of about 4 feet MSL rising to about 14 feet MSL in the vicinity
of the Fort Shafter SPS The western bank of Moanalua Stream is presently occupied
by a fenced lumber storage yard The eastern bank consists of undeveloped land
accessed by a dirt road from a nearby Army warehouse building and parking lot An
existing overhead electrical line easement runs parallel to the proposed sewer
alignment leading to the Fort Shafter SPS
Beginning at the Fort Shafter SPS the proposed alignment extends easterly to
cross the existing Kahauiki Stream channel and traverse the industrial area of Fort
Shafter Flats where storage offices and automobile maintenance facilities exist
The ground surface is generally flat with elevations on the order of about 10 feet MSL
The Kahauiki Stream channel is an earthen bank stream of low gradient The proposed
alignment traverses a paved roadway and open ground conditions extending to the
area beneath the Interstate Route H 1 Highway Viaduct
From Fort Shafter Flats the proposed alignment crosses beneath the Interstate
Route H 1 Highway Viaduct and Middle Street From Middle Street the alignment
traverses the congested Kalihi Kai industrial area before crossing beneath Kalihi
Stream to reach Dillingham Boulevard Ground surface elevations range from about
6 to 8 feet MSL The ground surface along this section of the alignment consists of

asphalt concrete pavement with some warehouse buildings and includes the Kalihi
Stream channel The Kalihi Stream channel is an earthen bank stream of low gradient
Between Dillingham Boulevard and Nimitz Highway the proposed alignment
traverses a congested industrial area occupied by warehouse structures and paved
driveways The ground surface elevations are on the order of about 7 feet MSL
After crossing Nimitz Highway the proposed alignment runs along the Ewa or
makai shoulder of Sand Island Access Road crosses the Kalihi Channel Bridge and

reach the WTP termination point Ground surface elevations range from about 5 to
10 feet MSL except at the elevated bridge crossing Kalihi Channel The proposed
alignment traverses terrain existing as asphalt concrete pavement and open ground
surface conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the existing sewer force main alignment appears to
be underlain primarily by fill materials over soft alluvial and loose lagoonal deposits at
shallow depths In addition shallow groundwater conditions between depths of about
4 7 and 10 3 feet below the surface were encountered in the borings drilled along the
existing force main alignment Therefore we envision that temporary shoring and
dewatering of the insertion point pressure manhole excavations will be required for the
project construction
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may be used for
temporary shoring at the insertion points especially where dewatering of the pits will be
necessary For shallow excavations and excavations in open areas we believe that
cantilever type sheet piling may be considered For deep excavations and excavations
located adjacent to buildings utilities and pavements we recommend that interlocking
steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing be considered for excavation support in order to
reduce the potential for adjacent ground movement
Based on our field exploration at both ends of the bypass crossing thick
compressible deposits consisting of soft lagoonal deposits were encountered to a depth
of about 90 feet below the ground surface Because of the moderate structural loads
anticipated for the bypass crossing a shallow foundation system for support of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3870 00 Page 13
proposed bypass crossing would be subjected to appreciable settlement on the order of
about 5 to 7 inches As a result we recommend that driven piles be used for the
foundation support of the proposed bypass crossing at Moanalua Stream
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we recommend that
12 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles embedding in the very stiff dense
materials encountered at greater depths be used to support the proposed new bypass
crossing An allowable compressive load capacity of about 30 tons with a preliminary pile
embedment depth of about 125 feet below the existing ground surface may be used for
the recommended pile foundation design We believe that the piles may be installed
without the need for predrilling Because of the potential for variation of subsurface
conditions such as the presence of boulders at the stream location we recommend that
predrilling be considered as a contingent item with additive and deductive unit prices to be
included in the contract documents
Due to the presence of soft clayey soils encountered in the boring drilled for the
bypass crossing we recommend that the new bypass line alignment alongside Moanalua
Stream be over excavated a minimum of 24 inches below the bedding layer in areas
where the soft soils are encountered at the pipe invert elevations The over excavation
should be replaced with No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped in a filter
fabric for more uniform support Based on our previous experience with similar soils the
construction methodology and equipment used may have a significant impact on the
proper installation of the new pipes underlain by soft clayey soils
In order to reduce the potential for pipe settlement we recommended that sewing
of the filter fabric and use of light compaction equipment be implemented during the
installation of the bypass alignment over soft soils Due to the highly sensitive nature of
the soft clayey soils some amount of settlement on the order of about 1 to 3 inches
should be expected even if these precautionary measures are observed during
construction If the anticipated settlement of the pipe is not acceptable it will be
necessary to consider supporting the new pipe on a pile foundation system Details of

Insertion Points Pressure Manholes
Our field exploration disclosed that the existing force main is generally underlain by
a surficial fill crust over soft recent alluvial and loose lagoonal deposits over the majority
of the alignment Shallow groundwater conditions between depths of about 4 7 and
10 3 feet below the surface were encountered in the borings drilled along the existing
force main alignment Therefore we envision that temporary shoring of the pressure
manhole excavations would be required for the project construction
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may be used for
temporary shoring at the insertion points especially where dewatering of the excavations
will be necessary For shallow excavations and excavations in open areas we believe
that cantilever type sheet piling may be used For deeper excavations and excavations
located adjacent to buildings utilities and pavements we recommend that interlocking
steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing be considered for excavation support in order to
reduce the potential for adjacent ground movement
A monitoring program should be developed and implemented by the Contractor to
detect ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the manhole excavations which
may result in damage to nearby structures and pavements It should be noted that during
and after installation of the sheet piles minor settlements may be possible Therefore it
is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the Contractor to
design and evaluate the shoring system used
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the insertion point pressure manhole excavations based on the subsurface
conditions encountered However it should be noted that existing abandoned
underground utilities and other below grade concrete structures may exist at the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3870 00 Page 15
site that would require heavy equipment for excavation or removal The method
and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the Contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in
order to reduce the potential for ground movement In addition the excavated
soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from any underground utilities to
reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence which may damage the
underground utility lines
Excavation Support
Based on the information provided to our office the insertion points pressure
manholes may extend to depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the existing ground
surface Based on the generally poor subsoil conditions encountered in our
borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavations will be necessary
to protect personnel working in the excavations Because a majority of the existing
force main alignment will be running along the existing roadway sloping of the
sides for an open cut excavation will not be possible
Temporary shoring of the insertion point pressure manhole excavations using
sheet pile shoring especially where dewatering of the excavations will be
necessary may be considered However it should be noted that the existing force
main alignment may likely traverse other utility lines therefore care should be
exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility lines if sheet piles are to be used
as shoring If sheet pile shoring is used it would also serve as a cut off wall for the
dewatering operations Therefore the sheet piles should be driven with a suitable
hammer to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence

As previously recommended the pipes and manhole structures over soft soils

fabric placed along the pipe alignment under dry conditions If the filter fabric is
not properly overlapped it is possible that the gravel placed may migrate through
the openings in the filter fabric and mixed with the underlying soft soils This may
result in settlement of the ductile iron pipe due to loss of cushion materials into the
soft subsoils
Since the construction below the pipe invert may be performed in wet conditions
especially for the portion of the bypass alignment with an invert elevation of 2 feet
MSL we envision that it would be difficult to observe the fabric installation process
below the groundwater level In addition we believe that it would be difficult for the
Contractor to assure that the proper minimum overlap is maintained Therefore it
is our opinion that if placement of the filter fabric in wet conditions is necessary the
seams of the fabric should be sewn to provide higher assurance that gaps will not
occur in the filter fabric As a result we recommend that sewing the seams of the
filter fabric be incorporated into the construction process to reduce the potential for
large scale settlements

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the existing sewer force main and new
bypass alignments were explored by drilling and sampling of sixteen borings
Boring Nos 1 through 16 to depths ranging from about 15 to 163 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
hollow stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who observed the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 16 1
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the

would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Geotechnical Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Twelve sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are presented on
Plates B 2 through B 4
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 5
through B 7
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on site soils The test results are
presented on Plate B 8

APPENDIX C
Environmental Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Environmental Laboratory Testing
As part of our scope of work on this project selected soil samples along the
existing 24 inch sewer force main alignment were obtained during our field exploration
and tested for indication of potential contamination by leakage from existing underground
fuel lines or other external paths A total of four soil samples were retrieved at selected
depths from Boring 12 and 13 for environmental laboratory testing The
sampling tools were washed with Liquinox detergent solution prior to each soil sampling
event
The environmental laboratory testing were performed by Environmental Laboratory
of the Pacific and included Total Purgeable Petroleum Hydrocarbons with BTEX
Distinction Total Extractable Petroleum Hydrocarbons Polynuclear Aromatic
Hydrocarbons Cadmium and Lead Results of the laboratory tests are provided in
Appendix C
