INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed apartment building located at 828 and 834 Lukepane Avenue
in Honolulu Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings resulting from our boring exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analysis based on the data
obtained from the field exploration and laboratory testing These recommendations are
intended for design input and are not intended for contractor s use for construction
estimation or bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Kapahulu area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii
The proposed project involves the construction of a four story apartment building
over an on grade parking area at the project site A park area is also planned on the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 3
ground floor It is our understanding that the building will be of concrete and masonry
construction with maximum column loads up to 275 kips
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the project site to develop a generalized soil rock data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the foundation and
earthwork design of the proposed apartment development
The work was done generally in accordance with our proposals of October 22 and
The scope of work was limited to
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and operators
to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of four 4 borings at the building site to depths from
45 0 to 61 0 feet below the existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a Field
Geologist from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
to determine the classification and pertinent engineering properties of the
materials encountered

5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to the proposed apartment building
development

7 Coordination of our overall work on the project and client design team
consultation by a Principal Engineer of our firm
8 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It is Our Opinion that the proposed apartment building may be constructed provided
our geotechnical engineering recommendations as presented in this report are followed
It appears that the underlying medium stiff to very soft alluvial deposits cannot
provide adequate support for shallow special footing foundations without inducing
excessive settlements under the proposed structure For the anticipated maximum
column load up to 275 kips estimated settlements for conventional spread footings are
the amount of settlement deep pile foundations embedded in the basalt formation are
recommended to provide foundation support of the project
A mat foundation may also be considered to reduce the amount of settlements due
to the anticipated structural loads However because the soft subsoils encountered in
two 2 borings could vary throughout the site we feel that a lesser amount of risk and
uncertainty is associated with the use of pile foundations
Pile Foundation
To reduce the potential settlements of the proposed structure pile foundations are
recommended
Pile Capacities
piles with an allowable vertical pile capacity of 70 tons per pile may be used to
support the proposed structure The piles would develop their load capacity
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 7
primarily from end bearing on the underlying basalt rock formation encountered at
depths of about 35 to 47 feet below the existing ground surface or at elevations
of 23 4 to 35 feet below Mean Sea Level
A minimum pile spacing of about three 3 feet on centers is recommended
An allowable lateral pile capacity of up to 12 kips per pile may be used in the
design
Pile settlements on the order of less than one half of an inch are anticipated
Pile Lengths
Based on the exploration results the estimated pile lengths may vary from
about 35 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface For preliminary estimation
an average pile length of about 43 feet may be used
The above estimated pile lengths are given for initial cost estimation purposes only
The actual production pile lengths should be determined after completion of a test
pile driving program
Pile Driving
We recommend that a pile driving hammer which can deliver a minimum of
32 000 foot pounds of energy be used for the pile driving The piles should be
driven to a resistance of about 100 blows per foot for the last foot of penetration
or 10 blows per inch for the last three 3 inches
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 8
The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile A pile
may be rejected or its design capacity may be modified if the driving process is
interrupted for more than four 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer stroke
or energy should be carefully controlled so that excessive tensile stresses are not
developed in the pile
The pile driving should be observed by a qualified geotechnical engineer to see
that the piles are driven to the sufficient depth or into competent bearing materials
Test Pile Program
A test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following objectives
1 To more accurately determine the production pile length s
2 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the pile driving criteria
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the test pile driving program
should consist of a minimum of ten 10 percent of the total pile numbers required
for the project The test piles may later be incorporated into the actual foundation
system
When the final foundation plan is available the project geotechnical engineer
should be consulted for selection of the test pile locations Generally the test piles
should be casted about ten 10 feet longer than the estimated pile lengths
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 9
Splice cans should be provided at the site during test pile driving to expedite
splicing if required Splice cans may be fabricated of high grade steel one fourth
Y of an inch thick and should be at least six 6 feet long
As part of the test pile driving program a minimum of one 1 test pile load test
should be budgeted The selected pile should be load tested to twice the
allowable load capacity in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1143
The test piles should be driven with the same hammer which will be used for
production pile driving later The test pile program should be conducted under
close and continuous field observation of the project geotechnical engineer who
will determine when piles may be stopped and to provide guidance should
additional measures be required due to unforeseen driving conditions
The geotechnical engineer should be consulted to evaluate the final pile load
carrying capacity and production pile lengths based on the results of the test pile
driving and the pile load test
Mat Foundation
To reduce the amount of potential settlements of the proposed structure a mat
foundation bearing on the medium stiff to stiff alluvial layer may be considered for the
project The mat foundation should be embedded a minimum depth of four 4 feet below
the lowest adjacent exterior grade
To provide more uniform bearing support for the base of the mat foundation the
soil beneath the mat foundation should be over excavated for a depth of 12 inches then
the bottom of the excavation should be proof rolled and recompacted to a minimum of
90 of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 10
D 1557 78 The excavation should then be backfilled and replaced with well graded select
granular material such as aggregate base course material compacted to a minimum of
95 of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78
For the design of a mat foundation bearing directly on the above compacted gravel
cushion an allowable bearing capacity of 2 000 p s f and a modulus of subgrade reaction
of 10 kcf may be used Based on our exploration and analyses we estimate that
settlements under the anticipated structural loads could be on the order of one 1 to one
and one half 11 inches
However Since soft loose subsoils were encountered in two 2 of the four 4
borings drilled for our exploration it is possible that these conditions could vary
throughout the site Therefore we believe that the use of end bearing piles is the
preferable foundation system
Site Grading
Minor site grading work is anticipated
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared of vegetation debris rubbish and other deleterious materials These materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Due to the moderately high expansion potential of the subsoils encountered near
the existing ground surface we recommend that the on site clayey subgrade soils at the
footing bottoms and below the slab subgrade level for footings and slabs not supported
on the pile or mat foundation be over excavated to a depth of at least eighteen 18
inches and backfilled with select non expansive material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 11
Imported fill material to replace the over excavated material should consist of non
expansive select granular materials less than three 3 inches in maximum size with less
than 20 passing the 200 sieve Any imported fill material should be tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to transportation to the site
The replacement fill should be placed in maximum 10 inch thick loose level lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78
The site should be graded to drain water away from the foundations

Retaining walls are anticipated for the proposed elevator structure The guidelines
for the retaining wall designs are listed below
1 The following lateral earth pressures may be used
Walls restrained at top 55 p c f equivalent
at rest case fluid pressure
Additional load due to traffic or surcharge should be included where
applicable
An allowable bearing value of up to 2000 p s f is recommended for
foundations bearing on the stiff on site soil
2 A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used for
the wail footing on level areas

3 A base friction factor of 0 4 may also be used to determine the resistance
of the wall against sliding
4 The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade On sloping
areas the wall footing should be embedded an additional amount to provide
a minimum 10 foot horizontal set back distance measured from the outer
edge of the wall footing to the slope face
5 Select granular backfill material such as ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel 3B
Fine is recommended behind the retaining wall to minimize excessive
compaction required and to aid in the drainage of the area behind the wall
The granular backfill should have a minimum horizontal thickness of 18
inches from the back face of the wall
The granular backfill should extend to a height of two 2 feet below finish
grade The remaining backfill should consist of an impervious capping of
non expansive soils with a ring swell of not greater than 2
6 A subsurface drainage system subdrains or weepholes should be utilized
behind the walls Subdrains should be properly constructed to drain water
away from the wall

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling four 4 borings to
depths ranging between 45 0 to 61 0 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted rig was used to
drill the borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations The classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A summary of the materials encountered are presented on the
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a standard penetration
sampler or a 2 4 inch D split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Boring
Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 90 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
Structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These results are summarized on Plate B 1
