REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
GEOLABS INC
W O 3980 20 Page 5
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield
Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield reached the end of its main
shield building phase about 2 million years ago Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast These alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded
with marine sediments and corai algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging
in thickness from zero feet in the area of Interstate Route H 1 to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach
This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the
artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from
earlier geologic time through the present
The project site is located on the northern half of the Waipio Peninsula and is
composed of a composite of alluvial sediments interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations as described above and of man made fills Man made fills were
generally placed in marshy areas to raise the grade and extend the limits of useable land
These man made fills generally consist of sandy clay material obtained from dredging or
excavations from nearby areas overlying garbage bagasse and slurry from sugar mills The
lower marshy soils generally consist of highly organic muck or peat material that accumulated
from organic marine sediments that deposited near coastal areas due to the low energy
environment

Excavation and Shoring
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The side slopes of
excavations must comply with applicable federal state and local requirements In general
the excavated soils should not be stockpiled within the zone of a 45 degree imaginary line
projected upward from the toe of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground
movement or subsidence toward the excavation
Where space is available an open cut excavation may be used to construct the various
structures A slope inclination of or flatter may be used for temporary cuts however
it should be noted that sloughing of the sides of the excavation may still be experienced during
construction This should be further evaluated by a qualified geotechnical engineer retained
by the contractor during construction
Where an open cut excavation is not possible the excavation for construction of the
structures will need to be adequately shored to reduce the potential for adjacent ground
movement The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable
safety requirements Two possible methods of shoring would be to use a sheetpile system
braced or free standing or a soldier pile and lagging system It is recommended that a
qualified engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to be continuously
present on site during excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsoil
conditions unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or
techniques etc which may affect shoring stability

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with a moderate swelling potential and a high shrinkage potential
Unless floor slabs are properly designed and constructed there is a potential for future distress
to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these soils due to
changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for distress to slabs on grade
resulting from swelling and shrinking of the clayey materials we recommend removal of the
clayey materials within 18 inches below the bottoms of interior and exterior i e sidewalks and
utility concrete pads slabs on grade The over excavation should be filled with properly
compacted select granular fill

Gravel pavements are anticipated for the proposed access road to the pump station

We anticipate that some of the proposed gravel pavement alignment may traverse the

2 The soft subgrade should be over excavated by 30 inches and replaced with a

Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project The
methods and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with all
applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation
should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should conform to

In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining
backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill immediately around utility
pipes is not recommended
For utility excavations that encounter soft soils or dark gray soils we recommend that
the No 3B Fine gravel bedding thickness be increased to 12 inches and wrapped with a
geotextile fabric AMOCO 4552 or similar
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site silty cinder sand or volcanic tuff or
select granular material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to a minimum
of 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The

Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to the building structures should be sloped to shed water
away from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised that gutter systems be installed around the building structures and that the discharge
be at least 3 feet from the foundations and diverted away from the foundation and pavement
areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided
Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to
reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Gravel strips adjacent to the building
structures should not be allowed
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the
potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from walls and foundations
