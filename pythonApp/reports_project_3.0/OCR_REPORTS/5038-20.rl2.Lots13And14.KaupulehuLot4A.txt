Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago covering an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai last erupted in Loa and Kilauea both still
active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation observed at the project
site prior to site grading activities appears to consist of both a a and pahoehoe type lava
flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava typically is
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders A a lava is generally
present at the ground surface of the project site Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a
smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially the pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets are generally
smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers Because the permeability of the clinker and cavities is high they
serve as the primary water transmission features
Subsurface Conditions
As mentioned previously the Kaupulehu Lot 4A Increment 1 project site is in the
Kaupulehu area in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii Generally the
original topography of the project site prior to site grading activities consisted of irregular
a a lava flow terrain at the ground surface
Based on observations during the mass grading operations the project site
generally is underlain by a a basalt at the original ground surface and medium hard to hard
basalt formation at greater depths It should be noted that lava tubes cavities and blisters
are commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows in the project vicinity In addition lava
tube features were observed at the project site during the mass grading operations It
should be noted that near surface lava tubes detected within the limits of grading for the
mass grading operations generally were collapsed and the loose materials from within the
collapsed lava tube were removed The resulting excavation was backfilled with

Discussion and Recommendations
Based on the updated grading plan for the project the original ground surface
elevations at Lot Nos 13 and 14 ranged from about 33 to 38 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL Lot Nos 13 and 14 generally were graded to finished pad elevations of about
36 0 feet MSL and 38 5 feet MSL respectively Grading at the two lots generally
consisted of minor excavations of up to about 1 foot deep and fills of up to about 5 feet
thick to achieve the design finished grades
During the mass grading operations extensive subgrade preparation procedures
were employed within the limits of grading at the commencement of the mass grading
operations for the project to reduce the potential for collapse of near surface cavities
and or voids within the lots The intent of the subgrade preparation procedures is to assist
in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials It should be noted that the subgrade preparation procedures
employed for this project are consistent with the standard of practice for similar type
projects and similar subsurface conditions along the Kohala Coast on the Island of Hawaii
In general the subgrade preparation in fill areas consisted of ripping the original
ground to a depth of about 3 feet or until refusal using a ripper tooth of a large bulldozer to
assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the
porous near surface materials After the fill surfaces have been ripped the ground
generally was leveled with the use of a Komatsu 375 and or Komatsu 475 bulldozer
working the surface for about four passes to provide a relatively level surface After
leveling of the rugged surfaces with the bulldozer the fill subgrades generally were
proof rolled with a Caterpillar 825 sheepsfoot roller compactor minimum 20 tons for about
eight passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids
In general Lot Nos 13 and 14 were graded to a fill condition with a small portion
at the southern corner of Lot No 13 graded to a cut condition Subgrades in the cut
areas generally were proof rolled with a Caterpillar 825 sheepsfoot roller compactor
minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to help detect and collapse near surface
cavities and or voids Therefore the presence of on site a a basalt should be anticipated
at or near the existing finished pad elevation at some portions of the lots
Based on the observations of our field representatives it is our professional opinion
that the earthwork operations during the mass grading for the lots observed by Geolabs in
the period covered in this report were performed in general conformance with the intent of
the project plans and specifications and the geotechnical recommendations presented in
our geotechnical engineering report
It should be noted this statement of professional opinion is based on the grades
existing at the completion of the mass grading at Lot Nos 13 and 14 in April 2005
