exploration performed for the proposed Life Skills Clinic

1 2 Project Considerations

is presently occupying space in a deteriorating wood framed plywood sheathed 1952
building Building 1105 within the Hickam AFB on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
building which also houses the Family Support Center has undergone numerous repairs
over the years leaving its finishes scarred and patched The original utility systems are
maintenance intensive and have exceeded their useful lifespan In addition sound
insulation is non existent in the building and results in lack of patient privacy

Skills Clinic facility at the corner of Signer Boulevard and Seventh Street The new facility
will be connected to the existing Medical Dental Clinic Building 559 by walkways The
new building will be a one story steel framed structure with concrete floor stucco exterior
and tile roof As part of the project development a new parking lot will also be constructed
to serve the new facility

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two large shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active thus Waianae s eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas
in the central Oahu region
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced less water
was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded a larger volume of water became
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level Thus the island
shorelines have transgressed and regressed in response to the fluctuations The
processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments were
affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the
erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths
below the present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were carried further
seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments When the sea level
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at
higher elevations In addition it allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as
coral and limestone in environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils and
rock
Interpretation of the historic geology of the coastal plain regions located on the
Island of Oahu such as the Hickam AFB project site is complex due to a long history of
changes in sea level that occurred in relation to the island land surface The overall
changes in sea level in relation to the land surface resulted from a combination of tectonic

forces such as uplift and settlement of the island landmass and the previously discussed
glacio eustatic ocean level changes In other words the rise and fall of sea level in
relation to the island produced shorelines of emergence and submergence In general
the overall changes of sea level in relation to the landmass also caused the erosional base
level to fluctuate producing features such as drowned stream valleys filled with marine
deposits and conversely incised marine deposits filled with terrestrial sediments or rock
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level changes
and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch Following the
cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The ancient sea level fluctuated several times both above and below the present
sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Epoch eruption
of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older
alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff
locally called mudrock layers Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those
mantles of tuff

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 2 0 and
6 2 meters below the existing ground surface A bulk sample designated as Bulk 1 of the
near surface soils was obtained to evaluate the physical and pavement support
characteristics The approximate locations of the borings drilled and the near surface bulk
sample collected are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain

proximity of the site to Pearl Harbor groundwater levels at the site will likely be affected by
tidal fluctuations and storm surge conditions In addition the groundwater levels are also
subject to change due to rainfall time of year seasonal precipitation surface runoff and
other factors

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the medium dense subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe
that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed
new building construction An allowable bearing pressure on the order of about
may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the
recompacted in situ silty sand soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ medium dense soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with on site granular soils compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying
competent materials
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 450 millimeters below the
lowest adjacent finished grades Footings located adjacent to planned or existing
retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall
foundations or the planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional
structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the footings should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge
of the utility trench or the footing should be embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the construction of
the new facility Based on our field and laboratory test results we believe that new
building slabs may be constructed on the in situ silty sands provided that the slab
subgrade is properly prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented below
and in the Site Preparation section of this report
In general we recommend that a minimum 100 mm thick layer of cushion fill
consisting of No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs
The open graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would
serve as a Capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration
through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture

3 3 Site Preparation
Based on the relatively flat topography at the project site we anticipate that the
majority of the site preparation work for the proposed project will consist of minor cuts and
fills to achieve the design finish grades As mentioned previously the project site is
generally underlain by near surface fills with potential presence for localized loose zones
Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation for the project
construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a designated area to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials with the cleared and
grubbed spoils In addition the surface clayey topsoil within the building and parking limits
should be removed to expose the underlying silty sand soils Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material moisture conditioned to
above optimum moisture content placed in 150 to 200 mm level loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned
in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete
under low pressure During our field exploration we have noted that the proposed building
location is traversed by an underground sewer line We understand that the sewer line will
be relocated outside the new building limits prior to the building construction Special
attention should be given to the backfilling operation of the excavated trench for proper
backfill placement and compaction

After clearing and grubbing the excavated subgrades underneath future slab and
pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of about 300 millimeters
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled with a
minimum 9 metric ton vibratory drum roller to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction to detect the presence of potential near surface loose zones The vibratory
drum roller should travel at a speed of about 60 to 90 meters per minute The proof rolling
operations should extend a minimum of 2 meters beyond the building and pavement
footprint of the new facility If the proof rolling operations detect a near surface loose
zone the loose materials should be removed to expose the underlying firm ground and
the resulting over excavation should then be backfilled with compacted granular fill
material
Fill materials required for the project construction should consist of non expansive
select granular material such as the crushed coral or basalt The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 75 millimeters in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of one percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The on site silty sand soils should be suitable for use as
non expansive select granular fill material provided that they are processed to meet the
above requirements Imported fill materials should be tested by a qualified geotechnical
engineer for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site
for the intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 250 millimeters in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density

3 5 Utility Trenches
We envision that new utility lines will be required to serve the proposed new facility
In general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the new utilities at the site The method and equipment to be used for
trench excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the applicable local
safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility of
the contractor
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 150 millimeters of
coral sands or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support
under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 300 millimeters above the pipes or 300 millimeters above the groundwater level to
provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 millimeters above the
pipes or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of
select granular material such as the on site silty sands The backfill should be placed in
maximum 250 mm level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground
subsidence

3 7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations in sandy soils should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to develop its bearing capacity to support the design footing load Drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
runoff away from the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 2 0 and
6 2 meters below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 75 mm
OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 150 mm of a 450 mm drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
