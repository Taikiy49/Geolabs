Our exploratory borings drilled near the top of the existing slope generally 
encountered a residual soil layer ranging in thickness from about 27 to 34 feet. The 
residual soil layer was underlain by saprolite and basalt rock formation extending to 
180 feet below the existing ground surface.  The residual soil consisted of stiff to hard 
clayey silt and sandy silt.  The saprolite was composed of medium dense to very dense 
silty sand and stiff to very stiff clayey silt and silty clay with traces of fine sand and 
completely weathered basalt fragments.  The basalt rock formation was soft to very hard.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our field observations during our slope reconnaissance and borings, 
slope stabilization and rockfall mitigation measures consisting of rock slope scaling and 
the installation of draped and anchored wire mesh systems on the slope are 
recommended.  The slope stabilization area is from about Sta. No. 35+00 to Sta. No. 
44+25 and exclusive of the existing waterfall area. 
Prior to the installation of the wire mesh, the existing slope should be cleared and 
grubbed, and rock slope scaling should be performed to remove loose rocks and 
precariously positioned rock fragments from the slope face.  Rock slope scaling work 
should be performed by an experienced crew and typically proceeds from the top of the 
slope and working downward in a controlled and orderly manner.  The rock slope scaling 
will typically require temporary closure of the roadway during the time that rock is being 
scaled or removed from the slope.  We understand that it is desired to have the roadway 
remain open during the slope scaling work.  Utilization of a movable temporary rockfall 
barrier within the active scaling area may be considered. 
The installation of a draped wire mesh system over the slope stabilization area is 
recommended to assist in the controlled release of loose rock that is shed from the slope 
face during the weathering and erosion process.  We anticipate the majority of the large 
blocks of rock and boulders would be removed following the slope scaling effort.  In the 
lower area at the western portion of the slope, we understand that this area is prone to 
falling rocks.  Therefore, the installation of an anchored wire mesh system is 
recommended in this area.  The installation of the anchored wire mesh system should be 
performed prior to the installation of the draped wire mesh system.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late 
Pliocene Epoch (more than 2¼ million years ago). Following the cessation of this main 
shield building phase, renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic 
lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of 
alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon 
Volcanic Series. These lavas consist of four distinct formations:  Napali, Olokele, 
Haupu, and Makaweli. These formations are comprised of thin-bedded a’a and 
pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with 
the vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
During the Pliestocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread continental glaciation. As the great continental glaciers 
accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the 
oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose 
because more water was available. The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable 
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to 
present sea level of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The project site is underlain by stiff to very hard residual soil followed by medium 
dense to very dense and stiff to very stiff saprolite, and soft to very hard basalt rock 
formation extending to the maximum explored depth of 180 feet below the existing 
ground surface near the top of the slope. We did not encounter groundwater in the 
drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our field observations and borings, slope stabilization and rockfall 
mitigation measures consisting of rock slope scaling and installation of draped and 
anchored wire mesh are recommended. Detailed discussions of these items and our 
geotechnical recommendations for design of the project are presented in the following 
sections. 
3.1 
Slope Failure and Rockfall Mechanisms 
Based on our understanding of past events at the project site and our site 
reconnaissance at the project site, it appears that there are three main mechanisms 
generating slope failure and rockfall events at the site.  The three mechanisms related 
to slope failure and rockfall are a large amount of surface water on the slope face, 
raveling failure, boulder fall, and differential erosion of alternating volcanic rock layers. 
The major slope failures at the project site appear to be shallow slope failures 
and not deep-seated slope failures. This type of slope failure is typically due to 
saturation of the near-surface soils creating weakened planes susceptible to shallow 
slope failure.  We believe that the slope failures observed at the project site were 
caused during the heavy rainfall event in March 2012 when the blocked drainage culvert 
at the top of the slope area failed and released a significant amount of water down the 
slope face.   
Raveling of one or several rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of 
causes.  The hazard develops when the raveling rock has sufficient velocity and volume 
to reach the roadway surface.  Raveling may be caused by water pressure behind the 
rock, differential weathering or erosion along faults or shear zones, weakening of rock 
due to temperature and moisture cyclic fluctuations, and loosening by animals or trees

and wind.  Raveling also may result from ground vibration.  Due to the weathered 
condition of the basalt rock slope surfaces at the project area, raveling is an important 
process that continually works to shed weathered and broken rock materials from the 
slopes. 
One of the most frequent causes of raveling is by invasive tree roots prying on 
the existing cracks located in the rock slope surface.  Tree roots (especially of the larger 
trees) frequently develop and grow larger in the discontinuities of rock materials.  The 
added force of wind action on tree limbs and branches may create leveraged forces that 
are transferred to the roots lodged within the discontinuity.  Over time, the repetitive 
leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock and cause it to fall.  Because of the 
numerous large trees at the project site, we believe that tree root pry is a significant 
mechanism for creating rock slope instability because of the hardy growth of trees and 
vegetation on the slope. 
In addition to raveling process, inter-layered rock with variable strength and rates 
of weathering commonly exhibit accelerated erosion and rockfall.  The weaker layers 
tend to erode and undercut the more weathering resistant rock located above.  This 
may lead to raveling of the harder layers or failure of undercut blocks of rock as support 
from the underlying weaker layers is removed.  This type of rockfall mechanism is 
especially common on the west side of the waterfall due to the alternating thin to 
medium thick layers of basalt rock composed of clinker and hard rock seams. 
We understand that the County of Kauai will be repairing the damaged drainage 
culvert near the top of the slope and that surface water from heavy rainfall will be 
flowing over the existing waterfall and not over the slope areas.  Therefore, our 
recommendations provided in this report are based on the above understanding.  Based 
on the mechanisms of rockfall observed at the project site, we believe that one of the 
most viable alternatives for a rockfall protection scheme for the rockfall hazard areas 
would consist of scaling the rock slope and the installation of a draped wire mesh to 
control falling rock from the slope face.  It should be noted that installation of a draped 
wire mesh over the slope face of the designated rockfall hazard areas is intended only 
to control the release of loose rock that may develop on the slope face in the years

3.2 
Rockfall Mitigation Measures 
Based on field observations and discussions with the State of Hawaii Department 
of Transportation personnel, there appear to be numerous instances of rocks falling on 
the sides and on the roadway and creating potentially hazardous conditions.   
Based on our observation of the existing site conditions, we believe that the 
potential exists for falling rock to occur at the project site.  Therefore, priority should be 
assigned to the implementation of a rockfall protection system at the project site.  We 
had previously recommended that a temporary rockfall protection system be installed 
until installation of a long-term rockfall protection system.  We understand that a 
temporary rockfall protection system has been installed at the project site. 
There are two potentially viable options for development of a long-term rockfall 
protection system for the Kalihiwai site.  The first option consists of major scaling of the 
loose rocks from the slope face only.  The second option consists of major scaling of the 
loose rocks from the slope face and installation of draped and anchored wire mesh on 
the prepared slope surface.  Because the weathering of rock is an ongoing process of 
erosion, the anticipated useful life of slope scaling alone is estimated to be generally 
less than about 5 to 6 years, after which time the slope likely will need to be scaled 
again to remove the build-up of loose rock. 
With the implementation of scaling and installation of draped and anchored 
mesh, the useful life of the improvements may be increased to about 10 to 15 years (or 
the life of the draped and anchored wire mesh based on the corrosive environment at

the project site).  The second option consisting of major scaling of the loose rocks and 
installation of draped and anchored wire mesh on the prepared slope surface is the

The installation of draped and anchored wire mesh covering the slope face 
serves to control the release of loose rock that may develop on the slope face in the

Therefore, the draped and anchored wire mesh should help retain some loose rock

draped wire mesh cover essentially contains broken rock material against the slope face 
as the fallen rock materials move downward by gravity.  In this way, the rock fragments 
are deposited along the mesh outlet located at the base of the slope.  Normally, a 
narrow containment area located along the road shoulder should be provided to collect 
the accumulation of debris and allow for periodic mechanized cleanup. 
3.3 
Rock Slope Scaling 
Rock slope scaling consists of experienced crews employing manual labor and 
hand tools to remove loose rocks and precariously positioned rock fragments from the 
slope face.  The scaling crews performing the rock slope scaling should be experienced 
in the performance of rock slope scaling by rappelling and/or working from crane 
baskets.  Rock slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting from the top of the 
slope and working downward in a controlled and orderly manner in conjunction with the 
combined progress of all the scaling crews working on the slope face.  Rock slope 
scaling should be typically performed on slopes with a slope inclination steeper than 
about two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V). 
In general, each rock slope scaling crew would consist of a scaling supervisor, 
who should be a specialist in rock slope scaling, and three experienced rock slope 
scalers.  The scaling supervisor should have a minimum of 5 years of high slope scaling 
experience with a minimum of 2,000 hours of demonstrated experience supervising 
slope scaling.  The scaling supervisor should have completed the American Red Cross

“basic first aid course” or equivalent, and should have experience or training in the use 
of emergency remote rescue techniques. 
A slope scaler is an individual who is engaged in accessing the slope face and 
removing loose rock and materials from the slope face using a variety of hand tools at 
locations that require modified rock climbing techniques for the safe execution of the 
work.  Each slope scaler should have a minimum of 2 years experience with a minimum 
of 1,000 hours of demonstrated experience performing similar slope scaling work. 
The scaling crews would be supported by an ancillary ground crew (such as a 
general contractor) that is responsible for cleanup of the removed loose materials 
deposited at the base of the slopes (on the roadway). 
Prior to the start of the rock slope scaling, a series of eyebolt anchors will need to 
be installed along the top of the slope.  The eyebolt anchors will be used as rappelling 
anchors.  The eyebolt anchors may also be used to anchor other slope scaling 
apparatus such as a grouser or other “spider” type platform.  These platforms may be 
used to supplement the manual slope scaling to loosen/remove topographic overhangs 
and ledges.  The scaling platforms are essentially dragged up and down the slope face 
to abrade and remove weakened ledges and overhangs that may be too difficult to be 
removed by hand and/or manual methods. 
It should be noted that rock slope scaling work may be considered “hazardous” 
work and would be classified as “high risk” work.  Visibility is the single most essential 
attribute to the successful performance of rock slope scaling by the rock slope scaling 
crews.  Therefore, night work for rock slope scaling at the site should be discouraged for 
safety reasons (of the scaling crews).  In addition, the difficulty in maintaining proper 
and complete illumination of the work site makes night work for rock slope scaling 
undesirable and virtually impossible.  Furthermore, it should be noted that night work for 
rock slope scaling should be avoided because of potentially poor visibility and shadow 
conditions, which may reduce the ability of the rock slope scalers to properly assess the 
rock slope conditions and the presence of loose materials and precariously located 
rocks.

The rock slope scaling will typically require temporary closure of the roadway 
during the time that rock is being scaled or removed from the slope.  We recommend 
conducting all the rock slope scaling work in the day with some temporary roadway 
closure.  In addition, we envision that access for emergency vehicles would be provided 
at all times during the scaling work.  We understand that it is desired to have the 
roadway remain open during the slope scaling work.  Utilization of a movable temporary 
rockfall barrier within the active scaling area may be considered.   
Due to the presence of inter-bedded layers of weak clinker materials, scaling with 
the use of a backhoe may be too aggressive to control the breakage of rock along 
existing fractures.  In addition, scaling with the use of a backhoe may unnecessarily 
over-excavate clinker materials and cause potential new slope instability.  Therefore, we 
believe that the use of a backhoe or other mechanized equipment to assist in the 
excavation and scaling work (in an effort to speed up the scaling work) should be 
performed with extra care in areas exposing the weaker rock materials, such as the 
clinker materials and highly weathered basalt rock formation. 
3.4 
Draped Wire Mesh Installation 
As mentioned above, the draped wire mesh cover should assist in the controlled 
release of loose rock that is shed from the slope face during weathering and erosion.  
Therefore, it is not the primary intent to closely pin the wire mesh to the slope face as a 
retention system.  The draped wire mesh should provide positive rockfall control for the 
useful life of the wire mesh material.  We anticipate the majority of the large blocks of 
rock and boulders would have been removed following the slope scaling effort.  
Therefore, we envision that some loose rocks that may be retained by the draped wire 
mesh would be generally less than 3 feet in diameter.  The following paragraphs provide 
a general description of the draped wire mesh installation procedure. 
Once the rock slope scaling work has been completed, installation of the draped 
wire mesh may proceed.  Grouted top anchors of about 20 feet in depth are grouted into 
the stiff residual soil located at the top of the slope.  The grouted top anchors are 
generally installed not more than about 20 feet on centers along the slope crest.

Galvanized steel wire rope (0.75-inch diameter) with a black colored PVC coating is 
strung between the grouted rock anchors.   
The wire mesh, which typically comes in rolls of about 6 to 13 feet wide by about 
200 feet long (up to 300 feet long is possible), is attached and draped from the 
0.75-inch cable ropes to cover the entire slope.  In general, a minimum of about 1 foot 
of overlap between adjacent rolls of wire mesh is provided (vertical seams).  For the 
horizontal seams, we recommend that the minimum overlap between rolls be increased 
to 2 feet.  The mesh overlaps should be secured using stainless steel ring fasteners 
with a minimum of six stainless steel ring fasteners for each square foot of mesh 
overlap.  It should be noted that the wire mesh should be colored black to approximate 
the general coloration of the natural slope. 
The rolls of wire mesh are usually draped starting from the top of the slope.  The 
wire mesh is hung to within about 1 foot (minimum) to 2 feet (maximum) above the base 
level to allow for removal of loose rock that may fall from behind the draped wire mesh 
during installation.  The draped lengths of wire mesh are then tied together by the rock 
slope scalers to complete the installation.  It should be noted that the shoulder and 
drainage ditch at the edge of the existing roadway is relatively narrow.  Even with the 
limited catchment area, maintenance of loose rocks that fall behind the draped wire 
mesh will still be required. 
To provide a neat appearance at the ends of the draped wire mesh, a 0.5-inch 
diameter stability cable should be used to tuck the edges of the draped wire at the sides 
and bottom of the draped wire mesh.  In addition, we also recommend that bottom 
anchors be provided at the bottom of the draped wire mesh system to keep the wire 
mesh close to the face of the slope.  The bottom anchors also assist in keeping the 
loose rocks that fall from the slope in the areas of the draped wire mesh to within close 
proximity of the bottom of the slope. 
Because of the exposed ocean environment, which is very conducive to 
corrosion, the draped wire mesh and hardware should be corrosion resistant.  
Therefore, we propose that the draped wire mesh and hardware be galvanized, as a

3.5 
Anchored Wire Mesh System 
Anchored wire mesh system is a slope treatment that involves the installation of 
steel wire mesh panels anchored onto the slope face with soil/rock nails in an effort to 
prevent the soils and rocks from moving/falling under the long-term rockfall protection 
system.  The primary intent of this stabilization system is to closely pin the wire mesh to 
the slope face as a retention system. The use of the anchored wire mesh system is 
recommended at the lower western portion of the slope between Sta. No. 35+50 to 
Sta. No. 37+50. 
The slope to be anchored with steel wire mesh must be trimmed of vegetation 
and scaled of loose rocks prior to the installation of the anchored wire mesh system.  
The anchored wire mesh system should be installed prior to the installation of the 
draped wire mesh. Detailed descriptions of the individual components of the anchored 
wire mesh system are presented below: 
High Strength Wire Mesh 
The wire mesh should be alloyed high strength carbon steel wire with a minimum 
tensile strength of 256,000 pounds per square inch (psi).  The wire mesh should 
be woven construction, diamond shaped, and made with 4-mm (0.157-inch) 
diameter wire.  The ends of each wire should be formed into a loop and should 
be twisted.  The loops of the wire mesh should be fastened together to prevent 
unraveling of the mesh.  The wire should be galvanized with a zinc/aluminum 
coating with a minimum weight of 200 grams per square meters (0.655 ounces 
per square feet).  The coating should be 95 percent zinc and 5 percent 
aluminum. 
Compression Claws (Press Claws) 
The compression claws (also known as press claws) should be 6-mm (0.24-inch) 
diameter carbon steel bar and hot dipped galvanized with a minimum layer

thickness of 85 microns.  The smaller (standard) compression claws should be 
used to fasten the mesh panels together, and the bigger (special) compression 
claws should be used to fasten the mesh panels to the boundary ropes. 
Spike Plates 
The spike plates should be made from 10-mm (0.4-inch) thick steel and should 
be hot dipped galvanized with a minimum layer thickness of 85 microns.  The 
spike plate should be diamond shaped with a width of 190 mm (7.5 inches) and a 
length of 330 mm (13 inches). 
Boundary Ropes 
Boundary ropes should have a diameter of 12-mm (0.5 inches) and should be 
PVC coated.  The rope should be 6 by 19 construction, IWRC and galvanized 
with a minimum breaking strength of 23,940 pounds.  The rope should meet 
Federal Specification RR-W-410D or equivalent including galvanizing. 
Grouted Soil/Rock Anchors 
The grouted soil/rock anchors should consist of Dywidag GEWI bolts with a 
diameter of 32 mm (1.25 inches), Titan 30/11, IBO R38 or equivalent.  The 
grouted soil/rock anchors should have a corrosion allowance of 2 mm (78 mils) 
zinc galvanization included in their diameter. 
The anchored wire mesh system and all associated hardware, such as the high

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 150 and 180 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations 
shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. The borings were drilled using 
truck-mounted drill equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using 
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a 
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the 
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the 
Logs of Borings as RQD = 50.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. 
Graphic presentation test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) 
were performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear 
strengths of the soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were 
used as the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  
The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Eighteen Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed 
on basalt rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the 
underlying basalt rock formation. The test results are presented on Plate B-7. 
Four sets of pH (ASTM G51) and minimum resistivity (ASTM G57) tests were 
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration.  The results of the 
tests are presented on Plate B-8.
