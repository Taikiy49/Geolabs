2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 to 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa which has been built up by
successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic materials The site is
near the contact between the southeast flank of Mauna Kea and the northeast flank of
Mauna Loa The near surface lava flows are from Mauna Loa and overlie the older
Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to be Pliocene
to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the order of only 750 to
1 500 years old
The lava formation encountered appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type
flows originating from the northeast rift of Mauna Loa A a lava is typically characterized
by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
Cavities form when lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow

2 4 Seismic Design Considerations
The building code currently in effect for the County of Hawaii is the 1991 Uniform
Building Code UBC with some amendments similar to the 1997 UBC The County of
Hawaii normally accepts buildings designed based on the 1997 UBC The Island of
Hawaii is within Seismic Zone 4 and has an effective peak ground acceleration of 0 40g
in general accordance with the requirements of the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC
Therefore the project site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design
considerations will need to be addressed The following sections provide discussions on
the seismicity of the Island of Hawaii and the soil profile for seismic design at the site
Earthquake and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments
However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause
minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes
on the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the active
volcanoes located in the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii

Site Preparation
As previously mentioned the project site is generally underlain by basalt lava
flows covered with volcanic ash soils at the ground surface The volcanic ash
soils which are relatively soft and will compress when subjected to structural and
vehicular loads generally have poor structural characteristics with respect to
foundation and pavement support In addition ash soils have thixotropic
properties i e they temporarily lose strength when remolded or are subjected to
vibrations such as seismic events Therefore to the extent practicable site
grading should be designed to remove the surface ash soils from structural or
pavement areas
Based on our experience in the area lava tubes and cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe basalt formations Lava tubes and cavities may vary
significantly in size from small blisters to large lava tubes of 20 feet in diameter or
more In general the majority of small cavities are limited in lateral extent However
it should be noted that there is a potential for the occurrence of long lava tubes
which may traverse the project site The general orientation of the long lava tubes
would be in a mauka makai direction Since lava tubes and cavities may be present
in the volcanic rock formation underlying the site we believe that special attention
should be given to the site preparation aspects of the proposed construction

3 9 Storm Water Runoff Disposal System
Field permeability tests were conducted at selected locations to evaluate the
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials across the project site for the design
of the storm water runoff disposal system The permeability tests were performed in

at each boring location were typically performed at a depth of about 20 feet below the
existing ground surface Constant head permeability tests were performed to determine
the average hydraulic conductivity of the underlying subsurface materials Based on our
field exploration the subsurface materials encountered at the site generally consist of
severely fractured basalt rock formation
The test results indicate that the subsurface materials exhibit a hydraulic
conductivity k ranging from about 3 1 x 10 to over 5 8 x 10 feet per minute The
permeability test results are presented in Appendix C These values generally correspond

and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along

3 10 Drainage

3 11 Corrosion Potential
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by dense
clinkers and basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 22 feet below the existing ground surface Our experience with basalt
rocks suggests that the potential for corrosion to ductile iron water lines and steel pipe
in contact with the rock formation would be minimal In addition we believe that either
Type or Type Il cement may be used for the concrete in contact with the basalt rock
formation planned
3 12 Geological Hazards
As discussed in our feasibility study there are geological hazards present with a
potential to have adverse impacts on the proposed project These hazards include but
are not necessarily limited to the following
e Lava flow encroachment and pyroclastic fallout ash deposition from
possible future volcanic eruptions
e Floodwater encroachment due to heavy storms
e Seismic activity
The US Geological Survey has divided the Island of Hawaii into nine lava flow
hazard zones with the highest risk assigned the designation of Zone 1 and the lowest
risk assigned Zone 9 The project site is situated in an area designated as Zone 3 In
this zone lava flows younger than the Year 1800 cover between about 1 to 5 percent of
the zone s surface About 15 to 75 percent of the land surface in this zone is less than
750 years old Lava inundation risk in Zone 3 is less than Zones 1 and 2 due to the
distance from active rift and vent areas Since the entire town of Hilo is within Zone 3
on a regional scale there is no greater risk for this project site than for any other
W O GEOLABS INC Page 35
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
location in Hilo However it appears that the site is situated on or very close to the
northeast rift zone of Mauna Loa and there are cinder cones located near the site
Therefore there would be a higher potential for hazard from volcanic activity in this area
than the southern portions of Hilo which are further away from the rift zone
For pyroclastic fallout it should be noted that Hawaiian volcanic eruptions are
generally quiescent in nature with very little explosive activity that would eject
pyroclastic material over widespread areas Most volcanic eruptions produce fragments
of lava that are airborne for at least a short time before being deposited on the ground
These fragments are called tephra and include ash cinders and Pele s hair In
Hawaii tephra is usually ejected by lava fountains and poses a serious hazard only in
the immediate vicinity of an erupting vent The ash soils generally identified as Pahala
Ash appear to be Pleistocene or earlier in age Therefore it is most likely that
pyroclastic fall is a very minor hazard for the site
The Federal Emergency Management Agency FEMA has mapped most of the
project site on the Flood Insurance Rate Maps FIRM as Zone X an area outside the
flood hazard area However portions of the site along the stream have been mapped as
being subject to a 1 percent chance of inundation Site layout and grading design will
have to account for these areas
With respect to seismic activity it should be recognized that since the Island of
Hawaii is the site of three active volcanoes there is an appreciable level of seismic
activity associated with the volcanism Since the earliest records in 1868 there have
been at least 14 seismic events on and around the Island of Hawaii that exceeded
Magnitude 6 on the Richter scale The Uniform Building Code UBC designates the
entire island as Seismic Zone 4 Therefore the level of seismic activity associated with
development of the site should be adequately addressed in the design of structural
elements for the project
The project site appears to be situated near the northeastern rift of Mauna Loa
Localized faulting and ground movements are common along active rift zones during

The subsurface conditions across the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 13 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 and P 1 through P 3

borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight

ground surface using a Komatsu D275A dozer equipment The approximate locations
of the borings drilled and test pits excavated are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings and test pits were classified by visual
and textural examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A

Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on the in situ volcanic ash soils in the
excavated test pits The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the volcanic ash soils Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1
Seven unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on
selected rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
basalt rock formation encountered at the project site Results of the unconfined
compression tests are presented on Plate B 2
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the subsurface materials were evaluated by performing
four constant head tests at selected locations Boring Nos P 1 through P 3 at a
depth of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the
boreholes until the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a
sufficient period of time The water height and the rate of pumping were then
measured to provide data upon which to base calculation of the subsoil
permeability
