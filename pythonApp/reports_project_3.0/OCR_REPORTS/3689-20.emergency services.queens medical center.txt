INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Emergency Services project at the Queen s Medical Center in
the Punchbowl area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be of middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is on the southern flank of Punchbowl Hill The Punchbowl Hill
Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of the
Koolau Range The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with scattered
fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

Slabs On Grade
The project site is generally underlain by silty clay soils which are considered to be
moderately expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless concrete slabs
supported by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to
the lightly loaded slab on grade construction resulting from the shrinking and swelling of
these soils due to changes in moisture content To reduce the potential for structural
distress resulting from the swelling of the on site soils we recommend that non expansive
select capping fill material be provided for a minimum depth of 12 inches below the
bottom of the slab cushion The capping fill should also extend beyond the ledges of the
slab a minimum of 2 feet
The non expansive select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation section of this report The compacted capping fill and the
underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of
concrete The select capping fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil
above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of
moisture from the subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and
wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the concrete slab to
provide more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a capillary moisture break
To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top
of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the
membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered
since they can better mask minor slab cracking

workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent ground should
be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
with instruments
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation As previously indicated shallow
groundwater levels were measured in Boring No 1 during our field exploration Based on
our past experience in the area we believe that the shallow groundwater levels
encountered in Boring No 1 may not reflect the static natural groundwater table in the
area and may be the result of subterranean seepage or localized perched groundwater
As a result it is our opinion that localized dewatering may be required for the
construction of the underground tunnel Therefore we recommend that provisions be
made for localized dewatering during construction Since the excavation may involve
discharge of groundwater a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES
permit may be necessary The Contractor should consult their independent consultant for
the latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit application

Existing Underground Utilities
It is anticipated that underground utilities may exist at or in the vicinity of the
proposed underground tunnel alignment Since relatively deep excavations will be
necessary the project construction could disrupt the operations of the existing hospital
facility if these utility lines are damaged Therefore we recommend that existing utilities
within the project site be accurately located by the contractor prior to construction The
contractor has the sole responsibility to devise and implement an effective program to
protect the utilities during construction
Utility Trench
New utility connections will be required for the proposed new development
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be
W O 3689 20 Page 20
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill
thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and
placement could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches
are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
finish grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the slabs
and foundations should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided
or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slabs and
foundations
