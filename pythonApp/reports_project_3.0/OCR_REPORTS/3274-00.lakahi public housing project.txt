SEISMIC_CONSIDERATIONS
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes
In this process when the loose saturated sand and or cohesionless silt deposit is
subjected to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and
decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable
to occur rapidly enough due to a large areal extent of the deposit to dissipate the
build up of pore pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soils is reduced
Under sustained vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave
as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements if not confined resulting in significant deformations Soils
most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and
loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction will not
occur if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground surface In deeper
deposits the greater overburden pressure is sufficient to prevent liquefaction from
occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil include
the following
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3274 00 Page 8
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine grained and uniform sands are generally
more susceptible to liquefaction than coarse grained or well graded
sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose fine sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the in situ moisture contents of the near surface silty soils ash
appear to be relatively low with the exception of Boring No P 3 Considering the
relatively thin ash layer encountered it appears that the potential for seismically
induced liquefaction at the site is low
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a surficial layer of stiff to very stiff
silty soils underlain by dense clinkers and basalt rock formation The near surface layer
of silty soils is relatively thin on the order of 1 to 5 feet in thickness Based on our field
exploration and laboratory test results we believe that the dense clinkers underlying
the near surface stiff silty soils encountered would provide adequate foundation support
for the proposed one to two story buildings provided that the soft and or loose near
surface soils are removed We believe that a shallow foundation system may be used
to support the proposed buildings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3274 00 Page 9
Because dense clinker layers and or basalt rock were encountered at shallow
depths of about 1 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface significant cuts relative
to the existing ground surface would require excavation into the dense clinker layers
and medium hard basalt rock As a result we recommend that significant cuts be
limited at this project site Cuts into the dense clinker layers would generate good fill
materials for use as fill however the ecavated clinker materials would include some
over sized materials greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension which should be
screened before use as fill
The near surface silty soils may be reused as a source of fill materials provided
that they are mixed with the on site clinker and or rock materials resulting from cuts
However the on site wet ash material encountered in Boring P 3 should not be reused
as fill materials We anticipate that the wet ash material would pose difficulties in
compaction during construction The wet ash materials should be over excavated to
expose firm materials and backfilled with engineered fill
We anticipate that some of the future building pads may straddle cut and fill
transitions Where the building pads straddle cuts in clinker and or basalt rock
materials and fills we recommend that the clinker and or basalt rock be over excavated
down to a depth of at least 12 inches where possible moisture conditioned and
replaced with compacted fill to provide more uniform bearing support to footings and
slabs on grades for the proposed buildings

Site Grading
The proposed finish floor elevations for the new buildings were not available at
the time this report was prepared However we understand that site grading will
consist generally of cuts and fills on the order of less than 5 feet to provide level
building pads for the proposed construction Site preparation and grading should be
performed in accordance with the following guidelines Additional earthwork
recommendations are presented in related sections of this report
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs
Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading to
evaluate whether any undesirable materials such as more extensive ash deposits are
encountered during the excavation process and whether the exposed soil rock
conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
During clearing and grubbing if wet ash materials and or loose to soft soils are
encountered these materials should be over excavated to expose firm soil and
the resulting over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill The over
excavated wet ash materials should be disposed of properly off site or stockpiled
for later use as topsoil
In areas to receive fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at
least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3274 00 Page 11
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures If the subgrade soils are pumping during
compaction a geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be
placed on the pumping subgrade prior to placement of fill material
Fills and Backfills
General fill material needed for the site grading should consist of well graded
materials less than 6 inches in maximum size The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 15 or more and should have a maximum swell of less
than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
General fill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The on site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material provided that
they are thoroughly mixed with the cut materials from excavation into the clinker
layers and or basalt rock However the wet ash materials near Boring No P 3
should not be used as fills Fill materials generated from cuts in the clinker and
basalt rock may be used as general fill provided that they are screened of over
sized materials greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension Over sized
basalt materials may be broken down to smaller sized materials and
incorporated into the fill material This requirement is to facilitate later
construction of building foundations and utility trench excavations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3274 00 Page 12
If needed imported fill material should consist of granular material such as
clinker mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension
and should contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material
should be tested for conformance with the recommendations by Geolabs Hawaii
prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Conventional compaction density testing may not be practicable in fills composed
of rocks and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes of a sheepsfoot type of compactor needed to achieve the desired level
of compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the
project under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii Test pits
should be utilized later to verify the desired level of rock fill compaction On the
basis of field observation and test pit verification program the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
Fill Slopes
All permanent fill slopes may be designed with an inclination of two horizontal
to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter All fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations
steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and
benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above recommendations for
fill placement Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Water should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3274 00 Page 13
be diverted away from the tops of slopes If slope planting is required it should
be provided as soon as possible to reduce potential erosion of the finished
slopes If rock fill material is used to construct the fill embankment slope the
slope face should be well choked off with well graded small sized rock
fragments to reduce potential loss of topsoil or fines into the void spaces
between large rock fragments
In general mid point slope benches should be considered for slopes steeper
than four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V with vertical heights greater than
about 20 feet
Excavation
We anticipate that minor excavations on the order of up to 5 feet will be required
for construction of the septic systems utility lines and site grading We
anticipate that some of the planned excavations will be in the clinker and or
basalt rock formation encountered at depths of about 1 to 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Excavation into the clinker and or basalt rock formation
may require ripping with large bulldozers and or hoerams and may result in
boulders with maximum dimensions exceeding 12 inches from the cut
Therefore significant cuts may not be desirable at this site The over sized
materials greater than 6 inches in maximum size resulting from the excavations
into the clinker and basalt rock should be processed prior to use as structural fill
materials as recommended above
Foundations
We understand that the proposed structures will generally consist of one to two
story wood framed structures with slab on grade construction Based on the subsoil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3274 00 Page 14
conditions encountered at the site shallow spread line foundations may be used to
support the proposed structures For design of shallow footing foundations an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be utilized for the footings bearing
on the recompacted clinker and granular subgrades and or bearing on compacted fill
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 14 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or below grade
structures they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or below
grade structure
It is recommended that the bottom of all footing excavations in soil be compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel
and concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over
excavation should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom be extended down to the firm
in situ materials
If the proposed building foundation straddles a cut in rock and fill transition
clinkers or basalt rock formation encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
should be scarified or over excavated by at least 12 inches and replaced with similar
type of fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to
provide more uniform support for the building foundation This requirement is to reduce
the potential for differential settlement to occur across the cut and fill transition All
footing excavations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs Hawaii prior
to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to evaluate the condition of bearing
materials and the embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grades may be used for the proposed
buildings We recommend that the subgrades for concrete slabs on grades be scarified
by a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction For more
uniform support we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting

moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture
vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the cushion
layer Two inches of moist fine sand should be placed above the plastic membrane to
provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Thickened edges of slabs if provided adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of No 3B Fine gravel

Percolation Testing
Eight percolation tests were performed at the project site during our field
exploration Two percolation tests were performed in Boring Nos B 1 and B 3 which
were drilled to about 20 feet below the ground surface Six additional percolation tests
were performed in Boring Nos P 1 through P 6 which were drilled to about 6 feet below
the ground surface
After the borings were drilled to the desired depths the borings were cased from
the ground surface to about 12 inches above the bottom of the boring to keep the hole
open About 1 inch of sand was placed on the bottom of the 4 inch diameter boring
The falling head method was used to test the subsurface percolation rates at the site
About 6 inches of water was added into the borings and the changes in water level in
the borings were recorded with respect to time The results of these tests are
presented in Appendix C
Based on the percolation test results the calculated percolation rates for basalt
rock formation range between about 10 and 40 minutes inch and the calculated
percolation rates for clinker formation range from 5 56x10 to 0 1 minutes inch It must
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3274 00 Page 19
be noted that the percolation rates in basalt rock formation are highly dependent on the
proximity of fracture in the basalt rock formation
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and pavement should be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and erosion and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavation
should be properly backfilled against the walls immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce potential water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
12 borings to depths ranging from 6 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
nia sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based
on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
