PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
An updated comprehensive wastewater facilities plan will be developed for the
island of Oahu. An integral part of this plan is the construction of a deep, large diameter
tunnel to convey wastewater between the facilities. One of the initial tasks consists of
establishment of a geotechnical testing plan for a geotechnical engineering exploration for
the tunnel. This task consists of research of existing boring data from the available files of
the City and County of Honolulu and State of Hawaii DOT as well as the internal files at
Geolabs, Inc.
The subsurface information obtained will be summarized on a plan and generalized
subsurface cross section will be developed.

available subsurface information along the tunnel alignment, to assist in the development
of a geotechnical testing plan for design of the tunnel. To accomplish this, we performed

EXISTING GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING DATA
The primary alignment follows existing City and County of Honolulu roads and
State Department of Transportation highways. Therefore, information from utility
installation, i.e. sewer, water, and drain, were a primary source of subsurface
information. However, these borings provide limited information due to the shallow
depths of the utilities. More meaningful subsurface information was obtained from
deeper borings drilled for bridge foundations along the alignment and from private
mid-rise building projects in the vicinity. Information from exploration for the previous
(1991) mass transit project provided the most valuable information through the Pearl
City area.
Two alternate alignments were developed after commencement of our study. The
alternate alignments are relatively close to the original preferred route and the geologic
units encountered are similar. Therefore, only the geologic profile for the original
alignment is presented. The primary tunnel alignment is shown on Plates 1 and 2.
The approximate boring locations and idealized subsurface profiles along the
alignment are presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.8. The boring data were combined with
geologic information from Stearns, et. al to develop the idealized profiles.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of weathered remnants of the extinct
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the
eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active
and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu.
The proposed corridor is located along the southern flank of Koolau and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio-eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of Pearl Harbor
and the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions along the majority of the
alignments are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral/algal reef deposits and
related deposits laid during high stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch which
are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in this
area is Koolau Basalt.
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene
Epoch and the early Pleistocene Epoch by thin bedded basaltic lava flows. The main
shield-building activities ceased approximately 2.5 — 3.5 million years ago. Evidence
from drilled wells indicates that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1,200 feet
since the cessation of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across the mouth of the bays. The
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded, or melted,
global sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both
higher and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu.
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quit waters
protected by fringing reefs.

The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted
in deposits of coral-algal limestone and maris within the predominantly lagoonal
sedimentary unit.
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions.
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments.
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series, including Aliamanu-Salt Lake-Makalapa,
Kamanaiki, Punchbowl, Rountop-Tantalus-Sugarloaf and other minor vents. These
post-erosional events, i.e., those following the cessation of shield building, were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene Epoch sea level fluctuations.
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions. which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material — predominantly ash and cinder. These deposits of
pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff, locally referred to as “mudrock”
One of these eruptions, Aliamanu, caused diversion of Halawa Stream to the north of its
original location into the area of what are now the Southeast and Magazine Lochs of
Pearl Harbor during the Kaena (+95 feet) high stand of the sea.
About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that
rise, the deep valleys in the project area were drowned. In the last 10,000 years or so,
sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in
thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in the areas which were formerly
valleys and drainageways.
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have
brought the Ewa — Honolulu area to its present form. A great many of those projects
were originally constructed for agricultural, residential and/or military development.
Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural
loads.

SUBSOIL CHARACTERISTICS
Based on our review of the available data, and previous experience in the areas
of the proposed alignment, the following discussions summarize the general
characteristics of the materials that may be encountered along the route.
Koolau Basalt (Tkb)
The Koolau Basalt is the oldest geologic unit within the project areas. It generally
consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a’a flows of basaltic lavas and may have a
mantle of cobbles and bounders, or residual soil overlying the top of the rock.
It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock sub-components, such as
clinker layers and lava tubes, could also be encountered.
The Koolau Basalt is the aquifer materials of the Pearl Harbor and Honolulu
Artesian Basins, which are the major developed groundwater bodies supplying fresh
water for the Island of Oahu.
The aquifer is the permeable lava rock while the upper confining member
(caprock) is the relatively impermeable alluvial and lagoonal deposits. There is no lower
confining member as is the usual case in most aquifers. At the bottom, the fresh water
level is confined by salt water in accordance with the Ghyben-Herzberg Principle which
is based on observations that fresh groundwater floats on saline groundwater in coastal
regions. The ratio of densities between fresh and salt water with salinity compared to
that of sea-water, causes the salt water to be displaced downward about 40 feet below
sea level for every foot that the fresh basalt water table or potentiometric head rises
above sea level.

Structurally, the Koolau Basalt and its residual soils are among the strongest of
all the units encountered within the study area. Therefore, the basalt and related
residual soils and saprolites make excellent bearing formations due to their high
strength characteristics in an undisturbed state. However, its use for foundation support
in many parts of the project area is severely limited by the considerable depths required
to reach the top of the basalt and the artesian conditions expected where the basalt is
encountered at relatively low elevations.
In this report and the generalized cross sections presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.8,
the following subunits of Koolau Basalt are used:
Residual Soil — Koolau Basalt
This subunit consists of soils created from the in-situ chemical weathering of
basic rock originating from Koolau Basalt. These soils generally consist of hard
to very hard red, brownish red or reddish brown clayey silts and silty clays. The
soils are usually structureless and may have a blockly texture.
Saprolite — Koolau Basalt
Saprolites are a form of residual soil, which exhibits a relict structure from the
parent rock. Structural features such as vesicles and joints are visible in
saprolites. Saprolite can grade to extremely weathered rock. For the purposes
of field classification, saprolite can be broken by hand into soil particles whereas
-extremely weathered basalt usually cannot be broken down by hand.
Clinker — Koolau Basalt
Clinker is fragmental basalt found on the tops, and usually at the bottom, of a’a
lava flows. Clinker is generally gravel to cobble sized fragments of scoriaceous
rock in a tight packed but open graded structure.
Basalt Flow — Koolau Basalt
This subunit is used to designate in-situ lava formation. In this report, no effort is
made to differentiate between pahoehoe and a’a flows.
Older Alluvium (Qa)
The Older Alluvium is assigned the position of being the second oldest unit in the
stratigraphic column of the project area even though some of the coral-algal reef
deposits may be intercalated with the alluvial deposits.
This unit generally overlies the Koolau Basalt and consists of terrigenous
sediments which have been transported by stream action from the upper reaches of the
streams. The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard brown clayey silts and silty

clays. Gravel lenses representing buried stream channels are sometimes encountered.
Portions of the Older Alluvium unit contain some weathered basalt boulders and
cobbles. These coarse grained parts of the unit may also contain artesian water from
the basal fresh water system or as a sub-aquifer.
The old lagoonal deposits of the Older Alluvium unit consist of marine or lagoonal
sediments which have been desiccated and consolidated into very stiff to hard gray
clayey silts.
The Older Alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength which leads to a
relatively high bearing capacity. Frequently, these materials show a moderate to high
shrink/well potential; however, this is not considered a constraint for this project.
Coral-Algal Deposits (Qis)
This unit consists of fossil remnants or coral-algal reefs and their related detrital
debris deposits. In Hawaii, the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant portion of any
coral rock assemblage.
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral debris consisting of
gravel, sand and silt sized clastic garments. Some of the coral fragments were
dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented and/or uncemented fine sands
and silts.
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral-algal deposits and the
degree of cementation frequently present, this formation generally has high in-situ
strength characteristics. Structurally, the coral-algal reef deposits are capable of
supporting heavy foundation loads.
In this report and the generalized cross sections presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.8,
the following subunits of coral-algal deposits are used:
Coral-Algal Detritus
This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations. The soils
are generally medium dense to dense sandy gravels and gravelly sands. Silts
and clays are only present in minor or trace amounts. Detritus may also be
weakly cemented.
Coral-Algal Reef Formation
This formation is composed of corals and calcareous algae in their original
growth positions or detrital deposits which have become moderately to well
cemented.

Lagoonal Reef Formation
This subunit consists of dendritic or branching corals in their original growth
forms. The interstices between the branches of coral have been filled with a fine
sandy organic silt.
Filled Cavit
This term is used to describe what appears to be a cavity in a reef formation
which has been filled by loose sand and soft silts.
Beachrock
During lower stands of the sea, beach sands and related littoral deposits were
exposed at elevations below current sea level. Frequently, when calcareous
littoral deposits remain relatively stable for extended periods of time, they will
become cemented into a compact sandstone material.
Honolulu Basalt (Qhb)
The Honolulu Basalts generally consist of interbedded pahoehoe and a’a flows of
basalt lavas and may have plastic alluvial clays with cobbles and boulders overlying the
top of the rock.
It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock sub-components, such as
clinker layers and lava tubes, could also be encountered.
The basalt frequently acts either as a perching or confining member for
groundwater in the Honolulu Aquifer system. Therefore, groundwater may be
encountered in excavations located within this unit.
Structurally, this basalt is also one of the strongest of the units encountered
within the study area. Therefore, the basalt makes an excellent bearing formation due
to its high strength characteristics in an undisturbed state.
Residual Soil — Honolulu Basalt
This subunit consists of soils created from the in-situ chemical weathering of
basic rock originating from the vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. These
soils generally consist of hard to very hard brown, brownish red or reddish brown
clayey silts and silty clays. The soils are usually structureless and may have a
blockly texture.
Saprolite — Honolulu Basalt
Saprolites are a form of residual soil which exhibits a relict structure from the
parent rock. Structural features such as vesicles and joints are visible in

saprolites. Saprolite can grade to extremely weathered rock. For the purposes

Clinker — Honolulu Basalt
Clinker is fragmental basalt found on the tops, and usually at the bottom, of a’a

scoriaceous rock in a tightly packed but open graded structure.
Basalt Flow — Honolulu Basalt
This subunit is used to designate in-situ lava formation. In this report, no effort is

Honolulu Tuffs (Qht)
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consists of volcanic ash which
consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard rock that resembles siltstone or
sandstone, locally called “mudrock”. The tuff is frequently found overlying coralline
deposits or alluvium units.
Since the formation is a medium hard rock, it has very high strength
characteristics. Structurally, the tuff is very competent.
This formation has relatively low permeability and groundwater is generally not
encountered in this formation. When it does occur, it is usually in the form of seepage
along joints.
Recent Alluvium (Ra)
The Recent Alluvium consists mainly of very soft to medium stiff, brown to dark
gray clayey silt and organic silt with varying amounts of sand and infrequent pockets of
gravel and cobbles.
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low energy
environment such as an estuary or bay. Frequently, the soils assigned to this unit
accumulated very rapidly under water and have never been exposed. Therefore, there
is a tendency to encounter underconsolidated sediments which settle under the weight
of a minimum overburden or structural loading.
Included in this unit are subunits of localized thin layers (about 10 feet) of
lagoonal marine sediments consisting mostly of very soft to medium stiff gray clayey
silts. These gray silts are identified as lagoonal facies subunits of the young alluvial
unit.

The soils assigned to this unit are generally considered to be poorly to very
poorly suited for use as support for foundation loads, due to their low in-situ strength
characteristics and the tendency for settlement to occur in structures overlying soils of
this unit.
The following subunits will be used to describe Recent Alluvium:
Estuarine Sediments — Recent Alluvium
This unit generally consists of dark gray and brown silts and clays in the
consistency range of very soft to soft. This soil also contains traces of fine
sands. They are also characterized by a weak to moderate organic odor and
also may contain small fragile mollusk shells.
Estuarine Peat — Recent Alluvium
This unit generally consists of dark gray and brown silts and clays in the
consistency range of very soft to soft. This subunit is differentiated from
Estuarine Sediments by the presence of fibrous organic material. This soil also
contains traces of fine to medium grained sands. They are also characterized by
a moderate to strong organic odor and also may contain small fragile mollusk
shells although these are usually rare.
Lagoonal Detritus — Recent Alluvium
This unit generally consists of light gray and medium gray sandy silts or silty fine
sand. Gravel sized fragment are rare. Frequently, the silts are organic in origin
and may have a mild to strong fishy odor. It is differentiated from the Lagoonal
Reef Formation by the predominance of sand sized material and the lack of
gravel sized fragments. Close examination of the coarse fragments will usually
show a limy coating on all surfaces.
Lagoonal Sediments — Recent Alluvium
This unit generally consists of light gray and medium gray silts and clays. Gravel
and sand sized fragments are rare. Frequently, the silts are organic in origin and
may have a mild to strong fishy odor. It is differentiated from Estuarine
Sediments by the lighter color and a relatively high content of calcium carbonate.
Recent Sand (Rs)
This mapping unit represents recent deposits of beach and near-shore marine
sands. These sands are generally composed of medium-grained fragments derived
from the coral-algal reefs which have been reduced in size and rounded by water
action. The consistency of the sands is generally on the order of medium dense to
dense.

These recent sands are generally capable of supporting moderately heavy loads in their
natural state. Densification of the sands usually improves their bearing capacity.
Man-Made Fill (Rf)
This mapping unit represents low areas which were filled during urbanization.
The majority of these low areas is underlain at relatively shallow depths by Recent
Alluvium and were originally marshy areas. A small part of the filled lands are underlain
by coral.
The quality and composition of the fill materials are variable. These fills were
frequently placed without control. Therefore, it is difficult to make any general statement
regarding their engineering properties or construction in these areas.
Groundwater in the fill areas is generally expected to occur as a basal water
table near sea level. However, higher perched water tables may be encountered.
The capability of these man-made fills to support structural loads is partly
dependent upon the design load and the quality of the fill and its placement and partly
dependent upon the materials underlying the fills. The actual fill materials may be of
suspect quality.
Quite frequently, in areas underlain by Recent Alluvium, fills were only intended
to support very light structures on shallow spread footings and therefore, only form a
thin crust over the soft alluvial materials.
Along Pearl Harbor and at the mouths of major streams, man-made fills were
generally placed over soft sediments for development along the shoreline areas. The fill
materials range from clays to gravels and intermediate mixtures of soils. The
consistencies of the fill range from soft or loose to hard or very dense.
LIMITATIONS
The subsurface information submitted herein are based, upon information obtained
from field borings for other projects. Variations of subsoil conditions between and beyond
the borings may occur. The information presented is intended for development of a
geotechnical exploration and testing program only. The information is not intended for
foundation design or construction.
