operation should be backfilled with general fill material compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  After the proof-rolling operations, the subgrades should 
then be scarified, moisture-conditioned, and compacted.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This geotechnical engineering exploration report presents the results of our 
analyses and geotechnical recommendations for the Kapolei Parkway, Harbor Access 
Road to Station 11+10 project located in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for site grading, design of 
pavements, manhole and headwall structures, concrete flatwork support, and 
underground utilities only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is in the west Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The 
project begins from the end of the existing Kapolei Parkway roadway near the Kapolei 
Commons shopping center and will extend to the west about 1,000 feet.     
We understand it is proposed to construct a new four-lane divided roadway 
consisting of three 10-foot wide lanes and 20-foot wide medians. In addition 16-foot 
wide grass shoulders are planned along both sides of the roadway. 
Based on the preliminary plans, the roadways will be constructed over areas with 
cuts on the order of about 5 feet or less relative to the existing ground surface in order 
to achieve the design grades. In addition, we understand a significant amount of 
excavations (on the order of up to approximately 10 feet deep) will be required for sewer 
and drainage line construction.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  As the 
volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked 
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield 
Plateau.  The project site is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast.  In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge.  The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach.  This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of

2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is at the west end of the existing Kapolei Parkway near the 
Kapolei Commons Shopping Center in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  
The project is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The existing ground surface elevations range from about +70 to +71 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL).  We observed an existing drainage ditch crossing the proposed 
roadway alignment between Station 8+50 and Station 9+00. Soil berms about 2 to 
4 feet high are present along the edges of the drainage ditch.  The bottom of the 
drainage ditch is about +68 to +69 feet MSL.  Based on our reconnaissance and 
probing, the soils exposed at the bottom of the drainage ditch are generally in a stiff 
condition.  The area around the drainage ditch is highly vegetated with tall grass and 
small trees. 
The new roadway extension will traverse through undeveloped land. It should be 
noted the surface soils are generally dry and friable. Relatively wide and deep shrinkage 
cracks were observed during our site reconnaissance. Based on field observations, we 
noted that substantial shrinkage cracks of up to about 6 inches in width and about 2 to 
3 feet in depth were present at the existing ground surface.

designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and

excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site materials may be re-used as a source of general 
fill material, provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and clay 
lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension.  
Imported fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select granular 
material, such as crushed coralline or basaltic materials. The imported materials 
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in 
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing 
the No. 200 sieve. The import materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 
or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less. Imported fill 
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use.  
Aggregate subbase materials required for the pavement section should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Section 31 of the City and 
County of Honolulu Standard Specifications (September, 1986).  
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to not 
less than 90 percent relative compaction below sidewalks.  The compaction 
requirement for aggregate subbase below the pavements, curbs, and gutters 
should be increased to 95 percent relative compaction. In addition, the compaction 
requirements for the roadway subgrades directly below the pavement structural 
section should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

3.1.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision cut slopes at the project site will generally expose the stiff to hard 
clayey soils encountered in the borings drilled. In general, cut slopes and 
permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may be designed with a slope 
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes 
steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding. 
Construction of earth berms, interceptor ditches, and the use of geotextile fabrics 
over the fill slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for significant 
erosion, thus enhancing the long-term stability of the fill slopes. In addition, 
appropriate slope planting or other erosion control measures to reduce the potential 
for significant erosion of the exposed slopes (including provision of a permanent 
irrigation system to maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant) should be 
implemented as soon as possible after the finished slope faces are completed.

may be encountered in localized areas embedded within the clayey alluvium 
present. Therefore, contractors may encounter some difficult excavation conditions 
in localized areas.

3.2 
Manhole and Headwall Structures 
We anticipate retaining structures, such as headwalls and manholes, will be 
required for the proposed project. In general, we recommend using shallow foundations 
bearing on the stiff to hard alluvium to support of the planned structures. The following 
general guidelines may be used for design of the structures. 
3.2.1 Foundations 
We recommend placing 6 inches of granular bedding material, such as No. 3B Fine 
gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), below the bottom of the manhole structures 
to provide uniform support.  
An allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used 
to design the foundations bearing on the on-site soils (headwall structures) or 
No. 3B Fine gravel (manhole structures). In general, we recommend embedding 
the foundations for the retaining structures a minimum of 24 inches below the 
lowest adjacent finished grade. If the footings are designed in accordance with our 
recommendations, total settlements of the structures are estimated to be on the 
order of 1 inch or less. 
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded 
below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the 
utility trench, or the footing should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the 
utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-
grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for 
appreciable foundation settlement. 
Lateral loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by frictional resistance 
between the base of the foundations and the bearing materials and by passive 
earth pressure developed against the vertical faces of footings and/or walls.

3.2.3 Drainage 
Headwall structures should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.  As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material.  The prefabricated drainage product should also 
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the structure. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or free-draining compacted fills, where specified by the designer.  Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should also consist of

3.4 
Underground Utility Lines 
It is anticipated most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in the stiff to 
hard clay and medium dense to dense silty coralline sand. In general, granular bedding 
consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is 
recommended below the pipes for uniform support. Free-draining granular materials, 
such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), also should be used for 
the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate 
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill. It is critical 
that free-draining materials be used around the pipes to reduce the potential for the 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes, which could result in backfill settlement. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrades or finished grade may consist of the on-site soils generally 
less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill material should be 
moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in 
maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to not less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the compaction requirement 
for the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be increased 
to at least 95 percent relative compaction.

3.5 
Concrete Sidewalks, Curbs, and Gutters 
We understand concrete sidewalks and gutters will be required. The near-surface 
soils encountered in the borings generally consist of silty clays, which exhibit a 
moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. 
Therefore, we recommend placing a minimum of 24 inches of non-expansive select 
granular fill or aggregate subbase material below the concrete flatwork to reduce the 
potential of appreciable structural distress to the lightly loaded concrete flatwork. 
The soil subgrades below the sidewalks, curbs, and gutters should be scarified to 
a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with 
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. The subgrade should be kept moist until 
covered by the select granular fill (aggregate subbase material). 
Due to the moderately to highly expansive nature of the subgrade soils, we 
recommend reinforcing the concrete sidewalks and concrete gutters with No. 3 
reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches in both directions as a minimum. In addition, we 
recommend providing crack control joints at intervals equal to the width of the sidewalks 
(or slabs) with expansion joints at right-angle intersections to reduce the potential for 
substantial shrinkage cracks in the slabs.

Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried

External Corrosion Standards,” Volume 3, dated 1991 may be used for the proposed 
project. Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, advanced to maximum 
depths of about 21.5 to 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were

approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by a geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-3.

general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving

addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification 
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on 
Plate B-2. 
Two direct shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture 
content. Dynamic compaction of the samples was performed by dropping a 10-pound 
hammer from a height of 18 inches. The samples were compacted in five equal layers 
with each layer receiving 10 blows of the hammer. The samples were soaked for 
four days prior to the penetration test. Test results are presented on Plates B-5 through 
B-7. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G 51) and Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G 57) were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration.  The test results are provided on Plate B-8.
