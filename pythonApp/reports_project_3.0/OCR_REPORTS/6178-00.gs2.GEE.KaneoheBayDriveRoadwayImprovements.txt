MSL respectively
The two distressed areas exhibited longitudinal cracks and depression at the

The surface cracking and depression in the distressed areas may be caused by the

about 1H 1V

included the installation of conventional walls with spread footing walls with micropiles

construction of the new wall and the close proximity of the existing 42 inch sewer force

Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Kaneohe Bay Drive Roadway Improvements project in the Kaneohe
area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing field
observations and engineering analyses for the proposed project The recommendations
presented herein are intended for repair of the distressed roadway sections and
pavement design purposes only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes beginning in the mid Pliocene Epoch The older
Waianae Volcano was the first to develop closely followed by the development of
Koolau Volcano For a period of time Oahu was comprised of two islands formed by
each of the volcanic centers The volcanoes continued to erupt and banked against the
slopes of each other with Waianae terminating its eruptive activity prior to the cessation
of Koolau The main shield building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million
years ago
The project site is near the base of the northwestern slope of Puu Papoa which
is essentially an erosional remnant of caldera filling lava flows of the Koolau Volcano
After the formation of the Koolau Volcanic Range and the associated
Kailua Volcanic Series in the Kaneohe Kailua area the constituent volcanoes were
deeply dissected by stream erosion Clastic debris eroded from the mountains was
re deposited along the mountain flanks and in Kaneohe Bay Glacio eustatic sea level
changes gave rise to varying configurations of Kaneohe Bay and sediment
accumulation

Potential Causes of Distressed Roadway
Both distressed areas exhibited longitudinal cracks and depressions at the
pavement surface near the top of slope along the makai lane of the roadway The
existing slope inclination ranged from about 1H 1V to 2H 1V Based on the borings
highly plastic clay soils were encountered near the slope area
For highly plastic clays the natural slope is relatively flat with inclination typically
about 4H 1V to 6H 1V For steeper slope inclinations the highly plastic clay soils tend
to creep downslope Since the existing slopes at the distressed areas are relatively
steep for the clay soils we believe that the potential cause of the surface cracking is the
downslope creeping of the highly plastic clay soils In addition surface water entering
into the cracks on the pavement surface softens the clay subgrade soils and increases
the downslope creep of the roadway

Remedial Measures for Distressed Roadway
Since surface water entering into the cracks on the pavement surface may cause
softening of the clay soils and increase the rate of downslope creep of the roadway we
recommend filling the existing surface cracks and subsequent cracks with sealant until
final repair construction We understand that the roadway needs to be open to traffic at
the end of the workday Because of the limited construction area available flattening of
the existing slope to a more stable slope is not feasible and the installation of a
permanent retaining wall system is recommended for the distressed roadway areas
The permanent wall system will be installed in a cut condition Several potential
retaining wall systems were evaluated for the distressed roadway areas These wall
systems included the installation of conventional retaining walls walls with micropiles
soil nailed walls and drilled shaft walls Discussion of these potential retaining wall
systems for use as remedial measures are provided below
Conventional retaining wall system generally consists of a cast in place concrete
wall structure utilizing shallow spread footings Temporary excavation will be
required for installation of this wall system Forms are typically used for the
construction of the cast in place concrete wall structure
Relatively high earth pressures are anticipated for the clay soils encountered in the
borings Therefore it is anticipated that the size of the wall footing will be significant
to resist the lateral wall loading We anticipate that the limited width available from
the top of the slope to the right of way of about 9 to 10 feet in some areas may not
be sufficient for the wall design and construction In addition the location of the
existing 42 inch sewer force main is in close proximity of the top of slope location in
some areas Therefore the stability of the existing 42 inch sewer force main needs
to be considered in the wall design and construction Because of the above we
believe that it would be difficult to design and construct a conventional retaining wall
for this project

Retaining Wall with Micropiles
The width of the wall footing may be reduced with the installation of micropiles
below the wall footing The wall loading is resisted primarily by the micropiles
instead of the footing
Because of the close proximity of the existing 42 inch sewer force main the
installation of temporary shoring will be required for the construction of the new
wall We anticipate that the temporary shoring will consist of continuous steel
sheetpiling Based on the borings boulders were encountered in the upper fill
material Therefore predrilling may be required for the installation of the steel
sheetpiling Because of the close proximity of the existing sewer force main there
is a potential risk of damaging the existing sewer force main during installation of
the steel sheetpiling using a vibratory hammer
We understand that the walls need to be designed based on the current AASHTO
Load Resistance Factor Design LRFD Method Because of the large lateral
design load anticipated using the LRFD method and the clayey subsoil at the site
we believe that designing the new wall supported on micropiles for these conditions
may be difficult Based on the above discussions we believe that a retaining wall
supported by a micropile system should not be used for this project
Soil Nailed Retaining Wall
A soil nailed wall system generally consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope A shotcrete facing is
generally applied to the face of the reinforced excavation to provide the appearance
of a conventional concrete retaining wall The soil nailed wall is typically installed in
a cut condition from the top of the slope down to the bottom of the wall The
reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil and
reinforce the sidewall of the excavation The reinforced soil mass behaves in a
manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing In general the face of
the soil nailed retaining wall system is slightly battered at a slope inclination of
about 1H 12V

Several existing utility lines including a 42 inch sewer force main are along the
roadway area and will be within the soil nail zone behind the wall It should be
noted that the soil nails should be installed below the existing utility lines to prevent
future excavation within the soil nail zone In addition the existing clay subsoil
where the soil nails will be installed has a tendency to creep at relatively low applied
loads Therefore the load capacity of the soil nails will be lower and a greater
number of soil nails will be required Because of the above the installation of a
soil nailed wall system is not recommended for this project
Drilled Shaft Retaining Wall
This type of wall consists of the installation of a series of closely spaced drilled
shafts along the retaining wall alignment The drilled shafts will serve as retaining
structures and temporary shoring would not be required during construction After
completion of the drilled shaft installation the soil in front of the drilled shafts may
be removed down to the design finished grade and shotcrete facing would be
applied to the drilled shaft retaining wall structure
The available construction area between the top of slope and the right of way
would be suitable for the installation of a drilled shaft retaining wall system since the
required width is relatively narrow compared to the conventional retaining wall
system In addition the potential disturbance to the existing sewer force main is
much less for the installation of the drilled shafts than the other retaining wall
systems Therefore we recommend using a drilled shaft wall system at the
two distressed areas of the project Based on the subsurface soil conditions
encountered at the project site it is our opinion that shaft diameters on the order of
about 3 to 5 feet with shaft spacing of about 4 to 6 feet on center may be used for
preliminary design purpose A typical wall section is shown on the Drilled Shaft
Retaining Wall Plate 3

Pavement Design Methodology and Parameters
The design pavement section was generally determined based on the
1993 AASHTO Guide Procedure for the Design of Pavement Structures As
specified by the City and County of Honolulu the pavement was designed for a
pavement life of 30 years
We understand that the design traffic parameters for this project were not available
at the time this report was prepared Research was conducted to estimate the
truck traffic quantity and distribution Based on TheBus schedule the Route 56 bus
travels along this portion of Kaneohe Bay Drive about 21 to 28 times per day per
direction Refuse recyclables and green waste collection along Kaneohe Bay
Drive occurs twice a week on Wednesday and Saturday Additional truck traffic to
Kaneohe Yacht Club and Keaalu Neighborhood Park are anticipated Based on the
above an average monthly truck traffic of 1 100 trucks per direction was used in
the analysis with 100 percent of the truck traffic consisting of 2 axle trucks
Based on a design period of 30 years the traffic volume and the truck distribution
information provided a design Equivalent Single Axle Load ESAL of
has been determined for the project Detailed analyses on the design ESAL are
presented in Appendix D
We envision the majority of the roadway will generally be constructed at or near the
existing grades or in a cut condition Based on our field exploration we anticipate
the majority of the pavement subgrade soils will generally consist of soft to stiff fill
materials composed of clay soils Laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR
values of 36 and 75 were obtained for near surface soils recovered adjacent to the
roadway The CBR samples consisted of clayey gravel with sand Based on the
CBR sample description and test results we believe that the CBR values do not
represent the clay subgrade soils below the existing pavement The results of our
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests are presented in Appendix B
Based on the Atterberg Limits test results Plasticity Index PI values of 34 and
39 were obtained for the clay subgrade soils These PI values correlate to CBR

ground and the excavation should be replaced with compacted aggregate base
course material
The aggregate base course materials should consist of materials as specified in
Section 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction
September 1986 The aggregate base course material should be compacted to a
density of at least 95 percent of the maximum dry density and the maximum
compacted thickness of each layer should not exceed 6 inches
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the roadway improvement site by
drilling and sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 each
extending to a depth of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 2
and B 3
Six Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
in situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 4 through B 9
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture
content of the soils Compaction of the samples was performed by dropping a 10 pound
hammer from a height of 18 inches The samples were compacted in five equal layers with
each layer receiving 56 blows of energy The samples were soaked in water prior to the
penetration test The test results are presented on Plates B 10 and B 11
