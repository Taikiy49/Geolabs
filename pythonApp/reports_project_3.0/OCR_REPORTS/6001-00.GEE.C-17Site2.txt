SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed C-17 Site 2 — New Security Lighting project located within
Hickam Air Force Base on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the new security lighting
project. These recommendations are intended for the design of light pole foundations
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The proposed lighting improvements project is within the Hickam Air Force Base
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project consists of installing new lighting facilities
around the C-17 parking ramp. Light poles will also be installed at locations within the
perimeter of the parking ramp.
1.3. Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions at the project site to develop a soil/rock data set to formulate
geotechnical recommendations for design of drilled shaft foundations for the proposed
project. In order to accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of
the following tasks and efforts:
1. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site.
2. Drilling and sampling of nine borings extending to depths of about
18.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
3. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our field
engineer.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active; thus, Waianae’s eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau
lavas in the central Oahu region.
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation. As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced, less
water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present
stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, a larger volume of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. Thus,
the island shorelines have transgressed and regressed in response to the fluctuations.
The processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments were
affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the
erosional base level was correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths
below the present sea level. As a result, terrestrial sediments were carried further
seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments. When the sea level was
high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations. In addition, it allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as coral and
limestone in environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils and rock.
Interpretation of the historic geology of the coastal plain regions located on the
Island of Oahu is complex due to a long history of changes in sea level that occurred in
relation to the island land surface. The overall changes in sea level in relation to the
land surface resulted from a combination of tectonic forces (such as uplift and
settlement of the island landmass) and the previously discussed glacio-eustatic ocean
level changes. In other words, the rise and fall of sea level in relation to the island

produced shorelines of emergence and submergence. In general, the overall changes
of sea level in relation to the landmass also caused the erosional base level to fluctuate
producing features such as drowned stream valleys filled with marine deposits and
conversely, incised marine deposits filled with terrestrial sediments or rock.
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level changes and
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following the cessation
of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor.
The ancient sea level fluctuated several times both above and below the present
sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Epoch, eruption
of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older
alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, which consolidated to form volcanic tuff
(locally called "mudrock") layers. Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those
mantles of tuff.
During the recent epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was
covered with layers of marine deposits. These deposits were subsequently covered by
man-made fill placed during the middle portion of this century for land reclamation
projects during construction of the existing Hickam Air Force Base.
2.2. Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic activity.
Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of
magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can
only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are
strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor-to-moderate damage.

In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent)
are related to volcanism, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has
experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however,
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii.
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in
the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and
Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small landslides occurred on the
Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (M6.8). In addition, some
houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai
Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). .
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of an earthquake
occurring on the Island of Oahu that is greater than Magnitude 6. An earthquake of
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island of
Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building walls,
and broken underground water mains.

2.4 Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
nine borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to depths of about
18.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations
are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3.
Our borings generally encountered 2 inches of asphaltic concrete pavement or
2 inches to 14 inches of top soil at the ground surface, underlain by coralline detritus
and coral formation, extending to about 5 to 13.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
Below the coralline materials, our borings encountered volcanic tuff, extending to the
maximum depths explored of about 18.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
It should be noted that we encountered about 1 to 4.5 feet of fill, consisting of coralline
sand with gravel, silt and clay below the pavement and top soils in Boring Nos. 3, 7, 8
and 9.
We encountered groundwater at depths varying from approximately
4.7 to 8.9 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. It
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal
precipitation, storm surge conditions, and other factors.
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered and water levels observed in
the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. The results of the
laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples retrieved during our field
exploration are presented in Appendix B.

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our borings drilled at the proposed light pole locations generally encountered a thin
layer of fill overlying coral formation and coral detritus underlain by volcanic tuff. We
encountered groundwater at various depths ranging from 4.7 to 8.9 feet below the
existing ground surface in the borings drilled.
Based on the loading conditions provided by the project structural engineer and
the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend supporting the new light poles
with drilled shaft foundations. Based on the light pole sizes, the available auger/core
barrel sizes from the local drilling companies, and the shaft lengths anticipated, drilled
shafts with minimum shaft diameters of 48 and 54 inches should be used to support the
proposed light poles.
The major geotechnical considerations in our foundation analyses included the
lateral loads and ground line moments induced in the drilled shaft foundations. The
recommended lengths of the drilled shaft foundations were about 20 feet below the
ground surface. Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
recommendations for design are presented in the following sections herein.
3.1 Light Pole Foundations
Based on the materials encountered in our field exploration, we believe that the
proposed light poles may be supported by drilled shaft type foundations to resist the
generally high ground line moments. We conducted foundation analyses based on a
combination of axial and lateral loads in addition to the ground line moments using the
computer program LPILE-Plus for Windows. In addition, the drilled shaft foundations
were also checked for the torsional moments induced by the light poles. The analyses
were based on a free-head condition and a maximum lateral deflection of no more than
0.5 inches at the top of the drilled shaft.
Based on our field exploration, medium hard to hard coral formation and volcanic
tuff will be encountered during the drilled shaft installation. The presence of coral
formation and volcanic tuff will likely require the use of special drilling tools (core drilling

3.1.3 Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the loading conditions assumed and the subsurface conditions
encountered, we recommend using drilled shaft foundations to support the new
security light poles.
Based on the pole sizes, the available auger/core barrel sizes from the local
drilling companies, and the shaft lengths anticipated, we considered drilled shaft
diameters of 48 and 54 inches in our foundation analyses for the single-shaft
foundations. The major geotechnical considerations in our foundation analyses
included the ground surface conditions, subsurface conditions, lateral loads and
ground line moments induced in the drilled shaft foundations.
In general, lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable deflection at the top of
the shaft, and induced moment in the shaft. Based on a maximum horizontal
deflection of 0.5 inches at the top of the drilled shaft subjected to the ground line
overturning moments and lateral forces, we evaluated the required drilled shaft
lengths using the computer program, LPILE-Plus for Windows. This program is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded pile program
originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program solves for
deflection and bending moment along a drilled shaft under lateral loads as a
function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of internally
generated non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection
was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths. The
analyses were performed for a laterally loaded, cast-in-place concrete drilled
shaft. The results of our engineering analyses are summarized in the following

3.1.5 Construction Considerations
The performance of the drilled shafts significantly depends upon the contractor's
method of construction, construction procedures, and workmanship. As a result,
a Geolabs representative should be present to observe the installation of drilled
shafts during construction. In our opinion, the following may have an impact on
the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations at the site.
It should be noted that volcanic tuff will be encountered during the drilled shaft
installation, and will likely require the use of special drilling tools (core drilling
tools) to advance the drilled shaft holes to the recommended embedment depths.
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends, to a large extent, on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper
construction techniques are important. The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes. The bottom of the

drilled shafts should be cleaned of disturbed material prior to constructing the
shaft. The bottom of the drilled shaft hole should also be relatively level. Backfill
against the drilled shaft foundations should not be allowed.
Groundwater conditions are anticipated at the site; therefore, placement of
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required. A low-
shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch slump range) should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace disturbed
material from the bottom of the drilled shaft.
Placement of concrete using a tremie pipe will likely cause some groundwater
and soil to discharge out from the top of the drilled shaft. The contractor should
be made aware that this fluid should be properly disposed of. Because the water
in the drilled hole will be turbid from the soil particles, disposal of the displaced
groundwater into storm drains will not be acceptable. The contractor should also
be made aware that a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES)
permit may be necessary, depending on the proposed method of groundwater
disposal. The contractor should consult their independent consultant or the
State of Hawaii - Department of Health for the latest regulations and information
pertaining to the permit application.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
nine borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to maximum depths of
about 18.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight auger tools. The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3.
Our engineer/geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by
visual and textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-9.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition,
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths.
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1.
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B-2.
