SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering services performed for the Kuhio Highway Resurfacing and Safety 
Improvements, Princeville to Waikoko project in the District of Hanalei on the Island of 
Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project involves pavement rehabilitation, pavement reconstruction, and 
localized widening of Kuhio Highway from MP 0.0 to MP 4.5 in the Hanalei area on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. This portion of the highway within the project scope of work 
measures about 23,600 linear feet of asphaltic concrete paved roadway. The highway is 
a two-lane roadway with one lane of traffic in each direction and minimal shoulder width. 
Based on the available plans, we understand that new concrete sidewalks and a soil 
reinforced swale also are planned at Hanalei Town. 
We understand that the proposed pavement rehabilitation and reconstruction 
project will be federally funded. Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to 
the requirements and guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation 
will be required. The new pavements will be designed based on the methodology 
described in the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 and approved amendments 
prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, 
Materials Testing and Research Branch.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our pavement design analysis are the geologic conditions of the 
project site, the existing site and pavement conditions, the thickness of the existing 
pavement structural section, the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the 
engineering properties of the materials encountered, and the variability of the subsurface 
conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following subsections provide a 
description of the geologic setting of the project site, the existing pavement conditions, 
and the surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano built 
by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 5 to 
6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 2½ million 
years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the cessation of this 
main volcano shield-building phase, there was renewed volcanic activity about 1½ million 
years ago with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series 
and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands and encompasses 
approximately 555 square miles with a maximum elevation of about 5,170 feet above 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) at Mount Waialeale near the center of the island. It is believed 
that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene Epoch), 
basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly circular 
island volcano from the ocean floor, which is estimated to have been about 20,000 feet 
deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this main shield building volcanic 
phase came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was 
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1½ million years ago) with the 
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This 
post-erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic 
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Because of

alluvial deposits. Basaltic rocks of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series are anticipated at 
greater depths.

undivided asphaltic concrete roadway except for the additional turn lanes at or near the

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 47 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 2 through 48, at selected locations along Kuhio Highway 
between MP 0.0 and MP 4.5 extending to depths of about 5 to 31.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. Six bulk samples of the subgrade soils, designated as Bulk-1 through 
Bulk-6, also were obtained below the existing pavement section to evaluate their 
pavement support characteristics and swell potential. A General Site Plan showing the 
approximate limits of the project site is presented on Plate 2. The approximate boring and 
bulk sample locations are presented on Site Plan-1 through Site Plan-24 (Plates 3.1 to 
3.24). 
Cores of the existing asphaltic concrete pavements were obtained from each 
boring location to determine the thickness of the existing asphaltic concrete layer. In 
addition, the approximate thickness of the subbase, where encountered, were measured. 
A table presenting a summary of the measured thickness of the asphaltic concrete and 
subbase materials observed at each boring location is provided on Plates 4.1 and 4.2.

consisted of medium stiff to stiff clayey soils. Localized soft zones were encountered in 
the residual soil layer. The residual and saprolitic soils extended to depths of about 15 to

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the current design concept and guidelines, reconstruction of the 
pavement will be considered in areas where moderate to severe structural distresses are 
present. These areas will be constructed with a new pavement. In addition, new 
pavements are planned at areas where roadway widening is being considered.  
In addition, rehabilitation of the existing pavement will be considered in areas with 
minor distresses. These areas generally are rehabilitated by cold-planing to remove a 
portion of the existing AC pavement followed by an AC overlay. We understand that 
polymer modified asphaltic concrete will be used for the AC overlay. 
In general, the following sections provide discussion on the areas necessary for 
pavement analyses and design for reconstruction and rehabilitation. In addition, 
geotechnical recommendations for design of concrete sidewalks and soil reinforced grass 
swales are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The procedures used in determining the new pavement sections for reconstruction 
and localized roadway widening generally were determined based on the methodology 
described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002. The 
Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. The 
pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed 
and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans).

3.7 
Concrete Sidewalks 
Based on the available plans, concrete sidewalks are planned in Hanalei Town 
between Station 396+37.64 and Station 401+06.42. The near-surface soils anticipated in 
this area likely consist of clayey alluvial soils. Based on laboratory testing, these clayey 
alluvial soils exhibit a moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture 
fluctuations. Therefore, we recommend providing a minimum of 18 inches of non-
expansive select granular fill or aggregate subbase material below the concrete sidewalks 
to reduce the potential of appreciable structural distress to these lightly loaded concrete 
slabs. 
The soil subgrades below the concrete sidewalks and concrete curbs and gutters 
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2% above the optimum moisture, and compacted to no less than 90% relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. The subgrade should be kept moist until 
covered by the select granular fill (or aggregate subbase material). 
Furthermore, the subgrades should be kept moist prior to the final placement of 
concrete. Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. 
The areas adjacent to the concrete sidewalks should be backfilled tightly against 
the slab edges with relatively impervious soils (on-site silty soils) and graded to divert 
water away from the slabs to reduce the potential for appreciable water ponding around 
the slab edges.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling 
and sampling 47 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 2 through 48, extending to depths 
of about 5 to 31.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on Site Plan-1 through Site Plan-24 (Plates 3.1 to 3.24). The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-
47. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend (Plate A-0.3). The Rock Description System is based on the publication

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seventeen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-1 and 
B-2. 
Twelve Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-3 through B-5. 
Eighteen laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. Three CBR tests with varying compaction efforts 
corresponding to 10, 25, and 56 blows per layer were performed for each bulk sample. 
The test results are presented on Plates B-6 through B-23. 
Six Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-24 through B-29. 
Six laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by Ninyo 
& Moore of California on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-30 through 
B-35.
