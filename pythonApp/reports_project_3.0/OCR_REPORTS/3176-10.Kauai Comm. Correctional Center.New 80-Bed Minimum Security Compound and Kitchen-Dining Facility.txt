INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed New 80 Bed Minimum Security Compound and Kitchen
Dining Facility project located within the Kauai Community Correctional Center KCCC
in the Wailua area on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation retaining wall site
grading and pavement only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas
of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island
of Kauai Rocks of the Koloa Voicanic series are generally characterized as thick flows
of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade construction will be used for the
proposed laundry room structure Based on a finish floor elevation of about 11 75 feet
MSL we anticipate that fills on the order of about 3 to 4 feet relative to the existing
ground surface will be required to provide a level pad for the proposed structure
Prior to filling operation the subgrade to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction In general the fill
material needed to raise the grades to the finish subgrade should consist of on site
sandy soils and or imported non expansive granular material The fill material should
be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In accordance with the requirements of D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61
dated a basaltic termite barrier BTB will need to be incorporated into
the design of new facilities Therefore concrete floor slabs should be supported on a
4 inch layer of compacted BTB above the compacted non expansive fill layer to serve
as both a slab cushion and a termite barrier Due to the cohesionless nature of the
BTB we recommend that the BTB be moisture conditioned and compacted with a
vibratory plate or similar equipment
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the compacted BTB to reduce potential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3176 10 Page 11
migration of moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more
uniform curing of the slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane
we recommend that a 2 inch layer of BTB be piaced above the membrane prior to
placing the slab concrete
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as driveways
and loading areas a 6 inch layer of base course aggregate is recommended below the
concrete slab to serve as a slab cushion in lieu of the basaltic termite barrier The
base course supporting the concrete slab should consist of crushed basalt aggregates
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
For structural slabs bearing on compacted base course a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used We recommend that the
concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should
be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate some amount of differential
movements The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
We understand that exterior concrete walkways will be required for the proposed
project We recommend that concrete slabs for the walkways bear on 4 inches of
select borrow or 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation Where select borrow
is used the select borrow should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction The on site subgrade soils should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of
the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections

Retaining Walls
Based on the information provided we anticipate that retaining walls with heights
of up to about 5 feet will be constructed for grade separation at the proposed laundry
room location We understand that the retaining walls are expected to be conventional
concrete retaining walls General guidelines for the design of the retaining wall
structures are as follows
1 Foundations for retaining walls should be designed in general accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this
report The footing embedment for retaining walls should be a minimum
of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
Where wall footings are to be located adjacent to the existing retaining
structures the footings should be embedded below the bottom of the
existing retaining walls This requirement is necessary to reduce the
potential for surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential of foundation settlement
2 All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth
pressures due to adjacent soils and surcharge effects An active lateral
earth pressure of 35 pounds per cubic foot p c f expressed in
equivalent fluid pressure is recommended for design of cantilever walls
For at rest earth pressure a value of 50 p s f equivalent fluid pressure
per foot of depth may be used
The value provided above is for on site sandy soils or imported granular
soils Backfills behind retaining walls should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to between 90 and
GEOLABS HAWAIi

95 percent relative compaction Over compaction of the wall backfill
should be avoided This lateral earth pressure does not include
hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped
behind the wails
3 The surcharge effect due to traffic areal surcharges line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the height of the wall should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the
loaded side of the wall a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure
equal to 30 percent of the surcharge pressure acting on the entire height
of the wall which is free to deflect cantilever may be used in the design
4 For lateral load resistance a passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per

compacted backfill material The drainage material should extend from
the base of the wall to a height within 2 feet below finish grade The
remaining backfill should consist of an impervious capping of select non
expansive soil to reduce water infiltration behind the walls

Site Grading
Based on the preliminary grading plans provided we anticipate that site grading
will consist of filis on the order of 3 to 4 feet at the proposed laundry room location At
the new housing and kitchen dining room locations very minimal grading consisting
only of foundation preparation is anticipated
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs
Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe whether the
exposed soil conditions during excavation are similar to those encountered in our
exploration The following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the
design of site grading work
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and properly disposed of off site Any soft weak or
yielding areas encountered during site grading should be over excavated to expose firm
ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with lean concrete or free
draining material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation due to the
presence of a shallow groundwater table
In areas to receive fill the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For
subgrades of improvements subjected to vehicular traffic such as pavements and
driveway the compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3176 10 Page 15
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The on site sandy soils generated from the excavation may be reused as a
source of fill and backfill material The on site soils should be properly moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
Permanent cut and fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched
If imported fill materials are required the imported fill and or backfill material
should consist of weil graded granular materials less than 3 inches in maximum
dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value
of 15 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain
between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill materials should
be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with our recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use Imported materials should be brought
to above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inches loose lifts and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines would be required for the proposed project It
should be noted that groundwater was encountered within 2 feet of the existing ground
surface Therefore any trench excavation deeper than 2 to 3 feet will likely encounter
groundwater In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
be used under the pipes The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes or 1 foot
above the groundwater level should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B
Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the
backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes or
1 foot above the groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should
consist of select granular material or the on site granular soils The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are
below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3176 10 Page 18
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water
infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface soils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings to depths of about 16 and 21 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Pian Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary drilling tools
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
