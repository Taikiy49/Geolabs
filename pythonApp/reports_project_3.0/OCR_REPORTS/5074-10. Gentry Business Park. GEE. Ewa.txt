2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice
Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As volcanic
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau Known as Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and to
the south these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations forming a sedimentary wedge The thickness
of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1
freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as
the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials

depths ranging from about 2 to 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface The coralline

3 3 Corrosion Potential
Two sets of laboratory corrosivity tests including Minimum
Resistivity ASTM G57 Chloride Content and Sulfate Content
were performed by our laboratory and TestAmerica Laboratories Inc on selected soil
samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the on site soils
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower
the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results the subsurface soils at the
project site have resistivity values ranging from about 1 000 to 1 400 ohm cm based on
the soluble soil sample prepared in accordance with Test Method ASTM G57 and pH
values of 7 8 to 7 3 in accordance with Test Method ASTM G51 corresponding to
corrosion ratings of 1 Extremely Corrosive to 2 Very Corrosive based on the guidelines
provided by the City County of Honolulu Board of Water Supply
Levels of the chloride content of the on site soils encountered near the existing
ground surface are about 250 parts per million ppm while the sulfate content ranged
from about 9 7 to 150 ppm Design of metallic substructures such as water line pipes or
other metallic piping should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the
substructure Based on the chloride content tests performed on the on site soils it may
be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results
and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection

In general we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of

The initial backfill up to 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills
such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill
settlement and pipe damage The use of on site soils as backfill immediately around utility
pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or select granular fill
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence The upper 2 feet below the finished grade in areas
subjected to vehicular traffic should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials
at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to compact the
backfill material
3 5 Future Slabs On Grade and Building Foundations
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with moderate swelling potential Unless floor slabs are properly
designed and constructed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these soils due to changes in the
moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the
shrinking and swelling of the subgrade soils and to facilitate future slabs on grade
construction we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 107 extending to depths of about
20 25 to 22 5 feet below the existing ground surface In addition three bulk samples of
the near surface soils designated as Bulk 101 through Bulk 103 were obtained to
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils The approximate
boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers and coring equipment
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Deviations made
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil
Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials encountered
are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test result is
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Core samples of the coral materials encountered at the project site were obtained by
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow
bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were described in
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice
Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As volcanic
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and to
the south these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations forming a sedimentary wedge The thickness
of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1
freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as
the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials

3 3 Corrosion Potential
Two sets of laboratory corrosivity tests including Minimum
Resistivity ASTM G57 Chloride Content and Sulfate Content
were performed by our laboratory and TestAmerica Laboratories Inc on selected soil
samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the on site soils
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower
the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results the subsurface soils at the
project site have resistivity values ranging from about 1 000 to 1 400 ohm cm based on
the soluble soil sample prepared in accordance with Test Method ASTM G57 and pH
values of 7 8 to 7 3 in accordance with Test Method ASTM G51 corresponding to
corrosion ratings of 1 Extremely Corrosive to 2 Very Corrosive based on the guidelines
provided by the City County of Honolulu Board of Water Supply
Levels of the chloride content of the on site soils encountered near the existing
ground surface are about 250 parts per million ppm while the sulfate content ranged
from about 9 7 to 150 ppm Design of metallic substructures such as water line pipes or
other metallic piping should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the
substructure Based on the chloride content tests performed on the on site soils it may
be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results
and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection

Findings and Recommendations
Based on the results of the level surveys and our observations our findings and
recommendations are provided below
The level surveys from 2012 to 2017 indicate similar movement trends The
interior concrete slabs are lower by 1 to 2 inches along the northwest and southwest
exterior walls of the building The interior portions of the concrete slabs are generally
fairly level
The major building movements appear to be along the western side of the
building along Haumea Street and the school driveway This corresponds to the major
movement observed inside the classroom building
There are signs of smaller movements along other ends of the building
The differential movements noted between many of the building footings and
adjacent concrete slab on grade indicate that either the footings settled or the concrete
slabs heaved or possibly both The building cracking visible on the exterior indicates
that footing settlement may be the major cause of the damage
The level readings indicate that the movements may have stabilized or at least
slowed considerably Therefore major additional movements or damage are not
expected However small movements may continue and cause cosmetic damage
Additional chemical stabilizer injection is not recommended as the interior
concrete slab seems stable
Extensive interior demolition and reconstruction of foundations is not
recommended at this time as the very high expense for interior work does not seem
justified as the building movements seem to have stabilized
Stiffening of the exterior building foundations and protection against water
infiltration is recommended to reduce future movements This may be accomplished by
the installation of exterior grade beams and sidewalks around the building The project
structural engineer has developed a preliminary design for this purpose
Limitations
Our findings and recommendations are based on visual observations and level
survey data and previous subsurface explorations only The actual conditions below the
building may vary

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the following
five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent geologic
time Hualalai last erupted in Loa and Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation observed at the project
site prior to site grading activities appears to consist of both a a and pahoehoe type lava
flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean Aja lava is typically
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders A a lava is generally
present at the ground surface of the project site Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a
smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are encountered commonly in basaltic lavas especially the pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state and they represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the primary water transmission features
Subsurface Conditions
As mentioned previously the Kaupulehu Lot 4A Increment 1 project site is in the
Kaupulehu area in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii In general the original
topography of the project site prior to site grading activities consisted of irregular a a lava
flow terrain at the ground surface
Based on observations during the mass grading operations the project site is

basalt formation at greater depths It should be noted that lava tubes cavities and blisters

operations It should be noted that near surface lava tubes detected within the limits of

from within the collapsed lava tube were removed The resulting excavation was backfilled
with compacted fill in general accordance with the recommendations outlined in the

Discussion and Recommendations
Based on the grading plan for the project the original ground surface elevations
ranged from about 28 to 34 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at Phase 2 Lot No 23A
Phase 2 Lot No 23A generally was graded to a finished pad elevation of about 30 feet
MSL Mass grading at Phase 2 Lot No 23A generally consisted of excavation of up to
about 4 feet and placement of up to about 2 feet of new fills to achieve the design grades
During the mass grading operations extensive subgrade preparation procedures
were employed within the limits of grading at the commencement of the mass grading
operations for the project to reduce the potential for collapse of near surface cavities and or
voids within the lots The intent of the subgrade preparation procedures is to assist in
detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials It should be noted that the subgrade preparation procedures
employed for this project are consistent with the standard of practice for similar type projects
and similar subsurface conditions along the Kohala Coast on the Island of Hawaii
In general the subgrade preparation in fill areas consisted of ripping the original
ground to a depth of about 3 feet or until refusal using a ripper tooth of a large bulldozer to
assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials After fill surfaces have been ripped the ground generally was
leveled with the use of a Komatsu 375 and or Komatsu 475 bulldozer working the surface
for about four passes to provide a relatively level surface After leveling of the rugged
surfaces with the bulldozer the fill subgrades generally were proof rolled with a
Caterpillar 825 sheepsfoot roller compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to
help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids
Fill materials consisting of processed on site materials including a a basalt and or
dense pahoehoe basalt were placed in the fill areas of the project site Structural fills
consisted of well graded granular materials generally smaller than 6 inches in dimension
Subgrades in cut areas generally were proof rolled with a Caterpillar 825 sheepsfoot
roller compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to help detect and collapse
near surface cavities and or voids It should be noted that some portions of Lot No 23A
subgrades were graded in a cut condition Therefore the presence of a a basalt should be
anticipated at or near the existing pad elevation
