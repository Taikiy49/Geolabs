INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our field percolation tests performed for the proposed
leach fields and infiltration basins to be located near the proposed Waimanalo Kupuna
Housing Project in the District of Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration and engineering analyses These recommendations are
intended for the design of leach fields and infiltration basins only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct leach fields for sewage disposal and infiltration basins for
storm water disposal in the area northeast of the proposed Waimanalo Kupuna Housing
Project near Kalanianaole Highway The existing ground elevations at the site for the
proposed leach fields and infiltration basins range from about 12 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
to 18 feet MSL The infiltration basins are anticipated to be about 5 to 10 feet in depth

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
GEOLABS INC
W O 3798 10 Page 4
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into
the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and
tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the ocean basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level was
high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations In addition when the stand of the sea was high and prolonged marine deposits
accumulated at elevations above the current sea level
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls which are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the
stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general stream flows in Hawaii are
intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very
short durations Because of this transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the
stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravels
sands and fines When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as
deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin to
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of
GEOLABS INC
W O 3798 10 Page 5
the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously
erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This
continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older
alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits
At the project site below the alluvial material are primarily coral algal deposits which
are composed of medium hard limestone with an open lattice like structure The coral
formation has also been subjected to subaerial weathering
Coral algal materials are chemically composed of calcium carbonate During subaerial
weathering slightly acidic rain and groundwater can slowly attack the carbonate dissolving
the weaker portions of the formation This creates solution cavities ranging from small pores
to large caverns

Leach Fields
Two falling head percolation tests were performed in two separate shallow probe holes
The testing was performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of
the Ten States Standards to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials
for wastewater disposal septic tanks or leach fields Our two percolation tests were
performed by drilling 4 inch diameter probe holes to depths of about 4 8 to 5 feet below the
existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment The
approximate locations of our probe holes are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the probe holes to
protect the bottom of the holes from scour and sediments During testing the holes were
carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the gravel pack The time
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which calculation of the
percolation rates were based The results of our percolation tests are presented in Appendix A
of this report
Based on the percolation tests conducted the slower percolation rate was about
4 minutes per inch This percolation rate corresponds to a recommended maximum rate of
sewage application of 2 5 gallons per square foot per day
To increase the probability of intersecting the more permeable layers we recommend
that leach fields be set at least 5 feet below grade In addition if clay material is encountered
during construction the excavation for the leach fields should be extended further down
During construction we recommend that percolation tests be performed to obtain approximate
percolation rates to confirm the absorption capacity of the disposal system

It should also be noted that based on the guidelines presented in the State of Hawaii
bottom of the absorption trenches should not be closer than 2 feet to the seasonal high
groundwater level Based on an approximate elevation of about 12 feet MSL for the leach
field site and assuming a water table fluctuation of about 3 feet the absorption trenches should
not be deeper than 7 feet below grade
Infiltration Basins
Three falling head percolation tests were performed in three separate probe holes The
testing was performed in general accordance with the Auger Hole method This method is a
variation of the borehole method in which water is added to fill an auger hole at a given level
and then the fall of the water level is observed with time The permeability is based on the
assumption that a unit hydraulic gradient causes the flow through the bottom and sides of the
hole Our three percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 inch diameter probe holes to
depths of about 5 3 to 6 7 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with auger equipment The approximate locations of our probe holes are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
The holes were then filled with water in an attempt to saturate the surrounding soils
The saturation phase was continued until the amount of head drop per a unit of time became
fairly constant During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to near its top The
time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation
of the permeability rates were based The results of our percolation tests are presented in
Appendix B of this report
Based on the results of our field percolation tests we believe that a permeability rate
basins This permeability rate corresponds to a disposal rate of about 20 gallons per square
foot per day For the purpose of design we recommend that only the bottom area of the
infiltration basin be used to determine the capacity of the infiltration basins The design depth
of the infiltration basin should be based on the anticipated amount of water retained prior to

It should be noted that some siltation may occur and therefore lower the permeability

the infiltration basin Otherwise some type of maintenance program should be implemented
We discourage the construction of infiltration units elsewhere along Road B as we

Slopes
Our field exploration indicate that the site is generally underlain by loose to medium
dense silty calcareous sand deposits calcareous sand deposits and coral formations
Therefore it is our opinion that excavations for the infiltration basins may be constructed with
slope inclinations up to two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V In addition we recommend that
the tops of the slopes be set back a minimum distance of 10 feet from roads and structures
The excavated sand and coral material may be suitable for fill material for other portions
of the project provided that the largest rock fragments in the material is less than 3 inches in
maximum dimension and that the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious materials

Excavation Method
The contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations should determine
the method and equipment to be used for excavation Generally the in situ coral formation
encountered in our exploration should be rippable by a large bulldozer or other normal
heavy excavation equipment However due to the nature of the coral formation hoerams
and or chipping may be required in localized areas of hard excavation Therefore
allowances should be made for additional time and expense that may be required by the
hard excavation
