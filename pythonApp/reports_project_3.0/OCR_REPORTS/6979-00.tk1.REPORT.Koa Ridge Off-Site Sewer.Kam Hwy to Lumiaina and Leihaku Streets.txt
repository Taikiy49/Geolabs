SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the proposed Koa Ridge off-site sewer alignment is 
generally underlain by residual soils, consisting of stiff to hard clayey silts, overlying 
saprolites and/or basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 48 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
We believe that the stiff to hard clayey subsoils generally encountered in the 
shallow subsurface along the sewer line alignment would provide adequate support for the 
new pipes without the need for special foundation support. In addition, the stiff clayey soils 
would allow the open-trench excavation to proceed with normal excavation 
method/equipment and shoring requirements. Based on the generally stiff subsoil 
conditions encountered and the anticipated depths of excavation, we believe that steel 
plates with horizontal struts may be considered for temporary shoring for the new sewer 
line planned for the project. For deeper excavations and excavations adjacent to existing 
buildings and utilities, we recommend considering interlocking steel sheet piling with 
horizontal bracing for excavation support in order to reduce the potential for appreciable 
adjacent ground movement. It should be noted that the potential for presence of boulders 
and shallow basalt rock formation in localized areas could pose some difficulties during the 
trench excavation and shoring installation. 
Based on our evaluation and engineering analyses, we believe that microtunneling 
method is a feasible construction alternative for the portion of sewer line traversing 
Kamehameha Highway. An assessment of the ground conditions likely to be encountered 
along the microtunneling excavations is important for evaluating the face stability of the 
excavations and to provide information on the selection of appropriate equipment and 
methods for the tunnel and shaft construction. The ground conditions should be assessed 
to reduce the potential for loss of ground, surface settlement, and damage to existing 
utilities, roadways, and other improvements as a result of the planned tunneling 
operations. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas 
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded 
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene 
Epoch. The dips of the lava beds are generally near horizontal (between 3 to 5 degrees 
from horizontal). The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in-situ weathering 
extending to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil.  
Along the sewer line alignment, in-situ chemical weathering of the lava has 
formed a mantle of residual soils that grade to saprolite with depth. In general, saprolite 
is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic 
rocks. It should be noted that spheroidally weathered boulders are typical in the 
Schofield Plateau. The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth.

Further discussions on the general characteristics of the geologic units 
encountered in the borings are presented in the following subsections.  
2.3.1 Residual Soils 
Residual soils are completely weathered volcanic rocks that have lost all visible 
rock texture characteristics. The residual soils at the project site are typically brown 
to reddish brown in color. The residual soils may be mottled with various colors.

Residual soils are comprised mainly of clayey materials with stiff to very stiff 
consistency. Typically, residual soils occur overlying saprolitic soils or weathered 
basalt formation. It should be noted that relict boulders of hard rock may be 
encountered in residual soils. In addition, there is potential for encountering hard 
basalt rock ledges in residual soils because the volcanic rock may not have 
weathered uniformly throughout the rock formation. Some relict boulders of hard 
rock occasionally may be encountered in residual soils. 
2.3.2 Saprolitic Soils 
Extremely weathered volcanic rock is referred to as saprolitic soils (or saprolites). 
Saprolitic soils represent rock that has been reduced by weathering to soil-like 
components with weathered rock fragments. The soil material retains the remnant 
rock texture such as layering, vesicularity, and some fracture patterns. Saprolitic 
soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and are composed of 
mainly silty materials. The coloring scheme of saprolitic soils depends on the type 
of parent volcanic rock from which the soils are derived and the degree of chemical 
weathering that the material has experienced. Because the parent material does 
not weather uniformly throughout the formation, relict boulders of hard rock and/or 
hard rock ledges occasionally may be encountered within saprolitic soils. 
2.3.3 Basalt Formation 
Basalt formations include a broad range of volcanic basaltic rock. The basalt rock 
material was extruded as sequential layered lava flows from volcanic vents. The 
basalt rock, which frequently contain interbedded porous clinker materials range 
from very hard to soft rock. Weathering (decomposition) ranges between highly 
weathered and slightly weathered. Fracture discontinuities within basalt rock 
formations range from severely fractured (shattered and broken) to relatively 
unfractured and massive in character.

3.1 
Microtunneling 
We understand that it is desired to construct the portion of sewer line traversing 
Kamehameha Highway using microtunneling method. Microtunneling is a trenchless 
process for installing underground pipelines, ducts, culverts, and utilities. The technique 
involves the use of powerful hydraulic jacks that push or jack successive pipe sections 
through the ground behind a remotely controlled excavating unit or cutting disk head at 
the same time as the excavation is taking place within the excavating unit. The following 
briefly describe the advantages and methods of construction for microtunneling. 
In general, use of microtunneling techniques may offer the following major 
advantages or benefits as compared to the conventional open trench method of 
construction. 
1. 
Reduced disturbance and potential damage to adjacent utilities, existing 
roads, and surrounding structures. 
2. 
Improved public and job site safety. 
3. 
Reduced surface disruption to vehicular and pedestrian traffic. 
4. 
Reduced disturbance to local residents and businesses. 
5. 
Reduced air and noise pollution and vibration. 
6. 
Greater flexibility in alignment selection and reduced need for utility 
relocations. 
7. 
Reduced need for extensive shoring system. 
8. 
Reduced need for significant dewatering. 
9. 
Smaller volumes of spoils that require transportation and disposal. 
10. 
Reduced potential for treatment of contaminated soil and groundwater. 
In general, the tunneling methods selected would depend on the stability of the 
ground. Where unstable ground conditions are likely to be encountered, the face of the 
tunnel must be made stable to prevent ground loss and to enable mining to take place 
safely. This may be achieved by the following methods: 
1. 
Dewatering by well points or deep wells. 
2. 
Grouting by the use of cement, cement/bentonite, or chemicals. 
3. 
Ground freezing. 
4. 
Using specialized tunneling methods, such as the following:

The choice of process is a function of the nature of the ground, the water content, 
and in particular the particle size analysis of the subsoils encountered. In order to install 
a pipeline using this technique, jacking and receiving pits will need to be constructed. A 
concrete thrust block, constructed behind the jacking frame within the jacking pit, 
provides the reaction to the hydraulic jacks. In weak/poor ground conditions, piling or 
other arrangements (ground improvement) may need to be employed to increase the 
reaction capability of the thrust block. The hydraulic jacks, pressing against the back of 
the pipeline, advance the disk-cutting head into the soil formation. To ensure that the 
jacking forces are distributed around the circumference of a pipe being jacked, a thrust 
ring is normally provided with the design depending on the number of jacks being used. 
The jacks are inter-connected hydraulically to ensure that the thrust from each jack is 
the same. The number of jacks used may vary depending on the pipe size, the strength 
of the jacking pipes, the length to be installed, and the anticipated frictional resistance. 
The disk-cutting head of the tunnel boring machine is controlled via cables from a 
cabin on the surface where the operator monitors speed, pressure, and tunneling 
accuracy. At the face of the tunnel, a rotating cutter head digs into the soil and rock and 
mixes it with groundwater and water pumped from the surface. Rocky material is 
reduced to a maximum ¾-inch size in a crushing chamber located directly behind the 
cutter disk. The excavated material is pumped out of the tunnel as slurry. The water 
introduced into the machine is matched in pressure and volume to the material being 
displaced. This prevents over-excavation of the tunnel face, thereby reducing the 
potential for cave-in of the soil surrounding the new casing. 
The cutting disk speed and direction of rotation, the hydraulic jacking system, as 
well as the slurry system operations, can be controlled from one control panel located 
remotely at the launch pit. The hydraulic steering cylinders located on the disk-cutting 
head can be extended to steer the head back on course should any deviation be

assessing the viability and feasibility of microtunneling techniques and in 
determining appropriate construction techniques. For the design and cost

3.1.3 Thrust Block Resistance 
The thrust block at the jacking pit provides the reaction for the main jacks when 
pushing the pipe forward. The allowable passive pressure resistance of the soil, 
which is a function of the strength of the soil, will control the thrust resistance that 
can be developed. It may seem reasonable to allow the thrust block to develop 
the full passive pressure resistance because this is a temporary construction 
loading condition. However, it must be recognized that relatively large strains will 
be required in order to develop the maximum passive pressure resistance. At the 
jacking loads that impose stresses approaching the maximum passive pressure 
resistance, there may be large movements of the thrust block in response to the 
strains required to develop full passive pressure resistance. 
In addition, large movements of the thrust block are not desirable for pipe jacking 
because the movements are usually not uniform and it becomes difficult to 
maintain the thrust block perpendicular to the pipe alignment. If the thrust block 
does not remain square, the force applied to the pipe may not be uniform, 
resulting in eccentric loading that could damage the pipe.  
Movements of the thrust block can also be controlled by limiting the allowable 
passive pressure resistance to about one-half of the maximum passive pressure 
resistance. This corresponds to a much lower level of strain and smaller soil 
movements (strain compatible design). If the project requires higher jacking 
forces than can be safely transferred to the soil mass behind the thrust block, 
then the soil mass should be improved or stabilized.

3.2.1 Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes 
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench, the size of the 
pipes, the unit weight of backfill material, and the frictional resistance between 
the backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads acting on 
the buried pipes, an average unit weight of 110 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) may 
be used for compacted backfill material within the trench excavation and above 
the pipes. In addition, a coefficient of friction of 0.25 may be used to calculate the 
frictional resistance between the compacted backfill and the sidewalls of the 
excavated trench. For the underground pipes, earth forces upon the pipe 
increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the trench 
should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be 
considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
3.2.2 Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load 
supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration. In general, a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes. The 
granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) the outside pipe diameter. Before the placement of bedding

3.2.3 Backfill 
In general, trench backfill should be performed in accordance with the Standard 
Specifications for Public Works Construction, Department of Public Works, City and 
County of Honolulu, September 1986. Free-draining granular materials, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be used for the trench 
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around 
the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of 
damaging the pipes.  
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils with particles 
less than 6 inches in maximum dimension or select fill material. If required, 
imported select granular fill should consist of crushed coral or basaltic gravel. 
Imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The imported fill material should 
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Imported materials 
should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to 
the project site for the intended use. 
The backfill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in maximum 8-inch horizontal loose lifts, and mechanically 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential 
for appreciable future ground subsidence. A typical section of the trench detail is 
presented on Plate 4.

3.2.4 Pipe Settlement 
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the difference 
in the unit weight of the excavated original earth and the compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipeline. The net increase in loading may cause settlement 
of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our exploration and 
analyses, small primary settlements on the order of about less than one-half inch 
may be anticipated for the new sewer line alignment.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
eleven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5 and Boring Nos. 201 through 206, 
extending to depths of approximately 25.4 to 48 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers 
and coring tools. 
Our geologist/engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the

would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the total core run in rock that 
is sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to 
describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

The excavation characteristic of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics. The test results 
along with the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-5 through B-7. 
One set of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), were performed on a selected soil sample obtained from our field 
exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-8.
