REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The isiand was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of wnich are still active
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3214 20 B Page 6
The project site is located on the northern flank of Hualalai mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric
basaltic lava flows of the Hualalai Voicanic Series which were most likely deposited
during the late Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and MacDonald 1946
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along
rift zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The project site is situated in the
northwest rift zone of Hualalai
The lava flows at the site are a a basalt types with dense zones of hard rock and
loose zones of clinker overlying and intermixed with older pahoehoe flows The a a
form of lava flow is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental
clinker underlain by a dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less
continuous layer of clinker below ihe massive core The clinker phase is highly
permeable and lava tubes are generaily not encountered in a a flows
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in
pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state
and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as
blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for
a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller
in horizontal extent
In general basalts are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can
transmit water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3214 20 B Page 7
transmitted through the porous matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts
cavities and along clinker layers

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicated that both a a and pahoehoe lava flows are present
near the ground surface at the project site The a a encountered at the project site
consisted of fragmental clinker at the surface which is very ragged and porous and
may collapse under fill or structural loads Therefore we recommend that the a a flows
under proposed structures and embankments be removed and replaced with properly
compacted choker fill materials The pahoehoe encountered at the site is characterized
by a very smooth and hard surface with an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Excavation into this material may be very difficult using conventional construction
equipment use of a jack hammer hoe ram and or blasting may be required
We anticipate that excavation into the basalt formation will result in a very jagged
and rough surface which will adversely affect the performance of the liner Therefore
we recommend that a minimum 12 inch thick choker fill layer consisting of well graded
6 inch minus materials be provided across the entire irrigation lake in both cut and fill
areas An underlining consisting of a geotextile fabric should be placed on the choker
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3214 20 B Page 12
choker layer below the impervious liner to reduce the potential for puncture of the
impervious liner from edges of the rocky fill material
The material type performance instaiiation and protection detaiis of the
impervious liner should be designed in accordance with the requirements of the liner
manufacturer Particular attention should be given to proper anchoring of the liner at
the top of the irrigation lake to reduce the potential for liner failure
Loose clinkers were observed at the proposed head tank area with pahoehoe

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be placed in ievel lifts not exceeding
12 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91
Conventional cornpaction testing is generally not applicable in fills composed of
rock and or cobbles instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes of a compactor needed te achieve the desired level of compaction should
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this
testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion
for adequate compaction
Placement of the choker fill on the slope surface and bottom of the lake will be
necessary as mentioned previously A compactor with sufficient vibratory and

Foundation Probing
In general the basaltic rock formation in Hawaii consists of lava flows prone to
contain tubes voids and or cavities In order to reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of voids below a footing we recommend that
a cavity probing program be carried out for the proposed head tank structure Probes
are generally drilled at each individual spread footing location and on 10 foot intervals
along wall footing locations The probes are generally 3 inches in diameter and should
be drilled to a minimum depth of 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at this site
we recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete Since the potential exists for encountering cavities

Irrigation Lines
We understand that new irrigation lines will be installed for development of this
project In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should
be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of granular material less
than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill should be moisture conditioned to slightly
above the optimum placed in maximum 10 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to 95 percent
relative compaction

subgrades be scarified to a depth of 12 inches or to the surface of dense basalt The
exposed surface should be compacted or proof rolled by a vibratory compactor
weighing at least 15 tons for a minimum of 6 passes prior to the placement of the base
course layer
We anticipate the vehicle loading for this access road will consist primarily of
light utility trucks for maintenance purposes We have made our preliminary pavement
design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will consist of compacted granular fill or
hard basalt formation Based on the above assumptions we recommend the following
pavement section be used for preliminary design purposes
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
st a
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of three borings to depths ranging between 20 0 and 26 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The
borings were performed using a truck mounted rotary drill rig by continuous core drilling
and sample recovery
Core samples of rock material encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs
of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length or the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would by 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four inches in length without any discontinuities discounting
any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from
a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring
Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock
its relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high
RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require blasting or splitting for
excavation
