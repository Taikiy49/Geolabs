INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Psychiatric Addition project within the existing Kona
Community Hospital in the Kealakekua area of Kailua Kona on the island of Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Kona Community Hospital facility in
the Kealakekua area of Kailua Kona on the island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a
two story Psychiatric Addition as part of the Kona Community Hospital Renovation and
Expansion project
The new addition with a total floor area of approximately 20 273 square feet will
be located immediately to the north of the existing main hospital building Based on the
information provided to us by the project structural engineer maximum exterior and
interior column loads are estimated to be on the order of about 150 and 350 kips per
column respectively with wall loads of approximately 4 kips per lineal foot
We understand that the finish ground and first floor elevations for the proposed
new building are set at 1 640 and 1 654 feet Mean Sea Level MSL respectively
Based on the existing topography and the finish floor elevations we anticipate that cuts

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southern flank of Mauna Loa which has formed
the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site appears
to be pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These
cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3766 00 Page 5
intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the
flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the existing Kona Community Hospital facility in
the Kealakekua area of Kailua Kona on the island of Hawaii Based on the regional
geology and our field reconnaissance the site vicinity consists of a gentle rocky slope
comprising the southwestern portion of Mauna Loa Volcano The project site is located in
an open area immediately to the north of the main hospital building
The existing terrain at the project site is relatively level with the existing ground
surface elevation at approximately 1 643 feet MSL At the time of our field exploration
the area was being used as a parking tot with the ground surface generally consisting of
clayey silt soils with basalt gravel The terrain slopes upward along the east side of the
project site at a gradient of about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V reaching an
elevation of approximately 1 654 feet MSL

Foundations
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new building
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 8 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used
for the design of footing foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation encountered
below the surficial soils and or new engineered fills This bearing value is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope
face Footings located adjacent to the planned retaining walls should be embedded deep
enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should
be designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement

W O 3766 00 Page 8
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
Resistance to the lateral loads due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This
value assumes that the concrete for footings is cast neat against the basalt rock A
coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the underlying basalt
rock formation Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the
upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be
present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site To reduce the potential
for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 10 foot on center for continuous footings The probe holes should be
at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3766 00 Page 9
bottom of footings If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the
probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help
delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with
a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operaticn is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the proposed building along the southern and eastern
sides of the project site may straddle cut and fill transitions To reduce the potential for
differential settlements at the cut and fill transition due to differing subgrade materials we
recommend that concrete slabs be supported on at least 12 inches of granular fill
material The granular fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the
proposed structure The intent of the 12 inch granular fill layer is to reduce the potential
for slab cracking due to differential settlements at the cut and fill transition

Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed addition site is underlain at
shallow depths by volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard It is
anticipated that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the

require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based

borings performed at the site We recommend that all contractors proposing to bid
on this project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to
make their own_ interpretation

Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines i e water and sewer lines will be required for
development of this project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be
excavated in hard basalt rock A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should be used for the trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material
generally less than 6 inches in maximum size as recommended in the Fills and Backfills
subsection of this report compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where
trenches will be located in paved areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the
Where the utility line crosses beneath the footings the utility pipes should be concrete
jacketed
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it is our opinion that trench work for utility installation may involve excavation
in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard ripping or hoerams Care must be
exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the
rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations

and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be

induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
