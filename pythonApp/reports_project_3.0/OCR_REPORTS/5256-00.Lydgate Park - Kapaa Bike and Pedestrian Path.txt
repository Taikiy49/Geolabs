presents our preliminary geotechnical considerations for the proposed Lydgate Park

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS
Based on our literature research performed for the proposed Lydgate Park Kapaa
Bike and Pedestrian Path project we anticipate that the proposed path alternatives will
traverse alluvial soils weathered volcanic rock and unconsolidated calcareous sediments
The majority of the project site is underlain by recent alluvial deposits that are typically soft
and compressible Portions of the path alignment for all the alternatives traverses
designated wetland areas In addition portions of the proposed alignments are underlain
by areas mapped as basalt formations We anticipate that the areas mapped as basalt
formations are covered by stiff residual and saprolitic soils at the ground surface
Several geotechnical considerations that may have a significant impact on project
cost and construction time were identified during our literature research In addition the
geotechnical considerations may have significant impacts to the project during
construction These geotechnical considerations include settlement due to soft soil
deposits steep shoreline areas potential rockfall hazards seepage of groundwater and
flooding associated with high rainfall storms Special attention should be given to soft
ground stabilization cut slope design subgrade stabilization drainage design and erosion

GEOLOGIC CONDITIONS
The Isiand of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lava of the Waimea Canyon
Voleanic Series These lavas consist of four distinct formations Napali Olokele
Haupu and Makaweli Formations These formations are comprised of thin bedded a a
and pahoehoe lava flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks generally are characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with
the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both
higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Kauai

The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series
generally are characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt inter bedded with
thin clinker layers The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils that
grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed of mainly silty materials
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to
basaltic rock formation with increasing depth
Erosion of the upper Koloa and Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series has deposited
alluvial sediments along streams drainageways and low lying areas These sediments
generally are unconsolidated to moderately consolidated non calcareous soil deposits
Agricultural residential and commercial developments within the last century have
brought the project area to its present conditions
Based on geologic references the path alignment alternatives will traverse
recent alluvial soils weathered volcanic rock and beach deposits in the order of most
to least encountered within the project area These soil deposits and rock formations
are described below
Recent Alluvium
The majority of the proposed path alignments are underlain by recent alluvial
deposits The recent alluvial deposits are characterized as unconsolidated
non calcareous soils These recent alluvial deposits tend to be soft in consistency and
compressible when subjected to loading These deposits typically are encountered
along drainage ways and low lying areas
Weathered Volcanic Rock
Based on the geologic maps portions of the path alignments are underlain by
basalt rock formation of the Koloa Volcanic Series We anticipate the presence of
residual and saprolitic soils near the ground surface These soils were developed and
derived from the in situ weathering of the basalt formation These volcanic deposits are
anticipated within the Wailua Houselot and Kapaa Homestead areas
Beach Deposits
The beach deposits generally consist of un consolidated calcareous sediments
composed largely of fragments of marine organism These deposits typically are
encountered along the shoreline areas

A series of existing drainage canals are situated within the project area The
drainage canals transport surface water from the wetland areas above Kapaa Town to
the shoreline through Uhelekawawa and Waikaea Canals The canals range from
about 30 to 90 feet wide
WETLAND AREAS
We understand that the proposed path alignment alternatives will traverse
several large wetland areas that were identified by the flora and fauna subconsultants
for this project In general these areas are characterized by swamp or marsh like
environment with possible presence of standing water Wetland sites may contain
various forms of unique wildlife which may call for preservation
The designated wetland sites for the project are located west of Kuhio Highway
A large wetland area is located between Waipouli Town Center and Kapaa Shopping
Center A smaller wetland area is situated between Wailua Shopping Plaza and the
existing Temporary Bypass Road The path alignments traverse or are situated near
the edges of the wetland areas
From a geotechnical engineering point of view swamps and marshlands often
imply the presence of soft soils Based on our desk top study of these designated
wetland areas the presence of soft soils in these areas are highly likely It should be
noted that areas not designated as a Wetland Site also may contain deposits of soft
soils These areas are further discussed in the Geotechnical Considerations section
of this letter report

Settlement Due to Soft Soils
It should be noted that areas underlain by recent alluvium are susceptible to
consolidation and settlement over time as man made fills are placed over these soft
ground areas Based on our literature research soft soils likely will be encountered at
the designated wetland sites stream crossings drainageways and other localized
areas A detailed field exploration should be performed at these locations where soft
soil deposits are suspected in order to determine the lateral extent thickness and
consolidation characteristics under the proposed fill loading conditions The soft soil
areas should be further evaluated to provide recommendations for design and
construction of embankments and or bridge structures over these areas
Steep Shoreline Area
We understand that the path will traverse the relatively steep shoreline areas
adjacent to the beach near the Wailua Bay View Hale Awapuhi Kapaa Sands and
Lanikai Condominiums Alternative No 1 Based on our visual observations during our
site reconnaissance we believe that the steep slope adjacent to the properties is
comprised of saprolitic materials highly weathered basalt rock formation with beach
deposits at the base of the steep slopes The steep existing slopes may have slope
inclinations of about 1H 1V

Rockfall Hazard
It should be noted that areas adjacent to relatively steep mountainsides and
hillsides are susceptible to rockfall Rockfall involves the detachment and fall of rock
materials from the slope face that can present potentially hazardous conditions for the
path user Steep slope areas are located near the Nounou Mountain Trail Easement
and Mahelona Memorial Hospital It should be noted that detailed geologic and
geotechnical studies should be conducted in these areas to evaluate the potential for
rockfall hazards along these stretches of the path during preliminary design and detailed
design of the path
Seepage of Groundwater
We anticipate that the soils encountered during path construction may have
relatively high water contents due to the high rainfall and high groundwater levels at the
project site In addition perched groundwater conditions may exist in some areas of the
project especially at the hillslopes Because of the high moisture anticipated in the
weathered materials earthwork cuts may expose permeable layers responsible for
transmitting seepage of subsurface groundwater which in turn may cause potentially
unstable cut faces Subsurface drainage will need to be installed in these areas if
these conditions are encountered during construction
Flooding by Rainfall
Based on a review of available topographic maps of the site the Island of Kauai
contains numerous rivers streams and drainage paths as a result of the high volume of
tropical rainfall Extended periods of heavy rainfall may swell drainage pathways to their
capacity and flood low lying areas We wish to emphasize that adequate modeling of
storm water runoff and the potential for scour at the new bridge structure locations should
be performed during detailed design of the project
