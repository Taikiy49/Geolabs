MICROPILE FOUNDATIONS
We understand micropiles will be utilized to augment the existing augered cast
in place ACIP pile foundations and to provide load bearing support for the new
structural elements planned for the building renovation project The load supporting
capacity of the micropile foundation would be derived primarily from skin friction
between the micropile bonded zone and the coralline materials anticipated at depths
starting from about 20 to 25 feet below the ground surface
A micropile consists of a small diameter usually less than 12 inches drilled and
grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile foundation typically is constructed by
drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the borehole
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In addition installation
of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures the
adjacent soils and the environment

Based on the local availability of equipment we envision a micropile system with
a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5 5 inches may be used We recommend designing
each micropile based on an allowable compressive load capacity of up to 40 or 80 kips
The allowable compressive load capacity for the micropiles is for supporting dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind or
seismic forces
Lateral load resistance contribution from the micropile should be discounted due
to the relatively small diameter of the foundation element Passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the foundation caps and or grade beams may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 and 150 pounds per square foot per
foot of depth pcf for above and below groundwater conditions respectively The static
groundwater level at the project site may be assumed to be at about Elevation 3 feet
MSL for design purposes
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the site we recommend
extending the micropiles through the soft and or loose lagoonal deposits and derive load
bearing support from the coralline materials encountered at greater depths To achieve
the allowable compressive load capacity with a factor of safety of 2 we believe each
micropile would need to extend a minimum of about 10 or 20 feet into the hard coral
ledge and or dense coralline materials to develop an allowable compressive load
capacity of 40 or 80 kips respectively Preliminary micropile lengths of 35 and 45 feet
may be used for cost estimation purposes for the 40 and 80 kip micropiles respectively
We also recommend casing the micropiles permanent casing at the top above the
coralline materials The permanent casing should have an outside diameter OD of at
least 5 5 inches same as the grout bulb size and the permanent casing should extend
3 feet into the coral ledge and or dense coralline materials The following table provides
a summary of the micropile recommendations for ease of reference

Micropile Construction Considerations
In general the micropiles should have a minimum center to center spacing of
about 30 inches to avoid reduction in vertical load capacity due to group action and to
facilitate drilling of the micropiles
Downdrag Loads
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction settlement of the compressible
deposits will occur at the project site When the loose soil deposits consolidate or move
downward relative to the micropiles the consolidation or downward movement will
create negative skin friction forces generally known as downdrag forces acting on the
micropile shafts The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the
liquefiable layer relative density of the potentially liquefiable deposits and the micropile
size
The allowable compressive load capacity of the micropiles has been reduced by
the expected downdrag loads in the micropile foundation analyses Based on our
foundation analyses we estimate the downdrag load imposed on the micropiles due to
settlement of the loose lagoonal deposit may be on the order of about 7 to 8 kips for the
5 5 inch diameter micropiles
Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by shear along the grout
bulb bonded zone and the adjacent soils In general an uplift load capacity of up to
40 kips per micropile may be achieved with a bonded zone extending a minimum of
10 feet below the bottom of casing The uplift load capacity provided is intended for
transient load applications only Therefore the uplift load capacity should not be further
increased for transient load applications For sustained uplift loads we recommend
reducing the uplift load capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift
loads The capacity of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project
structural engineer

Lateral Load Resistance
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the lateral
load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected Lateral loads
imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily by passive earth
pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade beams and by
shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth pcf above the groundwater level and 150 pcf below the
groundwater level Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by shear
resistance between the sides of the pile caps and grade beams and the adjacent soils
A side shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square feet psf may be used for
transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only
Micropile Foundation Settlement
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the micropile foundations to be less than 0 5 inches We believe these
settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the micropile load capacity is dependent on the drilling
procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to install the
micropile Therefore the micropile load capacity may vary considerably between
different contractors and micropile foundation systems To determine whether the
contractor s methods of micropile installation are adequate and to determine the
ultimate load capacity pre production load test generally is performed on a sacrificial
micropile is to fulfill the following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
To develop criteria for determination of the bonded zone and evaluate the
ultimate bonded stress
Based on our experience with the performance of micropiles installed in the
Ala Moana Center and our familiarity with the micropile subcontractor s method of
drilling and grouting we believe the compressive load testing on a sacrificial load test

Micropile Construction Considerations
A specialty subcontractor experienced in the construction of a cased micropile
foundation system minimum five projects should perform the micropile installation It
should be noted that hard drilling such as cobbles boulders and concrete may be
encountered locally within the fill materials at the site In addition coral formation and
coralline sandstone will be encountered during drilling of the micropiles
It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor to install the micropile Therefore the bond stress between the grout bulb
and the soil may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile
foundation systems
The micropile contractor should anticipate significant grout overage especially in
the coralline deposits and have sufficient contingency fund for this grout overage in the
bid For micropiles in this area we have experienced grout overage in the coralline
deposits on the order of about 200 to 400 percent of theoretical volume
Due to the specialized nature of the micropile foundation construction
observation of the micropile foundation installation system and testing of the micropiles
should be designated a Special Inspection item Therefore observation of the
micropile installation operations by Geolabs Special Inspector is necessary to confirm
our design assumptions and should be designated a Special Inspection item in
accordance with Section 1704 of IBC 2006
We envision the micropile installation would proceed near the bottom of the
existing pile cap which is near the groundwater table Construction of the micropile
foundation should consider containing the groundwater within the working area without
dewatering the project vicinity It should be noted that the dewatering operation at the

BELOW GRADE STRUCTURES
We understand that below grade structures such as a grease interceptor and
sand oil interceptor will be installed for the renovation project We envision soft and or
loose lagoonal deposits will be encountered at or near the bottom elevations of these
below grade structures planned Therefore we recommend providing a subgrade
stabilization layer consisting of 30 inches of No 2 Rock ASTM C33 No 4 gradation
wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent below the bedding layer
for uniform support In addition the stabilization layer should extend beyond the edges
of the below grade structures a minimum of 24 inches A 6 inch bedding layer
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation may then be placed
above the wrapped gravel stabilization layer prior to setting of the below grade
structures A soft ground subgrade stabilization detail is provided on Plate 1
