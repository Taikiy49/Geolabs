ANTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of an additional geotechnical
engineering investigation performed for the relocated maintenance hangar
and apron site at the U S Naval Air Station Cubi Point Republic of the
Philippines
The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1

PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
A previous geotechnical investigation report entitled
GEOTECHNICAL INVESTIGATION MAINTENANCE HANGAR AIRCRAFT RINSE FACILITY
NAVAL AIR STATION CUBI POINT REPUBLIC OF THE PHILIPPINES FY85 MCON
P 516 W O 1983 was issued by our office for the subject
project Based on the findings of that investigation marginal slope
stability was indicated for cuts located in the drainageway area situated
along the western side of the project site Therefore the hangar
structure was relocated toward the north and east where better soil

toward the east closer to San Bernardino Road
The purpose of this additional investigation was to explore the
subsurface conditions at the eastern portion of the relocated development
site which were outside the boundaries of the original previous field
exploration in order to provide pertinent soil data for foundation design
of the relocated hangar and revised site grading design
The scope of the investigation which was performed in general
accordance with our revised proposal dated included a
review of the previous investigation information field reconnaissance
drilling of six 6 borings installation and monitoring of piezometers
in some of the borings laboratory testing engineering analysis and
report preparation

Slope Stability
The area of concern included the portion of the natural slope above
the eastern half of the proposed hangar Based on the assumed earthquake
and seepage water saturation conditions our slope stability analysis in
this upper area indicated that the final cut slope has a factor of safety
greater than 1 15 This factor of safety is considered the minimum
acceptable by the U S Navy as indicated in DM 7 1
The major considerations and parameters used in our slope stability
analysis are described below
1
Groundwater Conditions
Our analysis indicates that groundwater level is one of the
most important parameters affecting the Stability of the
slope As discussed in our previous reports it is believed
that significant rise in groundwater level would occur
temporarily during heavy rains In an extreme condition the
water level could possibly rise to the ground surface
Our analysis indicates that drainage of the top 20 feet of soil
below the finished grade surface is essential for slope
Stability Therefore the 20 foot deep trench drains as
recommended in our report should extend into this area also
and be incorporated in the project design
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Q 1230 20 Page 7
In our drain design we have assumed that the groundwater level
in the entire area could rise up to the finished slope surface
during extreme rainfall conditions This was believed to be a
somewhat conservative assumption for the eastern slope area
Since a ridge extends through this portion of the site
generally in a north south direction and such a high water
level assumption seems less likely to occur
Previous piezometer readings following periods of moderate
rainfall generally indicated minimal rise in water level in the
borings located within the ridge area However the most
recent monitoring May 4 1984 of the water level in Boring
No 39 disclosed a substantial rise in water level up into the
20 foot zone following a period of relatively heavy rainfall
Long term piezometer monitoring particularly following heavy
monsoon rains is recommended to provide more data on the
characteristics of groundwater level fluctuations in the area
in order to efficiently locate the trench drains At present
there is no precise information on the time required for the
build up or dissipation of the water level The earthwork
contractor should be prepared to efficiently drain excess
surface and subsurface water during construction to minimize
potential hillside instability The location of the installed
piezometers as well as a water level measuring device were
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1230 20 Page 8
made available to the Staff Civil Engineer for continuous
monitoring of the groundwater levels The monitoring program
is considered highly important since it may provide the
earthwork contractor with more precise data in planning his
dewatering scheme during excavation of the hillside
For the present analysis we have assumed that the water level
could rise to the surface even in this ridge area and both
trench and horizontal drains are required

Aircraft Maintenance Hangar
The proposed hangar will be approximately 145 by 390 feet in plan
dimension Column loads for the structure vary trom 73 to 447 kips The
finished elevation of the hangar is planned at about 52 feet
The existing ground at the proposed hangar location varies from
about elevation 67 along the eastern side to 125 feet on the western
side Therefore deep cuts of about 15 to 60 feet are required
Based on the borings drilled the anticipated soil conditions at
finished grade for most of the hangar will consist of highly to
moderately weathered lithic tuff and agglomerate
Foundations
Based on the anticipated soil conditions and column loads spread
footing foundation bearing on the highly to moderately weathered
tuff or the stiff to very stiff saprolite is recommended for the
proposed structure
Footings should be placed a minimum of 30 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1230 20 Page 11
An allowable bearing capacity of 5000 p s f may be used for the
above foundation design The bearing capacity value is for total
dead plus live load only and may be increased by one third for
short duration wind or seismic loading
The proposed cutting will remove about 1500 to 4000 p s f of
over burden pressure thereby reducing the net load of the
building It is our opinion that relatively small settlements
would occur Our estimated settlements are on the order of
one half of an inch total and one fourth inch differential
Resistance to lateral loads for the hangar structure can be
obtained by passive pressure and frictional resistance A
frictional coefficient of 0 4 can be used between the bottom of the
footing and the stiff soil In addition an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used to determine
the passive resistance

The hangar floor and access apron will be subject to aircraft

weights

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling
six 6 borings to depths 20 75 to 80 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Truck mounted equipment was used to drill the borings which were
advanced by continuous flight augers rotary wash and coring
techniques
The soil encountered were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer and geologist who
continuously monitored the drilling operations The classifications
were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the
laboratory All soils were classified in accordance with the Unified
Soil Classification System Graphic presentations of the soils
encountered in the borings is presented on the Boring Logs Plates
A 39 1 through 44 1
Soil samples were obtained by driving a standard penetration
falling a distance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Rock core samples were obtained by using a rotary wash NW
core barrel The core recovery and rock quality designation RQD are
presented on the boring logs
Open standpipe piezometers with porous tips were installed in
selected bore holes to permit long term water level monitoring The
measured grounwater levels are summarized on Plate A 45
