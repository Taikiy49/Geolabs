2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As the volcanic
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as Schofield Plateau
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau Volcano Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
The Maloelap project site is on the south side of the Aliamanu vent and on the
southwestern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As
the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the groundwater
generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low cones
of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff formation locally referred to as
mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream After this phase of volcanic
activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the
next phase of volcanic activity began Then the Salt Lake eruptions began when the
sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below the present level The Salt Lake eruptive
event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the older Aliamanu events
Subsequently man made developments in the last century have brought the site to the
existing conditions

3 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we envision that the new
dwellings at the Maloelap site will be underlain by very stiff surface fill materials or hard
volcanic tuff formation Therefore we recommend supporting the new dwellings on a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings As an
alternative the foundations for support of the structural loads of the residential
structures may also consist of thickened edge slab footings An allowable bearing
pressure on order of about 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used in the
design of footings bearing on the very stiff surface fill materials new compacted fills
placed to achieve the finished grades or directly on the in situ volcanic tuff formation
This bearing value is for sizing footings based on dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 when considering transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
Typically we recommend embedding the footings at 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent exterior grade based on the subsurface conditions at the site Due to localized
fine grading for the drainage swales we believe that the footing embedment may be
reduced to a minimum of 14 inches locally provided that the surface is adequately
sloped minimum 2 percent for drainage purposes and maintained at all times In
addition the footing embedment may be reduced to a minimum of 12 inches if the entire
perimeter footing is founded on hard volcanic tuff formation Foundations next to utility
trenches or easements should be embedded below an imaginary 45 degree plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the footings should
extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement

Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the
Foundations section herein However retaining structure footings should have a
minimum width of 18 inches In addition wall foundations on relatively flat areas
should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade
For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a
minimum 6 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of
the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of
the slope should be constructed in stepped footings

Drainage
In general retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for
build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a
12 inch wide zone of permeable material such as open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed directly around a perforated pipe
perforations down at the base of the retaining wall The perforated pipe should
discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes
As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used instead of the permeable drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall Unless covered by concrete or
asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious materials such as the on site clayey soils to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the walls

cleared and grubbed thoroughly Based on the exposed surface conditions the
concrete slab and asphaltic concrete pavement within the contract grading limits
should be demolished and removed at the on set of earthwork Deleterious and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to
reduce the potential for contaminating the excavated materials
After clearing and demolishing the project area finished subgrades in cut areas and
areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
Where yielding areas or soft and or loose areas are disclosed during clearing and
grubbing operations or during the scarification process the soft and or loose
materials should be over excavated to expose firm dense conditions and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill materials
Therefore contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities

3 9 Corrosion Potential
A total of 12 soil resistivity surveys were conducted at three selected locations
designated as R 1 through R 3 on the Site Plan Plate 2 using the in situ
Wenner 4 Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by
Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins
electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 Results of the field soil resistivity surveys are presented on
Plate C 1 of Appendix C
Based on the test results the subsurface soils at the project site generally exhibit
a resistivity value ranging between about 2 930 to 5 340 ohm cm indicating that the
subsurface soils are corrosive to buried metallic structures Therefore we recommend

against potential corrosion A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed

3 10 Drainage
The finished grades outside the houses and other structures should be sloped
adequately to shed water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the
potential for ponding at all times In addition it is advised to install gutter systems
around the buildings and to divert the discharge away from the foundation and slab
areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs also should be
avoided Planters next to foundations within 3 feet of the edge of the slab should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive
water infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
backfilled properly against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface In
addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and positive
gradients should be maintained at all times to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and slabs

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the project
site on for the field survey Twelve soil resistivity surveys were
conducted at three locations designated as R 1 through R 3 on the Site Plan Plate 2
using the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter
manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Our field resistivity work was
performed under sunny conditions with generally damp surface conditions
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of various distances based on the anticipated utility
depths The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode
spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The average soil
resistivity in ohms cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then
be computed Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on Plate C 1 It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions soil
moisture existing underground utilities and other factors
