INTRODUCTION
This report represents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the foundation and site grading design of the proposed 1 0 M G reservoir
at an elevation of 1350 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL for the Kaupulehu Resort

Design Memorandum dated August 2 1991 The general location of the site is shown
on the project location Map Plate 1
Our original reconnaissance and geophysical surveys utilizing ground penetrating
radar GPR methods were for a site originally considered for the reservoir Results of
these efforts indicated that this originally considered site is underlain by a significant
number of anomalies as indicated by the GPR survey which appear to be cavities and
an open network of lave tubes
After discussions with representatives of the client and the civil engineering

approximately 250 feet southeasterly of the original site Selection of this second site was
based on surface observation which based on the surface features observed indicated
that there was a substantial chance of encountering more competent subsurface
conditions
The data obtained from the second geophysical exploration at the second site
indicated that this alternate location is underlain by rock formation with significantly less
anomalies as indicated by GPR methods which could be interpreted as voids On the
basis of these findings it was decided to implement a second phase of physically direct
methods of exploration at the alternate reservoir site consisting of drilling and sampling
of four 4 core borings to further explore the subsurface conditions at the site

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a one 1 0 million gallon M G concrete reservoir for
the storage of potable water The structure will be approximately one hundred 100 feet
in diameter with a finish floor elevation of approximately 1 340 feet above MSL Cuts into
the adjacent hillside of up to about twenty 20 feet in vertical height will be required to
level the site for construction of the reservoir
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located upslope easterly of the Kaupulehu Resort and Queen
Kaahumanu Highway State Route 19 and downslope westerly of the Mamalahoa
Highway State Route 190 on the Big Island of Hawaii The general location of the site
and overall project vicinity are shown on The Project Location Map Plate 1 A
topographic map of the area showing the two specific sites considered and the boring
locations is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The area is characterized by topography which slopes slightly to moderately
downward toward the west The topography is formed by the original structure of the

flows with open surface blisters and collapsed lava tubes to the north and west The area
is sparsely vegetated with silver oak trees and grasses
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which exhibited activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project vicinity is on the northern flank of Hualalai mountain which makes up
the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric basaltic lava
flows of the Hualalai Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late
Pleistocene Epoch Sterns and MacDonald 1946
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust This project is situated in the northwest
rift zone of Hualalai
The lava flows at the site are of the pahoehoe type with dense zones of hard rock
and loose zones of clinker and voids of varying degrees overlying and intermixed with
older pahoehoe flows The clinker phase is highly permeable Lava tubes are not
uncommon in pahoehoe flows Collapsed lava tubes cinder cones underlain and
surrounded by depositional scoriaceous cinder and spatter cones are located in the near
vicinity of the project area

Subsurface Exploration
The project sites were explored initially by a geophysical survey conducted by
ground penetrating radar GPR methods The equipment utilized was a Model SIR 8
manufactured by Geophysical Survey Systems Inc The GPR was used primarily to
obtain indications of the extent and degree of subsurface anomalies which might indicate
voids underlying the proposed project sites
In general our field reconnaissance indicates that both the originally proposed
reservoir site and the alternate site are underlain by volcanic rock formations
Geophysical exploration by the ground penetrating radar method was performed at the
originally considered reservoir location Our field reconnaissance and the data obtained
from this geophysical survey indicated that this area is underlain by a significant number
of anomalies that appear to be cavities and an open network of lava tubes
Another geophysical survey at an alternate location approximately 250 feet
southeasterly of the original site was performed This alternate site was selected for
further study based on observable surface features which indicated that there was a
substantial chance of encountering more competent subsurface conditions
The data obtained from this second geophysical exploration indicated that the
alternate location is underlain by rock formations with significantly less anomalies which
could be indicative of the presence of voids On the basis of these findings it was
decided to implement a second phase of direct physical exploration which consisted of
the drilling and sampling of four 4 borings to further explore the subsurface conditions
at the proposed alternate site

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our findings construction of the proposed reservoir at the alternate site
is feasible provided that our recommendations are followed The underlying volcanic rock
formation should provide adequate bearing for the proposed reservoir structure Due to
the existence of cavities within the volcanic formation we recommend that a program of
probing and grouting of the reservoir foundation subgrades be conducted during
construction This program is discussed in more detail under the subparagraph entitled
Probing and Grouting present below
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting stripped and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2583 00 Page 9
processed material should be stockpiled for use in landscaping and or general yard fill
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operations should be
overexcavated to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill
Based on our exploration the majority of the cut and materials generated from cuts
will be volcanic rock materials including clinker material The on site excavated rock
materials should be suitable for use as engineered fill if properly processed placed and
compacted If additional imported material is required the material should conform to the
requirements set forth in Section 30 Select Borrow for Subbase Course of the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction dated September 1986
If the volcanic rock materials encountered at the site are processed and reduced
to a relatively well graded select granular material with an average maximum size of about
six 6 inches and an absolute maximum size of twelve 12 inches the fills and
embankments may generally be considered to be equivalent to earthfill In this case no
special choking layer would be required if some losses of topsoil are considered
acceptable
Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the dry density as
determined in accordance by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should
be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to aminimum of 95 maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet of fills and backfills below the finished grade
of paved areas should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of the maximum dry density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2583 00 Page 10
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one
vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the underlying soil or basalt formation
to lessen potential instability of the new fill
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to determine the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate
compaction
Design slope ratios of up to two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may be used for
cut slopes in clinker material The slope ratio may be increase to one horizontal to one
vertical 1H 1V where competent rock formations are encountered Fill slopes of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may be used for fill slopes provided that on site granular
material is used
Reservoir Foundations
Based on the data obtained during our field exploration the reservoir foundations
will bear on in situ volcanic formations ranging from moderately hard basalt rock with
some voids to medium dense clinker
The selected reservoir location exhibits varying subsurface conditions at the
approximate proposed finish floor elevation of the proposed finished floor Therefore
special consideration should be given to subgrade preparation at this location Where
the structure will span between differing subgrade conditions i e hard basalt grading to
loose clinker the slab and footing areas should be overexcavated to a depth of three

3 feet below the bottom of the slab and backfilled with base course compacted to a
minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density to lessen the potential for differential
settlement across the structure On site materials processed to form a well graded gravel
with an average maximum size of about six 6 inches and an absolute maximum size of
twelve 12 inches may be substituted for the base course material The material selected
for use should be tested and approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to placement
Providing that these recommendations are followed an allowable bearing pressure
temporary wind or seismic loads are considered
Footings should be embedded a minimum of eighteen 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Footings located next to utility trenches should be extended
down to an elevation equal to or deeper than that of the trench bottom The trench
backfill which should be completed prior to foundation construction should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density as determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 or filled with concrete
All footing excavations should be properly backfilled and compacted to 95 density
as indicated above
Probing and Grouting
To reduce the possibility of footing settlement due to collapse of loose pockets or
cavities in clinker or lava tubes in the underlying lava rock the footing locations
should be probed at approximately ten 10 feet on centers with a minimum of one
probe drilled at each spread footing location If a cavity is located below a footing

additional probe holes should be made to delineate the horizontal extent of the
cavity The probe holes should have a minimum diameter of three 3 inches and
extend below the bottom of the footing to a depth of at least ten 10 feet The
probe holes should be grouted from the bottom of the hole to the top using low
pressures The probing and grouting operations should be monitored by the
project geotechnical engineer to see that these recommendations are carried out
properly and to locate potential voids near the footing excavations as well as to
recommend modifications to the grouting procedures which may expedite the
construction program

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Ground Penetrating Radar
Subsurface conditions at both the originally considered site and the finally selected
site were explored by ground penetrating radar
Ground penetrating radar utilizes impulse radar technology to obtain by indirect
sensing methods indications of the nature of the subsurface materials A radar frequency
pulse is transmitted into the subsurface media and when it encounters an interface
between two materials with differing electrical properties it is reflected back to a receiver
on the surface In a relatively homogeneous medium this reflection this phenomenon
is noted as an anomaly
The reflected pulse is intercepted by the receiver and then electronically processed
and printed on a graphic recorder producing a record similar to a marine depth recorder
chart As the transmitter and receiver antenna area is moved across the ground surface
a graphic record of the subsurface is generated Markers can be inserted onto the data
print to indicate distances as the array is moved over the ground surface providing a
means to physically locate anomalies
Ground penetrating radar is generally considered most applicable to the location
of subsurface voids and buried structures Depth of penetration and data resolution are
dependent upon soil type moisture content electrical properties antenna frequency and
power Under optimum conditions penetrations of about 15 and 50 feet are possible for
our 500 and 100 Megahertz antennae respectively
Core Borings
Subsurface conditions at the alternate selected site were explored by drilling four
4 core borings at the locations of which were selected based upon information obtained
from surface reconnaissance and the ground penetration radar surveys Summaries of
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
retained in the hollow central or core of the core barrel is mechanically recovered for
examination classification and testing

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Borings Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD
50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quantity RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability or foundation support characteristics of a rock mass is a function
of the relative hardness of the rock its relative quality brittleness and fissile
characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would
probably require blasting or splitting for excavation
