Micropile Foundations
Based on the relatively high structural loading we recommend supporting the
antenna tower with a group of micropiles In general the micropile foundation system
consists of a small diameter usually less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with
steel reinforcing The micropile foundation typically is constructed by drilling a borehole
placing reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the borehole Micropiles are desirable
because they can be readily installed in access restrictive environments and in numerous
soil types and ground conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally
causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the
environment
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our experience we believe
that a micropile system consisting of Titan 40 16 as a minimum size reinforcing bar with
a minimum drill bit diameter of 4 5 inches may be used for the project The capacity of
the micropiles will derive primarily from skin friction between the micropile shaft grout
bulb and the very stiff to hard clayey soils and extremely to moderately weathered basalt
rock encountered in the borings We believe that the micropiles would need to extend to
a minimum depth of about 30 feet below the ground surface to develop an allowable
compressive load capacity of 58 kips per micropile The allowable compressive load
capacity for the micropiles is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent
for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by shear along the micropile surface and
the adjacent soils Considering that the micropiles are designed based on adhesion
between the micropile and the surrounding soils we believe that ultimate uplift load
capacity of 77 kips may be used in the design This value should be used for
transient loads only

For sustained uplift loads acting on the foundations the ultimate uplift load capacity

Lateral Load Resistance
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore to
provide the required lateral load resistance the micropiles should be battered at an
angle of approximately 10 degrees from the vertical axis
Typically the lateral load capacity of a battered micropile will depend on the
compressive load capacity and the pile batter angle Based on a batter angle of
10 degrees from the vertical axis it is our opinion that a lateral load capacity of up
to 13 5 kips per pile may be used in the design of the battered micropiles
Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the micropile foundation will result from elastic compression of the
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered
Total settlements of the micropiles are estimated to be on the order of 1 inch
Therefore the potential differential settlements between adjacent antenna tower
legs may be on the order of approximately 0 5 inch We believe that a significant
portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

A specialty subcontractor experienced in the construction of micropiles should

Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the load capacity of the micropiles is highly dependent on
the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to
install the micropile Therefore the load capacity of the micropile may vary
considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation systems In
order to determine whether the contractor s methods of micropile installation are
adequate and to determine the ultimate load capacity we recommend performing a

pre production pullout load test on a sacrificial micropile In general the purpose of
the pre production load test on a micropile is to fulfill the following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the method and equipment proposed by
the contractor to install the micropiles to the depth required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
In general the pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 3689 Quick Loading Procedure Based on experience we believe that
the load test should be conducted no earlier than seven days after completion of
the micropile installation to allow the grout adequate time to cure
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the
allowable design load in compression We recommend holding the maximum test
load 200 percent of the design load for a minimum of 4 to 8 hours depending on
the recorded movements of the load test micropile The pre production load test is
an integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system Therefore a
Geolabs representative should observe the pre production load test
In addition to the pre production load test we also recommend performing pullout
tests proof tests on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the load
carrying capacity of the installed micropiles We recommend testing a minimum
10 percent of the total number of micropiles or minimum of four micropiles for
pullout The pullout tests should consist of subjecting the micropile to at least
150 percent of the design loads and the maximum test load should be held for at
least 60 minutes Pullout tests on the micropiles also are integral parts of the
design of the micropile foundation system

of the subsurface conditions at the site to develop a subsurface data set to formulate

Discussion and Recommendations
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by very stiff
saprolitic soils and extremely to highly weathered basalt Hard basalt formation was
encountered at deeper depths Based on the anticipated structural loads acting on the
antenna tower we recommend using a single cast in place concrete drilled shaft to
support each leg of the antenna tower
Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site
preparation are presented in the following sections herein
Antenna Tower Foundations
Due to the high structural loads anticipated for the antennal tower we recommend
using a single cast in place concrete drilled shaft to support each leg of the proposed
antenna tower The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support

from friction between the concrete shaft and the surrounding soils and end bearing on
the basalt rock formation
A 3 foot diameter drilled shaft extending to a minimum depth of 42 feet below the
existing ground surface should be used to support each leg of the antenna tower The
drilled shaft may be designed with an allowable compressive load capacity of
1 060 kips The allowable compressive load capacity for the drilled shafts is for
dead plus live loads The compressive load capacity may be increased by
one third 1 3 when considering transient loads such as wind or seismic forces A

The load bearing capacities of the drilled shafts will depend largely on the relative
density of the soils within the bearing strata Because local variations in the subsurface
materials likely will occur it is imperative that a Geolabs representative be present
during the drilled shaft operations to confirm the subsurface conditions and to observe
the drilled shaft installation In addition contract documents should include provisions
unit prices for additional drilling and extension of the drilled shaft during construction to
account for unforeseen subsurface conditions
Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of
the drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils
Considering that the drilled shafts are designed based on adhesion between the
shaft and the surrounding soils we believe that ultimate uplift load capacity of
1 375 kips may be used in the design This value includes the weight of the
drilled shaft and should be used for transient loads only
For sustained uplift loads acting on the foundations the ultimate uplift load
capacities should be reduced by a factor of safety of 2 0 The project structural
engineer should check the structural capacity of the shaft member in tension
Lateral Load Resistance
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the drilled shaft allowable deflection at the top of
the drilled shaft and the induced moment in the drilled shaft The lateral load
analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded deep foundation
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral
loads as a function of depth The analysis was performed using non linear p y
curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then computed using the
appropriate soil moduli at various depths

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
The performance of the shaft will significantly depend upon the contractor s method
of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend that a
Geolabs representative be present to observe the drilled shaft installation during
construction In our opinion the following may have a significant impact on the
effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
The load carrying capacity of the drilled shaft depends to a large extent on the
frictional resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soil Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling or excavating the shaft hole and in placing concrete in the hole In addition
the drilled shafts would derive vertical support from end bearing on the basalt
formation It is important that the bottom of the drilled hole be relatively clean of
loose material prior to concrete placement to derive the necessary support from
end bearing
The drilled shafts will need to be embedded into the basalt rock formation at about
28 feet below the existing ground surface therefore difficult drilling conditions
should be expected In addition potential hard zones consisting of hard boulders

between depths of about 10 and 28 feet below the existing ground surface The
drilled shaft subcontractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to
drill through these types of natural obstructions where encountered
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range should be used
to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils I n
general concrete placement may be accomplished by free fall methods provided
that the concrete is placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for

drilled holes If groundwater is encountered in the drilled holes then concrete
should be placed by tremie methods Concrete should be placed promptly after
drilling within 24 hours after drilling of the holes to reduce the potential for
softening the sides of the drilled holes and to reduce the potential for water to
collect in the drilled holes

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography we envision that the site grading work for the
project will generally consist of minor cuts and fills
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared thoroughly Debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing
below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated
to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed
off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
The materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of general
fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension If required import fill should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed basalt or coral The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the

No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Geolabs should test imported fill
material for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site
for its intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Compaction by water tamping
jetting or ponding should not be allowed for the on site soils
A Geolabs representative should observe site grading operations It is important
that our representative observe the site grading operation to evaluate whether undesirable
materials are encountered and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to
those encountered in our field exploration
