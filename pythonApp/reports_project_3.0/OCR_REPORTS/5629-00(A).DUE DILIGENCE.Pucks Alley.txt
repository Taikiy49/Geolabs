Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two primary
shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch in age.
Following the coalescence of the two volcanoes and after a long period of volcanic
inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, some
volcanic activity resumed with a series of basaltic lava flows followed by volcanic cinder

and tuff cone formations. These more recent eruption series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series. The Honolulu Volcanic Series, which began approximately
700,000 years ago (MacDonald and Abbott, 1970), produced several prominent cinder
and tuff cone structures with some basaltic lava flows that became inter-layered with the
developing coastal plain deposits.
Most of the southern Oahu coastal plain developed during the Pleistocene Epoch
when the sea level experienced significant fluctuations related to the continental glacial
stages. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic
basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers
melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to
above the present sea level. The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by
these glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional
base level was correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the
present sea level. When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such
that marine sediments and coral reef formations accumulated at higher elevations.
The advance and retreat of the sea levels also produced coralline reef deposits
located at varying elevation levels. In addition, the higher sea level stands caused the
formation of deltas and fans composed of accumulated terrigenous sediment in the head
region of old bays. The higher sea level stands also created the accumulation of coralline
reef deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. In contrast, the lower sea stands
caused streams to carve deeper in the existing coastal sediments and reef deposits. The
sub-aerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused consolidation of
the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. Finally, the
placement of man-made fills associated with the commercial and residential development
of the urban area within the last 60 years has brought the southern Oahu coastal plain
area to its present form.
The project site is situated near the mouth of Manoa Valley where the valley opens
onto the southeastern Oahu coastal plain. The coastal plain formed along the eroded
southern flank of the Koolau Volcano, which comprises the eastern portion of the Island of
Oahu. The project site is underlain by relatively thin accumulations of alluvial soils, which
are derived from upslope erosion of the Koolau Volcano. The near-surface alluvial soils
may be encountered along with some generally thin (less than about 5 feet thick)
man-made fills, which were placed during the development of the urban Moiliili area. In
general, the southern Oahu coastal plain located within the vicinity of the project site is
believed to be underlain by generally thin near-surface alluvial and man-made fill deposits
(sandy and silty soils), which overlie a wedge of buried marine deposits that includes
layers of soft to medium hard coralline-limestone formation and various loose/soft
calcareous sediments that are intercalated with other terrestrial sediments derived from
inland erosion.

Existing Conditions
The project site is located at the intersection of University Avenue and South King
Street within the Moiliili area of the District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii as
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The project site encompasses the existing
Pucks Alley commercial/residential development, which presently contains some low-rise
buildings and vehicle parking areas as shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2.
The majority of the project site is generally level to slightly sloping towards the
southwest with existing ground surface elevations ranging on the order of about +10 to
+15 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The ground surfaces at the project site are mostly paved
or covered by the existing building footprints.
Based on our research and review of available in-house soils and geological
information, the project site may be underlain by some thin silty and sandy alluvial and
man-made fills of generally less than about 3 to 5 feet in thickness. The stiff/medium
dense near-surface soils are believed to be underlain by a layer of medium hard coralline
limestone strata followed by various unconsolidated marine and terrestrial sediments at
depth.
The project site appears to be located in very close proximity to a previously
documented network of subterranean caves contained within the previously mentioned
near-surface coralline limestone strata. The cave network is commonly referred to as the
Moiliili Caves. The cave network is a_ naturally occurring dendritic subterranean
groundwater drainage system, which developed within the coralline reef limestone layer
generally located near the mouth of Manoa Valley.
Subsurface Conditions
Based on our review of available in-house soils and geological data, the project site
appears to be underlain by a near-surface layer of coralline reef limestone, which is
underlain further by various unconsolidated marine and terrestrial sediments. Hard basalt
rock sandwiched between other coralline reef layers may exist at greater depth below the
project site. The near-surface coralline limestone topography has been filled by generally
thin cover of silty and sandy alluvial soils and man-made fills.
The Moiliili Caves are a naturally developed karstic drainage system that formed
within the near-surface coralline limestone materials residing within the University/Moiliili
area. The dendritic branching, subterranean karst was formed by the dissolution of the
coralline limestone by fresh groundwater flowage. The calcareous coralline limestone
materials are susceptible to slow dissolution (erosion) by percolating and flowing acidic
fresh groundwater.

The coralline limestone containing the Moiliili karst features was deposited during
the Pleistocene Sangamon Interglacial Stage (+25 feet Waimanalo Sea Level Stand)
when the sea level was approximately 25 feet higher than the present day. It has been
suggested that the karst feature may have developed preferentially along an abandoned
drainage path of the Manoa Stream. The subterranean Moiliili karst encompasses the
highly urbanized area approximately centered about University Avenue extending from
about the H-1 Freeway in a southwesterly direction toward Kapiolani Boulevard as
generally shown on the Moiliili Karst Location Map, Plate 4. It is postulated that the cave
system may extend all the way to the southern shoreline although it has not been
documented.
A history of literature documentation by area residents and Board of Water Supply
personnel exists. Documentation includes description of exploratory travel through the
underground tunnels that are partially filled with water. Although many of the ground
surface features related to the buried karst topography have been covered over by
progressive urban development, a few undisclosed entrances to the cave system remain.
Previous exploratory observations performed by others indicate that some of the
interconnected tunnels have been partly filled with earth fill materials during development
of the surrounding area. There are also reports of visible concrete pilings associated with
building construction and other underground support structures used for waterline
construction, which breach and traverse portions of the caves. The cave water quality has
been reported to be generally fresh in character and sustains aquatic life with little
discernable pollution from the surface infiltration. Groundwater within the caves has been
reported to flow towards the south and southwest. Evidence of elevated discharges
occurring within the main karst conduits is evidenced by trash material having been
encountered periodically near the roof of the structure.
Based on our review of available published reports pertaining to the Moiliili Caves, it
appears that some of the larger branching network of conduits and passages range from
several feet wide up to 20 to 40 feet in width locally where “caverns” were encountered.
The height of the passable conduits appears to range generally on the order of about 5 to
10 feet. Estimates of the cave roof thicknesses are absent in the reviewed literature;
however, in some places the reported development of sinkholes at the ground surface
would indicate only limited overburden soil cover and cave roof thickness. Based on our
review of available literature, we anticipate that the overburden roof thickness composed
of thin surface soils and the underlying coralline limestone materials may range from
several feet to over 20 feet in height.
Based on our literature review, at approximately 1,500 feet makai of the University
Avenue and King Street intersection, a 1934 construction excavation encountered a large
cavern at depths between about -21 feet and -30 feet Mean Sea Level. Construction
dewatering occurred continuously for 4 months and resulted in significant regional
up-gradient ground deformations including additional sinkhole development, settled/broken
utilities and house foundations, and the drainage of pre-existing surface ponds. Other

Groundwater
The ground surface elevations at the project site range between about +10 feet and
+15 feet MSL. Due to the active groundwater recharge related to the presence of the
caves, groundwater is anticipated to be encountered at depths of about 6 to 10 feet below
the existing ground surface.
It should be noted that the shallow groundwater underlying the site is believed to be
fresh in character with recharge mainly emanating from upstream sources. Brackish
groundwater may also be encountered during periods of lower recharge flow. It should be
noted that groundwater levels may fluctuate considerably due to the presence of the cave
network and due to responses in precipitation and surface runoff, which may infiltrate the
cave system from surface sources.
Discussions and Recommendations
As mentioned previously, proposed redevelopment plans for the project site are not
available for our review. Therefore, we assume that redevelopment of the parcel may
consist of the construction of new low-rise buildings to replace the aging buildings
presently encompassing the site.
Based on our review of the subsurface conditions encountered in the vicinity of the
project site, we anticipate that new building construction is feasible but would need to be
supported on deep foundation systems because of the presence of loose/soft
unconsolidated materials interbedded with some harder coralline ledges underlying the
project site.

However, the potential presence of relatively shallow depth voids and caves (Moiliili
Caves) traversing beneath the project site are a major consideration in the redevelopment
of the project site. Past evidence from earlier construction projects located in the South
King Street/University Avenue area would indicate that construction dewatering and
foundation construction activity could potentially adversely affect the existing cave
structures and the groundwater contained therein by altering the established groundwater
flows. The altered conditions as a result of excavation and pile installation activity may
cause new conduit obstructions or open new flow paths in the karst, which in turn may
undesirably change the existing processes related to the coralline limestone dissolution by
groundwater. Other undesirable effects could include the development of additional
sinkholes off-site or the collapse of quasi-stable existing cave structures beneath the site.
Special consideration must be given to these potentially undesirable side-effects of
surface and deep foundation construction activity. The selected foundation system will
likely require special provisions to minimize impacts on the Moiliili Cave system and the
subsurface groundwater flow. Backfilling of voids encountered beneath the site during
construction may become a contentious issue with regulatory agencies or other parties
that oversee the documented Moiliili Cave system.
Finally, the possibility also exists that the natural evolutionary changes acting upon
the existing cave system could adversely impact overlying development with the passage
of time through the consolidation or collapse of existing shallow depth voids. In addition, it
appears that the coralline limestone dissolution processes that have operated to form the
karst system would continue to evolve the network of subsurface drainage and cave
development potentially creating future instability of the existing conduits.
Deep Foundations
Based on the limited information provided, we anticipate that redevelopment of the
project site may include new multi-story buildings. Based on our preliminary review of the
anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site, we envision the use of deep
foundations such as piles or cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts bearing on deeper
competent strata.
A detailed design-level subsurface exploration consisting of test borings
supplemented with geophysical exploratory methods would likely be required for the
site-specific design of the foundation system. Furthermore, the subsurface exploration
should also be performed to locate and identify the potential karst caves underlying the
entire project site to reduce the risk of encountering unforeseen voids during construction,
which could pose significant construction delays and require the implementation of
construction contingency plans.

Based on our experience in urban Honolulu, we envision there may be concerns
regarding excess noise and vibrations generated during pile driving operations. Therefore,
we believe drilled shaft foundations may be desired to support the proposed structures.
It is likely that subsurface cavities and voids will be encountered in the coralline
materials in the vicinity of the project site. Therefore, the actual volume of concrete
required to fill the drilled shaft foundation may be appreciably more than the theoretical
concrete volume. Based on our experience, we envision concrete overpours of up to about
30 to 40 percent over the theoretical concrete volume may be required for the drilled shaft
foundations.
We recommend the subsurface conditions be evaluated in greater detail by
conducting a design-level field exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling
additional borings during the project design phase when the site layout including support
facilities is better defined.
Soft and/or Loose Soils
Design of the new structures and improvements should consider the presence of
the soft and/or loose soils at the project site. In addition, drilled shaft excavations may
require the use of temporary casing to reduce the risk of the sidewalls caving in during
construction. As mentioned previously, a design-level field exploration program should be
conducted to confirm the presence of and delineate the approximate limits of soft and/or
loose soils.
Limitations
It should be noted that it is not possible to observe and/or anticipate all site
conditions based on a review of literature and performance of preliminary geophysical
exploration. Variations of subsurface conditions from those described in this letter report
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until a
formal design-level geotechnical engineering exploration is conducted or construction is
underway. A design-level geotechnical engineering exploration should be conducted to
confirm and/or modify the preliminary recommendations provided herein. Our services
were performed in accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical
engineering practice; no warranty is expressed or implied.
