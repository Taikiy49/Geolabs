REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the glaciers
melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to
above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
_ correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4115 00 Page 6
caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered the
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes consisting of
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered the natural condition
of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a
valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and
deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older
alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial
deposits or by the soil like highly weathered basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban
development has since altered the natural formation of the area to its present form

Microtunneling
Microtunneling is a trenchless process for installing underground pipelines ducts
culverts and utilities The technique involves the use of powerful hydraulic jacks that push
or jack successive pipe sections through the ground behind a remotely controlled excavating
unit or cutting disk head at the same time as the excavation is taking place within the
excavating unit The following subsections briefly describe the advantages and methods of
construction for microtunneling
Major Advantages or Benefits
In general use of microtunneling techniques may offer the following major advantages
or benefits as compared to the conventional open trench method of construction
BSP PNOaARWN
Reduced disturbance and potential damage to adjacent utilities existing roads
and surrounding structures
Improved public and job site safety
Reduced surface disruption to vehicular and pedestrian traffic
Reduced disturbance to local residents and businesses
Reduced air and noise pollution and vibration
Greater flexibility in alignment selection and reduced need for utility relocations
Reduced extensive shoring system
Reduced need for significant dewatering
Smaller volumes of spoils that require transportation and disposal
Reduced potential for treatment of contaminated soil and groundwater
Construction Methodology
In general the tunneling methods selected would depend on the stability of the
ground Where unstable ground conditions are likely to be encountered the face of
the tunnel must be made stable to prevent ground loss and to enable mining to take
place safely This may be achieved by the following methods
PON
dewatering by well points or deep wells
grouting by the use of cement cement bentonite or chemicals
ground freezing and
using specialized tunneling methods such as the following
a compressed air
b full face earth pressure balancing machines or
c slurry soft ground tunnel boring machines

The choice of process is a function of the nature of the ground the water content and
in particular the particle size analysis of the subsoils encountered In order to install
a pipeline using this technique jacking and receiving pits will need to be constructed
A concrete thrust block constructed behind the jacking frame within the jacking pit
provides the reaction to the hydraulic jacks In weak poor ground conditions piling
or other arrangements ground improvement may need to be employed to increase
the reaction capability of the thrust block The hydraulic jacks pressing against the
back of the pipeline advance the disk cutting head into the soil formation To ensure
that the jacking forces are distributed around the circumference of a pipe being
jacked a thrust ring is normally provided with the design depending on the number
of jacks being used The jacks are inter connected hydraulically to ensure that the
thrust from each jack is the same The number of jacks used may vary depending on
the pipe size the strength of the jacking pipes the length to be installed and the
anticipated frictional resistance
The disk cutting head of the tunnel boring machine is controlled via cables from a
cabin on the surface where the operator monitors speed pressure and tunneling
accuracy At the face of the tunnel a rotating cutter head digs into the soil and rock
and mixes it with groundwater and water pumped from the surface Rocky material
is reduced to a maximum inch size in a crushing chamber located directly behind
the cutter disk The excavated material is pumped out of the tunnel as slurry The
water introduced into the machine is matched in pressure and volume to the material
being displaced This prevents over excavation of the tunnel face thereby reducing
the potential for cave in of the soil surrounding the new casing
The cutting disk speed and direction of rotation the hydraulic jacking system as well
as the slurry system operations can be controlled from one control panel located
remotely at the launch pit The hydraulic steering cylinders located on the disk cutting
head can be extended to steer the head back on course should any deviation be
detected A laser guidance system provides information such as length of drive
vertical and horizontal heading vertical horizontal and roll deviation from the

Soft Loose Subsoil Deposits
Ground behavior for soft ground tunneling may be described based on six categories
as summarized on Plate 5 Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground Tunnels
Loose sandy soils can be characterized as firm running or flowing ground depending
on the degree of cementation and on the groundwater conditions Above the
groundwater level these soils will not stand unsupported but tend to ravel or run until
a stable slope is formed that buttresses the tunnel face If there are sufficient fines
present to give the soil some apparent cohesion these soils may be able to stand
unsupported in the heading for short periods of time Below the groundwater level
these soils may be flowing ground unless the cohesion or cementation of the soil is
high enough to resist the seepage forces Both running or flowing ground behavior
indicates potentially unstable ground conditions with low stand up time
Squeezing ground conditions may occur where the soil is observed to advance slowly
into the tunnel excavation without any signs of fracturing Squeezing occurs without
an increase in the water content or a volume change in the soil and is governed by

Potential Surface Settlement
Surface settlement is usually considered to be the primary source of damage to
adjacent streets utilities and other surface facilities during tunneling operations For
microtunneling technique loss of ground is generally associated with soil squeezing
or running into the heading losses due to overcutting by the shield shield steering
adjustments and from construction dewatering
Minor ground losses during the advance of the heading are unavoidable with normal
construction techniques and the actual magnitudes of these losses are largely
dependent on the type of ground groundwater conditions overload factor
in cohesive soils size and depth of the tunnel and the skill of the operator in
steering the shield or machine Use of an interlocking steel sheet piling shoring
support system with a tremie concrete plug or precast concrete caissons with tremie
concrete plugs are relatively watertight which should allow the groundwater levels
outside the jacking receiving pits to be maintained close to the original preconstruction
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 4115 00 Page 17
Ig nS a SIT aa oe
levels Therefore dewatering at the jacking and receiving pit locations should not
cause significant settlement
Empirical methods have been developed for estimating surface settlement due to soft
ground tunneling by study of observed settlements on past projects The typical
settlement pattern that develops above a soft ground tunnel is a trough shaped
depression resembling an inverted normal probability curve with the maximum
settlement occurring above the tunnel centerline The behavior of the ground is also
influenced by the cover above the tunnel When the cover is greater than about
two tunnel diameters arching develops in the ground above the tunnel reducing the
potential for significant ground movement and surface settlement Based on the
subsoil conditions encountered in our preliminary field exploration we believe that the
anticipated tunneling operations is not likely to result in significant damage to the
roadways or utilities above the tunnel or to the surrounding structures
Cobbles and Boulders
Ground that contains cobbles and boulders can be problematic for microtunneling
equipment A boulder or cluster of cobbles encountered in the heading of the tunnel
boring machine may severely hinder or even halt progress of the microtunneling
operations Boulders may be likely in glacial deposits and also in coarse grained
alluvial and or talus deposits Based on the geologic setting at the project site it is
probable that the alluvial deposits may consist of cobbles and boulders embedded in
the clayey soil matrix In addition remnant cobbles and boulders and other partially
weathered basalt rock materials may be present within the saprolite materials
In general microtunneling is expected to be suitable for pipe installation in the basalt
rock formation underlying the site However zones of severely fractured basalt rock
formation will be problematic and are expected to cause difficulty in drilling and
steering the heading Therefore the contractor working on this project should
carefully consider the expected subsurface conditions when selecting appropriate
equipment and methods for tunnel and shaft construction

Mixed Face Conditions
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the proposed depths of the new
sewer alignment we believe that the microtunneling alignment will run into and out
of the fill soils and hard basalt rock formation as shown on Plate 3 Due to the
difference in the relative density of the two materials deflection of the disk cutting
head causing difficulties in steering may be encountered at the interface between the
fill soils and basalt rock formation The disk cutting head may skid along the basalt
rock formation and fill soils interface which may result in the potential for deviation of
the microtunneling boring machine from the planned lines and grades Therefore
special attention should be given and adjustments should be made continuously
during microtunneling to Keep the disk cutting head on course
Another concern for microtunneling projects is the potential for encountering
mixed face conditions at the top of bedrock or base of the weathering profile The use
of geophysical techniques such as seismic refraction may be an efficient means of
estimating the location of such interfaces which should be subject to confirmation with
subsequent borings
Groundwater Control
Groundwater was encountered at depths ranging from about 8 9 to 14 3 feet below
the existing ground surface in the borings drilled Based on the design bottom
elevations of the jacking receiving pits groundwater control will be required at the
jacking and receiving pits especially when launching the tunneling machine and
advancing into the receiving pits
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated along the reconstructed sewer
alignment we believe that the use of interlocking steel sheet piles as a groundwater
cut off at the jacking receiving pits may be difficult due to the hard basalt rock
formation underlying the site at greater depths which may preclude the sheet piling
from attaining the proper embedment Therefore if steel sheet piling will be used for
shoring of the jacking receiving pits we believe that the sheet pile locations will likely
need to be predrilled to provide the required penetration below the ground surface

Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground Tunnels
After Terzaghi 1950
Firm Ground A heading may be advanced several feet or more without immediate
support Hard clays and cemented sand or gravel generally fall into this category
Raveling Ground After excavation material above the tunnel or in the upper part of the
working face tends to flake off and fail into the heading Slightly cohesive sands silts and
fine sands gaining their strength from apparent cohesion typically exhibit this type of
behavior Very stiff fissured clays may be ravelling materials also
Running Ground Cohesionless dry soils run from any unsupported vertical face until a
stable slope forms at the natural angle of repose i e approximately 3 degrees to
35 degrees Running ground consists of dry cohesionless materials such as clean loose
sand or gravel
Flowing Ground If seepage develops at the working face raveling or running ground is
transformed to flowing ground which advances like a viscous fluid into the heading Silt
sand or gravel below the water table without a high enough clay content to develop
significant cohesion will be flowing type soils
Swelling Ground A condition where the ground absorbs water increases in volume and
expands slowly into the tunnel This may occur in highly overconsolidated clays that
exhibit high volume change characteristics upon wetting
Squeezing Ground Squeezing ground conditions are analogous to plastic flow and the
soil is observed to advance slowly into the tunnel excavation without any signs of
fracturing Squeezing occurs without an increase in the water content or a volume change
in the soil and is governed by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden pressure
Squeezing ground may include soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on the
overburden pressure at the tunnel depth

The preliminary subsurface conditions along the proposed reconstructed sewer
alignment were explored by drilling and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1
through 3 extending to depths ranging from about 20 5 to 28 feet below the existing
pavement surface The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site
Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicate the number
of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be
factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Log
of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as REC 80

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two sets of corrosion tests were performed by E L Pacific on selected soil samples
obtained during our field exploration Each set of corrosion test includes the determination
of pH value chloride and sulfate contents The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Four sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 including two hydrometer tests
ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain
size distribution are provided on Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 4 and
B 5
