REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which has
formed the west central portion of the island The lava formation at the site appears to be
of pahoehoe flow which is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock Clinker layers are commonly found interbedded
between the pahoehoe flows
Cavities such as lava tubes or other void spaces are commonly encountered in
pahoehoe lavas Many of these cavities were formed when the still molten lava created the
structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava The cavities represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
The basalt lava rock encountered at the site is generally considered to be relatively
permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions
Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures inter flow
contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and
cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

Fills and Backfills
We recommend that a minimum of 18 inches of structural fill material be provided
below the areas under building pads burial sites pavements and other site
improvements to serve as a choking layer for the larger sized general fill below and
also to facilitate construction of the improvements The structural fill layer should
extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the building footprints
Structural fill materials should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension The on site cut and over excavated materials may be
used as structural fill or backfill materials provided that they are processed and
reduced to a relatively well graded granular material with a maximum size of about
6 inches Organic laden soils and other unsuitable materials should not be used as
structural or general fill or backfill materials imported materials to be used as
structural fill should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than
6 inches in largest dimension Imported materials should be observed and or tested
by Geolabs Hawaii for their suitability prior to being transported to the site
General fill materials may consist of the on site materials with a maximum dimension
of 12 inches On site materials greater than 12 inches in maximum dimension may
be used as general fills provided that they are reduced in size to meet the above
requirements Care should be exercised to avoid placement of boulders rock
fragments greater than 12 inches in maximum dimension in the proposed utility
alignment and within the depths of the proposed utility lines to reduce the potential
for encountering the boulders during excavation for the utility trenches and other
underground utilities Also boulders should not be exposed at the fill slope face

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For fills placed under
pavements the upper 3 feet of the fill materials below the pavement grade should be
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of rock
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing program the
number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

Cut and Fill Slopes

believe that the planned cut slopes will generally expose basalt rock formation In
general we believe that a cut slope inclination of 1H 1V or flatter may be used for
design of planned cut slopes exposing basalt formations We anticipate that up to

formations in some areas Therefore if the upper portion of the cut slope exposes the

attempting to backfill the slope to the design grade with sliver fills

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support the proposed new
buildings planned at the site As previously indicated we recommend that a minimum of
18 inches of structural fill be provided below the building footprints An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings
bearing on compacted structural fill materials and or basalt formations This bearing value
is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such
as those caused by wind or seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
Based on the sloping conditions of the existing terrain we anticipate that the new
buildings will be constructed adjacent to a new fill slope For footings constructed near the
tops of slopes or on slopes the footings should be embedded deep enough such that the
horizontal distance measured from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the slope
face is not less than 6 feet
Where foundations are located next to utility ttenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation placed immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the
base of the retaining structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As
an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain
may be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining structures
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill material
of less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce
the potential for water infiltration behind the retaining structures

It should be noted that the general fill materials recommended for the mass grading
operations generally consist of 12 inch minus materials When these larger sized materials
are excavated from the utility trenches and are re used as trench backfill materials the
over sized materials should be screened to provide only 6 inch minus materials for the trench
backfills
Pavement Design
We understand that parking areas and access roadways will be constructed for the
proposed project We anticipate that a flexible pavement section will be used for the new
parking areas We have assumed that the vehicle loading for the parking lots and access
roads will consist of primarily passenger cars and trucks with occasional heavy trucks We
understand that the following pavement section is generally required by the County of
Hawaii Department of Public Works
County of Hawaii Standard Detail R 34
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
4 0 Inch Base Course
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Subgrade
In general the purpose of the subbase course required in the County of Hawaii
Standard section is to serve as a foundation or stabilizing course such that the subsequent
base and surfacing layers may be constructed on top of an inferior subgrade soil The
standard pavement section does not take into consideration good quality subgrade
conditions Since a minimum of 18 inches of structural fill will be placed under the
pavements it is our opinion that a subbase course for stabilization foundation purposes is
not necessary due to the high quality of the subgrade materials anticipated at the subject
site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4074 00 Page 18
If a subbase course is mandatory we believe that the compacted structural fill below
the base course layer can be considered as a subbase material since its pavement support
characteristics with CBR value over 60 would be equal to or better than that of the select
borrow subbase course required by the County of Hawaii Standard section On this basis
we recommend the following preliminary pavement designs be used for the subject site
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
It is our opinion that the strength and performance of the proposed pavement section
of 2 inches of asphaltic concrete over 6 inches of base course is no less substantial than the
County of Hawaii Standard section for this site
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregates compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be
performed on the actual subgrade materials encountered during construction to confirm
the adequacy of the above section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface
water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches below the base
course to reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the pavement section

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings extended to about 15 1 to 15 7 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
_ quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks f 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
