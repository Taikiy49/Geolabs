Current Status of Earth Movements
The earth movements monitored at the Waiomao Landslide and study Areas A
and B are not stabilized and continue to experience slow downhill creep movements
Based on the history of the earth movement monitoring sudden catastrophic earth
movements are not likely but cannot be completely ruled out
Earth movements detected immediately after subdivision construction were
severe and occurred at substantially higher rates than the current movements recorded
during our monitoring at the project site Our record of the earth movements indicate
that the movements continue at a typically slow and relatively steady rate with some
inclinometers showing incremental patterns of accelerated and regressive movement
Extreme rainfall events and the increased subsurface groundwater seepage associated
with such rain events may have the potential to accelerate the movement in the
short term and cause significant disruption to the existing pavement and fixed structures
in the area

The on going earth movements will continue to adversely affect City
infrastructure such as roadway surfaces We have not been able to determine a
correlation between the occurrence of buried utility line damage and rate of earth
movement due to the variable rate of movements and complexity in the age of the
utilities having experienced multiple line repairs over the passage of time
Continued monitoring and maintenance of the utility infrastructure is important to
reduce sources of water infiltrating into the subsurface which can accelerate
movements High volume discharges in the storm drains have potential to scour and
create voids where line offsets or breaks occur Utilities such as water supply
wastewater and drainage should be notified of the active earth movement condition and
the need to promptly detect and correct possible continued utility damage as a result of
the ongoing earth movements Prompt action to detect and correct leaks is
recommended CCTV video survey of existing drain and wastewater lines in the Kuahea
Street and Waiomao Road vicinity should be undertaken periodically in an effort to
detect possible line breaks and the undesired discharges that could adversely impact
the active earth movements
It should be noted that there is currently no evidence indicating that the areas of
documented earth movement have increased in size and area coverage
Short Term and Long Term Landslide Repair Options
We understand that engineered mitigation efforts to slow the movements within
the public right of way will be performed as funds become available For long term
landslide mitigation retaining structures such as drilled shaft and tie back walls may be
designed to restrain the hillside areas adjacent to the public right of way For detailed
discussion of the long term landslide mitigation alternatives refer to Geolabs draft
report entitled Landslide Mitigation Alternatives Study Kuahea Street Area Movement
at Kuahea Place and Helo Place dated
Short term or interim stabilization methods may include construction of reinforced
earth structures to stiffen the upper portions of the landslide by reducing differential
movements near the surface A structure such as this was recently constructed on

Our rainfall summary is based on a review of the available rainfall data and
monthly precipitation summaries prepared by the National Weather Service Forecast
Office Honolulu NWSFOH During the past 12 month monitoring period the project
site experienced generally average to above average monthly rainfall ranging between
about 25 percent and about 149 percent of normal
In general our interpretation of the earth movement response to elevated rainfall
is that if a period of increased or above normal rainfall is experienced following a
prolonged period of normal or lower than normal rainfall the earth movement rates at
some of the inclinometers tend to increase abruptly following the period of elevated
precipitation and then settle back to normal levels In addition following extended
periods of below normal precipitation earth movements generally decrease slightly
Subsurface Water Monitoring
The monitoring of subsurface water levels groundwater is accomplished by
measuring the level of water head contained within the individual and nested
piezometers installed in the borings at the project site Sixteen piezometers were initially
installed in 8 boreholes at the project site A list of the piezometer installations for the
project is provided in the Table entitled Summary of Completed Instrumented
Installations on Pages 7 and 8 of this report Of the sixteen piezometers initially
installed eleven piezometers remain functional for current water level measurement
The eleven functional piezometers are installed in the borings identified as
Instrument Plan Plate 2
Three of the eleven functional piezometers identified as P 2A P 4A and P 8
contain dedicated vibrating wire piezometer installations that provide a near continuous
record of the water level encountered in the well casing The other piezometers consist
of simple standpipe well casings which are monitored manually on an annual basis
Charts depicting the water level records at Piezometer Nos P 2A P 4A and P 8 are
presented on Plates 5 1 through 5 6
Based on our monitoring of the groundwater levels from the eleven functional
piezometers over the past six month monitoring period the records indicate that

Based on our review and interpretation of the groundwater levels recorded
near continuously by the vibrating wire piezometers it appears that periodic
groundwater influxes resulting from normal rainfall events head spikes are relatively
abrupt followed by a gradual dissipation of the head The gradual head dissipation
typically occurs over a period on the order of several days to several weeks Very high
spikes in head level as a result of extreme rainfall and or prolonged heavy rain events
typically take several months to fully dissipate the elevated groundwater head level

Observations Pavement Structural Deformation
General reconnaissance of the Kuahea Street Area Movement study area was
conducted on to record observations pertaining to the existing site
conditions
Based on our observations current visible evidence of active earth movement
affecting the public right of way is most significant at Kuahea Street Area A between
Helo Place and Kuahea Place This area shows strong visible evidence of compounded
ground surface heave and or subsidence within the roadway corridor The pavement
distress has created a rough and irregular roadway surface Other paved roadway
surfaces which cross other areas of earth movement within the study area are in
generally good to fair shape as a result of recent construction repairs conducted at
Waiomao Road Year 2012 and the Waiomao Landslide head at Kuahea Street in
2013 Surface groundwater seepages were not observed within the study area at the
time of our reconnaissance
Kuahea Street Area A between Helo Place and Kuahea Place
The primary area of ground surface deformation within the public right of way at
Kuahea Street Area A was observed to extend between about
2326 Kuahea Street and about 2221 Kuahea Place Based on our observations
the temporary pavement repairs patches on Kuahea Street are in generally fair
condition but show signs of stress as evidenced by widening crack apertures and
some vertical offsets at the joints between adjacent concrete pavement slabs By
visual observation the undulatory condition of the existing concrete pavement
and deflections in the street curbing on Kuahea Street appears to be gradually
increasing in magnitude although no pavement surface rupture was observed
The ride over the undulating and patched pavement is rough and irregular and
should be negotiated at low travel speed Other observations are as follows

Kipona Place
Based on our observations the existing roadway and upslope conditions along
Kipona Place at the toe of the Waiomao Landslide appear to be generally static
with minimal visible signs of overt ground distress and deformation
The localized earth slump previously reported along the slope toe at
Kipona Place adjacent to the existing home at 2345 Kipona Place appears to be
static or very slowly creeping at this time Some slight seepage of groundwater
was observed along the toe of the slope at the edge of the pavement
Re activation of the soil slump may be experienced as the wetter winter season
approaches Because the slump has the potential to continue creeping
downslope remedial slope stabilization efforts generally consisting of slope
drainage and surface grading are recommended to reduce the potential for a
larger release of earth material to affect the roadway The slump triggering
mechanism appears to be rainfall infiltration and potential ponding of water within
dips on the slope above the slump area Future earth movements slumps
should be anticipated to occur following larger magnitude rainfall events

emanating from the existing groundwater subdrain outlets installed at the
Waiomao Landslide The approximate location of the subdrains is shown on
Plate 2 The subdrain system was installed as part of the early landslide
remediation work performed at the project site It appears the groundwater
subdrains are currently non functional
