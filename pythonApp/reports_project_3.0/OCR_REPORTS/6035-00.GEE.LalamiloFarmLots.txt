1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the New Power Pole P 6 Lalamilo Farm Lots project located in the
Waimea area on the Island of Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and recommendations resulting from our
field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These recommendations
are intended for the design of a new power pole foundation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
The proposed project involves the installation of a new power pole at Lalamilo
Farm Lots in the Waimea area on the Island of Hawaii Based on the information
provided the existing wooden Power Pole P 13 will be replaced by a new steel Power
Pole P 6 We understand that the new power pole will be located about 5 feet north of
the existing power pole Further details of the new power pole were not available at the
time this report was prepared

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and MaunaLoa and
Kilauea both still active
The site is situated in a saddle between Kohala and Mauna Kea where
Mauna Kea lava flows banked against the older Kohala rocks Based on available
geological information and geomorphological interpretation the site is located on
Mauna Kea rocks near the surface contact with Kohala
Following the cessation of activity in Kohala and Mauna Kea activity from the other
shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over much of the island This ash has
weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil which is typified by low in situ densities and high
field moisture contents In some locales when the field moisture content is sufficiently
high the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties i e the soil temporarily loses strength
when remolded and regains its strength after internal pore pressures dissipate In more
arid areas the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even though field moisture contents can
approach 100 percent of dry weight Our exploration indicates that the site is underlain by
a surface mantle of this ash soil which grades with depth to basalt formation and clinker

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling one boring
designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of about 30 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our boring indicates that the project site is generally underlain by ashy fill
materials underlain by clinker and basalt formation The fill materials generally
consisted of medium stiff to stiff clayey silts extending to about 5 feet below the ground
surface Fills were underlain by medium dense clinker with varying amounts of volcanic
ash Basalt formation was encountered at about 16 feet below the ground surface and
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 30 5 feet below the ground surface
The basalt formation encountered was generally very hard and relatively unweathered
We did not encounter groundwater in the boring drilled at the time of our field
exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels may fluctuate due to seasonal
precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors

Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the
cast in place concrete shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent
soils Considering that the shaft is designed based on adhesion between the shaft
and the surrounding soils we believe that an ultimate uplift capacity of 350 kips
may be used in the design The uplift load capacity provided includes the weight of
the cast in place concrete shaft
The ultimate uplift load capacities provided above should be used for transient
loads only For sustained uplift loads acting on the foundation the ultimate uplift
load capacities should be reduced by a factor of safety of 2 0 The project structural
engineer should check the structural capacity of the shaft member in tension

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the power pole foundation may be resisted by the lateral
load capacity of the cast in place concrete shaft In general lateral load resistance
of the shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the
shaft allowable deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for
Windows which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally
loaded cast in place concrete shaft program originally developed at the University
of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a
cast in place concrete shaft under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis
was Carried out with the use of non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The
lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various
depths

Construction Considerations
In general the performance of a cast in place concrete shaft depends significantly
upon the contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The
following conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of
the cast in place concrete shaft foundation
The load bearing capacity of the shaft depends to a significant extent on the
frictional resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques especially during the excavation operations are important
The contractor should exercise care in excavating the shaft hole and in placing
concrete into the excavated hole
It should be noted that the drilled shaft is designed to extend into the dense basalt
formation In addition localized hard zones of basalt core rock may be present in
the clinker layer Therefore some difficult excavation conditions may be
encountered and should be expected The cast in place concrete shaft contractor
will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to excavate through these
types of natural obstructions where encountered
The shaft excavation may also encounter perched groundwater or pockets of soft
zones The consistency of the clinker layer may significantly vary with depth
Therefore some caving in and or sloughing of these materials may occur during
the excavation operations In addition due to the depth of the shaft excavation up
to 18 feet below ground surface temporary casing may be required to reduce the
potential for caving in of the excavated hole during the cast in place concrete shaft
installation
We believe the contractor should thoroughly address construction safety and
anticipated methods of construction It should be noted that the contractor is solely
responsible for job site safety

A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range should be used

should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling one boring to a depth of approximately 30 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The boring was drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous
flight augers and coring tools at the approximate location shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Log of Boring Plate A 1
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Log of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests performed on selected samples are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of

classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on
