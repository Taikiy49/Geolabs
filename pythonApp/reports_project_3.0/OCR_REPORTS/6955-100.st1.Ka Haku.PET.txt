PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The new development requires installation of 184 24-inch diameter Augered 
Cast-in-Place (ACIP) piles.  During the ACIP pile installation, the contractor encountered 
a condition where rebar cages could not be lowered to the design elevations at Pile Nos. 
2, 3, and 8.  For those piles, the rebar cages were pulled out and the piles were redrilled 
and regrouted.  The rebar cage was successfully lowered to the design elevation on the 
second attempt at Pile No. 3.  Pile Nos. 2 and 8 took a total of 5 and 3 attempts before 
the rebar cage was successfully lowered to the design elevation, respectively. 
In addition, Pile Nos. 4 and 7 did not have grout return during the initial grouting of 
the pile.  During the second drilling and grouting attempt, Pile Nos. 4 and 7 were 
successfully installed with grout return. 
Pile Nos. 1, 5, 6, 9, and 10 were successfully installed during the initial installation 
attempt.  However, we did note that the rebar cages needed to be pushed down with 
assistance from a mini-excavator for Pile Nos. 1, 6, 9 and 10. 
Based on our observations during the installation of the ACIP piles, the piles that 
the rebar cages could not be installed easily are clustered around the northwestern 
portion of the site.  It should be noted that the other piles installed to date have not 
encountered a condition where rebar cages could not be lowered to the design elevations.

LOW STRAIN IMPACT (LSI) TESTING 
In general, the LSI testing was conducted in accordance with ASTM D5882 
utilizing a Pulse Echo Tester (PET) manufactured by PileTest. The PET system has an 
analog to digital resolution of at least 24 bits, a sampling frequency exceeding 50,000 Hz, 
contains available data storage so additional processing and further wave analysis are 
possible, and contains a display to present results in the field for evaluations of preliminary 
data quality and interpretation. 
The tops of the piles were prepared by grinding and smoothing testing areas for 
attaching the instrumentation. Special handheld hammer and/or rubber mallet were used 
to impact the pile top.  An accelerometer was attached to the top of the pile with coupling 
grease to record the accelerations of the compression strain waves generated by the

hammer strike.  Each LSI test consisted of striking the pile top several consecutive times 
until several blows with consistent compression strain wave signals were recorded.  
These signals were then averaged and stored by the PET system. Low Strain Impact 
tests were performed at least two locations on each pile.   
The resulting LSI velocity records were independently evaluated by Geosciences 
Testing and Research, Inc. (GTR).  The results of their analysis are presented on Plates 
2.1 through 2.35.  In general, the LSI velocity records were integrated, filtered and 
magnified to enable interpretation of the data along the pile.  The data were reviewed for 
responses that may be a result of the reflection from the tip of the pile or reflections that 
may have occurred prior to the anticipated length.  A downward reflection at the pile tip in 
the same direction as the impact indicates a decrease in impedance, where the pile tip is 
typically in a material that is less dense than concrete. An upward tip reflection in the 
opposite direction as the impact indicates an increase in impedance, typically indicating 
the pile tip in rock or a material that is denser than concrete. An early downward reflection 
in the same direction as the impact pulse suggests a reduction in pile impedance (area 
and/or modulus) or soil friction. An early upward reflection opposite of the impact pulse 
suggests an increase in pile impedance (area and/or modulus) or soil friction. 
FINDINGS AND CONCLUSION 
Based on GTR’s evaluation of the LSI test results, we present the following findings 
for Pile Nos. 1 through 10: 
Pile Nos. 1, 3, 5, 8 and 10: 
A clear to probable upward and/or downward reflection were observed near 
the expected pile tip elevation, indicating positive integrity of the pile to the 
installed pile lengths. Minor early reflections were observed in the top 5 feet 
of the piles, indicating that some micro-cracks may have formed during the 
chipping of the pile tops.   
Pile Nos. 2, 4, 6, 7 and 9: 
Minor early reflections were observed in the top 5 feet of the piles, except Pile 
No. 6, indicating that some micro-cracks may have formed during the chipping 
of the pile tops.  No other significant reflections were observed to a depth of 
about 50 to 60 feet.  Although upward or downward reflections were observed 
near the expected tip elevations, the integrity of the piles are assumed to be 
good to a depth of about 60 to 80 feet.  The integrity of the pile beyond these 
depths is inconclusive. 
It should be noted that the expected lengths of Pile Nos. 2, 4 and 6 range from 
about 80.5 to 83 feet below test elevation.  Therefore, the LSI test results 
nearly confirm positive integrity for the entire length of these piles.  In addition, 
Pile Nos. 7 and 9 did not encounter major problems during the installation of 
the piles.
