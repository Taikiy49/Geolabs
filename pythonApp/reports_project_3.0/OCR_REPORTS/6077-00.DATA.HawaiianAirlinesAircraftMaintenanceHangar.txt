INTRODUCTION
This report presents geotechnical data from our geotechnical engineering
explorations performed for the Design Build Request for Proposal of New Hawaiian
Airlines Aircraft Maintenance Hangar project west of the terminal building and Taxiway
L and north of Taxiway A within the Honolulu International Airport on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The approximate location of the new Hangar and the project vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report provides subsurface information that the project vicinity derived from
our field explorations and laboratory testing The information presented herein is
preliminary and is not intended to be used for design and construction The selected
design build contractor should conduct an independent geotechnical exploration and
analyses for their specific design The subsurface conditions presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
SOIL SAMPLING
A total of seven borings were drilled designated as Boring Nos 201 through 207
extending to depths of about 50 to 100 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools Our geologist classified the materials
encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in the field and observed
the drilling operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further
reviewed by visual observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A
of Appendix A Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM Test D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 or 24 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of coral and or rock materials encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral and or rock formation The
coral and or rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for
examination and description Photographs of the core samples are presented in
Appendix D
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil and or rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if
3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of coral and or rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run
that is sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50

LABORATORY TESTING
Laboratory testing was performed on the selected soil rock samples obtained
during the field exploration as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and
evaluating their engineering properties The tests performed are described in the
following
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Eight Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1 of Appendix B
Twelve Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are
provided on Plates B 2 through B 4 of Appendix B
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the silty
and clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure
was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed soil sample
The test results and the stress strain curve are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6 of
Appendix B
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on
selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the coral
formation encountered at the project site Unconfined compression test results are
presented on Plate B 7 of Appendix B
SEISMIC SHEAR WAVE VELOCITY TESTING
Shear wave velocity profiling using seismic piezocone SCPTu testing
equipment was performed at discrete intervals extending to a depth of approximately
100 feet below the existing ground surface at Boring No 204 as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 Based on the subsurface conditions the weighted average shear
wave velocity for the materials within the upper 100 feet of the soil profile is on the order
of about 1 620 feet per second at the test location

In order to conduct the shear wave velocity test in the boring the test boring was
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the borehole
Afterwards the borehole was backfilled with 0 25 inch diameter coated bentonite pellets
The temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the
bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward When the bentonite
pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the borehole the pellets started to hydrate
slowly As the bentonite pellets hydrate they swell and soften The probes were then
pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a depth of about 100 feet below the
existing ground surface using seismic piezocone testing equipment SCPTy
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected
surface area of 1 55 square inches 10 square centimeters into the ground at a rate of
approximately 20 mm second 5 mm second The depth of the cone penetration was
recorded at about 2 inch 5 centimeter intervals using an electronic depth sensor
attached to the cone rod
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear
wave generated and propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements
are taken when the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A
shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched
hammer The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was
measured at each discrete depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided
by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths provided the
average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average shear wave
velocity calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the boring location are
presented on Plate Nos C 1 1 through C 2 of Appendix C The weighted average shear
wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear wave velocity method
described in Section of the International Building Code 2006 Edition
