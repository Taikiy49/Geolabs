Koloa PV Solar Power Facility
Preliminary Geotechnical Recommendations
Original / File
This technical memorandum presents our preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations in support of the planning and design of the Koloa PV Solar Power Facility
project in Koloa on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. A detailed summary of our findings and
recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report. The final report
should be consulted when it becomes available.
Project Considerations
The project site is in the Koloa area of the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The site is former
sugar cane land that is currently being used for mixed agricultural purposes. It is proposed to
develop the site for a photovoltaic solar power generation facility.
A total of 19 borings were drilled and sampled at the site to depths ranging between
15.5 and 20.5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig. Soil
samples were collected using Modified California and standard split-spoon samplers. Rock core
samples were obtaining using diamond core drilling techniques, i.e., HQ wireline coring.
Subsurface Conditions
In general, our borings encountered medium hard to hard basalt rock at relatively shallow
depths (less than about 5 feet) in the western portion of the site; and, at somewhat greater
depths (approximately 10 feet) in the eastern portion.
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings during our field exploration. It should
be noted that the groundwater levels are subject to change due seasonal precipitation, storm
surge conditions, and other factors.

Foundation Support System Evaluation 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using either 
spread/mat foundations or drilled shafts for support of the solar panel structures. Since the site 
generally consists of medium hard to hard basalt rock near the surface, we believe that it is not 
practical to support solar panels with driven post foundations.  Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for design of the recommended foundation support systems are presented in 
the following sections. Design loads are not yet available; however, based on previous solar 
panel projects, we anticipate that the loads will be light. When structural loads are available, we 
should re-evaluate and finalize the foundation recommendations. 
Shallow Foundations 
For design of shallow spread or mat foundations, allowable bearing pressures of up to 
3,000 and 6,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for foundations bearing on the 
surface residual soil and medium hard to hard basalt rock, respectively.  These bearing 
pressures may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces. 
Moduli of subgrade reaction of 175 and 300 pounds per square inch per inch of 
deflection (pci) may be used for mat foundations bearing on the residual soil or medium hard to 
hard basalt, respectively.  The subgrade modulus is the modulus of vertical subgrade reaction 
for a 1-foot square test plate. 
For foundations bearing on the surface residual soil, the foundation subgrade should be 
recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the 
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures. 
In general, foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finish grades.  Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded 
below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench 
or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging 
adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for 
foundation settlement. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed between the 
bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting against the 
near-vertical faces of the foundation system.  Coefficients of friction of 0.35 and 0.55 may be 
used for footings bearing on the residual soil or basalt rock, respectively.  Resistance due to 
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soil around footings is 
well-compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 
12 inches of soil should be neglected.

Drilled Shaft Foundations 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, the proposed solar 
panels may be supported by a foundation system consisting of cast-in-place concrete drilled 
shafts.  The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from 
skin friction between the shafts and the surface soil and basalt. 
We recommend using a drilled shaft with a minimum diameter of 12 inches.  Based on 
the subsurface conditions and the anticipated structural loads, we recommend extending the 
drilled shaft 6 feet below the proposed finish grade.  An allowable compressive load capacity of 
up to 20 kips per shaft may be used for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces. 
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the shaft and shear 
along the shaft surface and the adjacent soil or basalt.  The recommended uplift load capacity 
(ultimate capacity) is 13 kips.  The uplift load capacity provided above includes the weight of the 
drilled shaft.  The uplift load capacity of the shaft provided is intended for transient load 
applications.  Therefore, the uplift load capacity should not be further increased for transient 
load applications.  The capacity of the shaft in tension should be checked by the project 
structural engineer. 
Lateral loads imposed on drilled shaft foundations may be resisted by resistance of the 
shaft-soil system to deflection.  Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of the 
stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable deflection at the top of shaft, 
and induced moment in the shaft.  The estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft, based 
on the anticipated structural loads, should be less than 0.25 inches.
