SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using
a deep foundation system consisting of driven piles to support the new building structure.
The driven piles should consist of 12-inch square and/or 16.5-inch octagonal precast,
prestressed concrete piles, which may be designed with allowable compressive load
capacities of up to 50 tons (100 kips) and 75 tons (150 kips) per pile, respectively. To
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement and over-stressing of the coral
ledge, we recommend that the piles be spaced a minimum of 4 feet from center-to-center.
The boring drilled on the east side of the site encountered a coral ledge at about
28 feet below the existing ground surface. Preliminary pile lengths for piles driven on the
east side of the new building would be on the order of about 30 to 35 feet to develop the
recommended compressive load capacity from end bearing on the coral ledge. It should
be noted that a coral ledge was not encountered in the boring drilled on the west side of
the site. Therefore, there is a potential that the piles driven on the west side of the new
building may not encounter the coral ledge. Driven piles that do not encounter the coral
ledge will have to extend down to minimum depths of 110 and 130 feet below the existing
ground surface for the 12-inch square and 16.5-inch octagonal concrete piles,
respectively.
Due to the limited budget for geotechnical engineering exploration, only two borings
were drilled at the project site. With the limited amount of subsurface information obtained
from our exploration, it is difficult to determine whether the coral ledge exists at the west
side of the new building. Therefore, we recommend conducting an extensive test pile
program, consisting of a minimum of 25 percent of the total number of piles to better define
the subsurface conditions below the new building structure. If reducing the amount of test
piles is desired, we recommend drilling additional borings to define the subsurface
conditions below the new building structure. The text of this report should be referred to
for detailed discussion and specific recommendations.

2.1. Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of
the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in
Central Oahu.
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building activities ceased
approximately 2% million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the Island
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early
volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across the mouth of the bays. The
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), there were many sea level
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As
the great continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less
water available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or
melted, global sea levels rose because more water was available. The landmass of
Oahu remained essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were
eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea,
which were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu.
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at

2.3. Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about
53 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the borings
drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The borings generally encountered fill materials extending to depths of about
5 feet below the existing ground surface. The fills consisted of silty sand with some silty
clay. Based on the borings, the fills encountered at the east side of the site were
generally medium dense to dense and the fills encountered at the west side of the site
were generally loose. The fills were underlain by very loose/very soft lagoonal deposits
extending to depths of about 25 to 28 feet below the existing ground surface. A hard
coral ledge was encountered at the boring drilled on the east side of the site.
The subsurface conditions at the east side of the site consisted of a hard coral
ledge below the lagoonal deposits. The coral ledge extended to a depth of about
37 feet below the existing ground surface. Below the hard coral ledge, silty coralline
sand, alternating in relative density of loose to dense, was encountered to the maximum
depth explored.
The subsurface conditions below the lagoonal deposits at the west side of the
site consisted of a thin layer of very loose alluvium. Below the alluvium, coralline
detritus consisting of clayey and silty coralline sand was encountered to a depth of
about 50 feet below the existing ground surface. The relative density of the coralline
detritus was generally medium dense. The coralline detritus was underlain by
weathered sandstone consisting of medium dense, moderately cemented coralline sand
extending to the maximum depth explored.
Groundwater was encountered at about 4.1 feet below the existing ground
surface at the time of our field exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels
are expected to fluctuate with variation in tides, rainfall, surface water runoff,
groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

2.4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code (UBC) (current code adopted by the
City and County of Honolulu), the Island of Oahu is located within Seismic Zone 2A with
an associated peak ground acceleration of 0.15g. Therefore, the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be
addressed for the project. The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity
of the Island of Oahu, the soil profile for seismic design at the site, and the potential for
liquefaction at the site.
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by
instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and
a few cause minor to moderate damage.
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over
90 percent of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential
Based on the 1997 UBC, the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and
should be evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered and a design peak ground acceleration of 0.15g,
liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable settlements to the ground surface.
Therefore, the effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into consideration in
the design of the new storage facility.

Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected
to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease
in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective
stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the
pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid.
During liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations.
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface. In deeper deposits, the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring. The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:
1. Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands.
2. Initial Relative Density - Loose sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction. Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to
relative density.
3. Magnitude and Duration of Vibration - Liquefaction potential is
directly proportional to the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake.
In general, the subsurface information obtained from the borings indicates that the
site is underlain by very loose/very soft lagoonal deposits overlying coral formation
and coralline detritus. The lagoonal deposits consisting of very loose silty sands
and very soft sandy silts with little cohesion are potentially liquefiable during a

3.1 Pile Foundations
The new storage facility will consist of an eight-story structure. Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered in our drilled borings and the anticipated structural
loads for the eight-story structure, we recommend using driven pile foundations to
support the new eight-story building structure planned at the project site.
We recommend using 12-inch square and/or 16.5-inch octagonal precast,
prestressed concrete piles to support the new building structure. The 12-inch square
and 16.5-inch octagonal concrete piles may be designed with allowable compressive
load capacities of up to 50 tons (100 kips) and 75 tons (150 kips) per pile, respectively.
These allowable compressive load capacities are for dead-plus-live loads and may be
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces.
To reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement and over-stressing
of the coral ledge, we recommend spacing the piles a minimum of 4 feet from center-to-
center. The minimum pile spacing recommended would also avoid the potential for
further reduction in vertical capacity due to group action and would also facilitate pile
driving in groups. In addition, we also recommend increasing the pile spacing as
appropriate to keep the bearing pressures (column load divided by the pile cap area) on
the coral ledge to about 12 kips or less per square foot.

3.1.1 Downdrag Loads
The lagoonal deposits at the project site may be susceptible to liquefaction during a
seismic event producing a horizontal peak ground acceleration of about 0.15g at
the project site. In the event of seismically induced liquefaction, settlement of the
lagoonal deposits will occur at the project site. When the lagoonal deposits move
downward relative to the stationary pile, the downward movement will result in
negative skin friction forces, generally known as downdrag forces, acting on the pile
shaft. The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the
liquefiable layer, relative density of the potentially liquefiable lagoonal deposits, and
the pile perimeter.
Due to the potential for liquefaction of the very loose/very soft lagoonal deposits at
the project site, settlement of the lagoonal deposits will result in downdrag loading
of the deep foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable materials.
Therefore, the allowable compressive load capacities of the piles have been
reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses. Based
on our foundation analyses, we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the
vertical piles due to the potential for liquefaction of the lagoonal deposits may be on
the order of about 25 kips per pile for both the 12-inch square and 16.5-inch
octagonal piles. We believe that the downdrag loads imposed on the pile
foundations as a result of soil liquefaction may be considered a transient load.

3.1.5 Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of

3.1.6 Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the
range of about 25,000 to 35,000 foot-pounds in the field. The hammer should be
equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the loose/soft soils
may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development, which
could damage or cause cracking of the piles.
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the “matching” of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven. This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles. Geolabs should review
the proposed pile driving equipment to evaluate whether the equipment is capable
of installing the piles to the recommended capacity. The contractor should forward
pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs for evaluation prior to
construction. Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the driveability of
piles with the use of the selected hammer.

3.2. Slabs-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the new storage
building. As previously indicated, the project site could be subjected to appreciable
ground settlements in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event
(M6+). Seismically induced ground settlements on the order of about 3 to 6 inches may
be anticipated, and the building slabs-on-grade may sustain some damage. Therefore,
there is a potential for damage and distress to the slabs-on-grade (not structurally
supported). However, we believe that the potential for damage may be significantly
reduced by increasing the building slab thickness and by incorporating steel
reinforcement into the concrete slab.
As discussed above, concrete slabs-on-grade may be subjected to ground
settlements and some distress in the event of seismically induced soil liquefaction. As
an alternative to slabs-on-grade, consideration may be given to structurally supporting
the ground floor slab on the deep foundation system to reduce the potential for damage
to the building slab in the event of seismically induced ground settlements.
In general, the subgrades for concrete slabs-on-grades should be scarified to a
depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture, and compacted to minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative

maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557.

maximum dry density.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2. The borings were advanced
to a maximum depth of about 53 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight auger and
rotary coring tools. The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition,
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C 136 & D 1140) was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on
Plate B-1.
