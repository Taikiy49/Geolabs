Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and it was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank
Kalihi Valley was formed by stream erosion of Tertiary Age Koolau Range basalt
flows This initial period of erosion and valley carving was followed by several periods of
relative rise and fall of sea level which permitted valley filling by alluvium followed by
subsequent periods of erosion During the Quaternary Age Honolulu Volcanic Series
lava flowed down the valley and was subsequently partially covered by Quaternary Age
alluvium The project site is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano on the slope of
Kahauiki Ridge and to the northwest side of Kalihi Valley Continued recent alluvial
processes have covered the site with black to dark gray adobe clay

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by highly
expansive adobe clay soils extending from the existing ground surface to depths of about
8 to 13 feet below the existing ground pavement surface Stiff to very stiff sandy silts were
then encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately
27 5 feet below the existing ground surface It should be noted that boulders and basalt
rock formation were encountered at greater depths in some of the drilled borings We did
not encounter static groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However perched groundwater was encountered at depths of about 12 and 24 feet in
two of the drilled borings Our experience in the area suggested that the groundwater
encountered could be subterranean seepage during and after heavy rains
Based on the generally stiff subsurface soil conditions encountered at the site
we believe that shallow foundations may be used for support of the new structures
planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for
the design of shallow footings bearing on 12 inches of structural fill placed over the
recompacted in situ soils The structural fill should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction and should extend a minimum of 12 inches beyond the
edges of the footing The in situ soils below the 12 inch structural fill material should be
compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction
Laboratory testing indicated that the adobe clay soils encountered at the project
site exhibit high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore new concrete walkways planned for the project should be supported on
24 inches of non expansive select granular fill The select granular fill should be
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction In addition crack control joints
should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints
at right angle intersections The subgrade soils beneath the walkways should be
recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction in lieu of the normal
90 percent relative compaction

Foundations
Based on the relatively light structural loads and anticipated subsurface soil
conditions at the project site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread
footings may be used to support the proposed new structures planned for the site and
dwelling improvement project An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the foundation design of the proposed new structures
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Due to the wet environment and potential for shallow subterranean seepage at
the project site a 12 inch thick layer of compacted structural fill material should be
provided below the bottom of footings The 12 inch thick structural fill will serve to
provide more uniform bearing support and facilitate the footing construction work during
wet season The over excavation for the compacted structural fill should extend beyond
the edges of the footing a minimum of 12 inches Prior to placement of the structural fill
the bottom of the footing over excavations should be recompacted to no less than
85 percent relative compaction Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom
of the structural fill layer should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction A Geolabs representative
should evaluate the amount of additional over excavation and the need for geotextile
fabric around the structural backfill material based on the actual exposed field
conditions during construction
The compacted structural fill should meet the requirements of the non expansive
select granular fill material presented in the Site Preparation section herein
Compacted structural fill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation
placed directly around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of select fill material
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or less Unless covered by concrete slabs
the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious soils to reduce
the potential for excessive water infiltration behind the walls

Our field exploration indicated that the in situ soils generally exist in a very moist
to wet condition Therefore it would be very difficult to achieve the normal 90 percent
relative compaction requirement without significant aeration of the in situ soils Since
the project site is at the toe of sloping ground with high surface run off and subterranean
seepage substantial aeration of the in situ soils may not be feasible and practical
during the project construction As a result we believe that the compaction requirement
will need to be lowered to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction for subgrade
preparation Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
After clearing and demolition areas at grade or designated to receive fill should
be recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
compacted general fill Use of the vibratory action of a compactor should be carefully
controlled to avoid inducing a pumping subgrade condition If the subgrade soils are
pumping or yielding during subgrade preparation the subgrade should be stabilized

Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage at the site are especially
critical considering the sloping terrain and wet environment at the site Therefore the
finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and foundations
and to reduce the potential for ponding around the structures Excessive landscape
watering near the slabs and foundations should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
twelve borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 The borings extended to depths
of about 15 to 27 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drill rigs and portable drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
Our engineer geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Five one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test
results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 2
Two Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 3
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D2850
were performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear
strengths of the soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures were
used as the applied confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil samples
The test results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 4 through B 6
One Direct Shear test ASTM D3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear test are presented on Plate B 7
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the shallow subsurface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 8 through B 12
Five sets of Corrosivity tests including Minimum Resistivity
ASTM G57 Chloride Content and Sulfate Content tests
were performed by our office and TestAmerica Analytical Testing Corporation on
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test results are
summarized on Plate B 13
