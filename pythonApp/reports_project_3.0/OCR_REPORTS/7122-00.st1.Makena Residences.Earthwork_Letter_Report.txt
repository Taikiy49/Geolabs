In accordance with the scope of services outlined in our fee proposal, we 
performed a geotechnical engineering exploration in support of the design of the 
Makena Golf and Beach Club project in the Makena area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. 
This letter report summarizes the work performed and presents our retaining wall and 
earthwork recommendations for development of the project. It should be noted that the 
retaining wall and earthwork recommendations provided are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is at the existing Makena Golf and Beach Resort in the Makena 
area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project generally consists of renovating the 
existing hotel building into luxury condominium units. In addition to the renovation, new 
construction at the site includes various cottages, condominiums, and hales. New 
parking areas, a restaurant, swimming pools, and other associated amenities also will 
be constructed. 
Based on the preliminary plans provided, new fills of up to about 32 feet in height 
may be required at portions of the site. In addition, new fills will be placed up against the 
existing hotel building in some areas. In order to reduce the lateral earth pressures 
acting against the existing building walls, installation of geotextile fabric retaining walls 
(GFRW) are planned. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration to date consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings 
extending to depths of about 20.5 to 28 feet below the existing ground surface. Based 
on our borings, the project site is generally underlain by surface fill materials placed

GEOTEXTILE FABRIC RETAINING WALL 
In order to reduce the lateral earth pressures acting against the existing building 
walls, we understand that it is desired to construct GFRW along the existing building 
walls. We understand that new fills of up to about 32 feet in height are planned along 
the existing building. The following contains our recommendations for design of the 
GFRW planned at the project site. 
The GFRW system is a composite wall system, which utilizes the woven 
geotextile fabric to reinforce the backfill zone and to improve the shear strength of the 
reinforced soil zone. This composite wall system essentially forms a gravity wall 
structure with an ability to carry appreciable structural loads. In addition, geotextile 
fabric retaining walls are desirable due to the flexibility of the wall, ease of construction, 
high load carrying capacity, and economy. 
In general, the reinforced zone should extend laterally behind the wall face to a 
distance of approximately 80 percent of the wall height. The woven geotextile fabric 
should be placed at about every 24 inches of fill height (maximum vertical spacing) for 
GFRW heights of up to 20 feet. For the portion of the GFRW greater than 20 feet in 
height, the maximum vertical spacing of the woven geotextile fabric should be reduced 
to 12 inches. The front face of the woven geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 
3 feet from the face of the wall. 
The reinforced fill material should consist of select granular fill materials with 
maximum particle size of 3 inches compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction (ASTM D1557). Within 3 feet from the wall face, the compaction 
requirement may be reduced to 90 percent relative compaction. The reinforced fill 
material should have an angle of internal friction of at least 38 degrees when compacted

to at least 95 percent relative compaction. We anticipate that excavated basalt rock

The majority of the GFRW will be constructed along the existing building wall. 
GFRW required behind the existing building wall may be constructed with a vertical face 
inclination. In order to prevent compaction of the backfill directly against the existing 
building wall and to reduce the transfer of lateral loads onto the building wall, backfill 
within 2 feet from the face of the existing building wall should consist of free-draining 
materials, such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation or similar material. The existing 
building walls also should be checked to resist limited lateral earth pressures due to the 
free-draining open-graded gravel. In general, the existing building wall should be 
designed for at-rest earth pressure of 10 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). 
For walls greater than 15 feet in height, a constant at-rest earth pressure 150 pounds 
per square foot (psf) may be used below a depth of 15 feet. 
We understand the new GFRW required at the loading dock and other areas will 
not be constructed behind an existing building wall. For the new free standing GFRW, 
the wall may be battered at an inclination of approximately one horizontal to twelve 
vertical (1H:12V). A fiber-reinforced shotcrete facing of at least 4 inches in thickness 
should be provided along the front face of the geotextile fabric retaining wall to protect 
the fabric from damage and exposure to ultraviolet light. 
The new GFRW should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the 
adjacent soils and surcharge effects. The recommended lateral earth pressure for design 
of the GFRW structure, expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of pounds of cubic foot 
(pcf), is 32 pcf. The incremental seismic lateral earth pressure for design of the GFRW 
structure is 16H2 pounds per linear foot of wall where H is the wall height in feet. 
In addition, the GFRW structure should be well drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 24-inch 
wide strips of prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, placed 
at 10 feet on centers placed behind the reinforced soil zone. The prefabricated drainage 
product also should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the 
wall. The perforated pipe should daylight into appropriate drainage system. In addition, 
the open-graded gravel backfill in front of GFRW behind the existing building wall 
should be hydraulically connected to the existing building wall drainage pipe. For the 
free standing GFRW structure at the loading dock and other areas, weepholes also 
should be provided to allow for drainage of water trapped behind the shotcrete facing. 
The use of a temporary form brace system at the front face of the wall during 
construction may be used to achieve the required compaction of at least 90 percent 
relative compaction (ASTM D1557) within 3 feet of the wall face.

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, debris, old foundations, old pavements, deleterious 
materials, existing structures to be demolished, and other unsuitable materials, should 
be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for contamination 
of the excavated materials. 
Areas to receive fills and finish grades in cut areas should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. The 
vibratory drum roller also should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. A 
vibratory drum roller with a static weight of up to about 5 tons should be used to 
proof-roll subgrades within 10 feet laterally from the existing building. 
The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during clearing and 
proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill 
materials. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation of the collapsed lava tube features (and other features) and backfilled 
with compacted fill to account for variations in the over-excavation and backfill 
quantities.

Fills and Backfills 
In general, the near-surface sandy and gravelly soils encountered during our field 
exploration should be suitable for use as general fill materials, provided that the 
maximum particle size is 3 inches or less in largest dimension. The on-site materials 
generated from the excavations may be used as a source of general fill or backfill 
materials, provided that they are screened of the over-sized materials and/or processed 
to meet the above gradation requirements (less than 3 inches in largest dimension). 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coral or basalt. The select granular fill should be well graded from coarse to 
fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a swell potential of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The material also 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
In general, fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirement for the last lift of fill (finished subgrade) below areas subjected to vehicular 
traffic should be increased to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the fills. 
Excavation 
As mentioned previously, we anticipate excavations of up to about 10 feet below 
the existing ground surface will be required for construction of the new structure 
foundations and GFRW. The field exploration at the site encountered a surface fill layer 
underlain by dense basalt formation and clinker materials near the existing ground 
surface. 
It is anticipated that the fill materials, highly fractured basalt formation, and 
clinker materials may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, 
such as excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers. However, some of the 
excavations in native ground will encounter dense basalt formation and may require the 
use of hoerams or chipping.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in 
part, upon information obtained from the field borings. Variations of subsurface 
conditions between and beyond the boring locations may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If 
variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented in this letter report.

This letter report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in support of the earthwork considerations for the project only. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated subsurface conditions are 
commonly encountered. Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as perched 
groundwater, soft deposits, hard layers, or loose fills, may occur in localized areas and 
may require additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in 
construction delays) to attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient 
contingency fund is recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs. 
The geotechnical engineering exploration conducted at the project site was not 
intended to investigate the potential for presence of hazardous materials existing at the 
project site. It should be noted that the equipment, techniques, and personnel used to 
conduct a geo-environmental exploration differ substantially from those applied in 
geotechnical engineering.
