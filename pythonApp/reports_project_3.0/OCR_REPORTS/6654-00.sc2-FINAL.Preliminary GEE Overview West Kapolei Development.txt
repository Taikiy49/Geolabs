SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our preliminary field exploration, the northern half of the project site is 
generally underlain by very stiff alluvial clayey soils and layers of dense alluvial/colluvial 
cobbles and boulders extending from the existing ground surface to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 13 feet below the ground surface.  The northwestern corner of 
the project site is generally underlain by some thin clayey alluvial soils overlying hard 
basalt rock formation at relatively shallow depth.  The southern half of the project site is 
generally underlain by thin silty and clayey soils overlying reef formation materials 
including hard coralline limestone interstratified with soft to medium hard consolidated to 
semi-consolidated 
calcareous 
sediments 
representative 
of 
calcareous 
siltstone, 
sandstone, and coralline detritus. The reef formation materials are believed to be 
overlapped by younger near-surface alluvial soils at the northern half of the site. 
The northern portion of the project site also contain appreciable basaltic boulder 
deposits and localized areas of boulder fill, undocumented greenwaste, and refuse dumps 
southerly of the existing Honokai Hale subdivision. 
Based on our laboratory test results, the on-site alluvial clay soils have moderate to 
high shrink/swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuation. Consideration may be 
given to excavating and processing the on-site reef formation materials for use as capping 
fill over the expansive clay soils. Based on our field exploration, the excavated reef 
materials produce mainly silty sand content components with portions of the stratified 
formation producing high silt content. The harder coralline limestone materials may 
produce a greater volume of cobbles and boulders with sand included in the material 
gradation. 
During our field exploration, we observed that abundant shrinkage cracks exist at 
the ground surface across the project site.  If left unattended, the existing shrinkage cracks 
may increase further in size under prolonged dry weather conditions. 
Due to the stratified subsurface condition of significant portions of the project site, 
additional test borings should be conducted to investigate the deeper subsurface 
conditions where larger proposed cuts are proposed.  Based on the current mass grading 
scheme, test borings should also be conducted to supplement the findings derived from 
the preliminary relatively shallow depth test pit exploration.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering 
exploration performed for the Kapolei West Development Overview Study project in the 
Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and preliminary geotechnical considerations 
derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The 
findings presented herein are intended to assist in the preliminary planning of mass 
grading for future development of the site.  The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The approximate 489-acre undeveloped project site is in the Kapolei/Honouliuli 
area in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  It is generally bounded by 
Farrington Highway and the Honokai Hale / Nanakai Gardens Subdivisions toward the 
north, Kalaeloa Harbor toward the south, Kapolei Commons toward the east, and the 
existing Ko Olina Resort toward the west as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the preliminary planning information provided, we understand that it is 
desired to develop the site as a future residential community including homes and a golf 
course with regional drainage improvements.  Detailed information for the planned 
development was not available at the time this report was prepared.  Based on the 
available conceptual grading plan, cuts may extend up to about 45 feet in depth with 
some fills also reaching about 45 feet in height, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  We 
understand it may be desired to utilize the on-site coralline reef materials as capping fill 
over the potentially expansive alluvial clay soil conditions covering a majority of the site.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was mainly built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western portion of the island.  
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the northern and eastern portions of the island.  
As the older Waianae volcanic activity ceased, younger lava flows from Koolau 
continued to bank against Waianae’s east facing slopes forming a broad upland, known 
as the Schofield Plateau. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main 
shield-building phase approximately 2 million years ago.   
Chemical weathering and physical erosion of the volcanic uplands generated 
copious terrestrial sediments, which were transported down slope toward the coastline. 
The terrestrial and embedded marine sediments accumulated to comprise the low 
topographic gradient coastal plain known as the Ewa Plain.  In the project vicinity 
(Ko Olina) and further towards the east and northeast (East Kapolei), the terrestrial 
sediments accumulated and became interbedded with other marine sediments and 
hardened coral-algal and coral limestone reef formations to form a thick sedimentary 
wedge of material fronting the mountain foothills.  The thickness of the collective 
sedimentary wedge ranges from nearly absent in the area underlain by the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at the shoreline near Ewa Beach.  This wedge of 
sedimentary and calcareous reef materials comprising the Ewa Plain serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu.  
Deposition of recent or Holocene age sediments has continued from earlier geologic 
time through the present.   
During the Pleistocene Epoch, the global sea level fluctuated in response to 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.

The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.  These changes in sea level, over periods of geologic 
time, was a major driving force in both the deposition and erosion of the terrestrial 
alluvial/colluvial deposits and coralline reef materials underlying the project site.  In 
addition, previous agricultural development conducted within the last 100 years or so 
have aided in the transformation of the relatively undeveloped project site to its present 
landform. 
The future Kapolei West Development project site is on the western margin of the 
Ewa Plain and borders the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano.  Therefore, 
terrestrial alluvial soils, rocky colluvial sediments, various coralline limestone and 
calcareous sedimentary deposits, and some basaltic rock formation should be 
anticipated to underlie various areas of the project site.  In addition, some near-surface 
reddish-brown to tan colored silty and clayey soils were encountered that resemble 
deposits of unconsolidated volcanic ash, possibly deposited during late stage Waianae 
eruptions from scattered cone vents near Makakilo.  The approximate surface coverage 
of various geologic materials mapped by others is presented on the Surface Geology 
Map, Plate 3. 
2.2 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity. 
Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions.  In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of 
magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt.  The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can 
only be detected by sensitive instruments.  However, some of the earthquakes are 
strong enough to be felt, and few cause minor-to-moderate damage.

In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii.  Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the 
Island of Hawaii.  Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) 
are related to volcanism, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii.  The Island of Hawaii has 
experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, 
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5.  Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in 
the County of Maui.  The effects of larger earthquakes occurring on the Islands of 
Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu.  For example, small landslides 
occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (M6.8). 
Some structures on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a result of the 
Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
Over the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes 
occurring on the Island of Oahu that is greater than Magnitude 6.  An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred in 1948 along the hypothesized Diamond Head Fault 
offshore and northeasterly of the Island of Oahu.  The moderate tremor resulted in 
some broken windows, ruptured building walls, and broken underground water mains.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
3.1 
Site Condition Overview 
In general, the project site northerly of the existing Pump Road is underlain by 
very stiff alluvial silty clay and dense alluvial/colluvial cobbles and boulders (basaltic) 
overlying reef formation materials generally consisting of medium hard consolidated to 
semi-consolidated calcareous sediments and coralline limestone.  Bulldozed windrows 
and piles of large basaltic boulders are present at the ground surface at the western half 
of the site northerly of Pump Road.  In general, across the northern half of the site, the 
thickness of the near-surface alluvial and colluvial deposits overlying reef formation 
materials appears to thicken towards the north and northeast and thin towards the south 
and west, as depicted on the Idealized Subsurface Profiles on Plates 4.1 and 4.2.  Hard 
basalt rock at relatively shallow depth was encountered on the gentle south facing 
slopes behind the existing Honokai Hale subdivision area.  The depth to the hard basalt 
rock and the thickness of overlying alluvial clays containing cobbles and boulders 
appears to gradually increase in the southerly direction away from the existing 
Honokai Hale subdivision boundary.  As mentioned previously, substantial large boulder 
fills were observed comprising the upper slopes and terraces adjacent to the rear of the 
Honokai Hale subdivision.  Furthermore, evidence of possible undocumented fills and 
mixed refuse disposal was encountered on the gentle slopes southerly from the 
adjacent subdivision. 
In general, the project site southerly of Pump Road is underlain by medium hard 
to occasionally hard coralline limestone and consolidated to semi-consolidated 
(generally medium dense) calcareous sediments which are collectively referred to as 
reef formation. Some generally thin surficial tan to light reddish-brown silty and clayey 
soils was encountered overlying the reef formation materials.  It appears the thickness 
of the silty and clayey soils overlying the reef deposits gradually thickens toward the 
east across the southern half of the site. 
The calcareous reef formation encountered at the project site is classified as 
sedimentary in origin owing to the sedimentary processes of reef formation on the

3.3 
Planning Considerations 
Due to the relatively widespread occurrence of expansive alluvial clay soils 
encountered at the existing ground surface, we anticipate the need to cap the expansive 
soils with non-expansive fill to facilitate development of the site. Therefore, we 
understand it may be desired to utilize the excavated on-site reef formation materials as 
a source of non-expansive capping fill to cover the moderate to high shrink-swell 
potential surface soils.  It should be noted that natural stratification (horizontal layering) 
and lateral transitions within the reef formation materials may produce coralline 
aggregate materials that are high in silt content with limited granular material 
components.  The excavated reef formation materials may provide suitable material if 
properly blended to provide a well-graded granular fill. 
Our laboratory tests on the near-surface clayey and silty soil materials (alluvium) 
indicate variable soil shrinkage and expansion potential ranging from low to high, when 
subjected to moisture fluctuations. Therefore, proper moisture-conditioning and 
compaction is very important. The shrink/swell characteristics of the surface clay soils 
are exacerbated by the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei 
area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content. Therefore, special 
attention should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade soil preparation 
below the slabs and pavements in order to reduce the potential shrink/swell effects of 
the surface clayey soils on the new structures and improvements. 
Based on our field observations, abundant shrinkage cracks exist at the existing 
ground surface across the project site.  The existing shrinkage cracks, if left unattended, 
may increase further in size under prolonged dry weather conditions.  Shrinkage cracks 
adjacent to the building foundations are potentially detrimental because surface water

W.O. 6654-00

Hawaii  California 
can readily infiltrate through the crack opening and soften the subsurface soils around 
the building foundations, thus affecting their performance.  Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the recommended site preparation procedures prior to placing fill at 
the site. 
As mentioned previously, we observed some substantial surface deposits of 
large basaltic boulders at the northern and northwestern portions of the site.  The 
observed boulder deposits represent boulder fills on the slopes south of the existing 
Honokai Hale subdivision and bulldozed boulder stockpiles on the low gradient slopes in 
the vicinity of Test Pit Nos. 6 through 8.  The piles and windrows of boulders may have 
been placed to aid in stormwater drainage because the deposits appear aligned parallel 
with the drainage channels traversing this portion of the site.  The boulder fills adjacent 
to the existing subdivision may require removal and/or processing for use as fill during 
site development.  A limited amount of boulders can be incorporated into the deeper 
fills.  It should be noted that many of the boulders exposed on the surface may have 
economic value as ornamental stone (“mossrock”).  Consideration could be given to 
harvesting the surface boulders to recover this economic resource. 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the existing gentle slopes southerly of the 
Honokai Hale subdivision appear to contain undocumented deposits of refuse, fill soils, 
and greenwaste materials that will require removal as part of the site development. 
Finally, ground settlements are anticipated when new fills in excess of about 
20 feet high are placed over the existing ground to raise the site to the finished 
elevations. The adverse effects of these settlements may mitigated by increased 
compaction and a waiting period prior to construction of permanent structures. These 
settlements will affect the construction schedule and the earthwork quantity estimates. 
3.4 
Groundwater Conditions 
Groundwater in Hawaii generally occurs as a basal water table in conformance 
with the Ghyben-Herzberg Principle, where, due to density differences, fresh water 
floats on salt water in dynamic equilibrium forming a lens of fresh water.  With sufficient 
recharge, the lens of fresh water will build up so that the water table surface is above 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 6654-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 17 
Hawaii  California 
sea level and the fresh water will displace salt water so that for every 1-foot of fresh 
water above sea level, there will be about 40 feet of fresh water below sea level. 
Our review of available subsurface information indicates that there is little specific 
information pertaining to groundwater occurrence at the site. Therefore, it is reasonable 
to assume that groundwater across the site generally occurs near the sea level 
elevation.  However, there may be locally high groundwater tables near significant 
hydrological features, such as ditches and streams. 
3.5 
Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the southern portion of the development site is 
generally underlain by stratified medium hard to hard coralline limestone and other 
generally very stiff/medium dense to loose calcareous sedimentary reef deposits, 
collectively referred to as reef formation deposits. Based on our experience, the reef 
formation in the project area is typically slightly to moderately weathered and ranges 
from soft to hard in nature. It is anticipated that the majority of the reef formation may be 
excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as a large backhoe excavator. 
However, reef formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones. 
Therefore, we anticipate that some difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized 
areas during construction. The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, 
which would disrupt the structure of the reef formation, resulting in a potential loss of 
bearing strength for improvements in the vicinity. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
experience of the existing coralline reef formation in the area. We recommend 
encouraging the contractor to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to 
make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
In addition, grading operations as well as some infrastructure excavation may 
involve cuts into the near-surface basalt rock formation, especially at the northwestern 
portion of the site.  It is anticipated that saprolitic and highly weathered basalt, such as 
clinker, may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as ripping 
with large bulldozers, where the material is near the existing ground surface.  However,

3.6 
Geological Hazards 
Our review of the available information indicates that there are only limited 
potential for geological hazards associated with development of the project site.  There 
has been no volcanic activity on the Island of Oahu for several hundreds of thousands 
of years and there are no known active faults in the project vicinity. According to the 
Uniform Building Code, the Island of Oahu is designated as Seismic Zone 1, the lowest 
in the State of Hawaii. 
No potential rockfall or slide areas have been identified within the site up to the 
time of this exploration. 
A primary geological hazard that may be associated with the project site is the 
potential presence of natural cavities or voids within the underlying reef formations and 
volcanic rock formations.  This hazard may readily be addressed during development of 
the site through cavity detection programs, such as heavy proofrolling or probing. 
In addition, due to the generally low gradient existing terrain, the presence of low 
permeability surface clay soils, and the presence of multiple existing drainage features 
passing through the site, drainage of stormwater and transmission of offsite flow 
through the project site may be considered a potential natural hazard.  Appropriate 
drainage structures should be implemented in the project design. 
3.7 
Cavities and Voids 
Coralline reef formations generally are highly variable in terms of hardness, 
weathering, and fracturing. In addition, cavities and/or voids are encountered commonly 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 6654-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 19 
Hawaii  California 
in coral formations within the project vicinity. In many cases, cavities and voids in the 
coral formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits and/or clayey alluvial soils. In 
areas where coral formation is anticipated near the finish ground surface, special 
attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for 
distress to the future improvements, such as sidewalk slabs and pavements. 
In areas where the excavated subgrade exposes coral formation, the excavated 
subgrade should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller to detect 
the potential presence of near-surface cavities, sinkholes, or loose zones. The vibratory 
drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. A Geolabs 
representative should monitor the proof-rolling operations. If the proof-rolling operations 
detect a near-surface cavity or loose material zones, the area should be ripped open, 
cleaned of the debris, and then backfilled with compacted fill materials or lean concrete. 
Cavities and voids are also commonly encountered in lava flow formations.  
Finished subgrades (in cut areas) and areas designated to receive fills should be 
proof-rolled with a vibratory drum roller (10-ton minimum static weight) a minimum of 
eight passes to help in detecting and collapsing near-surface cavities and/or voids.  The 
proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during clearing and 
proof-rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted fill materials 
(6-inch minus material).  Due to the potential for presence of cavities and/or voids, 
contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation and backfill quantities.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling three borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths ranging from about 31.5 to 32.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill equipment 
equipped with continuous flight augers and HQ wireline coring tools. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed by visual 
observation and testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance 
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance 
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

The rippability (or excavation characteristics) of a rock mass is a function of the 
relative hardness of the rock, its relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics. 
Dense basalt formations with high RQD values would be very difficult to rip and would

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings and 
the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths. 
Eight Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples of the upper clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits for soil 
classification.  The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on 
Plate C-1. 
Ten Sieve Analyses tests (ASTM C 117 & 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates 
C-2 and C-3. 
Nine laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils.  
The test results are presented on Plates C-4 through C-12. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557/698) were performed on 
bulk samples of the soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships. 
The test results are presented on Plates C-13 through C-16. 
Four sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G 51) and Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G 57), were performed by our office on selected soil samples obtained from our 
field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate C-17.
