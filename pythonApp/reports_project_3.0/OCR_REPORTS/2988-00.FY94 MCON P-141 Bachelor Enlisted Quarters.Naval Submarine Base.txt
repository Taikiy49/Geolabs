INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Bachelor Enlisted Quarters BEQ building to be located at
the Naval Submarine Base in Pearl Harbor on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for foundation design input and may
not be appropriate to form the basis for preparation of construction cost estimates nor
for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2988 00 Page 6
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central
Oahu
Pearl Harbor is located on the southwest flank of the Koolau shield and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch The stratigraphic history of
Pearl Harbor is quite complex due to its large areal extent the large number of streams
and drainageways entering it and a complex series of sea level changes The subsurface
conditions at the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef
deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in the Peari Harbor vicinity
is Koolau basalt
The Koolau voicanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 million
years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has subsided as
much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and
Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2988 00 Page 7
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the oid bays accumuiation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may
have aiso resulted in deposits of coral aigal limestone and maris within the predominantly
lagoonal sedimentary unit
The lower sea stands caused sireams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action extended the
alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa Kamanaiki Punchbowl
and other minor vents These post erosional events
following the cessation of shield building were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea
level fluctuations
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2988 00 Page 8
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder Some explosive
eruptions produced ash that cemented together rapidly to form a firm rock known as tuff
Some of the eruptions produced lava flows which filled the valleys and moved seaward
inundating coral reefs and alluvial deposits
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form A great many of those projects were
originally constructed for agricultural residential and or military development
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
a total of 10 borings to depths of about 5 to 61 feet below the existing ground surface
The 10 borings consisted of three shallow borings Boring and 7 and seven
relatively deep borings Boring through 6 and 8 through 10 The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our borings drilled for this project encountered a near surface fill layer
consisting of granular materials of approximately 1 to 2 feet in thickness Decomposed
tuff formation was encountered below the near surface granular fill layer The tuff
formation has decomposed into a stiff to hard soil formation The decomposed tuff
formation encountered consisted of silty sands sandy silts clayey silts and silty clays
with occasional rock fragments The different soil types encountered in our borings
appear to indicate the various degrees of weathering in the tuff formation Granular
materials consisting of coralline sands and coral gravels were encountered below the
GEOLABS HAWAII

basalt formation were encountered in the deeper borings below the granular materials

Mat Foundation
Based on the stiff to hard decomposed tuff formation encountered in our borings
we recommend that the proposed 17 story BEQ structure be supported on a rigid mat
foundation bearing on the decomposed tuff formation encountered in our borings The
near surface decomposed tuff formation grades from stiff to hard silts and clays to
medium dense to very dense sands Because the foundation soils consist of cohesive
soils silts and clays and cohesionless soils sands proper subgrade preparation of the
foundation soils will be required to provide more uniform bearing conditions for the mat
foundation Subgrade preparation for the mat foundation is discussed in the Site
Grading section of this report
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2988 00 Page 12
The elevation of the bottom of the mat foundation is a critical item As a minimum
we recommend that the mat foundation be embedded at least 3 feet below the existing
ground surface to bear on the stiff to dense decomposed tuff formation The existing
structures and pavements will be demolished prior to construction of the proposed BEQ
building Demolition of the existing structures may disturb the upper portion of the near
surface soils which may affect the performance of the mat foundation As a result we
recommend that the bottom of the mat founded at an elevation not higher than 9 feet
MLLW to bear on the relatively undisturbed decomposed tuff formation In addition we
also recommend that the bottom of the mat foundation be founded at an elevation not
lower than about 7 feet MLLW which is approximately 1 foot above the highest
groundwater level encountered in our borings This requirement is to reduce the potential
for encountering potential contamination of soil and groundwater since free phase product
was encountered floating on the groundwater table in the monitoring wells installed by
Clayton Environmental Consultants Therefore the bottom of the mat foundation should
be between elevations 7 and 9 feet MLLW
On the basis of our exploration and engineering analyses an allowable bearing
pressure of 4 000 p s f may be used for design of the mat foundation bearing on the stiff
decomposed tuff formation The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
The allowable bearing pressure provided above is a net value therefore the weight of the
mat foundation may be discounted On the basis of the pressuremeter tests and our
analysis a modulus of subgrade reaction of 150 p c i may be used in the design of the
mat foundation
Lateral loads imposed on the building foundation may be resisted by a combination
of passive earth pressure exerted against the near vertical foundation faces and soil
friction between the base of the mat and the supporting subgrade Passive earth
GEOLABS HAWAI

pressure equal to an equivalent fluid weighing 350 pounds per cubic foot p c f may be
used to evaluate lateral load resistance This assumes that the backfill on the sides of the
mat foundation is well compacted Unless covered by slabs or pavements passive soil
resistance in the upper 12 inches should be neglected A frictional coefficient of 0 4 may
be used to evaluate sliding resistance between the bottom of the mat and supporting
subgrade
Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the dead weight of the structure and

Foundation Settlements
Five pressuremeter tests were performed at selected depths in Boring Nos 9 and
10 to provide the stress strain relationship of the in situ soils at the site for mat foundation
design Results of the pressuremeter tests performed at the site are presented on Plates
C 1 through C 5 of Appendix C Based on our exploration data and the results of the

Slab on Grade
The ground floor slab on grade will be supported on compacted fill needed to
backfill the space between the top of the mat foundation and the ground floor slab We
envision that the compacted fill material will consist of imported granular material or on
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2988 00 Page 14
site granular materials both of which are considered non expansive The on site clayey
soils which are potentially expansive should not be used as the backfill material Slab
subgrades should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation
D1557 91
To provide more uniform support for the slab and to serve as a capillary break we
recommend that a 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation be provided below the slabs For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive
Surfacing we recommend that a moisture membrane be piaced over the cushion layer
to reduce potential migration of moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to
promote more uniform curing of the slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor
membrane we recommend that a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand be placed above the
membrane prior to placing the slab concrete The sand should be slightly moistened
prior to placing concrete
The existing near surface soils encountered in our borings consist of granular
materials which are ccnsidered non expansive Therefore exterior flatwork such as

encountered near the surface For more uniform support exterior flatwork also should

Retaining Wall Perimeter Wall
Retaining walls perimeter walls may be needed at the site An allowable bearing
pressure of 3 000 p s f may be used for design of wall foundations bearing on the native
soils or compacted fill Wall footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent grade The allowable bearing pressure above is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as wind or seismic
forces For footings located near the top of slopes or on slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance from the outside edge at the
base of the footing to the edge of the slope is not less than 8 feet
To resist lateral loads imposed on wall foundations a coefficient of friction of 0 4
may be used between tne footing and compacted fill or on site soils in addition
resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 350 p c f
Assuming that granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation or select borrow are used for backfill behind the walls an equivalent fluid
pressure of 35 p c f may be used to design cantilever walls with a level backfill condition
A level backfill condition is assumed to have a slope inclination of 6 horizontal to 1 vertical
or flatter For sloping backfill condition with a slope inclination up to 2 horizontal to 1
vertical a lateral earth pressure due to an equivalent fluid weighing 50 p c f may be used
to design the cantilever walls
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot to 2 foot wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations down at the base
of the wall discharging to a discharge facility A filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2988 00 Page 16
equivalent should be placed between the drainage material and the compacted backfill
material to reduce the potential for migration of fines As an alternative a prefabricated

connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall The upper 2 feet of wall backfill

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation rubbish old pavements
construction rubble and other deleterious and organic materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site For the purposes of this discussion
subgrade level should be considered the bottom of the base course for pavement
sections bottom of the cushion for slabs and bottom of foundations
After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or are designated to receive fill should
be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percent by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density All compacted subgrades
should be in a non yielding condition
Loose soft areas encountered at the foundation pavement and or slab subgrade
levels should be over excavated to expose firm soils and replaced with on site or
imported non expansive granular materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction and a non yielding condition
Any expansive clayey soil encountered at or near the pavement and slab subgrade
levels should be over excavated and the resulting over excavation should be
backfilled with compacted non expansive granular material The over excavated
expansive clays should be disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential for
contamination of other areas

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 10
borings to depths ranging between 5 and 61 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig advancing continuous flignt augers hollow stem augers or core
drilling tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentation of the
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The biow counts needed to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD
50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based
on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory tests were performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration as an aid to classifying the materials and to evaluate pertinent engineering
properties of the soil and or rock materials sampled
Moisture content ASTM Test Method D 2216 92 and unit weight determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation Tne moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the sample tested and dry unit weight are shown at the appropriate sample depths on
the Logs of Borings
Three sieve analyses were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
gradation characteristics of the soils The test results are plotted on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 were performed to
evaluate the plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Liquid Limit and Plasticity
Index of the material are used to classify the soil in accordance with the Unified Soil
Classification System Results of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on
Plate B 2
Three unconfined compression tests ASTM Test Method D 2166 91 were
performed on selected cohesive soil samples Results of these tests are presented at the
appropriate depths on the Logs of Borings
Three direct shear tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the shear
strength parameters Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plates B 3
through B 5

APPENDIX C
Pressuremeter Testing
The pressuremeter test basically consists of placing an inflatable cylindrical probe
in a predrilled hole and expanding the probe while measuring the changes in volume and
pressure in the probe The purpose of pressuremeter tests is to characterize the stress
strain relationship of the in situ soil
The tests were performed in general conformance with Procedure A of the ASTM
Test Designation D 4719 87 An NX size single cell pressuremeter manufactured by
Roctest was used in the tests The pressuremeter probe has a length to diameter ratio
of above 6 and the volume of the uninflated probe V is 75 in The borehole was
prepared by driving a 3 inch O D split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer free
falling 24 inches Approximately equal pressure steps were used to inflate the
pressuremeter probe in such a way that approximately 12 to 15 increments were obtained
for the test For every pressure step the probe volume readings were taken at 30 and
60 seconds after the pressure increment was applied The volume difference between
the 30 second and 60 second readings are referred to as the creep volume V
The test result was corrected for pressure loss due to the stiffness of the probe
membrane and volume loss due to the compressibility of the testing system Figure 1
depicts typical pressuremeter test curves The test curve is based on the 60 second
volume readings The creep curve is a plot of V versus the pressure applied to the
borehole wall P The P reading is believed to be associated with the total in situ lateral
stress The creep pressure P is related to the in situ yield stress Above P plastic
deformations become prevalent The test curve typically shows an almost straight line
section within the range of low volume increase In this range a constant soil
deformation modulus called pressuremeter modulus E can be evaluated as follows
poisson ratio A typical value of 0 33 is recommended by the ASTM Test
Designation D 4719 87
