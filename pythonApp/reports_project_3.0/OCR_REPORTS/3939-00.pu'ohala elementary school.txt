REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3939 00 Page 5
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general stream flows in Hawaii
are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water for
very short duration Because of this transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of
the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles
gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads
are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In
addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older partially
consolidated deposits
Based on our literature research it appears that the existing Pu ohala Elementary

appears to have been raised to the existing grades by filling with boulders and the
excavated saprolite soils generally encountered in the vicinity

Soil Nail and Tied Back Wall Systems
Soil nailing and tied back wall systems are two methods of in situ soil
reinforcement and retaining wall construction A major difference from the mechanically
stabilized earth systems comes from the construction procedures In the soil nail and
tied back wall systems the existing natural soil is reinforced as opposed to a backfill
reinforcement for the mechanically stabilized earth systems Soil nail and tied back wall
systems were also considered as remedial measures to stabilize the existing hillside at
the subject site However due to the need for permanent easement acquisitions behind
the face of the wall and potential construction difficulties at this site these options were
not further pursued for the subject project
Concrete Retaining Wall
Retaining walls are structures used to provide stability for earth or other material
where conditions disallow the mass to assume its natural slope Retaining walls are
classified based on the method of achieving stability into six principal types including
gravity cantilever counterfort buttressed crib and semi gravity walls It is our opinion
that a cantilever concrete retaining wall may be used as a remedial measure to stabilize
the existing hillside A cantilever retaining wall is a reinforced concrete wall that utilizes
cantilever action to retain the mass behind the wall from assuming a natural slope
Stability of this wall is partially achieved from the weight of soil on the heel portion of the
base slab to serve as a buttress The following general guidelines may be used for
preliminary design of the retaining wall structure at the subject site

Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed with an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf bearing on
2 feet of select borrow subbase material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The select borrow subbase material should extend a
minimum of 2 feet beyond the edges of the footing as shown on Plate 3 This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The in situ soils below the 2 foot select borrow subbase material should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the
select borrow subbase layer the soft loose materials should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled
with imported select granular fill material or select borrow compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structures may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the
foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for footings
bearing on the compacted select borrow subbase material Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil
around footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3939 00 Page 13
Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
2 feet below the lowest adjacent finished grade If foundations are located next to

the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall Unless
covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
non expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the walls
Keystone Reinforced Soil Retaining Wall
Based on the updated information provided we understand that a Keystone
Reinforced Soil Retaining Wall KRSRW system is also desired as an alternate solution
to the conventional concrete retaining wall option for the subject hillside stabilization
project The system combines the Keystone modular concrete wall units with geogrid
reinforcement and compacted soil to result in a reinforced soil mass that supports the
earth pressure and surcharge loads This composite essentially forms a gravity wall
structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and differential settlements In addition
KRSRW are also desirable due to the flexibility of the wall ease of construction high load
carrying capacity and economy
Design of the KRSRW will need to take into consideration both external and
internal stabilities of the structure In evaluating external stability the KRSRW structure
must satisfy four stability conditions 1 bearing failure 2 translational sliding 3
overturning stability and 4 overall slope stability Geotechnical design parameters to
evaluate these stability conditions are presented in the following sections Some of the
geotechnical parameters necessary in evaluating the internal stability of the KRSRW are

Overall Slope Stability
We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the KRSRW structure with the
improved foundation soils as discussed in the Concrete Retaining Wall section of
this report Slope stability analyses of two dimensional slope cross sections using
Bishop s simplified method of limit equilibrium analysis were performed on the
KRSRW structure at four selected locations along the wall alignment Based on
our analyses the factor of safety for short term and long term stabilities of the
KRSRW is at least 1 5 which is the minimum factor of safety normally
recommended
Based on our slope stability and external stability analyses a minimum base width
to wall height ratio of at least 1 0 is recommended for the KRSRW with a 2H 1V
sloping backfill condition We also recommend that the base width be at least
8 feet to develop sufficient length for overall stability

We recommend that the reinforced fill material for the KRSRW consist of imported
non expansive select granular fill material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder
sand similar to the granular material recommended for backfill of the concrete
retaining wall The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography and the current design concept we anticipate
that the majority of the site grading work for the proposed hillside stabilization project will
consist of clearing and grubbing as well as placement and compaction of backfill
materials behind the proposed concrete retaining wall structure or Keystone wall
structure Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation and scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are
similar to those encountered in our field exploration
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Vegetation on the existing
near vertical slope face should be removed with care to reduce the potential for inducing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3939 00 Page 19
additional slope failures We recommend that removal of the vegetation be conducted in
increments in conjunction with the keying and backfilling operations behind the retaining
wall
It should be emphasized that the sloughed material from the landslide should be
removed to expose the stiff underlying soils In addition soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill
should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with well compacted select granular fill material The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Soft and wet soils may be encountered near the ground surface along the
proposed retaining wall alignment We recommend that these soft and wet soils be
removed where encountered to expose the underlying firm soils prior to the placement
of the 2 feet of select borrow subbase and imported select granular fill materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with imported select granular fill material compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Contract documents should include
additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing the area within the retaining wall foundations should
be over excavated to provide a minimum of 2 feet of select borrow subbase The
over excavated subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the subgrade soil be
re scarified and prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may
require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted import
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3939 00 Page 20
select granular fill material at no additional cost to the State The need for
over excavation should be evaluated by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii in the field
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately
40 square feet of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a
woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of subsequent lifts of fill
As previously recommended backfill materials behind the retaining wall should
consist of non expansive select granular fill material such as crushed coral basalt or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
greater than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain less than 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of
25 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Select borrow subbase materials below the retaining wall foundations should meet
the material requirements for Subbase Course as specified in the Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Imported fill
material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
In general the non expansive select granular fill should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in 8 inch level lifts and compacted to between
90 and 95 percent relative compaction Select borrow subbase and base course
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3939 00 Page 21
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established
in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not
be allowed to compact the fill materials
The sloping backfill behind the retaining wall structure should be constructed at an
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter All fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
fill placement recommendations presented above Excessive water should be diverted
away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the fill
slopes are completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Excavation
We anticipate some excavations at the slope face and toe area will be required
to remove the surface vegetation and sloughed soils from the landslide area Based on
our field exploration the existing hillside is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff clayey
silt soils extending to a depth of about 60 feet below the existing ground surface
Therefore we believe that conventional excavation equipment such as a bulldozer or
other heavy equipment may be used for excavation purposes In general the method
and equipment used for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to
practical limits and safety considerations Excavated materials and heavy construction
equipment should not be allowed at the top of the existing hillside to reduce the
potential for ground movement
In general we believe that a temporary excavation with a slope inclination of
or flatter would be reasonable for short duration Special precautions should be
taken by the Contractor to divert water away from the excavations to reduce the potential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3939 00 Page 22
for saturation of the subsoils which may affect the stability of the excavations Depending
on the contractor s sequence of work and schedule temporary excavation support and
shoring system may be prudent and should be the responsibility of the Contractor
Temporary excavation and shoring support must comply with all applicable safety
requirements
Pavement Design
We understand that flexible pavement will be required for the driveway area in
front of the proposed retaining wall structure to serve the campus In general we
anticipate the vehicle loading for the new pavement will consist primarily of passenger
vehicles light pick up trucks and occasional heavy trucks Therefore we have assumed
medium traffic loading conditions for pavement design purposes Based on the subsoil
conditions encountered we recommend that the following flexible pavement section be
used for preliminary design purposes
2 5 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
20 5 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
92 percent relative compaction The compaction requirements for the pavement
subgrades have been reduced due to the moist to wet conditions of the in situ soils and
high rainfall in this area The base course and select borrow subbase should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests
should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to
confirm the adequacy of the above section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3939 00 Page 23
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a
minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the base and subbase course layers to
reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain
system could be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation
For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed
adjacent to paved landscaped areas
Drainage
Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area special attention should be
given to site drainage during and after construction The finished grades in front of the
retaining wall structure should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to
reduce the potential for ponding A subdrain system should be incorporated into the
design for the hillside stabilization project to reduce the potential for hydrostatic pressure
build up behind the retaining wall as shown on Plate 3 In addition surface drainage
should also be carefully controlled and discharged to avoid ponding and erosion
We recommend that an interceptor ditch be provided at the top of the slope to reduce the
potential for water flowing over the slope face
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the wall footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings to depths of about 49 and 60 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50
