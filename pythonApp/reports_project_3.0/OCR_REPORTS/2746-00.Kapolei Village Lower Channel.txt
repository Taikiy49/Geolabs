INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our field exploration and percolation testing
performed to obtain information for the preliminary design of the siting layout and depths
of proposed injection wells along the Kapolei Village Lower Channel in the District of
Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general project area is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and percolation testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses and
interpretation of the data obtained The recommendations presented herein are intended
for preliminary design input and are not intended to form the basis for preparation of final
construction cost estimates nor are they intended to be used for bidding purposes
This report supplements and elaborates upon our letter report entitled Preliminary
Findings Injection Well Design Kapolei Village Lower Channel
dated which presented our preliminary findings and recommendations
for the project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Kapolei Village Lower Channel will form a part of the drainage system for a
portion of the development of Villages of Kapolei Based on the current design concepts
storm water run off will flow from Kapolei Village 3 Makakilo Gulch and Makalapa Gulch

After passing through the golf course the run off will discharge into the drainage

the channel will be disposed of by infiltration through the sides and bottom of the channel
Excavation for the channel was in progress at the time of our field exploration and

SITE DESCRIPTION
The Villages of Kapolei are located in Ewa District on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The development which is currently under construction consists of four 4 villages a
golf course and roadways and utilities including the Kapolei Village drainage channel
The proposed drainage channel has a L shaped alignment The shorter leg of
the L or the upper leg of the channel begins near the south side of Waimanalo Road
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2746 00 Page 6
and extends southward to the longer lower leg of the channel which parallels the north
side of Franklin D Roosevelt Road The drainage channel terminates adjacent to the
Barbers Point Naval Air Station Coral Pit a former borrow site for coral materials on the
southern side of the westerly portion of the lower leg of the Lower Channel The general
location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Most of the proposed channel alignment was previously used for cultivation of
sugar cane except for the easterly half of the lower leg of the Lower Channel which was
the now abandoned Ewa Air Field runway
In general the topography is a relatively uniform and gently sloping terrain with
drainageways and irrigation ditches crossing the site The overall slope and drainage is
in a southerly direction
Original elevations along the approximate 1 23 mile reach of the proposed channel
alignment vary from about 53 to 58 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL along the upper
leg of the Lower Channel and from about 45 to 54 feet above MSL along the lower leg
of the channel The abandoned Coral Pit was excavated to a bottom elevation of about
six 6 feet above MSL
As noted above excavation for the proposed drainage channel was in progress
at the time of our field exploration and percolation testing A substantial portion of the
upper leg of the channel and the easterly half of the lower leg of the channel had been
excavated to about 10 feet below the original ground surface Therefore the term
ground surface throughout this report refers to the surface elevation existing at the time
of our field work Due to the excavation work in progress at that time it must be
recognized that topography and elevations may have been considerably altered by the
time installation of the proposed injection wells is initiated

General Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the Ewa Coastal Plain which abuts the southern flank
of Waianae and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pear
Harbor and the Ewa Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the majority of the
coastal plain are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during
high stands of the sea which intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Waianae Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2746 00 Page 8
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to
present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted in
deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary
unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the reef
materials and lagoonal sediments

Subsurface Conditions
Since thick surface clay and silt soils were encountered along the upper leg of the
channel during our previous geotechnical exploration no further exploration was
attempted along the upper channel alignment for our current efforts Instead eight 8
borings test wells were drilled to depths of approximately 51 5 to 66 5 feet to further
explore the subsurface conditions along the lower leg of the channel alignment and for
performance of field injection testing
The approximate locations of the test wells are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2
through 5 These plates also show the approximate locations of the temporary source
wells drilled to provide water for the injection tests
In general weathered coral reef formation was encountered in all of the borings
In the upper portions of Boring and 8 the upper soil materials consisted of
coral sand and gravel locally mixed with reddish brown sandy silt which are believed to
be alluvium deposited in old stream channels These materials generally have low
permeability characteristics In Boring No 8 a 12 foot thick layer of practically
impermeable reddish brown clayey silt was also encountered at the surface
In the coral materials above the existing groundwater levels at about 37 to 48 feet
below the ground surface the calcium carbonate appears to have been reworked and
recrystallized by solution processes forming cemented sandstone or limestone These
recemented coral formations generally have relatively low permeability with most water
being transmitted through cracks and solution channels
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2746 00 Page 10
Near and below the groundwater table it appears that the coral materials generally
grade to more porous coral algal reef formations and related detrital deposits which are
generally fairly permeable Locally particularly at Boring Nos 5 and 8 the underlying
coral formations appear to have high to extremely rapid permeability
Field Percolation Testing
Four inch diameter PVC casings and well screens were installed in the drilled
borings to depths of approximately 50 to 60 feet below the ground surface to enable
performance of the field percolation testing The bottom 10 or 20 feet of the PVC
consisted of well screen with slotted openings in order to allow water to flow into the
surrounding subsoils Water Source wells were drilled between each pair of test wells
which were approximately 400 feet apart as shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 through 5
Constant head injection tests were performed by continuously pumping water from
the source wells into the adjacent test wells for up to six 6 hours Various flow rates
were established and maintained for sufficient periods of time to measure rates of
infiltration with respect to various head levels in the test wells The constant head test
data and the corresponding calculated permeabilities of subsoils are shown on Plates B 1
through B 9
In two 2 selected test wells Boring Nos 2 and 4 falling head tests were also
performed by measuring the rate of water level drop after the constant head testing was
completed and pumping was stopped The falling head percolation test data and the
corresponding calculated permeabilities of subsoils are shown on Plate B 10
For the two 2 test well sets at Boring Nos 1 and 2 and at Boring Nos 3 and 4
two 2 additional observation wells were also drilled near the corresponding source wells
Nos S 1 and S 2 to observe the groundwater table drawdown during pumping The
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2746 00 Page 11
observation wells were located approximately five 5 and ten 10 feet from the source
wells However analyses of the water level drawdown data indicated high permeability
values which did not correspond with data obtained from the injection testing Therefore
these data are not included in this report nor were they considered in formulation of our
design recommendations
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general the injection test results indicate that Boring Nos 5 and 8 represent the
two areas with the highest rates of permeability along the Lower Channel The calculated
values of the subsoil permeabilities in the other borings are generally within a significantly
lower range
It appears technically and economically feasible to install injection wells in these
two 2 more permeable areas to facilitate disposal of storm run off However it does not
appear prudent to assume that the extremely high permeability of the subsoils at Boring
No 5 is representative of an extensive area On the other hand it appears overly
conservative to neglect the relatively high permeability of the subsoils at Boring No 8
Therefore we believe that it is prudent to install a large number of injection wells
in the area of Boring No 8 with additional injection wells installed in the vicinity of Boring
No 5 A relatively high factor of safety should also be used in design to compensate for
potential low efficiency of the well system which may be caused by the high variability of
permeability as normally found in coralline formations

Injection Well Design
Based on our theoretical analysis of the data obtained from the field injection tests
it appears that in the vicinity of Boring No 5 an infiltration rate of up to 9 800 gallons of
water per minute may be anticipated for an injection well 2 5 feet in diameter and 60 feet
deep A lower volume of approximately 1 050 gallons per minute may be anticipated for
a similar injection well installed in the vicinity of Boring No 8
Theoretically the required design rate of disposal could be accommodated by
installing five 6 such injection wells in the vicinity of Boring No 5 where an extremely
high permeability value was recorded with a factor of safety of two 2 However we
recommend that additional injection wells be installed in the vicinity of Boring No 8 to
increase the factor of safety for a more prudent design of the injection well system
Based on the design disposal quantity of 24 445 gallons per minute we
recommended that a minimum of six 6 injection wells be installed in the vicinity of Boring
No 5 and that a minimum of twelve 12 supplementary injection wells be installed in the
vicinity of Boring No 8
These eighteen 18 injection wells each 2 5 feet in diameter and 60 feet deep
would provide a total well capacity of up to 71 400 gallons per minute or a factor of safety
on the order of 3 We recommend that these injection wells be spaced a minimum of 50
feet apart to reduce the potential overlap of zones of influence that may reduce the
overall system efficiency due to the interaction of the flow regimes from individual wells
Design Limitations
Due to the inherent heterogeneity of coral formations it should be understood that
the ranges of calculated in situ permeabilities represent the percolation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2746 00 Page 14
characteristics of the underlying coral subsoils only for the specific test locations
depths and conditions that prevailed at the time of our field percolation testing
The calculated percolation capacities of the proposed injection wells presented in
this report are based on numerical analyses and extrapolation of the test data
obtained from field percolation testing in small diameter boreholes for relatively
short durations of time Therefore the calculated percolation capacities may vary
significantly from the actual injection capacities of the proposed larger diameter
production injection wells
In addition it should be noted that water entering injection wells typically contains
silt or other small size particles suspended solids micro organisms and other
material which may reduce the efficiency of the wells with time In Hawaii there
have been numerous reports of unsatisfactory performance of injection wells
These have been attributed to various causes e g lack of maintenance
accumulation of silt and well cave in
In view of the past history of failures we recommend that the injection wells be
over designed to allow for reduction in capacity or failure by using a large factor
of safety deemed appropriate
It is also understood that full scale percolation testing must be performed on
prototype injection wells as part of the process of obtaining the UIC permit If it
is found during this testing that the actual capacities of the full scale injection
wells vary significantly from those indicated in this report appropriate modifications
to the design must be made It is recommended that a sufficient contingency fund
be retained to accommodate the possible additional costs for the requisite design
and construction modifications

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed Lower Channel alignment were
explored by the drilling and sampling of eight 8 borings to depths ranging between 51 5
and 66 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plans Plates 2 through 5 The borings were drilled by two 2 truck mounted
rotary drill rigs advancing continuous flight augers or rotary and core drilling tools as
appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by field engineers and geologists from our firm The
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a standard split spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches
The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches or a shorter penetration are
shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample depths
A core sample of rock material encountered at the site was obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of the rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses The designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks

APPENDIX B
Field Percolation Testing
for use as the test wells Eight and one half 8 5 inch diameter borings were drilled to
install the source wells The test wells were drilled to approximately 50 to 60 feet below
the then existing ground surface in the Lower Channel
After drilling four 4 inch diameter PVC casing and well screens were installed in
the test wells and six 6 inch diameter PVC casing and screens were installed into the
source wells The bottom 10 or 20 feet of the PVC casings consisted of slotted screens
in order to allow infiltration of water into the surrounding subsoils
Following casing installation the wells were developed by surging and airlifting
utilizing an air compressor The supply of compressed air was turned on and off to
create a surge effect for cleaning out sediments from within the wells during airlifting The
surging and airlifting process was typically continued for approximately one and one half
1 5 to two 2 hours per well until minimal amounts of sediments were developed with
each surge At this time the well was considered clean and ready for percolation testing
After development constant head percolation tests were performed by
continuously pumping water with a 6 inch centrifugal pump from the source well into the
adjacent test wells for up to six 6 hours Various constant heads were created in the
test wells by adjusting and maintaining the various flow rates of water in the test wells for
a sufficient period of time as appropriate to measure the rates of infiltration The flow
rates were measured by the use of an in line paddle wheel flow sensor The constant
head percolation test data and the corresponding calculated permeabilities of the subsoils
are shown on Plates B 1 through B 9
In two 2 selected test wells Boring Nos 2 and 4 falling head tests were also
performed by measuring the rate of water level drop after the constant head tests were
completed and pumping was stopped The falling head percolation test data and the
corresponding calculated permeabilities of subsoils are shown on Plate B 10
