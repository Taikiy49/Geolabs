INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new mechanical building at Webster and Spalding Halls within
the Manoa Campus of the University of Hawaii on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1

W O 3109 00 Page 2
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our design level geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for
design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the Manoa Campus of the University of Hawaii on
the island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to renovate the existing Webster and Spalding
Halls by the construction of a new mechanical building at the northeast corner of Spalding
Hall
The proposed addition will be a one story structure approximately 35 feet by 80
feet in plan dimension The new building will consist of a mechanical room and a cooling
towers room covering approximately 1 093 and 1 227 square feet respectively We
understand that masonry walls with concrete floor construction will be utilized for the
proposed building Based on the information provided to us maximum column loads are
estimated to be on the order of 10 kips per column with wall loads of approximately four
4 kips per lineal foot Loadings from the cooling towers and chillers will be on the order
of about 12 to 18 kips each Construction of new utility lines are also anticipated for the
project construction
Based on the proposed finish floor elevations of 86 5 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
minimal site grading work is anticipated for the proposed construction Our previous
experience in the area suggest that the project site may be underlain by a surface fill crust
over highly decomposed basalt material Special attention will be given to the design of

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the Manoa Campus of the University of Hawaii on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The new mechanical building will be situated approximately
at the northeast corner of Spalding Hall as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field exploration and the Partial Site Plan provided to us the new
mechanical building will be situated partially on the existing grass lawn area and partially
on the asphalt concrete parking lot Our site reconnaissance indicated that an existing
electrical substation is located on the southwest side of the project site with numerous
underground utility lines and structures traversing the project area
The topography at the project site is relatively level with the existing ground
elevations in the vicinity of the future mechanical building ranging from approximately 85
to 87 feet MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two 2 borings to
depths of 28 5 and 30 feet below the existing ground surface
In general our exploration encountered a deposit of dense sandy gravel material
clinker to depths of 9 to 14 feet below the existing ground surface This was underlain
by moderately weathered basalt formation extending to the maximum depths explored
The ground surface at Boring No 1 is currently covered with approximately three inches
3 of asphalt concrete paving In Boring No 2 a surficial fill layer of sandy silt material
was encountered to a depth of approximately one foot 17 below the existing ground
surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration the project site appears to be underlain by dense
clinker material overlying weathered basalt rock formation with increasing depths It is our
opinion that the proposed renovation construction is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view provided that the recommendations presented in this report are
followed
We believe that the dense surficial clinker material encountered would provide
adequate foundation support for the new one story structure It appears that shallow
footings and slab on grade foundations may be used to support the new mechanical
equipment building Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to
the foundation design of the proposed addition are further discussed in the following
sections of this report
Shallow Foundations
We understand from conversations with the project structural engineer that loading
from the cooling towers and chillers will be on the order of about 12 to 18 kips each Wall
loads of approximately 4 kips per lineal foot are also anticipated for the proposed building
construction Because of the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3109 00 Page 7
site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings and slab on grade
foundations may be used for support of the proposed new mechanical building
An allowable bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be
used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted sandy gravel material
clinker encountered near the existing ground surface This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 2 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of eighteen inches 18 below the
lowest adjacent finish grade However footings adjacent to the existing structures should
extend at least to the same depth as the existing building footings or deeper to avoid
surcharging the existing foundations The actual depths of the existing footings should
be confirmed in the field during construction by the project geotechnical engineer
We estimate the footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the dense in situ soils to be less than 1 inch total and inch differential
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3109 00 Page 8
footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the dense in situ soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footing excavations be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over
excavated to expose firm materials The over excavation should either be backfilled with
granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the
footing bottom be extended down to the compacted firm in situ soil
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm foundation bearing
conditions and the embedment depths
Slab On Grade
We anticipate concrete slab on grade will be used for the proposed new
mechanical building In accordance with the requirements of D A G S Technical
Memorandum No 61 dated a basaltic termite barrier BTB will need to be
incorporated into the design of new facilities However we understand that the slabs for
the proposed project may be subjected to heavy loadings and vibration impacts from the
operation of the equipment therefore a six inch 6 layer of base course aggregate is
recommended below the concrete slab to serve as a slab cushion in lieu of the basaltic
termite barrier normally required
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3109 00 Page 9
The subgrade should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations and
guidelines discussed in the Site Preparation section of this report We recommend that
the concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should
be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate some amount of differential
movements
The base course supporting the concrete slab should consist of crushed basalt
aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For design of
structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i
may be used for the compacted base course The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to
any unpaved area should be embedded at least twelve inches 12 below the lowest
adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum four inch 4 thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel be
provided below the concrete slabs Construction joints should be provided at intervals
equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Site Preparation
Minimal site grading work consisting of minor cuts and fills to provide a level
building pad is anticipated for the project construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Slabs and asphalt concrete pavings that are to be demolished should be
completely removed However the base course below the asphalt concrete pavings may
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3109 00 Page 10
be left in place The thin surficial fill layer of sandy silt material in the grass lawn area to
be occupied by the proposed construction should be completely removed and disposed
of off site
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with horizontal lifts of compacted select granular fill material
Utilities that are to be abandoned in place should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete
under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs areas
should be scarified to a depth of eight inches 8 and compacted with a vibratory smooth
drum roller with a minimum weight of five 5 tons for a minimum number of six 6
passes If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are encountered at the over excavated
subgrade level deeper over excavation may be required to expose firm material The
resulting excavation should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material
Imported fill material to replace the over excavated material and raise the ground
surface to the proposed finish subgrade elevation should consist of non expansive
granular material placed in level lifts not exceeding ten inches 10 in loose thickness and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
The select granular fill material should consist of imported granular material such
as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles greater than three inches 8 in largest dimension The
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher
and a swell potential of not more than one percent 1 when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain less than 20 percent passing the No
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3109 00 Page 11
200 sieve Any imported fill material should be tested and approved for its intended use
by our firm prior to delivery to the project site
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of six inches 6 of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
is recommended under the pipes Self compacting granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up
to about one foot 1 above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and
to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging
the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot 1 above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material
The backfill should be placed in maximum ten inch 10 level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper two feet
2 of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to 95
percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3109 00 Page 12
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations
Design Review
Preliminary and final drawings and specifications for the proposed construction
should be forwarded to Geolabs Hawaii for review and written comments prior to
advertisement for bidding This review is necessary to evaluate conformance with the
intent of the earthwork and foundation recommendations provided herein If this review
is not made Geolabs Hawaii cannot be responsible for misinterpretation of our
recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that Geolabs Hawaii be retained to provide geotechnical
engineering services during the site preparation and construction of the foundations This
is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications or recommendations
and to expedite suggestions for design changes that may be required in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared
The recommendations provided in this report are contingent upon such observations
Any imported material required should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with the project specifications prior to hauling to the project site

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between
the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The location of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate having
been estimated by taping from features shown on the Spalding First Floor Plan New
Work dated prepared by Media Five Limited Elevations of the
borings were estimated by interpolation from the elevation contours on the Topographic
Survey Plan undated provided by Media Five Limited The physical locations and
elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the
methods used
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition
Water level data from the borings were measured at the times given on the graphic
representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed and
interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
2 borings to depths of 28 5 and 30 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
