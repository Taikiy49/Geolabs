Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the extinct
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
The project site is located across lowland coastal alluvial plains East Loch in
the lower distal southern flanks of the Koolau Mountain Range Ewa Plain The Ewa
Plain is a gently sloping land surface formed by the long term accumulation of alluvial
sediments consisting mainly of terrigenous clays and silts with some embedded cobbles
and boulders derived from the weathering and decomposition of basaltic rock residing
further upslope Some pockets of marine sediments and coralline detritus materials
were intercalated within the alluvial sediments as a result of past sea level fluctuations
The Ewa Plain is dissected by various drainage gullies which emanate in the
upland areas and discharge into the Middle and East Lochs of Pearl Harbor With the
passage of time some of the incised drainage channels meandered in course and were
filled in by more recent soft alluvial sediments The deeper basaltic rock of the
Waianae and Koolau Mountain Ranges is the primary aquifer and potable water source
on the Island of Oahu The subsurface geological conditions reflect past glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations which occurred most substantially during the Pleistocene Epoch
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The landmass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic

Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the International Building Code IBC 2006 current code adopted by
the City and County of Honolulu the project site may be subjected to seismic activity
and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed for the project The
following sections provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of Oahu and the
soil profile for seismic design
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that only sensitive instruments could detect
them However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few
cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of high seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have

Pile Foundations
Based on the highly compressible very soft estuarine deposit encountered in the
borings at shallow depths we believe that appreciable settlements would occur if a
shallow foundation system is used to support the new replacement bridge for the
project Therefore we recommend using driven pile foundation to support the new
pedestrian bridge structure planned for the project
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we recommend using
16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles for the pile foundation design
The piles would derive support from end bearing on the dense river delta deposit and
from skin friction between the pile and the surrounding stiff alluvial deposits
Based on our analyses we believe that an allowable compressive load capacity
of up to about 50 tons 100 kips per pile may be used for the 16 5 inch octagonal pile
design The allowable compressive load capacity is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces To avoid reduction in the vertical compressive load capacity due to
group action and to facilitate pile driving we recommend spacing the 16 5 inch
octagonal piles a minimum of 4 feet from center to center
In order to achieve the recommended allowable compressive load capacity the
16 5 inch octagonal piles may extend to a tip elevation of about 105 feet MSL For
preliminary estimating purposes the pile length may be approximately 103 feet
assuming bottom of the pile cap is at about 2 feet MSL Detailed recommendations for
the driven pile foundation are presented in the following table

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and the subgrade response We estimate that the total settlement of
the pile supported foundation to be less than 0 5 inches Differential settlements
between adjacent piles should not exceed about one half of the total foundation
settlement We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should
occur as the loads are applied
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the
range of about 25 000 to 35 000 foot pounds in the field Significantly higher
energy hammers hammers with energies greater than 50 000 foot pounds are not
recommended for this project The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the soft and or loose soils could be controlled
at a lower hammer energy setting to reduce the potential for tensile stress
development within the piles which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles Geolabs
should review the proposed pile driving equipment to evaluate whether the
equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs for
review prior to construction Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the
driveability of the piles with the use of the selected hammer A wave equation
analysis should be performed after selection of the pile driving equipment to assess
the driveability of the piles and to establish the preliminary pile driving criteria for the
test pile program

Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 16 5 inch octagonal prestressed precast concrete
piles pile driving may be terminated when aggregate blow counts are at least
50 blows per foot for the last foot or 5 blows per inch for the last 6 inches The
above preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and or modified after
selection of the pile driving equipment and performance of wave equation analyses
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the pile driving is interrupted for more
than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft
driving such as when the pile tip is within soft soils the hammer stroke or energy
should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop
within the pile during the pile driving operation
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field by a

not limited to slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed without
pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with
the shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in blowing or heaving of
the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the soft and or loose compressible soils Therefore
the use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the
groundwater level should not be allowed

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and
correlating the layers between the borings The moisture content tests were performed
in general accordance with ASTM D 2216 The unit weight tests were performed in
general accordance with ASTM D 2937 The test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Four
Unconsolidated
Undrained
Triaxial
Compression
TXUU
tests
ASTM D 2850 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
relatively undisturbed soil sample The test results and the stress strain curves are
presented on Plates B 2 through B 5
Two Consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on samples of the soft
compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered The test results are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
Two Organic Content tests ASTM D 2974 were performed on selected soil
samples to aid in soil classification The test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A
