INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed retail commercial complex in
metropolitan Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents
our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses of
the data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and not intended for construction estimation or
bidding
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop a retail commercial center at the project
site with more than an 86 000 square feet area
Based on the current design concept the proposed commercial center
will be a concrete structure with five stories above grade and two levels
of basement parking The building above grade will measure approximately
195 feet by 270 feet in plan while the basement parking levels will occupy
almost the entire project site and extend approximately 20 feet below the
existing ground surface
The proposed development will also include a porte cochere supported
on four columns at the front on grade concrete driveway and parkings and
perimeter planting areas around the building
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the project site The information obtained was
used to develop a generalized soil rock data set for the formation of
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the foundation and
earthwork design of the proposed commercial center

W O 2401 00 Page 3
The work was done in general accordance with our proposal dated
August 7 1989 and subsequent correspondences regarding additional
exploration dated October 24 and 26 1989 to Herbert Nakano Associates
The scope of work was limited to
Drilling and sampling of five 5 borings at the proposed
building location to depths ranging from 60 5 to 101 0 feet below
the existing ground surface for a total of 381 0 lineal feet of

Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the

Overall project management and design team consultation on an

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in metropolitan Honolulu on the island of
Oahu Hawaii Including the former Toyo Theater the site is bounded by
Aala Street on the north and College Walk promenade along Nuuanu Stream on
the south A small electric substation utility building is located
adjacent to the west corner of the site
The site is currently an open toll parking lot paved with asphaltic
concrete The surface at the former theater site consists of uncompacted
sand and gravel ground with a sparse cover of grasses weeds and low
bushes
The site is generally flat with ground surface elevations at
approximately 5 to 8 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is situated next to the northern bank of Nuuanu
Stream on the Honolulu Coastal Plain This plain is composed of
interbedded marine terrestrial and volcanic deposits on the southwest
side of the former Koolau Volcano
The base member encountered in our exploration consists of basaltic
lava flow from the Honolulu Volcanic Series This basalt was deeply
scoured by stream erosion at a time when sea level was as much as 300 feet
below its present elevation As the great continental glaciers of the Ice
Age melted and the sea level rose erosion of the basalt ceased and
extended periods of deposition followed The deposits generally consisted
of alluvium and ash transported from Nuuanu Valley upstream of the site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2401 00 Page 5
Subsequently the alluvium was covered by sandy beach deposits and
coralline reef formation which developed when the sea level rose to an
elevation higher than its present level As continental glaciers formed
again sea level dropped the reef and alluvial deposits were eroded
When sea level rose again to its present level the stream channel was
filled with fine grained organic soils deposited in a quiet lagoonal
environment
More recent alluvial deposits continued to accumlate over the
lagoonal sediments Finally in the past centuries the area was filled
for development
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface soil conditions at the project site were explored by
drilling and sampling five 5 borings to depths of 60 5 to 101 0 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The exploration encountered a thin fill crust of miscellaneous
materials underlain by interbedded layers of generally very soft loose
alluvial and lagoonal deposits which constituted a thick unit of highly
compressible subsoils over basalt rock at about 34 0 to 81 5 feet below
the existing ground surface
The miscellaneous surface fills are generally dense sand gravel to
stiff clayey sandy silts The highly compressible subsoil unit consists
of loose to very loose sand and gravel and soft to very soft organic
clayey and sandy silts
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2401 00 Page 6
The basalt rock appears to dip from north towards south from Aala
Street towards Nuuanu Stream at an approximate gradient of six horizontal
to one vertical 6H 1V or 16 percent The rock formation is moderately
to severely fractured on its surface and generally grades to less
fractured or massive with depth
Groundwater Level
During our field exploration groundwater levels were measured
between the approximate depths of 4 3 to 7 0 feet below the existing
ground surface corresponding to 1 0 to 1 9 feet MSL elevation in the
arilled borings Due to the proximity to Nuuanu Stream groundwater
levels at the project site are expected to fluctuate with tidal flood and
storm surge conditions in the stream Therefore the groundwater levels
measured in the borings during our field exploration may not reflect the
highest possible levels
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on the boring data the proposed building and basement parking
will be underlain by highly compressible subsoils Therefore we
recommend that pile foundations bearing on the underlying basalt rock
formation be used to support the proposed building and deep basement
parking structure Due to the soft subsoil and high groundwater
conditions it is our opinion that mat and spread footing foundation
support systems were not considered feasible for the proposed building
because significant building settlements would occur under the anticipated
heavy building loads
Due to the proposed deep basement excavation alternate pile driving
methods may be considered to optimize construction economy Longer piles
may be driven from the existing grade and the upper twenty 20 foot
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2401 00 Page 7
lengths be wasted or shorter piles may be driven from the excavation
bottom which however should be stabilized with a thick granular fill mat
over filter fabric to support the piling operations within the excavation
The deep basement excavation should be properly retained with
continuous interlocking steel sheetpile walls laterally supported by
rakers bearing on temporary raker footings or the driven piles It is our
opinion that cantilever sheeting alone will not be adequate to retain the
deep excavation sides since the sheetpiles may not penetrate deep enough
to develop sufficient resistance due to the shallow depths of rock in most
of the project site
However it is essential that the braced sheeting be driven deep
enough to reduce the potential of ground heaving and or piping at the
bottom of the basement excavation The sheetpile walls may also need to
be driven deeper to provide more control of groundwater drawdown on the
outside of the future construction excavation during dewatering
Due to the proximity of Nuuanu Stream and the prevailing high
groundwater levels at the site excavation dewatering will be a critical
concern although in general the subsoils below excavation may not have
very high permeability values Continuous dewatering is anticipated to
reduce the buoyant forces on the building basement during the initial
project construction period To shorten the required dewatering period
a thick structural basement slab to increase the weight of the building
prior to its completion may be required
Due to the complexity of excavation support and dewatering
anticipated it is recommended that a qualified contractor selected for
the project should conduct a more thorough study prior to construction

Pile Foundation Support
Since there is a competent basalt rock formation underlying the thick
compressible soil unit we recommend that end bearing pile foundations be
used to support the proposed building
The piles would derive their vertical compressional capacity
primarily from bearing on the hard basalt encountered at about 34 to 82
feet below the existing grade and partially from adhesion or friction
between the pile shafts and the various subsoil units above the rock
formation
concrete piles are recommended The piles would develop an allowable
vertical compression capacity up to 110 tons per pile if driven firmly to
the hard rock formation with a suitable pile driving hanmer
The end bearing piles may also provide an allowable vertical uplift
capacity of 0 4 tons per foot length of the pile including the weight of
the pile to resist the buoyant forces acting on the basement submerged
below groundwater table If a pile is spliced the uplift capacity
contributed by the pile length below the pile splice should not be
considered unless the pile splice used has the ability to transmit tensile
forces
It is our opinion that a high groundwater level at elevation 2 5
feet MSL may be used for the design of the uplift stability of the new
building

Pile Driving
The end bearing piles should be driven to a minimum resistance of 110
blows per foot or 15 blows per inch for the last 3 inches of pile
penetration
The pile driving hammer used should be capable of delivering a
minimum of 24 000 foot pounds of energy during final driving
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile
may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced if the driving
process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft soil
the hammer stroke or energy should be carefully controlled so that
excessive tensile stresses are not developed in the pile
The pile driving should be monitored by a qualified geotechnical
engineer to see that the piles are driven to sufficient depth and a
competent bearing formation

Resistance to lateral loads such as short duration wind loads acting
on the new superstructure may be obtained by lateral capacity of the pile
foundations and by passive earth pressure developed against the perimeter
basement walls of the proposed building We recommend that an allowable

p c f equivalent fluid pressure not including groundwater pressure
below groundwater may also be used

Excavation Support
Based on the soft subsoil and high groundwater conditions we
recommend that continuous interlocking steel sheetpile walls be used
to support the deep excavation
It is critical that properly designed interlocked steel sheeting be
driven with a suitable sheetpile hammer prior to excavation The
sheeting should be maintained laterally tight against the excavated
earth faces to reduce the potential of adjacent ground loss or
movements which may jeopardize existing adjacent structures
However our analysis indicates that cantilever free standing
sheetpiles alone without a lateral support system may not be
adequate The sheeting can not be driven deep enough to mobilize
sufficient passive lateral soil resistance due to encountering basalt
rock refusal at shallow depths in most areas within the project
site Lateral support by horizontal struts or bracing appears not
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 14
sufficient or practical due to the anticipated width of the
excavation Therefore other forms of lateral support such as
rakers should be considered to brace the sheetpile walls
At the excavation bottom the rakers may be supported on shallow
spread footings or driven piles We estimate that an allowable
footing bearing capacity of 1000 p s f may be used for the raker
footing design The driven piles may provide a lateral capacity up
to 2 kips per pile to support the rakers A schematic sketch of the
raker supported sheetpile wall system is presented as a general guide
on Plate 3
The braced steel sheeting should also be driven to a sufficient depth
to reduce the possibility of bottom heaving or quick conditions
Which may occur during dewatering of the excavation due to upward
seepage pressure
Based on our preliminary estimate the braced sheeting should be
driven to a minimum embedment of 10 feet below the excavation bottom
to reduce the potential of subgrade heaving or quick conditions
The required depth of sheetpile penetration will also be affected by
the method of dewatering used The above recommended sheetpile
penetration depth is based on the assumption that there is no
groundwater drawdown outside of the sheeting
If the dewatering system s used create groundwater drawdown outside
the sheeting the depth of sheetpile penetration may be reduced
However these dewatering systems are generally not recommended since
care must be taken to prevent excessive areal groundwater drawdown

Construction Precaution and Monitoring
The sheeting and raker support system used must comply with all
applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the
sheeting and raker bracing installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who is
continuously present on site will have the best opportunity to
observe any changing conditions during construction such as
unforeseen subsoils unexpected high groundwater level unsuitable
construction sequence or techniques etc which might affect the
excavation support stability
Even with good construction procedures some movement of the
sheetpile walls may still occur because of changes in earth stresses
during excavation This depends greatly on workmanship for example
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2401 00 Page 16
how quickly and tightly the sheetpiles and the raker support are
installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the depth and
width of excavation and also the progress or rate of excavation
In view of the complexity of the problem it is difficult to
accurately predict movement of a retaining wall system and its
adjacent ground Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation lateral support should be installed as early as
practicable and that vertical and horizontal ground movements outside
of the excavation should be continuously monitored and controlled
with settlement markers piezometers and inclinometers etc
Generally a damage survey of all the existing buildings and
structures adjacent to the project site should be conducted
including photographs and crack surveys prior to the commencement of
construction
During construction the ground adjacent to the excavation should be
continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of
movement such as sloughing of the excavation faces Such monitoring
is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures and
utilities not supported on pile foundations
Dewatering
The project site is located next to Nuuanu Stream Based on the
groundwater data the encountered groundwater levels at the site are more
than 15 feet above the proposed excavation bottom
Due to this high groundwater level a thick basement slab may be
required to add weight to the proposed building to resist buoyant forces
during construction if continuous and adequately controlled dewatering
operation is not feasible
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2401 00 Page 17
Therefore dewatering of the deep basement excavation will be a
critical design consideration It is imperative that the project
dewatering be adequately designed and performed since the project site is
next to a stream and amid dense urban development which may be jeopardized
by excessive areal groundwater drawdown caused by dewatering at the site
We recommend that a thorough study of the project dewatering scheme
be required from the selected contractor and that qualified consultants be
retained to evaluate the proposed scheme and monitor the dewatering
operations during construction
The following discussion and recommendations are presented only as a
general guideline for preliminary design considerations
The method and equipment to be used for the dewatering operations
should be the choice of the contractor within practical limits and
safety considerations It should be realized that the dewatering
system chosen will have an effect on the required penetration depth
of the steel sheeting used and also on the resulting areal depression
of the prevailing groundwater level
We suggest that the following three 3 basic selection criteria be
considered in determining a suitable method of dewatering
a The method should impose the least disturbance or damage to
existing adjacent developments
b The method should maintain adequate excavation stability and
provide safe working conditions
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O
2401 00 Page 18
c The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modification
to accommodate various ground conditions
It appears that sump pumping inside the adequately braced excavation
may be used if the sheeting is driven sufficiently deep to prevent
piping and heaving at the bottom of the excavation
Alternately dewatering by means of a deep well or well point system
along the outside of the excavation could be considered as a last
resort However the resulting areal depression of the groundwater
table could induce consolidation of subsoils and or settlements below
the existing adjacent structures
Therefore if a deep well or well point system is used recharging
injecting water back into the subsoils to balance the natural
groundwater levels and minimize areal drawdown in areas near the
existing adjacent structures should be evaluated to reduce the risk
of settlements to these buildings

Construction Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing
the groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor
prior to operation The impact and safety of the dewatering system
operations should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor
His representative who is continuously present on site will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering
during construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary
precautionary or remedial measures including but not limited to
stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the excavation bottom the relatively permeable
granular subsoils may be susceptible to piping and quick
conditions Therefore the project dewatering operations should be
done without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and
should be coordinated with the excavation support installation so
that the excavation is not jeopardized The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or
softening at the bottom of the excavation
Caution should also be taken during the dewatering operations not to
dewater utility trench es that connect to the dewatered excavation
The resultant areal groundwater depression may extend along the
granular bedding and or backfill of the trench es and could cause
settlements and damages to the utility lines and or other existing
structures

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
eS Ne ae me Sl
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling five
5 borings to depths ranging from 60 5 to 101 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate
2 Truck mounted equipment was used to drill the borings
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist and engineer who
continuously monitored the drilling operations The classifications were
further reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The
soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 2 inch 0 D
standard penetration sampler or a 2 4 inch I D split barrel sampler with
a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Rock core samples were obtained by an NX core barrel The core
recovery and RQD values are shown on the Boring Iogs at the appropriate
sample depths

samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results are
shown on Plate B 1

determine their gradation characteristics The test results are
summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3

evaluate its compressibility The test results are plotted on Plate B 4
