INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soils investigation for the
wharf subsidence correction project on Alava Wharf U S Naval Ship
Repair Facility in Subic Bay Republic of the Philippines The
vicinity of the Alava Wharf is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 A schematic overview of the wharf is shown on Plate 2 This
soils investigation is a part of the program to repair settlement
damage suffered at Alava Wharf Navy Contract No N62742 82 C 0063
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
It is our understanding that Alava Wharf has experienced subsidence or
settlement problems since shortly after its construction in 1955 The
settlements have occurred at various locations More detailed
descriptions of these settlements will be given and discussed in a
subsequent section of this report The worst settlement experienced
to date has been at the Sampson Road Access Ramp
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
Therefore this soils investigation is focused on the Sampson Road
Access Ramp area of the wharf for the repair work design purpose The
present repair work design covers only the access bridge and a 100
foot segment of the wharf straddling the Sampson Road center line
The general layout of facilities in the area of investigation is shown
on the Site Plan Plate 3
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF SERVICES
The purpose of our investigation was to explore the subsurface condi
tions review the available data evaluate probable cause or causes of
the subsidence and provide recommendations for the design of remedial
measures

approximate locations shown on the Boring Location Plan Plate 3 The
first boring Boring 1 was drilled in early November 1982 to a depth
of 196 feet below the deck or to Elevation 186 feet MLLW Datum
The second boring Boring 2 was drilled in December 1982 to a depth
of 207 feet below the deck or to Elevation 197 feet MLLW Datum
The delay in drilling the second boring was caused by a heavy use of
the wharf in the area of Boring 2 during the month of November The
drilling was done under the technical supervision of a field eng ineer
from our Honolulu office

As mentioned previously wharf subsidence was noted soon after con
struction in 1955 The elevation changes along the wharf have been
monitored since 1958 A typical subsidence profile along the wharf
is shown on Plate 4 The actual wharf settlement readings at the
outboard deck prior to 1972 and the outboard rail after 1972 at
Station 13 50 approximately at Sampson Road center line intersection
are plotted and shown on Plate 5
The following observations are noted from the above settlement
readings and profiles
1 The wharf settlements are larger where landfills either access
ramp fill or building fills are closer to the wharf
2 The radius of settlement influence is approximately 200 feet
from the landfill edge to the wharf
3 The settlements occurred Slowly over a long period
4 Different benchmarks were used Some of the benchmarks may also
be subsiding at different rates
5 Observed rates of subsidence in recent years are somewhat
irregular at various points on the wharf

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions in the Sampson Road access ramp area were
explored by drilling two deep borings The approximate locations of
these borings are shown on Plate 3 Logs of Borings drilled by others
for the substation near Sampson Road access ramp and at the cold
storage building were also reviewed However the boring depths were
only 140 feet in one boring and 100 feet deep in the others and did
not penetrate through the soft compressible estuarine sediments
Below a wedge of fill which is about 21 feet thick at Boring 1 and
essentially negligible at Boring 2 the subsoil consists of nearly 175
feet of soft highly compressible estuarine sediments of soft silt and
loose fine sands underlain by dense sandy gravel boulder deposits
Borings by others nearby also confirms the existence of the thick soft
sediments A generalized soil profile along the Sampson Road access
ramp is presented on Plate 7

The estuarine sediments may be grouped into two main categories The
upper 80 feet to Elevation 90 feet MLIW Datum consists of dark
gray loose silty fine sand with several interbedded soft gray sandy
and clayey silt layers of varying thickness The lower 85 feet of the
soft sediments to about Elevation 175 feet consist mainly of soft to
medium stiff gray silty clay with occasional sand seams and worn
burrow casts These categories of soft sediments are also shown on
the left side of Plate 7
The materials underlying the soft estuarine sediments consist of dense
sand and gravel with some boulders to Elevation 197 MLLW Datum the
maximum depth drilled in Boring 2 These materials are believed to be
alluvial in origin
No artesian conditions were encountered in our boring nor noted in any
of other previous borings
DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
PROBABLE CAUSE OF WHARF SUBSIDENCE
Based on our findings we believe that the observed wharf settlements
have occurred because the approach fills at Sampson Road and other
fills were too close to the wharf structure The weight of the fills
has caused consolidation settlements in the underlying thick soft
compressible sediments
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
W O 1175 00
Page 9
The soft compressible sediments extend to about 185 feet below the
wharf deck level However while no actual pile driving record has
been recovered the pile lengths shown on Bechtel s drawing Reference
10 strongly imply pile tip penetration of only about 140 feet below
the deck Therefore the pile tips may have been terminated in the
soft sediments and subjected to settlements of the soft sediments
caused by the weight of the nearby fills Additionally the settle
ment of the soils around the piles probably caused downdrag or
negative skin friction on the piles which induced additional vertical
load on the piles thereby increasing settlements
The above conclusion is substantiated by the pattern of wharf settle
ments as indicated by the inboard rail profile shown on Plate 4 It
is noted in this profile that large settlements occur at each of the
three approach ramp intersections while areas of the wharf away from
the land fills have small settlements
The probable magnitudes of wharf settlements may be inferred by the
pattern of settlements Based on the profiles of the rails we
believe that the maximum inboard rail settlement could have been
somewhat over 2 1 feet at Sampson Road which has the largest
landfills placed closest to the wharf The settlement of the
outboard rail is slightly less perhaps about 1 9 feet at this
location
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
W O 1175 00 Page 10
The elevation surveys References la to 8 generally indicated that
the inboard crane rail settlements are greater than the outboard rail
settlements In addition the August 1981 survey indicated that the
outboard rail was pulled out of horizontal alignment as much as four
inches inboard toward the landfill at Sampson Road ramp location
We believe that these observations also Support our conclusion that
the nearby landfills rather than Slope instability have caused the
wharf subsidence If slope instability was the cause there would
have been more lateral distortion of the wharf and the movement
pattern would have differed
It is believed that the soil support of the piles probably did not
fail under the crane load or dead load of the deck otherwise the
subsidence problem would have affected the entire wharf more uniformly
and other structural damage would have been reported
POSSIBLE REMEDIAL MEASURES
General
te
Our findings on the main causes of the wharf subsidence can be
summarized as follows
1 Landfills were too close to the wharf
2 Wharf piles were not deep enough to penetrate through the
compressible sediments
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
W O 1175 00 Page 11
Future wharf subsidence will likely to continue probably at a
diminishing rate and may become tolerable However the informa
tion developed thusfar is not adequate enough to enable us to
predict potential future subsidence rate with reasonable conf idence
It is felt that the maximum future settlements may range from 6 to
10 inches on the wharf and perhaps 9 to 15 inches at the Sampson
Road access ramp abutment These estimates are educated guesses
at best and need to be refined by additional settlement data from a
continued monitoring program as discussed later
Because the subsidence has occurred along the majority of the
original wharf we believe that any proposed repair scheme for
Sampson Road access ramp and the portion of the wharf in question
should consider its applicability and effect to other portions of
the wharf that may continue to settle
Discussed in the following subsections are three possible remedial
schemes
1 Removing fill to eliminate or minimize the settlement causing
fill weight
2 Reconstructing the wharf with longer piles through the com
pressible sediments to reach the more competent sand gravel
stratum
3 Taking a calculated risk and performing only minimal repairs at
distressed points
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
W O 1175 00 Page 12
From the geotechnical point of view the fill removal approach
would appear to be most preferable while the wharf reconstruction
and minimal repair schemes are less preferable because of the
problems associated with continued settlements These points are
discussed further in the following sections
Scheme No 1 Fill Removal
Ideally the most effective solution to avoid cont inued
settlements in the future would be to remove the portion of
the fills which are causing the settlements Based on the
settlement profile of the wharf it appears that the radius
of influence of the fill settlement is about 200 feet
Thus for a theoretical consideration the fill within a
distance of about 200 feet from the inboard edge of the
wharf should be removed to relieve the subsidence inf luences
on the wharf piles
We recognize the practical difficulty of removing all fills
within 200 feet of the wharf due to the large amount of fill
involved and the presence of existing structures located
within the areas In view of the settlements that have
already occurred in the last 27 years we believe that the
future remaining settlements would be relatively small and
that a partial fill removal would still be beneficial as a
compromise solution
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
As shown on the cross section sketch Plate 7 the existing ramp
fill at the Sampson Road approach to the wharf Slopes down from
the abutment and extends under the wharf Our calculations
indicate that even if this relatively small wedge of the fill is
removed to Elevation 35 feet and the abutment replaced with a
sheetpile wall a significant reduction in future settlements
may be achieved in this area
Additional fill removal and dredging in and around the wharf and
in the area between the wharf and the land would also be helpful
in reducing potential future settlements This type of approach
will be applicable to all other areas on this wharf
However trimming and or steepening of existing shoreline slopes
will tend to change the slope stability situation Therefore
we recommend that a soils investigation be conducted to evaluate
the technical feasibility of each different fill removal program
This generally will entail drilling a number of additional test
borings in the area performing laboratory soil testing and
evaluating the fill removal program Each slope configuration
should be developed in conjunction with the corresponding dredge
depth so that the overall stability would meet the minimum
criteria desired

C E Maguire Inc

On the other hand the feasibility of any fill removal will
depend on other factors such as base operations future
facility and planning in the area as well as reconstruction
cost considerations Therefore the possibility of partial
fill removal will need to be further evaluated by Facilities
Command
It is important to obtain accurate settlement readings in
order to better evaluate the future performance of this
approach We strongly recommend that such an accurate
settlement monitoring program be implemented as soon as
possible Our recommendations on the settlement monitoring
program are presented in subsequent section of this report
This scheme will also require localized repairs on the

Scheme No 2 Total Reconstruction
The second approach would be to totally reconstruct the approach
ramp deck and 100 foot wharf segment in question to avoid future
settlement The reconstructed portion should be on new long
piles that reach a competent bearing stratum such as the dense
sand and gravel layer at about 195 feet below the deck
Of particular concern for replacing only the approach ramp and
100 foot wharf segment will be the future differential settlements
that will develop Since these new piles and wharf segment will
be non yielding they will stick out as the surrounding portions
continue to settle The additional settlements outside the 100
foot segment now being studied may be somewhat smaller but the
magnitude can still be significant perhaps on the order of 4 to
10 inches It is felt that the decking between the new and
existing sections will not be able to spread the differential
settlement over several spans A severe differential settlement
may develop between the adjacent bents of new and existing wharf
segments therefore flexible joints would be needed
The magnitude of downdrag forces on the existing piles is
estimated to be about 100 tons per pile and these piles will
tend to pull down the decking Therefore all existing piling
should be cut off from the new wharf to eliminate potential
excess downdrag load from the existing piles
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
W O 1175 00 Page 16
The portal crane rails will still need to be relevelled
frequently to maintain a smooth grade change Thus this
approach has inherent maintenance problems that need to be
addressed periodically This concern on differential
settlements would significantly influence the feasibility of
this replacement approach
For the total reconstruction scheme it will be desirable to
remove as much as possible of the access ramp fill and
replace it with new ramp deck supported on new piles This
will reduce the potential differential settlement at the new
abutment
It is our understanding that 16 inch square prestressed
concrete piles are being considered for the total recon
struction scheme It is estimated that the length of the
new piles would be about 210 feet below the wharf deck
The potential downdrag force on a new 16 inch square pile
extending through the compressible sediment is presently
estimated to be on the order of 250 tons This is well
beyond the allowable structural capacity of the 16 inch
pile Therefore methods to reduce the downdrag force
should be implemented
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
W O 1175 00 Page 17
One such method is coating the pile with bitumen It has
been reported that the downdrag forces can be reduced by 60
to 90 with a bitumen coating Thus usable structural pile
Capacity beyond that needed to counteract downdrag force can
be provided and an allowable design capacity in the range of
60 to 80 tons on top of each pile may be considered The new
piles should be coated with bitumen in the section through
the compressible sediment to reduce downdrag force However
a 30 foot portion from the bottom the piles should be
uncoated to gain possible frictional resistance near the pile
tip
The new piles should be driven with a large pile driving
hammer delivering at least 50 000 foot pounds of energy per
blow In addition the hammer energy should be adjustable so
that driving energy could be Significantly reduced to avoid
damage to the pile while penetrating through soft sediments
The piles will need to be spliced It is felt that steel
splice sleeves may be used However coupling devices may be
needed to hold the lower section of pile securely to prevent
separation during driving in soft zone The number of splices
should not exceed 2 per pile This will require long pile
sections on the order of 70 to 80 feet each for vertical
piles to reach 210 feet below the wharf deck

C E Maguire Inc

pile hammer pile size and design capacities are finalized
Furthermore the criteria may need to be modified according to
the actual driving conditions

ADDITIONAL RECOMMENDATIONS
Settlement Monitoring Program
It is strongly recommended that both the vertical settlements and
horizontal movements at selected points be monitored All three
possible remedial approaches discussed above will require further
input of accurate survey readings particularly the settlement
readings to better evaluate their feasibilities and practica
bilities A better estimate of future settlements will provide a
sound basis to identify specific repair areas and schedule the
remedial work in a most cost effective manner
The vertical settlements should be monitored accurately for at
least another year preferrably another two years at several
selected points These settlement observation points should be
located along both Sampson Road and the wharf
The reading accuracy should be to 0 005 foot The accuracy of
transit level surveys may be improved by taking a set of repeated
readings under good weather conditions A stable bench mark away
from any subsidence area should be selected and referenced
Alternatively several telltale rods may be installed to monitor
settlements Each rod should be anchored in the sand and gravel
layer some 200 feet below the deck level and encased in oil filled
pipe Each rod should be able to move freely from the pipe casing
GEOLABS HAWAII
C E Maguire Inc
W O 1175 00 Page 20
These rods can detect minute elevation changes and can easily be
read by one man using a dial gage or micrometer We recommend that
the readings be taken at 2 to 3 month intervals Immediately after
each reading the settlement data should be forwarded to the soils
engineer for review and evaluation
The horizontal movements can be done by reading the inclinometers
installed in our borings The inclinometer readings should also
be taken at 2 to 3 month intervals For continuity and quality
assurance Geolabs Hawaii should continue to monitor the inc lino
meter casing assess the results of readings and interpret the field
data gathered
Design Review
Plans and specifications for construction should be forwarded to the
soils engineer for review and written comments prior to construction
to see that the intent of our recommendations is included in the
project plans and specifications
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that fill removal work be closely monitored by a
soils engineer The horizontal and vertical ground movements should
be carefully monitored during and after the planned construction

C E Maguire Inc
It is further recommended that the pile driving pile load test
production pile driving and backfilling if to be implemented be
observed by a soils engineer during construction The purpose of
monitoring by a soils engineer is to see that adequate bearing has

the findings of our investigation The recommendations given in
this report are contingent upon the above inspections

Moisture content and unit weight determinations were
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests
are presented on the log of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
To further identify soil characteristics additional
tests were performed These tests include 12 Atterberg Limits
5 Sieve Analysis to determine the grain size distribution and 9
Specific Gravity The results of these tests are presented on
Plates B 1 through B 4
Four Consolidation Undrained Triaxial Compression Tests
with porewater pressure measurements were performed on select
undisturbed samples to evaluate the soil strength characteristics
of the soil The stress paths of these tests are plotted on
Plates B 5 and B 8
Two Unconfined Compression Tests were performed on undis
turbed samples to further evaluate the soil Strength character
istics The peak compressive strength values for triaxial and
unconfined compression tests are summarized on Plate B 9
Eight Consolidation tests were performed on selected
undisturbed samples to evaluate the settlement characteristics of
the natural soils The test results are presented on Plates B 10
through B 17

APPENDIX D
2S NY A
Inclinometer Readings
An inclinometer system was installed in the borings to
monitor lateral movements in the underlying soil deposits
Initial readings were made during the field exploration and the
worksheets for the readings todate are presented on Plates D 2
through D 13 The indicator casings were left in place and
monitoring instruments are available for additional readings
if desired Additional readings should be made at regular
intervals to monitor possible lateral deflections of the
subsoils
After completion of the borings a 2 75 inch diameter
ABS Inclinometer casing manufactured by Slope Indicator Co of
Seattle Washington with four internal longitudinal grooves
was installed in the bore holes The annular space between
the casing and bore hole was grouted with a cement bentonite
mixture The casing grooves were oriented to provide displace
ment readings in the directions shown on Plate D 1 The top of
casing was cut just below the wharf deck and suitable covers
will be provided by the PWC
The deflection measurement System consists of a servo
accelerometer probe connected by special electrical cable to a
digital readout unit The probe has spring loaded guide wheels
which fit into the casing grooves The probe is lowered into
the casing and readings of the casing deflection are made at
2 foot intervals After readings are made in one direction
the probe is rotated 180 degree and read again The reading
process is then repeated for a second axis perpendicular to the
first axis
The inclinometer readings provide a measurement of
lateral displacement of the casing relative to the bottom of
the casing
W O 1983
Note Future monitoring of the installed casings is not
included in our scope of services
