PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located along Mailiili Road about ¾-mile northeast of the 
intersection with Farrington Highway.  The portion of Mailiili Road was damaged due to a 
20-inch water line break.  The water line invert is about 10 feet from the pavement surface. 
Three 36-inch drain pipe culverts cross under the roadway at the site.  The downstream 
headwall and portions of the downstream end of the drain pipes were damaged by the 
water line break. 
We understand that it is desired to replace the downstream headwall and drain 
pipes and restore the road.  It is also desired to install a concrete jacket around the 20-inch 
water line.   
Due to the emergency nature of this project and the extremely short design 
schedule, borings were not performed for this design. Geotechnical recommendations for 
design of the reconstruction project were based on our visual observations at the site and 
available soils/geology information.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially 
buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded 
lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials, which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series.  
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower, middle, and upper volcanic 
members.  The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks 
that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano.  The middle member consists 
of rock that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera.  The upper 
member is a relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield 
volcano late in its history of evolution.

Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed, a long period of deep erosion, 
sedimentation, and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred, which produced the 
large valleys of the western side of the shield volcano.  These erosional valleys were 
gradually filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a 
combined result of stream erosion, base level rise (sea level rise), and subsidence of 
the island mass.  Over time, and as a result of sea level fluctuation, some of the older 
alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation, 
formed relatively dense deposits.  However the current surface soil deposits above 
these older deposits are typically found to be unconsolidated. 
SITE DESCRIPTION/RECONNAISSANCE 
The project site is located along Mailiili Road about ¾-mile northeast of the 
intersection with Farrington Highway.  The project site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Based on our observations, the south side (downstream) of Mailiili Road, the headwall, 
and portions of the three 36-inch reinforced concrete drain pipes were damaged by the 
water line break.   
At the time of our reconnaissance, the south side headwall had been removed and 
the 20-inch water line repaired.  The pipe culverts were temporarily put back into service 
by placing PVC pipes into the remaining reinforced concrete pipes and backfilling above 
the PVC pipes with granular material to match the existing roadway grades. 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the soils observed along the drainage ditch 
bottom and sidewalls consisted of brown silty clay with varying amounts of sand, gravel, 
and cobbles.  Flowing water was not observed within the drainage ditch; however, 
standing water was observed at the downstream side of the culvert.  We probed the 
downstream area within the standing water with a ½-inch diameter steel-pointed rod.  
Penetration of the steel probe ranged between 18 and 24 inches.   
Based on our research, the anticipated surface soils underlying the project site are 
described by the U.S. Soil Conservation Service in their publication "Soil Survey of Islands 
of Kauai, Oahu, Maui, Molokai and Lanai, State of Hawaii," (1972). The major soil at the 
site and some of its general properties are, as follows: 
Pulehu Clay Loam (PsA) – These are located on alluvial fans and stream 
terraces. Slopes average between about 0 to 3 percent. The soils have moderate 
permeability and a slight erosion hazard.  The shrink-swell potential of the soils is 
considered moderate to low. 
The “Geologic Map of the State of Hawaii, Sheet 3 – Island of Oahu” by David R. 
Sherrod, John M. Sinton, Sarah E. Watkins, and Kelly M. Brunt, 2007 indicates that the 
site is underlain by Alluvium (Qa).

HEADWALL STRUCTURE 
In general, we recommend using shallow foundations to support the headwall 
structure. The following general guidelines may be used for design of the headwall 
structure. 
Headwall Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the site, we recommend an 
allowable bearing pressure of 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) to design the 
foundations.  Since the bottom of the headwall foundation will extend below the 
groundwater level (approximately -3.3 feet Mean Sea Level), we anticipate loose/soft soils 
at the bottom of the foundation excavation.  To provide uniform support, we recommend 
over-excavating the bottom of the foundation excavation a minimum of 24 inches and 
backfilling with No. 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 
180N or equivalent.  The over-excavation should also extend a minimum of 24 inches 
beyond the perimeter of the foundation. 
In general, we recommend embedding the foundations for the retaining structures a 
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. If the footings are 
designed in accordance with our recommendations, total settlement is estimated to be on 
the order of 1 inch or less and differential settlement on the order of about 0.5 inch. 
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 45-
degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the 
footing should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
Lateral loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by friction between the 
base of the foundations and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure 
developed against the vertical faces of footings and/or walls. A coefficient of friction of 0.3 
may be used for foundations bearing on the No. 3B Fine gravel. Resistance to lateral 
loads due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure 
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) above the water level and 130 pcf 
below the water level. This assumes that the on-site soils or select borrow compacted to 
90 percent relative compaction (ASTM D 1557) are used as backfill around the 
foundations.  The passive pressure resistance should be neglected if scour protection 
measures are not provided at the downstream end of the headwall structure.

A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to placing 
the reinforcing steel and/or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and 
the required embedment depths.  
Lateral Earth Pressures 
In general, soil backfill placed behind headwall structure will exert lateral earth 
pressures on the structure. To reduce the foundation width of the headwall structure, we 
recommend using controlled low strength material (CLSM) as backfill behind the wall in 
lieu of compacted soil fill. We envision that unconfined compressive strength of CLSM will 
be on the order of about 150 pounds per square inch (psi) at 28 days. We believe that 
CLSM backfill is self-supporting materials. Therefore, lateral earth pressure (static and 
dynamic) will not act on the headwall structure. 
The existing fill should be keyed and benched back at an inclination of one 
horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) inclination or flatter, and the area between the new wall 
and slope should be backfilled with CLSM.  To reduce the hydrostatic pressure exerted on 
the headwall from wet CLSM during placement, we recommend placing the CLSM backfill 
in maximum lifts of 24 inches.  The CLSM should be allowed to cure prior to placing the 
subsequent lift of CLSM. 
DEWATERING 
Since the headwall foundations will be located below the groundwater level, 
dewatering of the foundation excavation will be necessary. Dewatering provisions will 
need to be included in the contract documents for the proposed construction. Because 
the excavation will involve discharge of groundwater, a National Pollutant Discharge 
Elimination System (NPDES) permit may be required for this discharge. The contractor 
should consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations and information 
pertaining to the permit application. 
The contractor is responsible for dewatering for construction and should select 
the equipment and methods of dewatering. He/she should be aware that modifications 
to the dewatering system may be required during construction depending on the 
conditions encountered. The dewatering method selected should have minimal impact 
on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed excavation. The dewatering 
operations should be coordinated with the shoring support such that the stability of the 
excavations is not jeopardized. The operations should be carried-out without softening 
the bottom of the excavations. 
We believe the subsoils encountered below the groundwater may be considered 
low to moderately permeable soils.  It should be noted that the actual subsoil permeability 
may range broadly and also vary locally in terms of orders of magnitude.  Therefore, the 
contractor should pay special attention to the site-specific dewatering plan for the 
proposed excavation.

UNDERGROUND UTILITY LINES 
We understand that a concrete jacket will be installed around the existing 20-inch 
water line.  The concrete jacket will extend several feet beyond the ends of the new 
headwall structure.  Based on the available information, the bottom of the concrete jacket 
extending beyond the ends of the headwall structure is at Mean Sea Level. Soft and/or 
loose soils encountered at the bottom of the concrete jacket should be over-excavating a 
minimum of 24 inches below the bottom elevation of the concrete jacket and replacing with 
No. 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent.  
The over-excavation should extend a minimum of 24 inches beyond the edges of the 
concrete jacket.  
Backfill above the concrete jacket should consist of on-site soils (with a maximum 
particle size of 3 inches) or select borrow.  The backfill should be placed in maximum 
8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 95 percent relative 
compaction (ASTM D 1557) to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence.   
PAVEMENTS 
In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the new pavements for this 
project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick-up trucks with 
occasional large trucks.  We recommend using the following flexible pavement section 
for preliminary design purposes: 
Flexible Pavement 
 2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
 6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
8.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Controlled Low Strength Material 
The aggregate base should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  The recommended section assumes that 
good drainage will be provided adjacent to paved areas. 
Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface 
water off the site.  Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after 
construction.
