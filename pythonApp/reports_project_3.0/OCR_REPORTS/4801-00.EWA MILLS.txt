1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Miscellaneous Public Building Facilities Improvements project
located within the former Ewa Mills site on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed Miscellaneous
Public Building Facilities Improvements project These recommendations are intended for
the design of site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site former Ewa Mills site is located to the south of Renton Road near
the intersection with Park Row in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on
the information provided it is proposed to make various improvements to the existing
public building facilities at the site We understand that the project site encompasses an
area of approximately 10 acres
In general we understand that the project site will be capped with 2 feet of
controlled fill materials due to environmental considerations It is also desired to dispose
of the soil materials generated during the on site excavations on the project site to avoid
the costly disposal of contaminated soil materials In addition we understand that an
abandoned underground pump station is present at the project site Various remediation
methods will be evaluated for this project to backfill and cap the abandoned pump station
We also understand that new structures will not be constructed as part of the facility
improvements project

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau beginning in the mid Pliocene Epoch The older
volcano Waianae was the first to develop closely followed by the development of Koolau
Volcano For a period of time the present Island of Oahu comprised of two separate
islands formed by each of the volcanic centers The volcanoes continued to erupt and
bank against the slopes of each other with the Waianae Volcano terminating its eruptive
activity prior to the cessation of Koolau Volcano
Following extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch the island underwent a
long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and summits as we
know today During the period of erosion the Island of Oahu began to slowly subside by
more than 1 200 feet in elevation resulting in drowning and sedimentation of the valleys
and formation of the steep Koolau Pali In addition coral reefs continued to grow in the
surrounding shallow waters of the island
From the mid to late Pleistocene Epoch the sea level repeatedly rose and fell in
response to global glaciation and to the availability of surface waters to sustain the
oceans The various sea level elevations and their representative deposits are known as
stands and include from oldest to youngest the following the Kahuku Kahipa Kaena
Laie Waialae Waipio and Waimanalo stands Geologic deposits associated with the
various sea level stands including marine sediments and coral reefs were deposited and
subsequently altered or removed by later sea level fluctuation Therefore depositional
records reflecting the changes in sea level and the occurrence of emerged coral reef
deposits are often incomplete
The project site is situated on the Ewa Plain located to the southeast of the
Waianae Mountain Range The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the
deposition of alluvial silts and clays derived from the weathering of basalt rock further

upslope The alluvial deposits accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments

Basalt rock formation resides below the marine deposits at a substantial depth
The majority of the project site former Ewa Mills site is situated over the alluvial

site at relatively shallow depths beneath the alluvium and or fills Agricultural and
commercial developments within the last 100 years have brought the area to its present
form

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 extending to depths of about 20 6 to

shown on Site Plan Plate 2

borings the silty clay materials generally grade with coralline sands and gravel from about
10 to 15 feet below the ground surface The amount of coralline materials encountered in
the borings generally increased with depth

abandoned pump station location The void encountered in our boring may be part of the
existing underground structure and or underground piping emanating from the structure
at the site Therefore other voids may be present at the project site
Groundwater was encountered in Boring Nos 4 and 5 at depths of about 38 9 and
39 feet below the ground surface respectively during our field exploration It should be

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished using sheepsfoot
rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Fill Slopes
We understand that it may be desired to construct berms in order to dispose of the
excavated materials on site Permanent fill slopes constructed using the excavated
on site soils and imported fill materials may be designed with a slope inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than
5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of
the new fill against sliding It should be noted that the 2 foot capping fill layer
should also be placed at the surface of the berms
Construction of earth berms interceptor ditches and the use of geotextile fabrics
over the fill slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for significant
erosion thus enhancing the long term stability of the fill slopes Appropriate slope
planting or other erosion control measures to reduce the potential for significant
erosion of the exposed slopes including a permanent irrigation system should be
implemented as soon as possible after the finished slope faces are completed

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation
of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was
performed on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the quality and
pavement support characteristics of the soils The results of the test are presented on
Plate B 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the test are presented on Plate B 4
