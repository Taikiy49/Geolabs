SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered very stiff to hard, highly expansive clays 
overlying medium dense clayey sands extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 22 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our laboratory test results, the on-site clayey soils exhibit very high 
shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. 
Therefore, we recommend providing at least 36 inches of non-expansive select granular fill 
materials below the concrete walkway and parking area due to the expansive nature of the 
on-site clayey soils. If RAP is used for the 3-foot capping layer, the material should contain 
at least 10 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  Otherwise, the upper 12 inches 
should be capped with materials meeting the above fines content requirement to reduce 
the potential for water infiltration through the bony RAP material down to the highly 
expansive clayey subgrade soils. 
Due to budgetary constraints and temporary nature of the housing project, we 
believe that the following alternate design may be adopted for the project, provided that 
the owner is made aware of the need for periodic maintenance and accepts the risk of 
potential distresses such as heaving, settling, and/or cracking of the concrete walkway and 
parking area.   
In lieu of providing the 3-foot thick capping fill materials as recommended above, an 
alternate design for the concrete walkway may consist of 18 inches of non-expansive 
select granular fill materials.  In addition, consideration should be given to structurally 
connecting adjacent concrete panels with dowels or other structural connections, to 
reduce the potential for differential movement across the traverse joint that may result in 
potential tripping hazard. An alternate design for the parking area may consist of 12 inches 
of aggregate base course or RAP material with at least 10 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve.  In addition, a layer of reinforcing geogrid, such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX-7 
or equivalent, should be provided in the mid-depth of the aggregate base course/RAP 
layer to provide additional support for the parking area. 
The clayey subgrade below the concrete walkway and parking area should be 
scarified to a minimum depth of about 10 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
compaction.  For the alternate design, a layer of non-woven geotextile fabric, such as 
Mirafi 180N or equivalent, should be provided on top of the clayey subgrade to reduce the

performed for the New Modular Housing at 86-537 Halona Road project in the Waianae 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown

presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas 
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials, which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series. 
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower, middle, and upper volcanic 
members. The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks 
that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano. The middle member consists of 
rock that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera. The upper member 
is a relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in 
its history of evolution. 
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed, a long period of deep erosion, 
sedimentation, and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred, which produced the 
large valleys of the western side of the shield volcano. These erosional valleys were 
gradually filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a 
combined result of stream erosion, base level rise (sea level rise), and subsidence of 
the island mass. Over time, and as a result of sea level fluctuation, some of the older 
alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation, 
formed relatively dense deposits. However, the current surface soil deposits above 
these older deposits are typically found to be unconsolidated. 
The project site is at the foot of Paheehee Ridge, an erosional remnant ridge 
from the western flank of the Waianae Mountain Range. Chemical and mechanical

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the project 
site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile 
type for seismic design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground

W.O. 7368-00 
GEOLABS, INC.

movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains.

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of 
strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose 
saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the 
soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced.

Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the 
overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and 
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a 
mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not 
confined, will result in significant deformations.

sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

3.4 
Shrinkage Cracks 
Our field observations indicated that substantial shrinkage cracks are present at 
the existing ground surface of the project site.  In general, shrinkage cracks will start to 
develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the 
soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions.  The development of 
shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic 
conditions in the Waianae area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture 
content.  Detailed discussions of the shrink/swell behavior of the on-site soils and the 
mechanism of shrinkage crack development are presented below. 
3.4.1 Shrink/Swell Behavior of On-Site Soils 
Based on the laboratory test results performed for the project, the on-site clayey 
soils generally have a very high shrink/swell potential when subjected to 
fluctuations in the moisture content and may be classified as expansive soils.  In 
addition, it will likely lose substantial strength when saturated and will shrink 
appreciably when subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks.  In general, the 
mechanism of shrink/swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is 
influenced by a number of factors, including soil characteristics and environmental 
factors. 
Expansive soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering 
properties, such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink/swell potential 
of soils.  In general, the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 7368-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 17 
Hawaii • California 
soil to retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure.  
Therefore, a highly plastic soil, such as those encountered at the project site, will 
have a greater potential for volume change (shrink and swell) due to the loss or 
absorption of moisture under cycles of drying and wetting.  In addition, increased 
soil densities through compaction or natural depositional history (such as 
desiccation) generally have greater shrink/swell potential. 
Environmental factors include the initial moisture content of the soil and moisture 
variations over time.  The initial moisture content generally impacts the potential of 
the soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system.  In general, a dry soil will 
have a higher capacity to absorb water and swell, while a wet soil will lose water 
more readily upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more.  Moisture 
variations can generally be caused by climate, surface drainage, vegetation, 
permeability, etc. 
3.4.2 Shrinkage Crack Development Mechanism 
Due to the high plasticity of the on-site clayey soils, shrinking of the near-surface 
soils starts to develop as soil moisture evaporates from the soil system under the 
prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Waianae area.  These 
shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils 
expand during periods of wet weather or heavy rains.  However, shrinkage of the 
on-site soils and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will recur when the dry 
weather returns.  Therefore, the development of shrinkage cracks will recur when 
the on-site clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry climatic conditions, which 
causes large fluctuations in the moisture content, unless some measures are taken 
to maintain the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level. 
In areas where ponding water occurs, the wet ground will have a higher potential 
for shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily upon 
exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more.  Therefore, it should be recognized 
that this phenomenon will recur and continue unless measures are taken to 
maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant in order to reduce the 
shrink/swell behavior of the on-site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations.

Utility Trenches 
We understand utility connections for the temporary housing project will consist 
of new water lines. In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the 
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new underground utilities. The 
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench

System Standards by the Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the

to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could 
result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular fill 
material. The on-site expansive clay soils should not be used for trench backfill.  The 
backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically 
compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the 
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 22 feet below the 
existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample, designated as Bulk-1, of the 
near-surface soils was obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics. The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill equipment equipped with continuous flight 
augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the 
soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-1. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
of the clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits for soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.  Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plate B-3. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-4.
