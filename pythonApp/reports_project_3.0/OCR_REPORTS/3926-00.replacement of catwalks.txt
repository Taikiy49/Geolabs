for the proposed Replacement of Catwalks T F1 H C 1955 located at Kewalo Basin in
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design of the pile
foundations and site preparation only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the existing coastal plains developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below
the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments and coral algal reef
growth accumulated at higher elevations
The project site is located on the coastal plain of southern Oahu The coastal plain
was built on the eroded flanks of Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern two thirds 2 3

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The catwalk replacement project site is located at Kewalo Basin in the District of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is bounded by Ala Moana
Boulevard to the north and Kewalo Basin to the south as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The existing catwalks are currently being used for commercial cruises and fishing
excursions At the time of our field exploration we understand that Pier X Y was
unoccupied The other five piers were in full operation and the boats loaded and unloaded
passengers as frequently as every 30 minutes at some piers
The existing catwalks are made of a concrete deck and supported by 12 inch square
and 16 5 inch octagonal reinforced concrete piles A concrete sidewalk and apron runs
along the waterfront bulkhead at the project site Inland from the sidewalk is an asphaltic
concrete access road and loading unloading zone At the time of our field exploration traffic

adjacent piers and sidewalk was generally moderate

the access roadway of about 5 5 to 6 5 feet Mean Lower Low Water MLLW The deck
surface elevations of the existing catwalks are approximately 4 5 to 6 feet MLLW

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered the mudline at about 18 to 22 feet below
the existing pier decks Very soft and or loose lagoonal deposits were generally
encountered from the mudline to about 34 feet below the existing pier decks The lagoonal
deposits were underlain by interbedded layers of soft to very hard coral ledge and loose
to dense coral detritus coralline debris deposits The first coral ledge was generally
encountered at about 34 feet and extended to about 48 to 55 feet below the existing pier
decks In Boring No 1 a second coral ledge was encountered at about 80 feet and
extended to about 93 feet below the existing pier deck
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our drilled borings we
recommend that driven pile foundations be used for support of the new replacement
catwalks Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we recommend that
20 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles be used to support the new
catwalks Due to the higher structural load demands on the pier foundations we
recommend that a larger pile section than those currently exist at the site 12 inch square
and 16 5 inch octagonal be used to support the new catwalks Due to the relatively high
lateral loads imposed on the catwalks the piles will need to be battered at an inclination
of one horizontal to eight vertical 1H 8V and extend to a depth of approximately 80 feet
below the pier decks The piles would derive vertical compressive load support from skin
friction between the piles and surrounding soils and bear on the medium dense to dense
coralline materials
Since battered piles will be used to support the new catwalks we recommend that
the conventional static load test be omitted for this project due to the difficulties in
performing the test on battered piles In lieu of the conventional static load test we
recommend that dynamic load tests using a pile driving analyzer be performed on the test
piles to confirm the capacity of the battered piles for this project

5 Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 50 000 foot pounds per blow The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled at a lower
energy to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which could damage or
cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to
the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs Hawaii should
review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of
installing the piles to the recommended capacity

6 Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 20 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles
pile driving may be terminated when the aggregated blow counts are at least 3 blows
per inch for the last 12 inches or 8 blows per inch for the last 4 inches The
preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and or modified after selection of the
pile driving equipment and performance of a wave equation analysis In addition the
pile driving criteria may be modified during the test pile driving to accommodate
variations of soil conditions in the field
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for
more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft
driving such as when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer stroke
or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not
develop within the pile
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils the supporting capacity of each pile should be confirmed in the field by a
representative from our office during the driving operation It should be noted that the
pile design was developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration data A
test pile program should be carried out to confirm our preliminary pile design and
establish the driving criteria The production pile driving criteria may be revised based
on the test pile program
7 Test Pile Program
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a test pile
program be conducted for the proposed catwalk foundation system In general we
recommend that the test pile program consist of a minimum of 2 test piles per catwalk
for a total of 12 test piles six catwalks The test piles may be incorporated into the
actual foundation system upon the evaluation of the dynamic load test results The
test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following main objectives
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3926 00 Page 14
1 To examine the ability of the selected hammer to drive the piles to the
recommended depths and driving resistance
2 To assess if the pre drilling depth needs to be modified or revised
3 To evaluate and modify if necessary the preliminary driving criteria from the
wave equation analysis
4 To more accurately determine the probable production pile tip elevations
Test piles should be cast at least 10 feet longer than the estimated pile length to
account for potential variation in the subsurface conditions Each test pile should be
instrumented for monitoring by a pile driving analyzer PDA to observe the stresses
developed in the test piles and to evaluate the pile bearing capacity Instrumentation
and monitoring of a pile by a pile driving analyzer is also known as dynamic load
testing In addition the test pile program should provide flexibility to allow for addition
or relocation of the test piles as may be deemed necessary by our office to suit the
conditions encountered during the test pile driving Re striking of the test piles a
minimum of 72 hours after the initial pile driving will be required as part of the test pile
program
Pre drilling should be conducted for the test piles similar to the production piles as
discussed in the Predrilling subsection above in order to determine the presence
of subsurface obstructions which would hinder pile driving or damage the piles
The cuttings generated from the predrilling may be left in the predrilled hole
The test piles should be driven with the same hammer which will be used for
production pile driving The test pile program should be conducted under close and
continuous observation of the project geotechnical engineer to assist in determining
when the test piles may be stopped and to provide guidance should additional
measures be required due to unforeseen driving conditions Geolabs Hawaii should
evaluate the load carrying capacity of the test piles and production pile embedment
lengths based on the results of the test pile driving and dynamic load testing

We envision that new utility lines such as electric duct lines would be required for the
proposed project In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches
of sand or S4C be used under the duct lines for uniform support The initial backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining materials such as sand or
S4C to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill The
sand and or S4C should be densified using a vibratory plate or similar light compaction
equipment
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils or select
granular fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIXA
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of the two borings were about 101 5 feet below the existing
pier deck surface The borings were drilled with a trailer mounted drill rig equipped with
casing and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Torvane shear tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved in
the field The torvane shear test provides a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength
of the soil sample Results of the torvane shear tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the hard coralline materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The coral material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of coral masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
