2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The base
of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
Renewed volcanism during the Pleistocene Epoch resulted in the deposition of
volcanic materials in various portions of the Island of Oahu Eruption of the
Tantalus Round Top Sugarloaf vents above Makiki laid a thick mantle of pyroclastic
deposits that predominately consist of fine black cinder sands over much of the
Honolulu area between Liliha and Kaimuki from the approximate alignment of
King Street to the Koolau pediment
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world The higher sea
level stands caused the accumulation of sediments in the heads of the old bays
accumulation of reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Renewed erosion
deposited alluvial soils in the lagoons and bays
Rapid rise in the erosional depositional base level caused the rapid deposition of
poorly consolidated sediments consisting of terrigenous materials eroded coral
materials and some volcanic materials in the old valleys forming alluvial channels
Some of these channels are 100 feet or more deep and vary from a few tens to

2 2 Existing Site Pavement Conditions
Ala Moana Boulevard is a major roadway extending from about Fort Street in the
west to the intersection with Kalakaua Avenue in the east on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The approximate limits for this phase of the resurfacing project generally extend from
about Piikoi Street in the west to the intersection with Kalakaua Avenue in
the east as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
This portion of the Ala Moana Boulevard has gone through various phases of
upgrading and expansion throughout the years The existing roadway within the project
limits is generally a six lane divided roadway except for the additional turn lanes at the
intersection areas Based on our field observations and the information provided the
traffic volume at this portion of the roadway may be considered high with the average
daily traffic count on the order of about 25 000 to 45 000 vehicles in the Year 2006
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in December 2006 to collect general
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under
consideration Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following
subsections herein It should be noted that the term Structural Defects refers to areas
where the pavement deterioration are believed to be caused by failures in the
underlying base subbase and or subgrade materials The term Ride Characteristic
refers to the response of a vehicle when passing at normal speed over the roadway

3 3 Design Subgrade Conditions
As previously mentioned the majority of the borings drilled between Piikoi Street
and Hobron Lane Boring Nos 21 through 28 generally encountered very stiff clayey
silts in the shallow subsurface with soft sandy silts and organic clays in localized areas
Based on our experience with similar clayey subsurface soils we believe that the
subgrade material for this section of roadway would exhibit a design Resistance
R value of about 10 which was adopted for our pavement design analyses
For the section of roadway extending from Hobron Lane to Kalakaua Avenue our
drilled borings Boring Nos 29 through 33 generally encountered medium dense poorly
graded sands and gravelly sands below the existing pavement section Laboratory
Resistance R Value test performed on the near surface sandy soils obtained from our
field exploration indicated that the materials exhibited a R value of 80 The test results
are presented on Plate B 5 of Appendix B Based on the test results a design R value
of 60 was adopted in our pavement analyses for the subgrade materials for this section
of roadway If site grading exposes soils other than those assumed additional tests
should be performed to confirm and or revise the recommended pavement sections for
actual field conditions

to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 or
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
We believe that some of the pavement failures may be attributed to the presence
of soft clayey subsurface soils encountered at the pavement subgrade level Ideally the
soft clayey subsurface soils should be removed and replaced with select granular fill

It should be noted that additional areas requiring structural repairs with new

3 7 Surface Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry all surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage
