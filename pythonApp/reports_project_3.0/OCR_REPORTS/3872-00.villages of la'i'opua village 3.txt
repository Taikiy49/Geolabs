INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Villages of La i opua Village 3 subdivision in the Kealakehe
area within the District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations based on
our review and analyses of the site conditions the grading plan and the subsurface
information obtained for this project These geotechnical recommendations are intended
for site preparation mass grading foundation and pavement design input for the subject
project only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which has
formed the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
appears to be of a a flow type which spread downslope from vents further up on the
mountain flank A a lavas are typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow
surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the
lava flow core
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3872 00 Page 5
Cavities such as lava tubes or other void space are commonly encountered
buried within a a lavas Many of these cavities were formed when the still molten lava
created the structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava The cavities represent
both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets
Lava tubes are formed when molten ava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow
tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow
Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
The basalt lava rock encountered at the site is generally considered to be relatively
permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both the horizontal and vertical
directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures
inter flow contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers The permeability of the
clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission
features
SITE DESCRIPTION
The Villages of La i opua Village 3 subdivision site is located to the east mauka
of Queen Ka ahumanu Highway and immediately northwest of the new Kealakehe High
School in the Kealakehe area of North Kona on the island of Hawaii Presently the site is
bounded by Waiena Road to the west and by undeveloped land of adjacent properties on
the other sides as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our site reconnaissance the project site is located on vacant
undeveloped land situated in a semi arid climate At the time of our reconnaissance the
site was observed to be vegetated with native grass brush and a scattering of trees
Some preliminary field survey work had been completed related to the marking of
property boundaries No access road trails were observed leading into the site from the
existing Waiena Road prior to the trail clearing efforts to provide access to our planned
boring locations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3872 00 Page 6
The site slopes down gently to moderately in undulating form to the west from the
eastern property boundary Existing ground surface elevations at the site range from
about 520 feet MSL at the eastern boundary to about 390 feet MSL at the western
boundary The site exists as undeveloped land covered by a a basalt rock and
vegetation The ground surface exists as a rough and jumbled rocky surface of cobbles
boulders and irregularly shaped rock fragments associated with the surface character of
the lava flows Soil deposits were generally observed to be absent except as localized
pockets of thin soil accumulation derived from erosion and transportation of ash soils
The ash soil where visible may be identified as brown silt with some fine sand
Due to the extremely porous nature of the surface rock layer it appears as though most
of the pre existing thin ash soil had been washed down into the surface clinker formation
by rainfall and erosion During the bulldozer clearing efforts traces of the ash soil were
observed buried in the fractures and void spaces within the surficial rock layers Some of
this soil material was uncovered and brought to the ground surface by the bulldozer work
The presence of the ash soil may be considered to be insignificant based on the limited
amount of soil observed
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our subsurface exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings
to depths ranging from about 15 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface A
Komatsu 355 bulldozer was utilized to provide access to the proposed boring locations
Access trails were prepared by crushing the surface clinker layer combined with some
ripping of the underlying dense rock formation with a single bulldozer shank During trail
clearing a portion of a narrow lava tube of approximately 15 to 20 feet in length and
about 5 feet in diameter was penetrated just beneath the existing ground surface causing
the bulldozer to collapse the structure Based on the occurrence it may be anticipated
that other shallow buried voids may be encountered during the mass grading work The
approximate locations of the borings and lava tube encountered are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3872 00 Page 7
In general our field exploration encountered volcanic rock consisting of medium
hard to very hard basalt with interbedded layers of weaker clinker material The basalt
ranged from severely to slightly fractured and unweathered to slightly weathered The
basalt formations encountered generally consisted of inter layered dense vugular basalt
and medium hard strongly vesicular scoria basalt In addition clinker seams of weakly
cemented basalt fragments with some void space were also encountered Some traces
of a brown silty soil were observed deposited in the near surface fractures of the rock
core recovered
Based on our reconnaissance and observation of the trail clearing effort the
existing ground surface may be underlain by a relatively weak surface rock layer
consisting of jumbled loose deposits of cobble and boulder material grading to more
competent rock with increasing depth The weak surface layer appears to be about 1 to
4 feet in thickness and represents the upper flow surface of an a a lava flow The surface
material generally appears to be easily flattened by a bulldozer and contained some hard
rock requiring ripping as well
Voids and or cavities were encountered at variable depths within the basalt
formation in five of the ten borings drilled at project site In general the cavities
encountered ranged from about 1 to 2 feet in vertical height in the borings and were
encountered at depths ranging from 7 to 22 feet below the existing ground surface
Some of the cavities were encountered embedded within clinker layers making the
detection of the void during drilling difficult It must be emphasized that the vertical and
lateral extents of these cavities are highly variable
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of the field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on seasonal
precipitation groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site
generally consist of basalt rock formation with frequent clinker seams and cavities Due
to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in the basalt lava flows
at the site we recommend that finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to
receive fills be proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment
such as a minimum 20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of 6 passes to aid in
detecting and collapsing near surface cavities In general the basalt rocks generated
from cut areas during site grading may be reused as a source of fill material provided that
they are properly processed moisture conditioned and placed as recommended in the
Site Grading section of this report
In general the cut slopes planned at the project site may be designed with a slope
inclination of 1H 1V or flatter with the exception of the upper 5 feet of the cut extending
down from the pre existing natural ground surface Because the upper 5 feet of natural
ground surface is generally composed of loose clinker rubble the upper 5 feet of the cut
slope should be flattened to an inclination of to account for the less competent
material In addition the upper 2 feet of all fills should be capped with compacted
structural fill materials 6 inch minus materials to serve as a choker layer of fill material
above the larger sized general fill materials and also to facilitate construction of the house
structures and other improvements on the fill materials
Because of the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered at the

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 24 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot
rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks traversing
previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be by bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rock
The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing
in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during
placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes to be made to achieve
proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading by the
performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests

Excavation
Based on our field exploration at the site the project site is underlain by fractured
volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard The volcanic rock near the
existing ground surface is generally porous and friable Therefore it is anticipated
that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the existing ground
surface However excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of
hoerams chipping or blasting
Based on the available subsurface information we anticipate that the excavated
rock materials from near the existing ground surface would experience some
shrinkage on the order of about 10 percent during handling and compaction
depending upon the actual gradation of the processed material We also believe
that the excavated rock materiais would likely break down to boulders and cobbles
with some gravels and few fines However it should be noted that some large
boulders may be generated during excavation and should be expected For the
denser volcanic rocks a volume increase expansion on the order of about
10 percent may be expected when excavated and processed into structural fill
materials
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials and
volume change are based on our visual observation of the existing rock formation
and field data from the borings performed at the site We recommend that

make their own interpretation

House Foundations
We understand that post and beam foundations will be utilized for support of the
future house structures Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project
site we believe that the in situ hard basalt formation and compacted structural fill as a
result of site grading would provide adequate foundation support for the proposed
residential structures The post and beam foundations will be supported by shallow
isolated spread footings bearing on the basalt formation and compacted structural fill For
foundations bearing on the basalt formation and or compacted structural fills an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design of
foundations This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 8 inches below the lowest adjacent
grade Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should
be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of
at least 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope If
foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded
below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines

Drywell Percolation Tests
A total of eight constant head percolation tests were conducted in four drilled
borings Boring 6 and 8 to provide data for design of the proposed drywells
The borings selected for percolation testing were chosen in an effort to test boreholes that
appeared to encounter varying degrees of rock fractures and embedded clinker seams
The results of our percolation tests are presented in Appendix B of this report
Based on the percolation test results it appears that the County of Hawaii
standard drywell disposal capacity of 6 cubic feet per second c f s should be attainable
at this site for drywells constructed of a minimum of 20 feet deep and 5 feet in diameter
Furthermore it appears that in most cases a capacity of up to 10 c f s may be attainable
for the new drywells if it should be necessary to meet site drainage requirements
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava
or the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of
a drywell In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or voids encountered
by the drywell excavation the higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity
It is our experience that drywells on the island of Hawaii may be constructed by
conventional excavation or by drilling methods In general the type of drilling operation
may alter the hydraulic characteristics of formation materials at the edges of the borehole
and may result in significant reduction of the hydraulic conductivity of the drilled hole We
envision that the drilling fluid may seal the permeable surfaces of the drywell Therefore
pressure washing or water jetting to remove the seal should be included in the
specifications if the drywells are to be installed by drilling The contractor should forward
the proposed excavation method and equipment to Geolabs Hawaii for evaluation and
approval Geolabs Hawaii should review the submittal and assess the impact of drywell
construction procedures on the permeability characteristics

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling ten
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 at the approximate locations shown on

the existing ground surface They were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Project Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were performed by using the constant head method in four of the
drilled borings Boring 6 and 8 The drilled borings have a diameter of 4
inches and depths of about 6 and 19 feet at the time of testing A water truck was used to
pump water into the test holes The flow rate was recorded by timing and volume
calculations as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved The head levels within the
test borings were monitored by an electric water level sounder during the pump injection
of water The results of the percolation tests are presented on Plates B 1 through B 4
