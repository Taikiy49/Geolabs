INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering services
performed for the proposed resurfacing and reconstruction of Mokapu Saddle Road from
N Kalaheo Avenue to Mikiola Drive in the Kaneohe area on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location of the project area is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings from our site reconnaissance and our review
of available information and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our analyses for the proposed project These geotechnical findings and
recommendations are intended for pavement design purposes only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
GEOLABS HAWAII
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered
the basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes
consisting of detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered
the natural condition of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the
central portion of valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older
alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial
deposits or by the soillike weathered basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban
development has since altered the natural formation of the area

Methodology of Pavement Design
The methodology used to determine the required AC overlay thickness for
rehabilitation and thickness of reconstructed pavement sections generally follows that
outlined in the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation Material Testing and
Research Branch s Pavement Design Manual dated May 1995
In the method outlined for AC overlays the strength of the existing AC and base
materials are evaluated based on the type and severity of the distresses observed For
example the existing AC may be reduced to that of ACB untreated base UTB or may
be assumed to have the similar strength characteristics as new AC
GEOLABS HAWAII
Reconstruction of portions of the existing pavements will be required in those
areas where the observed distresses were likely caused by failures in the underlying
base subbase and or subgrade materials New pavement sections were determined
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the design manual which is based
on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of
Transportation

The subgrade should be moisture conditioned to between 2 and 5 percent above

and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction Development of good shoulder drainage
along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly
recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of
the pavements
We envision that an edge drain will be needed for the untreated permeable base
layer to collect and daylight the infiltrated water from the pavement structural section
The edge drain should consist of a 6 inch PVC perforated pipe with perforations facing
down surrounded by an untreated permeable base The permeable separator
non woven filter fabric conforming to Section 648 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications
for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 should be placed around the
untreated permeable base to reduce the potential for migration of fines into the drainage
materials In addition a permeable separator should also be placed between the
untreated permeable base and aggregate subbase Care should be exercised when

placing the subbase base and or construction of the surface course to avoid damaging
the edge drain collector pipe A typical edge drain detail is provided on Plate 2
