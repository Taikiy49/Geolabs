In accordance with your request we have performed a seismic hazard analysis and
site response evaluation with respect to seismic design considerations for the Kahala Nui
project This letter report presents the findings of our seismic hazard analysis for the
project site and presents our recommendations for seismic analysis and design of the
structures for the above project
The purpose of our seismic hazard analysis and site response evaluation is to
establish appropriate design elastic response spectra for the project site taking into
consideration the proximity of the site to seismic sources major historical earthquakes
regional seismicity and site specific subsurface conditions A response spectrum is used
by the structural engineer to perform an analysis of the structure given the dynamic
loading conditions and the ground motions at the foundation elevations
The response spectrum is a graphical representation relating the maximum
response of a single degree of freedom damped elastic system with different fundamental
periods to dynamic forces The anticipated earthquake ground motions and dynamic
response of the subsurface materials at the project site are represented by site specific
elastic response spectra

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions These later eruptions are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
the detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time
chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or
further weathering of the material resulting in old alluvium Chemical processes also
cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting
in a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion
continues in areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This continued
erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older alluvial
deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
recent alluvium generally are transient in terms of geologic time In addition their
consistency and density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated
deposits

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Field explorations at the project site were conducted by and
Dames Moore 1992 Information on the subsurface conditions at the project site are
contained in the report entitled Geotechnical Engineering Exploration Kahala Nui
Kahala Oahu Hawaii dated August 6 2001 prepared by Geolabs Inc The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site is underlain by a relatively thick deposit of mixed alluvial
and colluvial materials consisting of rock fragments boulders and clay with sand starting
at or near the existing ground level extending to depths ranging from about 40 to 65 feet
below the existing ground surface These alluvial and colluvial deposits generally are
older in geologic age and appear to be more consolidated with increasing depth It
should be noted that the consolidated nature of the alluvial and colluvial deposits
encountered at the project site makes the consolidated materials to behave much like rock
rock like with respect to dynamic response during a seismic event
Below the alluvial and colluvial deposits basalt rock formation was encountered
and extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately 75 feet below the existing
ground surface As mentioned previously we believe that the basalt formation
encountered in the borings likely is the basalt formation of the Koolau Volcanic Series
which forms the stratigraphic base of the Island of Oahu
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of the field
exploration However subterranean seepage is common in the area due to the sloping
terrain and the proximity of the two drainage canals bordering the site In addition
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall time of year seasonal
precipitation surface water runoff storm surge conditions and other factors

In general most earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by the
movement of the rigid tectonic plates comprising the crust and upper mantle lithosphere
of the earth In contrast most of the earthquake activity experienced in Hawaii is caused
by volcanic activity related to the underground movement of molten lava magma As a
result significant earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs just prior to volcanic
eruptions at the earth s surface This type of earthquake activity is indicative of the
movement and upwelling of underground magma deep within the faults and fractures
comprising the volcano structure
The earthquakes on the Island of Hawaii are closely related to the volcanic activity
associated with the active volcanoes and Loihi that compose a
large portion of the Island of Hawaii Most of the smaller earthquakes volcanic
earthquakes may occur as a result of the underground movement of magma and surface
eruptions associated with the presently active volcanoes Other earthquakes including
the larger and more devastating ones tectonic earthquakes occur within the areas of
structural weakness located near the base of volcanoes or deeper within the earth s crust
beneath the Island of Hawaii
Earthquakes may originate beneath volcanic land surfaces and from undersea or
offshore source regions such as rift and fracture zones Some earthquakes in Hawaii
also are caused by the burden of the island masses overlying the lithosphere which
cause the lithosphere to sag beneath the immense weight The isostatic adjustment of
the Pacific Plate lithosphere beneath the islands burden causes some moderate to high
intensity earthquakes The last earthquake to occur as a result of the lithosphere
adjustment was the 1973 Honomu Earthquake Magnitude 2 beneath the
Hamakua Coast of the Island of Hawaii This earthquake was widely felt on the Island of
Oahu located approximately 200 miles away Other lithospheric origin earthquakes have
occurred closer to the Island of Oahu in the past such as the Maui Earthquake of 1938
M6 5 centered north of the Island of Maui and the 1871 Lanai Earthquake M6 8 both
of which were felt on the Island of Oahu The 1871 Lanai Earthquake reportedly caused
extensive but mostly minor damage on the Island of Oahu to mainly un reinforced
masonry structures
Because the movement of underground magma is responsible for most
earthquakes in Hawaii significant earthquake activity is typically concentrated in the
vicinity of the younger volcanoes of Hawaii such as those comprising the Island of Hawaii
and to a lesser extent the Island of Maui Typically the Island of Hawaii experiences
thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes are so smail that they
may only be detected by seismograph instrumentation However some of the larger
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few have been known to cause mostly
minor to moderate damage to structures

DESIGN GROUND MOTIONS
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC current code adopted by the
City and County of Honolulu the Island of Oahu is located within Seismic Zone 2A with
an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 15g Alternatively site specific design
ground motions may be developed based on a site specific probabilistic seismic hazard
analysis PSHA or determined based on seismic hazard maps In any case the
site specific design ground motions should be based on a 10 percent probability of
exceedance in 50 years This level of seismic hazard is equivalent to an annual
probability of occurrence of 0 00211 events per year or a return period of approximately
475 years
As mentioned previously the subsurface conditions at the project site generally
consist of relatively thick deposits of mixed alluvial and colluvial materials The mixed
alluvial and colluvial deposits generally appear to be older and more consolidated with
increasing depth The basalt rock formation encountered below the mixed alluvial and
colluvial deposits appear to be of the Koolau Volcanic Series which forms the
stratigraphic base of the region The majority of the subsurface materials encountered in
the borings drilled for the project semi consolidated mixed alluvial and colluvial deposits
and basalt formation appear to have rock like characteristics in terms of their bulk shear
modulus and damping ratios The limited extent thickness less than 20 to 30 feet below
the ground level of the soil like materials above the rock like subsurface materials
generally consist of the unconsolidated portions of the alluvial and colluvial deposits and
the surface fill materials
Based on our review of the site specific ground conditions and the specified annual
probability of occurrence we believe that the design ground motions for the project site
may be adequately determined based on the seismic hazard maps produced by the
United States Geological Survey Seismic Hazard Mapping Project 1996 Based on the
seismic hazard maps for 10 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years we believe
that a design peak ground acceleration of 0 13g may be adopted as the basis of ground
motions at the Kahala Nui project site

ELASTIC RESPONSE SPECTRUM
It should be noted that site specific response spectra are dependent on the
magnitude of the earthquake and the distance of the seismic event to the project site in
addition to subsurface conditions As indicated previously there are no known significant
mapped fault zones located on the Island of Oahu As a result we do not anticipate
seismic events to occur within 5 miles of the project site therefore we do not expect that
the site specific elastic response spectrum to be affected by near source effects from
seismic events
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the
rock like nature of the subsurface materials within close proximity to the ground surface
we believe that the site specific elastic response spectrum 5 percent damping ratio
presented on Plate 3 of this letter report may be used for seismic analysis and design of
the project The general shape of the response spectrum curve is a simplification of the
shape developed by Ugas Seed and Lysmer 1976 The shape corresponds to the
expected shape presented in the ATC3 06 and Uniform Building Codes 1988 1991 and
1994 for sites underlain by shallow soils with rock like subsurface conditions
Based on previous studies we believe that the site specific elastic response
spectrum presented on Plate 3 generally should encapsulate the average ground motions
produced by earthquakes recorded on the Island of Hawaii The site specific elastic
response spectrum 5 percent damping ratio is presented in tabular format on Plate 4 of
this letter report for ease of reference
To better illustrate the relationship between spectral acceleration Sa pseudo
relative velocity Sy and pseudo displacement Sp as a function of the undamped
natural period of vibration we have plotted the site specific elastic response spectrum for
5 percent damping ratio in tripartite form on Plate 5 The tripartite response spectrum
takes into consideration the dependence of spectral accelerations on the amplitude
frequency and duration of earthquake ground motions
