SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surface fills and 
cultivated soils approximately 1 to 3 feet thick overlying stiff to hard alluvial soils extending 
down to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  
Based on our laboratory test results, the near-surface clayey soils generally exhibit 
low to very high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil 
moisture contents. Based on the preliminary mass grading plan provided, we anticipate 
the near-surface soils underlying the planned buildings and pavements will consist of the 
clayey soil with low to very high shrink/swell potential. Considering the lightly loaded 
structures planned for the Phase 6 development, it is anticipated that the low to very high 
shrink-swell potential of the on-site soils could result in structural distresses to the new 
buildings and pavements. To reduce the risk of structural distress, we recommend 
implementing special subgrade preparation procedures during construction.  
After the clearing and grubbing operations to remove the existing vegetation, 
debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials, the upper 12 inches of the 
cultivated soils below the existing ground surface should be removed and disposed of 
properly off-site (or used in landscaped areas, if appropriate) because they contain an 
appreciable amount of organic materials.  
Upon removal of the upper 12 inches of cultivated soils, we recommend 
over-excavating an additional 12 inches of soils in areas designated to receive fill and 
moisture-conditioning the soils to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content for use as general fill. Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soils as 
general fill, the bottom of the over-excavation should be scarified to a minimum depth of 
12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative compaction. It should 
be noted that the 2-foot thick reworked soil layer should also be constructed below the 
finished subgrade and pad grade in cut areas.   
The intent of the above site preparation recommendations is to create a 2-foot 
thick reworked clayey soil layer to close-up the existing shrinkage cracks observed 
throughout the site and reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing 
the clayey soils with elevated moisture contents and compaction reduction. In addition, 
the reworked clayey soils with elevated moisture contents would decrease the affinity of 
the dry clayey soil for water and create a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the 
underlying dry clayey soil.

Due to the hard, dry and friable in-situ clayey soils, the materials generated from 
the over-excavation and scarification process and/or from cut areas would likely contain 
large clumps. To moisture-condition the soils effectively, the clayey clumps should be 
thoroughly broken down and mixed by the compaction equipment, such as large 
sheepsfoot compactors and/or large dozers, until achieving a relatively uniform moisture 
condition. Contractors bidding on this project should anticipate processing the on-site soils 
to break down the hard clayey clumps and achieve a relatively uniform moisture content. 
The on-site clayey soils should not be allowed to dry out and should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site 
clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks develop, the soils should be scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and compacted again. 
Based on our field exploration and analyses, we believe the lightly loaded 
residential building structures may be supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation 
system bearing on the 2-foot thick reworked soil layer or general fills placed below the 
finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. 
We recommend using 12-inch high Waffleboxes to provide a stiffer foundation system in 
order to reduce differential movement and resulting angular distortion. We also 
recommend installing a vapor barrier underneath the Waffleboxes and extending it beyond 
the slab perimeter a minimum of 2 feet to reduce water infiltration beneath the slab 
foundations. 
It is critical to keep the subgrade soil moist during the entire mass grading and site 
work phases. The individual homeowners and Homeowner’s Associations should be made 
aware of the potential for recurrence of shrinkage cracks, and appropriate maintenance 
measures should be implemented to reduce the potential for recurrence of shrinkage 
cracks around the building foundation. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Ho`opili Development – Phase 6 project in the Ewa area on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project

This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting

These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of site 
grading, building foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, and pavements only. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this report.

Project Considerations

H-1 Freeway and west of Fort Weaver Road in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The Ho`opili Development is a master-planned community encompassing

preliminary mass grading plan provided, we anticipate site grading work consisting of

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, 
lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a 
broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the 
southeastern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time 
through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is generally situated at the west-central portion of the Ho`opili 
Development, southeast and northwest of the future Phases 2 and 4 development sites, 
respectively, in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is generally 
bordered on all sides by existing agricultural land.  
At the time of our field exploration, the site was generally being used for 
commercial farming of various vegetable and herb crops; however, portions of the 
farming areas appeared to have been recently tilled possibly in preparation for future 
planting. Several undeveloped pockets of vacant land were observed throughout the 
project site with light vegetation ground cover consisting of weed grasses.  
Several unpaved access roads were observed traversing the site and were 
generally covered with coralline sand and gravel. Field observations during our 
exploration indicated the surface soils at the project site were dry and friable. In 
addition, we observed shrinkage cracks about 6 inches deep at the existing ground 
surface at various locations throughout the project site at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on our site observations and topographic survey map provided, the project 
site generally slopes gently down from north to south at an average slope gradient of 
about 2 percent. Based on the information provided, ground surface elevations range 
from about +125 to +108 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the northwestern and 
southeastern portions of the project site, respectively.

3.1.2 Site Perimeter Cut-Off Trench 
Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa/Kapolei 
area during prolonged dry weather conditions and were observed at the existing 
ground at the time of our field exploration. Since shrinkage cracks tend to be a 
conduit for water infiltration into the subsurface soils, we recommend implementing 
special site preparation procedures consisting of installing a cut-off trench along the 
perimeter of the grading limits at the project site. We recommend a cut-off trench at 
least 3 feet deep and 5 feet wide consisting of the on-site clayey soils placed and 
compacted with elevated moisture contents, as illustrated on Plates 5.1 and 5.2. As 
an alternative, the cut-off trench may be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength 
Material (CLSM). 
The on-site soils used for this cut-off trench should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The intent of this cut-off trench is to create a barrier to reduce water 
infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil from the shrinkage cracks on the 
adjacent properties.

3.1.7 Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
As indicated above, we observed shrinkage cracks at the existing ground surface of 
the project site based on field observations. In general, shrinkage cracks will 
develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content 
as the soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions. The 
development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the 
prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Kapolei/Ewa area resulting in large 
fluctuations in the soil moisture content.

over-watering of the graded surfaces should be avoided to reduce the potential for 
saturation and softening of the surface soils.

Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300.0), were performed (by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc.) on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of 
the on-site soils. Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at 
the project site have resistivity values ranging from approximately 870 to 900 ohm-cm 
and pH values ranging from approximately 5.5 to 6.8 within the upper 8 feet of soils, 
corresponding to a corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) based on the guidelines 
provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply.  
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a 
corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Levels of the chloride and sulfate content of the on-site soils encountered near 
the existing ground surface are relatively low on the order of about 93 parts per million 
(ppm) and 280 ppm or less, respectively. Based on the chloride and sulfate content 
tests performed on the on-site soils, we believe that either Type I or Type II cement may 
be used for concrete in contact with the ground planned for the project. It may be 
appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results 
and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

3.7.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
As mentioned above, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry and 
friable and exhibit low to very high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend properly scarifying the subgrade 
to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioning to about 2 to 4 percent above 
the optimum moisture content, and recompacting between 85 and 90 percent 
relative compaction, as specified in Section 3.1.1 – Site Preparation herein. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to 
re-compacting. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to

3.7.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
The 18-inch capping layer below the pavement sections should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent 
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material requirements 
for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 31 and 30, 
respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, 
City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test imported fill 
materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the 
project site for the intended use. 
3.7.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirement for the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least 
95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course 
should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional check for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the

3.7.8 Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to 
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend 
extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base 
or subbase layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water 
into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be constructed to 
collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term performance, we 
recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the paved/landscaped 
areas.

thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the 
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench 
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent 
relative compaction.

Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed 
water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It 
is highly recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert 
discharge away from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near 
the foundations and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. 
Planters next to foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and 
drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 106, extending to depths of about 20.1 
to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, four test pits, designated as 
Test Pit Nos. 101 through 104, were excavated to a depth of about 10 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Four bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as 
Bulk-101 through Bulk-104, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring, test pit, and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. The test pits were 
excavated using a Hitachi Zaxis 450 Excavator. 
Our engineers classified the materials encountered in the borings and test pits by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling and 
excavation operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were 
further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings and 
Test Pits, Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Eight Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings and Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of 
the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Ten one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Four Expansion Index of Soil tests were performed on selected samples in 
general accordance with ASTM D4829. The test is a standardized test used to 
determine the expansive characteristics of the soil. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-4. 
Sixteen one-dimensional swell or settlement potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is 
remolded to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to 
simulate the anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle 
until it has stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil 
sample to evaluate its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test 
results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-8. 
Four Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples in accordance with ASTM D2850 to evaluate 
the undrained shear strength of the soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” samples. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-9 through B-12. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method A) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and 
moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-13 through 
B-16.
