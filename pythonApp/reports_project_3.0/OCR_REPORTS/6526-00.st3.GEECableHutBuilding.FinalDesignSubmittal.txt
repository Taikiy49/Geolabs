encountered the piles would not be able to penetrate through the limestone formation

elevation of 7 0 meters MLLW to facilitate driving of the piles to the design tip elevation

Regional Geology
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island
archipelago which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming
the Mariana Trench The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches which extend from
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench Mariana Trench Yap Trench
and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces
the southern mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The island
formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic
rocks and conglomerates tuffs and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene Epochs
Lava flows and limestone formations of Miocene Epoch make up the majority of
the southern one third of the island The plateaus of the northern portion of the island
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level During the
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits Tectonic activity has lifted these
coral algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau
The central portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic
materials of the southern mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau
The project site at Apra Harbor is located in the south central portion of the island The
local geology is dominated by the volcanic materials and associated materials of the
southern mountains Due to surface drainage courses of the southern mountain terrain
the near surface soils in the area consist of Holocene age alluvium and associated
deposits such as coralline detritus alluvium and lagoonal deposits These younger
unconsolidated soil deposits generally are more susceptible to liquefaction and slope
stability issues especially around the shoreline areas

Seismic Design Considerations
The project site will be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for
the potential for soil liquefaction Seismic design of the proposed project will be based
on the Unified Facilities Criteria Seismic Design of Building 04 which
references the 2009 International Building Code IBC 2009 and UFC Structural Load
Data 01
Earthquakes and Seismicity
Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms the Mariana Trench
The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately
20 earthquakes exceeding a magnitude of 7 0 since 1902 Of these events
fifteen occurred prior to 1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date
Several of the larger faults and fault zones that traverse the Island of Guam
include the following Pago Bay Adelup Point Fault River
Valley Fault Zone and Tamuning Barrigada Fault In addition to the larger
faults several faults and fault zones within proximity to the project site include
the Cabras Fault and Cocos Fault
Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction is a phenomenon during which loose saturated cohesionless soils
such as sand and non plastic silt temporarily lose their shear strengths due to
the build up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress
applications induced by earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated
sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil
tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water
pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up
of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is
reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could
equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil

Significant factors known to affect the liquefaction potential of soils are the
characteristics of the materials such as grain size distribution and relative
density the plasticity of the fine grained soils the degree of saturation the initial
stresses overburden pressures acting on the soils and the characteristics of
the earthquakes such as the intensity and duration of ground shaking The major
factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of soil deposits are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
Because the project site will be subjected to seismic activity we have evaluated the
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site The subsurface conditions at the site
generally consist of surface fills over very soft lagoonal deposits and weathered
limestone formation Based on the subsurface conditions encountered the
subsurface materials at the site are not susceptible to liquefaction
The very soft lagoonal deposits consist of clayey silts Based on our laboratory
tests the clayey silts have liquid limits LL of about 34 and plasticity index PI of
about 10 In general materials with LL of 32 or greater and PI of 7 or greater are
not considered susceptible to liquefaction Therefore the lagoonal deposits
encountered at the site are not susceptible to liquefaction because of their cohesive
soil behavior
The weathered limestone encountered generally consists of soft to medium hard
formation with pockets of sandy and silty soils The sandy and silty soils
encountered in the limestone formation generally occur in small pockets and
generally do not appear as continuously layered materials Therefore we believe

Pile Foundations
The new Cable Hut building will consist of a one story structure with a finished
floor elevation of about 2 7 meters MLLW We understand the new building will have
structural column loads dead plus live of about or 110 kips
Based on the moderately heavy column loads and the presence of very soft
lagoonal deposits we recommend driven pile foundations deriving support from skin
friction and or end bearing in the weathered limestone formation be used to support the
structure Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions piles extending through the
upper fill crust and compressible lagoonal deposits and embedded in the limestone
formation to a depth of approximately 11 meters below the ground surface will be required
to develop the recommended capacity to support the proposed structure

Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression
of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of
the pile supported foundations to be less than 13 mm with differential settlements
between columns supported on piles not exceeding about 6 mm We believe that

In general the 420 mm octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles should be
driven full depth to the estimated tip elevation of about 8 5 meters MLLW Refusal

be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is

Floor Slabs
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered floor slabs for the new building
may consist of concrete slabs on grade However we recommend increasing the
building slab thickness and incorporating steel reinforcement into the concrete
a thickness of at least 125 mm and reinforced with No 13 metric designation
reinforcing bars spaced at 300 mm in each direction
For interior building slabs not subjected to machinery vibration all rooms except
the Generator Room we recommend placing a minimum 100 mm thick layer of
cushion fill consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the
slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs
and would serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for future
moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier should be provided on top of the open graded gravel
cushion fill layer Interior walls should be designed to incorporate some flexibility to
accommodate a small amount of possible ground movements

layer of aggregate subbase course below the slabs in lieu of the 100 mm thick gravel
cushion layer The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural
slabs supported on 150 mm of aggregate subbase a modulus of subgrade reaction of
about 543 kilopascals per centimeter of deflection 200 pounds per cubic inch may be
used for the slab resting on the compacted aggregate subbase
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend providing a minimum 100 mm thick cushion layer of aggregate subbase
course below the slabs unless the slab subgrade exposes the existing granular fill The
aggregate subbase course should consist of crushed aggregates compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal
to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed properly off site to reduce the potential for contamination of
the excavated materials designated to be reused as fill and or backfill If soft or wet
soils are encountered during clearing over excavation may be required to remove
the soft or wet materials to expose firm soils The resulting over excavation should
be backfilled with compacted granular fill material
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with granular fill material placed in 200 mm loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
After demolition and site clearing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 200 mm moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
