The existing pavement structure generally consisted of 5 to 6 inches of PCC over 
silty and clayey soil subgrade.  The silty and clayey soils were generally medium stiff to 
stiff and moist to very moist.   
Based on the assumed traffic data and the subgrade conditions encountered, the 
new rigid pavement sections for the project should consist of the following:  
7.0 inches of PCC on compacted subgrade (Ragsdale Place) 
7.0 inches of PCC on compacted subgrade (Kamuela Place) 
7.0 inches of PCC on compacted subgrade (Kumu Street/Place) 
Due to the relatively high the in-situ moisture of the soils (40 to 50 percent) 
encountered at each area, we believe that it would be difficult to achieve a relative 
compaction of 95 percent or higher without significant aeration of the subgrade soils; 
therefore, we recommend reducing the compaction requirement to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  If it is still difficult to achieve a relative compaction of 
90 percent, we recommend compacting the pavement subgrade to a firm condition and 
placing a woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi HP370, on the subgrade. 
The existing roadways are located on steep inclines.  Based on our experience, we 
have observed soft soils near the downslope end of roadways on steep inclines.  We 
believe that the soils become saturated as a result of seepage water flowing down the 
slope and collecting at the point where the roadway gradient flattens out.  To reduce 
saturation of the subgrade soils at the downslope end of the roadways, we recommend 
placing a transverse subdrain (across the entire roadway width) near the bottom of the 
sloping roadway. In general, the subdrain should be a minimum of 18 inches wide and 
12 inches deep below the pavement subgrade.  The subdrain should consist of a 6-inch 
PVC perforated pipe (with perforations facing down) near the bottom of the trench.  The 
perforated pipe should be surrounded by No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) to reduce the 
potential for migration of fines into the drainage material.  The text of this report should be 
referred to for detailed discussions and specific pavement design recommendations.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  The project site is at the southern flank of 
the Koolau Mountain Range. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.  
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited 
as alluvium.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The design procedures used in determining the new rigid pavement sections 
discussed herein are based on the “Guide for Design of Pavement Structures” (1993) 
developed by the American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials 
(AASHTO). 
3.2 
Design Traffic Loading Conditions 
We understand that traffic counts and truck distribution information will not be 
available for the project. The roadways are in residential areas and are not subjected to 
bus traffic; therefore, we have assumed that these pavements will support relatively few 
vehicular loads over the design life.  Based on the above, we believe that the Equivalent 
Single Axle Loads (ESALs) in these areas should be no more than 1,000,000 
(25,000 ESALs per year) over 40-year design life of the pavement. 
3.3 
Design Subgrade Conditions 
 Laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed 
on bulk samples at each area to evaluate the swelling potential and pavement support 
characteristics of the pavement subgrade soils.  The samples were remolded to near 
the optimum moisture content and saturated.  In addition, DCP testing was conducted in 
the borings to estimate the in-situ CBR value of the subgrade soils.  Due to the very 
moist to moist conditions of the subgrade soils encountered at each area, we believe 
that aerating the subgrade soils to achieve optimum moisture may not be feasible. 
Therefore, we have used the estimated in-situ CBR values to correlate an 
Effective Modulus of Subgrade Reaction, k, for use in our rigid pavement analysis. 
Since the subgrade soils consist of fine-grained soils, a Loss of Support value of 2 was 
used to determine a Reduced Effective Modulus of Subgrade Reaction.

Design Pavement Section 
To reduce the potential for encountering existing underground utilities during 
pavement construction, we recommend placing the new PCC pavement section directly 
on the silty/clayey subgrade soils.  Placing the PCC pavement section on the subgrade 
soils would reduce the amount and depth of excavation.  Based on the ESALs and the 
reduced effective modulus of subgrade reaction, the following rigid pavement sections 
may be used for the project. 
Ragsdale Place Rigid Pavement Section 
7.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete (Flexural Strength = 650 psi) 
7.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness Over Compacted Subgrade 
Kamuela Place Rigid Pavement Section 
7.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete (Flexural Strength = 650 psi) 
7.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness Over Compacted Subgrade 
Kumu Street/Place Rigid Pavement Section 
7.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete (Flexural Strength = 650 psi)

Typically, pavement subgrade soils are compacted to a minimum of 95 percent

are located in moderate rainfall environments.  We believe that it would be difficult to 
achieve a relative compaction of 95 percent or higher without significant aeration of the 
subgrade soils.  It is not feasible or practical to aerate the subgrade soils since this 
would disrupt the local vehicular traffic for an extended period of time.  Due to the high

3.5 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subgrade soils. It should be noted that the silty/clayey subgrade soils 
are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. Therefore, special 
attention should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements. As 
a minimum, the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.  
The existing roadways are located on steep inclines.  Based on our experience, 
we have observed soft soils near the downslope end of roadways (transition between 
AC and PCC pavements) located on steep inclines.  We believe that the soils become 
saturated as a result of seepage water flowing down the slope and collecting at the 
point where the roadway gradient flattens out.  To reduce saturation of the subgrade 
soils at the downslope end of the roadways, we recommend placing a transverse 
subdrain (across the entire roadway width) near the bottom of the sloping roadway. In 
general, the subdrain should be a minimum of 18 inches wide and 12 inches deep 
below the pavement subgrade.  The subdrain should consist of a 6-inch PVC perforated 
pipe (with perforations facing down) near the bottom of the trench surrounded by 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation). A non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be placed around the No. 3B Fine gravel to reduce 
the potential for migration of fines into the drainage material.  A typical subdrain detail is 
provided on Plate 4 for reference.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation by drilling 
and sampling eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 201, 202, 301, 302, and 501 
through 504, extending to depths of about 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 
through 2.5. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-8. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing was performed in the boring 
locations to obtain pavement support characteristics of the in-situ soils. The tests were 
performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone Penetrometer to a depth of about 
1.4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the 
lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The 
upper rod holds a 17.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning 
of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone 
is just below the testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. 
The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the 
strength of the soil at the test location.  The number of blows for a minimum of 1-inch 
penetration is recorded.  The process is continued until the desired depth of testing is 
reached or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as millimeters 
per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the DCP Logs, 
Plates C-1.1 through C-8.2.
