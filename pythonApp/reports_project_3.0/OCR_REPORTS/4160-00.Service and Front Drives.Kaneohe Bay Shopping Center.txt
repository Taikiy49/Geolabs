INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the service and front drives of the Kaneohe Bay Shopping Center in the
District of Kaneohe on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of pavements only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
W O 4160 00 Page 4
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered
the basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes
consisting of detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered
the natural condition of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the
central portion of the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or
weathering of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the
older alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time

deposits or by the soil like weathered basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Possible Causes of Distress
Based on the results of our exploration it is our opinion that the pavement distress
is due to one or more of the following
e Inadequate pavement section for heavy vehicle traffic
Inadequately compacted base course
Softening of the subgrade soils from poor drainage
Penetration of the pavement section into the subgrade soil
Asphalt pavement deterioration
These above listed possible causes of distress are discussed in the following
paragraphs
The original pavement section was based on subgrade soils compacted to
95 percent relative compaction However based on the very high natural moisture
content of the on site subgrade soils compaction to 95 percent relative compaction may
not have been achieved in the subgrade soils Based on a subgrade with a lower density
and higher moisture content a thicker pavement section may have been required
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4160 00 Page 10
Based on the low blow counts and dry densities encountered in this exploration it
appears that portions of the base course material may not have been adequately
compacted This would reduce the structural capacity of the pavement section
Based on the presence of surface water ponding and the presence of groundwater
in the boring at the southern end of the service drive at the time of the exploration it
appears that the project site is not graded to drain water away from the pavements
During medium to heavy precipitation surface water ponding can also be observed at
other areas of both drives The nearly level grades and the low occurrence of subsurface
drainage inlets contribute to the surface water drainage problem Poor surface drainage
allows water to infiltrate into the pavement section reducing the pavement strength The
high groundwater level encountered in Boring No 2 suggests that significant surface
water infiltration has occurred at the project site and subsurface drainage is poor
The poor surface drainage may have led to softening of the subgrade and the
softening of the subgrade may have in turn led to penetration of the pavement section
into the underlying subgrade soil This appears to be the situation at the front drive as the
lower 6 inches of base course contains a high percentage of clay material
The original pavement may not have not been designed for heavy truck traffic
Therefore pavement damage may have occurred due to the inadequately designed
pavement section The pavement damage may have also been accentuated by the
problems associated with poor surface drainage
The original pavement is over 20 years in age Normally pavements require
overlays every 10 years due to deterioration of the asphalt pavement wearing surface
Unravelling of the asphalt pavement surface can normally be attributed to its
deterioration However this does not account for the major distress observed at this time
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4160 00 Page 11
Recommended Pavement Design
Based on the high natural moisture content of the on site clayey subgrade soils
compaction of the subgrade and the pavement materials above 90 percent relative
compaction will likely be difficult Therefore we recommend that the pavement design be
based on in situ field conditions of the subgrade soil and base course compacted to a
minimum 90 percent relative compaction Also due to the potential for the on site clayey
soils to become saturated we recommend that the pavement design be based on the
soaked strength of the soil at in situ field conditions
Pavement sections normally consist of thick sections of base course and select
borrow subbase material and thin sections of asphalt pavement However air drying and
compaction of the clayey soils may be difficult due to the high moisture content of the
clayey soils and the high precipitation in the Kaneohe area Aijso the probability of
encountering of underground utilities during construction is greater for thicker pavement
sections Therefore we recommend that the pavement design incorporate thicker
asphalt pavement layers and asphalt treated base course layers to avoid exposing the
underlying clayey soils This will also likely allow for easier and faster construction as
reworking of the on site clayey soils will be reduced

After excavation to allow for the above recommended pavement section the
exposed existing base course should be scarified to a depth of 9 inches below grade
moisture conditioned to about optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
Due to the varying thickness of the base course there may be areas where the
underlying clayey soils are exposed during excavation or scarification For those areas
that expose the underlying clayey soils or where 90 percent relative compaction cannot
be achieved we recommend over excavation of 12 inches placement of a geotextile
fabric AMOCO 4557 or similar followed by placement of 12 inches of base course
material compacted to 90 percent relative compaction
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the compacted materials due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
recompaction
Aggregate base course materials should meet the material requirements for Base
Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 30 and 31 respectively of the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Department of Public Works City
and County of Honolulu September 1986 Imported fill material should be tested by

The asphalt treated base course should consist of asphalt treated basalt

that a representative from our office observe the site grading operations to evaluate

exploration
Drainage
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Due to the fairly level grade of the area construction of
drainage inlets to a subsurface drainage system may be necessary to accommodate
proper drainage of surface water
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above pavement recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and dry density ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Four 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the soil under a surcharge load The tests were performed on
air dried samples Results of the swell tests are presented on Plate B 1
Six sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification
The results of these tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for
pavement subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 4
