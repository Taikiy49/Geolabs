In general accordance with the project contract documents, we performed wave 
equation analyses to evaluate whether the concrete piles may be driven to the specified 
pile tip elevation with the proposed equipment for the above project. The wave equation 
analyses were conducted based on the information provided by your office, the subsurface 
conditions contained in the Request for Proposal (RFP) documents, and project plans and 
specifications. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is within the existing Haleiwa Small Boat Harbor in the Haleiwa 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project consists of restoring Pier 300 and floating 
docks that were damaged by the tsunami in March 2011. The construction involves 
installing four 24-inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles with a cast length of 
70 feet.  An allowable compressive load capacity of 240 kips per pile is required for the 
project. 
Subsurface conditions presented in the RFP project plans are reflected in the wave 
equation analyses, to account for the various soil/rock conditions encountered in the 
borings. 
Hammer and Driving System 
We understand that a Delmag D19-32 and/or  D30-32 single acting diesel hammer is 
proposed for the pile driving operations. Based on the pile driving equipment data forms 
provided, the Delmag D19-32 hammer has a ram weight of 4,190 pounds and a maximum 
stroke of 10.2 feet for a maximum rated energy of 42,800 foot-pounds at the maximum pump 
setting. The Delmag D30-32 hammer has a ram weight of 6,615 pounds and a maximum

Pile Details 
As mentioned previously, the four specified concrete piles consist of 24-inch 
octagonal precast, prestressed concrete piles with a cast length of 70 feet. We understand 
the piles will be cast from concrete with a minimum 28-day unconfined compressive 
strength of 7,000 pounds per square inch (psi) for the 24-inch octagonal concrete piles. 
Based on precast shop drawings, we understand that the effective prestress level of the 
24-inch octagonal piles is approximately 1,060 psi. Therefore, the maximum allowable 
compression and tensile stresses in the 24-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete 
pile can be calculated to be 4,890 and 1,310 psi, respectively.

Driving Stresses 
Graphs showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during 
driving are provided on Plates 2.1 and 2.2. Please note that the graphs were prepared 
based on kips per square inch (ksi), which is equivalent to 1,000 psi. 
Based on our analysis, the compressive stresses during pile driving for 24-inch 
octagonal concrete piles are less than the allowable compression stresses of 4,890 psi. 
The compression stresses developed during pile driving did not exceed the allowable 
compression stresses for the full range of driving resistance at Pump Settings 1 through 
4 for the Delmag D30-32 diesel hammer. 
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving calculated for 24-inch 
octagonal concrete piles are also less than the allowable tensile stress of 1,310 psi for the 
full range of driving resistance at Pump Settings 1 through 4. 
Based on the above results and the anticipated subsurface conditions, it is our 
professional opinion that the proposed pile driving equipment (Delmag D30-32 diesel 
hammer) is capable of installing the 24-inch octagonal concrete piles to the required tip 
elevation, achieving the specified design ultimate compressive load capacity of 480 kips 
without over-stressing the concrete piles. 
Based on our analyses, we believe that the 24-inch octagonal concrete piles may 
achieve the design intent when installed with the following driving criteria. The driving 
criteria may be modified according to the results of the PDA test.

                   ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS                      
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by         
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer.  The program is based on          
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving         
system, pile and soil under the hammer action.  Under certain conditions,        
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.          
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents        
normal situations.  In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the        
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order.  All of the input      
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time      
when the analysis is performed.  However, input data and therefore results       
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.                           
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.     
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or         
dynamic testing and measurements.  Estimates of bending or other local           
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the       
user.                                                                            
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,       
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity         
assessment of a driven pile.  Soil setup occurring after pile installation       
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those         
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation.  This    
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers.  The GRLWEAP user   
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow    
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil           
resistance and blow count during early restriking.                               
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values.  They MUST be reduced by    
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.      
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the           
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties      
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.
