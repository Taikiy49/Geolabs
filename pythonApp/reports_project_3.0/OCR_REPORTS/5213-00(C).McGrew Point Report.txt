performed for the McGrew Point portion of the Hawaii Regional Housing Privatization
project located in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano
ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope
forming a broad plateau now known as Schofield Plateau
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Mountain
Range which is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava from the Koolau
Volcanic Series McGrew Point is a small peninsula located between the mouths of
Kalauao and Aiea Streams at the East Loch of Pearl Harbor
During the later Pleistocene Epoch ice Age there were many sea level
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a
relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area
were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present
stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments such as Pearl Harbor and the bay margins
resulting in thick deposits of soft and or loose sediments along the coast in the areas
that were formerly valleys and drainage ways
In addition alluvial soils associated with Kalauao and Aiea Streams which
discharge into Pearl Harbor are present along some portions of the project site With
the urbanization of the area man made fills were placed to reclaim marshy areas and
lagoons for development Land development and reclamation type projects within the
last 100 years have brought the area to its present form

3 2 Settlement Monitoring Program
For a deposit of highly compressible soils such as the alluvial and lagoonal
deposits encountered at the project site it should be noted that the estimated
settlement period could vary by as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual
settlement period required Therefore the actual settlement rate should be monitored
and provisions should be made for potential delays in the project construction schedule
if a longer period is required A proper contingency plan should be devised to
accommodate the potential delay in construction schedule and the possible extra costs
that may be involved
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that a minimum of
10 settlement gauges be installed in the fill areas at the project site The approximate
locations of these recommended settlement gauges are shown on Site Plan Plate 2 A
typical settlement gauge detail is provided on Plate 3 for reference The gauges should
be read optically by a professional surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to
Geolabs for a review in a timely manner The settlement gauges should be established
on the existing ground surface prior to the filling operations A baseline elevation of the
gauges should be established by two surveyed readings taken a minimum of 24 hours
apart at the commencement of the grading operations Subsequently readings should
be taken once a week until the settlement rate has stabilized

settlement waiting period recommended we recommend that shallow spread and or
continuous footings be used to support the new structures planned at the project site
As an alternative the foundations for support of the structural loads of the residential

lowest adjacent exterior grade For footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on
slopes the footings should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance

refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Eight One Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized
on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One Consolidation test with time rates of consolidation ASTM D 2435 was
performed on a sample of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the
compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate B 3
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture
content Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 4 through B 6
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 7 through B 9
