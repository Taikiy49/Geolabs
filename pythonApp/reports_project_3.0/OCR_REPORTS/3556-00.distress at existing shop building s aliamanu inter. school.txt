INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the evaluation of the distress observed at the existing Shop Building S
within the Aliamanu Intermediate School in the Salt Lake area on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for input on
remedial measures only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
The project site is located at the existing Shop Building S within the Aliamanu
Intermediate School in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
Substantial wall damage distorted window sills and slab separation were reported at the
subject building A site visit was made on to obtain an overview of the
distress encountered at the subject site Architect
Structural Engineer and Mr Taumaoe School Custodian were
present at the site during our visit
_ Based on our field observations and the memorandum by of
Mitsunaga and Associates Inc dated it is believed that the distress
observed is probably caused by the translational movement of the clay tile wall
independent of the steel columns in which the top two courses of the wall pushed the
flange of the steel column The stress resulted in damage to the wall and concrete
window sill Therefore it was desired to conduct a geotechnical engineering exploration

recommendations for remedial measures pertaining to the distress observed at the

REGI Y
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3556 00 Page 5
The project site is located near the southeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and on
the northeastern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As
the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the groundwater
generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low cones of
ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated
over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase of volcanic activity began Then the
Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its
present level The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from
the older Aliamanu events
The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate from
depressional springs and rain water During periods of little rainfall the shallow ponded
water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the mineral salt content of the
ponded water increased Recently man has developed the crater floor by filling and
grading the area for residential and golf course purposes

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the existing Shop Building S within the Aliamanu
Intermediate School in the Salt Lake area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The subject
building was built in the 1960 s during the 4th Increment development of the Aliamanu
Intermediate School campus Shop Building S is a single story structure constructed of
clay tile and concrete block walls with steel columns and concrete floor Substantial wall
damage distorted window sills and slab separation were observed at the south and
southwest sides of the building as indicated on the Site Plan Plate 2 The area adjacent
to the building distress are generally landscaped with drainage flowing towards the
building foundations Based on the plans provided to us a sewer force main traverses
the area at the southeast corner of the building In general the topography of the area is
relatively level with existing ground surface elevations on the order of about 31 to
32 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 along the south end of the existing Shop
Building S to depths ranging from about 16 to 21 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of these borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered stiff silty clays throughout the depths
of our borings drilled at the subject site Soft soil zones were encountered in Boring
Nos 2 and 3 at depths of approximately 8 and 4 feet below the existing ground surface
respectively Based on our visual observations and laboratory testing the in situ clay
soils at greater depths exhibit high moisture contents ranging from about 40 to 50
percent

Groundwater was not encountered in Boring Nos 1 and 2 drilled during our field
exploration However in Boring No 3 water was encountered at a depth of
approximately 8 feet below the existing ground surface Our field observations and
review of the construction plans indicated that a sewer force main traverses the area in
the vicinity of this boring location It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject
to change due to seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in our field exploration are

testing was performed on selected samples obtained and the test results are presented

DISC ION
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we believe that the
in situ clay soils underlying the clay tile wall along the west side of the building may have
softened due to the accumulation of excess water It is our opinion that the excess water
may have come from the following potential sources
1 Water from the downspout of the building gutter system discharges into the
ground immediately adjacent to the clay tile wall
2 Irrigation water from the landscaped area along the west side of the
building
3 Water from the hose bib located at the south end of the building Ponding
of water at the surface is evident in this area
4 Potential leakage from the sewer force main as suggested by the water
level encountered in Boring No 3
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3556 00 Page 8
These potential sources of water may flow toward the building wall along the west
side as a result of the negative drainage established by the raised grades and reverse
gradient of the landscaping adjacent to the damaged wall Due to the clayey nature of
the subsoils encountered at the project site the subsoils underneath the clay tile wall
have likely softened and lost their bearing strength upon saturation from these potential
sources of water infiltration resulting in appreciable settlements of the clay tile wall
Since the wing wall at the south end might have been subjected to greater amount
of moisture infiltration from the use of the hose bib located in this area settlement of the
wing wall would be more pronounced as indicated by the tilting and separation of the wing
wall from the building We believe that softening of the subsoils has resulted in
settlement of the clay tile wall which translated into lateral wall movement towards the
south side due to the tilting and separation of the wing wall Because of the presence of
the elevated concrete walkways along the east side of the building the underlying soils
are subjected to significantly less moisture infiltration from irrigation water and roof water
runoff Therefore damage to the existing wall along the east side did not appear to be as
extensive
Cc DATION
In order to reduce the potential for the observed distress from recurring it is our
opinion that positive drainage should be re established at the subject building This will
help to reduce the potential for ponding of water which results in bearing strength loss of
the foundation soils due to saturation Therefore we recommend that the following be
implemented at the subject site as early as possible preferably prior to the remedial work
for the existing building distress
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3556 00 Page 9
1 The finished grades of the landscaped area along the west and south sides
of the building should be sloped to shed water away from the walls and
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding
2 Gutter systems should be installed around the building and the discharge
should be diverted away from the walls and foundations
3 Excessive landscape watering near the walls and foundations should be
avoided In addition automatic sprinkler system should be discouraged
since there is a strong potential for over watering with the system left
unattended Otherwise a significantly shorter duration of watering by the
sprinkler system should be implemented
4 Care should be exercised in using the hose bib to avoid flooding the
existing ground below the hose bib In addition the area should be
regraded to provide positive drainage away from the wall and to avoid
ponding of surface water
5 The existing sewer force main should be checked for leakage and or the
source of water encountered in Boring No 3 should be determined to avoid
continued saturation of the subsoils in that area
6 Any new footings required for the remedial work should be designed with an
allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 p s f and embedded a minimum of
2 feet below the lowest adjacent finished grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3556 00 Page 10
These drainage requirements and recommendations are essential for the proper
performance of the building foundation since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength We believe that the potential for
structural distress should be significantly reduced if these measures are properly
implemented in the maintenance of the building structure

LIMITATIONS
The findings and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between
the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until remedial construction work is underway If variations then appear evident it
will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The location of the borings indicated in this report are approximate having been
estimated by taping from the features shown on the Existing Site Plan Details dated
March 15 1960 by Law Wilson Elevations of the borings were estimated by
interpolation from the elevation points shown on the same map The physical locations
and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied
by the methods used
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed
and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths ranging from about 16 to 21 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 thru A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
