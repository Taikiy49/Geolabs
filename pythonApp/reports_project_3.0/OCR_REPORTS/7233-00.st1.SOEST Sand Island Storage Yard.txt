SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered loose to medium dense clayey sands with 
some gravel at the ground surface extending down to about 3 feet below the ground 
surface.  The clayey sand soils are generally underlain by beach sands, existing 
coralline detritus, and lagoonal deposits extending down to the maximum depth 
explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Beach sands, coralline 
detritus, and lagoonal deposits generally consisted of loose to medium dense silty 
sands. We encountered groundwater at about 4.3 to 10 feet below the existing ground 
surface in our boring at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the assumed traffic 
loading, we recommend over-excavating the concrete storage pad areas to a depth of 
at least 12 inches below the bottom of the subbase layer.  The over-excavated 
subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, and re-compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction.  The over-excavation may be backfilled with on-site materials, provided 
that the materials are well-graded granular soils with maximum particle size of 3 inches 
or less and are free of vegetation, expansive soils, rubbish and other deleterious 
materials.  The concrete pad may consist of 6 inches of Portland cement concrete over 
6 inches of compacted aggregate subbase course. 
Based on the anticipated vehicle loading consisting of primarily passenger 
vehicles and light trucks with some forklift traffic, the gravel storage yard areas may 
consist of 6 inches of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, No. 2 gradation) placed over 
recompacted subgrade.  It should be noted that pavements consisting of open-graded 
gravel, such as No. 2 Rock, would likely experience some rutting and erosion.  In 
addition, periodic maintenance consisting of re-grading and placement of additional 
gravel would be required for the unpaved storage yard.  If better performance and 
longevity of the pavement is desired at the storage yard, consideration should be given 
to providing an asphaltic concrete surface. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the design of this project.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Marine Center Relocation for the SOEST project at 
Sand Island Site in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are show on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of site grading and 
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is along the western side of Sand Island Parkway on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  The project consists of a storage yard for the University of Hawaii’s School 
of Ocean and Earth Science and Technology (SOEST).  
The Marine Center Relocation for the SOEST at the Sand Island Site consists of 
providing a fenced storage yard with a concrete pad.  The concrete pad will have a 
footprint of about 58 feet by about 151 feet and will be located at the southern portion of 
the site.  We understand that 40-foot storage containers and various equipment will be 
stored at the site. 
Based on the preliminary grading plans, we envision minor site grading 
consisting of cuts and fills of up to about 1 foot in height will be required to achieve the 
design finished grades at majority of the site.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the extinct 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below the 
Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The Sand Island project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and the 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the area are directly related to 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the 
genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain and its fringing reefs. The subsurface conditions 
in the project vicinity are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral/algal reef deposits 
and related deposits laid during high stands of sea. The coral/algal reef deposits and 
related deposits are inter-layered with terrigenous sediments and late series volcanic 
deposits. The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and 
early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building 
activities ceased approximately 2.5 to 3.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells 
indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the 
cessation of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The growth of the 
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of lagoons formed 
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the 
lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell because less water was

available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher 
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal 
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. The lower sea stands caused 
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial erosion of the 
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively 
high energy conditions within and along the streams. During periods of no significant 
sea level changes, the continued stream action extended the alluvial deltas and fans 
seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments. 
The southern coast of the Island of Oahu forms a broad embayment referred to 
as Mamala Bay. At the head of this bay, the mouths of Kalihi and Nuuanu Streams 
formed an open lagoon behind the extensive fringing reefs on the seaward side of the 
island. This lagoon served as an anchorage for vessels coming to Oahu and lead to the 
settlement of Honolulu (Hawaiian: “sheltered bay”).  
The area now occupied by Sand Island was formerly an extensive area of reef 
flats between the seaward margin of the fringing reef and the lagoon. The flats were 
characterized by an accumulation of detrital material broken from the hard reef front and 
softer organic material from the lagoonal area. 
Around the beginning of the 20th century, a small island was built out on the reef 
flats by using dredge spoils to expand an existing islet. This island was named 
Quarantine Island and was used to quarantine incoming passengers, livestock and 
cargo prior to debarkation onto Oahu. Later, the general area of the present US Coast

2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is along Sand Island Parkway in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The new storage yard will be about 350 feet south from the Sand Island Access 
Road bridge. 
The project site is currently a vacant land.  Vegetation at the site generally 
consists of wild grasses and some kiawe trees.  Based on the topographic survey map, 
the site is generally level, with ground surface elevations ranging from about +8 to 
+10 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).   
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of 
about 11.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the borings drilled, the subsurface conditions at the Sand Island 
storage yard site generally consist of loose to medium dense clayey sands with some 
gravel at the ground surface extending down to about 3 feet below the ground surface. 
The clayey sand soils are generally underlain by beach sands, coralline detritus, and 
lagoonal deposits extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Beach sands, coralline detritus, and lagoonal 
deposits generally consisted of loose to medium dense silty sands.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the earthwork operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification 
process and to confirm whether the exposed subsurface conditions are similar to those 
encountered in our field exploration. 
3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
As previously mentioned, a concrete storage pad will be constructed at the site. 
In general, we recommend that concrete pad subgrades be over-excavated to a 
minimum depth of 12 inches below the bottom of the subbase layer and backfilled 
with well-compacted fill materials. 
After clearing, grubbing, and over-excavation of concrete pad subgrades, the 
subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as 
determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the near-surface granular soils encountered during our field exploration 
should be suitable for use as general fill materials, provided that the maximum 
particle size is less than 3 inches in largest dimension. The on-site granular 
materials generated from the excavations may be used as a source of general fill or 
backfill materials, provided that they are free of vegetation, expansive soils, and 
deleterious materials and screened of the over-sized materials and/or processed to 
meet the above gradation requirements (less than 3 inches in largest dimension). 
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should be non-expansive 
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The select granular fill should be 
well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The material should have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 
20 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D 1883. The material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. Imported fill materials should be tested for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use. 
Where groundwater is encountered (within the excavations), backfill materials 
should consist of free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped on all sides with non-woven filter fabric. 
The free-draining granular materials should be used up to a level of about 
12 inches above the groundwater level to facilitate compaction of the fill materials. 
Aggregate subbase course required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should meet the requirements of Section 30 of the City and 
County Standard Specifications (September 1986). Imported fill materials should be 
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project 
site for the intended use.

Fill materials required for the project should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture

3.2 
Pavement Design 
We understand that the majority of the project site will be used as a gravel 
storage yard.  A concrete pad of about 58 feet by 151 feet will be provided at the 
southern portion of the site.  In general, we anticipate the vehicle loading for the gravel 
storage yard would consist of primarily passenger vehicles and light trucks with some 
forklift loads. Therefore, we have assumed generally light to medium traffic loading 
conditions for pavement design purposes. 
In addition, we have assumed that the pavement subgrade soils will be similar to 
the clayey sands with some gravel encountered during our field exploration with a 
laboratory CBR value of over 85 and swell of less than 1 percent. 
CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade 
materials during construction to confirm that the above design sections are adequate. 
The aggregate subbase course should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
In general, the storage areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavements. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on the site during or after construction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5. The borings extended to depths of 
about 11.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-
mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-7. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557A) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-8 and B-9.
