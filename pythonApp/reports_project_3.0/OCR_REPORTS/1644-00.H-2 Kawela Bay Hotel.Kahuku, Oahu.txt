INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed H 2 Kawela Bay Hotel at the Kuilima
Development at Kahuku on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed in general accordance with
our proposal dated
This report summarizes the findings of the exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based
on the data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and are not intended for construction estimation
or bidding purposes

The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the project site in order to develop a
generalized soil rock data set for the formation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to site grading and foundation
design of the project

The site of the H 2 Kawela Bay Hotel is situated on the eastern side
of the planned Kawela Bay Resort Development and west of the existing
Turtle Bay Hilton The hotel development will involve the following
construction

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the northern tip of Oahu adjacent to
the existing Turtle Bay Hilton resort The site is adjacent to Kawela Bay
and Kawela Point
The topography of the site slopes gently from south to north with
ground elevations ranging from 6 to 20 feet above MSL
There is an existing lowlying wet area in the center of the site with
ground elevations of about 0 to 3 feet above MSL

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built during the late Pliocene early
Pleistocene by two basaltic shielded volcanoes Waianae and Koolau
Waianae is the older of the two and forms the bulk of the western half of
the island The younger Koolau forms the eastern half of the island
extending from Kahuku to Koko Head
After the main shield building phase of the two volcanoes the island
began to subside in response to tectonic stresses imposed by the loads of
the mountain masses Concurrently reefs began to grow on the flanks of
the subsiding volcanoes forming fringing and barrier reefs of coral algal
limestone
During the middle and late Pleistocene or Ice Age sea levels
fluctuated in response to the advance and retreat of the continental
glaciers As the glaciers grew and advanced there was less water to fill
the ocean basins so sea levels fell to below the current level When the
glaciers melted and retreated more water was available and sea levels
rose often to levels above the present stand of the sea
The geologic conditions at the project site are a direct result of
these glacioeustatic sea level changes during the Pleistocene
Most of the site is underlain by coral algal reef materials believed
to have been deposited during the Waimanalo 25 feet high stand of the
Séa

In some areas of the site this reef material is overlain by somewhat
cemented dunes of calcareous sands believed to have been deposited during

In the lowlying areas of the site the Waimanalo reef material is

Some amount of fill were encountered on the project site which were

Site Preparation
en a te ts a a ts is
Significant site filling is planned in the lowlying wet ground
area where a thin surface layer of soft mud and loose sand was
encountered Settlements on the order of 1 to 3 inches are
anticipated due to the compression of the soft and loose soil from
the weight of 15 feet of new fill
It is very difficult to accurately predict the actual time required
for the above fill settlements to occur It depends on minute
characteristics of the soil structure and actual thickness of the
compressible layer We estimate that 95 of the total settlements
would have occurred within one month of the fill completion Because
of the anticipated fill settlements we recommend that the site
preparation and fill placement for this area be given first priority
to minimize delays prior to the commencement of the building
construction
If the above waiting period is not tolerable the soft organic
deposits could be removed and replaced with granular fill to
eliminate the above fill settlement waiting time

Clearing and grubbing of the lowlying wet ground area should consist
of removing vegetation and trees above the ground surface Due to
the very soft soil conditions we anticipate that most of the
clearing will be done by hand Construction equipment could be used
after either removal and replacement of the upper soft mud or the
completion of special ground stabilization procedures as recommended
below
The existing surface soil in the area is too soft and wet to support
construction equipment therefore we recommend that ground
stabilization procedures be utilized The approximate outline of the
existing lowlying area is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The ground stabilization procedures consist of establishing a working
platform or drainage blanket after clearing and grubbing of a section
of the existing ground area prior to the site filling operation
The working platform should consist of a combination of a synthetic
filter fabric overlain by a gravel layer The required thickness of
the gravel layer will be affected by the water level conditions at
the time of placement Under normal conditions with the water level
at or below the ground surface an 18 inch thickness is anticipated
A sketch of the drainage blanket working platform within the lowlying
area is shown on Plate 3
The gravel should be generally between one fourth and three fourths
of an inch in size
The filter fabric will reduce the required gravel thickness and the
gradation requirements of the drainage blanket since it will act asa
filter medium to prevent the mud from contaminating the granular

blanket material It also provides tensile reinforcement to the
gravel layer and permits use of heavy equipment on a minimm
thickness of compacted aggregate
The filter fabric is usually rolled onto the cleared soft ground by
laborers The gravel is then placed on the fabric by light
equipment Fill layers are added until a suitably stable platform
for heavy equipment is constructed

Fill Material and Placement
It is anticipated that off site borrow from a designated site near
the project will be used for fill materials Based on the previous
field exploration at the borrow site and laboratory test results in
general the borrow materials are suitable for use in compacted fill
and backfill The laboratory swell tests indicated that most of the
borrow soils have moderately high swelling potential depending on
the initial compaction density and molding moisture content prior to
saturation At or below the optimm moisture content the soil
exhibits moderate or slightly higher swell potential However it
was noted that at a molding moisture content slightly above the

Fill Placement and Compaction
The fill should be placed in maximum 10 inch thick loose level lifts
and compacted to a minimm of 90 maximm dry density per ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 Each layer should be spread uniformly and
blade mixed to attain uniformity of the material and water content
Additional fill material should not be placed on any fill layer which
has not been properly compacted
Cut and fill slopes of 2 1 H V are recommended
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling then cutting back
to the design slope to obtain a welli compacted slope face
Slopes greater than 20 feet in height should have benches at their
mid points or at about 20 feet intervals

We recommend that about four 4 settlement gauges be installed

Building Foundations
Based on the results of our field exploration a short pile
foundation system appears to be the most feasible means to transmit the
heavy multi level structural loads to the underlying coral formation For
the one story Fine Dining Building placed over eight 8 feet of fill
spread footings bearing on compacted structural fill may be used
A spread footing foundation system was also considered for this
project Based on available boring data footing excavations down to the
underlying coral formation would vary possibly up to ten 10 feet below
the water level Intensive dewatering is anticipated and would most
likely be difficult in sands and coral formation It would also require
shoring and bracing to protect the deep footing excavations In addition
to the above construction difficulties there is a high possibility of
encountering cavities or previous stream channels which may be in filled
with soft alluvial compressible deposits

Slab On Grade
Slab on grade construction may be used for the development The
subgrade soils of the on grade floor slabs should be placed over a 4 inch
thick cushion layer of clean gravel material generally between a quarter
to three quarters of an inch in size to provide uniform support and a
capillary break Below the cushion layer the subgrade soil should
consist of a 2 foot minimum of non expansive select borrow A moisture
barrier membrane is recommended between the slab and the cushion layer to
reduce the possibility of moisture damage to the floor coverings
The thickened edges of the on grade slabs should be embedded a
minimm of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade The bottom
of all thickened edge excavations should be wetted and kept moist until
concrete placement The backfill against the concrete slab should be
adequately compacted and graded to shed water away from the slab
perimeter
Retaining Structures
It is our understanding that retaining and basement walls are planned
for grade separation between the various structures
Well graded granular material such as base course rock sand or
equivalent should be used as backfill material directly behind the
retaining walls This would minimize the compaction effort required for
the backfill and reduce the build up of additional lateral pressures on
the walls The granular backfill should be placed in the area within a
line drawn upward at a 45 degree angle from the heel of the retaining wall
structure

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
and probes Variations of conditions between the field data points may
occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The location and elevation of the field data points were determined
by estimation from the topographic map by Engineer s Surveyors Hawaii
The physical location and elevation of the field data points should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on

formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
