SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration and available information disclosed that the lower portions of
the project site is generally underlain by highly expansive adobe clay soils with cobbles
and boulders extending to depths of over 20 feet below the existing ground surface The
thickness of the clay layer reduces in the upslope direction to a few feet thick along most
of the upper site boundary Laboratory tests indicate that the in situ adobe clay soils
exhibit high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in soil moisture content
In addition these soils are known to be susceptible to sliding or creeping on sloping
terrain Therefore special attention should be given to the site grading subgrade
preparation and slab on grade design for this project
The mass grading concept developed for the site includes substantial cuts and fills
in the upslope and downslope areas respectively to create a relatively flat terrain for the
proposed construction This will result in an overall site gradient of about 10H 1V to
13H 1V which is highly desirable to reduce the long term downhill creep movement of the
adobe clay soils In addition this grading concept will require removal of the adobe
clay soils in the upslope areas and capping over the adobe clay soils in the downslope
areas thus reducing the driving force and increasing the resisting force in the creeping
soils It is our opinion that this mass grading concept in conjunction with the drainage
measures planned for the subject development will significantly reduce the potential for
downhill creep movement of the adobe clay soils present at the site and improve the
overall stability in the project area including the existing Kealaula Kai residential
subdivision which is located immediately downslope of the subject site
To reduce the potential for distress to the future residential structures and
pavements resulting from swelling of the clayey soils we recommended that the
expansive clayey soils be capped with a minimum of 3 feet of non expansive select
granular capping fill material below future slab and pavement areas The non expansive
select granular capping layer may be omitted if weathered tuff or basalt rock formation is
exposed at the slab or pavement subgrade level Prior to placement of the non expansive
select granular capping fill the clayey subgrade should be moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to between 85 and 90 percent
relative compaction Scarification of the slab pavement subgrade and recompaction to
over 90 percent relative compaction should be avoided to reduce the potential for future
slab pavement distress resulting from the swelling shrinkage of the expansive clayey soils

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu encompasses an area of about 604 square miles and generally
consists of four major geomorphic provinces 1 Koolau Range 2 Waianae Range
3 Schofield Plateau and 4 Coastal Plain Of the four provinces Kamilonui Valley is
located within the Koolau Range province The Koolau Range was formed by the Koolau
shield volcano which comprises the majority of the eastern and northeastern portions of
the Island of Oahu The elongated shape of Koolau Range is approximately 37 miles in
length and has been deeply eroded by stream activity over the past several million years
The Koolau Range generally consists of massive accumulations of thinly bedded lava
flows which began to build after the older Waianae Range of western Oahu was
established
The Koolau Range erupted in Tertiary time approximately 2 to 5 million years ago
Following the formation of the Koolau Range the Island of Oahu began to submerge
under the weight of the volcanic mass This slow submergence combined with long term
sea level fluctuations as a result of the worldwide continental glacial advance and retreat
worked to erode the Island of Oahu to what it is now today For example during periods of
higher than normal present sea level the raised sea level served to promote the
deposition of shallow water marine sediments and coral deposits on the ground which
today may be exposed at or above the present day sea level In contrast during periods
of low sea level terrestrial streams were forced to cut deeper into the exposed ground to
carry the terrestrial sediment further out into what once was a marine or shallow lagoonal
environment Thus a complex series of marine and terrestrial deposits were formed
around the coastal and relatively low elevation portions of the Island of Oahu The varying
types of deposits can be found interiayered making reconstruction of the coastal area
development history of the Island of Oahu very complex
Kamilonui Valley is described as a relatively mature stage U Shaped erosional
valley formed as the streams draining the Koolau summit cut down through the rock layers

to incise and form the present day valley walls and floor The walls of the valley provide
exposure of the bedded lava flows of the Koolau shield volcano The valley floor is
generally composed of alluvial and colluvial deposits of volcanic material that had been
weathered eroded and transported downslope for deposition The valley floor typically
contains soil and rock materials that have been transported by stream activity and buried
over long periods of time forming thick deposits of mixtures of clay and boulders The
buried clayey cobbles and boulders have since undergone additional chemical weathering
in the depositional environment As a result of in situ weathering and burial the older
deeper deposits have become somewhat consolidated and tightly packed to form a
sedimentary conglomerate
More recent Quaternary time volcanic activity occurred in the vicinity of eastern
Oahu after the main Koolau volcano activity ceased The most recent volcanic activity is
part of the Honolulu Volcanic Series and is represented by the eruption of pyroclastic
material ash and cinder and some lava flows The cinder and ash cones located nearest
the Kamilonui project site comprise the Koko Head fissure which forms the extreme
southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu Although the cone and crater deposits are
concentrated to the southeast of the project site some of the pyroclastic materials are
believed to have been locally deposited in the vicinity of the site These localized deposits
may be buried beneath some of the more recent alluvial and shallow marine deposits of
the area

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping the proposed development
site is generally underlain by adobe clay soils with cobbles and boulders varying from
zero near the upper project boundary to depths of over 20 feet below the existing ground
surface in the lower areas These clayey soils which are classified as CH according to
the Unified Soil Classification System exhibit high shrink swell potential and will lose
considerable shear strength when saturated Under extended period of wet weather the
adobe clay soils will eventually get saturated and lose considerable shear strength thus
initialing downhill creep movement in areas where the terrain is steeper than about 4H 1V
When the adobe clay soils experience significant movement shear slip surfaces will
then develop which will further reduce the shear strength along the slip surfaces to a very
low residual shear value and may result in an accelerated downhill movement or slope
failure In general these downhill creep movements may be attributed to a combination of
the following factors
1 Poor subsoil conditions such as the highly plastic adobe clay soils
commonly encountered in the area
2 Steep hillside terrain especially in areas where the terrain is steeper than
4H 1V
3 Excessive water infiltration particularly if the project site is located in a high
moisture environment
In principle arresting the ground movement would require measures to reduce the
driving force and or increase the resisting force in the creeping soils These may include
1 removal or replacement of adobe clay soils with suitable fill materials 2 flattening of
existing site grades to a stable slope ratio 7H 1V or flatter by selective excavation and
embankment fills and 3 installation of surface and subsurface drainage measures to
intercept and divert water away from the adobe clay soils

3 1 Mechanism of Downhill Creep Movement
Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping the project site is generally

penetration of the excavator the actual thickness of the adobe deposits in some areas
could not be determined from the current field exploration
The adobe clay soils encountered at the project site which are classified as CH
according to the Unified Soil Classification System exhibit high shrink swell potential when
subjected to moisture fluctuation In addition the adobe clay soils are very weak soils
which are significantly affected by an increase in moisture content i e it will lose
considerable shear strength when saturated As a result they are subject to slope creep
or landslide movement in a sloping terrain during extended wet periods
Due to the relatively low permeability of the clay soils run off from heavy rainfall
may flow overland and concentrate in ditches and gullies where it would flow turbulently
downslope accelerating erosion of the slopes Due to the frequent rainfall in the valley
increasing in frequency and intensity to the north soils were observed to be relatively wet
most of the time Dry weather periods were observed to cause the surficial soils to
desiccate and form shrinkage cracks as the soil contracted Subsequent rainfall would
cause the cracks to fill with water and cause swelling of the clay minerals The repeated
shrinking and swelling process often causes the soils to overturn and creep slowly
downslope creating potential slope stability problems
Due to the clayey nature of the adobe soils and the relatively steep terrain at
portions of the project site it is likely that the near surface soils were slowly creeping
downhill as evidenced by the slickensided zones and wet surfaces observed in some of
the excavated test pits indicating possible shear creep movement of the deposit in the
past Generally slope creep movements are initially very slow and may go undetected for
many years However with time the clay soils may eventually get saturated due to
continual infiltration of rainfall seepage etc and lose a portion of their initial soil shear

coefficient of friction of 0 45 a minimum 2 foot thick layer of non expansive select
granular fill should be provided between the wall foundations and the underlying
adobe clay soils Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In
addition wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum
depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground
conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 10 foot setback
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of
the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings

3 7 Pavement Design
Flexible and rigid pavements are anticipated for the new access road planned at
the project site We anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed roadways will

tuff or cemented coralline materials is exposed at the pavement subgrade level If site
grading exposes soils other than those assumed additional tests should be performed to
confirm or revise the recommended pavement sections for actual field conditions Based
on the above assumptions we recommend the following pavement sections be used for

Granular Fill over Moist Compacted Clayey Subgrade
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a
high expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content Therefore
we recommend that the subgrade be properly prepared and kept moist prior to placement
of base course and capping fill material The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a
depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
All subgrades should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered by the
non expansive select granular capping fill If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade

3 8 Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project In
general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of
the trenches for the new utilities at the site The method and equipment to be used for
trench excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the applicable
federal state and local safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation
should be the responsibility of the contractor
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site The
clayey soils encountered at the site are generally stiff to very stiff However some of the
utility trench excavations will likely encounter the dense to very dense cobbles and
boulders as described in the test pit logs which may pose some excavation difficulties
during the trench excavation In addition the contractor should anticipate significant
W O INC Page 36
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
over break of the sides of the trench excavations considering the nature of the materials at
the site
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction
effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use
this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum
8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
3 9 Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface
run off away from the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation

and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after
setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration
3 10 Corrosion Protection for Water Lines
Seven sets of pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed to evaluate the
corrosivity of the on site soils generally encountered at the subject site The results of
these tests are presented on Plate C 10 of Appendix C The minimum resistivity values of
the soil samples tested ranged from 155 to 637 ohm cm which correspond to a Corrosion
Rating of 1 extremely corrosive based on the guidelines provided by the City County of
Honolulu Board of Water Supply Therefore we recommend that all metallic
substructures such as ductile iron water lines in contact with the soils be properly
designed to be protected against potential corrosion Due to the substantial amount of fills
anticipated for the proposed development some of the utility lines may be embedded
within the upper fill materials Therefore we recommend that additional testing be
conducted on the proposed borrow materials to evaluate the corrosivity of the imported
fills

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the
appropriate sample depths
Five one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plate C 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C 2
One residual direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample
of the clayey soils to evaluate the residual shear strength of the material tested Results of
the residual direct shear test are presented on Plate C 3
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates C 4 through C 6
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 A were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 7 through C 9
Seven sets of corrosion tests including and minimum resistivity
were performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc on selected soil
samples obtained from our field exploration The results of these tests are summarized on
Plate C 10
