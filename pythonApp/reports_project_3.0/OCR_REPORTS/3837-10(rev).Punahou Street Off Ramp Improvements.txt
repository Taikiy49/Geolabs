INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Punahou Street Off Ramp Improvements project located at the existing
Punahou Street off ramp in the Makiki area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
A geotechnical engineering exploration was previously conducted for this project
and the findings and recommendations are presented in our report entitled Geotechnical
Engineering Exploration Punahou Off Ramp Improvements Basic Contract Honolulu
Oahu Hawaii dated March 11 1998 Subsequent to the issuance of our report we
understand that the scope of work for the subject project has been expanded to include
new pavements for the entire off ramp in addition to the construction of a new lane
Therefore additional engineering analyses were conducted in support of this revised
scope of work The findings and recommendations present in this report supersede the
recommendations presented in our March 11 1998 report These recommendations
presented herein are intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations such as Punchbowl These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of Punchbowl Hill The
Punchbowl Hill Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end
of a spur of the Koolau Range The tuff consists mainly of brown palagonitized vitric ash
and lapilli with scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

new lane within the Shriners Hospital for Crippled Children vary from about 46 5 to
48 feet MSL

Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 to depths of about 20 5 and 21 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration along the Punahou Street off ramp site generally encountered
stiff to very stiff clayey silts and silty clays overlying medium dense to dense cinder sands
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface
As discussed earlier the project has expanded to provide new pavements for the
entire off ramp in addition to the new lane planned for the subject project Therefore we
have performed additional pavement analyses to include both flexible and rigid pavement
sections for the entire off ramp Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey
soils exhibit poor pavement support characteristics with a relatively low California
Bearing Ratio CBR of about 5 Therefore the pavement structural sections are
relatively thick Based on our cost comparison between the various options studied it
appears that a rigid pavement structural section consisting of 10 5 inches of Portland
Cement Concrete PCC on 6 inches of UTPB over 6 inches of ASB would be the most
economical for the subject project A flexible pavement structural section consisting of
5 inches of AC on 9 inches of ACB over 6 inches of UTPB on 12 inches of ASB may be
used for the transition zone between the existing AC and new PCC pavements where
needed
We understand that a portion of the off ramp will traverse an existing underground
box culvert near the property line between the Shriners Hospital for Crippled Children and
the Jodo Mission of Hawaii For the design of new pavements over the existing box
culvert we understand that a concrete slab bridge spanning over the box culvert will be
used for the subject project The concrete slab bridge should be designed as a
simply supported beam with shallow foundations supporting the structural slab In order
GEOLABS HAWAII

to avoid surcharging the walls of the existing box culvert excavations to depths at or
below the bottom of the existing box culvert and backfilling with Controlled Low Strength

New retaining walls will be required to provide grade separation between the new
off ramp lane and the adjacent properties on the makai side of the existing off ramp

Pavement Joints
Considering the large areal extent of the rigid pavements for the off ramp an
adequate amount and pattern of joints should be provided In general joints should be
provided in rigid pavements for the following reasons
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused
by temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction
Paragraph Pavement Joints in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design
Manual prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation should be
referred to for joint details and construction requirements
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the
pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction To collect infiltrated water into the
pavement structural section we recommend that a pavement edge drain system be
provided along the side of the new travelway

We envision that an edge drain will be needed to collect and daylight the infiltrated
water from the pavement structural section from the untreated permeable base layer
The edge drain should consist of a 6 inch PVC perforated pipe with perforations facing
down surrounded by untreated permeable base A permeable separator non woven
filter fabric conforming to Section 648 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 HSS should be placed around the

horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the
design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 40 percent of the vertical
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall which is free to deflect
cantilever may be used in the design For walls that are restrained at the top a
rectangular distribution equal to 55 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure
acting over the entire height of the wall may be used Additional analyses during
design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line
loads

Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic

For footings bearing on the stiff soils the bottom of the footing excavations should

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration and laboratory testing disclosed that the proposed new chiller
site is generally underlain by near surface clayey soils with a slight shrink swell potential
when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural distress to
future slabs on grade resulting from swelling of the near surface clayey soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180
ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
We envision that the slabs on grade will be subjected to equipment loads and
vibration Therefore a 6 inch layer of aggregate base course compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction is recommended for support of the concrete slabs For
design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch
pci may be used for the compacted base course The thickened edges of slabs

Site Preparation
We envision that excavations up to about 6 feet below the existing ground surface
will be required for construction of the new off ramp lane and retaining wall In addition
excavations on the order of about 15 feet will be needed to construct the concrete slab
bridge foundations at the existing box culvert location Minimal site grading work is
anticipated for the construction at the new chiller site In general grading work should
conform to Section 200 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and
Public Works Construction 1994 HSS and the site specific recommendations
contained in this report
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements
should be over excavated to expose firm or stiff material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or
organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Existing structures and pavements that are to be demolished should be completely
removed The over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with
compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed
and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with granular fill material
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Utilities to be

subbase layer The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180 ASTM

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project In general
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of AASHTO Designation M 43 Size No 67 is
recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as AASHTO
Designation M 43 Size No 67 should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
To reduce the potential for loss of pipe support increased pipe deflection and trench
settlement resulting from the migration of fines into the open graded material under the
action of hydraulic gradient we recommend that the open graded backfill material be
wrapped around with a non woven filter fabric or permeable separator Section 648
of HSS
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or select
granular fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented on
Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are presented on Plate B 2
One Standard Proctor compaction test ASTM D 698 or AASHTO T 99 was
performed on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and
moisture content relationship The test results are presented on Plate B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test was performed on a bulk sample of
the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The
sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture content determined by ASTM D 698
AASHTO T 99 Dynamic compaction of the sample was performed by dropping a
10 pound hammer from a height of 18 inches The sample was compacted in 5 equal
layers with each layer receiving 10 blows of the hammer The sample was soaked for
4 days prior to the penetration test Results of the test are presented on Plate B 4
