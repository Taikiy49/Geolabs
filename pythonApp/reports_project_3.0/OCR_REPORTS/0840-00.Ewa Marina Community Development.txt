SINTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soils engineering and geology feasibility
investigation and groundwater study The purpose of our study was threefold to
determine the suitability of the site for the proposed development to determine
probable groundwater conditions that will be encountered during excavation of the
proposed marina and to evaluate possible effects that the proposed development
may have on groundwater in the area
A Soil and Geology Feasibility Investigation had Been performed at the site for
the proposed marina commmity in 1973 by Ltd In 1974
extensive field borings were made to determine the soil and foundation conditions
for the Barber s Point Ocean Outfall System which traversed the property from
north Mauka to south Makai direction
In addition numerous published reports on soil geology and hydrology were
available in the general project area
Based on data obtained from previous investigations performed at and near the
site and research of available soil and geologic literature engineering and
geologic analysis were made to evaluate surface and subsurface conditions at
the site and to develop conclusions and general recommendations for the Ewa Marina
Community project based on the latest development scheme

PLANNED DEVELOPMENT
The proposed Ewa Marina Community development will encompass approximately
1 000 acres of land The development will include residential areas of
different densities commercial areas parks schools roads waterways and
a small boat marina open to the ocean as shown on Plate 1 General Land Use
Plan
In general grading on the site will include a large scale excavation to depths
ranging from 6 to 15 feet below sea level to create a marina and waterway
system a shallow lake and various fills for building pads roads and landscaping
It is anticipated that maximum cuts will be less than 25 feet
New drainage structures or swales will be constructed to intercept surface
runoff and convey water to a waterway that is connected to the marina and the
ocean
The location of proposed structures and improvements and probable building loads
are not finalized at this time

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed Ewa Marina Community development includes about 1 000 acres on
the south Makai shore of the Island of Oahu about 4 miles west Ewa of
Pearl Harbor and about 2 miles south Makai of the town of Ewa The land
surface slopes gently southward from about 20 feet above sea level at the
northerly boundary to about sea level at the beach which comprises the southerly
boundary with the exception of Oneula Hau Bush Beach Park
The central western and northeastern parts of the property are covered by
sugar cane fields Dense brush and Keawe growth covers much of the southern
part of the area between the ocean cane fields and the park boundary
The land portion of the Barber s Point Ocean Outfall System consisting of
84 inch diameter reinforced concrete pipe has recently been substantially
completed The outfall system traverses the development area as shown on Plate 1
The pipe invert is generally about 15 feet below land surface

_ GEOLOGY
The proposed Ewa Marina Community project is located at the southern Makai
edge of the Ewa Coastal Plain about 4 miles west southwest of the entrance to
Pearl Harbor In the project area the coastal plain surface slopes very gently
southward toward the ocean at a gradient of about 1 foot in 260 feet In the
north central west and northeast parts of the area the natural land surface
has been leveled ditched and cultivated for sugar cane The southern part of
the area is largely covered by native brush vines and sand
Geologic units of importance in the proposed project area are summarized on
Figure 1 For purposes of this investigation the rocks underlying the Ewa
Plain will be referred as cap rock or cap rock aquifer and basal basalt
or basal basalt aquifer The term cap rock includes algal limestone coral
reefs breccia sand and marl derived from the breakup and decay of coralline
deposits stream laid gravel sand and silt peat carbonaceous silt and
clay formed in fresh water lagoons and tidal marshes dune sand and beach
deposits The term basal basalt includes the Koolau and Waianae lava flows
Figures 2 and 3 show the lithology and thickness of the cap rock on the Ewa
Plain and indicate that the basal basalt is deeply buried in the proposed

project area The log of an exploratory boring and Chamberlain
1967 Table 1 p 158 and Cox and Lao 1967 p 5 indicates that the cap rock
unit is more than 1000 feet thick along the Pacific Ocean Shoreline near the
proposed project area Stearns and Chamberlain 1967 p 157 reported that
seismic refraction studies indicated that at a distance of 4 6 miles offshore
south or Makai of boring Ewa 1 the Base of the cap rock unit was about 2 900
feet thick in a water depth of 1 200 feet about 4 000 feet below sea level
Thus the base of the cap rock unit and the top of the basal basalt should not
be an important consideration in the proposed Ewa Marina Community Project area
Published geologic and geophysical reports do not indicate that faults or rock

and geophysical studies made during investigations for the ocean section of the
Barber s Point Ocean Outfall System R M 1976 did not
indicate that faults or rock offsets would be a problem to be considered along
the outfall pipeline which extends more than 7 000 feet south Makai beneath
the ocean from Oneula Beach near the center of and just south of the proposed
project area

WATER QUALITY
According to Swain 1973 groundwater on Oahu occurs in 3 major aquifers
volcanic bedrock calcareous sedimentary deposits and non calcareous sedimentary
material In this report the volcanic rocks were called the basal basalt or
basal basaltic aquifer and the calcareous and non calcareous materials were
lumped together and referred to as the cap rock or cap rock aquifer The last
two units interfinger and intertongue along the landward Mauka side of the
Ewa Coastal Plain and are interbedded beneath the plain
The most important parts of the cap rock aquifer are coralline limestone units
that contain cavities and solution channels caused by chemical and mechanical
W O 840 00
Page 13
erosion processes In places interconnected cavernous sections of the limestone
may permit extensive sea water intrusion which greatly limits usuage of water
Chloride levels for sources within the limestone unit in the Ewa Plain area are
shown on Figure 4 Available chemical analyses indicate that water from lime
stone units in the cap rock aquifer is either a sodium chloride or sodium magnesium
chloride type All groundwater samples have chloride concentrations that exceed
drinking water limits 250 ppm In addition all samples have bicarbonate
concentrations greatly exceeding that of fresh or sea water indicating solution
of the calcareous rocks Swain 1973 p 35
The chloride concentrations in water from wells located in the vicinity of the
proposed project area Fig 4 suggest some mixing of saline and fresh water
According to the data shown Fig 4 no significant salt water intrusion has
occurred in the coastal plain east of the Barber s Point area

Site Preparation
All deleterious materials i e trash vegetation topsoil etc should be
stripped from areas to be developed and removed from the site Topsoil may
be stockpiled and spread over landscaped areas as needed
Stripping operations may expose random soft spots and surface voids which may
require additional excavation and backfilling with compacted structural fill
before site development

Excavations
It is anticipated that rocks above the water table can be excavated with
conventional construction equipment such as a D 9 bulldozer Excavations
that extend much below the water table may require dragline equipment
especially in places where the cap rock is highly permeable
In places blasting may be required to break up large very hard blocks of
coral algal reef rock or limestone ledge coral It should be noted that
blasting may be inefficient because large amounts of energy may be absorbed by
voids and soft areas in reef material
The excavated materials reef rock gravel sand and clay would have con
siderable economic value Possible offsite uses of these materials include
aggregate riprap hard limestone sand clay lime for cement plants agricultural
lime for acid soils and other uses On site uses could include fill to raise
beach front buildings above high water levels using dredged material for con
structing groins and for normal fill and base course aggregate it may be necessary
to establish a quarrying operating to obtain suitable gradation and arrange
with a local cement plant to trade cement for limestone
Slopes
It is anticipated that constructed slopes will not be higher than about 25 feet
with at least 6 feet of this height being below sea level Although competent
coral reef rock will stand vertically to this height soft areas are anticipated

Foundations
In general the coral algal reef rock should provide adequate foundation
support using conventional spread footings for light and medium weight structures
However solution cavities and soft spots are quite common in the reef rock Such
areas will require adequate backfilling to provide suitable foundation supports
High rise or heavily loaded structures will require detailed subsurface investi
