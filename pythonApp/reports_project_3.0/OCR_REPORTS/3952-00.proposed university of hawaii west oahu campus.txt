Regional Geology
The majority of the island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt lavas from
two primary shield volcanoes known as Waianae and Koolau The older volcano
Waianae is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in geochronologic age and forms the
bulk of the western third of the island The younger volcano Koolau is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau remained active thus the
westward flowing lavas from Koolau continued to bank against the Waianae volcano to
create the elevated area known as the Schofield Plateau between the two volcanoes
Therefore the island of Oahu was formed by the coalescence of the two volcanoes
Following the eruption of the bulk of the lavas the island of Oahu was partially
submerged and emerged cyclically due to large scale fluctuations of the sea level in
response to periods of worldwide glaciation As a result of the sea level fluctuations the
erosional baseline likewise fluctuated and major streams were drowned and buried and
later emerged to renew their erosional activities This cyclic activity during the
Pleistocene age generally accounts for the present topography of today including the
sedimentary coastal plains such as Ewa and the area surrounding Honolulu and Waikiki
Today both the Waianae and Koolau mountain ranges have lost most of their
original shield volcano outlines and exist as elongated mountains shaped largely by
continuing erosional forces The Koolau Range with its highest peak of about 3 105 feet
above sea level is approximately 37 miles in elongated length and is deeply incised and
eroded by active stream cutting In contrast the Waianae Range with its highest peak of
about 4 025 feet above sea level is about 22 miles in length and has been carved into
huge valleys separated by steep ridgelines by a long history of stream erosion and
island wide submergence
The project area is located on the middle to lower southeastern flank of the
Waianae Range The site is situated near the mapped ground surface coalescence of
the Koolau lavas on top of the Waianae lavas In addition the site is located just
northerly of the northern limit of the Ewa Plain region As such the majority of the project
site is underlain by volcanic residual and saprolite soils in addition to basalt rock The
basalt rock may include some bedded cinder and spatter lavas as well as dense rock
formation layered with clinker seams existing in varying degrees of weathered states

The in situ chemical weathering of the lava flows has continued for the past one to
two million years As a result the weathering process has transformed the surficial rock
to a mantle of residual soil which grades to saprolite soil and finally less weathered rock
with increasing depth In general the residual soils are found closer to the ground
surface and are composed of clayey silt and silty clay with few distinguishable parent rock
features In contrast the saprolite soil is composed mainly of silty material and shows
relict patterns of the parent rock material such as vesicles or fractures These soil
products are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks Furthermore the
saprolite soil generally grades to basalt rock formation of increasing competency with
increasing depth
Climate
The project site is situated on the middle to lower mountain slope of the Waianae
Range on the leeward side of the island of Oahu The nearest weather recording station
in the vicinity is located near Barbers Point a distance of about 5 miles to the south of the
project site The weather station is located near an elevation of about 40 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL whereas the project site is located at elevations ranging between 200 to
1 200 feet MSL
Based on the Barbers Point climate records the average annual precipitation is
about 21 inches of rainfall The average maximum annual temperature is 85 degrees
Fahrenheit and the average minimum annual temperature is about 67 degrees
Fahrenheit Because the project site is located at a higher elevation on the Waianae
mountain slope the precipitation amounts may actually be higher than those recorded at
the Barbers Point station Similarly the temperature ranges may be generally lower at
the project site due to the difference in elevation The overall climate may be summarized
as semi arid
Site Description
The project site is located on the southeastern lower flank of the Waianae
mountain range in the district of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the
available topographic plan the ground surface elevations range between about 1 150
feet MSL at the upper western limit of the site falling to about 225 feet MSL at the lower
eastern limit of the site Based on our observations the site appears to have been used
for the cultivation of sugar cane in the past as evidenced by the lack of native vegetation
and occurrence of multiple access roads and some abandoned sugar cane growth
Based on our brief site visit the ground surface conditions were observed as
residual and saprolite soils derived from the weathering of volcanic rock materials
Outcroppings of hard basalt rock material were observed at the ground surface In

In addition to the generalized geologic conditions described above the hillslopes
comprising Puu Kapuai have been mapped by others as being composed of bedded
basalt rock with cinder pumice and other spatter lava deposits Puu Kapuai is believed
to represent a volcanic vent from which the local cinder and basalt rock materials were
extruded However during our site visit the cinder and pumice deposits were not
observed at the existing ground surface Instead residual and saprolite soils mixed with
surface outcroppings of basalt rock formation and scattered large boulders were
observed The boulders appeared to be concentrated near the Puu Kapuai summit area
Therefore we believe the potential for the cinder and pumice materials to be buried in the
subsurface exists
In general the entire project site appears to exist as a late stage mature
topography of rounded hills and uniform slopes incised by drainage gullies emanating
from the area up slope of the site Some alluvial soil and boulder deposits may exist
within the invert of the drainage gullies The streams contained within the gulches and
gullies appear to be ephemeral in character flowing during rainfall events but remaining
generally dry at other times

Soil Survey
Based on information available from the United States Department of Agriculture
Soil Conservation Service approximately 12 surface soil types or soil series have been
defined and mapped as deposits existing within the general project limits The soil types
generally consist of silty clay and clay loam of low to moderate swell potential and may be
classified as ranging from CL ML to MH in accordance with the Unified Soil Classification
System
In general the soil series are developed based on the soil physical properties
steepness of land at the field occurrence depth to bedrock and other factors The Soil
Survey Map provided with this report shows the mapping definition by the soil
conservation service and includes a map legend briefly describing each of the soil series

Soil Swell Potential
Based on our research and field observation of the surficial soil deposits at the
site the clayey soils may possess a moderate to high swell potential The swell potential
of the near surface soils may have an impact on surface foundations such as
slab on grade or pavements Special considerations may be necessary for preparation of
the subgrade and support of slab on grade considering the potential for swelling of the
soils near the ground surface

Floodwater Runoff
The project site contains three relatively large drainage gulches that traverse
portions of the site In addition several smaller drainage gullies are present within the
project limits Although the drainage features are typically dry during heavy rainfall
events it may be anticipated that the features could contain significant runoff of a
flash flood type discharge Special consideration should be given to flood control and
drainage of these areas
In addition much of the project site consists of moderate slopes on the order of
about 15 percent These existing slopes have the potential for significant
erosion due to the velocity of runoff during heavy storm flows Special consideration
should be given to runoff control and erosion in these areas
Finally the broad gently sloping area located at the central portion of the site
bordering the Interstate Route H 1 Highway appears to show the potential for flood
inundation based on the present morphology of the vicinity Most of the significant
drainage features of the site appear to converge at this location from the surrounding
steep slopes Special consideration should be given to the flood potential in this area
Slope Stability
Approximately 75 percent of the land area within the project site is composed of
sloping terrain To provide for the development of a 500 acre campus site we anticipate
that significant earthwork including cutting and filling of the existing slopes and
construction of walls for grade separation would be necessary Because the ultimate
campus site may be located adjacent to the existing rock quarry consideration should be
given to the potential effects of blasting operations on slope stability
Special consideration should be given to the design of the earthwork and retaining
structures based on the geotechnical constraints of the earth materials involved We
anticipate that the site may be underlain by saprolite soils and rock formation with some
cinder or clinker deposits
Rock Excavation
Based on our site visit it is believed that basalt rock formation may exist at
relatively shallow depths The rock materials may vary from medium hard clinker and
cinder material to very hard fractured lava flows Excavation for mass grading or utility
installation may encounter the rock at variable depths in the subsurface In addition the
design of cut slopes may be affected by the rock quality of the materials exposed at the
cut slope face

Soft Soil Deposits
Localized areas of soft or under consolidated soil deposits may be encountered
locally throughout the site In general the potential exists for the deposits to occur in
several typical areas 1 abandoned reservoir sites 2 drainage channel bottoms and
3 the alluvial plain and other areas presently under agricultural cultivation
Additional exploration should be performed during the design work at locations
suspected of receiving undocumented fills abandoned reservoir sites or at areas where
alluvial depositional processes may have occurred over time alluvial plain The
exploration would serve to evaluate the thickness and type of potential soft soil deposits
Boulder Deposits
Large boulders with diameters in excess of 6 to 8 feet were observed at the
ground surface of low elevation southeastern portion of the site Many of the boulders
were located in large piles or as elongated berms The deposits appear to be the result
of previous site clearing in preparation of utilizing the land for agricultural cultivation
Consideration should be given to the removal and disposal of the boulders should the
area be considered for development
In addition boulders may be scattered across the summit areas of the site and
buried at random in the shallow residual and saprolite soils of the majority of the site
Site Development
Based on our preliminary site evaluation we do not believe that there are
significant geologic and or geotechnical constraints present at the 1000 acre site which
would preclude development of the proposed 500 acre West Oahu Campus of the
University of Hawaii However based on the geotechnical considerations presented in
the previous section it appears that the eastern through southern portions of the site may
be the more favorable areas for the development of the proposed campus
The eastern through southern portions of the site appear to contain lower hill
slopes and more uniform topography which may be less costly to develop in terms of
mass grading and infrastructure development The terrain appears to be better suited for
the development of a campus in terms of total earthwork and foundation costs Slope
stability issues also appear to be less significant as opposed to developing in other
portions of the site that are steeper and may entail larger earthwork efforts
We do not envision significant concerns with the development of the upper
northwestern hill slopes of the site should they be deemed more desirable from other
development perspectives However there may be higher costs associated with the
earthwork and infrastructure development in this portion of the site
