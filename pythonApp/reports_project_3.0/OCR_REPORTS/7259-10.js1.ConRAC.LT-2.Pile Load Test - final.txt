Dear Mr. Arana: 
In accordance with the project design requirements, a static compressive pile load 
test was conducted on an augered cast-in-place (ACIP) test pile (Test Pile No. 2) on 
June 13 and 14, 2016.  This letter report presents the results of our field observations, test 
data, engineering analysis, conclusions, and our recommendations. 
In general, the Kahului Consolidated Car Rental (ConRAC) Facility involves the 
construction of a new multi-story consolidated rental car facility at Kahului Airport on the 
Island of Maui, Hawaii.  The project site is generally bounded by Aalele Street to the 
west, an air cargo facility to the southeast, and Keolani Place to the northwest. The site 
is also traversed by Lanui Circle, Hemaloa Street, and a large box culvert oriented in a 
northwest-southeast direction.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
We provided geotechnical engineering exploration services previously and 
recommended using ACIP pile foundation to support the new building structure. Our 
recommendations were presented in the report entitled, “Geotechnical Engineering 
Exploration, Consolidated Car Rental (ConRAC) Facility, Kahului Airport, Kahului, Maui, 
Hawaii” dated April 29, 2016. 
The pile load test was conducted on an 18-inch diameter ACIP pile with a length of 
approximately 36.4 feet below the existing ground surface.  The ACIP pile was installed by 
Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company (HDCC) using a Bauer BG-28 drill rig equipped 
with automatic monitoring equipment providing the drilling information, such as drilling 
depth, drilling rate, auger torque, stroke count, etc., on a near-continuous basis. HDCC 
used continuous flight augers to drill the pile.  As the augers were removed, the hole was

To provide reaction support for the load test, six 24-inch diameter ACIP reaction 
piles, with lengths ranging between approximately 34.5 to 38.8 feet long, were installed in 
a similar fashion as the load test pile with the exception of using 3-inch diameter center 
bars in lieu of rebar cages.  The load test pile was completed on June 1, 2016, while the 
reaction piles were completed on June 6, 2016. Locations of the reaction piles are also 
shown on the ACIP Test Pile Location Plan, Plate 2. 
To better understand and predict the load transfer mechanism at the pile-soil 
interface, the load test pile was instrumented with six vibrating wire embedment strain 
gages (Model No. 4200A-2) manufactured by Geokon, Inc.  The strain gages were 
connected using a multiplexer to a Geokon Micro-10 data acquisition system to record the 
strains during the compressive load test.  The embedment strain gages were installed in 
the load test pile at the following depths below the existing ground surface.

The pile load test was conducted in general accordance with the Quick Load Test 
Method of ASTM D1143 and modified by our office to suit the design intents.  In general, 
the test pile was loaded to about 640 kips in approximately 40.0-kip load increments. Each 
load increment was held for at least 15 minutes or until the rate of the pile head movement 
has generally stabilized.  In addition, the static load test was held for approximately two 
hours at the 320-kip load increment, and 4 hours at the 480-kip and 640-kip load 
increment to evaluate the effects of creep. After reaching the maximum test load of 
640 kips, the load test pile was unloaded in approximately eight decrements and held for 
at least 5 minutes at each decrement. The results of the compressive pile load test are 
summarized below.

The load test readings and the load-displacement curve are presented on Plates 4 
and 5, respectively. Based on the shape of the load-displacement curve and our 
interpretation of the load transfer mechanism of the compressive load test, it appears that 
the 18-inch diameter ACIP pile underwent 1.1 inches of creep displacement over a 4-hour 
period at the maximum compressive test load of about 640 kips. It should be noted that 
the load test pile was terminated in weathered rock to determine the load carrying capacity 
of the ACIP piles bearing on the weathered rock due to early refusal conditions 
encountered during drilling.  
The load test results confirmed our design parameters for the 18-inch diameter 
ACIP piles bearing on weathered rock.  We recommend that the drilling contractor installs 
the ACIP piles with a maximum effort to advance through the weathered rock layer when 
possible. In addition, we recommend conducting an additional ACIP pile load test on an 
18-inch diameter ACIP pile bearing on the hard basalt formation to verify the allowable 
load capacity of the 18-inch diameter ACIP piles terminated on the basalt formation.
