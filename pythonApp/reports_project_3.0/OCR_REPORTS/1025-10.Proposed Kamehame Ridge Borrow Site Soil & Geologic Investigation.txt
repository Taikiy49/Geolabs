As requested we have performed a soil and geologic investi
gation at the above referenced borrow site to evaluate the

and engineering analysis
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed earthwork consists of excavating a strip of land
approximately 80 feet wide along the northern mauka side of
Hawaii Kai Drive for use as borrow material

between During the late Pleistocene Ice Age eruptions of
ash and cinders Honolulu Volcanic Series built large tuff

Basalt Bedrock
Kamehame Ridge is composed of seaward dipping about 10 degrees
pahoehoe and aa basalt flows Pahoehoe puka puka rock is a
smooth hummocky highly vesiculated rock containing lava tubes
Aa basalt consists of dense blocky core rock surrounded by
clinker In the area of the proposed cut along Hawaii Kai
Drive there is about 80 to 90 percent aa basalt and clinker
with about 10 percent interbedded pahoehoe basalt This rock
outcrops over about 30 to 40 percent of the proposed cut area
Tuff
Tuff is consolidated volcanic ash The site is close to Koko
Head When the hot volcanic ash fell the melted glass which
comprises it welded together forming a relatively coherent mass
Weathering of the upper portions of the tuff has produced an
orange brown silt residual soil on the ash up to about 5 to 6
feet thick
SOTL EXPLORATION

two thirds of the proposed cut slope area is underlain by volcanic
tuff with varying degrees of cementation while the remaining area
is underlain by lava rock formation

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Excavation
The field exploration disclosed relatively competent soil
conditions at the site
Although some weathered zones and clinker layers were noted
in the exposed rock formation hard ripping should be antici
pated
Major excavation difficulties by bulldozers are not antici
pated in the volcanic tuff
Cut Slopes
The proposed site is mostly in hard sound rock basalt
and tuff and well suited to cut slopes
Aa and Pahoehoe Basalt
These rocks are generally dry hard to very hard
and sound Cuts at slopes of up to 4 horizontal
to 1 vertical 1 could be utilized
Volcanic Tuff
These rocks are generally dry moderately hard and
sound The recommended cut slopes would depend on
the amount of cementation in the formation

Generally cuts at slopes of 1 horizontal to 1 vertical
1 1 are recommended Some adversely dipping rock
layers slanting into the cut may be encountered and
cause localized sloughing
The tuff formation may be susceptible to erosion and
therefore should be vegetated soon after cutting
Generally slope benches should be utilized for slope
heights greater than 20 feet A cut slope about 30
feet high is anticipated below the existing retaining
wall at Lots 7 and 8 Due to the localized nature of
this cut slope height it is our opinion that the slope
bench can be omitted here
The upper layers of the tuff has been weathered to a
Silty sand sandy silt It is distinguished by its
orange brown color This soil is generally stable at
slopes of about 2 horizontal to 1 vertical 2 1
Where cut the soil is erosion prone and should be
vegetated and or protected soon after cutting Some
areas of this soil may be underlain by adversely dipping
rock layers
Due to the complex nature and variability of the
soil and rock formations at the site it appears that
a general design cut slope ratio of 1 1 H V would
be suitable for the proposed cut

All cuts should be inspected by a qualified repre
sentative of the soils engineer
Some variations in the soil conditions should be
anticipated and some slope adjustments may be re
quired in the field during construction
The materials generated from the cutting operations generally
less than about 8 inches in size should be suitable for borrow

The smaller excavated rock materials are also non expansive

Design Review
The plans for site grading should be forwarded to the soils
engineer for review and comments prior to construction to see
that the intent of our recommendations is included in the
project plans and specifications
CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the site grading particularly slope
cutting operations be observed by a soils engineer during con
struction to see that the subsurface conditions are consistent
with the findings of our subsurface investigation
