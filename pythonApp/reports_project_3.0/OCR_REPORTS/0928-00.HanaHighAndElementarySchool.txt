INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our subsurface inves
tigation performed at the site of the proposed Gymnasium
and Community School Library The location of the pro
posed building site with respect to the surrounding area
is shown on the Site Plan Plate l
The purpose of this investigation was to evaluate the soil
conditions at the proposed development sites and to pro
vide recommendations for the design and construction of the
foundations of the proposed structures
The results of our field explorations and laboratory tests
which were used in the engineering analysis to formulate
our conclusions and recommendations are presented in the
appendix of this report
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
The proposed gymnasium building will be a single story
structure approximately 116 feet by 195 feet in plan
The building will have masonry walls and structural steel
framing Column loads up to 160 kips are anticipated
Due to the existing sloping ground at the proposed gym
site a suspended structural floor slab is planned

The proposed library building will be a single story
structure approximately 90 feet by 90 feet in plan The
building will have masonry walls and structural steel
framing Column loads up to 75 kips are anticipated
Also included in the proposed construction is a new 800
foot roadway and parking area
At the time of our investigation grading plan and finished
floor elevations were not available We anticipate that
shallow cuts and fills will be utilized for the site
SITE DESCRIPTION
The Hana High and Elementary School is located in Hana
Maui approximately 1 5 miles east from the Hana Airport
and on the makai northeast side of the Hana Highway
The project site is located in the northwestern portion of
Hana High and Elementary School
The project site is bounded by an adjacent pasture land on
the north and northwest The adjacent school yard and exis
ting buildings are to the south and southeast

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
After the major formation of East Maui by the Honomanu Vol
canic Series and the Kula Volcanic Series a long queiscent
period followed during which canyons and large valleys were
carved in the East Maui volcanic shield
W O 928 00
P 5
Volcanic eruptions then resumed creating new lava flows
cinder cones and ash deposits which represented a new series
called the Hana Volcanic Series The cones built by the
Hana Volcanic Series eruptions ranged from small spatter
cones a few feet high to big cinder cones more than a mile
across at the base and 600 feet high The resulting lavas
poured down the major valleys such as
and Waihoi and along the slopes of the older volcanic shield
burying a large portion of the eroded topography on the
southern and eastern ends of East Maui Although the Hana
lavas were generally more fluid and the lava flows tended
to be thinner and less massive than the preceeding Kula
lavas thick accumulations of lava did occur in the valleys
and valley heads These eruptions were often separated by
intervals long enough to allow weathering and erosion of
the lava surface before it was buried by the next flow thus
creating weathered layers between successive flows
The lava rock from these Hana eruptions comprises the rock
encountered at the project site beneath a relatively thin
layer of soil This rock probably originated from the Hana
lava flows that came down the older volcanic slope surface
and built its thickness gradually by the hardening of suc

cessive lava flows adding a relatively thin lava layer
each time a flow occurred in contrast to the build up of
lava in the older valley areas by ponding
Soil Conditions
The soil conditions at most of the site can generally be
described as a thin layer of soil over lava rock formation
The soil layer generally consists of medium to stiff brown
clayey silt and gravel probably of alluvial origin Cobbles
and boulders were also present in this soil layer
These cobbles and small boulders are better described as
being lava rock fragments ranging in size from several in
ches to about 1 foot in diameter that probably originated
from the underlying fractured lava rock formation
Beneath the brown soil layer a fractured partly vesicular
lava rock formation was encountered Most of the rock that
was recovered was of a fractured nature with brown sections
indicating that a degree of weathering has occurred within
the fractured zones
Small cavities were noted in some of the borings at varying
depths

The field exploration disclosed that the proposed gym site is
situated partly over an old buried drainageway and partly
over the rock formation at shallow depths Special founda
tion design is required for this structure to reduce the
differential settlement caused by the variable subsoil con
dition
Rock formation was encountered at shallow depths below the
proposed library building Spread or continuous footing
foundations bearing on the lava rock formation can be utilized
for this building

this building site is underlain by dense rock formation at
shallow depths while loose to medium density silty gravel
over rock at about 15 foot depths was encountered in the
central portion of the proposed site It appears that the
northern half of the building site is situated over an old
buried drainageway
Due to the difference in subsoil conditions below the gym site
differential settlements would occur between the footings
bearing on the dense rock formation and footings bearing on
the drainageway deposits Settlement estimates in this type
of gravelly drainageway deposits are very difficult due to
the heterogeneous nature of these deposits Estimated settle
ments for the anticipated column loads are on the order of 1
to 2 inches for the gravelly alluvial deposits Since very
little settlements are anticipated for the footings bearing on
the rock formation anticipated differential settlements would

be on the order of 1 to 2 inches
In order to reduce the total and differential settlements
overexcavation and recompaction of the soils below all of the
footings are recommended to provide a more uniform bearing
layer below the foundations This overexcavation and recom
paction would reduce the loading on the alluvial deposits
thereby reducing the total settlements This overexcavation
and recompaction is also required where the rock formation
is close to the surface to provide more uniform bearing sup
port An allowable bearing value of 2000 p s f is recom
mended for footings on this recompacted layer
The amount of reduction of the anticipated total and differen
tial settlement would depend on the depth of overexcavation
and recompaction performed If the soil and rock within 3
feet of the bottom of the footings is overexcavated and recom
pacted to 95 of its maximum dry density it is estimated that
settlements would be on the order of to 1 inch total and
about inch differential
It should be emphasized that proper compaction of the back
filled material is essential to the proper performance of the

Community School Library
The soil exploration disclosed a fractured rock formation at
shallow depths of about 1 to 3 5 feet below this building site
Spread or wall footings bearing on the underlying rock forma
tion are recommended for this library building
An allowable bearing value of up to 5000 p s f is recommended
for footings bearing on this fractured rock formation The
footings should extend a minimum of 12 inches in to the frac
tured rock formation

Small cavities were noted in the rock formation during the
drilling operations It is advisable to probe the footing
excavations to determine if there are any large near surface
cavities which could cause footing settlement The probe
holes should have a minimum diameter of 2 inches and should
extend below the bottom of footing a minimum depth of 5 feet
The probe holes should be grouted from the bottom of the hole
using low pressures
If a cavity is suspected below a footing additional probe
holes should be made to delineate the horizontal extent of the
cavity The probing and grouting operations should be moni
tored by the soils engineer or the State project inspector
to see that these recommendations are carried out properly and
to locate any potential voids near the footing excavations
Anticipated settlements for these footings on rock formation
are very small
Surface water should be diverted away from the foundations

concrete floor slab Friction may be taken at normal dead
load times a coefficient of friction equal to 0 3 for the
brown silty soil or 0 6 for rock formation
Slab On Grade
Based on the laboratory test results the on site soil have
low expansion potential therefore no special provisions for
expansive soils are required for slab on grade construction
To reduce differential settlements where the slab rests par
tially on rock and partially on existing soil or fill the rock
should be overexcavated 12 inches below the floor slab and re
placed with compacted granular material
Slab subgrades close to the existing grade should be free of
topsoil The subgrade soil should be kept moist and recom
pacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum density established
by ASTM D 1557 A 4 inch layer of base course should be pro
vided above the subgrade and compacted to 95 of its maximum
dry density

To protect against moisture damage of the flooring material
a polyethylene moisture barrier should be placed below the
concrete slab
Earthwork and Compaction Requirements
Footing excavation backfills should be placed in 8 inch thick
loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry den
sity within the slab areas
All areas to receive fill should be undercut to remove the
surface vegetation and topsoil Prior to placement of any
new select backfill and fill the ground surface should be
brought back to near optimum moisture content and compacted
to a minimum of 90 of maximum dry density Additional fill
should be non expansive select fill placed in 8 inch thick
loose lifts and compacted to the above specified density
The on site silty and sandy soil can be used as fill and back
fill material provided that no rocks in excess of 6 inch in
diameter are incorporated into the fills
