In accordance with our fee proposal dated August 21 2001 we have conducted a
geotechnical engineering exploration at selected locations within the parking lot of the
Neal Blaisdell Center on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This report summarizes our work on
the project and presents our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our analyses for the subject project
Project Considerations
The on grade parking area of the center consists of asphaltic concrete pavement
Over the years several pavement areas have experienced continued settlement li is
desired to evaluate the possible cause s and possible remedial measures for these
distressed pavement areas Six separate pavement distress areas are covered in this
report The approximate locations of the distressed areas are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 1 Photographs of the distressed areas are included in Appendix C
Area 1
This area is located in the parking stalls near the rear of the Concert Hall along the
access driveway A section of the pavement approximately the size of one and one half
parking stalls has experienced settlement despite two pavement overlays Previous
borings were drilled by Ernest K Hirata and Associates and encountered up to 24 inches
of asphaltic concrete underlain by soft soils
Area 2
This area is located in the loading zone between the Concert Hall and the
Exhibition Area This area is subjected to very heavy vehicular truck traffic A linear
pattern of pavement settlement was visible with depressions as deep as about 8 inches It
was reported that this area experienced continual settlement that eventually resulted in a
water main break It is our understanding that the pavement was removed along with the
underlying soft soils for a depth of 5 to 10 feet and replaced with concrete The concrete
backfilled area has stabilized however adjacent pavement areas continue to settle

these objectives we conducted an exploration program generally consisting of the
following tasks and work efforts

subsurface conditions prior to the construction of the Arena

operators to the project site and back
3 Drilling and sampling of 12 boreholes to depths of approximately
6 3 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface
4 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
engineer geologist from our firm

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed variable subsurface conditions at the site There
appears to be a relatively consistent coral layer at about 6 to 10 feet below the
pavement level in the northern portion of the facility deepening to about 13 to 18 feet
toward Kapiolani Boulevard However the materials between the pavement and the
coral formation varied from very loose silty sand to stiff clay within short horizontal
distances This layer represents natural lagoonal deposits fills placed prior to the
development of the Neal Blaisdell facility and fills placed during construction of the
facility
Possible Causes of Pavement Distress
Based on our field observations discussions with the maintenance staff and the
results of the borings several possible causes of the pavement distress have been
identified and are discussed below

Inadequate Pavement Thickness
The surface asphaltic concrete pavement thickness was generally observed to
be about 2 inches thick below the parking areas along Kapiolani Boulevard and
Ward Avenue Areas 4 5 and 6 and about 4 inches thick in the loading areas
between the Concert Hall and Exhibition Area Areas 2 and 3 and 5 inches thick
in the open parking adjacent to the Concert Hall Area 1 Thicker asphaltic
concrete layers encountered in the borings represent overlays placed to
compensate for the pavement settlement The AC thickness appears to be
adequate for the anticipated traffic however due to the age of the facility the
pavements are deteriorated and may be near the end of the design life
The base course material below the surface pavement was observed to be
inadequate in material quality and density The material was not well graded and
contained excessive fines Additionally the material was poorly compacted
greatly reducing the bearing capacity
Soft Subsoils
In most of the distressed areas the material below the pavement section
consisted of loose silty sand to clayey silt Ground settlements could have
occurred due to compression of this layer from the weight of the fill placed during
construction of the facility However we would expect that these settlements
would have essentially stopped by now The loose subsoils could also adversely
affect the pavement areas due to reduced support of the pavement under heavy
wheel loads resulting in localized subgrade bearing failure
Rubbish Fills
It was reported that during previous repairs of Area 2 behind the Concert Hall
miscellaneous materials including wood concrete and rubbish were excavated
A review of an old airphoto taken prior to the construction of the facility indicates
that a house was situated at this location The debris from the house demolition
could have been buried in this area Boring No 4 which was drilled in this area
encountered concrete rubble from depths of about 4 feet Debris fills could
cause pavement distress and settlement due to decomposition of organic
materials reduced bearing capacity and loss of finer materials through voids
between concrete rubble
Water Seepage
Wet subgrade conditions were noted in the borings in Area 4 We noted that the
sprinklers near the ponds in this area were leaking Excessive water will soften
and reduce the strength of the subgrade soil increasing the susceptibility to
bearing failure

Tree Root Uplifting
The root system of large trees can cause uplifting of the pavement This appears
evident in Area 6 where the pavement and concrete curb has been raised and
buckled
Underground Stream
Due to the continued settlement of the pavement despite the age of the facility
and the linear feature of some of the distressed areas it was speculated that
there may be an underground stream below portions of the site Flowing water
could cause loss of finer material resulting in ground settlements However our
field exploration did not encounter evidence of the underground stream
Recommended Repairs
The scope of our work included localized repair recommendations for the
six areas __ identified However considerations should be _ given to
rehabilitation reconstruction of the entire open parking pavement due to the age of the
facility It appears that portions of the pavement may be near the end of the design life
In particular the pavement between the Concert Hall and Exhibition Area is severely
deteriorated Repair recommendations for each area are presented below
Area 1
Significant settlement on the order of 2 feet may have occurred in this area
Since this area is used only for parking with minimal heavy traffic the settlement
is attributed to compression of the subsoils Loose soils extending down to about
7 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface were encountered in the previously
drilled borings Removal and replacement of these loose soils with structural fill
would greatly reduce the potential for future settlement
The affected area is approximately 22 feet by 23 feet in plan and covers about
two parking stalls However the subsidence appears to extend beyond the
landscaped median and into the access road to the parking structure This
additional area is approximately 17 feet wide by 23 feet across the road
The loose material should be removed down to stiff material or the water level
Imported select borrow should be used for the structural fill The select borrow
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed
in level lifts not exceeding eight inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 The reconstructed
pavement section should consist of 3 inches of asphaltic concrete over 6 inches
of base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Area 2
The severely distressed area is about 20 feet wide by 100 feet long extending from
the Concert Hall rear entrance across the driveway Loose soils with concrete
rubble were encountered in this area down to about 8 5 feet This area is subjected
to very heavy traffic loading therefore the pavement distress and settlement may
be due to subgrade bearing failure as well as ground settlement It was reported by
maintenance personnel that previous pavement repairs were unsuccessful until the
soft material and debris fills were removed and replaced with concrete
The loose material and debris below the affected area may be removed and
replaced with compacted select borrow as described for Area 1 The reconstructed
pavement section may consist of 5 inches of asphaltic concrete over 8 inches of
base course
Area 3
This is a very localized distressed area approximately 6 feet by 7 feet in plan The
two borings drilled did not encounter thick deposits of soft material We
recommend that a new pavement section be installed consisting of 4 inches of
asphaltic concrete over 12 inches of base course underlain by a layer of high
strength geotextile such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent
Area 4
Significant pavement settlement was noted in this area extending in a linear
pattern across the width of the parking lot The distressed area is approximately
90 feet long and 65 feet wide the width of the parking area The borings indicated
soft soils ranging in depth from 1 5 feet closer to the Arena to 6 feet along the
outside edge of the parking area Removal and replacement of these soft soils
with structural fill as described for Area 1 would greatly reduce the potential for
future settlement The reconstructed pavement section should consist of
3 inches of asphaltic concrete over 6 inches of base course compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The leaking sprinkler system noted in this area should be repaired
Area 5
This is a localized distressed area approximately 3 feet by 16 feet in plan with a

significant thickness of soft material It appears that this area is situated behind the
rubbish bins and may be subject to periodic heavy traffic We recommend that a
new pavement section be installed consisting of 4 inches of asphaltic concrete over
12 inches of base course underlain by a layer of high strength geotextile such as
Mirafi 500X or equivalent

Site Preparation
Soft soils encountered should be over excavated to expose firm material or the
groundwater level and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with select borrow
placed in 8 inch maximum loose lifts moisture conditioned to at least the optimum
moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
ASTM D 1557 The excavated fill soils should be disposed of properly off site and or
used in landscape areas where appropriate
Aggregate base course required for the project should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate and should conform to Section 703 06 of the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Aggregate base
course materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from the field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between and
beyond the field borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident Geolabs
should be contacted to re evaluate the recommendations presented in this report
The boring locations in the field were determined by taping from the Neal Blaisdell
Center Site Plan undated The physical locations of the field borings should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict
the approximate boundaries between the soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed
and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to tidal fluctuation variation in rainfall temperatures and other
factors
