This letter report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical evaluation

Ahuwale Street in the Wailupe Valley area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project

presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
Project Considerations
The project involves improvements to the Ahuwale Ditch drainage system
adjacent to properties along the west side of Ahuwale Street and Aipuni Street and the
connected drain line that outlets to Wailupe Stream drainage channel. The properties
adjacent to the drainage ditch are Tax Map Key (TMk): 3-6-17:065 to 3-6-17:067
and 3-6-20:001 to 3-6-20:010. The drain line is located in an easement between
TMK: 3-6-20:002 and 003 and 3-6-17:035, 036 and 3-6-20:104. An intake structure from
the drainage ditch to the drain line is on the upslope portion of the easement.
ParEn, Inc. dba Park Engineering performed a drainage study on the drainage
ditch and pipe drainage system. The drainage ditch was divided into two sections,
Ditch “A” and Ditch “B”. Ditch “A” and Ditch “B” are to the north and south sides of the
intake structure, respectively. Based on the drainage study, several areas along
Ditch “A” and Ditch “B” would encounter overtopping during the peak runoff event.
Based on the results of the drainage study, Ditch “A” and Ditch “B” will require
improvements. The proposed drainage ditch improvements will consist of replacing the
existing concrete rubble masonry (CRM) wall with a higher wall. The proposed wall

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. The project site is located at the
southwestern flank of the Koolau Mountain Range.
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level.
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations.
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as
colluvium.

Site Description/Reconnaissance
The drainage ditch is upslope of the properties along the west side of
Ahuwale Street and Aipuni Street. The drainage ditch is adjacent to TMK: 3-6-17:065 to
3-6-17:067 and 3-6-20:001 to 3-6-20:010. The pipe drainage system is in an easement
between TMK:3-6-20:002 and 003 and 3-6-17:035, 036 and 3-6-20:104. The drainage
ditch intake structure to the pipe drainage system is on the upslope portion of the
easement. The project location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
As previously mentioned, the drainage ditch has been divided into two sections,
Ditch “A” and Ditch “B”. Ditch “A” extends about 505 feet north from the intake structure.
Based on our site reconnaissance and the topographic survey map provided, Ditch “A”
slopes down toward the intake structure. The existing elevations of the bottom of
Ditch “A” range from about +185 to +258 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The lower portion
of Ditch “A”, extending from about 221 feet from the intake structure, is bounded by an
earth berm on the east side and the existing slope face on the west side. The bottom of
the ditch along this portion was not lined. The bottom of the ditch and the existing slope
face exposed medium hard basalt rock formation and consolidated colluvial deposits.
The consolidated colluvial deposits were present from the intake structure to about
210 feet north of the intake structure. The consolidated colluvial deposits consist of light
brown highly cemented sand with boulders and cobbles. The earth berm ranges in
height from about 2 to 3 feet and consists of the unconsolidated colluvial deposits that
may have been excavated within the surrounding areas. The unconsolidated colluvial
deposits consist of brown silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles, gravel,
and sand. The upper portion of the ditch is bounded by a CRM wall on the east side.
The CRM wall is about 284 feet long and about 1 to 2 feet high measured from the
bottom of the ditch. The bottom of the ditch along this portion has a concrete lining. The
existing slope face exposed medium hard basalt rock and unconsolidated colluvial
deposits consisting of brown silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles,
gravel, and sand.
Ditch “B” is to the south of the intake structure and extends about 205 feet.
Based on our site reconnaissance and the topographic survey provided, Ditch “B”
slopes down toward the intake structure. The existing elevations of the bottom of
Ditch “B” range from about +183 to +195 feet MSL. Ditch “B” is bounded by the existing
slope face on the west side and alternates with a CRM wall and an earth berm on the
east side. The bottom of the ditch has a partial concrete lining along the portions
bounded by the CRM wall and is unlined along the portions bounded by the earth berm.
The bottom of ditch along the unlined portions and the existing slope face exposed
medium hard basalt rock formation and unconsolidated colluvial deposits consisting of
brown silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles, and sand. The earth berm is
about 1 foot high and consists of the unconsolidated colluvial deposits that may have
been excavated within the surrounding areas. The CRM wall ranges from about 1 to
2.5 feet high.

Discussion and Recommendations
The major considerations for this project are the very limited access for construction
equipment and the proximity of existing houses adjacent to the drain line. Additionally, the
general area has a history of landslides. The exposed conditions in the existing drainage
ditch consisted mostly of basalt rock and consolidated older alluvium. However, less
competent clay soils may be present along the lower drain line alignment.
Based on the conditions encountered at the site, two options may be considered for
the improvements of the existing drainage channel. The two options are a gravity wall
constructed from concrete rubble masonry or a concrete wall with a reverse L-footing.
Detailed discussions and recommendations for preliminary evaluation of the project are
presented in the following sections herein.
Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) Wall
The CRM wall foundations may be designed using an allowable bearing pressure
of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot bearing on the colluvial deposits. This bearing value
is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such
as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
The wall foundations should be at least 18 inches wide and should be embedded a
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. For sloping ground
conditions, the footing should:extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance
measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall
footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped
footings. The bottom of footing elevations should be recompacted to provide a firm and
unyielding base.
Lateral loads acting on the CRM wall may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of
0.35 may be used. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 100 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for sloping
ground conditions. This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted.
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected.

Since the CRM walls will be along a slope, we anticipate that the embedment depth
of the wall will be relatively deep to provide the minimum 6-foot setback distance
recommended above. In addition, we anticipate that the base of the wall will be relatively
wide. Typically, the base of a gravity wall is about 60 percent of the height of the gravity
wall. Based on the relatively deep embedment depth and wide base of the wall, we
envision significant excavation and backfilling will be required to construct the CRM wall.
Concrete Wall
A concrete wall with a reverse L-footing may be used in lieu of a gravity CRM wall.
A concrete wall with a reverse L-footing should reduce the amount of excavation and
backfilling required to construct the wall since a deep embedment depth is not required.
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the site, a concrete wall with a
reverse L-footing may be used for the channel side on the east side. An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot bearing on the colluvial deposits may be
used. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by
one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
The reverse footing should be at least 48 inches wide. In addition, the reverse
footing should have a minimum thickness of 6 inches. The bottom of footing elevations
should be recompacted to provide a firm and unyielding base. Lateral loads acting on the
wall may be resisted by friction developed between the bottom of the foundation and the
bearing soil. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used.
Ditch Lining Drainage
The sidewalls of the drainage ditch along the existing slope should be well drained
to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of
a 12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at
the base of the sidewalls discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be
used instead of the drainage material.
Site Grading
Based on the existing topography and the available information, we understand that
some cuts below the existing bottom of the ditch and into the existing slope will be required
to provide the minimum channel width.
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered
during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled

with well-compacted fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed
properly off-site. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over-excavation and fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation
quantities.
After clearing and grubbing, the bottom of the ditch (in soil conditions) should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Compaction by water tamping, jetting, or
ponding should not be allowed for the on-site soils.
A qualified geotechnical technician under the supervision of a licensed geotechnical
engineer with a minimum of 10 years of experience should observe site grading
operations.
Cut Slopes
Based on our site reconnaissance, the existing slope face along Ditch “A” and
Ditch “B” exposed medium hard basalt rock formation and colluvial deposits. The
colluvial deposits consist of brown silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles,
and sand and light brown highly cemented sand with boulders and cobbles. The
approximate boundaries of the basalt rock and colluvial deposits are shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 2.
Since a lining consisting of shotcrete or GRP is planned along the cut slopes of the
ditch, we believe that the slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of one horizontal
to one vertical (1H:1V) or flatter. However, if a lining is not planned, the cut slopes should
be flattened to 2H:1V or flatter where colluvial deposits consisting of the brown silty clay
with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles, gravel, and sand are exposed at the slope
face.
Excavation
The site is generally inaccessible for large construction equipment. Therefore, we
anticipate that very small equipment and hand tools will be used. Therefore, excavation
into the colluvial deposits consisting of highly cemented silty sand with boulders and
cobbles and brown silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles, gravel, and sand
may be difficult. Additionally, excavations into the medium hard basalt may require the use
of pneumatic hand excavators.

Drain Line
As previously mentioned, the proposed drain line is planned within an easement
between residential structures. The majority of the invert depths of the drain line will
extend to about 4.5 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our
reconnaissance, we anticipate that the drain line excavations may encounter colluvial
deposits consisting of boulders and cobbles in a silty clay matrix. The contractor should
anticipate difficulty in excavation and significant over-break of the sides and bottom of the
trench excavations considering the nature of the materials at the site.
Due to the limited space, we believe that sloping of the trench excavation will not be
possible; therefore, temporary shoring will be required for the trench excavation. Based on
the anticipated subsurface conditions (boulders and cobbles), sheet piles for use as
temporary shoring may not be feasible. The sheet piles may not be driven to its required
depth if boulders are encountered during installation. A temporary shoring system
consisting of soldier pile and lagging may be considered for shoring support.
The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the
shoring installation. His/her representative, who should be required to be continuously
present on site during excavation and construction work, will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsurface
soil conditions, unexpectedly high groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence
or techniques, etc., which may adversely affect shoring stability.
Since residential structures are adjacent to the excavation, the existing residential
structure should be underpinned to reduce settlement due to potential lateral movement of
the excavation shoring system and vibrations during excavation. There are several
different methods that can be implemented to underpin the foundations of the existing
residential structures. Some of the common types that are used include micropiles and
underpinning piers. In general, design of the underpinning system is outside the scope of
this report and should be made the responsibility of the contractor. Additionally, a damage
survey should be conducted before and after construction to document the effects of the
construction.

Limitations
The analyses and preliminary recommendations submitted herein are based in part
upon information obtained from our site reconnaissance. Variations of subsoil conditions
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction is underway. An additional design-level geotechnical engineering exploration
of the proposed project should be conducted for final design by a licensed geotechnical
engineer.

attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs.
