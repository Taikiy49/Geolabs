In accordance with your request we have performed percolation tests
for the above referenced project in order to provide subsurface
permeability data for use in evaluating the capacities of the existing
cesspools and for the design of new cesspools
Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal of
This report summarizes our findings and test results
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
Our previous report for the foundation and site grading design
aspects of the above referenced project had been submitted on July 22
1987 Subsequently it was decided to determine the feasibility of
utilizing the existing cesspools for the proposed development

The purpose of the percolation tests was to evaluate the permeability
of the underlying soils adjacent to the existing cesspool locations and
at a proposed cesspool site It is our understanding that the cesspools
will be utilized in conjunction with a septic tank
The scope of our services included a review of information obtained
from our previous borings and the drilling of two additional test holes at
the site The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 1
Percolation tests were then performed in each of the borings to
evaluate the permeability characteristics of the comparatively more
permeable zones encountered at approximate depths of 7 5 to 10 0 feet
below the existing ground surface
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions were explored by reviewing information
obtained from our previous borings and the drilling of two 2 additional
percolation test holes B 303 and B 304 to a depth of 10 0 feet below the
existing ground surface
The previous borings generally encountered a 3 foot thick surface
fill layer consisting of dense brown silty sand with gravel to stiff
brown clayey silt underlain by dark gray organic clayey silt to a depth of
about 7 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Underlying the organic clayey silt was loose to medium dense brown
silty gravel grading to stiff tannish brown sandy silt to a depth of 16 5
feet the maximum depth drilled

PERCOLATION TESTS
Percolation tests were performed in Borings 303 and 304 in order to
evaluate the in situ soil permeability After completion of the drilling
a 3 inch I D steel casing was inserted in the borings for the
percolation tests The falling head test procedure was used for the tests
and the test data are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
From the test data the calculated average soil permeabilities in
Borings 303 and 304 are 1 1 x 107 and 2 4 x
respectively
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATTONS
Based on our field exploration and test results it appears that the
percolation rate of the underlying soils adjacent to the existing cesspool
B 304 is low while at the eastern end of the existing building B 303
near the proposed cesspool percolation rate is fair to good This is
further confirmed by visual examination of the soils encountered in the
borings
The low percolation rate for the underlying soils adjacent to the
existing cesspools B 304 may be the result of silting and clogging of
the receiving formation due to the past discharge of the existing
cesspools Therefore it is our opinion that the existing cesspools may
have a limited service life
The capacities of the new cesspools would be governed by the average
permeability of the underlying soils and the dimensions of the cesspools

It should be pointed out however that the loss of infiltrative
capacity and soil clogging can occur in the future by combined physical
chemical and biological factors Other factors such as rise in

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the proposed sites were explored by
drilling two 2 percolation test holes at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 A truck mounted drilling rig was used
to drill the borings to a depth of 10 feet below the existing ground
surface
The soil encountered in the borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by one of our engineers who
continuously monitored the drilling operations The classifications were
further reviewed by visual observation and testing in the laboratory
The soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 4
In the borings soil samples were obtained by driving a 2 inch
standard split spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches
The blow counts used to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on
the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
