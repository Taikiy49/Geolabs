leach fields to be located uphill of the existing restroom at Fern Grotto of the Wailua River
State Park in the District of Wailua on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct leach fields for sewage disposal in the area uphill of the
existing restroom at Fern Grotto of the Wailua River State Park The sewage will therefore be
pumped up from the existing restroom to the proposed leach field site Utilities are proposed
to be installed along the hillside between the restroom and leach field site The existing
ground elevations at the proposed leach field site range from about 274 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL to 282 feet MSL The ground elevation of the existing restroom is about 27 feet MSL

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial sediments
of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai including Wailua These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense
basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends at various locations
Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones
at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the growth
and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water was
available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below its current stand As the glaciers
melted and retreated more water became available thus the sea level rose and the streams
aggraded their floors to form fills When the sea level fell the streams removed part or all of
the fills resulting in superimposed complex valley in valley deposits of gravel boulders and
mud
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lava of the
Koloa Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense
vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers The weathering process has formed

a mantle of residual soils near the surface In general residual soils are composed mainly
of clayey and silty materials which are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks
The residual soils generally grade to saprolite and basaltic rock formation with depth

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field percolation tests we believe that a percolation rate of
60 minutes per inch may be used in the design of the septic tanks and leach fields This
percolation rate corresponds to a recommended maximum rate of sewage application of
0 6 gallons per square foot per day
To increase the probability of intersecting the more permeable layer orange brown
clayey silt material we recommend that the septic tanks and leach fields be set at least 5 feet
below grade and lined at the bottom with at least 3 feet of open graded gravel material

Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project to
connect the existing restroom to the proposed leach field The methods and equipment to be
used for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations The excavation should comply with all applicable local state and
federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should be the
responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate
health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining
backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill immediately around utility
pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finished subgrade should consist of approved on site soils or select granular material The
backfill material should be moisture conditioned to a minimum of 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4362 00 Page 7
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction
equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting
or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill material
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey soils are critical elements
of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by Geolabs Inc during
site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the
proper moisture content Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction It should be noted that the moisture requirement a minimum of 2 percent above
the optimum moisture is an important requirement for the on site clayey soils

APPENDIX A
Field Percolation Testing for Leach Fields
Three percolation tests were performed in three shallow probe holes designated as
Probe Hole Nos P 1 through P 3 near the proposed leach field location The probe holes
extended to depths of about 3 3 to 3 9 feet below the existing ground surface The testing
was performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s
Administrative Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the
Ten States Standards to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials
for wastewater disposal septic tanks and leach fields The approximate locations of the
probe holes are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The probe holes were drilled using
3 5 inch diameter hand auger equipment
The materials encountered in the probe holes were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineers who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System Representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Site
Evaluation Percolation Test Plates A 1 through A 3
Prior to performing the tests approximately one inch of gravel was placed at the bottom
of the probe holes to protect the bottom of the holes from scour and sediments During
testing the holes were carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the
gravel pack The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon
which the calculation of the percolation rates were based The results of our percolation tests
are presented on the Site Evaluation Percolation Test Plates A 1 through A 3
