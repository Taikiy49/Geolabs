INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the Komohana Street Bridge Replacement project at the Alenaio Stream crossing in the
District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of the box culvert
foundations retaining structures and site grading only and are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report A Pavement Justification Report is required for this project
and has been issued separately
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Town of Hilo in the District of South Hilo on the Island
of Hawaii It is proposed to remove the existing culvert structure and construct a new
concrete box culvert structure along Komohana Street at the Alenaio Stream crossing We
understand that during periods of heavy rainfall the storm water overtops Komohana Street
and floods adjacent properties Due to the inadequate capacity of the existing corrugated
metal pipe culvert construction of a new box culvert structure is desired
Based on the available information the new box culvert will be designed to
accommodate a 100 year storm producing approximately 7 000 cubic feet per second cfs
of peak flood flow In addition the design of the new box culvert structure will take into
account the future widening of Komohana Street to four lanes
We understand that the new reinforced concrete box culvert will have dimensions of
approximately 32 feet in width and 9 feet in height Based on a study conducted by the
U S Army Corps of Engineers we understand that the new structure may concentrate the
flow of Alenaio Stream increasing the water velocities from approximately 6 feet per second
fps to about 33 fps To reduce the potential for escalated erosion caused by the increased

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area
of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the eruptive activity of five
major shield volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had
some activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna
Loa and Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The most recent lava flows that comprise the ground surface and
shallow subsurface of the project site are believed to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age
approximately 12 million years to 2 million years before present time These lava flows
belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The lava formation at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which is characterized
by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities or voids are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities were formed
when the lava was still in a molten state and represent lava tubes intra flow cavities and
blisters and or pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains
from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
GEOLABS INC
W O 3940 00 A Page 5
distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and or pockets inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

Box Culvert Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the on site basalt rock formation would provide suitable foundation support for the proposed
box culvert structure As previously mentioned it is generally difficult to provide a smooth
level bearing surface when the bearing surface is composed of basalt formation Therefore
we anticipate that the excavated surfaces on the basalt formation will likely be irregular and
over excavated in localized areas Due to concerns regarding the potential for scour we
recommend that placement of fill materials to fill in the irregular and over excavated surfaces
be avoided When over excavation of the basalt formation occurs the subgrade surfaces
should be leveled and or backfilled to the bottom of the box culvert structure using concrete
or lean concrete
For the design of foundations bearing on the basalt rock or on concrete fill an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 6 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces For design of the concrete box
culvert slab a modulus of subgrade reaction of 350 pounds per square inch per inch of
deflection pci may be used for the slab bearing directly on the basalt rock
The bottom of the foundations should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below
the lowest adjacent finished grade Where foundations are to be located adjacent to
below grade structures or utilities the foundations should extend to a depth below an
imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein total settlement of foundations is estimated to be on the
order of about 0 5 inches with differential settlements on the order of about 0 25 inches
GEOLABS INC
W O 3940 00 A Page 9
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance between
the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations
A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the basalt rock or
concrete fill Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be calculated using
an equivalent fluid pressure of 500 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf for
foundations embedded in the basalt rock These values assume that the concrete for
foundations is cast neat against the basalt rock Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic lava
flows we recommend that a program of probing and grouting the foundation footprint be
implemented during construction A representative from Geolabs should observe the probing
and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Probing and
grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of this
report In addition we recommend that foundation excavations be observed by a
representative from Geolabs prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to
confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area voids and or cavities may be
present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below foundations we recommend
that a program of cavity probing and grouting of the foundation locations for the box culvert
structure be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that one probe hole be drilled for every
75 square feet of foundation footprint The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in
diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation If
cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional
probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the vertical and lateral
GEOLABS INC
W O 3940 00 A Page 10
extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be
backfilled with controlled low strength material CLSM with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches
The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures As an alternate to CLSM
sand cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches may also be used
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are completed The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time
between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii dynamic
lateral earth forces will need to be considered in the design of retaining structures
We understand that the project will be designed based on an acceleration
coefficient of 0 45g The dynamic lateral earth force due to seismic loading
may be estimated by 22H pounds per foot of wall length for level
backfill where H is the height of the wall in feet The dynamic lateral earth forces
presented above are in addition to the static lateral earth pressures An
appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth
forces are accounted for in the design of the retaining structures
Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation

retaining structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should
also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the retaining
structures
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill material

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum

Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of rock

passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be

less than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill material should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches will
be located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of approximately 50 and
51 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
