for the Scour Evaluation of Oahu Bridges, Phase 3 project located at the various bridge locations

SECTION 3.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest for the geotechnical analysis are the subsurface materials encountered at the 
project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, and the variability of the 
subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following subsections provide a 
description of the geologic setting of the project site and the surface and subsurface conditions 
encountered at the site. 
3.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity at Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau 
Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming the Schofield Plateau. The project sites 
are situated between the northern and-western coasts of the Island of Oahu, which is in the 
general area of the contact between the Waianae and Koolau Shields. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene Epoch and early Pleistocene 
Epoch by the extrusion of successive thin bedded lava flows. The main shield-building stage 
ceased approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from historic drilled wells indicates that the 
Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of the early volcanic 
activity (MacDonald and Abbott, 1970). During the period of island subsidence, coral-algal reefs 
began to grow along the southern coast of Oahu forming embayments protected by barrier reefs. 
A series of lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments 
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental glaciers 
accumulated, the level of the oceans fell because there was less water available to fill the oceanic 
basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded (melted), global sea levels rose because the volume 
of water increased. The land mass comprising the Island of Oahu remained essentially stable

during these water level changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of sea that were both higher and lower relative to present 
sea level on the Island of Oahu. 
The process of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level 
fluctuations. The higher sea level stands caused landform changes including the accumulation of 
deltas and alluvial fans composed of terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, the 
accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and the accumulation of 
lagoonal and/or marine sediments in the quiet lagoonal waters protected by barrier reefs. The 
concurrent growth of reefs and the accumulation of lagoonal sediments also resulted in the 
deposition of coral-algal limestone and marl materials within the predominantly lagoonal 
sedimentary unit. 
The lower sea level stands caused streams to carve drainageways into the coastal plain 
platforms composed of sedimentary and coral reef deposits. In addition, subaerial exposure of 
the calcareous sediments caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits 
and the induration of calcareous reef materials. Furthermore, renewed subaerial erosion acting 
at the upper elevations of the volcanic shield caused the downstream deposition of terrigenous 
alluvial sediments under relatively higher energy conditions. 
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued meandering stream action 
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvial materials overlying the 
marine-lagoonal sediments. 
As discussed above, the majority of the Island of Oahu was formed during the main 
volcanic shield building stage, which eventually experienced a hiatus. After a long period of 
volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield Volcano 
along the accumulation of the terrigenous and lagoonal deposits along the coastal area, volcanic 
activity returned to portions of the Island of Oahu as series of localized lava flows followed by 
explosive cinder and tuff cone formations. These late eruptions belong to the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series and are believed to have occurred between about 30,000 and 800,000 years ago.

Site Description

3.2.1 Kamehameha Highway Aiea Interchange #2 (West ID No. 1) 
The bridge is located on the Interstate Route H-1 Highway on-ramp that spans over Aiea 
Stream before the Interstate Route H-1 Highway crosses Kamehameha Highway 
(Route 99) in the Aiea area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The Interstate Route H-1

the Aiea Stream with Pier No. 1 located generally in the middle of the stream.

The Kahalu`u Stream Bridge is located on Kahekili Highway and spans over the Kahalu`u

generally by Waihee Road on the west and the parking lot entrance of the Calvary Chapel

roadway is covered by asphaltic concrete. Based on the provided as-built plans, the 
Kahalu`u Stream Bridge consists of three spans with two piers and two abutments. Based

Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at each bridge site by drilling and sampling two 
borings at each location (a total of sixteen borings), identified as Boring Nos. B-1 through B-16. 
The borings extended to depths of about 56.5 to 121.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans (Plates 3.1 through 3.8). 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of Borings are 
presented in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples and 
rock cores are presented in Appendix B. Photographs of the core samples retrieved from our field 
exploration are presented in Appendix C. 
The subsurface conditions at the selected bridge locations are presented in the following 
subsections. 
3.3.1 Kamehameha Highway Aiea Interchange #2 
Boring Nos. B-1 and B-2 were drilled in the vicinity Piers 2 and 1a of the Interstate Route 
H-1 Highway on-ramp, respectively. The borings generally encountered 4.5 to 5 feet of fill 
consisting of stiff silty and sandy clays. The fill was underlain by lagoonal deposits and/or 
marsh deposits consisting of very soft silty clays, sandy clays, and clayey silts and loose 
clayey sands extending to depths of about 42 to 53 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Alluvial soil deposits were encountered below the lagoonal and marsh deposits extending 
to the maximum depth explored at this bridge location of about 83.1 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The alluvial soils generally consisted of very soft to hard gravelly 
clays, silty clays, and clayey silts, and medium dense to very dense clayey gravel, gravelly 
boulders, and cobbly boulders. A layer of shallow marine deposit generally consisting of 
stiff silty clays and medium dense silty gravel was encountered from 57 to 68.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface in Boring No. B-1. Groundwater was encountered in Boring

GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM 
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the project generally consisted of 
the following field and laboratory work components. A brief description of each work component 
is presented below. Detailed discussions, findings, and test results of the various components are 
provided in the respective appendices herein. 
4.1 
Soil Borings, Rock Coring, and Associated Field Testing 
The field exploration is described in more detail in the introduction to Appendix A. The 
following discussion provides an overview of the methods and procedures. Typically, soil borings 
were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight, solid-stem augers (SSA) to the first 
encounter with groundwater and then continued with PQ-size wireline coring methods to 
advance the PQ-size casing from the ground surface. In general, auger drilling was used in areas 
where medium dense to very dense granular soils exist and in locations where rock coring was 
not anticipated.  
Sampling of the soils was performed using AWJ drill rods (1.75 OD/1.25 ID) at about 
5 to 10-foot intervals. Drive samples were obtained using the modified California sampler 
(ASTM D3550) and the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) split-spoon sampler (ASTM D1586).  
Where the borehole was advanced utilizing the PQ core barrel in the soil formations, 
samples of the soils recovered from the PQ core barrel also were retained in core boxes for visual 
observation. For intervals where the borehole was advanced utilizing the PQ casing only as a 
rotary wash drilling method, there were no soil samples collected. The borings were advanced 
into the rock formations with the PQ-size wireline coring methods. The PQ-size wireline coring 
methods produce rock core samples of 3.35 inches (85 mm) in diameter. 
In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the SPT, which was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. Standard Penetration Test (SPT) sampling provides 
a measure of the resistance or consistency of a soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-value, 
which is defined as the number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon sampler 
12 inches with a 140-lb. hammer, after 6 inches of initial seating. The penetration resistance of

A P P E N D I X   A 
Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at selected locations at or near the various bridge 
sites on the Island of Oahu by drilling and sampling sixteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. B-1 
through B-16. At each bridge location, two borings were typically drilled – one boring at each 
bridge abutment. The borings extended to depths of about 56.5 to 137.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface using either a truck-mounted drill rig and/or a track drill rig equipped with 
continuous-flight augers and rotary coring tools. The field exploration was conducted from 
December 5, 2022 through January 20, 2023. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.8. 
Soil borings were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
(SSA) to the first encounter with groundwater and then continued with PQ-size wireline coring 
methods to advance the PQ-size casing from the ground surface. In general, auger drilling was 
used in areas where medium dense to very dense granular soils exist and in locations where rock 
coring was not anticipated. Additionally, SSA drilling was switched over to the wireline coring 
method when coralline detritus or coral formations were encountered or when soft and/or loose 
soils that caved in were encountered. After the borings were advanced to the maximum depth of 
the borings, the bored holes generally were backfilled with cement-grout. 
Our field personnel classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the 
laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for 
Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on 
the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered and other observations, such as groundwater levels, circulation 
water condition, drilling rate, etc. are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through 
A-16.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Standard Practice for Thick Wall, Ring-Lined, Spilt Barrel, Drive 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer 
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer 
and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by using 
diamond core drilling techniques, consisting of a PQ wireline coring system with double tube core 
barrel (core size of 3.35 inches), in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling 
for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the 
rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for 
examination and description. The coring rates (where indicated) on the Logs of Borings refer to 
the time required to complete that particular core run at that depth. Rock cores were described 
in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based generally on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring attempt 
versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings 
as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality of 
rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the total core run in rock that is sound material 
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, mechanical, 
and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. It should be noted that RQD does not apply to coral material.  However, 
the RQD values of the coral material are included in the boring logs for rough characterization of 
the coral material. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by 
Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Geotechnical Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for the project were 
visually examined and classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected samples were 
tested to evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils 
encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included determination of natural moisture content, dry 
density, Atterberg Limits, grain size distribution, uniaxial compressive strength of rock, Direct 
Shear, and Unconsolidated-Undrained (UU) Triaxial Compression. Results of the tests performed 
are presented on Plates B-1 through B-103. Detailed descriptions of the various tests performed 
for this land boring exploration are presented below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid 
in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between test borings. The moisture content and unit weight tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D2216 and ASTM D7263, respectively. The test results are presented on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
Sixty Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the liquid 
limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an aid in classifying the soils. The Atterberg 
Limits tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D4318 (Method A). The test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations 
of the test results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-6. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, 
98 Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6912), including 80 Hydrometer tests (ASTM D7928), were 
performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-7 through B-26. 
Uniaxial Compressive Strength – Rock Cores 
Nine Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests were performed on selected rock cores to 
evaluate their uniaxial compressive strength of the rock core in general accordance with 
ASTM D7012, Method C. Uniaxial compressive strength test results for the selected rock cores 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and also presented on 
Plates B-27.

Direct Shear 
To evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils, five strain-controlled, 
consolidated-drained Direct Shear tests were performed on the in-situ soil samples in accordance 
with ASTM D3080. The test results are presented on Plates B-28 through B-32.  
Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the clayey soils, seventy-one Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected relatively undisturbed 
soil samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” samples. The test 
results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-33 through B-103.
