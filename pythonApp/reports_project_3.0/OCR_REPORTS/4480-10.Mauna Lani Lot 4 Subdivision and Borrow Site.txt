REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
of which are still active
The Mauna Lani Lot 4 subdivision project and the borrow site are situated nearly due
west of Mauna Kea Volcano and are located adjacent to the northwest facing coastline of the
Island of Hawaii However the project site is actually underlain by basaltic lava flows that
erupted from Mauna Loa Volcano which is located further away than Mauna Kea Volcano and
towards the southeasterly direction from the site The Mauna Loa lava flows deposited at the
project site are believed to be on the order of about 3 000 to 5 000 years old These lava flows
are believed to overlap on the older buried lava flows that erupted from Mauna Kea The lava
flows located within the borrow site consist of both the rubbly severely fractured a a clinker
type and the smooth surfaced less fractured pahoehoe type of basalt rock
The borrow site is located on very gently sloping to nearly flat terrain located at the foot
of hills composed of thick a a lava flow which form significant positive topographic relief The
thick a a lava flows are located toward the northeast through southeast directions from the
project site as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2 Some significant sized drainage
gulches have dissected the thick a a flows and appear to discharge onto the broad low relief
plain comprising the project site and vicinity
As a result it is believed that the thick alluvial soil deposits consisting of silty volcanic
ash soil with rounded stream gravels encountered at the borrow site may have been
deposited in part by stream processes and historic flood events The silty soil materials
appear to mantle the underlying rock formation and have aided in the formation of the broad
silt covered plain that is anomalous to this portion of the island As a result of the thick surface
soil coverage and potential influx of stormwater runoff from the gulches the area supports a
broad zone of mature kiawe trees and grass growth that is conspicuously noted on aerial
photographs
The lava formation encountered by our exploration of the subdivision site appears to
be of the a a type and a transitional a a pahoehoe type of lava flow The transitional
a a pahoehoe flow type exhibits characteristics of both types of lava Specifically the flow
surface resembles both a loose rubbly surface typical of a a lava and areas of hard smooth
rock hummocks typical of pahoehoe lava The lava flows spread downslope from volcanic
vents located much further up on the flank of Mauna Loa The a a lavas are typically

characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation
of rock fragments including tabular plates cobbles and boulders Typically more massive
and significantly more dense layered lava rock material is contained within the central lava
flow core of the thicker flow units
Cavities such as lava tubes open fractures or other void space are commonly
encountered buried within the a a lavas but more predominantly within the pahoehoe lavas
Many of these cavities were formed when the still molten lava created the structures by means
of flowage and ponding of the lava The cavities represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal
extent
The a a pahoehoe basalt lava rock and clinker encountered at the subdivision site is
generally considered to be relatively permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both
horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along
joints fractures inter flow contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers The
permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the primary water
transmission features

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential
for contamination of the excavated materials We wish to point out that caution should
be exercised by the contractor while clearing and grading near collapsed features
which may represent potential lava tubes or cavities that may further collapse under
the load of heavy equipment
After clearing and grubbing we recommend that the fill areas of the project site and
finished subgrades in cut areas be ripped to a minimum depth of 3 feet below the
existing ground surface to assist in detecting and collapsing the cavities and or voids
contained in the porous near surface materials After the ground surfaces have been
ripped to a depth of at least 3 feet below the ground surface the ground should be
leveled with the use of a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar size bulldozer a minimum
of four passes to provide a relatively level surface
After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer we recommend that the fill
subgrades be proof rolled with a large vibratory drum roller minimum 20 tons for a
minimum of six passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids
The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute
The proof rolling operations would also serve to consolidate the porous a a pahoehoe
basalt formation surface clinker materials exposed at the ground surface and to
provide a firm base for the placement of fills
The proof rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a representative
from Geolabs Yielding areas loose areas uncontrolled fills or cavities disclosed

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials 6 inch minus fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
12 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 24 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density We wish to emphasize that moisture conditioning of the fill materials is an
integral part of the fill placement recommendations provided herein Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of each layer of the rock fill should be performed by a bulldozer to facilitate
seating of the rock The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water
trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously
during placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or
similar heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized to compact the rock fill The number of passes to be
made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading
by the performance of a trial compaction test section or by plate bearing tests

encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring and test pit data to make
their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the available grading plan some cut slopes are planned at the northwestern
corner of the project site and some cuts located along the northern roadway entrance
to the site Based on our field exploration it appears that the roadway cuts may
expose the loose to medium hard a a basalt rock located along the existing roadway
entrance The other proposed cut slopes may expose the medium hard a a pahoehoe
basalt rock located at the northwestern portion of the site In general we believe that
the cut slopes planned at the northwestern portion of the site that expose the
a a pahoehoe basalt rock may be designed with a slope inclination of one horizontal
to one vertical 1H 1 V or flatter Loose or porous areas that may be exposed on the
face of the cut slope should be removed and backfilled A representative from Geolabs

White Sand Beach Limited Partnership Page 14
W O 4480 10
should observe the cut slopes during construction to evaluate whether the exposed cut
slope materials are consistent with our assumptions
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and structural fill materials
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched The fill slope face
should be finished to provide a relatively smooth and well compacted face The fill
slope face should be free of large boulders greater than 18 inches in largest
dimension and should be free of large voids which would allow erosion and migration
of fines to occur In addition slope planting or other means of slope protection should
be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished
slopes
Underground Utilities
We envision that new underground utility lines such as sewer water and electrical
lines will be required for development of this project In addition some drain lines associated
with the anticipated drywell or sump pit system for disposal of storm water runoff are also
anticipated It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in the
compacted fill material placed for the proposed construction As previously recommended
placement of boulders within the utility trench corridors and within the anticipated depths of the
utility lines should be avoided to reduce the problems associated with excavating boulders
from within a utility trench
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes As an alternative the granular bedding
layer may consist of 12 inches of structural fill material Free draining granular materials
No 3B Fine gravel should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes Itis critical that free draining
materials be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes
and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could
result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of granular materials generally less than
6 inches in maximum size structural fill The backfill material should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 12 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper
3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction
For the proposed water lines the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the water line pipes should consist of 1 inch minus well graded

initial trench backfill should be moisture conditioned and compacted to not less than

It should be noted that the general fill materials recommended for the mass grading
