INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed family housing improvement project within the Hickam Air
Force Base in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of residential housing foundations
site preparation and pavements The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located on the east and west sides of the Main Gate
at Hickam Air Force Base in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the
information provided it was originally proposed to provide improvements to 102 housing
units under the Basic Contract Due to the available funding for the Basic Contract 22
additional housing units including Building Nos 184 through 187 were transferred from
the Option Contract to the Basic Contract
We understand that the housing units were constructed in 1964 65 and have not
been renovated since their original construction The currently proposed improvement
work will include renovation of kitchens and bathrooms upgrades of electrical and
plumbing systems and other interior configurations The existing old gang type

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Following the cessation of
activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine
environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and
rising about 95 feet above the present sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level
during the late Pleistocene Epoch eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3388 00 Page 6
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers Subsequent
erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of tuff
During the recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits These deposits were
subsequently covered by man made fill placed during the middle portion of this century
for land reclamation projects during construction of the existing Hickam Air Force Base

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by a
surficial fill layer of stiff silty clays Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the
project site it is our opinion that a shallow foundation system bearing on the stiff near
surface fills may be used to provide support for the proposed residential additions
However the near surface soils exhibit highly variable shrink swell characteristics
ranging from slight to very high expansion potential when subjected to fluctuations in
the soil moisture contents As a result we recommend that the near surface soils be
capped with a minimum of 24 inches of non expansive select capping fill material below
all building slab areas In addition special attention should be given to the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3388 00 Page 8
recommended procedures for subgrade preparation of slabs and pavements to reduce
the potential shrink swell effects of the near surface soils The project site
encompasses a relatively large area with near surface soils ranging from slightly to
highly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we recommend
that additional near surface soils be obtained to perform swell tests to delineate the
areas that are underlain by the highly expansive clayey soils in order to reduce the cost
of over excavation and replacement throughout the project site
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of
residential housing foundations site preparation and pavements are further discussed
in the following sections
Foundations
Because of the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site
we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the
proposed new residential expansion An allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds
per square foot p s f may be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the
stiff soils encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind
or seismic forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
they should be embedded below a one horizontal ton one vertical 1H 1V imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3388 00 Page 9
We estimate the footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the stiff soils to be less than 1 inch total and inch differential
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that
the soil around footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff
near surface soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footings bear on the stiff near surface
soils If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over
excavation should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom be extended down to the
underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Slab On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by near
surface silty clay soils with swelling potential ranging from slight to high when subjected
to moisture fluctuations Uniess floor siabs constructed above the highiy expansive
soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from the shrinking and swelling of these expansive clayey soils
due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress
resulting from the swelling of the expansive subgrade soils we recommend that the
subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with a layer of non expansive select
granular fill material for support of slabs on grade The non expansive capping fill
should extend laterally at least 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed structures
and pavement areas The upper 6 inches of capping fill outside the limits of the house
structure should consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce water
infiltration into the underlying select granular fill material as shown on Plate 5
Because the project site encompasses a relatively large area and the variable
expansion potential of the near surface fill materials it is our opinion that additional
near surface soil samples should be obtained for swell tests to delineate the areas that
are underlain by the expansive soils Unless additional swell tests are performed it is
our opinion that all slabs on grades should be supported on a minimum of 24 inches
of imported non expansive select granular fill material
The non expansive capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after
scarification moisture conditioning and compaction of the underlying clayey subgrade
soils Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The non expansive capping
fill and the underlying subgrade soil should be kept moist prior to final placement of
concrete The non expansive select granular fill material should provide a capping
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3388 00 Page 11
layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby
reducing the loss of moisture from the clayey subgrade soils We believe that the
reduction in potential drying and wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the impact
on the slab due to potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when
wetted
To reduce the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce the costs
of form construction we recommend that the slab edges be cast neat against the
soils The slab edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and bottom
longitudinal steel reinforcement The top and bottom steel reinforcement bars should
be connected by stir ups The ends of the stir ups should extend into the floor slab
area and should be tied to the welded wire mesh A structural engineer should be
consulted for design details of this thickened edge which is intended to function both
as a moisture barrier and as a perimeter bearing wall footing The welded mesh in the
slab on grade should be embedded at the proper height in the concrete to provide
adequate slab reinforcement Blocks or saddles may be used to raise the wire mesh
A stiff grade of wire mesh is thus recommended A typical detail showing the slab on
grade design on expansive soil is presented on Plate 5
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation cushion layer be placed below the slab to provide more uniform
support for the slab To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
the floor coverings a moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recom
mended on top of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand should be
placed above the plastic membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to aid
in curing of the slab concrete The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved
area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3388 00 Page 12
Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered
since they can better mask any minor slab cracking It is also recommended that
interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate small amount
of possible ground movements
Where the slab will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the driveways and
garage slabs a 6 inch layer of base course is recommended in lieu of the 4 inch
cushion fill layer vapor barrier and 2 inch sand The base course should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The 24 inch capping layer recommended above for slabs on grade construction may
be reduced to a minimum of 12 inches for driveways and garage slabs
Asphalt paving for the basketball court should be underlain by a 6 inch layer of
base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction A minimum
12 inch thick layer of non expansive select granular material should also be provided
below the base course unless further swell test results indicate otherwise
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel be
provided below the concrete slab Construction joints should be provided at intervals
equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish grades minimal site
grading work is anticipated for the project construction At the on set of earthwork the
area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All
vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3388 00 Page 13
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with
select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted Utilities that are
to be abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by
pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs and
pavement areas should be over excavated to a depth of at least 24 inches below the
bottom of the gravel cushion layer or the base course layer and replaced with
compacted non expansive select granular fill material The depth of over excavation
may be modified if additional swell tests are performed to better delineate the areas
underlain by the highly expansive soils If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are
encountered at the over excavated subgrade levei deeper over excavation may be
required to expose the underlying firm material The resulting over excavation should
be backfilled with compacted non expansive select granular material
The over excavated subgrade surface should be scarified to a minimum depth
of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Non expansive select granular fill material required for the 24 inch capping layer
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture

conditioned and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction under slab
on grade and paved areas
Non expansive select granular material required under the slabs and pavement
areas should consist of imported granular material such as select borrow crushed coral

particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a

subject site The anticipated subgrade soils for the proposed new roadways as

soils The thickness of the non expansive capping fill may be modified if additional
swell test results suggest otherwise Considering the medium traffic anticipated for the
proposed roadways we recommend the following preliminary pavement design for
budgeting purposes

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill
up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes
and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of the on site soils or select
granular material The backfill should be placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage

away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should
be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the
subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 11
borings to depths between 15 and 25 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 through 4 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field geologist who observed the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual obser
vation and testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representa
tion of the materials encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 11
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown
on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
