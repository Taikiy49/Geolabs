INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed New Aloha Council Facility project for the Boy Scouts of America in
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site preparation and
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island and is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age The
younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island and is
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age It is believed that Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava
in Central Oahu After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised
deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
The project site is located in Nuuanu Valley an amphitheater headed valley carved
by erosion into Koolau Basalts Filling the base of this valley is a thick sequence of alluvium
interbedded with deposits of lavas and pyroclastics of the Honolulu Volcanic Series At the
project site the drilled borings encountered surface layers of alluvium overlying basaltic lavas
believed to be of the Honolulu Volcanic Series at greater depths

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff
clayey soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 18 feet below the existing
ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted in situ stiff soils or engineered fill materials may be used to support the
proposed new Aloha Council Facility for the Boy Scouts of America
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit slight to low
shrink swell potential when subjected to normal fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To
reduce the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from the
swelling of the near surface soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Use
of the vibratory action of a compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid inducing a
pumping subgrade condition The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage
cracks prior to placement of cushion fill and or slab concrete Our geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations site preparation and pavements
are further discussed in the following sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new
construction An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted stiff clayey soils
or engineered fills needed to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
GEOLABS INC
W O 4339 00 Page 7
Where footing subgrades are located in cut areas footings bearing on the undisturbed
in situ soils the bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
For footing subgrades in these conditions a visual observation and probing of the footing
subgrade should be performed by a representative of Geolabs to confirm the subgrade
conditions of the footings The footing subgrades should be firm and unyielding A
compaction test of the footing subgrade is not required for these conditions since the
subgrade exist in a relatively high moisture condition
For footings bearing on new engineered fills where the footing subgrades are located
in fill areas the bottom of footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete In the fill
conditions compaction tests should be performed in the footing excavations to confirm that
the compaction requirements of the subgrades are met
It should be emphasized that if the footing subgrades are saturated or softened after
the visual observations probing or compaction testing due to inclement weather the footing
subgrades may need to be over excavated to expose firm ground and backfilled with
compacted fill As an alternative to backfilling with compacted fill the bottom of footing may
be extended deeper to bear on the firm over excavated subgrade
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades Footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining
walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads If foundations are located
next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts
of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the in situ stiff soils or engineered fills
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with slight to low expansion potential when subjected to normal moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture
content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared in accordance with
the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The
prepared subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
GEOLABS INC
W O 4339 00 Page 9
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted
engineered fill
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide
more uniform support of the slabs To reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the No 3B Fine gravel Two inches of moist sand may be
considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid
in curing of the slab concrete Provisions of the 2 inches of moist sand above the vapor
membrane will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask
minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete walkways will be required for the proposed project We recommend
that concrete walkways be supported on 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill or
aggregate subbase The select granular fill or aggregate subbase should be compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Control joints should be provided at intervals equal
to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The thickened edges of slab adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should
be backfilled tightly against the slab with on site soil and graded to divert water away from
the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations

of the new facility Based on the appearances and sizes of these trees we envision that their
root systems would be well established and might encroach into the proposed footprint of the

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately
around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated
drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site clayey silt or
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches
of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls

also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from
the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths ranging from about 6 5 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected soil layers in the field The
pocket penetrometer test provide an indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the
sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
