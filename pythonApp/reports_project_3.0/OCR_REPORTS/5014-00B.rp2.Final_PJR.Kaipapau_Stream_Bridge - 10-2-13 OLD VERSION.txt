SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be constructed as part of the bridge replacement project to
transition from the existing pavements to the new bridge structure Based on our field
exploration we anticipate that majority of the pavement subgrade soils likely will consist of
medium stiff clayey silt and silty clay soils with an R value of about 26
Typically new DOT pavements include a drainage layer to facilitate drainage and
increase the pavement life However the existing road does not have a drainage layer or a
drainage system to collect and discharge the infiltrated water Incorporating a drainage
layer below the two short sections of new pavements at the bridge approaches would not
be feasible nor practical If a drainage layer is included in the new pavement section we
believe the build up of subsurface water within the permeable base layer may result in
saturated soil conditions which would cause the pavement subgrade soils to soften and
result in premature pavement problems Therefore we have considered only pavement
structural sections without a permeable drainage layer in our analyses
Based on the traffic data provided the R value obtained for the subgrade soils and
the economic analyses performed we recommend using a flexible pavement structural
section consisting of 3 inches of AC on 5 inches of ACB over 10 inches of ASB for the new
pavements
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped adequately and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific pavement design recommendations for the replacement bridge
project

Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed
for the design of new pavements for the Kaipapau Stream Bridge Replacement project
in the District of Koolauloa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavements
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The bridge replacement project site is along Kamehameha Highway Route 83
at about Milepost 20 9 in the District of Koolauloa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based
on the information provided we understand the existing Kaipapau Stream Bridge which
spans across Kaipapau Stream will be demolished and replaced with a wider concrete
bridge structure
As part of the project a portion of the roadway pavement will be reconstructed at
the bridge approaches to transition from the existing roadway to the new bridge
structure Therefore this pavement justification report was prepared for the design of
the new pavements
Based on our experience in the project vicinity we anticipate the new pavements
will be underlain by surface fills overlying alluvial deposits at relatively shallow depths

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the
two prominent volcanoes the Wai anae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two the
Wai anae Volcano is the older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years
ago The Ko olau Volcano rose above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago
making it the younger of the two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly
bedded a a and pahoehoe lava flows which erupted from elongate and linear trending
volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes The
Ko olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai anae Volcano to form the
Schofield Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains
which form the western third of the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt rock
formations on the Island of Oahu The Ko olau Mountains form the eastern two thirds of
the island and include the rock layers exposed along the extending from
Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of
Wai anae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet
under the tremendous weight of the rock In addition to the subsidence of the island the
force of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and slumping
of portions of the land mass to form the topography of today Erosion by streams and
ocean waves has continued to work on the exposed land surfaces to form the
topography of the present day

The project site is at approximately Milepost 20 9 along Kamehameha Highway
Route 83 in the District of Koolauloa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The existing
bridge spanning across Kaipapau Stream is a two lane two span concrete bridge
structure measuring about 82 feet long The bridge abutments and wing walls are of
cement rubble masonry CRM and concrete construction

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 near the proposed bridge location extending to
depths of about 112 5 feet below the existing ground surface In addition four borings
designated as Boring Nos 4 through 7 were drilled and sampled along the existing
roadway extending to depths of about 13 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the borings encountered a layer of surface fill consisting of medium
stiff clayey silts and silty clays extending to depths of about 5 to 9 feet below the
pavement grade The surface fill was underlain by recent and older alluvial deposits
The recent alluvium consists of very loose to loose sands and gravel and the older
alluvium consists of medium dense and dense sands gravel and cemented cobbles
and boulders extending to about 68 to 112 feet below the existing ground surface
Below the alluvial deposits our borings encountered saprolites and weathered basalt
formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of approximately 112 5 feet below the
existing ground surface We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths of about
5 9 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface during our field exploration However
groundwater was not encountered in Boring Nos 6 and 7 It should be noted that
groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate depending on tides seasonal rainfall time
of year and other factors Considering the bridge is adjacent to a stream the
groundwater level will vary in response to the water level in the stream

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As part of the bridge replacement project new pavements will be constructed at
the bridge approaches to transition from the existing roadway to the new bridge
structure Typically new DOT pavements include a drainage layer to facilitate drainage
and increase the pavement life However the existing road does not have a drainage
layer or a drainage system to collect and discharge infiltrated water Incorporating a
drainage layer below the two short sections of new pavements at the bridge approaches
would not be feasible nor practical If a drainage layer is included in the new pavement
section we believe the build up of subsurface water within the permeable base layer
may result in saturated soil conditions which would cause the pavement subgrade soils
to soften and result in premature pavement problems Therefore we have considered
only pavement structural sections without a permeable drainage layer in our analyses
Based on the traffic data provided the R value obtained for the subgrade soils
and the economic analyses performed we recommend using a flexible pavement
structural section consisting of 3 inches of Asphaltic Concrete AC on 5 inches of
Asphalt Concrete Base ACB over 10 inches of Aggregate Subbase ASB for the
project In general the following sections herein provide discussion on the areas
necessary for pavement analyses and design

Methodology of Pavement Design
In order to be compatible with the existing asphaltic concrete pavement section
and the relatively short sections of new pavements at the bridge approaches we
considered only flexible pavement structural sections in the pavement analyses The
flexible pavement sections presented herein generally were determined based on the
methodology described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design Manual dated
March 2002 The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii
Department of Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and Research
Branch The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation Caltrans

Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our field exploration we anticipate the majority of the pavement
subgrade soils generally will consist of medium stiff clayey silt and silty clay soils
Laboratory Resistance R Value tests performed on the near surface soils obtained
from our field exploration indicated that the materials exhibited R Values of 26 and 28
The test results are presented on Plates A 1 and A 2 of Appendix A
Based on these assumptions and laboratory test results a design R Value of 26
was adopted in our pavement analyses for the subgrade materials If site grading
exposes soils other than those assumed in the pavement design additional tests should
be performed to confirm and or revise the recommended pavement section for actual
field conditions
3 4
Design Pavement Section
The revised Pavement Design Manual requires a highly permeable drainage
layer below the pavements unless the site has a very low annual rainfall or the site
consists of pavement subgrades that are free draining permeability greater than
100 feet per day Because the project site is not located in a low rainfall environment
and the pavement subgrade soils are not free draining a drainage layer should be
incorporated into the pavement structural section
Based on the information provided we understand an existing drainage system
catch basins inlets pipes swales etc does not exist at or near the project site In
addition a drainage layer is not present below the existing pavement section Because
an existing drainage system is not present at or near the project site and a drainage
layer is not present below the existing pavements daylighting subsurface water from
the drainage layer below the new pavements is not feasible nor practical To daylight
the subsurface water we envision that additional infrastructure work will be required to
incorporate a drainage layer into the new pavements We believe that it is not feasible
nor practical to incorporate a drainage layer into two very short sections of the highway

Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of
general fill materials Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive
select granular material such as crushed coralline or basaltic materials The materials
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site
for the intended use
3 7
Compaction Requirements
In general fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Aggregate
subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to the requirements
stipulated in Subsection 703 17 of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
and Bridge Construction 2005 The aggregate subbase should be moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts
and compacted to no less than 95 percent of the maximum density determined in
accordance with AASHTO T 180
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basaltic
aggregates placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined
in accordance with AASHTO T 209 ASTM D 2041
Asphaltic concrete AC material should be constructed in general accordance
with Section 401 Hot Mix Asphalt Pavement of the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005 and subsequent amendments

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore special attention
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements
As mentioned previously an existing drainage system nor a drainage layer is
present below the existing pavements at the site The afore mentioned Pavement
Design Manual requires the incorporation of a drainage layer into the new pavements
planned at the project site Additional infrastructure work such as new drain lines and
inlets would be necessary in order to incorporate a drainage layer into the two short
sections of new pavements We believe this would not be feasible nor practical and that
it would result in a build up of subsurface water within the drainage layer if proper
drainage outlets are not provided The subsurface water likely would cause saturated
soil conditions which would cause the pavement subgrade soils to soften and result in
premature pavement problems
Therefore it is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately and to
maintain positive drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface water
may be carried off the pavement quickly for proper discharge Surface water ponding
should not be allowed on site during or after construction
