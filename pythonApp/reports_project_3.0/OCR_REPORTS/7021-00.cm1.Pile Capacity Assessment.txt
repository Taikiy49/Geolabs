Project Considerations 
Matson Navigation Company has container operations at Piers 51B, 52, and 53 
in Honolulu Harbor. These piers were constructed in the 1980’s and early 1990’s and 
are supported by precast prestressed concrete piles. Future plans call for larger cranes 
to unload/load the ships. These new cranes will exert more loads on the existing crane 
rails and their supporting piles.  
Liftech Consultants, Inc. was retained by Matson to evaluate the effect of the new 
cranes on the existing crane rail girder system. Liftech’s analyses indicated that the 
crane rail girder is adequate for the new crane loads but the supporting pile foundations 
in the western portion of Pier 52 would be overloaded based on the design pile capacity. 
The piles in this western portion of the pier were designed as friction piles with a lower 
capacity than the end bearing piles in the remainder of the pier. 
It was desired to evaluate the current ultimate capacity of the friction piles based 
on available pile driving information and boring data.  
Brief Summary of Pile Design 
The pile foundations for Piers 52 and 53 crane rails were designed by Fewell 
Geotechnical Engineering (FGE). The recommendations are presented in their

Subsurface Investigation Report dated December 7, 1989. The eastern two thirds of the 
crane rail were designed using end bearing piles into the upper coral ledge at about 
minus 10 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) to provide a design axial capacity of 75 tons each. 
The western portion of the crane rails was designed using friction piles extending to 
about minus 45 feet MSL to provide a design axial capacity of 50 tons each. 
Subsequent to the report, it appears that the pile capacity for the friction piles was 
increased to 75 tons each. 
A pile load test was conducted on a test pile at Sta. 1+30 (Test Pile No. 1) in the 
friction pile zone. The pile failed at about 90 tons of load. Therefore, the design pile 
capacity was reduced to 45 tons (factor of safety = 2).  Additional piles were added to 
support the crane rails in the friction pile zone. These piles were designated with an “A”, 
i.e. 22A. The friction pile zone was established as Sta. 0+05 to 2+99.5, see attached 
plans.  
Methodology of Analysis 
The following information was used in our analyses.  
1. 
Report by FGE entitled, “Subsurface Investigation Report, Pier 52 and 53, 
New Crane Rail and Waterfront Tower, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii”, dated 
December 7, 1989.  The log for Boring No. 1, closest to the pile load test, is 
attached. 
2. 
Pile Load Test results from Ahsing & Associates, dated January 23, 1991. 
The plot of the load versus deflection is attached. 
3. 
Available pile driving records by FGE obtained by Matson Terminals. The 
pile driving record for the test pile is attached.  
The reduced friction pile capacity was based on the results of the pile load test that 
failed at 90 tons. The pile driving record for this test pile is attached. We noticed that most 
of the production pile driving resistances (blow counts) in the friction pile zone were a little 
higher than the driving resistance of the test pile that was load tested and failed. This 
indicated that the actual pile capacities for the installed piles could be a little higher than the 
design capacity.  
We performed a Wave Equation Analysis on the load test pile using the data from 
the load test results (ultimate capacity) and subsurface information from the closest boring 
(Boring No. 1) to “calibrate” the analysis. We then used the “calibrated” wave equation 
analysis to evaluate the pile capacity for the production piles in the friction zone. The 
results of the estimated ultimate pile capacity for Pile Nos. 1 through 38 and 4A to 38A are 
presented in Table 1. Pile driving records were not available for Pile Nos. 39 through 62. 
However, pile summary sheets were available which indicated the pile penetration depth 
and final blows per inch. These data were used in the wave equation analyses to estimate 
the ultimate pile capacity as shown in Table 2 (Plate 11).

Except for a few isolated piles, the analyses indicate that the actual pile capacity
for the friction piles could be 20 to 30 percent higher than the design capacity. Due to the
uncertainties in pile driving and numerical analyses of piles, we recommend that the
increased capacity be limited to no more than 20 percent of the design value.
Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon
information obtained from boring and pile driving data by others. Variations of subsoil
conditions between and beyond the borings may occur, and the pile driving records
may not disclose conditions that may affect the driving condition.
