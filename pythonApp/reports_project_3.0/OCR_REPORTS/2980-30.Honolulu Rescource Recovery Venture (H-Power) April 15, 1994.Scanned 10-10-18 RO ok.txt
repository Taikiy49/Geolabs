INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of we have conducted a current
inspection of the injection wells for the Honolulu Resource Recovery Venture H POWER
facility in Campbell Industrial Park This report summarizes our findings from the site
inspection
Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal dated April 6
1994
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Honolulu Resource Recovery Venture H POWER injection wells are utilized
for disposal of cooling water and demineralizer effluent generated at this waste to energy
generation plant The majority of the injected fluid is from the cooling tower used for
cooling of the condenser units in the generating plant Generally the wells are used on
a continual basis
As acondition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit issued for the
facility UIC Permit No UO 1376 it is required that a semi annual inspection of the wells
be made by a professional engineer or geologist familiar with injection well operation
This inspection is intended to ascertain that the wells remain operational
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our work on the project was to conduct an inspection of the
injection wells to make visual observations of their operational status

GENERAL INFORMATION
The facility has been granted a permit to operate injection wells under the
Underground Injection Control program In this permit the facility is identified by Facility
ID No and the wells are identified as Well Nos 1 and 2 Personnel at the
facility identify Well No 1 as the North Well and Well No 2 as the South Well
The H POWER plant is a waste tc energy generation plant in which municipal solid
wastes are burned for the generation of electrical power The waste is shredded
segregated and fed into the boiler fire box where it is burned The heat in the waterwall
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2980 30 Page 3
boiler produces steam which is used to turn generator turbines After the steam is
expended in the turbines it is cooled in condenser units before being returned to the
boiler
The heat exchangers in the condenser units are cooled by water from the cooling
tower Waste thermal energy in the cooling tower is removed through evaporative losses
The make up water for the cooling tower is from on site brackish water source
wells Evaporative losses in the cooling tower result in an increase in concentration of the
mineral content of the water to about twice the original level The cooling water is
continuously drained to the injection wells to maintain flow through the system
The wells are approximately 150 feet apart and are located in an open yard area
easterly of the cooling tower The ground surface is covered with gravel and there is no
vegetation in the vicinity of the well heads Based on the current information provided to
us by Honolulu Resource Recovery Venture the combined flow over the past 6 months
to the two wells averages about 686 000 gallons per day This flow rate is calculated by
the amount of make up water used with respect to the concentration ratio of the water
as it leaves the cooling tower A copy of the flow records and an injection data summary
from to March 31 1994 are provided in Appendix A
DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
Well Site Conditions
The wells are located on the easterly side of the cooling tower The wells are
situated in an open yard area between the cooling tower and the RDF Storage building
The well heads are above ground tee assemblies with a shutoff valve mounted on
the side of the tee The tees are bolted to the well casings which are set in concrete well
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2980 30 Page 4
pads The tops of the tees are capped with blind flange plates secured by bolts These
plates were removed for the inspection
The well heads appear to have been constructed in accordance with good
workmanship and no overt signs of defects were noted However some minor external
corrosion was observed on the well head assemblies See Appendix B for photographs
of the well head assemblies
There is no vegetation in the vicinity of the wells nor was there any visible evidence
of leaks ponding of water or other unusual surface conditions
Well Inspection
The general conditions of the wells were observed by sounding of the open wells
and by bailing of water from the wells to allow visual observation of the amount of
turbidity The following are the results of those observations
Well 1 North Well
The visible portion of the interior of the casing appeared to be in good condition
with no overt signs of corrosion or other damage The well was last redeveloped
by pumping in September 1991 According to plant employees this well continues
to experience a slight reduction in capacity since the last inspection in
October 1993
At 0920 hours on April 6th the static water level in the well was measured at a
depth of 18 0 feet below the top of the well head The well had been shut down
since about 0800 prior to the inspection Sounding of the well with a weighted line
indicated that the well was open to a depth of about 253 feet below the top of the
well head
W O 2980 30 Page 5
A bailer sample of water taken from a depth of about 40 feet below the top of the
well casing showed that the water was relatively clear with few suspended
particles
A bailer sample of water taken from a depth of about 250 feet below the top of the
well casing showed that the water was relatively clear with some suspended black
ash like particles This sample contained a slightly greater concentration of
suspended solids than the previous shallower sample collected
A bailer sample collected from the bottom of the well indicated a deposit of
approximately three to four inches of soft black material with a sulfur odor
Upon inspection of the valve assembly and gasket seal it appeared to be in sound
condition with no overt signs of distress or corrosion
Well 2 South Well
According to plant employees this well continues to experience a slow decline in
capacity over time Although the decreased capacity has been noted thus far it
has not been significant enough to warrant immediate action
The well was isolated from the injection system at 1010 hours At 1017 hours the
well cover was removed with difficulty due to an apparent vacuum seal in the well
The water level was observed to be falling within the well At 1017 hours on April
6th the water level in the well was measured at a depth of 17 8 feet below the top
of the well head and falling At 1023 hours the water level had stabilized and was
measured at a depth of 19 1 feet below the top of the well head The water level
had not begun to fall until the well cover was removed and the pressure had
equalized within the well casing

injection System
The injection system is regulated by valves which can be used to isolate the wells
so that either or both of the wells may be used for injection Generally both wells are
used for injection simultaneously
Currently there is no system for monitoring the head levels in the wells These
data would be helpful in monitoring the long term performance of the wells and serve as
an early detection system in the event that one of the wells begins to lose capacity
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2980 30 Page 7
Without these data it is not possible to provide any detailed evaluation of the
performance of the wells
Recommendations
As recommended in our previous report dated we suggest that
recording piezometers be installed in the wells in an attempt to continuously monitor head
levels in the individual wells This apparatus would aid in the evaluation of capacity and
recovery of the well system
To maintain the injection capacity of the wells we recommend that an operation
and maintenance program be instituted There is a tendency for injection wells to clog
over time as a result of suspended solids in the effluent in the wells
To partially restore capacity lost by the introduction of suspended solids we
recommend that the wells be redeveloped on a periodic basis It is our opinion that airlift
pumping could be effective for redevelopment of the wells Other methods of
redevelopment such as jetting or scrubbing the interior of the well may be too abrasive
and damage the uncased portion of the wells Another possibility would be to introduce
a bleach into the well via a tremie pipe to kill any algae or other organic materials which
may be clogging the formation pores or well screen
Redevelopment should be scheduled and conducted based on the head level data
obtained from the recommended recording piezometers Generally redevelopment
should be conducted if the head level shows an average increase of about 10 to 15
percent over conditions established after a redevelopment event The actual amount of
increase needed to initiate redevelopment will need to be determined based on the data
derived from monitoring of the head levels in the wells
