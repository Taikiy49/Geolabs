SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered 10 inches of asphaltic 
concrete and fill material consisting of soft to stiff silty clay and clayey silts extending to a 
depth of approximately 14 feet below the existing pavement surface. The fill was generally 
underlain by alluvial deposits consisting of medium stiff to very stiff sandy silt, and basalt 
boulders. The alluvial deposits were underlain by residual, saprolitic materials, basalt rock 
formation, and clinker, extending to the maximum depth explored of about 50.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of 
our field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to 
change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
A new 12-foot wide, 10-foot tall, and 30-foot-long concrete box culvert structure 
with headwalls up to 7 feet in height will be constructed at the project site. A service limit 
state bearing pressure of 3,000 psf may be used for the design of the culvert bearing on 
recompacted on-site materials and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design 
finished grades. The culvert and headwall structures should be designed to resist the 
lateral earth pressure due to adjacent soil and surcharge effects. In general, an at-rest 
condition should be used for box culverts because the walls are not free to deflect or are 
restrained. An active condition may be used for the wing walls that are free to deflect 
(cantilever) by as much as 0.5 percent of the wall height. For a level backfill condition 
above water, an equivalent fluid pressure of 33 pcf may be used for the active condition, 
and 50 pcf may be used for the at-rest condition. 
A temporary bypass road will be constructed on the downstream side of the culvert 
by installing a temporary embankment from the stream level up to the existing roadway with 
heights up to 26 feet. We recommend utilizing geotextile and geogrids to mechanically 
stabilize and reinforce the fill soils for the temporary bypass roadway embankment. A vertical 
spacing of 1 to 2 feet for the primary geosynthetic reinforcement, with lengths varying from 
14 to 16 feet, is recommended for the new embankment. Due to the steepness of the new 
fill slope with gradients of about 1H:4V, intermediate geogrid reinforcements, such as 
Tensar BX1200 Geogrids or equivalent with a minimum length of 4 feet and vertical spacing 
equal to half of the primary geotextile spacing, are recommended to be provided between 
the primary geotextile reinforcements. We recommend utilizing an edge retaining system, 
such as Tensar Sierrascape or equivalent to facilitate construction and prevent bulging of 
the temporary embankment face.

Project Considerations

the Island of Maui, Hawaii. We understand that heavy rains damaged the upstream side 
of the existing culvert. It appears that the existing rock wall structure above and on the 
sides of the culvert eroded away, exposing the underlying soil. We understand that 
shotcrete was applied to the exposed soil for temporary stabilization. Prior to shotcreting, 
rebar and welded wire mesh fabric were placed. We understand that the existing culvert 
will be replaced with a new larger culvert structure. 
To avoid complete closures of Hana Highway during the reconstruction of the 
existing culvert, a temporary bypass road will be constructed on the downstream side of 
the culvert. Based on the information provided, the approximate height of the bypass road 
will be up to 26 feet in height.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes: the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these 
two volcanoes. The project site is located on the northeastern portion of the island. 
The East Maui Volcano is represented by the older rocks of Honomanu Volcanic 
Series, buried beneath younger volcanic deposits. The Honomanu Series is overlain by the 
Kula Volcanic Series, which generally consists of thick lava flows with appreciable 
embedded pyroclastic (ash and cinders) materials. Rocks of the Kula Series cover much of 
the present-day surface of the East Maui Volcano. 
A long period of deep erosion and sediment deposition occurred between the end of 
the Kula Volcanic Series and the beginning of the Hana Volcanic Series. The Hana Series 
generally consists of the youngest lava flows and cinder cones presently encountered at the 
East Maui ground surface. Although the Hana lavas were generally more fluid and the lava 
flows tended to be thinner and less massive than the preceding Kula lavas, thick 
accumulation of lava did occur in the valleys and valley heads. 
The erosional process of volcanic islands is dominated by the detachment of 
weathered soil and rock materials from the interior steep mountain slopes, and the 
movement of the eroded materials downslope by gravity toward the lowland mountain 
perimeter areas. Once these eroded slope materials (colluvium) reach a valley stream,

In general, mountain stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
stream flows may transmit large volumes of water for a very short duration. Because of this, 
the transport of sediment is intermittent, and the bulk of a stream's hydraulic load may 
consist of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fine soil particles. 
When the erosional base level of a land mass changes, through long-term changes in 
sea-level or the sinking/raising of the island mass, sedimentary deposits may either be 
reactivated, renewed incision and erosion, or remain deposited in-place as unconsolidated 
alluvial and colluvial deposits. 
When unconsolidated sedimentary deposits are buried in-place for long periods of 
time, chemical processes begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing additional 
breakdown and weathering of the materials. The continued upland erosion generates new 
eroded materials, which are transported downslope and may cover the older alluvial and 
colluvial soil deposits. Depending on the erosional base level and the rate of sediment 
transport, these newer sediments may be generally transient in terms of geologic time.  
The project site is along Hana Highway traversing steep slopes on the sides of a 
large drainage ravine. Because the culvert site is located in steep terrain containing 
excavated cut slopes, surface alluvial and colluvial soil cover is anticipated to be limited 
at the project sites.

 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island 
of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most of the 
earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are 
related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii.  
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui also has experienced earthquakes 
greater than M5+. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in the 
County of Maui. 
 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the AASHTO 2020 LRFD Bridge Design Specifications, 9th Edition, the 
project site may be subject to seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at 
the project site will need to be evaluated. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore 
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In 
this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such 
as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume, causing

an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to 
dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) 
of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up 
could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to 
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires 
a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not 
confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands 
and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

3.2 
Temporary Bypass Road 
To avoid complete closures of Hana Highway during the reconstruction of the existing 
culvert, a temporary bypass road will be constructed on the downstream side of the culvert. 
The bypass road will be constructed by installing a temporary embankment from the stream 
level up to the existing roadway. In addition, a temporary drainage structure will be installed 
within the embankment to allow for water to flow down Hanehoi Stream without interference. 
Based on the information provided, the approximate height of the bypass road will be up to 
26 feet. 
Due to the surcharge loading from the proposed temporary bypass roadway and 
limited right-of-way, relatively steep fill slopes with gradients of about 1H:4V will be 
required for the construction of the proposed temporary bypass road embankment.  Due 
to the relatively steep new fill slope gradient required, we recommend utilizing geotextile 
and geogrids to mechanically stabilize and reinforce the fill soils for the temporary bypass 
roadway embankment. 
As the roadway embankment is constructed, horizontal layers of geotextile and 
geogrids are placed in the compacted fill at predetermined levels.  The lengths of the 
geotextile and geogrid layers are designed to provide resistance to potential failure zones 
by extending into stable interior sections of the embankment.  The geotextile and geogrids 
transfer tensile forces from the unstable embankment slope face back into the stable 
portions of the embankment, and stability is thus maintained. 
Based on our analysis, we recommend using a woven geotextile fabric, such as 
Mirafi HP570 or equivalent, for the primary reinforcement of the proposed bypass road 
embankment planned for the project. A vertical spacing of 1 to 2 feet for the primary 
geosynthetic reinforcement, with lengths varying from 14 to 16 feet is recommended for 
the new embankment. The primary reinforcement should extend to the outer edge of the

 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by alluvial 
deposits consisting of basalt boulders at relatively shallow depths. Therefore, the 
contractor will likely encounter some difficult excavation conditions during 
construction where large basalt boulders or very hard basalt rock formation is 
encountered. 
We anticipate that the surface fill, alluvial deposits, residual soils, and saprolitic 
material encountered during our field exploration along the project alignment may be 
excavated readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators. 
However, cobbles and boulders are frequently encountered in these types of soil 
deposits and should be expected. Excavations that encounter cobbles and boulders 
within the on-site soils and excavations extending into the underlying basalt rock 
formation may require the use of hoerams or chipping.

based on our field and laboratory data from the borings drilled. Contractors should 
be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make 
their own reasonable and prudent interpretation.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to a depth of about 50.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample, designated as Bulk-1 was obtained 
near the ground surface to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight auger and coring tools. The boring and bulk sample locations are 
presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual length 
of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For 
example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality

recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by performing Dynamic 
Cone Penetration (DCP) tests extending to depths of about 0.9 to 11.6 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  The approximate locations where DCP tests were performed are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The DCP tests were then performed in general accordance with ASTM D6951, 
Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow Pavement 
Applications. The Dynamic Cone Penetrometer device consists of two 16mm-diameter 
stainless steel rods with the lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60° pointed tip 
and depth markings. The upper rod holds a 17.6-pound drop hammer with a 22.6-inch 
drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer 
until the widest part of the cone is just below the testing surface, at which point the initial 
penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more 
times depending on the strength of the soil at the test location. Following each sequence 
of hammer drops, a penetration reading is taken. The process is continued until the 
desired depth of testing is reached, or the full length of the lower rod is buried. Results 
are shown on the Logs of Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests, Plates B-1.1 through B-5.3.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate C-1. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are summarized 
on Plate C-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate C-3. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were 
performed on selected relatively undisturbed soil samples in accordance with 
ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” samples. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates C-4 and C-5. 
Three Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive 
strength of the basalt rock formation encountered. The test results are presented on 
Plate C-6. 
One laboratory Resistance (R) Value test (ASTM D2844) was performed by Ninyo 
& Moore on a bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate C-7.
