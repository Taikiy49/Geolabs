EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is at the existing Kalaeloa Boulevard in the Kapolei area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The project limits extend from Malakole Road to just north of
Franklin D Roosevelt Avenue for a distance of approximately 6 000 lineal feet The
existing Kalaeloa Boulevard is a four lane divided roadway serving as the only
thoroughfare into the Campbell Industrial Park At the time of our field exploration we
observed the majority of the existing pavements within the project limits to be in fair to
good condition with relatively smooth ride with the exception of the lane adjacent to the
median Honolulu bound which exhibits alligator cracking and depressions in several
areas
The proposed widening area generally consists of existing shoulder areas and
vacant land with a sparse cover of vegetation The existing ground surface of
Kalaeloa Boulevard within the project limits generally slopes down gradually from the
north Franklin D Roosevelt Avenue towards the south Malakole Road with
elevations ranging from about 63 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at about north
end of project to about 14 feet MSL at about south end of project
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling thirteen
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 13 extending to depths of about 5 to
11 5 feet below the existing pavement surface In addition three bulk samples of the
tests to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the pavement subgrade soils
The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration disclosed that the majority of the roadway improvement
alignment is underlain by dense to very dense coralline sands and gravel extending to the
maximum depth explored of approximately 11 5 feet below the existing pavement surface
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall seasonal
precipitation surface water runoff and other factors

Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our field exploration the proposed roadway improvement alignment is
generally underlain by dense to very dense coralline sands and gravel in the
shallow subsurface Laboratory testing performed on the near surface coralline
materials indicated a R value of 35 54 and 72 Based on these test results a
design R value of 50 was adopted in our pavement analyses for the coralline
materials subgrade condition

structural section may be used for preliminary design and cost estimating
purposes

4 0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete
10 0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base 91 Percent Relative Specific Gravity

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and proof rolled with a minimum
20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to detect the presence of
potential near surface cavities or loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be
operated at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute If the proof rolling operations
detect near surface cavities or loose zones the area should be ripped open cleaned of
debris and then backfilled with compacted fill material or lean concrete
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basalt
aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to greater than 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with ASTM D 2041 The aggregate subbase should consist of
crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction
CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered
during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section The recommended
pavement section assumes that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches below the
asphalt concrete base layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape
water into the pavement section
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades we
anticipate minimal site grading work may be required to achieve the design finish
grades for the project In general the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris
deleterious material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose
firm and or stiff material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with

Hawaii California

well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed
off site
After clearing and grubbing the existing ground should be over excavated
where necessary to the bottom of the aggregate subbase layer The over excavated
subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of 10 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory
drum roller a minimum of eight passes to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface
voids and loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of
about 200 to 300 feet per minute The proof rolling operations should be performed in
the presence of a qualified geotechnical engineer or a Geolabs representative
Yielding areas which may be indicative of subsurface cavities and or voids
loose areas or cavities disclosed during the proof rolling operations should be
over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials The depth of
over excavation should extend until dense underlying materials are exposed and should
be evaluated in the field by a qualified geotechnical engineer or a Geolabs
representative
The aggregate subbase materials should meet the requirements of Section 31 of
the City and County Standard Specifications September 1986 The materials should
be moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level
loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Imported fill materials below the subbase course layer should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension In addition the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883 The materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Geolabs should test imported fill materials
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification
process and to confirm whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration

and catch basins In general good construction practices should be utilized for the

installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities The contractor should
determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation subject to practical
limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the
applicable federal state and local safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears conventional excavation
methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation However some of the utility
trench excavations may encounter localized areas of hard to very hard coral formation
which will likely require the use of chipping with hoe rams
We recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement
and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils with a maximum
particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction

designated as Boring Nos 1 through 13 extending to depths of about 5 to 11 5 feet
below the existing pavement surface We drilled the borings using a truck mounted drill

representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 13

a 3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition we obtained some samples from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
