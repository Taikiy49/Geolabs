Keystone Reinforced Soil Retaining Wall
Based on the current design concept we understand that a Keystone Reinforced Soil
Retaining Wall KRSRW is being considered along the southeast side of the new turnaround
area planned at the subject site The system combines the Keystone modular concrete wall
units with geogrid reinforcement and compacted soil to result in a reinforced soil mass that
supports the earth pressure and surcharge loads This composite material essentially forms
a gravity retaining wall structure In addition the KRSRW is also desirable due to the
flexibility of the wall ease of construction high load carrying capacity and economy
Design of the KRSRW will need to take into consideration both external and internal
stability of the structure In evaluating external stability the KRSRW structure must satisfy
four stability conditions
e Bearing Failure
e Translational Sliding
Overturning Stability
e Overall Slope Stability
Geotechnical design parameters to evaluate these stability conditions are presented
in the following sections Some of the geotechnical parameters necessary in evaluating the
internal stability of the KRSRW are presented in the Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials
subsection of this letter The external and internal stability of the KRSRW will need to be
evaluated by the design engineer We have evaluated the overall slope stability for the
KRSRW and the results are summarized in the Overall Slope Stability subsection

Foundations
In general the foundation requirements and recommendations presented in the
Retaining Structures section of our letter dated May 4 2001 may be used in the
design of the Keystone Reinforced Soil Retaining Wall system Similar to the
conventional retaining walls we recommend that the KRSRW be supported on at
least 18 inches of compacted structural fill materials A coefficient of friction of 0 55
may be used between the reinforced fill and the 18 inches of compacted structural fill
layer to evaluate the resistance to sliding
Lateral Earth Pressures
The retained soil behind the KRSRW structure will exert lateral earth pressures on the
KRSRW structure The active earth pressure components presented in the
Retaining Structures section of the afore mentioned letter may be used to design the
internal and external stability of the KRSRW The values provided do not include
surcharge effects from vehicular loading Lateral earth pressures due to uniform
surcharge loads imposed on a level backfill should be taken as 33 percent of the
vertical surcharge pressure acting uniformly over the entire height of the KRSRW
structure The lateral earth pressure is based on a friction angle of 30 degrees and
a retained fill unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot pcf
The lateral earth pressure values provided above do not include hydrostatic pressure
that may be caused by groundwater trapped behind the KRSRW structure A
subdrain is recommended at the base of the KRSRW structure to collect and
discharge excess water that may infiltrate the wall structure A typical subdrain should
consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable drainage material such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation with a perforated pipe with perforations
down at the base of the KRSRW discharging to a proper discharge facility
Overall Slope Stability
We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the KRSRW structure with the 18 inch
compacted structural fill material Slope stability analyses of two dimensional slope
cross sections using Bishop s simplified method of limit equilibrium analysis were
performed on the KRSRW structure Based on our analyses the factor of safety for
short term and long term stability of the KRSRW is at least 1 5 which is the minimum
factor of safety normally recommended
Based on our slope stability and external stability analyses a minimum base width to
wall height ratio of at least 0 75 and 1 0 is recommended for the KRSRW with a level
backfill and two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V sloping backfill condition
respectively We also recommend that the base width be at least 6 feet to develop
sufficient resistance for overall stability

Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials
We recommend that the reinforced fill material for the KRSRW consist of imported
non expansive select granular fill material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder
sand similar to the structural fill material recommended below the foundations The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a California Bearing Ratio
CBR value of 25 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 it should also contain less than
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve In general we believe that
non expansive select granular fill materials conforming to these requirements will
likely have a unit weight of at least 130 pcf
In addition the reinforced fill material should have an angle of internal friction of at
least 35 degrees when tested by the standard direct shear test ASTM D 3080 The
sample to be tested should be compacted to 90 percent relative compaction at
moisture contents above the optimum The backfill materials should also have a
Plasticity Index of less than 6 when tested in accordance with ASTM D 4318
Reinforced fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in loose lift thickness not exceeding 8 inches and compacted to at
least 90 percent of the maximum dry density established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91
Passive Earth Pressures for Sloping Ground Conditions
Based on the existing site terrain and grading concept we envision that some of the
foundations will be located on sloping grounds As stated in our letter dated May 4 2001
the passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a passive earth pressure of
180 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf be used for foundations located on sloping
ground with gradients up to 2H 1V
Existing Sewer Tunnel
Based on the information provided we understand that an existing sewer tunnel
measuring approximately 4 feet by 5 feet in cross section traverses a portion of the
maintenance facility site at depths of about 30 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on the substantial depth and the relatively small size of the sewer tunnel when
compared to the depth we believe that the proposed construction and the existing sewer
tunnel would have minimal impact on one another
