In accordance with our fee proposal dated August 25 we have conducted a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the proposed resurfacing reconstruction project for
the existing pavements at the Aircraft Rescue and Fire Fighting Station No 2 premises inside
the Honolulu International Airport This report summarizes our work on the project and
presents our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the subject project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Aircraft Rescue and Fire Fighting ARFF
Station No 2 off Lagoon Drive within the Honolulu International Airport on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
Based on the information provided it is proposed to resurface the existing parking lot
to accommodate the heavy vehicular loads from the fire fighting equipment Alternatively
the existing pavements may be demolished and replaced with a new pavement section to
accommodate the vehicular loads from the fire fighting equipment We understand that both
flexible and rigid pavements will need to be evaluated for the new pavement alternative
Based on the information provided and our site reconnaissance the existing asphalt
concrete pavement was observed to be in fair condition with no significant structural defects
Overall pavement deterioration is restricted to light alligator cracking of low severity

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program for the existing pavement resurfacing reconstruction
project consisted of drilling and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1
through 3 extending to a depth of about 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration disclosed that the existing pavement is generally
underlain by 2 to 4 inches of asphalt concrete overlying dense coral gravelly sands extending
to the maximum depth explored of approximately 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Detailed descriptions of the subsurface materials encountered from our field exploration are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3 of this report

precipitation groundwater withdrawal and or injection and storm surge conditions

existing pavements at the ARFF Station No 2 will be a 2 axle fire truck with a total gross

subjected to 5 passes of the fire truck on a daily basis over a design period of 20 years
AC Overlay Design
Our field surveys indicated that the majority of the existing pavements appear to be
in fair condition with minor distresses of low severity We believe that the existing pavements
may be strengthened with an AC overlay to accommodate the fire fighting equipment with
the assumption that the strength of the existing AC is equivalent to that of asphalt concrete
base ACB Based on the existing pavement surface conditions we recommend that cold
planing of the existing AC be performed prior to the overlay work The depth of cold planing
required would be on the order of about 1 0 inch Based on our engineering analyses the
AC overlay required for the existing pavements is on the order of about 1 5 inches The
recommended overlay thickness has already taken into account the reduced thickness of the
existing AC resulting from the cold planing operation

consist of the recompacted near surface granular soils described in the boring logs If site

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 AASHTO T 180 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The Portland Cement Concrete should have a minimum flexural strength of
600 pounds per square inch psi when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
C 78 The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should
conform to Sections 703 06 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and
Public Works Construction 1994 Aggregate base course should be moisture conditioned

thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction We
recommend that CBR and field density tests be performed on the actual pavement
subgrade materials encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
recommended pavement sections

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the aggregate base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should
not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where landscaping is planned
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed
to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation and to reduce the potential for
migration of landscape water into the pavement section The recommended pavement
sections assume that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas
Pavement Joints
Due to the rigidity of the Portland cement concrete pavements significant stresses
may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture content In order
to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for cracking adequate joints
should be provided in the Portland cement concrete pavement alternative The following
describes the various types of joints and general guidelines that should be implemented
in the design of concrete pavements
Crack Control Joints Contraction Joints
In general decreases in temperature or moisture content such as that during
concrete curing will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack Crack
control joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and
relieve the stresses caused by curling or warping Crack control joints such as saw
cut joints or formed grooves are provided to a depth of generally one fourth the
slab thickness These saw cut joints or formed grooves crack control joints
provide a weakened plane which will likely crack through the full depth as the
concrete shrinks during the curing process
In general the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of
20 feet Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and or life span
of the pavement The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal
and transverse directions The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent
Expansion Joints Isolation Joints
Expansion joints are typically used to relieve compressive stresses developed at
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature Expansion joints are cut to the
full depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in the cut
These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the

slabs
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of
pavements with fixed structures Because expansion joints generally do not provide
for load transfer across the joint placement of dowels with lubrication on one end
of the dowel should be considered for load transfer across the expansion joint
where required
Construction Joints
Construction joints are generally required when two abutting slabs are placed at
different times Therefore construction joints usually form the edges of each day s
work For the relatively thick concrete slab planned for this project the construction
joints should consist of a keyed or butted joint In general construction joints
should be aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to reduce the number
of pavement joints required
