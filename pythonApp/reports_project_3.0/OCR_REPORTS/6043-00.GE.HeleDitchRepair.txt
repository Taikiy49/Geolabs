Project Considerations
The project consists of repairing the existing concrete lined drainage ditch in the
vicinity of Lekeona and Hele Streets in the Kailua area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
We understand that a 52 foot long section of the concrete lining along the west side of
the ditch was damaged during previous storms We understand the City and County of
Honolulu is considering two options to repair the drainage ditch Option 1 would be to
limit the repair to the damaged portion of the wall by constructing a new concrete
retaining wall along the exposed side slopes of the drainage ditch Option 2 would be to
reconstruct the channel section both sidewalls within the damaged zone This would
require less excavation during construction
Based on our discussions with the property owner we understand the slope
above the damaged area showed signs of instability erosion The slope above the
drainage ditch is within the private property designated as 35 1 The signs of
instability include ground loss at the top of the slope and erosion along the slope face
Based on the information provided by the owner of the private lot the potential instability

Site Description Reconnaissance
The project site is in the vicinity of Lekeona Street and Hele Street in the Kailua
area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The existing drainage ditch is between 35 1
and 9 and 10
The existing drainage ditch is a concrete lined channel that aligns in a southeast to
northwest direction At the time of our site reconnaissance the west sidewall was
damaged The damaged portion of the west sidewall begins from about 71 feet from
Lekeona Street in the northwest direction The damaged sidewall extends downstream for
about 52 feet
The dimensions of the drainage ditch at the ends of the damaged portion range
from about 3 to 3 5 feet and about 8 to 8 3 feet wide at the bottom and top of the ditch
respectively and about 3 5 to 3 8 feet high The thickness of the sidewall ranges between
5 to 7 inches The downstream end appeared to be patched with grouted rubble paving A
chain link fence with fence posts was observed along the damaged area The slope above
the damaged portion of the ditch which has an average inclination of about 1 5 to
2 3 horizontal to 1 vertical was moderately vegetated with wild grass and some shrubs It
appears that erosion was occurring on the slope above the damaged portion of the
drainage ditch Large cracks as wide as 3 inches and the loss of ground were observed
at the top of the slope within the lot designated as 35 1
Based on our site reconnaissance we observed some silty clay soils along the face
of the slope and the bottom of the drainage ditch We anticpate the subsurface conditions
at the site consist of fills underlain by alluvial deposits The silty clay soils contained
varying amounts of basaltic gravel and sand The consistency of the silty clay soils was
generally stiff and moist However there were localized soft areas along the bottom
portion of the drainage ditch due to water ponding
Based on our research the anticipated surface soils underlying the project site are
described by the U S Soil Conservation Service in their publication Soil Survey of Islands
of Molokai and Lanai State of Hawaii 1972 The major soils at the
site and some of their general properties are as follows

Retaining Walls
Option 1 would consist of leaving the undamaged concrete lining in place and
constructing a 3 foot high concrete retaining wall along the damaged portion of the
drainage ditch This option would require a deeper excavation to construct the retaining
wall footing and key A deeper excavation would require temporary shoring of the
excavation to reduce potential slope instability In addition there is a higher potential of
encountering seepage water which would require dewatering of the excavation The
following general guidelines may be used for design of retaining walls
Retaining Wall Foundation
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site we believe that
the retaining wall may be supported on a shallow foundation system An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
design of foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ clayey soils This bearing
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded
a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade The
bottom of footing excavation should be recompacted to provide a firm and
unyielding base At the time of our reconnaissance ponded water was observed
along the damaged portion of the drainage ditch The ponded water created soft
soil conditions in the area Soft and or loose soils encountered at the bottom of
footing elevation should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm and or
dense materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction or lean concrete
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or the footing should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the retaining wall may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
in situ clayey soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
pcf The passive earth pressure value assumes that the soils around footings are

Drainage
The retaining wall should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed directly around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential
for significant water infiltration into the subsurface In addition the upper 12 inches
of the retaining wall backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining wall unless
covered by concrete slabs at the surface
Concrete Lining
Option 2 would consist of demolishing the remaining concrete lining along the
damaged portion of the drainage ditch and constructing a new concrete lining This
option would require less excavation since the lateral soil pressures could be resisted
by the opposite lining wall thereby eliminating the wall key In addition the potential for
encountering perched groundwater would be lower due to the shallower excavation
depths required to construct the lining Based on the above discussions we would
recommend using Option 2 to repair the drainage ditch
The recommendations presented in the Retaining Wall Foundation and Lateral
Earth Pressures subsections of this report should be used to design the concrete lining
The concrete lining subgrade should be proof rolled to provide a firm an unyielding base

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography we envision that majority of the site grading work
will consist of backfilling behind the new retaining wall We envision some minor site
grading work along the slope face above the new drainage structure
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared thoroughly Debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below
areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to
expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted fill or lean concrete The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
disposed properly off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over excavation and fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
The materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of general
fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension If required import fill should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed basalt or coral The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Geolabs should test imported fill material for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Compaction should be accomplished by mechanical rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment Compaction by water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be allowed for the on site soils

Excavation
Based on the borings drilled the site is generally underlain by stiff clayey soils In
general we believe that conventional excavation equipment may be used to excavate the
clayey soils
The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for
excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated soils
should not be stockpiled on the slope
If Option 1 is considered we anticipate that construction of the retaining wall will
require near vertical cuts into the existing slope To reduce the potential for significant
ground movements along the slope and at the top of the slope temporary shoring will be
necessary during construction of the retaining wall Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered a shoring system consisting of vertical pile and lagging may be one of the
systems used for shoring support Sheet piles installed by impact or vibratory hammers
should not be used for shoring the near vertical cuts Sheet piles installed by the above
methods could result in excessive vibrations which may cause significant ground
movements along the face and top of the slope
The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the
shoring installation His her representative who should be required to be continuously
present on site during excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsurface
soil conditions unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence
or techniques etc which may adversely affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship such
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed the subsoil
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation It is important to realize
that the excavation shoring should be installed properly and as early as practical
