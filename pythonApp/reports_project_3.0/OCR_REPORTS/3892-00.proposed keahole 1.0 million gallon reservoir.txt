INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Million Gallon MG Reservoir in the District of
North Kona on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design of
foundations and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located mauka of the Keahole Airport in the District of
North Kona on the island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a 1 0 million gallon
reservoir adjacent to the existing 0 5 million gallon reservoir as part of the North Kona
Water Master Plan The general project area is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
Based on the information provided the new reservoir will be a circular tank with a
diameter of about 92 feet We understand that the proposed reservoir will be a reinforced
concrete structure with a height of approximately 20 feet The finished floor elevation for
the reservoir will be at approximately 260 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
Based on the preliminary topographic map provided the existing ground surface
elevations at the project site vary from about 260 to 265 feet MSL In general we
anticipate that construction for the proposed reservoir structure will involve minor site
grading consisting of cuts on the order of about 5 feet or less and minor fills In addition
an asphalt concrete paved access road will be provided around the tank structure

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes
up the west central part of the island The lava formation encountered at the site appears
to consist of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities
and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore
they serve as the major water transmission features

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site consist
generally of slightly weathered medium hard to hard pahoehoe type basalt formation
extending from the surface to the maximum depth explored of approximately 30 feet
below the existing ground surface Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions
with respect to the proposed reservoir construction the principal geotechnical engineering
considerations for the proposed reservoir project include the following
e foundation support for the proposed reservoir tank
e probing and grouting for potential cavities beneath the foundations and
e site preparation and grading
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration we
believe that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the proposed reservoir
structure To provide more uniform support for the foundation we recommend that the
materials below the bottom of the tank floor and foundations be over excavated and be
replaced with at least 12 inches of compacted base course
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic
lava flows we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the foundation
locations be implemented during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii
should observe the probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities if
encountered Special attention should be given to the Foundation Probing section of
this report
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the on site basalt rock would provide suitable foundation support for the proposed
reservoir structure To provide more uniform support for the shallow foundation system
we recommend that the materials below the bottom of the tank floor and foundations be
over excavated and be replaced with at least 12 inches of base course materials The
12 inches of compacted base course should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond
the limits of the tank structure The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
For design of foundations bearing on the 12 inch base course over basalt rock an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 6 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces For design of the tank
floor a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of
deflection p c i may be used for the compacted base course
Foundations and thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should
be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade If the
foundations are located adjacent to a slope the foundations should be embedded deep
enough to provide a minimum 8 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the
outside edge of the foundations to the slope face Where foundations are to be located
adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the foundations should extend to a depth
below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the
below grade structure

presented herein the total settlement of foundations is estimated to be on the order of

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of
foundations A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the
compacted base course Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f for foundations embedded in the compacted base course These values
assume that the concrete for foundations is cast neat against well compacted base
course Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
Foundation Probing
Based on our site reconnaissance and previous experience in the area voids
and or cavities appear to be present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project
site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the
cavities below a foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and
grouting be conducted during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and 10 foot on centers for the perimeter foundation The probe holes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3892 00 Page 9
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected
during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacing to help
delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to
9 inches
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Following clearing and grubbing areas designated to receive fill or finished
subgrades in cuts should be proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy equipment such as a 20 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes
to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities Soft loose
weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during site preparation operations
should be over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with structural fill material well graded 6 inch minus materials
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The material resulting
from the over excavation should be removed and properly disposed of off site
The proof rolling operations should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii
Fill and Backfill Materials
As previously indicated we recommend that the tank floor and foundations for
the proposed reservoir be supported by a minimum of 12 inches of base course
for more uniform support Fills required outside the tank structure or below the
base course layer may consist of structural fill materials Structural fill materials
should consist of well graded granular material of 6 inches in largest dimension
with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass
The excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of structural fill
materials provided that they are processed to meet the above gradation
requirements

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The base course layer should also be moisture conditioned
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
Where fills are composed of rock boulders and or cobbles conventional
compaction testing is not practicable Therefore a testing program to evaluate the
number of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of
this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion
for adequate compaction

Service Road Pavements
Considering the light automobile traffic anticipated for the service roadway
around the perimeter of the tank the following pavement section may be used based
on the results of the borings and visual observations
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The subgrade soil under the pavement area should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches where possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction CBR tests and or field
observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soil samples during
construction to confirm that the above design sections are adequate The base course
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The recommended section considers only light traffic conditions
and assumes that good drainage will be provided adjacent to paved areas
Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines will be required for development of this project It
is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in medium hard to
hard basalt rock For utility lines located outside the reservoir limits a granular bedding
consisting of Ginches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended below the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3892 00 Page 13
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finish grade should consist of well graded materials less than
6 inches in maximum size or select granular material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about 12 inch loose lifts
and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches
are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
For utility lines located below the footprint of the reservoir tank the utility trenches
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the base course
layer The base course layer recommended below the tank floor and foundations should
be placed above the concrete to provide more uniform support for the tank foundations
The concrete around the utility lines below the reservoir tank structure should be placed
neat against the excavation
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at the surface the trench work for
utility installation will likely involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and will
require hard ripping or hoerams Care must be exercised by the contractor to avoid
over ripping especially within the reservoir tank footprint which would disrupt the
structure of the rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for
foundations in its vicinity

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 30 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
