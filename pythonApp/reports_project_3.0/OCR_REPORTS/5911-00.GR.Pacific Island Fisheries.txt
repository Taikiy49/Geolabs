PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The National Marine Fisheries Service NMFS Pacific Islands Fisheries Science
Center PIFSC is at 2570 Dole Street in the Manoa Upper Campus of the University of
Hawaii in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The PIFSC includes a main building
Annex 1 and Annex 2 As part of the Facility Condition Survey for the PIFSC some
undermining of the Annex 1 foundation adjacent to Manoa Stream was noted
Therefore further geotechnical reconnaissance was requested to review the conditions
and to provide recommendations for stabilization of the undermined foundation The
overall project location is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Our field reconnaissance generally focused only on the undermining area of
Annex 1 adjacent to Manoa Stream Generally we observed that the thickened edge slab
footing for the new addition added in the mid 1960s was exposed and undermined
Currently the bottom of the thickened edge is up to about 12 inches higher than the
adjacent ground elevation We measured as much as 14 inches of exposed soils below
the thickened edge slab footing Generally the condition is most severe toward the north
side of the addition toward the mechanical equipment pad and grades to less severe
toward the south The bottom of the thickened edge slab footing is very close to or
embedded below the existing ground level where it connects to the south patio A cross
section sketch depicting the existing slab condition is provided on Plate 3

Based on our field reconnaissance and the available information provided we
believe that the undermining of the slab footing at Annex 1 may be attributed to one of the
following reasons or a combination of the following reasons
Erosion
The new addition added to Annex 1 in the mid 1960s is on the east side of the
building next to Manoa Stream It appears that this addition area is the low spot for both
Annex 1 and Annex 2 Generally the existing grade of Annex 2 is about 2 5 feet higher
than Annex 1 Both buildings do not have a functioning roof gutter system and a proper
surface drainage system is lacking Therefore we can see that some of the surface water
runoff and roof water from both Annex buildings flow to this small area before discharging
toward the stream Some signs of erosion were observed near the top of the slope where
the new addition connects to the concrete pad for the mechanical equipment Based on
our observations we believe erosion is the primary contributor to the cause of the
undermined footing
Slope Stability
Based on our field observations the current embankment slopes down at about
1H 1V with some vegetation covering the stream bank No apparent signs of distress
such as cracks along the slope and or cracks in the building wall were observed during
our site reconnaissance Therefore the existing embankment or stream bank appears to
be relatively stable based on the existing conditions and environmental factors
However it should be noted that the surface soils on the relatively steep slope may
creep toward the stream Furthermore the stream bank may be susceptible to erosion and
scour from extreme events within Manoa Stream Manoa Stream is subject to flash flood
conditions and the high water level in the stream is unknown We wish to point out that
we did not consider this extreme event in our qualitative evaluation of the slope stability
discussed above
PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
We understand the thickened edge slab footing for Annex 1 was exposed and
undermined In order to reduce the potential for further undermining and or erosion and to
maintain the integrity of the existing structure we recommend protecting the partially
undermined thickened edge slab footing from further erosion and exposure to the
elements The edge of the footing is also relatively close the top of the bank within about
3 to 4 feet lateral distance Based on the limited space to work we recommend utilizing
grouted rubble paving GRP to stabilize the slope to minimize erosion of the footing
subgrade soils

Based on the current site conditions we recommend installing the GRP on the east
side of Annex 1 where the current thickened edge slab footing is exposed and is higher
than the adjacent grade The GRP should be sloped at 1H 1V or flatter toward the stream
The thickened edge slab footing should be covered by GRP in order to reduce the
potential for further erosion and undermining of the footing An 8 inch deep by 12 inch
wide key should be provided at the toe of the GRP to anchor the new GRP into the stiff soil
subgrade to reduce the potential for sliding Please note that the outside edge of the key
should be set back at least 12 inches away from the top of the slope A sketch of the GRP
and key design is shown on Plate 4
In order to further stabilize the embankment stream bank from erosion due to an
extreme event condition caused by flash floods consideration may be given to installing
GRP to cover the entire embankment stream bank below the undermined area Prior to
installation of the GRP on the stream bank the slope surface should be cleared and
grubbed thoroughly The GRP should be a minimum of 12 inches thick and should extend
full length to the toe of the slope A key should be provided at the toe of the stream bank
and the key should be at least 6 feet wide and 2 feet deep The GRP should also be
properly drained to reduce the potential for build up of excessive hydrostatic pressures
Appropriate outlet or weepholes should be provided at about 8 foot spacing horizontally
and vertically
At this time we are not aware of the property boundaries for the PIFSC In the
event that providing GRP over the entire embankment stream bank is desired the
issue of property ownership should be clarified
We noted some signs of surface water runoff from both Annex 1 and Annex 2
flowing through the Annex 1 undermined area before discharging into the stream
Therefore the grades outside the buildings and other structures should be sloped to shed
water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding
We also noted that a proper roof gutter system is missing for both buildings In
general we recommend installing gutter systems around the buildings and diverting the
discharge away from the foundation and slab areas Blockage of the gutters and swales
could cause ponding of water resulting in foundation distress Because ponded water
could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface
soils we believe these drainage requirements should be properly maintained for the
proper performance of existing structure foundations
