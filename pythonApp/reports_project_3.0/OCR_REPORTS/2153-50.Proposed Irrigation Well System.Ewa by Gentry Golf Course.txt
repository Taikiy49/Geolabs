INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Irrigation Well System project within the Ewa by Gentry
Golf Course in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of manhole wet well and pump
station foundation and trench excavation and backfill only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2153 50 Page 6
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We understand that the final design grades at the location of the proposed
irrigation well system will be lower relative to the existing ground surface We envision
that excavations on the order of about 5 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface
would be required to achieve the final design grades Since excavation to final grades
will be required for the proposed construction we recommend that the grading to near
the finish grades be conducted first prior to trenching excavation for the skimming well
and wet well installation to reduce the amount of earthwork necessary Grading the site
to near the finish grades would reduce the depth of the trench excavation and shoring
needed for installation of the skimming well and wet well The depths of the proposed
excavations would be about 25 to 42 feet after grading to final grades in comparison
to about 40 to 50 feet relative to existing grades In addition due to the relatively
deep trench excavations anticipated shoring will be necessary to support the trench
excavation for the proposed construction
Groundwater levels were generally encountered during our field exploration at
depths of about 33 and 38 feet below the ground surface corresponding to 1 to 2 feet

clayey silts and medium dense to dense coral sands and gravels over weakly

below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration Groundwater levels were

It is anticipated that conventional excavation equipment may be used for the
proposed excavation based on the subsurface conditions encountered It should be
noted that hard horizons within the coral detritus formation may be encountered in
localized areas which may require more arduous efforts for the proposed excavation
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated
materials should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth
of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground
movement
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings and the relatively
deep excavations anticipated we believe that shoring of the sides of the trenches with
bracing will be required to support the excavation and to protect personnel working in
the trench All excavations shoring support and construction works should conform
to Federal State and other safety requirements
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements Because of the depths of the proposed excavations a qualified
geotechnical and or structural engineer should be retained by the contractor to design
and evaluate the shoring system used The adequacy and safety of the shoring

Dewatering
Based on the proposed invert elevations of the pipes the bottom of the trenches
will extend about 6 to 12 or more feet below the groundwater level Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered near the pipe inverts the coral detritus formation
ranges from medium dense to dense and should be considered to be highly permeable
Because of the highly permeable coral detritus formation encountered we believe that
dewatering the trench excavation for installation of the irrigation well system would not
be economically feasible Therefore we believe that installation of the irrigation well
system in a wet environmental should be allowed However care must be exercised
by the contractor for the proper installation of the pipes If the contractor elects to
dewater the trench excavation it should be noted that the dewatering operations may
require a permit under the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For pipes bearing on the medium dense to dense coral
materials a minimum of 6 inches of free draining granular bedding material should be
provided below the pipe The bedding material should consist of clean granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation A typical
section of the trench is presented on Plate 4
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 As _ previously
mentioned the inverts of the pipes will be about 6 to 12 feet below the groundwater
level To facilitate the backfill operations in a wet condition the first zone of the backfill
up to 12 inches above the groundwater level should consist of free draining granular
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2153 50 Page 12
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation In general the
free draining granular material should be less than 1 inch in maximum size and should
have less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the groundwater level to the finished
subgrade may consist of the excavated on site granular soils provided that they contain
no lumps greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The backfill should be placed in
thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 If the
trenches are located in structural areas the trench backfill within 3 feet below the
finished subgrade elevation should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of
the pipes the unit weight of backfill material above the top of the pipe and the frictional
resistance between the backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical
loads on the buried pipes an average unit weight of 125 pounds per cubic foot p c f
above the groundwater table and 63 p c f below the groundwater table may be used
for the backfill material A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used between the
backfill and trench sidewall Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with
increased width of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a
minimum If an open cut method is used to install the pipes the additional earth
pressures due to the larger extent of backfill should be taken into consideration in the
design

Settlements
Primary settlement of the pipe lines is normally caused by the difference in the
unit weight of the excavated original earth and the compacted backfill material placed
over the pipes The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying
subsoils below the trench invert Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in
our borings and our analyses primary settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is
anticipated for the pipeline The above estimate assumes that proper construction
procedures and good workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional
settlement could occur if improper trench support procedures are used
Manhole and Wet Well Structures
In general we believe that the bottom of the manhole and wet well structures will
bear on the medium dense to dense coral detritus formation as encountered in our
borings To provide more uniform support of the structures we recommend that the
manhole and wet well structures be supported on a 6 inch layer of cushion fill
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation For the foundation
design of the manhole structure of about 25 feet below the proposed finish grade an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 p s f may be used An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 6 000 p s f may be used for foundation design of the wet well of
about 42 feet below the finish grade It should be noted that the allowable bearing
pressure values provided above are based on the subsurface conditions encountered
in our borings and the embedment depths assumed If the actual conditions are
different than those we assumed Geolabs Hawaii should be consulted to provide
revised allowable bearing pressures for the actual conditions Settlements on the order
of less than 1 inch are anticipated for these structures
We envision that an open cut method may be used to install the manhole
structure For the installation of the wet well structure we understand that open cut or

around the manhole and wet well structures should be properly backfilled in accordance

the wet well and the drilled excavation be backfilled with fine granular material such

Pump Station Foundation
Based on the grading plan excavations on the order of 8 feet will be needed to
achieve the final grade for the proposed pump station Removal of approximately
8 feet of materials will likely expose medium dense coral gravels at this location
Based on this we recommend that spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted coral materials be used to support the pump station The footings may
be designed using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f This allowable
bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for
transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces Footings should be
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade We
estimate that the total footing settlements under the anticipated design loads to be on
the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements of approximately inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the spread footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings and the
supporting materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f for
footings embedded in the coral materials These values assume that the concrete for
footings is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade
should be neglected

Since the pump station will be located above the wet well structure which is
bearing on a deeper stratum we recommend that the wet well structure be separated

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the interior floors of
the pump station In general the subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified
to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density We recommend that the subgrade be kept
moist prior to placement of cushion fill and slab concrete
For more uniform support of slabs we recommend that the concrete slab on
grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch cushion fill The cushion fill layer should
consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation which will also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion fill
should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification and recompaction of the
subgrade soils as recommended above For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2153 50 Page 17
surfacing we recommend that an impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion
fill to reduce potential migration of moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order
to promote more uniform curing of the slab concrete and to provide protection of the
vapor membrane we recommend that a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand be placed
above the membrane prior to placing the slab concrete The sand should be moistened
slightly prior to placing concrete The thickened edge of slabs adjacent to any unpaved
area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade

APPENDIX _A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings to depths of about 44 and 49 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drilling rig equipped with hollow stem augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was formulated to aid in evaluating pertinent
engineering properties of the soil and materials sampled
Moisture content ASTM Test Method D 2216 is used as an aid to soil classifica
tion and to aid in evaluating certain properties such as relative saturation The
moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample is
shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two sieve analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
