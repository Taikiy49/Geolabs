PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves construction of a new Tunnel Influent Facility located within 
the existing Kaneohe Wastewater Pre-Treatment Facility in Kaneohe on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. Structures including a new Odor Control Facility, Generator/Electrical 
Building, Transformer Pad, and Access Ramp/Fuel Storage Tank will be constructed as 
part of the new Tunnel Influent Facility project. 
Based on the project plans and specifications, we understand the new structures 
will be supported on 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete piles, designed 
for an allowable compressive load capacity of 60 kips and an allowable uplift capacity of 
4 kips, with estimated production and test pile tip elevations of -66 and -75.5 feet MSL 
(Mean Sea Level), respectively. Based on the information provided, the existing ground 
surface at the project site during pile driving will be at about Elevation +3.5 feet MSL.  
The subsurface conditions depicted in relevant borings presented in the project 
plans are reflected in the wave equation analyses to account for the soil and/or rock 
conditions encountered in the borings.

Hammer and Driving System 
We understand the piling subcontractor proposes using a Delmag D16-32 single 
acting diesel hammer for the pile driving operations. Based on the pile driving 
equipment data forms provided, the Delmag D16-32 hammer has a ram weight of 
3,530 pounds and a maximum stroke of 11.2 feet for a maximum rated energy of 
39,400 foot-pounds at the maximum pump setting.  
A pile cushion consisting of approximately 6 inches of plywood cushion block is 
proposed for the pile driving operations. The plywood cushion blocks generally have a 
cross sectional area of approximately 342 square inches for the 16.5-inch octagonal 
concrete piles. Schematic details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and 
Driving Equipment Data Form in the Appendix, Page 1. 
Pile Details 
As mentioned previously, the specified piles consist of 16.5-inch octagonal 
precast, prestressed concrete piles with a cast length of 85 feet for the test piles. 
Based on the precast shop drawings, the effective prestress level of the 
16.5-inch octagonal piles is approximately 1,045 psi (pounds per square inch). The piles 
will be cast from concrete with a minimum 28-day unconfined compressive strength of 
6,000 psi. Therefore, the maximum allowable compression and tensile stresses induced 
during pile driving in the 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete pile are 
estimated to be 4,000 and 1,270 psi, respectively. 
It should be noted that the maximum allowable compressive stress is defined as 
85% of the 28-day concrete unconfined compressive strength minus the effective 
prestress value, while the maximum allowable tensile stress is defined to be three times 
of the square root of the 28-day concrete unconfined compressive strength plus the 
effective prestress value.

Driving Stresses 
A graph showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses

on ksi (kips per square inch), which is equivalent to 1,000 psi. 
Based on our analyses, the compression stresses developed during the pile 
driving did not exceed the allowable compression stresses of 4,000 psi for the full range 
of driving resistance for the Delmag D16-32 diesel hammer. 
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving estimated for the 16.5-inch 
octagonal concrete piles are less than the allowable tensile stress of 1,045 psi for the 
full range of driving resistance for the Delmag D16-32 diesel hammer except during 
driving using Pump Setting 4 when the driving resistance is less than 5 blows per foot. 
Based on the above results and the anticipated subsurface conditions, it is our 
professional opinion that the proposed pile driving equipment (Delmag D16-32 diesel 
hammer) is capable of installing the 16.5-inch octagonal concrete piles to the specified 
tip elevations, achieving the ultimate compressive load capacity of 150 kips without 
over-stressing the concrete piles, provided that the pump setting of the Delmag D16-32 
diesel hammer is reduced to a pump setting of 3 or less during low resistance pile 
driving (pile tip in softer soils), such as 5 blows per foot or less. 
Care should be exercised during pile driving, such that when the pile driving 
resistance is low or within the predrilled depth, to reduce the potential for pile damage 
due to the development of high tensile stresses in the pile. The pump setting of the 
diesel hammer should be carefully controlled to reduce the potential for pile damage 
and/or over-stressing during pile driving.

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer.  The program is based on
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving
system, pile and soil under the hammer action.  Under certain conditions,
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents
normal situations.  In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order.  All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed.  However, input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.     
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or
dynamic testing and measurements.  Estimates of bending or other local
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user.
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile.  Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation.  This    
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers.  The GRLWEAP user   
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow    
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking.
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values.  They MUST be reduced by    
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.
