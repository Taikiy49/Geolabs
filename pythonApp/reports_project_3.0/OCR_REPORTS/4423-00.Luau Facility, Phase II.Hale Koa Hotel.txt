INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Luau Facility Phase II project within the Hale Koa Hotel in the Waikiki area
of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of
foundations slabs on grade retaining walls and site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the Hale Koa Hotel in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided we understand that it is
planned to construct a new 100 foot by 100 foot covered structure for the proposed Luau
Facility Phase II project The new covered structure will be supported on 12 columns with
concrete floor slabs We also understand that some low retaining walls will be required for
grade separation between the lower and upper levels of the project as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2

grading were not available at the time this report was prepared
Based on field observations the existing site is covered with lawn areas with a few

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its
eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during
the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface
conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits
and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous
sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes due
to widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level
occurred causing numerous valleys in the project area to be submerged n the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments
GEOLABS INC
W O 4423 00 Page 5
resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly
valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy wetlands As
the city of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were placed to
reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits consisting
of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was deposited during periodic
influxes of non marine alluvial debris which apparently smothered the coral reef development
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki
area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting
heavy structural loads

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously indicated our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer
of approximately 5 feet thick consisting of medium dense sandy soils with coralline gravel The
surface fill layer was generally underlain by coralline sand and gravel deposits consisting of
beach sands overlying lagoonal deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of about
20 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater was encountered at depths of about
4 5 to 7 feet below the ground surface at the time of our exploration
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for
support of the proposed new Luau Facility covered structure planned at the site In general
the medium dense surface fill iayer encountered at the site should provide adequate support
for a shallow foundation system The bottom of the footing excavations should be
re compacted to provide a dense and unyielding subgrade for support of the footings Due
to the potentially erratic nature of the fills localized areas of soft and or loose soils may be

encountered in the bottom of footing excavations Where unsuitable materials are
encountered they should be over excavated and replaced with well compacted fills
Due to the granular nature of the on site soils slabs on grade for the project may be

Foundations
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the new Luau Facility covered structure planned at the site An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used in the design of foundations
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
In general we recommend that footings be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent finished grade Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements they should be embedded below an imaginary 45 degree plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
The bottom of the footing excavations should be re compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations the soft
and or loose materials should be removed to expose the underlying firm or dense materials
The over excavation may be backfilled with the excavated on site materials compacted to a
GEOLABS INC
W O 4423 00 Page 8
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
lf the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of footings to be less than 1 inch
Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on the re compacted on site soils
should be on the order of about 0 5 inches
We envision that the covered structure may be subjected to uplift forces created by
wind conditions Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the following 1 dead
weight of the structure and slab 2 weight of backfill material above the footing where
applicable and 3 side shear between the sides of the footings and surrounding soils
Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of
105 pounds per cubic foot pcf For side shear between the sides of the footings and the
adjacent soils a shear value of 100 psf may be used It should be noted that the groundwater
level is encountered at about 4 5 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore the
bottom of footings should be limited to a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground
surface to reduce the potential for encountering groundwater during the footing excavation
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used for footings bearing on the re compacted on site granular soils Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive pressure resistance in
the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be provided for the proposed new luau
facility covered structure In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion
layer be placed below the slab to provide for uniform support The 4 inch thick cushion layer
should consist of free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the
cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the plastic membrane
to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of
the 2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project
structural engineer
The subgrade soils below the concrete floor slabs should be scarified a minimum depth
of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Fill materials needed to raise the
existing grades to the bottom of the cushion fill layer below the floor slab should consist of
select granular fills Fill materials below the slab should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to not less
than 90 percent relative compaction
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils on site soils and graded to
divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations

Retaining Walls
We envision that low retaining walls might be required to provide for grade separation
between the lower and upper levels of the project In general we believe that the low retaining
walls may be supported by shallow foundations bearing on the re compacted on site granular
soils The design parameters and foundation requirements presented in the Foundations
section of this report may be used to design the wall foundations Wall foundations should
have a minimum width of 24 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions the footings should be
extended deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the
outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Footings oriented parallel to the direction
of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings
Retaining walls should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the
adjacent soils and surcharge effects The recommended lateral earth pressures for design
of retaining walls expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of pounds per square foot per foot
of depth pcf are presented in the following table

to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the permeable drainage material
The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the retaining wall Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches
of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material compacted on site soils to reduce
the potential for water infiltration behind the retaining wall
Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated at the site At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4423 00 Page 12
other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and disposed of
properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or
dense material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with the excavated on site granular materials The
on site granular materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
After clearing and grubbing the subgrade soils below slabs and other improvements
should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In general
the excavated on site granular soils may be re used as a source of fill and backfill materials
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction
In addition the project site should be adequately sloped to allow for proper drainage
of the area during construction Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage during construction may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner

Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material

Utility Trenches
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for the proposed project
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill
up to about 12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches above the groundwater level to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support
for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 20 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using portable drilling equipment using

examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
