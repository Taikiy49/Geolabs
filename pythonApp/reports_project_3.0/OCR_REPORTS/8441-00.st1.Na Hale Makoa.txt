SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered surface fills underlain by clinker materials and 
basalt formation.  Surface fills generally consist of medium dense to dense sandy gravel 
with some silt and cobbles extending down to about 1 to 4 feet below the ground surface.  
Surface fills appear to consist of volcanic ash materials mixed with clinker and rock 
fragments.  Surface fills are generally underlain by clinker materials or basalt formation.  
Clinker materials generally consist of medium dense sandy gravel with some cobbles.  In 
some areas, various amounts of silty volcanic ash are mixed with clinker materials. 
Depths to basalt formation vary significantly across the project site.  Our borings 
encountered basalt formation from about 1 to 17 feet below the ground surface.  It should 
be noted that we did not encounter basalt formation in one of our borings (Boring No. B-6) 
that extended down to about 22 feet below the ground surface. Basalt formation, where 
encountered at the site, is generally hard to very hard and relatively unweathered to 
slightly weathered. It should be noted that cavities and/or voids are commonly present in 
the basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids 
should be expected during construction. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new buildings planned. As 
an alternative, foundations for support of the structural loads of the new structures 
planned also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
In general, the new structures should be constructed on similar foundation 
subgrade materials in order to reduce the potential for differential settlement and to 
provide for uniform support across the structures. The cut subgrades within the new 
building pads should be over-excavated to provide at least 2 feet of compacted structural 
fill. The over-excavation should extend at least 2 feet laterally beyond the limits of the 
new building footprint. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Na Hale Makoa development planned in the District of South Kohala 
on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our 
engineering analyses for the design of the proposed development. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of mass 
grading, foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, underground utility lines, and 
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is in the Waikoloa area on the Island of Hawaii. A new affordable 
housing development is planned at the southwest corner of Paniolo Avenue and 
Kamakoa Drive.  The project site encompasses an area of over 10 acres.  We understand 
that the project site was previously cleared and graded by others. 
A total of 15 residential buildings are planned for the new development.  The new 
residential buildings will generally consist of two-story 8- and 12-Plex structures.  In 
addition, a single-story recreation center is also planned.  Access roadways, parking 
areas, stormwater disposal systems, underground utility lines, and associated 
improvements also will be required for the development.   
Based on the preliminary plans provided, site grading consisting of cuts and fills of 
up to about 8 feet high will be required to achieve the design finished grades.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which 
makes up the north-central portion of the island. Based on available geologic information 
and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic 
lava flows of the Laupahoehoe Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were 
deposited during the Holocene and Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in the Kohala and Mauna Kea 
volcanoes, activity from the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over 
much of the island. This ash has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified 
by low in-situ densities and high field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field 
moisture content is sufficiently high, the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the 
soil temporarily loses strength when remolded and regains its strength after internal pore 
pressures dissipate). In more arid areas, the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even 
though field moisture contents can approach 100 percent of the dry unit weight. Prior to 
previous site grading, the site was underlain by a surface mantle of this drier volcanic ash 
soil, which should transition to basalt formation and clinker at a deeper depth. 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe 
basalt type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava 
typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a 
jagged accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a 
denser and more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. 
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe 
lavas. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is in the Waikoloa area of the District of South Kohala on the Island 
of Hawaii. The project site encompasses an area of over 10 acres. The project site is 
bounded by Paniolo Avenue to the east, Kamakoa Drive to the north, and Iwikuamoo 
Drive to the west. 
The project site is generally an open, vacant land.  We understand that the project 
site was previously mass graded by others.  Based on the information provided, previous 
mass grading consisted of excavations of up to about 30 feet in height and embankments 
of up to about 12 feet deep.  In general, deep excavations were generally located on the 
southern end of the project site.  Fills were placed mostly at the northern portion of the 
site. 
The ground surface is generally sloping down from south to north, with relatively 
flat areas in the center portion of the project site. Based on the topographic survey map 
provided, the existing ground surface elevations range from about +770 to +800 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) within the area of new development.

Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement 
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii

only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong 
enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma

to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes 
with increased distance from the active volcanoes located in the southern portion of the 
Island of Hawaii.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills. The contractor should exercise caution while clearing and 
grading near collapsed lava tube features and/or cavities, which may represent 
potential lava tubes or cavities that may further collapse under the load of heavy 
construction equipment. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to be filled should be ripped using a tooth of a 
large bulldozer to a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the 
commencement of grading. The purpose of ripping the subgrades is to assist in 
detecting and collapsing cavities and/or voids contained in the near-surface 
materials encountered at the project site.

After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of about 3 feet below the surface, 
the ground should be leveled with the use of a Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer or similar 
size bulldozer working the surface a minimum of four passes to provide a relatively 
level platform. After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer, we 
recommend proof-rolling the fill subgrades with a large vibratory drum roller 
(minimum 20 tons static weight) for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and 
collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids. The vibratory drum roller should be 
operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute (3½ miles per hour). 
Finished subgrades in cut areas should be proof-rolled with a minimum 20-ton 
(static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. The vibratory 
drum roller also should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. The 
requirement for ripping the ground surface by a depth of about 3 feet (in fill areas) 
may be omitted for cut subgrades. In addition, proof-rolling of the subgrade with a 
Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer for a minimum of four passes may be omitted in the cut 
subgrades, provided that the subgrades are relatively level to operate the vibratory 
drum roller. 
In general, the new structures should be constructed on similar foundation subgrade 
materials in order to reduce the potential for differential settlement and to provide for 
uniform support across the structures. In order to provide similar foundation subgrade 
materials for the future structures, the cut subgrades within the new building pads 
should be over-excavated by at least 2 feet during mass grading. The 
over-excavation should extend at least 2 feet laterally beyond the limits of the new 
building footprint. The over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted structural 
fill. 
3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a minimum 
2-foot thick layer of structural fill materials. As mentioned above, the cut subgrades 
within the new building pads should be over-excavated to provide at least 2 feet of 
compacted structural fill. Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded 
granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with

sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In 
addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. 
In general, the excavated on-site materials, including volcanic ash materials, may 
be reused as a source of structural fill materials, provided they are processed to

Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2-foot thick structural fill layer may 
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches, with 
an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 12 and 
18 inches in particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total 
volume of general fill materials. The excavated on-site materials, if less than 
18 inches in maximum dimension, may be used as a source of general fill or backfill 
materials (except for trench backfill), provided that they are processed to meet the 
above gradation requirements for general fill. 
The maximum particle size of the fills and backfills should be limited to a maximum 
of 3 inches for fills and backfills to be placed in confined locations. Confined locations 
are defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less than 
2 tons in static weight. In addition, if the contractor elects to use smaller-sized 
compaction equipment (less than 2 tons in static weight), the maximum particle size 
of the fill and backfill materials should be limited to 3 inches in dimension. 
Select granular fill should be non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coral 
or basalt. The select granular fill should be well-graded from coarse to fine with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should have 
a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The material should 
also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.

3.1.3 Boulder Disposal 
Boulders (i.e., rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than

1. 
Boulders less than 3 feet in the largest dimension may be utilized as 
rock fills, provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least

maximum dimension should not be used as rock fill unless reduced 
in size. 
2. 
Boulders greater than 18 inches in the largest dimension should not

3.

3.1.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction. General fills and backfills should be placed 
in level lifts of about 18 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction should be 
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable 
compaction equipment. 
"Rock" fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the 
majority of the rocks or 3 feet, whichever is less. Placement should be by 
haul-trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the 
currently placed lift. Spreading of the "rock" fill should be by a large bulldozer to 
facilitate "seating" of the rocks. The rock fill should be watered heavily during 
placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and 
spraying water continuously during placement. Compaction equipment (such as a 
Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer or similar heavy compaction equipment) providing 
suitable energy to achieve the required compaction should be utilized. The number 
of passes (usually six to eight passes) to be made to achieve proper compaction 
should be determined at the on-set of site grading by the performance of a trial 
compaction test or by plate bearing tests. Subsequent layers of rock fill above the 
previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of smaller-sized boulder 
materials to fill in the voids between the boulders of the preceding rock fill layer. 
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks, 
boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the number of 
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be 
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a

3.1.6 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing fills, volcanic 
ash, and clinker materials may be designed with a slope inclination of two 
horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. In areas where dense basalt formation 
is exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes may be steepened to inclinations 
of 1H:1V or flatter.  Where cut slopes with inclinations steeper than 2H:1V are 
planned, provisions for slope protection measures, such as placement of grouted 
rubble paving (GRP), should be considered in the project design in the event dense 
basalt formation is not exposed at the cut slope face.  A Geolabs representative 
should observe the conditions of the finished cut slope face and evaluate the need 
for slope protection measures. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill materials recommended herein 
may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. In addition, fills placed 
on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be benched. The fill slope face should be 
finished to a relatively smooth and well-compacted surface. The fill slope face 
should be free of large boulders (greater than 18 inches in largest dimension) and 
should be free of voids, which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur. 
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back 
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In addition, slope 
planting or other means of slope protection should be provided as soon as possible 
to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes. 
For cut and fill slopes steeper than 3H:1V with vertical heights greater than 30 feet, 
we recommend providing a minimum 8-foot wide bench at the mid-point of the 
slope or at every 30-foot height interval to reduce the potential for significant 
erosion due to surface water runoff. The 8-foot wide benches may be omitted for 
cut slopes in dense basalt formation. The 8-foot wide benches may also be omitted 
for cut and fill slopes less than 30 feet in height. Where slope benches are provided 
(especially for fill slopes), concrete-lined swales should be provided on the

Foundations 
The new residential buildings will generally consist of two-story structures.  In 
addition, a single-story recreation center is planned for the project. Based on the 
subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using shallow spread 
and/or continuous strip footings to support the new buildings planned. As an alternative, 
foundations for support of the structural loads of the new structures planned also may 
consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
In general, the new structures should be constructed on similar foundation 
subgrade materials in order to reduce the potential for differential settlement and to 
provide for uniform support across the structures. As mentioned above, the cut subgrades 
within the new building pads should be over-excavated to provide at least 2 feet of 
compacted structural fill. The over-excavation should extend at least 2 feet laterally 
beyond the limits of the new building footprint. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on at least 2 feet of compacted structural fill. This 
bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (⅓) when considering transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches 
below the lowest adjacent exterior grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or 
on slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured 
from the outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is not less than 
6 feet.

3.3 
Probing and Grouting 
As mentioned above, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the 
collapse of cavities below the foundations, consideration should be given to implementing 
a program of cavity probing and grouting of the building foundations during construction. 
Based on the relatively light structural loads anticipated for the residential 
buildings, we believe the risk of a potential collapse of cavities below the foundations 
would be low.  Therefore, we believe probing and grouting may generally be omitted for 
the new residential structures planned at the site. However, if a higher degree of 
assurance against the potential collapse of cavities below the foundations is desired, a 
probing and grouting program below the foundations may be implemented. 
Where the probing and grouting program is implemented, we recommend drilling 
probe holes at 10-foot on centers for the continuous strip footings and/or thickened-edge 
wall foundations. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each isolated spread footing 
(or column) location. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should 
extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation. 
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical 
and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities discovered 
should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a slump of about 
6 to 9 inches. The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures. As an alternate 
to CLSM, sand-cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches also may be used for 
the grouting operations. 
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the site, we 
recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding. In 
addition, the probe drill should be available on-site until the probing and grouting 
operations are completed.  The contractor also should be made aware that a longer lag 
time between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might be required 
in the construction schedule.

3.4 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the 
new buildings. For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. 
The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to 
serve as a capillary moisture break. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above 
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum 
of 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase materials (3-inch minus granular 
materials) should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. 
The select borrow subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The impervious moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs. 
In addition, exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork will be required for the 
project. Generally, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer of 
open-graded 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or select borrow subbase 
below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic. Select borrow 
subbase, where utilized, should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, 
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways (or 
slabs) with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

3.8 
Permeability Testing 
Two constant head permeability tests were conducted in selected drilled borings 
to provide data for the design of the stormwater disposal system planned at the project 
site. The permeability tests were performed at a depth of about 6.5 feet below the ground 
surface. The permeability test results are presented in Appendix C. 
Based on our field permeability tests, the subsurface materials generally have 
coefficient of permeability (k) of 1.2 x 10-2 to 2.1 x 10-2 feet per minute at the locations 
tested. Based on the field permeability test results, we believe an average hydraulic 
conductivity of about 0.02 feet per minute may be assumed for the subsurface materials 
encountered at a depth of about 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The hydraulic 
conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 415 gallons per day 
per square foot of surface area (0.00033 cubic feet per second per square foot) at those 
locations. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is 
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids/fractures in the lava or 
the presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal capacity of 
the seepage pits and infiltration trenches. In general, the greater the number of clinker 
seams and/or voids/fractures encountered at the seepage pits and infiltration trenches, 
the higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 12 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12, extending to depths of about 20 to 22 feet below 
the existing ground surface. In addition, two borings, designated as Boring Nos. I-1 and I-2, 
were drilled extending down to about 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface to perform 
field permeability tests.  The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-14. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (September 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) determinations were performed on selected 
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties. The test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected samples 
of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-1. 
Twelve Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012) were performed on 
selected rock core samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the basalt rock 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-2. 
One set of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was 
performed on a selected soil sample obtained from our field exploration. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-3. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557 C) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-5.

A P P E N D I X   C 
Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted two permeability tests in 
selected borings using the constant head test method. In general, the borings had a 
diameter of about 3.5 inches and extended to a depth of about 6.5 feet below the ground 
level at the time of the tests. 
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The 
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to 
base calculation of the subsoil permeability. Results of the permeability tests conducted in 
selected borings are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
