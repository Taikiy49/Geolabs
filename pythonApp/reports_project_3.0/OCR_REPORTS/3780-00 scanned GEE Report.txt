INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Ewa Shopping Center project located at the intersection of
Geiger and Fort Weaver Roads within the Ewa by Gentry development in the Ewa District
on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project is located on the mauka side of the intersection of Geiger
and Fort Weaver Roads in Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed site
encompasses an area of approximately 6 67 acres We understand that it is proposed to
develop the site for a shopping center Based on the information provided the proposed
development will consist of a Foodland Supermarket a Longs Drugs store and three
retail stores In addition driveways a parking lot and utilities are also planned as part of
the proposed project
Based on the preliminary site plan provided the proposed Longs Drugs store and
Foodland Supermarket will have footprint areas of about 25 000 and 35 000 square feet
respectively The three retail stores will have areas ranging from 3 000 to 7 000 square
feet We anticipate that the proposed buildings will be one story structures constructed of
concrete masonry with on grade slabs

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes
came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3780 00 Page 5
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumu
lated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials
The project area is situated on the Ewa Plain located to the southeast of the
Waianae mountains and was previously mass graded The project site is underlain by
thin surficial silty and clayey soils overlying coral reef deposits intercalated with marine
sediment and coral detritus at relatively shallow depth

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to hard silty clay over medium
dense to dense sand and coral gravel at the subject site Based on the relatively
competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the stiff to
hard in situ soils or compacted new engineered fills may be used for support of the
proposed buildings at the site
Laboratory test results indicate that the near surface soils exhibit slight to
moderate swelling and shrinking potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore we recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on at least
6 inches of non expansive select granular fill material to reduce the effects of shrink swell

Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed buildings at
the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f
may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the stiff to hard in situ soils or new
engineered fills needed to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum
of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grades
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the stiff in situ soils or new engineered fills as recommended herein to
be less than one inch total with differential settlements on the order of inch

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing material Resistance
due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations

For footings bearing on the in situ soils or new engineered fills the bottom of the

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures The near surface soils encountered at the site exhibit slight to moderate
expansion potential when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content Therefore to
reduce the potential of structural distress to lightly loaded slab on grade construction
resulting from swelling of the on site slightly to moderately expansive soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and a minimum of 6 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material be provided below concrete slabs on grade
The non expansive select fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the limits
of the structures
The subgrade to receive the non expansive select granular fill should be scarified
to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The 6 inch non expansive select fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve All select granular fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by
the cushion fill or base course
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3780 00 Page 10
For more uniform support of interior slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slab above the 6 inch thick
select granular fill The cushion fill layer may also serve as a capillary moisture break
To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top
of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand should be placed above the
plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to concrete placement Flexible
floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask minor slab cracking
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as at the truck
loading areas we recommend that a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction be provided below the slabs in lieu of the
4 inch thick cushion layer The 2 inch moist fine sand and vapor barrier may be omitted
for these slabs The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For design of structural slabs a
modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for
the compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across
the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs
adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be planned for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 6 inch thick layer of select granular material or select borrow
subbase be provided below the concrete walkway slab Contraction joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right
angle intersections

Retaining Walls
We anticipate that low retaining walls may be needed for grade separation at the
site Foundations for retaining walls should be designed in general accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this report The footing
embedment for retaining walls should be a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade We recommend that wall footings have a minimum width of
18 inches
Where wall footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or
utilities the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane
projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations

The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between
the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate having
been estimated by taping from the features shown on the Site Development Plan
provided by Sueda Associates Inc undated The physical locations of the borings
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
nine borings to depths of about 10 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
