GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EVALUATION 
REPLACE SEWAGE LIFT STATION B1670 
KANEOHE, OAHU, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Dimaya: 
We have provided geotechnical engineering services in support of the replace 
sewage lift station project in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
This letter report summarizes the work performed on the project and presents 
testing results and recommendations derived from the percolation testing on borehole. 
Location of the borehole is showed on Approximate Boring Location Plan, Plate 1. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves replacement of an existing sewage wet well adjacent to 
Building 1698 at Marine Corps Base Hawaii (MCBH) Kaneohe. Based on the information 
provided, we understand that the new replace sewage lift station consists of a precast 
concrete wet well with an inside diameter (I.D.) of about 5.5 feet and a depth of about 
9.5 feet, a submersible pump, and electrical piping system. 
A layer of compacted gravel about 12 inches thick is required to be placed under 
the precast concrete wet well providing uniform support. To provide a working platform, we 
envision that a minimum of 7 feet of the dewatering depth would be required to be 
maintained during the construction, assuming high tide elevation of about +103 feet. We 
understand that the dewatering effluent is planned to be pumped into water trucks and/or 
into a nearby excavated trench, that is routed to an approved lay-down area. 
We also understand that the subsurface conditions were not provided in the 
contract documents. Therefore, a geotechnical exploration including soil boring is required 
for design shoring and preparing the dewatering plan. Based on our experience in the 
vicinity, we envision that the project site is underlain by a thin surface fill and loose sandy 
soil over coralline deposit. Special attention was given to characterize the permeability of 
the sub-stratum within the dewatering depths.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its 
main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which erosion cut valleys deep into 
the Koolau Shield Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by 
cinder and tuff cone formation. These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. 
The MCBH site at Kaneohe Bay is located on the Mokapu Peninsula, which was 
built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. These vents are believed to 
be among the oldest of the series. The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from the vents 
formed salients permitting growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of the 
Island of Oahu. 
An extensive deposit of man-made fills composed of marine deposits overlying 
consolidated calcareous marine sediments cover the southwestern portion of 
Mokapu Peninsula along Kaneohe Bay adjacent to Nuupia Pond. Man-made fills 
consisting of marine sediments dredged from the ocean floor were used to build up the 
area around the southern and western shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one Boring No. 1 to a depth of approximately 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The approximate boring location is shown on the Plate 1. 
In general, the project site is underlain a man-made fill about 5 feet thick over 
loose sandy coralline detrituts extending to a depth of about 26.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. We encountered groundwater level in the drilled boring at a depth of 
about 4.9 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration, corresponding 
to an elevation approximately +100.8 feet. It should be noted that groundwater level at the 
site is subjected to change due to tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, storm surge 
conditions, and other factors. 
Details of descriptions of material encountered in the boring are presented on Log 
of Boring, Plate 2, for reference.

FIELD PERMEABILITY TESTING 
Two permeability tests (falling head and constant head testing methods) were 
conducted in the drilled boring to evaluate site permeability characteristics for dewatering 
and disposal. 
The field permeability tests were performed at depths of about 5 and 15 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Based on our field permeability tests, the subsurface 
materials generally have coefficient of permeability (k) ranging from about 0.0018 to 
0.5 feet per minute, which correspond to about 0.0009 to 0.25 centimeters per second at 
the location tested. Details of the percolation tests are presented on Plates 3.1 and 3.2. 
It is our opinion that the above permeability values should be used with caution 
due to the normally heterogeneous nature of the coralline subsurface soils. The actual 
subsurface soil permeability may vary locally in terms of orders of magnitude. It should be 
noted that the permeability of the subsurface soils at the site appears to be highly 
permeable based on field observations and previous experience at the site. Therefore, 
special attention should be given to the site-specific dewatering plan for the project. 
DEWATERING 
We understand that the new precast concrete wet well may require excavation up 
to 11 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on the groundwater level anticipated 
and the permeable nature of the on-site soils within the excavations, dewatering will be 
required at the planned excavation. 
Because the sandy granular soils encountered are relatively permeable, 
dewatering of the excavation may involve large volumes of water. Dewatering by means 
of a well point system along the outside of the excavations is generally not 
recommended. The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater table could 
induce consolidation of the compressible subsurface soils resulting in potential ground 
settlements, which could affect the existing structures and roadways. The contractor must 
carefully evaluate the potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing 
the natural groundwater table prior to dewatering. 
Traditionally, a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking sheet piles, should be 
considered to aid in dewatering the excavation. Sumps will be needed to collect water 
that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates through the sheet piles. 
The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for the areal 
ground subsidence and the dewatering volume within the excavations in the areas 
underlain by granular subsurface soils with generally high permeability. 
The size of the infiltration basin must be carefully quantified with the amount of the 
dewatering. Based on our preliminary evaluation, we anticipate dewatering rates ranging 
from 100 to 200 gallons per minute (gpm) in order to achieve about 7 to 8 feet of 
dewatering, with varying sheet pile lengths from 25 to 35 feet below the ground surface.

As mentioned previously, the dewatering effluent is planned to be pumped into water 
trucks and/or into nearby excavated trench. Therefore, some type of groundwater control 
requirement should be specified in order to reduce the amount of the dewatering. 
Alternatively, the shoring plan may consist of combination of installing shorter 
interlocking sheet piles and placing a concrete mud-slab near the bottom of the 
excavation. To minimize the water infiltration and resist the upward buoyancy force, the 
concrete mud-slab should be at least 5 feet thick and placed by tremis within the sheet 
piles, as shown on the Schematic Dewatering Section, Plate 4. The 12-inch gravel layer 
required by the contract may be omitted, if the concrete mud-slab can provide level 
surface supporting the precast concrete wet well. 
The selection of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the 
contractor, and he/she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may 
be required during construction depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering 
method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the 
proposed excavation. The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the shoring 
support such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized. The operations should 
be carried-out without softening the bottom of the excavations. 
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular subsoils may 
be susceptible to piping and "quick" conditions. The dewatering operations should be 
carried-out without creating a "quick" condition or softening at the excavation bottoms. 
Therefore, the project dewatering operations should be performed without pumping out 
soil fines (pumping clear water only). Excessive pumping, which removes soil fines, may 
result in "blowing" or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides. 
It is important to install adequate sheet pipes and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly braced sheet pipe is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements, such as the roads or utilities. 
The contractor should be soley responsible for the adequacy and safety of the 
shoring installation. His/her representative, who should be required to be continuously 
present on site during excavation and construction work, will have the best opportunity to 
promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsurface 
soil conditions, unexpectedly high groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence 
or techniques, etc., which may adversely affect shoring stability. 
In addition, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical, if necessary, and that the adjacent ground should be 
continuously monitored for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with 
instruments.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from our review of the available information and our field boring 
conducted for the replace sewage lift station project. Variations of subsurface conditions 
from those assumed in our evaluation may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway.  If variations then 
appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented herein. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Mega-Healy Tibbitts A JV 
for specific application to the Replace Sewage Lift Station B1670 project in accordance 
with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices.  No warranty 
is expressed or implied.  This letter report has been prepared solely for the purpose of 
assisting the contractors and engineers in the shoring and dewatering design for the 
proposed project only. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered.  Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft 
deposits, hard layers, or loose fills, may occur in localized areas and may require 
additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to 
attain a properly constructed project.  Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is 
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs. 
This geotechnical evaluation conducted at the project site was not intended to 
investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials existing at the site.  The 
equipment, techniques, and personnel used to conduct a geo-environmental exploration 
differ substantially from those applied in geotechnical engineering.
