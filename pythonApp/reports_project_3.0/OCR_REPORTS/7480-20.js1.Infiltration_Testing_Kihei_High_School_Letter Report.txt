Project Considerations 
The project is located east of the existing Piilani Highway, between Kulanihakoi and 
Waipuilani Gulches in Kihei on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project site encompasses 
approximately 70 acres of undeveloped land and is located east of the Piilani Highway and 
Kulanihakoi Street intersection. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Previously, our office conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration in 
support of the infrastructure work planned at the proposed campus and performed two 
Low Impact Development (LID) infiltration tests at the planned western detention basin 
locations. Results of the previously performed LID tests are contained in the

Exploration, Kihei High School, Phase 1 Infrastructure (On-Site & Off-Site), Kihei, Maui, 
Hawaii," dated December 8, 2018, and are included in this report for reference.

Field Exploration 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers. Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate 3.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate 3.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered in the drilled borings are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates 4 
through 14. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate 3.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

The excavation characteristic of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness 
of the rock, its relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics. A dense rock 
formation with a high RQD value would be very difficult to excavate and probably would 
require more arduous methods of excavation. 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling 11 borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. I-1 through I-11, extending to depths ranging between about 2 and 17 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a surface residual soil layer about 1.4 to 2.5 feet 
thick consisting of medium stiff to very stiff sandy silt, underlain by a saprolite layer about 
0.5 to 2.0 feet thick, consisting of medium stiff to hard sandy silt and medium dense silty 
sand. The saprolite layer was generally underlain by basalt rock formation and clinker 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 17 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The basalt rock formations encountered are generally medium hard to very hard 
and slightly to moderately weathered. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, 
time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

Field Infiltration Testing 
The field infiltration tests were performed by advancing PQ-size casing (3.35-inch 
inside diameter) at each boring location to the selected test depth, generally near the 
proposed LID locations provided by G70. The inside of the PQ casing was then cleaned 
out to remove any cuttings or loose material within the casing. The casing remained at the 
bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the soil or basalt rock exposed at 
the bottom of the boring. The cased boreholes were then pre-soaked prior to performing 
the percolation testing. 
Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the average infiltration 
rates of the underlying subsurface materials at different depths. In general, each test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic 
readings over a 1 hour trial period. The infiltration rates are then calculated based on the 
results of the fourth and last trial for each test location. It should be noted that at least 
24 inches of water immediately drained out of the casing in Borings I-3 and I-10 for all four 
trials. Since the actual percolation rate could not be established over a period of 1 hour, a 
percolation rate greater than 60 inches per hour was noted.  The calculated infiltration 
rates at each test location are summarized in the following table. Field infiltration test 
results are presented on the attached Plates 15.1 through 15.11. Infiltration test results 
performed at the western detention basins contained in our previously referenced report 
are presented on the attached Plates 17.3 and 17.4

It should be noted that the infiltration values presented above are the rates of 
infiltration through the soil/rock exposed at the bottom of the tested borehole and reduces 
the lateral spreading of the water in the subsurface materials. Based on the results of the 
field infiltration testing, the average infiltration rates at these locations indicate that an 
infiltration/detention system and similar methods of storm water management would be 
feasible for the project. Due to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the absorption 
capacity of the disposal system should be confirmed by conducting additional infiltration 
tests during construction. 
Limitations 
The geotechnical field data submitted in this letter report are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and infiltration tests. Variations of the 
subsurface conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the findings provided in this letter report.
