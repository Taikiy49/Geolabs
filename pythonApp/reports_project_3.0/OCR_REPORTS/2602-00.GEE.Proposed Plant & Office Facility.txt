INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
percolation testing for the engineering design of the proposed Brewer Environmental
Industries Incorporated plant and office facility at Kaomi Loop in Campbell Industrial Park
on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity is
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work on the project was performed in general accordance with our proposal
to Brewer Environmental Industries Incorporated dated This report
summarizes our findings from our field exploration and percolation testing and presents
our geotechnical engineering recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the northerly end of Kaomi Loop in Campbell
Industrial Park in the Ewa District on the island of Oahu Hawaii Currently the site is the
existing Brewer Environmental Industries Barbers Point Plant and is occupied by several
structures tanks and pits
It is proposed to demolish the existing structures and other site improvements and
to construct a new plant which will include industrial office and warehouse space It is
our understanding that the planned structure will be a one 1 story metal building with
a mezzanine level The proposed new site improvements will also include asphaltic
concrete and portland cement concrete pavements
Detailed plans of the proposed site grading were not available for use in this
exploration However it is our understanding that site grading will probably involve the
GEOLABS HAWAI

placement of about two 2 feet of fill to raise the grade of the site above the estimated
inundation elevation
It is also proposed to install a septic tank system with leachfields for the treatment
and disposal of sanitary wastes generated in the new facility
It is our further understanding that the owner intends to acquire two 2 small
parcels of land which are contiguous to the site to provide additional area for access
roads driveways and parking These additional parcels are not included in the scope of
this exploration

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated along the westerly or ocean side boundary of
Campbell Industrial Park in the Ewa District on the island of Oahu Hawaii The site is
located in the northwesterly corner of the Kaomi Loop Industrial Subdivision which was
under construction during the time of our exploration The general location of the site and
its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The site is currently used for the manufacture and storage of agricultural chemicals
The project site is occupied by several single story wood framed buildings storage tanks
bins and stockpiled materials In addition there is an existing lagoon or surface
impoundment in the northwesterly corner of the site Most of the site is occupied by
these existing features The majority of the remainder of the site is used for access roads
or driveways and is either paved or covered with gravel Our field exploration was limited
to accessible areas within the site
In general the topography of the site is nearly level It appears that the site was
originally graded by the placement of about three 3 or four 4 feet of fill over the original
ground surface There is a low CMU wall around the boundaries of the site Although
this wall retains about a foot or so of soil it appears that its primary function is as a
containment wall
Elevations at the site vary from about five 5 to about seven 7 feet above Mean
Sea Level

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Waianae and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Ewa Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the majority of the coastal plain are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during high stands of the sea which
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Waianae Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2602 00 Page 7
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials
The site is situated in the northwesterly corner of the Kaomi Loop Industrial
Subdivision and along the southerly boundary of the existing Chevron refinery Our work
for others in the Kaomi Loop subdivision which was under construction at the time of our
field exploration show that the original terrain in the area is karstic in nature
Karstic terrains form in areas underlain by carbonate rocks such as coral algal reef
limestone and are the result of the chemical dissolution of the carbonate rock by naturally
acidic rainwater These terrains are typified by open cavities or sinkholes at the surface
Frequently these surface cavities extend underground forming voids or caves

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of six 6 borings at the project site to depths ranging between about 16 5 and about 21 5
feet below the existing ground surface within the proposed building area and the drilling
of two 2 percolation test holes to depths of about seven 7 feet below the existing
ground surface in the vicinity of the proposed septic system leachfields
In general most of our borings at the proposed facility site encountered a surface
layer of fill underlain by a relatively competent ledge of coral algal reef limestone and
medium dense to dense coral sands and gravels to the maximum depths explored
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths ranging between
about 3 8 and 7 9 feet below the existing ground surface during our exploration
Falling head percolation tests were conducted in the drilled percolation test
borings These tests indicate that the subsoils at the site generally have relatively slow
absorption rates

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our findings development of the proposed project site is feasible
provided that our recommendations are followed Spread or strip footings bearing on the
upper existing fill may be used to support the proposed structures after proofrolling and
recompaction in accordance with the recommendations presented herein As an
alternate the foundations may bear on new fill provided that the fill is select material
placed and compacted in accordance with the recommendations presented herein
Site Preparation
It is our understanding that the majority of the site grading work for the proposed
project will consist of the placement of about two 2 feet of fill to raise the grade of the
site above the estimated inundation elevation
The following recommendations are general guidelines for the design of the
grading and for the development of general specifications
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the proposed grading limits
should be cleared of the existing structures stockpiled materials all vegetation and
deleterious materials The resulting grub and spoil material should be disposed
of properly off site
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
overexcavated to firm natural material and the resulting depression backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2602 00 Page 10
The existing open lagoon should be cleaned of sediment and sludge to firm
ground and backfilled with select granular fill placed in level lifts not exceeding
eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of its
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Test Method If the
placement of fill below the groundwater table will be required we recommend that
self densifying material such as ASTM C 33 No 67B gravel 3B Fine be used
to a level of at least one 1 foot above the highest water level
Following the clearing of the site and backfilling of the lagoon the ground surface
should be scarified brought to near optimum moisture content and proofrolled with
a heavy vibratory roller for a minimum of ten 10 passes Any soft weak or
yielding areas disclosed during proofrolling operations should be over excavated
to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill Upon completion of the proofrolling operations the
area should be further compacted if necessary to obtain a minimum of 90
maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary It is also advisable to institute a dust control program
during grading operations
In general excavated on site soil materials should be suitable as fill material
Additional imported material should be well graded granular material less than 3
inches in size with less than 25 passing the No 200 sieve
Any boulders or cobbles larger than twelve 12 inches in maximum dimension
should be removed from the excavated material and disposed of off site

Foundation Support
Spread footing foundations bearing on the recompacted existing fill or new fill may
be used to support the proposed structures
An allowable bearing capacity of 3 000 p s f may be utilized for the design of
foundations bearing on the existing fill which has been proofrolled and recompacted or
on new fill which has placed and compacted in accordance with the recommendations
presented herein This value may be increased by one third for temporary wind and
seismic loads The footings should be embedded a minimum of two 2 feet below the
lowest adjacent grade
A minimum footing width of 18 inches is recommended
The bottom of all footings should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of its
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 70 and inspected by the soils
engineer prior to concrete placement
If a footing is situated next to a utility trench it should be extended to the bottom
of the trench or the trench backfill must be well compacted to prevent footing settlement
due to settlement of the trench backfill
Estimated settlements are anticipated to be on the order of less than one half of
an inch total and one quarter of an inch differential
Cavity Detection
As discussed above the site is situated in a karstic coral algal limestone terrain
Subsurface cavities and other voids are commonly encountered in coral formations To
reduce the possibility of foundation or structural settlements resulting from the collapse
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2602 00 Page 13
of such voids we recommend that a program of probing and grouting the footing
locations be considered for the project
The probes should be drilled at each column location and on 10 foot centers for
line footings The probe holes should be a minimum of three 3 inches in diameter and
should extend a minimum of 10 feet below the footing subgrade The holes should be
backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low pressures
If a cavity or void is suspected at a footing location additional probes should be
drilled to attempt to delineate the extent of the suspected void
In order to avoid having to drill the probes inside the footing excavations which
would increase drilling costs it is suggested that the probing and grouting work be
performed after either site clearing or site grading but prior to the commencement of
excavation work
The probing and grouting operations should be observed by the geotechnical
engineer to see that the above recommendations are properly implemented and to
evaluate other solutions to backfill potential voids
As an alternate to conventional probing and grouting potential cavities could be
detected by geophysical methods Our own experience indicates that ground penetrating
radar can be a very effective method in coral algal formations
This method uses impulse radar technology to obtain a continuous profile of the
subsurface The impulse propagates through the subsurface materials until it encounters
a soil interface void or embedded object Depending upon the dielectric constant of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2602 00 Page 14
interface void or object a portion of the signal is reflected back This reflected signal can
be intercepted and recorded
Strong reflections show up as anomalies within the recorded data field Anomalies
may be interpreted as interfaces between materials of different consistency composition
or structure Generally strong anomalies within consolidated coral formations may be
considered to be voids The site could be geophysically surveyed to map strong
anomalies potential voids within the site The geophysical work should be conducted
after the site has been proofrolled and prepared for fill placement
Slabs On Grade
It is anticipated that the floors of proposed buildings on the project site will be
concrete slabs on grade It is also anticipated that some of the slabs will be subject to
vehicular traffic e g forklifts and therefore will function as rigid pavements Pavements
will be discussed in a subsequent section
Building slabs and their cushions may be supported by the upper fill provided the
subgrade is proofroiled and recompacted as recommended above A 4 inch layer of 3B
Fine gravel or basaltic termite barrier should be provided below the slabs as cushion fill
To reduce the potential of the subgrade soils heaving due to subsequent
saturation we recommend that the subgrade soils should be kept continuously moist by
water sprinkling prior to concrete placement
in order to prevent future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor
coverings an impervious moisture membrane may be considered below the slabs

provided an inch of moist fine sand is placed above the plastic membrane to help
concrete curing

Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were conducted in the vicinity of the proposed leachfields In
general the field testing indicated that the subsoils at these locations have relatively slow
permeability or infiltration characteristics The tests generally indicate that the absorption
capacity for leachfield design is about twenty 20 minutes per inch
Based on our field percolation testing and the methods given in Public Health
Service Publication No 526 Manual of Septic Tank Practice it is our opinion that an
infiltration rate of 1 0 gallons per day per square foot of leachfield trench bottom may be
used for the design of the proposed septic system leachfields

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded to the
geotechnical engineer for review and written comment prior to construction This review

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the sitework and excavation
phases of the work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The
recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and approved by
the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing were performed on selected samples obtained during our
field exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule
appropriate tests to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil
and or rock materials sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content Determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and
to aid in determining certain properties such as relative saturation The moisture
content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample is shown
at the appropriate locations on plates summarizing test results and at the appropriate
sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve analysis tests are performed to determine the grain size distribution of
the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in the Unified Soil
Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering properties The
results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical
parameters of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering
analyses pertaining to the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation
support and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate B 2
