PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Kaneohe area on the island of Oahu It is
proposed to construct a new sewage pump station at the corner of Kahekili Highway and

will include a basernent with a finish floor elevation of approximately 36 feet below the
existing ground surface or at an elevation of 9 5 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL
Portion of the pump station will consist of on grade slabs near the existing ground
surface
A new six inch 6 force main and 18 inch gravity sewer line are also planned The
new six inch 6 force main will be installed from the new pump station to the existing
Ahuimanu Sewage Treatment Plant It is our understanding that the new force main invert
will be approximately five feet 5 below the existing ground surface The new 18 inch
gravity line is planned from the new pump station to the existing pump station on
Ahuimanu Road The proposed gravity sewer line invert elevations range from 15 5 to
17 5 feet above MSL or at depths of about 12 to 26 feet below the existing ground
surface

General
It is our opinion that the proposed project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering standpoint provided that our geotechnical engineering recommendations as
presented in this report are incorporated into the project design
Sewage Pump Station
Based on our field exploration and current project design it is anticipated that the
basement floor of the proposed pump station will bear on the very stiff to hard clayey silt
saprolite deposit Therefore we recommend a mat foundation system for support of the
pump station Foundations for the adjacent emergency generator room will be near the
existing ground surface Because of the presence of moderately expansive
undocumented fills we recommend that the emergency generator room foundation be
founded on conventional spread and or continuous footings bearing on at least two feet
2 of compacted select granular fill

Due to the depth of the planned excavation the excavation will likely require
shoring support and dewatering during the proposed construction
Detailed discussions are presented in the following subsections
Foundation
In order to provide uniform support for the proposed pump station structure we
recommend that a one foot 1 thick cushion layer consisting of 3B Fine gravel
should be provided below the mat foundation for the bottom level of the pump
station
Mat foundations bearing on the stiff clayey silt may be designed with an allowable
bearing pressure of 3 000 p s f For the generator room building conventional
foundations may be designed using an allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 p s f
All footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade and should have a width of not less than 18 inches The allowable
bearing pressures may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such
as wind or seismic
Based on the groundwater level encountered in the drilled borings a high
groundwater level of up to elevation 27 feet MSL is recommended for the uplift
design due to the hydrostatic head It is anticipated that the pump station will be
subjected to relatively large buoyant forces due to a submerged condition of the
structure It is our opinion that the pump station be designed such that the
buoyant force will be resisted entirely by the dead weight of the pump station
structure Dewatering operations should be continuous until completion of the

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
sewer pipes an average unit weight of 120 p c f for compacted backfill material
and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 are recommended Earth forces upon the pipe
increase rapidly with the width of the trench and therefore the width of the
sewerline trench should be kept to a minimum
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2692 10 Page 15
Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered for the gravity sewer
line since the proposed sewer line alignment is located within travelled ways
The proposed gravity sewer line inverts are to relatively deep depths of up to 26
feet below the existing ground surface If the loads on the sewer line pipes are
significant the use of stronger pipe or a concrete jacket should be considered
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new sewer line construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the sewer structures The net increase in
loading will cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert
Based on our calculations small primary settlement on the order of inch is
anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be provided Additional settlement could occur if improper trench
support or dewatering procedures are allowed

Trench Support
Because of the relatively deep depth to the gravity sewer line invert from the
existing ground surface it is our opinion that continuous inter locking sheetpiling

support may be used for the installation of the gravity sewer line It is important

excavation which could result in potential settlements of adjacent structures This

was assumed for estimating the sheetpile penetration for bottom heaving

on the use of sump pumping inside the excavation with no groundwater drawdown
outside the excavation The use of a deep well system outside the excavations to
draw down the natural groundwater level to reduce the required sheeting
embedment is not recommended

Construction Dewatering
As indicated dewatering will be necessary for the gravity sewer line since the
groundwater level encountered in our field exploration were above the proposed
sewer pipe inverts The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the
trench shoring support so that the stability of the excavations are not
compromised The operations should be carried out without softening the bottom
of the excavations
The groundwater table was measured in our borings between elevations 26 and
36 feet MSL which may be as high as 13 feet above the deepest excavation
bottom
Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
the responsibility of the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents be
written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit construction of the
proposed waterline structures under safe and dry conditions These works or
systems should include but not be limited to pumping
We suggest that the following three 3 basic criteria be considered in selecting a
suitable method of dewatering
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2692 10 Page 20
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing structures
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and dry
working conditions in the excavations
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations
is generally not recommended even though this method could reduce the required
sheetpile penetration The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater
table could induce consolidation of subsoils resulting in potential ground
settlements affecting the adjacent roadways and strictures
Construction Considerations
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering it is also recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative wno should be
required to be continuously present at the site will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but
not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2692 10 Page 21
Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project dewatering
operations should be done without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water
only and should be coordinated with shoring installation so that the excavation

Extra caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches that connect

trenches could act as subdrains and cause areal settlements and damage to the
utility lines and or other existing adjacent structures

well graded less than one inch 1 in size and have no more than 10 percent
passing No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed
provided the granular material is self draining
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within two feet 2

Structures supported on the stiff to medium dense alluvial deposits may be
designed using an allowable bearing value up to 2000 p s f Settlements on the order of
less than one half inch 2 are anticipated for these structures
If it is necessary to place the manhole structures on the very soft lagoonal
deposits an 18 inch layer of gravel e g 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 wrapped
in a filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N should be provided below the structure to provide
uniform bearing support An allowable bearing value of 1000 p s f may be used on the
18 inch gravel layer The increase in load on the subsoils due to the manhole structure
is small Therefore significant settlements are not anticipated if proper shoring and
dewatering techniques are utilized
The lateral earth pressure acting on the proposed underground manhole structures
will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the compactive effort

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling nine 9 borings to
depths of 16 5 to 81 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted rig was used to drill the borings
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the borings
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in accordance with ASTM
Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch standard split sooon sampler or a 2 4 inch D split barrel sampler with
a 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches Soil samples were also obtained by
hydraulically pushing a 3 inch Shelby tube sampler The blow counts to advance the
sampler the last 12 inches and hydraulic pressures are shown on the Boring Logs at the
approximate sample depths
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD
50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits test ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs and are summarized on
Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Torvane Shear tests are used to determine the coefficient of cohesion in a soil
sample The results of the torvane shear tests are presented on Plate B 1
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads Th sse tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate B 2
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data are used
in various engineering calculations The result of the direct shear test is presented on
Plate B 4
Consolidation tests ASTM Test Method D 2435 80 are used to evaluate the
consolidation characteristics of the soil The result of the test is summarized on Plate B 5
