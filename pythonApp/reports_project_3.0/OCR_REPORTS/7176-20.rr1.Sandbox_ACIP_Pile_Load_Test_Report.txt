PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
The project involves the development of Lot “C” on the Ewa side of the existing 
John A. Burns School of Medicine (JABSOM) campus in the Kaka’ako area of Honolulu 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project area was formerly occupied by an asphalt 
concrete paved on-grade parking lot bounded in a clockwise direction by Ilalo Street to 
the north, Keawe Street to the east, existing warehouses to the south, and Pier 1 facility to 
the west. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
Previously, we provided geotechnical engineering consultation for the ACIP pile 
foundation design, and our recommendations were presented in a report entitled 
“Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, Kewalo Incubation Center and Entrepreneur’s 
Sandbox, HTDC Office Facility - Phase 1, Kaka’ako, Oahu, Hawaii”, dated 
January 13, 2016. 
Based on the foundation plan, the ACIP piles will have a diameter of 18 inches 
and lengths of about 35 to 55 feet below the bottom of pile cap elevation in order to 
develop an allowable compressive load capacity ranging from 100 to 300 kips (design 
load). The ACIP piles would extend through the medium dense to dense fill, loose

STATIC COMPRESSIVE PILE LOAD TEST 
In order to evaluate the adequacy of the recommended design lengths of the 
ACIP pile foundation, a static compressive pile load test was conducted on a dedicated 
18-inch diameter ACIP pile, designated as Test Pile No. 1. The load test pile was 
installed to a depth of about 77.1 feet below the existing ground surface by Kelikai Inc., 
the pile installation subcontractor, using a Bauer BG28 drill rig equipped with automatic 
monitoring equipment providing drilling information, such as drilling depth, drilling rate, 
auger torque, stroke count, etc., on a near-continuous basis.  
The test pile was drilled utilizing continuous flight augers. As the augers were 
removed, the hole was grouted from the bottom upward. A truck-mounted hydraulic 
concrete pump was used to pump the grout through the continuous flight augers. After 
sound quality grout (based on visual observation) appeared at the top of the pile, a 
reinforcing cage was inserted into the grouted hole. An 18-inch diameter steel casing of 
about 26 inches in length was then set around the top of the ACIP pile, extending about 
6 inches above the existing ground surface. The top of pile provided a stable platform 
for supporting the load testing apparatus, including the hydraulic jack, load cell, 
spherical bearing and steel spacer plates.  
To provide support for the load test, four 18-inch diameter and approximately 
50 feet long ACIP reaction piles, designated as RP-1 through RP-4, were installed in a 
similar fashion as the load test pile with two 56-foot long and 1.41-inch diameter center 
bars in lieu of a rebar cage.  The reaction piles and load test pile were completed on 
May 2 and 3, 2018, respectively, and their approximate locations are shown on the ACIP 
Test Pile Location Plan, Plate 2.  Installation records for the load test and reaction piles are 
presented in the Appendix.  It should be noted that the load test pile was redrilled due to 
substantial grout intake, possibly due to the encountering of potential cavity. The initial 
grout was allowed to set overnight before redrilling on the following day.  Reaction Pile 
No. 4 was redrilled twice due to rapid grout subsidence and loss of auger into the drilled 
hole by the subcontractor. 
To better understand and predict the load transfer mechanism at the pile-soil 
interface, the load test pile was instrumented with seven vibrating wire embedment 
strain gages (Model No. 4200A-2) manufactured by Geokon, Inc. (as specified on the 
structural drawing). The strain gages were connected using a multiplexer to a Geokon 
Micro-10 data acquisition system to record the strains during the compressive load test. 
The embedment strain gages were installed in the load test pile at the following depths 
below the existing ground surface.

The ACIP piling subcontractor, Kelikai, Inc., provided the load testing equipment, 
including a reaction beam, hydraulic jack, hydraulic pump, reference beams, and other 
accessories. Geolabs, Inc. provided the load cell, digital dial gauges, survey level 
equipment, and a spherical bearing. The test loads were measured by a 390-ton 
capacity load cell manufactured by Geokon. 
Geolabs provided the data acquisition system including a multiplexer, datalogger, 
and data acquisition software program to collect the strain gage readings during the 
load testing. The strain gage readings were further used in the analyses and evaluation 
of the static compressive load transfer. Displacements at the top of the load test pile 
were measured and recorded by the digital dial gauges.  
The pile load test was conducted in general accordance with the Quick Load Test 
Method of ASTM D1143. In general, the test pile was loaded to about 675 kips (225% of 
the design load) in approximately 37.5-kip load increments. Each load increment was 
held for at least 8 minutes or until the rate of the pile head movement generally has 
stabilized. In addition, the static load test was held for approximately 2 hours at the 
300-kip, 600-kip and 675-kip load increments to evaluate the effects of creep. After 
reaching the maximum test load of 675 kips, the load test pile was unloaded in 
approximately five decrements and held for at least 8 minutes at each decrement. The 
results of the compressive pile load test are summarized below.
