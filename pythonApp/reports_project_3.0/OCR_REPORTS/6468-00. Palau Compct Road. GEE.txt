SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field explorations at both failed embankment areas generally encountered
embankment fill underlain by soft alluvial deposits and/or soft residual soils overlying
dense, highly to extremely weathered breccia and saprolitic soils. Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered and our slope stability analyses, we believe the
failed embankment areas may be reconstructed using a combination of drain rock fill
materials beneath the toe of slope and geotextile reinforcement placed within the
reconstructed slope. In general, we believe the primary geotechnical considerations for
the embankment repair include the following:
° Constructing a keyway below the toe of the new embankment that extends
through the soft soils to bear on the underlying stiff and/or dense
materials.
° The keyway should be backfilled with free-draining materials lined with
non-woven geotextile to reduce the potential for the buildup of pore water
pressures below the fill embankment and/or toe of slope.
° Installation of a subdrain system to relieve the excess pore water
pressures below the embankment and in the keyway.
° Installation of a reinforced soil slope to rebuild the fill embankment above
the keyway.
Typical sections depicting the above key elements of the embankment repair are
shown on the typical sections for Section 46 and Section 16 on Plates 4.1 and 4.2,
respectively. The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and
specific geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1. GENERAL
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Palau Compact Road Package B Embankment Repair project located
in the Republic of Palau. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting
from our field explorations, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project.
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for design of the
landslide mitigation to restore the roadway to its previously functional state. The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report.
1.1. Project Considerations
The Package B Embankment Repair is located along a portion of the Palau
Compact Road project in the States of Airai and Ngchesar on the Island of Babeldaob in
the Republic of Palau. Two landslides along portions of the Package B Palau Compact
Road occurred following heavy rains.
The first landslide area (designated as Section 46) occurred around August 2009
at a large fill embankment spanning approximately between Sta. 23+910 and
Sta. 24+010. The second landslide area (designated as Section 16) is at another fill
embankment spanning approximately between Sta. 18+920 and Sta. 19+070. This
second landslide area was cracked and had subsequently failed prior to our field
exploration in August and September 2011. The intent of the project is to mitigate the
two landslide areas and to restore the roadway to its previously functional state.

The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an
overview of the surface and subsurface conditions at the two landslide project sites. The
subsurface information was utilized to develop a soil and/or rock data set to formulate
the geotechnical recommendations for design of the landslide mitigation to restore the

2.1 Regional Geology
The Palau Islands are a part of Micronesia located in the western portion of the
North Pacific Ocean, as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. It lies about
885 kilometers to the east of Mindanao, Philippines, and approximately 1,300 kilometers
to the southwest of Guam. The Palau Islands consist of a small, elongated, arc-shaped
island chain in the Western Caroline Islands, which trend generally north to south
extending from Latitude 8 degrees, 12 minutes North to Latitude 6 degrees, 53 minutes
North and from Longitude 134 degrees, 8 minutes East to Longitude 134 degrees,
44 minutes East. The major islands comprising the Republic of Palau generally trend in
a north-south direction and consist of Babeldaob, Koror, Urukthapel, Eil Malk, Peleliu,
and Angaur.
The Palau Compact Road Package B Embankment Repair project is located on
the Island of Babeldaob, which lies approximately 2 kilometers north of Koror across the
Toachel Channel. Koror is the main city of the Republic of Palau. The Island of
Babeldaob is composed of ten states including Ngarchelong, Ngaraard, Ngardmau,
Ngiwal, Ngeremlengui, Melekeok, Ngatpang, Ngchesar, Aimeliik, and Airai, as shown
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
The Palau Islands form a curved chain of islands totaling about 350 in number
and covering approximately 458 square kilometers in area. The island chain extends
approximately 161 kilometers in length and 64 kilometers in width. Babeldaob, the
largest island of the chain, is an elongated, volcanic island trending north to south and
encompasses about 208 square kilometers of land area. Elevations on Babeldaob
range from sea level along the coastal areas to about +242 meters Mean Sea Level
(MSL) at Mt. Ngerekelehuus on the northwestern side of Babeldaob.
The Palau Reef encircles the Island of Babeldaob as a barrier and fringing reef,
creating extensive quiet water lagoons and bays with some deep channels and
passages adjacent to the island. Beyond the fringing reef, the ocean floor drops steeply
to greater than 300 meters in depth within a distance of about 1 kilometer.

The Island of Babeldaob is primarily of volcanic origin with some localized
occurrence of limestone rock formation, particularly in southern coastal Babeldaob. The
relief generally is characterized by hilly terrain of elongated ridgelines frequently incised
by streams and gullies exposing extremely weathered volcanic earth materials. Flatter
alluvial areas are restricted mainly to the mouths and banks of larger streams and along
the coastline situated behind sandy beaches where inland hills do not abruptly terminate
at the shoreline. More rugged terrain consisting of sharp ridges and cliff lines occur on
the western and southeastern portions of the island. Both rounded hilltops and sharp
ridgelines rise above the generally rolling terrain at scattered locations, offering
prominent geographic landmarks among the generally homogenous backdrop.
The coastline of Babeldaob is irregularly shaped with many small bays and
elevated headlands. Swamp areas consisting of dense mangrove within shallow tidal-
influenced mudflats exist throughout approximately 70 percent of the coastline areas.
The width of the mangrove swamps along the coastline varies from less than 10 meters
to greater than 800 meters in some places. The mangrove swamps encompass much of
the lowland coastline and large areas bordering the bays and estuaries. Marsh areas
exist in some inland areas generally bordering major river systems and broad basins.
The drainage pattern on Babeldaob is essentially trellised with the larger streams
and rivers aligned with the longitudinal axis of the island. Ngermeskang River is the
longest river measuring about 15.8 kilometers in length, which flows into
Karamando Bay from the northeast. The Ngerdok River is the largest watershed on
Babeldaob encompassing some 29 square kilometers in area.
The Philippine Sea and the Pacific Ocean Basin are separated by a series of
elongated, submarine ridge structures. The Palau Islands are situated at the exposed
crest of the southwestern most submarine ridge of the area. The Palau Ridge is part of
the Palau-Kyushu ridge, which extends from Kyushu (Japan) southward towards Palau
where a complementary trench structure forms to the southeast of Palau. These ridge
and trench structures constitute the Palau Arc.

Babeldaob Island exists as a result of the accumulation of volcanic materials
along the crest of the Palau Ridge. The form of the island is principally controlled by
many factors including the following: 1) faulting associated with past tectonic activity;
2) numerous historic land submergence and emergence as a result of sea level
fluctuation; 3) submarine erosion; 4) erosion by streams; and 5) deep tropical
weathering of volcanic materials comprising the island.
In general, Babeldaob consists of a central strip of the oldest volcanic rock,
overlapped on the east by the next younger unit, and separated by a fault zone on the
west from the youngest volcanic rocks, which are arranged in a rough circle around a
former caldera at Karamando Bay. The regional ridge and trench structure, extensive
volcanism, and the presence of numerous faults cutting the pyroclastic deposits confirm
past tectonic activity. However, at the present time, there is little evidence of on-going
deformation of the island arc system.
Based on the "Geology Map of Babelthuap and Adjacent Islands, Palau Islands"
prepared by the U.S. Army and the U.S. Geological Survey, the geologic materials
mapped on Babeldaob include various volcanic, alluvial, and estuarine/lagoonal or
beach deposits. The most recent earth material deposits include man-made fills,
mangrove swamp mud, alluvial sediments, and coralline beach deposits. These
materials continue to be deposited at the present time. Terrestrial and marine sediments
deposited in historic Miocene through Pleistocene time include the alluvial Airai clay
with interbedded lignite and the Palau limestone formation. The volcanic materials
comprise the oldest deposits and consist of the Babeldaob, Aimeliik, and Ngeremlengui
Formations. These volcanic units comprise the bulk of the land area on Babeldaob
Island.
The Babeldaob formation consists of massive basaltic-andesitic volcanic breccia,
with some tuff breccia and layers of tuff (U.S. Army, 1956). Breccia is a coarse-grained
volcanic rock composed of angular, broken rock fragments or clasts welded together in
a finer-grained mineral rock matrix. Breccia is formed by volcanic explosions and/or
debris flows of super-heated rock mass. Rocks and soils associated with this formation

The Aimeliik formation consists of andesitic-basaltic volcanic breccias, tuff

The Ngeremlengui formation consists of andesitic and dacitic volcanic breccia

The formation occurs as a broad strip of area along the west-central and northwest side
of Babeldaob, and along the northern tip of Arekalong Peninsula.
Other volcanic rock types include intrusive geologic bodies such as dikes, sills,
and volcanic plugs, which occur widely distributed in the volcanic units described above.
The larger intrusive rock structures can be found in the area around Karamando Bay
associated with the most recent volcanic center of that area (U.S. Army, 1956). Many of
these intrusive features are less weathered than their surrounding host rock material,
and are erosional remnants exhibited as prominent outcroppings.
Because of the humid tropical climate, the volcanic rock exposed at the surface
is usually extremely weathered to substantial depths with fresh surface outcroppings of
rock being generally rare in occurrence. The majority of the volcanic materials observed
on Babeldaob Island have been weathered to residual and saprolite soils with only
scattered localized pockets of hard, indurated rock. The deep tropical weathering of the
island has resulted in a mature stage topography with rounded, rolling hills separated by
scoured stream channels.
The volcanic rocks of Babeldaob have been extensively faulted during and
following their deposition. Faulting is believed to have been a major factor in
determining the distribution of volcanic formations as many geologic contacts occur
along fault lines (U.S. Army, 1956). Post-volcanic faulting has modified the distribution

2.2 Climate
The Palau Islands have a maritime tropical, rainy climate characterized by small
seasonal changes. Temperatures are generally uniform throughout the year with
abundant rainfall reaching a maximum in July and a minimum from February through
April. The mean annual temperature is about 27 degrees Celsius with a relative
humidity of about 82 percent. Rainfall typically occurs as intense showers of relatively
short duration and averages about 250 to 500 mm per month. Periods of prolonged,
heavy rainfall lasting several days at a time occur as typhoon storm centers frequently
pass to the north of Palau during the summer months. Other pronounced rainy periods
occur as the equatorial convergent zone advances northward within the vicinity of Palau
around June and July and again in September and October as the convergent zone
retreats southward.
2.3. Site Description
The project site is located along the east route of the Palau Compact Road
(Package B) from Airai to Ngchesar, on Babeldaob Island, Republic of Palau.
Section 46 and Section 16 of the embankment repair are located along Package B from
approximate Sta. 23+910 to Sta. 24+010 and Sta. 18+920 to Sta. 19+070, respectively.
The existing Package B roadway in these areas consists of one traffic lane each
in the northbound and southbound directions. The roadway is about 7.2 meters wide
and is covered with an asphaltic concrete pavement. Cement treated subbase course
(CTSB) shoulders, each about 1.2 meters wide, are located adjacent to both sides of
the asphaltic concrete pavement.

According to the original grading plans for the project, Section 46 and Section 16

At the time of our investigation, most of the affected area at Section 46 was

roughly 1 meter in height. DL-46 was almost completely filled with mud at the

The affected area of Section 16 was only partially covered with vegetation at the
time of our investigation, exposing miscellaneous fill and pavement materials. A
landslide scarp of about 1.5 meters high was observed near the crown of the landslide.
DL-16 was only partially filled with mud; however, a section of drainpipe was observed
to be disconnected near the downstream headwall.
Based on the topographic survey map provided, the existing ground surface of
the affected area at Section 46 of the project site ranges from about Elevation
+15.5 meters MSL near the toe of the embankment to about Elevation +30.5 meters
MSL at the top of roadway. By comparison, the existing ground surface at the affected
area of Section 16 ranges from about Elevation +98 meters MSL near the toe of the
embankment to about Elevation +107 meters MSL at the top of roadway.

Our field explorations at both failed embankment areas generally encountered

° Constructing a kKeyway below the toe of the new embankment that extends

° Installation of a subdrain system to relieve the excess pore water

° Installation of a reinforced soil slope to rebuild the fill embankment above
the keyway.

3.3. Reinforced Soil Slope
Following the excavation and backfill for the embankment keyway, reconstructing
the embankment with geotextile reinforcement to form a reinforced soil slope may
commence. Reinforced soil slopes utilize woven (and/or non-woven) geotextile fabric to
reinforce the slope face and to improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone.
This composite soil slope system essentially forms a slope with an ability to carry
appreciable structural loads.
As the slope is constructed, horizontal layers of reinforced slope geotextiles are
placed in the compacted fill at predetermined levels. The lengths of the reinforced slope
geotextile layers are designed to anchor potential failure zones into stable interior
sections of the embankment or hillside. As forces develop within a soil mass, the high-
strength geotextiles are immediately pulled into tension. The reinforced slope
geotextiles transfer this tensile force from the unstable soil back into the less-stressed
portions of the slope, and stability is thus maintained.
Based on our slope stability analyses, we recommend the vertical spacing of the
high-strength geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi PET 70/70 or equivalent) be provided at a
maximum of 1 meter vertical spacing. We recommend that a minimum of 2 meters of
unreinforced soils below the pavement surface elevation be provided to reduce the
potential for encountering the geotextiles during the installation of the guard rails or
poles for signs, etc. The upper portion of the unreinforced fill slope also may be sloped
at an inclination of 2H:1V. Typical reinforcement sections showing the layers of
geotextiles, kKeyway, and approximate elevations are shown on the Elevation View of
Keyway and Reinforced Soil Slope for Section 46 and Section 16 on Plates 6.1 and 6.2,
respectively.

excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as general fill materials.

and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

depth of about 200 mm, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
fourteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 14. The borings extended to depths
of about 9.6 to 19.7 meters below the existing ground/pavement surface. The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. The borings were drilled

Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with

were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586,

ROCK FRACTURE CHARACTERISTICS
The following terms describe general fracture spacing of a rock:
Massive: Greater than 600 millimeters apart
Slightly Fractured: 300 to 600 millimeters apart
Moderately Fractured: 150 to 300 millimeters apart
Closely Fractured: 75 to 150 millimeters apart
Severely Fractured: Less than 75 millimeters apart
DEGREE OF WEATHERING
The following terms describe the chemical weathering of a rock:
Unweathered: Rock shows no sign of discoloration or loss of strength.
Slightly Weathered: Slight discoloration inwards from open fractures.
Moderately Weathered: Discoloration throughout and noticeably weakened though not able to break by hand.
Highly Weathered: Most minerals decomposed with some corestones present in residual soil mass. Can be broken by hand.
Extremely Weathered: Saprolite. Mineral residue completely decomposed to soil but fabric and structure preserved.
HARDNESS
The following terms describe the resistance of a rock to indentation or scratching:
Very Hard: Specimen breaks with difficulty after several "pinging" hammer blows.
Example: Dense, fine grain volcanic rock
Hard: Specimen breaks with some difficulty after several hammer blows.
Example: Vesicular, vugular, coarse-grained rock
Medium Hard: Specimen can be broked by one hammer blow. Cannot be scraped by knife. SPT may penetrate by
~25 blows per 25 millimeters with bounce.
Example: Porous rock such as clinker, cinder, and coral reef
Can be indented by one hammer blow. Can be scraped or peeled by knife. SPT can penetrate by
~100 blows per 300 millimeters.
Example: Weathered rock, chalk-like coral reef
Crumbles under hammer blow. Can be peeled and carved by knife. Can be indented by finger Plate
pressure.
Example: Saprolite A-0 2

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Tests
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for the project
were visually examined and classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected
samples were tested to evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering properties
of the soils encountered. The laboratory testing program was formulated with the
emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, plasticity, gradation, shear strength, and
moisture-density relationships of the soils encountered.
The laboratory tests performed included determination of natural moisture
content and dry density, Atterberg Limits, sieve analysis, triaxial compression, direct
shear, CBR, and modified Proctor tests. Detailed descriptions of the various tests
performed for this exploration are presented below.
Moisture and Density
Moisture content and dry density tests were performed on selected soil samples
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and
correlating the layers between test borings. The moisture content tests were performed
in general accordance with ASTM D 2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Atterberg Limits
Nineteen (19) Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an aid in
classifying the soils. The Atterberg Limits tests were performed in general accordance
with ASTM D 4318. The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on
Plates B-1 and B-2.
Sieve Analysis
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered,
four Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and
to aid in soil classification. The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C 117
and C 136. Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-3.
Consolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression
To evaluate the drained and undrained shear strength of the clayey soils, five
Consolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXCU) tests were performed on
selected in-situ soil samples in accordance with ASTM D 4767. The soil samples were
saturated and consolidated to approximately 100, 200, and 300 kPa effective pressures

Direct Shear
To evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils, two strain-
controlled, consolidated-drained Direct Shear tests were performed on the in-situ soil
samples in accordance with ASTM D 3080. The test results are presented on Plates B-9
and B-10.
California Bearing Ratio (CBR)
Four CBR tests were performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the
strength of the in-situ materials for pavement support. The tests were performed in
accordance with ASTM D 1883. The test results are presented on Plates B-11 through
B-14.
Modified Proctor
Four laboratory compaction tests were performed on the bulk samples obtained
to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry density of the
near-surface soils. The Modified Proctor tests were performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 using the moist preparation method (wet to dry). The test results are
presented graphically on Plates B-15 through B-18.
