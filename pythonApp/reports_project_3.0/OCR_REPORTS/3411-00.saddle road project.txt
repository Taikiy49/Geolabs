SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our site reconnaissance and literature research for the proposed
Saddle Road project the proposed roadway alignments are generally
underlain by four types of geologic materials including Volcanic Ash
Deposits VA Cinder Sand Deposits VC A a Lava Flows ALF and
Pahoehoe Lava Flows PLF
Volcanic ash deposits generally consist of near surface light brown to brown
silty sand and sandy silt soils intermixed with some a a basalt rock
fragments The soil when wetted has relatively low strength
characteristics and is highly susceptible to erosion in a dry state In
addition if the in situ moisture contents are high enough they behave in a
thixotropic manner i e they lose strength temporarily when remolded
and or disturbed Therefore appropriate soil erosion protection dust
control provisions and design and construction procedures should be
observed and implemented for the earthwork operations when working with
volcanic ash deposits
Cinder sand deposits generally consist of poorly graded to well graded
coarse sands and gravels underlain by basalt rock formation Excavated
cinder sand is a good source of fill and backfill material and may be blended
with volcanic ash and basalt rock fragments to provide a relatively well
graded material for fills and backfills Based on previous experience and
discussions with contractors the material may experience a volume
decrease of 15 to 25 percent when excavated processed and used as fill
material Cut slopes in cinder sand deposits may be designed with a slope
ratio of 2H 1V or flatter
A a lava flows generally consist of light gray to grayish brown basalt with
predominantly clinker material and occasional dense basalt layers The
material forms a rough and broken ground surface with high relief consisting
of mound or piles of very spiny plates and blocks of rock piled in tumbled
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3411 00 Page 2
heaps The material is relatively easy to excavate and compact with heavy
construction equipment Based on previous experience and discussions
with contractors on the Island of Hawaii the material may experience a
25 to 35 percent decrease in volume when excavated processed and used
as fill material Cut slopes in clinker materials may be designed with a slope
ratio of or flatter
Pahoehoe lava flows generally consist of more fluid lavas of dense basalt
interbedded with relatively small clinker seams The rock varies from highly
vesicular to massive with lava tubes blisters and other void spaces which
are commonly encountered in pahoehoe flows during excavation work
Depending on the average particle size and the amount of void spaces in
the fill mass dense basalt may experience an increase in volume of about
10 to 20 percent when excavated processed and used as fill materials Cut
slopes in dense basalt may be designed with a slope ratio of or
flatter Fills consisting of basalt cobbles and boulders should be designed
with a slope ratio of or flatter
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and
preliminary grading recommendations
INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
evaluation performed for the proposed Saddle Road project extending approximately from
Mamalahoa Highway to Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the proposed Saddle Road alignments are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations based on our site reconnaissance and literature research performed
for the proposed Saddle Road project These geotechnical findings and recommen
dations are intended for preliminary earthwork and grading design purposes only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The existing Saddle Road was initially constructed in 1942 by the United States
Department of the Army to provide access to the Pohakuloa Army Base on the Island of
Hawaii The road was released by the United States and turned over to the Territory of
Hawaii in 1945 The Territory of Hawaii maintained the road until 1957 and then
transferred the maintenance responsibility to the County of Hawaii In 1965 Saddle Road
was included in the State Highway System established by the State of Hawaii However
we understand that the County of Hawaii has continued to provide maintenance work for
Saddle Road up to this date
Saddle Road is the only paved arterial roadway serving the U S Department of the
Army s Pohakuloa Training Area PTA and the astronomy complexes located atop
Mauna Kea Saddle Road has also become an important cross island link between Hilo
center of the County government on the east side of the Island the Town of Waimea to
the north and Kailua Kona and developing resort areas on the west side of the island
Until recent reconstruction efforts by the County of Hawaii the route has received only
minimal upgrading since it was first constructed Between 1988 and 1992 the County of
Hawaii widened and repaved 22 5 kilometers of the existing alignment from Hilo westerly
However geometric deficiencies i e substandard horizontal and vertical curvature still
exist We understand that the existing Saddle Road alignment remains inadequate in
terms of currently acceptable roadway design standards
Based on the available information we understand that it is proposed to improve
the existing Hawaii State Route SR 200 locally known as Saddle Road on the Island of
Hawaii The proposed improvement will consist of a two lane highway with enough
capacity to handle both current and projected traffic volumes up to and beyond the year
2014 The proposed Saddle Road project will extend approximately from SR 190 the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3411 00 Page 4
Mamalahoa Highway western terminus to about milepost 5 the intersection of Saddle
Road with the proposed Puainako Street extension near the town of Hilo eastern
terminus The approximate length of the existing route is about 78 kilometers In
addition to the planned improvements on the existing route several new alignments on
the east and west ends as well as through the PTA will need to be evaluated as part of
this study The proposed improvements to the existing Saddle Road route and the
various new alignments are shown on the Saddle Road Alignment Plan Plate 2
Due to the large areal extent of the corridors and length of the roadway alignment
it is desired to perform a preliminary site reconnaissance and background information
research to identify potential geologic hazards and or geotechnical constraints that may
affect the planning and preliminary design of the proposed roadway alignment Based on
this study a geotechnical materials report providing preliminary geotechnical
recommendations pertinent to slope stability of cuts and fills and general earthwork
requirements in the preliminary engineering phase will be prepared and submitted for
input in the selection of the final roadway alignment It is our understanding that
recommendations for pavement structural sections and materials evaluation for potential
borrow sites are not required under the current phase of this study

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 16 360 square kilometers The island was formed by the activity of five
shield volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had
activity during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both of which are still active
The proposed Saddle Road project is located along the southern flank of Mauna
Kea Volcano which is the second oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the Island of
Hawaii Mauna Kea is considered a dormant volcano which has not erupted in recorded
historical time Mauna Loa remains active to this date and is responsible for the fresh
lava flows visible from the existing Saddle Road The Humuula Saddle located between
Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa volcanoes was formed by lavas erupted from both
volcanoes which coalesced and formed this broad upland apron The majority of the
surficial ash and cinder deposits located within the Saddle Road project area were
erupted from Strombolian type cinder vents associated with the volcanism of Mauna Kea
The alignment of these cinder vents suggest possible buried rift zones
The majority of the surficial soil rock of the project site is believed to be derived
from three main volcanic events referred to as the lava flows of the Hamakua and
Laupahoehoe Volcanic Series associated with the Mauna Kea Volcano and the Kau
Volcanic Series associated with the Mauna Loa Volcano Recent historic eruptions from
Mauna Loa have flowed to the north toward the flank of Mauna Kea where they were
then diverted down slope to the east toward Hilo These flows typically consist of
pahoehoe lava Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and
an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered
within pahoehoe lavas Most cavities are formed when the lava remains in a molten state
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3411 00 Page 8
and may represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the
cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally localized and smaller in
horizontal extent
GENERALIZED GEOLOGIC FORMATIONS
Based on our aerial photograph evaluation field reconnaissance and research of
available information pertaining to the geology within the proposed roadway corridors the
proposed Saddle Road alignments traverse various earth materials which have been
generalized and grouped into four categories according to their physical characteristics
modes of occurrence and engineering properties relative to site grading and earthwork
construction These generalized groups may be described as follows
Volcanic Ash Deposits VA
For this project these deposits are represented by the symbol VA Volcanic Ash
The approximate extent of these deposits in relation to the proposed roadway
alignments are shown on the Geologic Base Maps Plates 3 through 17 The
mapped limits of the volcanic ash deposits are approximate and may be
gradational or contain anomalous zones within the mapped limits
Surface conditions generally consist of brown to light brown silty fine sand and
sandy silt soils intermixed with some a a basalt rock In general the volcanic ash
deposits were observed as a dry and powdery soil cover which appeared to be
deposited in layers primarily as volcanic air fall deposits generated from pyroclastic
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3411 00 Page 9
vents associated with Mauna Kea Volcano The volcanic ash deposits appeared
to be thicker in accumulation closer to the source vents and in previous
topographic lows or other localized basin structures
The terrain associated with these deposits appears as low relief rolling hills and
basins vegetated with short grass and scattered shrubs The volcanic ash material
appeared to be easily erodible as evidenced by ruts made from moving cattle and
automobile tracks Once the volcanic ash is exposed by a loss of vegetative
cover wind and rainfall tend to accelerate the natural weathering and erosion of
the material
Many shallow swale like drainage features were observed at the ground surface
however their source watersheds were observed to be relatively small It is our
opinion that these drainage features are the result of natural erosion caused by
rainfall runoff which easily carves into the unconsolidated volcanic ash deposits
Many of the drainage features appear to be substantial in size however their
actual discharge flow may be rather small due to the relatively small land area
drained by each of the drainage
The relatively gentle rolling topography created by the deposition of the volcanic
ash and cinder materials is punctuated by the occurrence of scattered
outcroppings of basalt rock typically observed as topographic mounds or hill tops
The exposed rock features appear as piles of basalt boulders and cobbles These
hill top features may represent the tops of older a a lava flows now buried by the
more recent deposition of the volcanic ash materials A a lava flows typically form
an undulating surface topography as the flow advances down slope Subsequent
wind and rainfall may have eroded the volcanic ash capping the flows and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3411 00 Page 10
exposed the lava as topographic highs As a result the adjacent topographic low
areas may contain greater thicknesses of volcanic ash soils generated by alluvial
deposition processes
It should be noted that the area mapped as volcanic ash soils may include
localized zones of a a basalt or cinder cones Because of the lack of subsurface
data under the current phase of our study the thickness of volcanic ash overlying
the basalt rock formation is uncertain and may vary considerably We believe that
all areas mapped as volcanic ash soils are generally underlain by basalt rock of
the a a variety although the depth is not certain without more detailed study
The larger gulches observed at the site such as Popoo and Waikii Gulches offer
good exposure of the thickness of the surficial volcanic ash deposits These
gulches have been eroded over time to expose the underlying basalt rock
formation at the invert The thickness of the volcanic ash was estimated to range
from about 1 to 10 meters depending on the location of the deposit in relation to
the source vent and subsequent alluvial reworking and deposition of the material
The gulch exposures reveal that the in situ volcanic ash soil may stand at near
vertical slopes however it is subject to erosion by raveling and animal burrows
The volcanic ash soils were observed to be weakly consolidated in localized
exposures however the material is typically friable and easily broken by hand
The ground surface covered by the volcanic ash deposits appear to be able to
support the growth of grass and shrubbery which aid in increasing the surface
resistance to erosion by wind and water runoff The climate of the area was
observed to be generally arid and the sandy and silty character of the volcanic
ash appeared to impart fair infiltration and drainage

Cinder Sand Deposits VC
These deposits are represented by the symbol VC Volcanic Cinder The
approximate extent of these deposits in relation to the proposed roadway
alignments are shown on the Geologic Base Maps Plates 3 through 17 The
mapped limits are approximate and may be gradational or contain anomalous
zones within the mapped limits It was observed that ground surfaces covered by
cinder material also contained substantial a a basalt rock protruding through the
surficial mantle of cinder especially in hilly terrain at areas of high relief
The cinder deposits generally consist of bedded pyroclastic materials including
poorly graded to well graded coarse sands and gravels pumice cobbles and
volcanic ash which generally mantle the older underlying basalt rock The ground
surface topography in areas underlain by the cinder sands is relatively flat to gently
sloping with low surface relief except where the deposits make up the actual cinder
cone vents The cinder deposits exposed on the margin of the cinder cones were
observed as both unconsolidated layered deposits as well as weakly cemented
deposits Near the PTA and at several borrow sites located in the vicinity of the
project vertically excavated cuts made into the cinder cone slopes reveal that the
material may stand at near vertical slopes however over time the deposit may
undergo chemical weathering and ravel more easily
It should be noted that because cinder deposits originate from volcanic explosions
within a vent the coarser sand and gravel materials may be found generally closer
to the source vent while the finer cinder and volcanic ash materials are more
prevalent down wind and further away from the source vent
The ground surface where comprised solely of coarse cinder sand and gravel
support some vegetation such as grasses and shrubbery but more often it is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3411 00 Page 12
typically barren and fairly level in topography Rainfall infiltration is high and
surface runoff is low as evidenced by a general lack of erosional drainage features
in these materials
A a Lava Flows ALF
These basalt rock formations are represented by the symbol ALF A a Lava Flow
The approximate extent of these types of lava flows in relation to the proposed
roadway alignments are shown on the Geologic Base Maps Plates 3 through 17
The mapped limits are approximate and may be gradational with anomalies
contained therein It was observed that land mapped as a a lava flow may be
covered by thin cinder and or volcanic ash deposits which mantle the underlying
a a rock topography
These rock formations include lavas extruded from both Mauna Kea and Mauna
Loa Volcanoes and are of varying ages In general the rock is composed of light
gray to grayish brown basalt forming a rough and broken ground surface with high
relief consisting of mounds or piles of very spiny tabular plates and blocks of rock
piled in tumbled heaps The rock is frequently termed clinker because of its
jagged appearance and the audible sound generated when traversing a flow The
piles of rough spiny rock may be loose in deposition at the ground surface but
usually become more dense and consolidated with depth as evidenced by the
occurrence of larger blocks of very hard blue rock material Some a a flows
remain as thick accumulations of weakly consolidated cobble and boulder sized
materials encountered throughout the entire thickness of the flow Other a a flows
may exhibit a spiny or rubbly flow surface texture which grades to a massive
relatively unfractured blue rock lava flow core again followed by a lower layer of
rubble clinker forming the flow bottom Lava tubes and void spaces may occur
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3411 00 Page 13
within the interior of a a flows although the voids are less prevalent than those
found in pahoehoe lava A a rock is generally easier to excavate except where the
core of the flow is encountered The core of the flow often requires greater effort
to excavate including blasting and intensive chipping and ripping
Due to the highly fractured and jumbled mass of the surface of these flows rainfall
runoff is generally low as most of the water percolates freely into the fractured
rock The downward percolating water commonly carries with it some of the thin
volcanic ash soil covering the flow leaving behind an exposed rocky surface visible
as hill crests and ridge line topographic highs
Pahoehoe Lava Flows PLF
These basalt rock formations are represented by the symbol PLF Pahoehoe Lava
Flow The approximate extent of these types of lava flows in relation to the
proposed roadway alignments is shown on the Geologic Base Maps
Plates 3 through 17 The mapped limits are approximate and may be gradational
with anomalies contained therein It was observed that some areas of land
mapped as pahoehoe lava flow may be covered with thin dark brown organic soil
deposits which mantle the pahoehoe topography These thin surficial soils were
observed to be generally located in the forested areas of the eastern portion of the
project toward the Town of Hilo
The PLF rock formations include lavas erupted from Mauna Loa Volcano and are
of varying ages In general the rock is composed of light gray to black basalt
forming a relatively smooth but hummocky surface topography of low to moderate
relief The pahoehoe rock is typically devoid of soil cover except in the forested
areas of the eastern portion of the project
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3411 00 Page 14
In general pahoehoe consists of more fluid lavas during deposition interbedded
with relatively thin clinker seams as opposed to a a lava which is composed
predominantly of clinker The rock varies from highly vesicular to massive with the
highly vesicular material being generally easier to excavate than the massive or
nonvesicular blue rock Lava tubes blisters and cavities are more common in
pahoehoe flows and are often encountered in the subsurface during excavation
work

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials Volcanic ash soils
encountered in cut areas may be stockpiled and reused as a source of general fill
material provided that the recommendations presented in the Fill Placement and
Compaction Requirements subsection are followed
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill The excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off site This condition
is highly likely for the portion of Alternate Route E 3 since soft and compressible
soils are likely to be encountered
The volcanic ash encountered along certain sections of the proposed alignments
has relatively low moisture contents and exists in a friable state as compared to
the Pahala Ash found in other locations on the Island of Hawaii The volcanic ash
soil when wetted has relatively low strength characteristics and is highly
susceptible to erosion in a dry state In addition if the in situ moisture contents are
high enough they become thixotropic i e they lose strength temporarily when
remolded and or disturbed Therefore appropriate soil erosion protection dust
control provisions and construction procedures should be observed and
implemented during the earthwork operations when volcanic ash soils are
involved

In areas where volcanic ash soils are exposed and designated to receive fill the
subgrades should be moisture conditioned to about optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D T 180 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density Due to the generally thixotropic nature

wetting of the soils It should be noted that the volcanic ash soils are generally
sensitive and may be difficult to achieve the specified compaction Therefore
additional compaction effort with proper moisture control may be necessary to
meet the specified compaction criteria

General fills and backfills consisting of volcanic ash and cinder up to 1 meter below

the optimum moisture content Therefore water trucks should be made available

compaction The State and Federal governments have enacted stringent

also advisable to institute a dust control program during grading operations to
reduce the amount of airborne dust due to the nature of the on site soils

Potential Geologic Hazards
Special attention was given to the presence of potential geologic hazards along
the proposed alignments during our site reconnaissance Geologic hazards that may
affect the selection of the final alignments may include low lying areas with potential
flooding hazard steep slope with potential instability and landslides soft soils and or
wetlands with potential fill ground subsidence and known faults that may traverse the
proposed roadway alignments Based on our site reconnaissance and literature research
of the proposed Saddle Road project it appears that no significant geologic hazards were
observed or identified along the proposed alignment except along portions of the
proposed Alternate Route E 3
Based on information provided by the Soil Conservation Maps it appears that soft
and highly compressible silty clay soils may be encountered along the following
areas Between and of Alternate Route E 3 and between
and of Alternate Route E 3 The soils may be perpetually wet
with high shrink swell potential and thickness of up to about 2 5 meters
In general the soft and wet materials may be over excavated where practical to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
compacted fill in controlled lifts up to the design finish grades In areas where over
excavation and replacement of the soft soils is not feasible due to the thickness of soft
soils encountered stabilization of the soft soils will be required prior to fill placement A

on which to place fill In general the stabilization layer should consist of a minimum
1 meter thick layer of surge rock or gravel with a geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 600X or

gravel layer should be embedded at least 1 meter below the lowest adjacent grade to

Due to the presence of soft subsoils at the toe of the planned fill slope a slope

Installation of utility lines at the new fill embankment is not recommended due to the long
term settlement effects of the underlying compressible soil which may result in ruptures
of the utility lines If these sections of the alternate routes are to be included in the final
roadway alignment we recommend that a detailed field exploration program involving
drilling test borings be conducted to provide additional information for the formulation of
specific recommendations pertaining to the wetland stabilization
