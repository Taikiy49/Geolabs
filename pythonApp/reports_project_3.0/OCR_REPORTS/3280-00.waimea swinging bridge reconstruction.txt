INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Waimea Swinging Bridge Reconstruction in the area of
Waimea on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed bridge reconstruction project These recommendations are
intended for design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for
construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project involves the reconstruction of the Waimea Swinging
Bridge which was damaged by Hurricane Iniki in 1992 Currently only remnants of the
bridge such as the bridge abutments exist at the site It is proposed to reconstruct a
similar bridge at the exact location of the previously damaged bridge Where feasible
the existing bridge abutments at the site will be used in the construction of the
replacement bridge
The proposed replacement bridge will be a suspension type bridge supported by
two towers with deadman anchors The bridge stretches across Waimea River with a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3280 00 Page 3
span of 240 feet and is about 3 feet wide The bridge is a pedestrian foot bridge We
anticipate the structural loads for the bridge to be generally light Based on information
provided to us we understand that the maximum compression load on the tower
foundations will be on the order of about 46 kips The estimated maximum pullout load
due to the cable tension is on the order of about 60 kips
We anticipate that grading of the site will not be required in the construction of
the replacement bridge However minor earthwork such as backfill around foundations
and abutments are anticipated in the bridge reconstruction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this
main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island
of Kauai Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series are generally characterized as thick flows
of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the west abutment site is underlain by stiff silty
soils over basalt gravels and cobble fragments to the maximum depth drilled of
approximately 20 feet below the ground surface The east abutment is underlain by
stiff to hard clayey and sandy silts overlying loose to dense sandy soils extending to the
maximum depths drilled of about 58 feet below the existing ground surface
As previously indicated a swinging bridge of similar dimensions and loads to the
damaged bridge is planned to be reconstructed at the same location The exposed
portions of the existing tower foundation and the anchor system appear to be in
relatively good condition Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered in the
borings and the assumed foundation depths as described in the Existing Bridge
Structures section we believe that the existing tower foundation at the west abutment
Tower No 1 may be reused to support the structural loads of the new bridge provided
that the footing is in good condition It should be noted that the existing footing is
located near the edge of the steep bank of Waimea River therefore protection of the
bank from scour will be necessary to reduce the potential for loss of support to the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3280 00 Page 10
footing due to scour As previously noted the size and depth of the rock anchors at
the west abutment are not known therefore we are unable to comment on the
structural load carrying capacity of the rock anchors If distress is found in the existing
foundations on the west bank use of the existing shallow foundation to support the new
bridge should be reevaluated In general the structural integrity of the foundation and
anchor system should be verified by the structural engineer when reused as
foundations for the replacement bridge
We understand that Tower No 2 at the east abutment was founded on timber
piles In general timber piles are susceptible to deterioration due to dry rot especially
near the groundwater level We understand that the existing pile foundation has been
in place for many years The existing condition of timber piles are not known and
would be very difficult to evaluate since they are embedded below the ground surface
Therefore we recommend that a new foundation system consisting of cast in place
drilled piers be used to replace the existing timber pile foundation to support the new
tower at the east abutment We believe that the existing deadman anchor may be
reused to support the structural loads imposed by the new bridge provided that the
existing deadman anchor is in relatively good condition

Rock Anchor for Tower No 1 West Abutment
The rock anchor system is used to resist both lateral and uplift loads imposed
on the bridge Lateral and uplift loads acting on the rock anchor will be resisted by the
frictional resistance between the anchor and the surrounding rock As previously
indicated the size and length of the rock anchors are not known Therefore we are
unable to comment on the load carrying capacity of rock anchors In general we
believe that the existing rock anchor system may be reused to support the new bridge
provided that the loads are similar to the previous bridge However it may be prudent
to evaluate the load carrying capacity and structural integrity of the rock anchors by
load testing if they are to be reused for support of the replacement bridge
Foundations for Tower No 2 East Abutment
As previously indicated Tower No 2 is supported on a pile foundation system
consisting of three 12 inch round timber piles of approximately 30 feet long We
believe that the driven piles are deriving their capacities from friction between the pile
shaft and the surrounding soils Because the existing timber piles have been in place
for many years and potentially subjected to dry rot the structural integrity of the timber
piles is questionable Therefore we recommend that a new deep foundation system
consisting of 24 inch diameter cast in place drilled piers be used to support the new
Tower No 2 on the east abutment
The cast in place drilled piers would derive vertical support from skin friction
between the pier shaft and the surrounding soils The drilled piers may be designed
with an allowable skin friction of 500 p s f The allowable skin friction value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as wind
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3280 00 Page 13
or seismic forces The end bearing component of the drilled piers should be discounted
due to the presence of groundwater and potential cave in of the silty sand layer near
the anticipated tip of the drilled piers that would preclude obtaining a clean bottom
during drilled pier installation Drilled piers should have a minimum center to center
spacing of 2 times the diameter to avoid capacity reduction due to group action
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations can be resisted by a combination of
the following 1 resistance of the pier soil system to deflect 2 passive resistance on
the vertical faces of pier caps where applicable and 3 side shear between the pier
caps and soil Lateral load resistance of pier is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the pier shaft allowable deflection at the top of pier
and induced moment in the pier We have estimated the lateral load capacity of the
24 inch diameter drilled piers for the free head and fixed head boundary conditions

The load bearing capacities of drilled piers depend to a large extent on the
contact between the pier shaft and the surrounding soils Therefore proper con
struction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the

Deadman Anchor for Tower No 2 East Abutment
The deadman anchor is used to resist both lateral and uplift loads imposed on
the bridge Lateral loads acting on the deadman anchor may be resisted by a
combination of 1 sliding resistance of the deadman anchor and the supporting
subgrade 2 passive pressure against the front face of the anchor and 3 side shear
on the sides of the anchor block The frictional sliding resistance between the
deadman and the supporting subgrade may be evaluated using a coefficient of sliding
friction of 0 30 Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p c f for the embedded portion of the deadman anchor
in level ground conditions The passive pressure should be reduced to 180 p c f for
sloping ground conditions two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Resistance
to lateral loads due to side shear along the sides of the anchor block may be estimated
using an adhesion value of 150 p s f Uplift loads may be resisted by the gravity load
of the deadman anchor and by side shear 150 p s f along the vertical faces of the
anchor block
These values assume that the concrete for the anchor is placed directly against
well compacted levee fills or the excavation in front of the anchor block is properly
backfilled and compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance of the anchor block in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be
neglected
Earthwork
We understand that site grading is not planned at the site As previously
indicated additional filling at the site is not recommended and should be avoided If
new fills are planned Geolabs Hawaii should be consulted for supplemental recom
mendations pertaining to the new fills and bridge foundations We anticipate that some
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3280 00 Page 16
earthwork such as backfill of the foundation excavation and abutment structure may
be required in the bridge reconstruction project
In general areas that are to receive fills or are subgrades for improvements
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grub spoil
material should be disposed of properly off site After clearing and grubbing all
subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
The on site materials may be used as backfill materials provided that they
contain no lumps or fragments greater than 3 inches in greatest dimension Cobbles
and boulders if encountered should not be used as backfill material Where used as
backfill material the on site soils should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Backfills behind abutment
walls should be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over
compaction of the abutment wall backfill would exert additional lateral earth pressures
on the walls and should be avoided
Any imported soil and structural fill should be select fill Import materials
should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the job site
Imported granular materials such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand may
be used The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3280 00 Page 17
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 15 or more with a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition the fill material should
contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve Imported soils
should be brought to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction All surfaces should be finished to create smooth
unyielding subgrades and should be kept moist until covered by concrete slabs or
pavements
Excavations
We envision that excavations of about 5 to 6 feet below the ground surface may
be required for the construction of the drilled pier cap In general the method and

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths ranging from about 16 to 58 feet below the existing ground
surface at the proposed project site The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring
Logs as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and on
Plate B 1 Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two sieve analyses were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification The test results
are summarized on Plate B 1 Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is
presented on Plate B 3
One direct shear test was performed on a selected sample to evaluate the shear
strength characteristics Results of the test are presented on Plate B 4
One unconfined compression test was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the material Results of the
unconfined compression test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depth
Corrosion tests such as pH and minimum resistivity were conducted on a soil
sample to evaluate the potential corrosivity of the soil The test results are summarized
on Plate B 1
