This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Replace POL Hydrant Fueling System Phase 3 project located within
Andersen Air Force Base in Guam Mariana Islands The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Vicinity Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Climate
The Island of Guam is located in the North Pacific Ocean between 144 degrees
36 minutes and 144 degrees 58 minutes longitude and between 13 degrees 13 minutes
and 13 degrees 40 minutes latitude as shown on the Project Location Vicinity Map
Plate1 In general the climate of Guam is warm and humid with little variation in
temperature from month to month Based on information obtained from the National
Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration NOAA Guam s mean annual temperature is
about 27 degrees Celsius throughout the year Typically annual rainfall averages from
about 2 000 to per year The rainy season is during the months of July through
November when monthly rainfall can average over 300 mm per month
Tropical storms and typhoons often pass through or near the Island of Guam
Tropical storms are defined as closed pressure systems with wind speeds ranging from
60 to 120 kilometers per hour Typhoons are similar phenomena with wind speeds
exceeding 120 kilometers per hour During the rainy season tropical storms and typhoons
are common in the vicinity of Guam Tropical storms and typhoons bring high intensity
rainfall when their zone of influence is near Guam In extreme events in the past a
typhoon has produced winds of over 280 kilometers per hour over 3 5 meters of
inundation and 650 mm of rainfall in one day
2 2 Regional Geology
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island
archipelago which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming the
Mariana Trench The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches which extend from
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench Mariana Trench Yap Trench
and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces
the southern mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The formation
W O INC Page 5
SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
of the island started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic rocks
and conglomerates tuffs and limestones of Eocene and Oligocene in age Lava flows
and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority of the southern
one third of the island The plateaus of the northern portion of the island were formed
while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level During the Oligocene and
Miocene Epochs a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an elongated platform
of limestones and related deposits Tectonic activity has lifted these coral algal limestones
above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau The project site is situated in a
relatively level portion of the northern plateau which forms the main portion of Andersen
Air Force Base
Weathering and solution working of the limestone has partially re crystallized the
calcium carbonate of the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and poorly
to well cemented detrital limestones in others Typically the detrital limestones are highly
variable in strength characteristics and are frequently karstic or cavernous in nature
In addition the limestone formations are generally porous Except during heavy storms
the majority of the precipitation naturally percolates through the formation producing little
or no surface water runoff
Typically the surface of the underlying limestone is irregular due to solution
working Frequently formations such as those encountered at the project site will have
buried pinnacles of massive limestone with spaces between the pinnacles filled with
weathered material and detrital material The surface soil horizon on the limestone
plateau is very thin generally less than 50 mm thick

impact the development of the proposed project An evaluation of geologic and seismic

As previously discussed Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that
forms the Mariana Trench The island is tectonically active and has experienced

The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration

3 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the underlying limestone formation or structural fill needed to raise the site to the
finished subgrade elevations would provide suitable foundation support for the proposed
fuel tanks and pump house Therefore we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread footings and or continuous footings may be used to support the fuel
tanks and other structures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the
design of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the limestone formation and or
compacted structural fill This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 500 mm below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Foundations located next to utility trenches should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench As an alternative to embedding the footings deeper the trench should be
extended deeper and backfilled with concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in limestone
formations we recommend that a program of probing and grouting at the proposed fuel
tanks be conducted during construction A qualified geotechnical engineer should observe
the probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered
Probing and grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing
section of this report

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present in the
limestone formations encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that
a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during construction for the
proposed fuel tanks considering the critical nature of these tanks
For estimating purposes we recommend that probeholes be drilled along the
perimeter ring foundation spaced at approximately 3 meters on center In addition we
believe that a series of probeholes with a spacing of about 3 meters on center should be
drilled starting at the center of the tank and radiating out to the perimeter in about 3 meter
increments in radius The probeholes should be at least 75 mm in diameter and should
extend to at least 3 meters below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities
and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional
probeholes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral
extent of the cavity and or void The probeholes and cavities discovered should be

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed pump
house building In general the subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a
depth of at least 200 mm moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
We consider the slabs on grade for the pump house building to be industrial type
slabs Therefore we recommend that the concrete slabs on grade be supported on a
minimum 150 mm cushion fill to provide uniform support under the slabs The cushion fill
layer should consist of aggregate subbase course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction For design of structural slabs bearing on the aggregate subbase
course a modulus of subgrade reaction of per square meter per
meter of deflection kN m may be used in the design

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation including lawn grass debris
rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of
properly off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed structure and fuel line
limits should be excavated where necessary to the design finished subgrades
Finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fill should be scarified to a
depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
for areas subjected to vibration and vehicular traffic such as the pump house slab
The excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of controlled fill
materials provided that the materials meet the gradation requirements presented in
the Fills and Backfills subsection of this report
Loose or soft spots encountered at the excavated subgrade levels should be
removed and replaced with controlled fill materials compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical
engineer be present to observe the finished subgrades prior to placement of fills
Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials to be used as controlled fill and structural fill up to the
foundation or slab on grade subgrade level should consist of well graded granular
materials less than 75 mm in largest dimension In addition controlled fill and
structural fill should contain not more than 30 percent particles less than
in dimension The excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of
controlled or structural fill or backfill materials provided that they are processed to

Imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles

intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Controlled and structural fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Excavation
The proposed project will involve the installation of approximately 6 400 lineal
meters of stainless steel pipes We understand that the average fuel line invert will
generally be located approximately 1 5 meters below the existing ground surface
Therefore we anticipate that excavations of up to about 3 meters in depth may be
necessary for installation of the new fuel lines
Based on our field exploration the Phase 3 project site is underlain by limestone
formation near the existing ground surface The limestone formation encountered
in our borings appeared to be severely fractured and ranged from soft to medium
hard Our boring information indicated that the limestone formation generally
breaks down into gravelly sands and sandy gravel It is anticipated that the majority
of the limestone formation may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment such as excavating with large backhoe excavators However
limestone formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose the existing fills consisting of sandy silts with
coralline gravel or limestone formations In general we believe that cut slopes may
be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter Permanent fill slopes constructed of controlled fill or structural fill should also
be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed
and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Water should be
diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished
slopes

3 7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised that gutter systems be installed around the building structures and that the
discharge be diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water
infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
23 borings designated as Boring Nos 201 through 223 extending to depths of
approximately 1 5 to 16 meters below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 23
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer
falling 760 mm In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled
borings in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using
the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
300 mm of a 450 mm drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the limestone formations encountered at the site were obtained
using core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if of
material is recovered from a core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 100 mm in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 750 mm of sound material is recovered from a core run
the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Eight sets of and minimum resistivity tests were
performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc on selected soil samples obtained from
our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the in situ soils to buried metallic
substructures In addition four sets of chloride contents and sulfate contents
were performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc to evaluate the
corrosivity of the in situ soils to concrete in contact with the soils The results of these
tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 2
Seven Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates B 3 and B 4
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test result Is presented on Plate B 5
One Modified Proctor Compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship The test result is presented on Plate B 6
