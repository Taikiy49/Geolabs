2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As the volcanic
activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded eastern
slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau
As the main shield building on the Island of Oahu drew to a close during the
Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the sea levels fluctuated fringing coral algal reefs began to grow on the
seaward margins of the island After long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations The series are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked the Schofield Plateau generating
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and
further south these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine sediments
coral algal reef formations and volcanic deposits of the Honolulu Volcanic Series to
form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero meters in the area of H 1
freeway to over 305 meters 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa
Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal
aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier
geologic time through the present

3 2 Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the subject project we believe that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed building
structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to 500 psf may be used for
the design of foundations bearing on compacted granular fill material The bearing
pressure provided above is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 600 mm
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Should footings need be
extended and bear on the underlying coral formation the footing bottom should be
relatively level and clear of sharp points that may result in concentrated loading
Footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls or other below grade
structures should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall
foundations or below grade structure Foundations located next to utility trenches or
easements should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the footings are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein the total settlement of footings is estimated to be
on the order of less than inch with differential settlement on the order of less
than 7 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings

3 3 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities commonly exist in the
coral formation In addition small voids were encountered in the coral formation in
three of the borings drilled To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting at the foundation footing locations be implemented during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
9 square meters 100 square feet of foundation area or at 3 meters 10 feet on center
along the wall foundations The probe holes should be at least inches in
diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 3 meters 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected
during the probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing
to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe
holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at
low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout
mixture with a slump range of 150 to to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting
operations As an alternative to sand cement grout Controlled Low Strength Material
CLSM may also be used for the grouting operations

No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 300 mm
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort on the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It
is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level inches above
the pipes to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select on site soils
or select fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum inch level
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finished grade
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Since coral formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it appears that the trench work for utility installation will likely involve
excavation in the coral formation The method and equipment to be used for excavation
should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations Generally the near surface coral formation may be excavated with normal
heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers However the use of
hoerams or chipping may be required in some areas Care must be exercised by the
contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation
resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity
3 10 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new missile assembly and test facility structures
should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for
ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that
the discharge be diverted away from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling sampling
and probing 16 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 16 extending to depths
between about 3 to 12 2 meters 10 and 40 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist wno monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 16
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
falling inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled
borings in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test
and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a inch OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third inches of an inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample The result of the pocket penetrometer test is
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Core samples of the volcanic tuff and coral formations encountered at the site
were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is
a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the volcanic tuff and coral
formations The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for
examination and classification

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 1 2 meters
4 feet of material is recovered from a 1 5 meter 5 foot core run the recovery would
be 80 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks If 0 8 meter 2 5 feet of sound
material is recovered from a 1 5 meter 5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent
and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is
used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of
Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Excellent

relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation
It should be noted that RQD is used for rock masses only and does not apply to

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation
of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three sets of resistivity ASTM G 57 and pH tests ASTM G 51 were performed
on selected samples to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soil The results of these
tests are presented on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits Test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Results of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 2
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The test results and the shear stress
versus normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Three California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of these CBR tests are presented on Plates B 6 through
B 8
Two unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the coral formation
encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B 9
