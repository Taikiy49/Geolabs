INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have performed a preliminary
injection well exploration and permeability testing for the above
referenced project The approximate location of the project site and its
vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Our work was
performed in general accordance with our revised proposal dated June 6
1988
This report summarizes our findings for the installation of injection
wells at the project site
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a shopping mall the Koloa Marketplace
at the project site This mall if constructed will consist of shops and
restaurants The geotechnical foundation exploration for this project
was performed previously by others
Sanitary domestic wastes will be generated by the shops
restaurants and restroom facilities in the proposed mall The current
estimated design flows for the entire complex are about 43 000 gallons per
day In accordance with Act 282 Session Laws of Hawaii 1985 treatment
must be provided on site for these wastes It is proposed to dispose of
the effluent from the treatment plant by injection

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this exploration and testing was to provide
geohydrologic data for the siting and installation of injection wells for
the disposal of treated effluent from the proposed wastewater treatment
plant which will serve the proposed shopping center
The data obtained from this exploration and testing are for use in
the preparation of the preliminary application submittal for an
Underground Injection Control UIC Permit as required by Chapter 23 of
Title 11 Department of Health Administrative Rules Underground
Injection Control
The scope of our work on the project included but was not limited
to
1 The drilling and sampling of three exploratory borings to depths
ranging from 77 0 to 1710 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
2 The performance of short term 2 to 4 hour duration
permeability tests in the drilled borings
3 Calculation of the average subsurface permeability
4 Research and report preparation

testing are presented in the appendices of this report

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the intersection of Maluhia and Koloa
Town Roads on the northern side of the town of Koloa on the island of
Kauai The site is roughly trapezoidal in plan view with sides
approximately 680 by 320 by 680 by 600 feet in length and is bounded by
Maluhia Road to the west Waihohonu Stream to the north vacant land to
the east and Koloa Town Road to the south The Koloa Post Office building
is situated to the southeast of the site
The topography of the site is generally level with ground surface
elevations ranging from about 205 to 209 feet above Mean Sea Level
Most of the southern portion of the site is open and is used as a
parking area This area is generally devoid of vegetation except for a
few large monkeypod trees The remainder of the site is vacant land and
is vegetated by tall grasses and trees
The UIC Line which forms the regulatory boundary between aquifers
designated as underground sources of drinking water and aquifers exempted
from being such exempted aquifers transects the site along a dirt road
running from beyond the eastern property line to Maluhia Road
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Kauai was built during the middle to late Pliocene by
lava flows from a single basaltic shield volcano The rocks of this main
shield building stage are known as the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series or
Waimea Basalts
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1984 00 Page 4
Dk No 52
After the end of the main shield building phase there was a long
period of erosion which was followed by a renewal of volcanic activity
This renewed activity deposited lava flows over the Waimea Basalts and
their weathering products These younger rocks are known as the Koloa
Volcanic Series or Koloa Basalts
Coeval with the Koloa Basalts and frequently underlying and or
intercalated with the Koloa lavas are an assemblage of breccias and
conglomerates which are believed to be mass transfer deposits resulting
from seismic activity generated at the start of the Koloa eruptions
These breccias and conglomerates are known as the Palikea Formation of the
Koloa Volcanic Series
The rocks in the vicinity of the project site have been mapped as
Koloa Basalts In general Koloa Basalts are poorly to moderately
permeable The associated Palikea Formation rocks are generally poorly
permeable
Substrata Conditions

Groundwater Conditions
In general the exploratory borings encountered groundwater at a
depth of approximately 15 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface
It is believed that this groundwater exists in a perched condition and
that the perching member may be Palikea rocks or underlying weathering
products of the Waiméa rocks

The groundwater is probably locally recharged by base flow from

PERMEABILITY TESTING
The subsurface permeability conditions at the site were tested by the
injection of clear fresh water from a nearby fire hydrant into the three
3 test borings
During the injection of water the changes in water level in the
borings were recorded with respect to time and rate of injection
Preliminary tests were performed during the drilling of the test
borings These tests were generally performed in the borings in an
undeveloped condition When the test borings reached their intended
depths the borings were developed by airlifting and surging with
compressed air Then additional tests were performed
The test results are shown in Appendix B Permeability Testing of
this report
FINDINGS AND CONCLUSIONS
General
Based on our exploration and preliminary permeability testing it
appears that the subsurface formations below the project site are capable
of accepting the injection of moderate flows of clear fresh water The
calculated average subsurface coefficient of permeability is on the order
of 7 although there appear to be localized horizontal and
vertical variations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1984 00 Page 6
Dk No 52
Therefore subsurface disposal of treated sewage effluent by
injection appears to be feasible
Injection Wells
In accordance with Act 282 Session Laws of Hawaii 1985 the disposal
component of an on site treatment and disposal system must provide a
capacity equalling six times the design flow plus one hundred percent
back up capability For the design flow of the subject project 43 000
gpd this means a disposal capability of about
Based on our exploration and testing we developed a model unconfined
aquifer with a total thickness of 80 feet and a saturated thickness of 65
feet Observations made during injection testing indicate that the radius
of the cone of impression of the test borings was about 30 feet
Utilizing these parameters and the formulae for recharge well hydraulics
we estimate that a 4 inch diameter well could accept about 60 gpm of
injection with 15 feet of head build up Increasing the recharge well
diameter to 6 inches would only yield about 9 percent increase in the
maximum injection rate
Therefore it appears that a 4 inch nominal diameter and approximate
depth of 80 feet below the existing ground surface could be used for the
production disposal wells Solid casing should be used to support the
drilled holes through the surface soil layers down to the underlying Koloa
Basalts This surface casing should be grouted into place in a minimum
6 inch diameter hole
Based on the observations made during the preliminary testing it
appears that the optimum well spacing should be sixty 60 feet on centers
to minimize interference between the cones of impression of the individual
wells However since the space available for the wellfield is limited a

spacing of thirty 30 feet on centers may be adequate provided that the
design flow per well is reduced to 30 gpm This leads to a need for
twelve 12 production disposal wells to meet the statutory requirements
of Act 282
Historical research indicates that common causes of injection well
failure are lack of maintenance and clogging by solids carried into the
wells including solids produced from other wells during redevelopment
The effluent disposal system and the operations and maintenance
manual for the on site sewerage system could include provision for
periodic redevelopment and maintenance of the disposal wells The
disposal system could also be designed to isolate the individual wells in
times of maintenance or during a plant upset The fluids produced during
redevelopment or during an upset could be routed to a large diameter 6 to
8 feet seepage pit constructed for such purposes
Effluent Quality
Based upon the cores recovered from the test borings it appears
that most of the transmissive capability of the subsurface rocks is from
fracture flow with some fluid movement through the rock fabric The
introduction of solid matter and or gas bubbles into such a media may
result in a reduction of injection capacity Therefore the injected
effluent should be as clean as possible It is preferable to inject
effluent with lower suspended solids and biological oxygen demand than
required under the state statutes i e less than 30 mg l
In the event of a treatment unit upset or bypass the effluent should
be routed to the seepage pit rather than to the wells to reduce solids
carryover

UIC Line
The UIC Line as previously discussed transects the project site and
is delineated by a dirt road running from the eastern property line to
Maluhia Road Section c of the UIC Rule states
In areas where the UIC Line is defined by a roadway a setback of
one lot or one hundred fifty feet whichever is less from the mauka
property line of that roadway may be considered to be within the
exempted area If the roadway is within a property the setback
shall extend to the mauka property line or to one hundred fifty feet
from the mauka edge of said roadway whichever is less This
interpretation of the UIC Line shall be subject to all other
conditions of this chapter The applicant on the permit
application shall request this interpretation approval of which
shall be based on the proximity and sensitivity of drinking water
sources
Since development of the site will essentially end the existence of
the dirt road the UIC permit application should request the referenced
interpretation so that there will be a documented history of the original
boundary in the event that there is ever a necessity to construct
injection wells in other portions of the site
Construction Observation
It is recommended that a geologist and engineer be retained to
provide geologic and engineering services during construction of the
injection wells This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event
that subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of
construction The recommendations given in this report are contingent
upon such observations

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were determined by taping from
reference points shown on the plans provided to us Elevations of the
data points were determined by estimation from the same plans The
physical location and elevation of the field data points should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross section are based upon interpolation between field data
points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points were collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
