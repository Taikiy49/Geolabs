PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The City and County of Honolulu Board of Waiter Supply has extensive watershed
properties on the Island of Oahu under its jurisdiction The watershed lands are designated
as State of Hawaii Forest Preserves and are utilized for the purpose of rainfall collection to
support underground water resources The watershed lands are generally located at the
interior mountainous regions of the Island of Oahu where rainfall is higher and land
development is restricted As a result the watershed lands generally exist as undeveloped
volcanic terrain in a naturally evolved geologic condition Due to encroaching development
adjacent to the watershed lands the potential for naturally occurring hazards related to the
geology and hydrology of the watersheds which may afiect neighboring development is often
a possibility especially because most land development exists downslope and downstream
of the watershed areas
The purpose of this watershed hazard assessment study is to provide the BWS with
general information pertaining to potential geologic hazards associated with some of the
watershed properties located on the Island of Oahu The primary objectives of this project
include the following

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our work on the project is to conduct a phased regional scale geologic
hazard mapping study for the various watersheds under consideration The mapping study
includes our assessment of the watershed lands in order to identify potential areas of earth
movement hazard such as debris flow landslide and rockfall activity In general it is
desired to evaluate the regional extent of the potential hazards by means of mapping to
identify the potential impact of these hazards on the existing BWS facilities and property
located downslope and downstream of the BWS watershed properties
Our work was performed in two phases of geologic evaluation and site
reconnaissance Future phases of work may include additional geotechnical exploration and
engineering analyses and design for possible remedial solutions to the geologic hazards
This report summarizes our findings and work efforts performed for the Phase 1 and
Phase 2 portions of this watershed hazard assessment study The work was performed in
general accordance with our fee proposal dated and the Scope of

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Hawaiian Archipelago consists of an elongate island chain comprising the eight
major islands of the State of Hawaii and hundreds of small islets and atolls extending for
more than 1 000 miles toward the northwest of the Hawaiian Island of Niihau The age of the
islands in the Hawaiian Archipelago increases progressing from the southeast toward the
northwest direction Of all the Hawaiian Islands the Island of Hawaii which is located
approximately 150 miles toward the southeast of the Island of Oahu is the youngest
Because of it s young age and active volcanism the Island of Hawaii is covered by relatively
less weathered volcanic rock and generally thinner soil profiles derived from rock
decomposition In addition the Island of Hawaii has a lower occurrence of deeply incised
erosional valleys On the contrary the Island of Kauai which is located approximately
90 miles toward the northwest of Oahu is one of the older Hawaiian Islands and contains
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 8
more deeply weathered rock and thicker residual soil deposits The Island of Kauai has a
high occurrence of deeply incised erosional canyons and well developed oceanic fringing
reefs The Island of Oahu bears more physical resemblance to the Island of Kauai than to
the Island of Hawaii due to the island s age
The Hawaiian Archipelago is believed to have formed as a result of plate tectonic
movement working in combination with a relatively deep stationary focal point of volcanic
heat and magma lava generation The Pacific Plate containing the upper earth s crust
underlying the central Pacific Ocean continues to slowly move in a northwesterly direction
while the nearly stationary hot spot of magma generation continues to produce eruptive
emissions beneath the plate As a result a continuous chain of volcanic islands has formed
that have slowly moved off in the northwesterly direction carried away from the active center
of volcanism by plate movement The Island of Hawaii is presently located adjacent to the
deep seated hot spot region The most distant volcanic island remnants located far to the
northwest of Hawaii have been reduced to submerged seamounts as a result of a long
history of land erosion and subsidence
Geology of the Island of Oahu
The Island of Oahu encompasses approximately 604 square miles of land area that
may be divided into four major geographic provinces as shown on the General Project
Location Map Plate 1 The geographic provinces include the following 1 Koolau Range
2 Waianae Range 3 Schofield Plateau and 4 Coastal Plain The majority of the island
of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two primary shield volcanoes known
as Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in geochronologic age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island
Waianae became extinct while Koolau remained active therefore the westward
flowing lava from the Koolau Volcano continued to bank against the weathered products of
the Waianae Volcano to create the elevated area known as the Schofield Plateau located
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 9
between the two volcanoes Therefore the Island of Oahu was formed by coalescence of
the two volcanoes The map of the Island of Oahu Plate 1 shows the relative relationship
between the geographic provinces mentioned
The regions identified as Zone Nos 1 and 2 of this study are situated within the
Koolau Mountain Range province and include BWS watersheds located on the windward and
leeward sides of the Koolau Mountain Range The region identified as Zone No 3 is situated
in the Waianae Mountain Range province on the leeward side of the Waianae Mountain
Range Therefore the three study zones are primarily underlain by volcanic deposits erupted
from either the Koolau Volcano or the Waianae Volcano depending upon location
Following the eruption of the bulk of the lavas the coastal areas of Oahu were partially
submerged and emerged cyclically due to large scale sea level fluctuations in response to
periods of worldwide glaciation In addition to the sea level fluctuations the island mass was
undergoing a gradual subsidence due to the island s mass and isostatic adjustment of the
earth s crust beneath the island As a result of the cyclic sea level fluctuations and the
gradual subsidence of the island the erosional baseline likewise fluctuated and major
streams were drowned and buried and later emerged to renew their incision The lowering
of base level resuits in an increased stream gradient and consequently an increased rate of
stream down cutting The effect of changes in erosional baseline impact the evolution of
landforms by influencing the rate of stream down cutting and sediment deposition in addition
to changing the coastal exposure to ocean wave erosion The combined effects are
evidenced by the evolution of steep walled amphitheater shape valleys filling of valleys to
form broad gently sloping alluvial floors and the initiation of renewed stream dissection into
the older alluvial deposits to form terraces at the bottom of the valley floors This cyclic
activity during the Pleistocene Age partially accounts for the present topography of today
including the Ewa Honolulu and Kahuku sedimentary coastal plains and the coastal
perimeter of the Island of Oahu
Today both the Waianae and Koolau Mountains have lost most of their original shield
volcano outline and exist as elongated mountain ranges that are evolving largely by erosional
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 10
processes The Koolau Range with its highest peak of about 3 105 feet above sea level
is approximately 37 miles in length and is deeply incised by active stream cutting and mass
wasting processes In contrast the Waianae Range with its highest peak of about 4 025 feet
above sea level at Mount Kaala is about 22 miles in length The Waianae Range has been
carved into large valleys separated with steep ridgelines by a long history of stream erosion
mass wasting and island wide submergence and re emergence
Geology of the Koolau Range
The Koolau Shield Volcano was built by Pliocene Age eruptions along the principal
northwest trending rift zone and two secondary rift zones A dike complex series of eruption
feeder conduits consisting of thousands of narrow dikes are embedded along the northwest
rift Zone and surround the vicinity of the large Koolau Caldera structure now only partially
exposed located in the vicinity of Kailua and Kaneohe The rocks composing the Koolau
Volcano as a whole are referred to as the Koolau Volcanic Series Stearns and Vaksvik
1938 and generally consist of tholeiitic and olivine basalts with associated dike complexes
and interbedded volcanic ash Large quantities of alluvium and colluvium soils and
weathered rock derived from erosion have been deposited in the deep valley structures of
the Koolau Range The deeply weathered older age alluvial and colluvial materials are often
found deposited high on the valley walls having been exposed by the rejuvenation of
erosional forces
The Koolau Pali steep fluted cliff line located on the windward side of the Koolau
Range is believed to have formed by a complex history of stream and ocean wave erosion
along with the deposition of thick alluvial deposits on the amphitheater shaped valley floors
The Koolau Pali is essentially a series of closely spaced amphitheater valley heads
separated by lower inter stream divides which are partially buried in thick alluvial deposits
The Koolau Pali may have initially formed as a result of the faulting and collapse of a large
portion of the actively building Koolau Summit The collapse of the summit formed a vast
caldera stretching from about Waimanalo toward Kaneohe along the central portion of the
Koolau Pali Subsequently the caldera was filled with lava which was rapidly altered and
decomposed by rising volcanic gas
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 11
Erosion of the caldera filling lavas by stream and ocean wave attack resulted in the
disintegration of the Koolau caldera structure and a southwestward directional retreat of the
evolving amphitheater valleys Erosion was slowed when the amphitheater valleys eventually
reached the outer southwestern flank of the old Koolau caldera which is composed of
relatively unaltered more resistant lava flows Other peripheral valleys located along the
same general alignment that had been eroding less rapidly eventually caught up with the
faster eroding valleys to form the valley headlands visible today In some places along the
Koolau Pali summit the large windward amphitheater valleys continue to erode back toward
the summit and occasionally pierce through the Pali to meet the eroded leeward Koolau
valley
Examples of such erosional features include the meeting of the Kalihi and Nuuanu
Valleys with the windward valley of Luluku The existing drainage patterns reflect the stream
capture and coalescence processes which work to form the steep parallel fluted slopes
containing local waterfall and plunge pool features Thus the Koolau Pali is believed to have
indirectly formed as a result of faulting and more importantly developed as a result of stream
erosion and headland erosion by ocean waves during periods of time when the sea level was
much higher than present
The parallel oriented southerly and southwesterly directional trending valleys that are
incised into the leeward southern side of the Koolau Mountain Range developed as a result
of stream erosion and changes in erosional base level related to island subsidence and sea
level fluctuation The valley walls generally show exposure of the slightly inclined lava flow
bedding planes The valley floors typically contain significant thickness of alluvial and
colluvial deposits which generally consist of clayey and sandy materials with weathered
boulders The deposits are locally incised by multiple tributary stream channels which drain
from the adjacent slopes The tributary streams coalesce into primary stream channels that
drain the valley floor toward the southerly direction
Approximately 30 secondary volcanic vents have erupted on Oahu since the major
Koolau valleys were dissected by erosion and partially filled with alluvium The younger
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 12
eruptions are identified as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Stearns and Vaksvik 1938 The
eruptions and resulting deposits occur mainly on the southern portion of the Koolau Shield
Volcano The eruptions are believed to have occurred during the middle Pleistocene to
Recent Age The vents are scattered near the ancient Koolau Shield s center of volcanic
activity and generally spread outward along the southwest rift zone of the Koolau Volcano
The late stage eruptions are generally represented by pyroclastic materials which include
cinder lava spatter ash tuff and also some lava flows Some of the volcanic deposits are
located within localized areas of the study watersheds of Haiku Kalihi Nuuanu Pauoa
Manoa and the Kaau Crater of upper Palolo Valley

Geology of the Waianae Range
The Waianae Shield Volcano began to erupt in the Tertiary Age prior to the eruption
of the Koolau Volcano making it the older shield volcano of the two The Waianae Range
is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and some pyroclastic materials which are
grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series Stearns and Vaksvik 1938 The
Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the Lower Middle and Upper Volcanic Members
The Lower Member comprises thinly bedded pahoehoe lava flows and associated pyroclastic
rocks that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The Middle Member is
separated from the Lower Member by an angular unconformity and talus breccia and by a
significant erosional unconformity in a few places The Middle Member consists of at least
2 000 feet of rock including a large volume of a a lava that accumulated and gradually filled
the vast volcanic caldera The Upper Member is approximately 2 300 feet in thickness and
consists of mainly a a lava flows capping the entire top of the shield volcano late in the history
of the Waianae Range volcanic evolution
A large caldera complex situated near Kolekole Pass located at the head of Lualualei
Valley is believed to have been the center of volcanic activity for the Waianae Range In
addition hundreds of dikes dike complexes are visibly exposed at the heads of the valleys
and in the western ridgelines forming the valley walls Most are oriented vertically which is

lava flows Like the Koolau Range lavas the Waianae Range lavas contain generally few

Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred which produced the large
valleys located on the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were
gradually filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium Qac deposited as
a combined result of stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the
island mass Over the passage of time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the
older alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and consolidated to form mildly indurated
deposits
Today many of the older alluvial and colluvial deposits are being uncovered by
renewed stream incision and are exposed as sedimentary conglomerate consisting of a mix
of weathered rock fragments and soil material Typically some younger surface deposits

deposits These unconsolidated and consolidated deposits Qac Qls and Ra are found

Erosion and Island Landform Evolution
The present day geographic form of the Island of Oahu is largely a result of slow
on going erosional processes that work to denude the existing landscape No new volcanic
materials are currently being erupted on the Island of Oahu However in terms of relative
geologic time the Island of Oahu could possibly experience another episode of volcanism
similar to the recent eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series which occurred about
70 000 years ago Hence the forces of erosion are now the predominant processes working
to alter the existing landscape
The natural processes of erosion are closely related to the development and activation
of the geologic hazards discussed in this study The geologic hazards discussed in this
report pertain to various types of earth movements such as hillslope failures that include
rockfall slow landslide soil creep debris flow and stormwater runoff resulting from fiash
flooding The earth movements may be viewed as potential geologic hazards because of the
latent or possible effects they may incur on human activities such as land development and
usage
Ground surface erosion may be defined as the process of land denudation by the
wearing away of soil and rock by both physical and chemical processes The processes may
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 16
include the weathering of rock to form soils the mass wasting of blocks of earth materials
and the erosive activity of streams waves wind and underground water on the soil and rock
materials The naturally occurring processes of landform evolution are usually responsible
for the formation of geologic hazards In Hawaii as well as other tropical regions the
processes of mass wasting combined with stream and ocean wave erosion are the most
important forces acting to continually erode and stabilize landforms
Mass wasting may be defined as the downslope movement of a mass of rock and or
soil debris under the influence of gravity by means of events such as rockfall landslide
mudflow soil avalanche and soil creep The driving force behind mass wasting is the force
of gravity Many of the geologic hazards that are experienced in Hawaii are related to earth
movement events
The process of mass wasting and the resultant landform evolution differs somewhat
between the drier Waianae leeward Oahu region and the wetter Koolau windward Oahu
region The differences are due in part to the variation in the amount of rainfall received and
the resultant type of runoff experienced from the predominant rainfall patterns The general
landscape appearance and character of stream flow in the leeward Waianae Range and the
Koolau Range show evidence of the difference in erosional processes occurring between the
arid and wetter regions of the Island of Oahu
Climate
This watershed assessment is a study of the potential geologic hazards associated
with earth movements that are usually related to the occurrence of higher rainfall and
stormwater runoff conditions As a result a brief discussion of some climate characteristics
of the Island of Oahu is provided to facilitate the discussion of the potential geologic and
hydrologic hazards that generally become more active during periods of higher rainfall
associated with storm weather conditions
The Island of Oahu has a warm humid tropical climate with distinct dry and wet
seasonal variations in rainfall For much of the Island of Oahu and particularly the lower
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 17
elevations the drier season generally extends through the summer months Most of the
rainfall occurs during the winter months that extend from November through March The
summer months are typified by generally lighter rainfall with occasional tropical storm
systems passing to the south of the Hawaiian Islands At these times the higher elevations
Koolau Summit continue to receive significant orographic rainfall with local accumulation
averages that exceed about 100 inches per year of rainfall The winter months are typified
by greater rainfall induced by an active storm track located to the north and west of the
Hawaiian Islands over the Pacific Ocean extra tropical storm systems
Heavier and prolonged rainfall may be expected with the slow passage of tropical
storms extra tropical low pressure systems and upper level atmospheric low pressure
systems Surface level extra tropical low pressure systems that move in close proximity to
the Hawaiian Islands are locally referred to as Kona type storm systems These weather
systems have the potential to enhance normal rainfall volume shift the direction of moisture
bearing winds and cause flooding conditions to occur As a result the effects of geologic
and hydrologic hazards may become more pronounced and widespread during and following
the slow passage of such storm systems
Based on available National Weather Service NWS rainfall data the leeward side
of the Waianae Range including the Makaha and Waianae Valley project areas generally
experience an arid environment typified by relatively infrequent and overall lower rainfall
quantities The average annual rainfall for the Makaha and Waianae project areas leeward
Waianae Range ranges from about 20 inches at the coast to about 100 inches at Mount
Kaala The rainfall normally occurs as infrequent light passing showers carried by the
prevailing tradewinds and as infrequent afternoon showers resulting from daytime land
heating and cloud convection Often periods of several days even weeks may pass without
measurable rainfall occurring especially at the lower elevations located closer to the
coastline Because of the drier climatic conditions the mountains have evolved with
generally thinner soil development and a greater occurrence of exposed rocky slopes with
larger accumulations of bouldery talus

The arid environment of leeward Oahu is created because the prevailing northeasterly
tradewind carried rainfall is wrung out by the moisture bearing air mass passing up and over
the high elevation obstructions orographic lifting of the up wind located Koolau Mountain
Range and the windward slopes of the Waianae Range Therefore generally less moisture
is available for precipitation on the leeward slopes of the Waianae Range because of the air
mass drying effect as it descends and warms from the mountain summits Occasionally
during Kona type storm events mainly in the winter months the prevailing winds switch
direction and blow from southerly directions When this wind pattern develops the moisture
bearing winds reverse direction from the prevailing northeasterly wind to allow the Waianae
Range to temporarily become the wetter windward facing slopes During these storm
events rainfall usually becomes more frequent and often heavily concentrated on the
normally dry leeward Waianae slopes
During the Kona type weather pattern the normally dry ground condition of the
leeward Waianae Range may become temporarily very wet and prone to flash flooding
Because the areas of Makaha and Waianae Valleys normally do not experience prolonged
rainfall or frequent heavy downpours it allows for the gradual accumulation of loose soil rock
materials and vegetation debris on the mountain slopes The materials which had been
deposited under relatively stable and dry ground conditions suddenly become subjected to
rapid erosion and transport by the abnormally high stormwater runoff As a result the
mountain slopes of the Makaha and Waianae Valleys are prone to an increased potential for
geologic hazards related to rockfall slope failure and debris flow during and following high
rainfall
Based on available NWS rainfall data there are contrasts in climate between the
Koolau Mountain Range and the Waianae Range in terms of rainfall frequency and overall
quantity Thus the erosional processes physical and chemical weathering operate
somewhat differently and the ground terrain may exist under different conditions of inherent
stability In general the windward Koolau Mountain receives frequent and abundant rainfall
in the form of prevailing tradewind induced light to moderate intensity showers Rainfall
occurs almost on a daily basis though it occurs more predominantly overnight or in the early
GEOLABS INC

morning hours when air temperatures have lowered Some typical annual rainfall totals for
the windward Koolau Range vary from about 40 inches to over 300 inches depending on
location and elevation Because of the frequent windward rainfall and prevalent runoff the
landforms and streams have adapted to attain equilibrium with the wetter climatic conditions
Orographic lifting where the tradewind air mass abrupily rises up the face of the
Koolau Pali to condense and form clouds is responsible for the frequent rainfall In addition

Summary of Geologic Hazards
In general the geologic hazard identified as slope failure and debris flow is known to
occur where steep hillslopes composed of soil and weathered rock are exposed to high
rainfall and rapid drainage This type of geologic hazard is a primary form of mass wasting
that operates on a range of scale from relatively benign to catastrophic in size Because
slope failure and debris flow is a natural process of erosion dependent upon many factors
it is difficult to predict the location and magnitude of this type of slope failure
For this study we have based our geologic hazard assessment on the occurrence of
small scale events since they are most commonly experienced and have a shorter
recurrence interval Storms of the 100 year and 500 year recurrence interval would almost
certainly be of sufficient magnitude to trigger large scale widespread and large size slope
failure and debris flow events that would realize the anticipated risks identified for this study
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 22
Detailed studies have been performed by other agencies for a range of environmental
settings in an effort to evaluate the rainfall intensity threshold necessary to initiate slope
failure and debris flow It appears that rainfall intensity threshold information may be a useful
tool to predict when the potential hazard for slope failure and debris flow is greatest In
general the transmission of debris flow materials may be a higher risk to property and human
activity located in and adjacent to established drainageways where flood control measures
may be inadequate The hazard is also a higher risk to persons or property located near the
base of steep hillslopes To illustrate the mechanism and process of slope failure and debris
flow the following discussion is provided using the Koolau Mountain geologic conditions as
an example
The slopes of the Koolau Pali are incised by multiple closely spaced drainage ravines
that resemble chutes headed by hanging valleys where stream capture processes originate
Each ravine has the potential to collect stormwater runoff and debris flow material for
transmission to the base of the steep mountain slopes Some of the main fluted ravines
intersect smaller ravines to intercept the discharge and grow in size By the capture of
discharge from adjacent ravines the stormwater runoff containing eroded earth materials
may be concentrated into larger flows that have the potential to move further downslope as
a swiftly moving mass of debris and runoff
Small scale slope failures such as soil avalanches that are also capable of triggering
debris flows are a common occurrence on the very steep mountain faces of the Koolau Pali
especially near the summit regions Small scale soil avalanches are also common on the
lower elevation hillslopes composing the bounding ridgelines of the valley basin The slope
failures occur most frequently during and following intense or prolonged rainfall when the
likelihood for ground saturation is highest Most soil avalanche slope failures and the
resulting debris flows are relatively small in size ranging from several cubic yards to about
1 000 cubic yards of earth material The average size of soil avalanche type slope failures
typically involve about 150 cubic yards of earth material Ellen et al 1993

In general the geologic hazard identified as rockfall is known to occur where
moderate to very steep hillslopes are composed of fractured rock or loose rock materials
Rockfall is another form of mass wasting by means of the dislodgment of rock material from
a slope face and the free fall or rolling of the material downslope Rockfall is difficult to
predict because the process occurs under both wet or dry conditions with many other
contributing factors involved in rockfall initiation In general rockfall inundation is a higher
risk to property and human activity located at the base of rock slopes especially at the base
of fluted ravines plunge pool zones and deep narrow gulches A higher risk for rockfall
inundation can also be expected below steep slopes composed of layered rock exposed as
ledges and terraces The layered ledges and terraces are susceptible to fractured rock

rockfall or launch rock material to fall greater distances
The initiation of rockfall may be caused by one or more of the following conditions
such as ground vibration physical impact excessively wet or dry ground conditions or the
prying effect of tree roots and wind Once a boulder or boulders separate from the slope and
fall the tumbling action usually causes additional rock to come loose by the force of impact
and abrasion In addition debris such as topsoil tree limbs and roots may be entrained by
the cascading mass to add to the volume of material invoived

Hazard and Risk Definition
For the purpose of discussion in this report the definition of hazard and risk is
explained The potential hazard refers to the menace or threat imposed by the potential
occurrence of a geologic process or activity For instance a high potential hazard indicates
that the hazard is significant and awareness of the hazard should be heightened such that
future use of the site should strongly consider the potential impacts of the hazard on the
planned activity A moderate hazard potential indicates that the hazard is noteworthy and
awareness of the potential hazard should be heightened A low hazard potential indicates
that the hazard is relatively inconsequential due to the lower possibility of experiencing the
potential hazardous condition
The risk refers to the chance or probability that damage economic loss injury or
death will occur as a result of the occurrence of the hazard For instance a high risk
indicates that the probability of property damage or injury is relatively high and some program
of user occupant awareness or remedial measure should be considered for implementation

A moderate risk indicates that probability for property damage or injury is moderate and a
user occupant awareness program may be considered if appropriate A low risk indicates
that the probability for property damage or injury is low and should be of little consequence
to users occupants

widespread throughout Palolo Valley due to the pervasive occurrence of the
expansive soils covering sloping terrain
Landslides involving the detachment and movement of a soil mass along a boundary
plane are known to occur on the lower hillslopes of the valleys in the District of
Honolulu where vertisol soils cover sloping terrain The driving force of many slow

groundwater in the subsurface soils The landslides typically affect irregularly
shaped blocks of land that detach along a basal slip plane and move downslope at
relatively slow rates The rate of movement often increases during and following
periods of wet weather when subsurface water pressures increase as a result of
surface water infiltration

The potential hazard associated with stormwater runoff and debris flow within the

potentially unstable ground surface conditions
e the potential for accumulation of loose soil rocks and debris on the
moderately to steeply inclined slopes during drier conditions
the presence of unconsolidated soils and boulder deposits overlying
consolidated less permeable deposits on the lower mountain flanks
e the presence of very steep high and exposed rock slopes that may
produce concentrated runoff during storms
The relatively high level of risk to life and property from stormwater runoff and
associated debris flow is attributed to the extensive area of steep mountain slopes
and high volume of runoff that may occur during large storm events from these
slopes In addition some residential development and BWS facilities exist in close
downstream proximity to streams and drainage channels that emanate from the
Makaha watershed Furthermore there are documented cases of flood inundation
and debris flow damage to existing structures and land within and bordering the BWS
parcels comprising the Makaha watershed
Because of the existing geologic condition type of deposits and the arid climate of
the southwestern portion of Makaha Valley the very steep rock slopes and
moderately inclined talus aprons are susceptible to high flows of stormwater runoff
that could generate concentrated runoff and debris flows during high rainfall events
The high volume of runoff from the rocky slopes during large storms works to flush
the ravines and stream channels of debris that may have accumulated during periods
of normal to light rainfall and infrequent runoff The normally drier slopes when
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 90
saturated by prolonged rainfall tend to produce greater runoff as a result of the
reduced infiltration capacity of the saturated ground Furthermore the narrow fluted
rock ravines channelize the increased runoff into intense streams of runoff Waterfalls
may develop over the scarp and cliff sections of the mountain face which adds to the
erosive power velocity of the flowing water As the runoff reaches the base of the
steep mountain face the normally dry stream channels and gullies located below
each of the fluted ravines become active with turbulent flows of stormwater runoff
The stormwater runoff conditions are more hazardous due to the occurrence of
ground surface deposits that consist of unconsolidated soil and boulders located at
the base of the steep mountain face cliffs The unconsolidated materials generally
overlie more consolidated less permeable colluvial deposits buried in the
subsurface The rapidly flowing sediment and debris laden runoff has the potential
to scour and widen the existing ditches and channels to incorporate additional debris
into the runoff As the runoff flows down the moderately inclined slopes composed
of unconsolidated alluvial colluvial materials the entrained mud debris and large
boulders gains momentum and additional scouring power At this point the flowing
mass is called a debris flow Because of the velocity and mass of material the
flowing debris flow is very hazardous to anything located in the downstream path
The debris flow may continue moving downstream until the slope gradient becomes
gradual enough to dissipate the energy and allow deposition Debris flows may also
lose momentum upon entering a containment basin or other substantial barrier

LIMITATIONS OF THIS STUDY
This regional geologic hazard evaluation addresses thirteen 13 BWS watershed
areas comprised of several thousand acres of undeveloped land located in various regions
of the Island of Oahu Within the thirteen watershed project areas there were 71 separate
land parcels under the jurisdiction of the BWS that were evaluated for the presence of
potential geologic hazards For each of the 71 land parcels we conducted a qualitative
evaluation and assessment for the anticipated occurrence of potential geologic hazards In
addition the anticipated relative levels of risk to life and property were assessed for each of
the potential hazards identified
This geologic hazard assessment study was conducted on a regional scale to provide
the BWS with widespread and general information that may be used to aid in identifying the
presence of potential geologic hazards and the level of hazard and risk associated with the
hazards The results of this study could be used as an aid in identifying the BWS properties
that may pose an unacceptable level of hazard or risk to existing BWS facilities and other
property located adjacent to the potentially hazardous land areas The results of this study
should be used as a basis for additional investigation and detailed mapping definition of the
hazards on a scale compatible with the degree of potential hazard risk and the size of the
property
The evaluation and resulting assessments performed for this study are based on a
review of available geologic and hydrologic information including our analyses of aerial
photographs topographic maps technical reports and some field reconnaissance of
accessible locations The interpretations presented in this report and on the accompanying
set of maps are professional opinions based on our research of available information and
experience with the evaluation of geologic hazards in similar conditions No warranty is
expressed or implied
Because of the regional scale of this study and the methods of remote investigation
of land area employed for our evaluation a wide range of geologic and hydrologic conditions
beyond those indicated in this report should be expected to exist Some variable and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4067 00 Page 107
potentially unanticipated conditions that may effect the occurrence of geologic hazards and
the risks involved might include the following
variation of geologic materials and the physical properties of soil and rock
drainage system alteration or evolution over time
obscured geologic conditions responsible for the development of ground
instability
e occurrence of geologic hazard triggering events such as large storms seismic
activity and changing groundwater conditions
The generalized findings presented in this study may not address all possible geologic
hazards or risks involved with the potential hazards at the various BWS properties For
instance the large BWS parcels of several hundred acres in size are difficult to assess other
than in general terms due to the very wide range of site conditions slope inclinations and
soil rock types that exist within the parcel For these large forested properties localized
terrain may vary widely with respect to the level of potential hazard and level of risk involved
Where appropriate and within the overall context of the study objective attempts were made
to describe the occurrence of potentially hazardous conditions some of the contributing
factors and the general regions that pose greater hazard and risk A more detailed and
site specific investigation would be necessary to better define the extent and possible effects
of specific geologic hazards Such an intensive site evaluation is beyond the scope of this
regional watershed hazard assessment study and is better suited for a later phase of
investigation that would build on and refine the current findings
Other differing site conditions that were not detected in the course of this regional
scale study may not become apparent until the geologic hazard may manifest as observed
ground disturbance or property damage Therefore as a proactive measure we suggest that
additional geologic hazard investigation be directed toward the BWS properties that are
assessed as having a high potential hazard with an anticipated moderate to high risk to life
and property Additionally geologic hazard investigation for remedial measures should be

of hazardous conditions that occur within BWS property
