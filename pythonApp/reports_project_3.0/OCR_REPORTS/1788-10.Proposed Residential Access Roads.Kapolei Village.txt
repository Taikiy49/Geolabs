engineering exploration performed along the proposed Kapolei Village
residential access road alignments in The general

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this geotechnical engineering exploration was to
examine the shallow subsurface soil conditions along the proposed
residential access road alignments in order to provide soil data for the
design of the site grading utility excavation and roadway pavements
The scope of our work included
1 review of soils and geologic information pertinent to the project
site and its vicinity
2 drilling and sampling of seven 7 borings to a depth of 16 5 feet
below the existing ground surface
3 logging of the borings and reconnaissance of the project site and its
vicinity by a field engineer from our firm
4 laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field
exploration to determine the pertinent engineering properties and
classification of the materials encountered
5 engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data and
6 preparation of this report presenting our findings and
recommendations
PROJECT CONSTDERATTIONS
It is currently proposed to develop the 889 5 acre Kapolei Village
site into a multi use development including residential commercial
recreational and school areas Access roads are planned to provide access
to the proposed developments from Farrington Highway and Fort Barrette
Road In this study our services have been limited to the proposed
access road aspects of the development
W O 1788 10 Page 3
At the time of our exploration the project was in the planning
stages and a preliminary grading plan was not available However based
on current drawings showing the access road alignments and the existing
gently sloping topography at the site relatively small cuts and fills are
anticipated Drainage structures are anticipated where the proposed
roadways cross natural drainageways
SITE DESCRIPTION
The site is situated between Makakilo Community and Barbers Point
Naval Air Station in the district of It is bounded on
the north by Farrington Highway west by Fort Barrette Road Barbers Point
cane fields
Topographically the site slopes down gently toward the south
Ground elevations range from approximately 110 feet above Mean Sea Level
MSL near Farrington Highway to 50 feet along the railroad right of way
Most of the site is covered with mature sugar cane with the exception
of the northeastern portion which has been recently harvested and
replanted The site is traversed by several cane haul roads unmaintained
dirt roads drainage ditches and irrigation trenches
The Makakilo and Makalapa Gulches cut through the site and carry
storm runoff from the mauka areas Plate 2 The gulches merge at the
southwestern corner of the site and the storm water is led to a coral pit
located inside the Barbers Point Naval Air Station via a box culvert The
gulches are usually dry and carry water only during rainstorms

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by two basaltic shield volcanoes
Waianae and Koolau Waianae is the older of the two and reached the end
of its activity before Koolau
As volcanic activity on the island of Oahu drew to a close the
island began to subside slowly While the island was sinking fringing
coral algal reefs began to grow on the seaward margins of the island
During the Pleistocence or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell
in response to the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew the
global sea level fell below the existing level and as the glaciers melted
and retreated the sea level rose to heights above the current stand of
the sea
Locally the fringing coral algal reefs continued to grow during the
high stands of the sea leaving fossil reefs which are now exposed above
sea level At the project site and in its vicinity there are exposures
of such fossil reefs which most likely grew during the Kaena 95 feet
stand of the sea Overlying and interbedded with these coral algal
materials are alluvial sediments which were transported from the nearby
Waianae Range
The eroded late stage Waianae cones Puu Makakilo and Puu Kapolei
lie immediately to the north and northwest of the site Basalt rock
formation may occur at relatively shallow depths near the cone perimeters

Subsurface Conditions
Based on the geologic reconnaissance and field exploration the
subsurface conditions at the project site may be generalized into four
distinct groups as follows
Fill
A surface layer of fill was encountered at various locations
throughout the site and primarily consists of very stiff to hard
brown to reddish brown clayey silt and silty clay with medium dense
tan silty sand occurring locally This fill was probably derived
from nearby alluvial and coralline deposits and was placed during the
early development of the site for the purpose of sugar cane
cultivation
Alluvial deposits were encountered throughout most of the site at the
surface as well as interbedded with and underlying the fill The
alluvium sampled primarily consists of stiff to hard brown silty
clay to clay and clayey silt with some sand and gravel interbeds
In general most of the stiff to hard silty clays and clays may be
classified as having moderate to high swelling expansion potential
when wetted
Coral Aigal Formation
Medium dense to dense coralline sand and gravel was encountered in
the central and southern portions of the site at elevations ranging
between 42 9 to 57 0 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL Near surface
coralline sediments were encountered in Boring No 7 at a depth of 1
foot below the existing ground surface and were observed at depths of
1 to 2 feet in existing pits in the southeastern portion of the
site Therefore it appears that coralline deposits may occur near
the surface less than 2 feet in the southern and southeastern
portions of the site
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1788 10 Page 6
Cavities were observed in coral exposures at the southwestern end of
the site and also were encountered in borings by previous
investigations drilled in West Beach and Barbers Point Naval Air
Station areas
Hard probably jointed basalt may occur at shallow depths in
localized areas within the site fringing Puu Kapolei and along
Farrington Highway
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by stiff
to hard clayey silts silty clays and medium dense to dense hard coralline
deposits Basalt rock formation may be present at shallow depths along
the northern and northwestern boundaries of the site
In general relatively easy excavation is anticipated for utility
trenches planned in areas underlain by stiff to hard alluvial deposits and
medium dense to dense coralline sediments However due to the
variability of coralline deposits localized areas of hard reefrock should
be anticipated Hard excavation is also anticipated for the deeper
portions of the trenches along the northern and northwestern boundaries
where basalt rock formation may be encountered
The near surface stiff to hard alluvial soils and fills generally
have moderate to high swelling expansion potential and therefore
relatively thick pavement sections are recommended
Based on our limited preliminary exploration it is difficult to
delineate the moderate expansion potential soils and the high expansion
potential soils Therefore it is our opinion that additional exploration
should be performed at the site following the harvesting of sugar cane to
delineate areas of highly expansive clays

Site Grading
At the time of our exploration a preliminary grading plan was not
available However based on the proposed access road alignments and the
existing gently sloping topography at the site relatively small cuts and
fills are anticipated
The upper silty clay and clay soils generally exhibit moderate to
high swelling potential when excavated and recompacted as fill
Therefore should this material be required for use as fill it should be
used in the deeper portions of the fill a minimum of 3 feet below the
finished grade
The excavated coralline deposits less than 8 inches in size may be
used as general fill material To facilitate construction of utilities
the upper 3 feet of fill should be limited to material smaller than 6
inches
The following recommendations present general grading guidelines for
the site
1 Areas to be graded should be thoroughly cleared of vegetation
boulders debris and other deleterious materials Topsoil should
be removed and stockpiled for later use and to avoid
contamination of fill material
2 In areas to receive fill the ground should be scarified to a
depth of six 6 inches brought to near optimum moisture content
and recompacted to 90 of its maximum dry density as established
by ASTM D 1557 78 If soft areas are encountered they should be
over excavated and stabilized by backfilling with select material
placed in 10 inch maximum loose lifts compacted to 90 maximum
dry density

Fill material should be placed in 10 inch maximum loose
horizontal lifts and compacted to 90 of its maximum dry density
established by ASTM D 1557 78
Should additional off site borrow be required it should he
examined by the geotechnical engineer for its suitability as
fill
Imported select material for any non expansive capping layer
required should be low expansion granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock or cinder The material should be less

exceeding 1 and CBR value of 20 or more It should not contain
large percentages of boulders organics or other deleterious
material should contain sufficient fines so that there is a
minimum of 20 passing the No 200 sieve and should be capable
of forming a competent stable mass when compacted
Where the existing ground is steeper than 5 horizontal to 1
vertical keying and benching are required to properly bond the
new fill to the slope The filling operations should start at
the lowest point and continue up in level compacted layers as
recommended above For construction details refer to Plate 3
Cut and fill slope ratios of 2 horizontal to 1 vertical are
recommended for the site Fill slopes should be constructed by
over filling past the design slope and then cutting back
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope
planting utilized to reduce erosion
Generally slope drainage benches should be provided for slopes
having a vertical height greater than 20 feet

Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The underlying stiff to hard alluvium and medium dense to dense
coralline sediments have good bearing capacity Therefore minimal
settlements are anticipated for structures supported on these layers
The utility line construction in general will not materially
increase the vertical loading upon these underlying soils if no
additional fills are placed upon the existing ground surface The
net difference in loading between the weights of the excavated
material and backfill will be essentially small Therefore
significant settlements are not anticipated if proper construction
techniques particularly for shoring are utilized
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1788 10 Page 10
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great
effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the
pipe bedding is an important design consideration
For pipes bearing on the stiff to hard alluvial soils and dense
coralline sediments at the invert level a minimum of six inches of
compacted granular bedding material should be placed below the pipe
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum
width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend half
way up the pipe sides The bedding material should consist of clean
granular material such as 3B Fine gravel
Trench Excavation
It is our opinion that conventional excavation methods can be
utilized for the proposed trench excavations However due to the
variability of coralline deposits local harder coral reefrock layers
should be anticipated and may require special excavating techniques
such as hoe ram excavation Hard excavation should also he
anticipated along the northern and northwestern boundaries of the
site where basalt rock formation may be encountered
The excavation should be provided with proper bracing in accordance
with the anticipated loading conditions traffic and construction
materials stockpile and equipment and to satisfy the applicable
safety requirements The design and safety of the trench supports
should be the responsibility of the contractor
The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should be
continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of
movements and possible sloughing of the trench walls Such
monitoring is particularly critical in areas where existing
structures and utilities are adjacent to the trench excavation

Backfill
ce a re
In general the trench backfill should be in accordance with Section
11 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction
September 1986 City and County of Honolulu
Granular material such as 3B Fine gravel or sand should be used
for the backfill around the bottom half of the pipe to reduce the
compactive effort required and possible damage to the pipe and to
provide bearing support for the pipe
Except for the boulders and expansive clay most of the excavated
on site soil can be re used in the trench backfill up to within 2
feet of the finished grade provided adequate compaction is achieved
Backfill should be compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry
density established by ASIM D 1557 78 Where the pipes cross the
roadway areas the backfill within 2 feet of finished grade should
consist of select borrow and be compacted to 90 of its maximum dry
density
The trenches should be kept free of water during backfilling

Drainage Culvert Structures
It is anticipated that box or pipe culvert structures of varying
diameters will be required below the proposed road alignments at the
drainageway crossings
General guidelines for the construction of the culvert structures and
fill placement are presented below
Excavation
In order to provide sufficient compaction around the culvert and to
minimize possible damage to the culvert from the compaction
operations and potential fill settlement the general embankment
should first be placed in the drainageway prior to construction of
the culvert Upon completion of the filling a trench should then be
excavated through the compacted fill to the culvert invert and the
pipe placed in the trench
Trench Backfill
Selected well draining granular material such as 3B Fine gravel or
sand which could be compacted by water tamping may be used as
backfill to the top of the culvert Above this level general
embankment fill may be used in accordance with our previous
recommendations for fill

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
upon information obtained from the borings Variations of conditions
between the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until construction If variations then
appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations
given in this report
The locations of the borings were determined by taping from
structures indicated on the topographic survey Elevations of the borings
were approximately determined by interpolation from elevation points on
the topographic survey map The physical location and elevation of the
borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the
method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
boring points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and
as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between borings
and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the boring points were collected at the times
given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this report
These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation
of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due
to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

V Placement and Compaction of Fill
A
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers which when
compacted will not exceed ten inches 10 in thickness Each
layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain
uniformity of material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer
which has not been properly compacted Subsequent removal of
any unacceptable layer shall be done at the Contractor s
expense
Rock fragments or boulders less than eight inches 8 in
diameter may be utilized provided they are not concentrated in
any one area and that there is sufficient fine grained material
to fill the voids between the rocks
Rocks greater than eight inches 8 in diameter shall be
removed from the fill unless specifically approved for placement
in the fill by the Geotechnical Engineer
All fill shall be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the
maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
unless otherwise specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s
Report
The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or
pneumatic tire rollers suited to the soil type and capable of
producing the specified results in an expedient and efficient
manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
