INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the FY96 MCON Project P 386 Proposed Elevator and Stair Tower
project located on the northwest side of the Joint Intelligence Command Pacific
JICPAC Building 352 in the Makalapa area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation and site grading
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that it is proposed to construct a new elevator and stair tower for
the existing JICPAC Building 352 Based on the information provided the proposed
elevator and stair tower will be a 5 story structure with plan dimensions of about 16 by
40 feet We understand that the proposed tower will be constructed of reinforced
concrete with precast concrete panels on the exterior to match the existing texture of
Building 352 In addition the proposed construction also includes installation of a fire
protection system and new air conditioning system and relocation of water sewer and
drainage lines

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with
a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Many of the eruptions were rather
explosive in nature due to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma
resulting in steam explosions which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material
predominantly ash and cinder Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented
together rapidly to form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as mudrock
The proposed project site is located on the coastal plain of southern Oahu The
coastal plain was built on the eroded flanks of Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium
derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated
deposits During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response
to the cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew
and advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels
fell below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an
excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present
sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3438 00 Page 6
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by a surficial fill
crust overlying soft and compressible alluvial soils and lagoonal deposits with
weathered tuff mudrock located at a depth of approximately 36 feet below the existing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3438 00 Page 8
ground surface We understand that the existing JICPAC Building 352 was founded
on a driven pile foundation system bearing in the weathered tuff mudrock underlying
the site Because the new elevator and stair tower structure will be structurally
connected to the existing building we believe that a deep foundation system should be
provided for support of the new structure to reduce the potential for differential
movements between the new and existing structures
A deep foundation system consisting of cast in place drilled shafts was
considered for the support of the new elevator and stair tower However because of
the relatively shallow groundwater table and soft soils encountered installation of the
drilled shafts will require casing of the drilled shaft and placement of concrete by tremie
methods In addition in order to develop the required frictional and end bearing
capacity drilling into the relatively hard tuff formation would be required for the drilled
shafts Therefore we believe that the construction of the drilled shaft foundation would
be difficult and may not be cost effective due to the subsurface conditions encountered
at the project site
In comparison to drilled shaft pier foundation a driven pile foundation is
dependable and may be installed with less construction difficulties Therefore we
recommend that a driven pile foundation system be used for support of the new
elevator and stair tower However it should be noted that the pile driving operations
may cause some noise at the site and may also induce vibration of the existing
building Therefore we recommend that the vibration waves generated during the pile
driving operation be monitored to evaluate their potential effects on the existing JICPAC
Building 352 In addition a medium dense gravel layer and stiff near surface fills were
encountered in our drilled boring therefore predrilling the pile locations will be required
to reduce the vibration to the existing building due to pile driving Detailed discussion

Driven Pile Foundations
In general we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of 16 inch
octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles be used to support the proposed elevator
and stair tower structure The piles will derive support primarily from end bearing on
the tuff formation underlying the site as encountered in our boring drilled for the
project An allowable compressive load capacity of 80 tons may be used for the
foundation design of the piles The allowable compressive load capacity for the piles
is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such
as wind or seismic forces Piles should be spaced a minimum of 2 times the diameter
of the pile center to center to avoid reduction in capacity due to group action
It should be noted that only one boring was drilled at the site for this project
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the tuff formation slopes
down from the southeast to the northwest In addition the depth of weathering of the
tuff formation bearing stratum may be variable and should be expected Based on the
results of our exploration we estimate the pile tip to be at an elevation of about 75
feet U S Navy Datum For cost estimating purposes we recommend that the piles be
casted a minimum of 10 feet longer than estimated pile length to account for potential
variation of the bearing stratum The proposed pile lengths should be casted in one
piece for pile installation to avoid splicing of the piles

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from the elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate that the
total settlement of the pile supported foundation to be less than inch with
differential settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that amount
We believe these settlements to be essentially elastic and should occur as the
loads are applied
Predrilling
Medium dense gravels were encountered in our boring from about 12 to 17 feet
below the ground surface Additionally boulders may be encountered in the stiff
near surface fills based on our field exploration Because of the potential
presence of boulders and the medium dense gravel layer predrilling of all pile
locations to approximately 18 feet below the existing ground surface is
recommended The diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited to
approximately 20 inches or less to provide the pile with sufficient soil contact for
lateral load resistance If an over sized predrill hole is encountered during
construction the annular space between piles and predrilled hole should be
grouted with lean concrete to provide the recommended lateral load capacity
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy
between 30 000 and 50 000 foot pounds A hammer with higher energy is not
recommended because of the potential strong vibrations which may be
generated in the existing JICPAC Building during pile driving with a larger
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3438 00 Page 12
hammer The hammer should also be equipped with an energy level control
such that driving within the loose soft soil may be controlled at a lower energy
to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which could damage or
cause cracking of the piles
Geolabs Hawaii should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate
if the equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs
Hawaii prior to construction for evaluation Geolabs Hawaii should review the
submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected
hammer Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices should
be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Vibration Monitoring
Due to the proximity of the existing JICPAC Building 352 to the proposed project
site we recommend that the vibrations generated during the pile driving
operation be monitored to evaluate their potential effects on the existing JICPAC
Building 352 The criteria for vibration damage thresholds for pile driving
induced motions are related to the type of construction age and general
condition of structures and are adapted from a variety of studies of actual
damage thresholds observed for blasting operations and other vibration inducing
sources Based on our previous experience we estimate that a peak particle
velocity between 1 and 3 inches per second may be generated within the
JICPAC Building 352 when pile driving operations are within 10 feet of the
existing JICPAC Building 352 The effects of vibrations from the pile driving
operations should be evaluated by the Structural Engineer

Pile Driving Criteria
Due to the limited number of piles anticipated for this project we believe that a
test pile program would not be feasible for the proposed project Therefore we
strongly recommend that the pile driving operation be observed by Geolabs
Hawaii so that driving resistances tip elevations and axial load bearing capacity
may be confirmed during construction In this case the test pile program and
pile load test may be omitted for this project
For preliminary design purposes pile driving may be terminated when steady
aggregated blow counts are at least 80 blows for the last foot or 7 blows per inch
for the last 3 inches This preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed during
pile driving and may be modified to accommodate variations of soil conditions
in the field
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be
rejected or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is
interrupted for more than four hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required
depth During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the
hammer stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant
tensile stresses do not develop within the pile
It should be noted that the pile design was developed from our pile analysis
using the field exploration data Because variations in rock weathering and
strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing tuff formation the
supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field by a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii during driving This should be accomplished
GEOLABS HAWAII

by using a driving resistance procedure such as Wave Equation Analysis to
evaluate pile bearing capacity
Site Grading
Final grading plans were not available to us at the time this report was prepared
However we anticipate that minimal cut and fill of about 2 feet relative to the existing
ground surface would be required for the proposed construction
At the on set of earthwork all trees utility lines and rock walls within the limits
of the proposed structure should be removed and the area should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of off site
The exposed surface following stripping should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the moisture content corresponding to the maximum
dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Prior
to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth
of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3438 00 Page 15
The on site clayey soils generated from the excavation may be reused as a
source of fill and backfill material When used as fill and backfill material the on site
soils should be properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For fills and backfills
within 2 feet below the finish pavement subgrade the compaction should be increased
to a minimum of 95 percent Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes
steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched
If imported fill materials are required the imported fill and or backfill material
should consist of well graded granular materials of less than 3 inches in maximum
dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value
of 12 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain
between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should be
tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery
to the project site for its intended use
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs
Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading to
evaluate whether any undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those

Utility Trench
We understand that new utility lines would be required for the proposed project
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes Where soft and or loose soil deposits are encountered near the pipe
invert elevations we recommend that the utility pipe be supported on an additional
18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a geotextile
fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes
should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of
the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or
the approved on site soils The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling one
boring to a depth of about 50 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The boring was drilled using a truck mounted
drilling rig equipped with solid stem augers hollow stem augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft to medium stiff soil deposits were
retrieved using a thin wall Shelby tube sampler The Shelby tube sampler using a
3 inch diameter and 30 inch long thin wall stainless steel tube was hydraulically

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
One 1 inch ring swell test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized on
Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presenta
tion of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One sieve analysis with hydrometer test was performed on a selected sample
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 3
One consolidation test with time rates of consolidation was performed on the
potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
