INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for The Birch and Elm condominium development in the Honolulu district on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed in general accordance
with our fee proposal to W C W Corporation dated
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and our geotechnical
engineering recommendations which were derived from analyses based on data obtained
during the field exploration and laboratory testing The recommendations are intended
for design input and are not intended to form the basis for preparation of final construction
cost estimates or for bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Birch and Elm condominium project involves the construction of
a nine 9 story residential building The proposed development will also include asphaltic
concrete pavement for on grade parking
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface soil
conditions at the project site in order to develop a generalized soil rock data set for the

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the northeastern corner of the intersection of Birch
and Elm Streets in the Honolulu district on the island of Oahu Low rise residential
buildings are located to the north and east of the project site which is bounded by Birch
and Elm Streets on the west and south sides respectively
An existing abandoned wooden dwelling is located in the southern half of the project
site The northern half of the site is a yard area with grasses and trees
The project site is generally level with existing ground elevations ranging from
approximately 6 to 9 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the proposed project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view It appears that the upper strata of loose and soft sandy to silty
deposits encountered in our field exploration cannot provide adequate support for shallow
spread footing or mat foundations without ensuing excessive settlement under the
proposed structure Therefore twelve 12 inch square concrete piles founded in the coral
detritus layer or in the underlying bouldery alluvium are recommended to provide
foundation support for the proposed nine 9 story condominium building
Pile Foundations
Based on the data obtained from our field exploration and laboratory testing
foundations of piles driven through the loose and soft sandy to silty deposits occurring
at approximate depths of seven 7 to eighteen 18 feet are recommended to support the
proposed condominium building It is anticipated that pile foundations bearing in the
underlying relatively competent coral detritus layer or in the underlying dense bouldery
alluvium layer can develop sufficient support for the proposed nine 9 story mid rise
structure

Pile Capacities
Twelve 12 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles embedded in the coral
detritus layer with an allowable vertical pile capacity of 40 tons per pile may be
used The piles may develop the allowable vertical pile capacity by a combination
of end bearing on the bearing stratum and of the frictional resistance of the pile
shafts embedded in the surrounding soils A minimum pile spacing of three 8 feet
on centers is recommended
An allowable lateral pile capacity of up to 10 kips per pile and an allowable uplift
capacity of 10 kips per pile may be used in the design
Pile Lengths
Based on the preliminary exploration results estimated pile lengths may vary from
about 35 to 40 feet below the existing ground elevation For the initial construction
cost estimate an average pile length of about 35 feet may be used
However due to the erratic natures of the coral detritus formation some piles may
penetrate through less competent portions of the coral detritus layer without
meeting the pile driving criteria for the recommended pile capacity of 40 tons It
is our opinion that if the piles punch through the upper coral detritus layer the piles
should be spliced and driven to bear on the next competent bearing stratum of
bouldery alluvium at approximate depths of 80 to 90 feet Theoretically the vertical
pile capacity can be increased to 80 tons per pile for the longer piles end bearing
on the lower bouldery alluvium layer with estimated driven lengths of about 80 to
90 feet below the existing ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2754 00 Page 7
The above pile lengths are for initial cost estimation purposes The actual
production pile lengths should be determined after completion of a test pile
program
Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of about 30 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control so that driving within the loose deposits may be performed at
a reduced energy level to minimize the development of tensile stresses within the
piles which could cause damage cracking to the piles
Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose sandy soil the
hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with reduced energy level so
that excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
3 The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts steadily increase
with depth and reach 3 blows per inch for the last three inches or when
aggregated blow counts are 50 blows for the last foot A minimum pile
embedment length of 25 feet below the existing ground surface is
recommended
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2754 00 Page 8
For piles that punch through the upper coral detritus layer without meeting
the above driving criteria the piles should be spliced and the pile driving be
continued till the blow counts steadily increase with depth and reach 5 blows
per inch for the last three inches or when aggregated blow counts are 70
blows for the last foot
Test Pile Program
A test pile program should be undertaken to satisfy the following objectives
1 To examine the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
2 To more accurately determine production pile order lengths and to further
verify delineate potential areas requiring different production pile lengths
3 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the driving criteria
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the test pile program should
comprise a minimum ten 10 percent of the total number of piles as test piles for the
project The test piles may be incorporated into the actual foundation system later
When the final foundation plan is available the project geotechnical engineer should
be consulted for selection of the test pile locations The initial test piles should be cast
ten 10 feet longer than the 35 foot estimated pile length or to about 45 feet long Also
the test pile program should provide flexibility by allowing for addition or relocation of the
test piles as deemed necessary by the project geotechnical engineer to suit the conditions
encountered during the test pile driving
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2754 00 Page 9
A number of splice cans should be made available at the site during the test pile
driving period Splice cans may be fabricated with 1 4 inch thick high grade steel and
should be at least five 5 feet long
As a part of the test pile program a minimum of one 1 pile load test should be
budgeted The selected pile should be load tested to twice the allowable load capacity
in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1143
The test piles should be driven with the same hammer which will be used for
production pile driving The test pile program should be conducted under close and
continuous field observation of the project geotechnical engineer who will determine when
piles may be stopped and provide guidance should additional measures be required due
to unforeseen driving conditions
During the test pile driving period the geotechnical engineer may select to redrive
retap certain piles to evaluate the soil set up freeze effect on pile capacity
The geotechnical engineer should be consulted to evaluate the final pile bearing
capacity and production pile lengths based on the results of the test pile driving and the
pile load test
Site Grading
Based on the available grading plan it is our understanding that site grading
involving fills of 1 to 2 feet will be required for the proposed project
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared of vegetation rubbish old footings and pavements construction rubbles and other
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2754 00 Page 10
deleterious materials The resulting grub and spoil material should be disposed of properly
off site
The exposed subgrade after grubbing should be scarified to a depth of six 6
inches The scarified subgrade should then be thoroughly moisture conditioned and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78
Our laboratory testing results indicated that some of the on site surface clayey soils
are moderately expansive particularly if compacted and or dried Therefore excavated
on site clayey soils should not be used as fills within the upper eighteen 18 inches of
finish subgrade under the building and pavement areas The excavated on site clayey
soils may be used in landscaping areas The surplus clayey soils should be disposed of
properly off site
Fill materials should be non expansive well graded granular material with maximum
particle size less than three 8 inches in diameter and less than 15 percent passing
No 200 sieve Fill materials should be tested and approved by the project geotechnical
engineer prior to delivery to the job site
The fill backfill should be placed in horizontal loose level lifts up to a maximum of
10 inches thick and compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
All fill or backfill below the water level should consist of clean granular material such
as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 This material is generally self compacting
Additional densification may be obtained by use of a vibratory compactor on the final level
of the material

Retaining Walls
It is our understanding that CMU walls will be constructed along the northern and
eastern property boundaries The perimeter walls of the elevator pits will be subjected
to lateral earth loads and possibly groundwater pressures depending upon the pit bottom
elevation The elevator basement perimeter walls will be non yielding members and should
be designed for at rest condition The guidelines for retaining wall design are as follows
1 For conventional retaining walls an active earth pressure of 35 p s f
equivalent fluid pressure per foot of depth above groundwater level is
recommended
2 For at rest earth pressures the following values may be used

Utility Trench
Construction of new utility lines is anticipated for the project A granular bedding
consisting of six 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 is recommended
under the pipes

Granular materials should be used for trench backfill The backfill up to about one
1 foot above the pipes should be lightly tamped to reduce the possibility of damaging
the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one 1 foot above the pipes
to the finish grade should be placed in maximum 10 inch horizontal loose lifts mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 compaction as determined by ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78 to protect against potential future ground subsidence
Drainage
The finished grades should be shaped to shed water away from foundations and
pavements to avoid ponding conditions Also it is advised that a gutter system be
installed and that the gutter run off water be diverted as far away from the foundations and
pavements as feasible Excessive landscape watering near the foundations should also
be avoided
Planters next to the foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to prevent water from entering the foundation and pavement subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
recommended foundation and pavement construction since ponded water could cause
excessive subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength
Drainage swales must be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the foundations and pavements

Design Review
Drawings and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded
to the geotechnical engineer for review and written comments prior to construction This
review is needed to determine adherence to the earthwork and foundation recommenda
tions given herein If this review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot assume
responsibility for misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during site grading test pile and production pile driving
of the project This is to observe compliance with the recommendations design concepts
or specifications and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface conditions differ
from that anticipated prior to start of construction The recommendations given in this
report are contingent upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and approved by
the geotechnical engineer prior to delivery to the site
If soil rock conditions encountered during construction are different from those
assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the design should
be made
