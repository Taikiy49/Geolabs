SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our geotechnical reconnaissance performed for the
proposed OSA EHA and OB OD Access Road alignments the
shallow subsurface of the project sites are generally underlain by
stiff residual and saprolitic soils The residual and saprolitic soils
consist of silts and clays with fine sands The near surface soils
exist in a relatively dry state and are susceptible to erosion due
to wind and or surface water runoff In addition some basalt
cobbles and boulders may be locally encountered in the shallow
subsurface
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be
re used as a source of general fill or controlled fill provided that
the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious materials
and the maximum particle size is less than 150 mm in maximum
dimension The excavated on site materials may also be used
as a source of materials for the soil cement pavement structure
provided that an adequate amount of cement is added to the soil
to produce a soil cement product with a minimum unconfined

encountered along the EHA Access Road alignment

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of Maui and about
27 kilometers to the southeast of Lana i as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 1
Kaho olawe is approximately 117 square kilometers in land area with a maximum elevation
of about 450 meters above sea level at the Lua Makika summit on the eastern portion of the
island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layered thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the rift zones and caldera at Lua Makika The primary eruptive rift zones of
the former volcano extend westerly from the summit and to a lesser degree to the north and
east from the summit Two cinder cone remnants can be seen located at Pu u Moiwi and
Pu u Kamama The area at Lua Kealialalo is believed to represent the weathered remains of
GEOLABS INC
W O 4073 00 E Page 6
a collapsed crater at the crest of the broad lava shield Some of the more recent aged rocks
composing the upper caldera filling layers of the island have been dated at about 1 million
years old
Intermittent stream flow has incised gullies and some deep gulches into the residual
soil and weathered rock surface of the island The climate is generally arid due to the island s
leeward location with respect to the Island of Maui Most rainfall occurs during large Kona
storm events and also as light misting showers carried by the trade winds The
geomorphology of the island is most significantly affected by wind erosion which has created
a generally barren hardpan soil surface The lower slopes of the island show exposure of
bare rock with thin soil covering suggesting that some ocean wave erosion had occurred
during higher stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch It is believed that much of the
soil cover existing below about Elevation 240 meters Mean Sea Level MSL was historically
swept into the ocean by a large tsunami Similar large scale soil erosion and coral fragment
deposition have been observed on the Islands of Maui and Lana i

Soil Cement Pavement
We understand that the access roads may be surfaced with soil cement pavement
using the on site fine grained soils The access roads are to be designed for use by relatively
light to moderate duty vehicles such as pick up trucks light vehicles and occasional
moderately loaded trucks Based on the anticipated vehicle use we recommend that a
150 mm thick soil cement pavement be used for the access road surface
Our laboratory tests conducted for the OSA EHA and OB OD Access Road project
indicate that an unconfined compressive strength of day curing period may be
achieved based on a cement content of 22 percent by weight in the laboratory with the
exception of the EHA Access Road which may contain some potentially unsuitable soils Due
to the low CBR value of Sample S 25 we were unable to achieve the desired unconfined
compressive strength of 5 MPa with up to 30 percent cement content by weight Therefore
we recommend that the areas in the vicinity of potentially unsuitable soils materials with CBR
values of less than 12 be further evaluated and delineated in the field during construction
The potentially unsuitable materials with CBR values less than 12 should be over excavated
down to a depth of about 400 mm below the finished pavement grade to remove the
unsuitable soils and should be replaced with borrow of better soils CBR values greater than
12 The desired unconfined compressive strength of 5 MPa may be readily achieved with the
recommended cement content or adjusted based on further soil cement tests using the
better soils

Cracking in Soil Cement
Cracking in soil cement products pavements is primarily the result of shrinkage
caused by drying Cracks are natural characteristics of soil cement and are evidence that the
cement mixture is producing a hardened material with significant strength properties The
GEOLABS INC
W O 4073 00 E Page 12
shrinkage in the soil cement is due to a loss of moisture and will occur soon after construction
similar to a concrete pavement The shrinkage in the soil cement pavement will be restrained
by friction between the soil cement and the subgrade Therefore when the tensile strength
of the soil cement is exceeded cracks will occur It should be noted that the load carrying
capability and performance of the soil cement are generally not significantly reduced by
shrinkage cracking
Because cracking in the soil cement is primarily the result of drying and shrinkage the
least amount of cracking should occur in soil cements having the lowest moisture content and
highest density at the time of compaction It should be noted that the optimum moisture
contents of the soils at the project site are relatively high about 30 percent Consequently
the moisture contents of the soil cement pavements at the time of compaction will also be
relatively high Therefore considering the higher moisture contents of the soil cement
pavements required at the time of compaction more shrinkage cracking in the soil cement
pavements than usual should be anticipated
Based on consultation with our soil cement expert we anticipate that the amount of
transverse shrinkage cracking in the soil cement pavements constructed with the on site
fine grained soils may be on the order of about 4 to 6 meters on center based on well cured
soil cement Longitudinal shrinkage cracking of the soil cement pavements is anticipated to
be generally along the middle of the pavement The typical crack widths could be on the order
of about 1 to wide The above discussions regarding crack spacing and crack widths
are based on previous studies performed on soil cement by the Portland Cement Association
In order to reduce the potential for significant shrinkage cracking of the soil cement
pavement constructed with the on site fine grained soils we recommend that special attention
be given to the curing of the soil cement pavements One of the most critical aspects of
proper curing of the soil cement pavement is early and effective retention of moisture within
the soil cement pavement The soil cement pavement should not be allowed to dry out for the
first 7 days after compaction This may be accomplished by keeping burlap pieces
continuously moist or by frequent applications of water from a water truck As an alternative
GEOLABS INC
W O 4073 00 E Page 13
to water curing consideration may be given to sealing the soil cement pavement surface with
asphalt emulsion if the use of asphalt emulsions is permissible on the island
Use of Seawater
We understand that consideration is being given to the use of seawater for the mixing
of the soil cement and grading operations since a source of large volumes of fresh water is not
readily available at the site In general it is possible to use seawater in lieu of fresh water to
mix and produce a soil cement pavement Presence of chlorides in the seawater will likely
promote high early strengths of the soil cement which is generally not detrimental to the
performance of the soil cement pavement
Use of seawater for the soil cement mixing is not necessarily detrimental to the
soil cement pavement mixture especially since the soil cement pavements have no metallic
structures embedded in the pavements such as reinforcing bars However it should be
noted that using the seawater to mix the soil cement would make the soil cement more
corrosive to metallic structures embedded in the soil cement or even reinforced concrete
structures in contact with the soil cement Appropriate design of the metallic and or reinforced
concrete structures would be necessary in these conditions
We also believe that the grading operations may use seawater in lieu of fresh water for
moisture conditioning the soils for the earthworks Use of seawater for the earthworks would
be reasonable provided that the compacted soils will be covered by paving or other
improvements However it should be noted that the soils would have a higher salt content
which contributes to corrosion making buried metallic structures more susceptible to
corrosion In addition the soils may also be more corrosive to buried and or on grade
reinforced concrete structures
It should also be noted that the use of seawater for the grading operations would also
make vegetative growth on the soils with higher salt content more difficult An appropriate
soil conditioning expert should be consulted on this issue of vegetation growth on soils with
high salt content due to seawater

Some potentially unsuitable soils for soil cement pavement construction may underlie
portions of the EHA Access Road alignment The limits of these potentially unsuitable
soils will need to be defined in the field during construction by means of additional
laboratory testing In general we recommend that the potentially unsuitable soils for
soil cement pavements be over excavated to a depth of about 400 mm below the
finished pavement grade and be replaced with borrow soils with a CBR value of at least
12
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted general fill or controlled fill The excavated soft soils
should be properly disposed of off site and or stockpiled in a designated area where
appropriate Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the
over excavation quantities
Finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 200 mm moisture conditioned to between 5 percent below the
optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 698 Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

rock formation we recommend that the subgrades be proof rolled with a vibratory drum

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill or
controlled fill materials provided that they are free of vegetation and deleterious
materials and the maximum particle size should be less than 150 mm in maximum
dimension In addition the soil materials to be used for the soil cement pavement
mixtures should also have a CBR value of at least 12 It should be noted that the
on site soils appear to be relatively dry between 12 and 20 percent below the optimum
moisture content Therefore an ample water supply and number of water trucks will
be necessary during the grading operations to provide adequate water to achieve the
specified moisture and compaction requirements
Based on the remote location of the project site from available sources of imported fill
material we do not envision the need for imported general fill or controlled fill materials
for this project Where additional general fill or controlled fill materials are necessary
we envision that the fill materials would be obtained from nearby projects on the island
Therefore borrow materials should conform to the requirements of general fill or
controlled fill as described above Borrow materials to be used for the soil cement
pavement should have a CBR value of 12 or more when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill and or controlled fill materials should be moisture conditioned to between
5 percent below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 698 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut and fill slopes on the order of about 1 to 2 meters high may be constructed at the
site for the proposed construction We envision that the cut slopes would generally
expose the residual and saprolitic soils In general we believe that cut slopes exposing
the very stiff soils may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one
vertical 2H 1V or flatter It should be noted that graded surfaces exposing the on site
materials are highly susceptible to erosion by wind and surface water runoff
Therefore freshly graded cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope planting
or other means to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed materials as soon as
possible after the slope faces are exposed
Permanent fill slopes constructed using compacted general fill or controlled fill materials
may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes
steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide
stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill
placement recommendations presented herein In general fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well compacted slope face In addition slope planting should be provided as soon as
possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the soil cement pavements for the
roadway should be sloped to shed water away from the pavements to reduce the potential for
ponding Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the soil cement
pavements since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent
heaving or loss of strength of the underlying subgrade soils The edges of the soil cement
pavements should be properly backfilled to reduce the potential for water infiltration In
addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained
to drain surface water runoff away from the soil cement pavements
