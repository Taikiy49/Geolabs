performed for the 34048 Information Systems Facility ISF project within

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The
dips of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees The lava flows on the plateau
have weathered to depths of 15 to 30 meters and are characterized by the red colors of
the soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are
a result of this weathering The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient
alluvial deposits overlying the weathered rock

3 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings be used
to support the new building planned for the project An allowable bearing pressure of up to
may be used for the design of shallow footings bearing on the stiff to
hard clayey soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should have a minimum width of 300 millimeters In addition
footings should be embedded a minimum of 450 millimeters below the lowest adjacent
finished grade Foundations located next to utility trenches and or easements should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or embedded as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be recompacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Soft and or loose materials that are
encountered at the footing subgrade level should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select
granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
footings may be extended down to the firm or dense materials
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the stiff to hard
clayey soils may be on the order of about 25 millimeters We estimate that differential
settlements between adjacent footings supported on the stiff to hard clayey soils to be on
the order of about 15 millimeters

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our laboratory test results the clayey soils encountered at the site exhibit
a slight potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils we recommend that the subgrade soils
underneath the slab areas be scarified to a depth of about 200 millimeters moisture
conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In addition the slab subgrade area should be capped with at least 150 millimeters
of non expansive select granular fill materials The non expansive select granular fill
materials should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non
expansive select granular fill materials should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
above discussions The non expansive select granular fill materials and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of concrete

3 7 Pavement Joints
Due to the rigidity of the Portland cement concrete pavements significant stresses
may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture content In order
to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for cracking adequate joints
should be provided in the Portland cement concrete pavement alternative The following
describes the various types of joints and general guidelines that should be implemented in
the design of concrete pavements
Crack Control Joints Contraction Joints
In general decreases in temperature or moisture content such as that during
concrete curing will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack Crack
control joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and
relieve the stresses caused by curling or warping Crack control joints such as saw
cut joints or formed grooves are provided to a depth of generally one fourth the
slab thickness These saw cut joints or formed grooves crack control joints
provide a weakened plane which will likely crack through the full depth as the
concrete shrinks during the curing process
In general the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of
6 meters Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and or life span
of the pavement The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal
and transverse directions The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent
Expansion Joints Isolation Joints
Expansion joints are typically used to relieve compressive stresses developed at
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature Expansion joints are cut to the
full depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in the
cut These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the
W O 5040 00 B GEOLABS INC Page 20
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
potential for damage to the adjoining concrete spalling of the edges of the concrete
slabs
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of
pavements with fixed structures Because expansion joints generally do not
provide for load transfer across the joint placement of dowels with lubrication on
one end of the dowel should be considered for load transfer across the expansion
joint where required
Construction Joints
Construction joints are generally required when two abutting slabs are placed at
different times Therefore construction joints usually form the edges of each day s
work In general construction joints should be aligned with the crack control or
expansion joints to reduce the number of pavement joints required

3 9 Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided In addition planters next to foundations should be avoided
or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration
into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of our
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration Drainage swales should
be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff
away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Five 50 mm Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test
results are presented on Plate B 1
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two sets of Corrosion tests including pH ASTM G 51 and Minimum Resistivity
ASTM G 57 were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field
exploration The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 6

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing one falling head
test in Boring No 9 at a depth of about 4 3 meters below the existing ground surface
During the falling head testing the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top
of the borehole The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data
upon which to base calculation of the permeability value of the subsoils The test result of
the field permeability test is presented on Plate C 1
