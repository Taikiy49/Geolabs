SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered volcanic ash underlain by dense basalt 
formation at both Waiohuli and Kaonoulu sites. The volcanic ash materials generally 
consisted of stiff to hard silts with some sands and gravel and extended down to depths 
of about 2.5 to 10 feet below the ground surface. In general, the basalt formation, where 
encountered, was medium hard to hard and relatively unweathered to moderately 
weathered, and extended down to the maximum depth explored of about 30 feet below 
the ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in our borings drilled at both 
Waiohuli and Kaonoulu sites at the time of our field exploration. 
Pressure relief valve vaults will be constructed at Waiohuli and Kaonoulu sites. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow footings to support the new PRV vaults. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of footings bearing on 
re-compacted on-site materials. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by 50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
A new ford crossing consisting of rigid pavements are planned at the Waiohuli 
site.  The recommended rigid pavement section for the new ford crossing is 6.0 inches 
of Portland cement concrete over 6.0 inches of aggregate subbase course.  Flexible 
pavements required for other areas of the site may consist of 2.0 inches of asphaltic 
concrete over 6.0 inches of aggregate base course. The volcanic ash soils at the site 
may have very high moisture contents.  Therefore, we anticipate that there may be a 
strong potential for pumping subgrade conditions during the subgrade preparation. If the 
subgrade soils are pumping during preparation of the subgrade soils, a woven 
geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent) or geogrids (such as Tensar TriAx 
TX-7 or equivalent) may be required on the subgrade prior to construction of the 
pavement section.   
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Upcountry Maui Irrigation System Improvements project in 
the District of Makawao on the Island of Maui. The project location and general vicinity

only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The proposed project is in the Waiohuli and Kaonoulu areas in the District of 
Makawao on the Island of Maui. Based on the information provided, improvements will be 
made for the Waiohuli and Kaonoulu irrigation systems.  The proposed improvements will 
include the following: 
Approximately 2,520 and 4,645 lineal feet of new 6-inch irrigation lines for 
Waiohuli and Kaonoulu irrigation laterals, respectively. 
Construction of new roadway along portions of the Waiohuli irrigation lateral, 
including a ford crossing across the existing drainage gulch. 
Pavement restoration along Kaonoulu irrigation lateral. 
Minor site grading along the proposed irrigation lateral alignments. 
Construction of pressure relief valve (PRV) vaults at Waiohuli and Kaonoulu 
sites. 
Based on the anticipated finished floor elevation, we understand only minor site 
grading consisting of about 1 to 2 feet of cuts and fills are anticipated at the majority of the 
site.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary 
Epoch) 
and 
the 
more 
recent 
East 
Maui, 
also 
known 
as 
Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain 
located between these two volcanoes. 
The older West Maui volcano is generally comprised of three lava flows. The lower 
unit was formed during the Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch and is identified as the 
Wailuku Volcanic Series. The lavas of the Wailuku Volcanic Series generally consist of 
thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe lavas. Overlying this unit are lavas of the Honolua Volcanic 
Series (late Pleistocene Epoch) that consist of alkalic lavas and stiff trachyte. Volcanic 
inactivity occurred during the Middle Pleistocene Epoch where weathering of the lavas, 
carving of canyons, and deposition of materials occurred. During the late Pleistocene 
Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred near the southern coast with 
Lahaina Volcanic Series lava flows generally consisting of olivine-rich basalt and nepheline 
basanite. 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Haleakala. Haleakala was 
generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows. The lower unit, the 
Honomanu Volcanic Series (late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch) consist of 
thinly bedded, tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a’a lavas. These lavas tend to be very 
permeable and carry basal water. Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series 
(early Pleistocene Epoch), composed primarily of thicker alkalic a’a flows, which may 
contain many inter-stratified, thin ash-soil layers. A long quiescent period followed, 
during which time canyons were carved in the volcano, although a few eruptions may 
have occurred during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes 
(higher and lower stands of the sea level). At the end of the rest period, copious flows 
erupted along the southwest and east rifts. This is the third and upper rock unit, known 
as the Hana Volcanic Series (Pleistocene Epoch). The lavas range in composition from 
ultra-basic olivine porphyries to non-porphyritic alkalic basalts.

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is in the District of Makawao on the Island of Maui. The project

The proposed water lateral starts from about 700 feet west of Koheo Road and

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most of the earthquakes are so small that only sensitive instruments could detect 
the small tremors. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, 
and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5; therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. Due to the relatively short period of documented 
earthquake monitoring in the State of Hawaii, information for earthquakes on the 
Island of Maui may not be complete. However, the following are two examples of 
reported earthquakes within the County of Maui. A Magnitude 6.8 earthquake 
occurred on the Island of Maui in 1938, and the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 was 
estimated at Magnitude 7 or greater. Based on these two reported earthquakes, the 
Island of Maui may be considered seismically active.

3.1 
Foundations 
We understand that PRV vaults will be constructed at Waiohuli and Kaonoulu 
sites.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
using shallow footings to support the new PRV vaults. An allowable bearing pressure of 
up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of footings bearing 
on re-compacted on-site materials. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and 
may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the 
lowest adjacent grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on slopes should 
be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured from the 
outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is no less than 6 feet. 
In addition, foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, easements or 
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane 
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the footings 
should be lowered to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement 
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional 
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated until dense materials are exposed in the footing excavation. 
The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may 
extend down to bear directly on the underlying competent material. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than

3.2 
Retaining Structures 
Retaining structures, such as below-grade PRV vaults, may be required for the 
project. Therefore, the following general guidelines are provided and may be used for 
design of retaining structures at the project site. 
3.2.1 Retaining Structure Foundations 
In general, we believe retaining structure foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the 
"Foundations" section herein. However, the retaining structure footings should have 
a minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, wall foundations on relatively flat areas 
should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade. A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations 
during construction to evaluate the exposed conditions. 
For sloping ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to obtain a 
minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of 
the footing to slope face. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope 
should be constructed in stepped footings.

Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may need to be considered 
in the design of the retaining structures. Forces caused by dynamic lateral earth 
pressures due to seismic loading (amax= 0.269g) may be estimated using 9H2 
pounds per linear foot of wall length for level backfill conditions, where H is the 
height of the wall in feet. The resultant force should be assumed to act through 
the mid-height of the wall.  
Please note that the force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented 
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures. An appropriately reduced 
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are 
accounted for in the design of the retaining structure. The design parameters 
provided for the level backfill condition may be used for slightly sloped backfill 
inclinations of six horizontal to one vertical (6H:1V) or flatter. 
It should be noted that the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures provided 
assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally by about 2 inches in the event 
of an earthquake. For a restrained condition (less than 0.5 inches of lateral 
movement), forces caused by dynamic lateral earth pressures due to seismic 
loading may be estimated using 18H2 pounds per linear foot of wall length for level 
backfill conditions.

3.3.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site materials may be re-used as a source of general 
fill materials, provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum 
dimension. 
It should be noted that the on-site volcanic ash materials may have relatively high 
in-situ moisture contents. Therefore, good earthwork construction practices will be 
necessary for the grading operations at the site. In addition, the on-site materials 
may need to be aerated to reduce the moisture content prior to their use as general 
fill materials in order to achieve the specified minimum degree of compaction. 
Imported fill should consist of non-expansive select granular fills. The 
non-expansive select granular fills should consist of crushed coral or basalt and 
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in 
largest dimension. The material should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 
percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. It should be noted 
that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may

3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Compaction should be accomplished with sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or 
ponding should not be allowed for compaction. General and select granular fill 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement for the 
last lift of fills placed under the pavement areas and compacted fills under the 
footings should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
The aggregate base and subbase courses should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
3.3.4 Excavation 
The project site generally is underlain by volcanic ash over basalt formation. It is 
anticipated that the volcanic ash materials may be readily excavated with normal 
heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators. However, some of the deeper 
excavations in the dense basalt formation likely will require the use of hoerams or 
chipping.

3.3.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose volcanic ash

The fill slope face should be free of voids, which would allow erosion and migration

The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back 
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. The fill slope face 
should be finished to a relatively smooth and well-compacted surface. In addition, 
slope planting or other means of slope protection should be provided as soon as 
practicable to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes.

3.6 
Underground Utility Lines 
We envision new underground utility lines will be required. We anticipate most of 
the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in the volcanic ash materials and 
basalt formation encountered in the drilled borings. 
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, 
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches

formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement 
of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.

to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular 
materials less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch loose lifts, 
and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where 
trenches are located in paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the 
pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
thirteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 13, extending to depths of about 
10 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-13. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
A one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-1. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-2. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Three Modified Proctor Compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-9.
