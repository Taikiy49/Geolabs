REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two separate
submarine shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and Koolau
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 5 million years
ago After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed
by cinder and tuff cone formations These more recent series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series and include some prominent landform features as the Diamond
Head and Koko Head Craters
During the latter stages of the Koolau Volcano eruption the central Koolau Caldera
was filled with thick ponded lavas and intruded by a myriad of narrow lava feeding volcanic
dike structures which have been collectively identified as the Kailua Volcanic Series The
Koolau Caldera structure is believed to have encompassed the entire Kailua area
extending roughly from the existing Koolau Pali northeastward toward the existing
coastline and beyond Following the deposition of the caldera filling lavas the caldera
structure subsided and the basaltic rocks of the Kailua Volcanic Series were deeply altered
by percolating underground fluids and gases In addition surface streams were incised
and dissection and erosion of the exposed rock materials commenced Therefore only
scattered erosional remnants of the original caldera structure and lava filling deposits
remain exposed as relatively low hills surrounding the Kailua area which include
Mt Olomana located in the vicinity of the project site
The project site is situated on the lower northeasterly facing foothills of
Mt Olomana as indicated on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on our review of
available geologic map information the majority of the project site is underlain by
weathered basaltic rock and derived soils generally consisting of reddish tan to brown
colored silty and clayey residual saprolitic soils The near surface soils represent the
extremely to completely weathered basaltic rock products belonging to the Kailua
Volcanic Series
The basalt rock underlying the project site is unlike the typical layered basalt rock
belonging to the Koolau Volcanic Series Sequential layers of interbedded basalt clinker
and hard basalt rock layers typify the Koolau Volcanic Series The basalt rock of the
Kailua Volcanic Series generally consists of thick lavas that are locally brecciated and
intruded by volcanic lava feeder dike structures The near surface basalt rock
exposures encountered at the project site appear to be generally devoid of weaker
interbedded clinker layers As a result of the deep weathering and erosion of the Kailua
Volcanic Series parent rock materials the lower foothills of Mt Olomana typically are
underlain by decomposed residual and saprolitic soils containing remnant surface rock
outcroppings and isolated boulders

SITE RECONNAISSANCE
A ground surface reconnaissance was conducted in an effort to evaluate and map
the existing field conditions pertaining to the occurrence of surface rock outcroppings and
boulder deposits which may present potential rockfall hazards for the proposed residential
subdivision development Our geologist and geological mapping assistant performed the
site reconnaissance under dry weather and ground conditions using the available field
reference staking supplemented with a handheld Garmin Global Positioning System
GPS a barometric altimeter and compass navigational aids Due to the dense canopy
of tree cover the GPS device was of limited use in locating features
The purpose of our mapping effort was to obtain an overview of the general
location size and spatial distribution of the surface boulders and rock outcroppings
residing on the hillside In addition the field reconnaissance was performed to evaluate
probable boulder rolling paths that could affect the proposed downslope development
within the study area The field information also was used to support the computer
simulation and statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior at the project site using
the CRSP engineering analytical tool
Generally isolated detached surface rocks larger than about 2 feet in dimension
were identified as boulders Other scattered attached outcroppings of basalt rock
protruding greater than about 2 feet above the ground surface were identified as areas of
rock outcropping Rock outcroppings consisting of isolated relatively flat low relief rock
surfaces were not included in the mapping effort because potential rockfall activity from
these sources is considered inconsequential Although a relatively intensive effort to
locate existing boulders and significant rock outcroppings on the hillside was performed
some rock features may have been missed due to occasional thick vegetation growth
limited sight distance in the rolling terrain and limited field survey reference points
Therefore the mapping effort may not reflect all of the boulders and rock outcroppings
potentially residing within the study limits It should be noted that our preliminary estimate

Rockfall Simulation Analysis
As discussed previously site reconnaissance was performed to visually identify and
locate potential rockfall hazards such as loose surface boulders and unstable rock
outcroppings residing within the project study area The site reconnaissance also
permitted us to evaluate probable rolling trajectories in relation to the existing topographic
features encountered in the field Following the site reconnaissance selected information
recorded in the field was input to the CRSP to validate the field evaluation
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
by experienced personnel to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall
activity based on input parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input
parameters for this project were assigned based on the observations and measurements
collected in the field The program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall
behavior based on the typical hillslope topographic profile and other input information such
as rock size shape and parameters used to describe the ground surface irregularities
Information obtained from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity
bouncing height kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is useful to
assist in the site specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall
impact barriers or other rockfall containment systems
Topographic information input to the CRSP was obtained from the available project
site topographic survey map which appears to be derived from an aerial orthophoto
survey The map contours were provided at 5 foot intervals except where incomplete
topographic data was produced In the areas of incomplete topographic data the contour

Hawaii California
Belt Collins Hawaii Ltd Page 10

was set at a 25 foot interval As a result the hillslope profiles developed from the
topographic map data provide a generalization of the actual terrain irregularities
Four selected hillslope profiles identified as Slope Profiles 1 through 4 were
developed to support our CRSP analysis The approximate surface location of the
four slope profiles used in our analysis is shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The graphic
presentation of the slope profiles produced from the available topographic survey
information is shown on the Slope Profiles Plate 4
Our CRSP analysis was performed using spherical shaped boulders conservative
scenario ranging in size from 2 to 6 feet in dimension rolling from the source area s
identified during the field reconnaissance For each simulation run 1 000 source rocks
were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results Numerical input coefficients
were used which approximated the typical low relief and low roughness condition of the
ground surfaces underlain by stiff soils with some rock
Based on our review and interpretation of the statistical results obtained from the
CRSP models we conclude that approximately 20 to 80 percent of simulated rolling
spherical shaped rocks smaller than about 4 feet in dimension may pass below
Elevation 400 feet MSL considering the present site conditions Elevation 400 feet
MSL was selected as a rockfall simulation analysis point to represent the anticipated upper
reach of future building areas within Lot Nos 1 and 2 For spherical rocks of 5 and 6 feet
in dimension the rockfall simulation shows the percentage passing Elevation 400 feet
MSL decreases to less than about 15 percent
Because the modeled hillslopes generally are not critically steep 1H 1V or flatter
within and downslope of the potential rockfall source area boulders smaller than about
3 feet in dimension were found to bounce more and roll further due to lower energy losses
induced by less ground contact Furthermore boulders larger than about 3 feet in
dimension were found to roll along the ground rather than bounce therefore the energy
losses are greater and the larger boulders tend to stop on flatter portions of the hillside
Although the percentage of larger boulders 5 and 6 foot dimension passing the rockfall
analysis point at Elevation 400 feet MSL is considered to be low the kinetic energy of
those that do pass the analysis point is very high

Rockfall Mitigation Conceptual Alternatives
Based on our rockfall hazard evaluation we propose three viable conceptual
alternatives for rockfall mitigation to protect the downslope lots which may be exposed to
rockfall hazards residing within Lot Nos 1 and 2 In general the proposed conceptual
alternatives involve boulder rock outcrop demolition and removal and the construction of a
rockfall impact barrier across the hillside
Rockfall impact barriers are used worldwide as an effective rockfall mitigation and
protection system Rockfall impact barriers commonly are constructed on hillsides where
the protection of downslope areas from various sources of potential falling rock is
necessary Rockfall impact barriers are considered to be a viable rockfall protection
measure where a specific and limited source of potential rockfall cannot be readily
identified and stabilized or where the source area may encompass disturbance sensitive
land that makes other mitigation measures impractical to implement
The rockfall impact barrier is a specialized barrier structure designed to flex and
absorb the impact energy of falling boulders The barrier system essentially consists of a
series of steel posts linked by wire rope cables with steel ring nets and wire mesh material
spanning between the posts Lateral and upslope wire rope anchors that are installed in
drilled holes and grouted in place with cement grout restrain the barrier posts Rockfall
barrier manufacturers fabricate the barrier components with varying capacities and
dimensions based on the anticipated design load rockfall impact energy and probable
rock bounce heights Therefore it is critical to carefully evaluate the existing site
conditions and perform a statistical simulation of the rockfall activity to assist in the design
of an appropriately sized rockfall impact barrier system
Rockfall impact barriers are designed to non destructively contain multiple small
quantity rockfall events without the need for barrier maintenance and repair However
extreme rockfall events involving large quantities of rock material or very large boulders
may require that the system be inspected for damage and the debris promptly removed to
restore system effectiveness As with any outdoor structure composed of steel
components deterioration and corrosion of some system components over time may
require the repair or replacement of worn components In the event that very limited
rockfall activity is experienced the barrier system should be routinely inspected for signs of
corrosion at a minimum of 5 to 10 year intervals In Hawaii s wet and humid environment
the lifespan of a rockfall barrier system is anticipated to range between about 20 and
40 years after which replacement of some system components may be required
We envision that the hillside rock removal commonly referred to as a boulder
sweep by rockfall practitioners and rockfall impact barrier installation work could be
performed by a rockfall mitigation specialty contractor or other experienced general
contractor equipped to perform the remote hillside area work using mainly portable
equipment Typical rock demolition work such as that envisioned for this project is
accomplished usually by labor crews using pneumatic drills hydraulic rock splitters pry

bars and small explosive charges to fracture and break up the target rocks into
manageable pieces for the safe and controlled removal of the rock from the slope followed

work area to contain large rocks and falling rock fragments that may become detached
from the slope Construction of the rockfall impact barrier fence essentially proceeds with

rope cables are then strung through the posts to which cable ring nets and wire mesh
panels are attached by shackles The barrier system is restrained by a series of steel
braking rings and wire rope ground anchors which are drilled and grouted into the ground

The rockfall mitigation alternative consists of the construction of a
higher capacity rockfall barrier system across the hillside located
between about Elevation 400 to 450 feet MSL within Lot
Nos 1 and 2 No rock demolition and removal would be performed
We estimate that about 300 lineal feet of medium high capacity about
129 foot ton capacity rockfall impact barrier should be installed across
Lot No 1 In addition about 500 lineal feet of very high capacity
about 295 foot ton rockfall impact barrier should be installed across
Lot No 2

Advantages
Considered to be a comprehensive long term
rockfall mitigation scheme combining rock removal
with rockfall barrier protection
Removal of the larger block size potential rockfall
hazards which may be the most dangerous and
difficult rockfall to control with a barrier system
Easier to locate and identify the larger block
potential hazards on the hillside
Potential for encountering unforeseen unstable rock
conditions during rock removal is reduced due to the
lower volume of rock needing removal from the
hillside
Reduces ground and vegetation disturbance on the
forested upslope areas
A lower capacity and less costly barrier system may
be used to control rockfall produced from the
smaller block size materials remaining on the
hillside
Barrier maintenance costs should be lower due to
smaller size barrier components and lower number
of ground anchors for the medium and medium high
capacity barriers
Considered to be an effective long term rockfall

capacity barrier system only
Complete preservation of the upper slope natural

conditions during rock removal work is eliminated

Disadvantages
The rockfall barrier will require periodic inspection by the owner to
identify heavy rock debris accumulations about once per year or
following a large rockfall event which may necessitate the prompt
removal of the debris to maintain system effectiveness
The rockfall barrier will require inspection and maintenance of the
system for signs of long term corrosion degradation and possible
re tensioning of cables about 5 to 10 year interval
Replacement of worn or corroded components or removal of large
rock debris may require a contractor s assistance
Vehicle access around the barrier toward the upper slope areas
may be reduced Designated openings must be incorporated into
the barrier design
Some disturbance to the natural ground and vegetation is
anticipated in the upper slope areas where rock demolition and
removal would occur
Periodic evaluation of the remaining rock outcrops and boulders
by a qualified geologist or engineer is recommended to evaluate
possible changing conditions related to erosion and rock
weathering about 5 to10 year interval
