Preliminary Soils Investigation
Proposed Waimea Swinging Bridge Reconstruction
This report presents the results of our preliminary soils
investigation for the proposed Waimea Swinging Bridge reconstruction
over Hanapepe River at The general location
of the site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The investigation was performed in accordance with our proposal
dated It consisted of drilling one boring at the
northern bridge abutment and laboratory testing This report
summarizes our findings and preliminary recommendations
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located along the northern bank of the
Hanapepe River near Awawa Road and directly opposite access from Pa
Lane on the southern bank in
W O 1278 00 Page 2
Prior to our field exploration the existing pedestrian sus
pension bridge that spanned the river for a distance of about 290 feet
collapsed during Hurricane Iwa
An earthern levee along the river separated the adjacent
subdivision lots from the river The levee was about 12 to 15 feet
high with side slopes of about 2 1 H V on each side A maintenance
road 10 to 12 feet wide was located along the top of the levee
A relatively level area about 20 feet wide and 2 feet above the
river was situated between the river bank and the levee toe
Ground cover consisted of dense vegetation of tall grass and
Haole Koa on the slopes and along the level base Several cracks were
also observed parallel to and along the top of the levee slopes
PROPOSED CONSTRUCT ION
A new suspension foot bridge is planned to replace the existing
collapsed structure Due to budgetary restraints only one boring was
drilled in the northern bridge abutment area on the Waimea side
levee for this investigation The existing southern abutment will be
reused for the new bridge

The new bridge structure will span about 290 feet over the
Hanapepe River The cable tower foundations are anticipated to be
Situated in the relatively flat area located between the river bank
and levee toe Tieback anchors for the suspension cables will be
placed into the earthen levee It is our understanding that the tower
foundation loading will be on the order of 30 kips each and cable
anchor loading will be relatively small
SUBSURFACE EXPLORATION
The site of the potential cable tower foundations was heavily
overgrown and access to the site could not be provided during the time
of our field exploration Therefore one boring was drilled by a
conventional truck mounted rig atop the levee to a depth of 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The drilling was directed by one
of our engineers at the site who examined the site conditions logged
the borings and obtained soil samples for laboratory testing
The location of our boring is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The boring log is presented on Plate A 1
Soil samples obtained were brought back to our office and
selected samples were tested for moisture and density determinations
The results are shown on the log of boring at the appropriate sample
depths Two direct shear tests were also performed on relatively
undisturbed samples to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of
the on site soils The results are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2

SOIL CONDITIONS
The boring encounteed about 13 feet of levee fill consisting of
10 feet of reddish brown very stiff clayey silt and about 3 feet of
stiff silty clay Under the levee fill was about 7 feet of dark brown
medium stiff to stiff silty clay and organic silt to a depth of about
20 feet below the top of the levee Below the organic silt black
silty medium dense basaltic sand containing pockets of decayed wood
and other organic matter were encountered from 20 to 26 5 feet the
maximum depth drilled
Groundwater level measured was at about 17 feet below the top of
the levee about the same elevation of the river water It is probable
that the ground water level fluctuates with the stream level

It is our understanding that a new swinging foot bridge similar
to the damaged one will be built According to Mr Kent Kitabayashi

and recommendations presented herein apply only to the northern
abutment
The northern bank abutment structure will consist of cable

Deadman Anchor Support
For the anchoring of the cables we recommend that cast in place
deadman anchors be used The deadman anchor may either be a vertically
drilled shafts caisson or excavated blocks to an appropriate diameter
or dimensions to resist lateral forces imposed by the suspension
cables To develop the lateral passive soil pressure the anchor
should preferably be embedded in the center of the embankment away
from the slope facing Hanapepe River
For the design of the deadman anchors we recommend a trapezoidal
distribution of allowable lateral pressures Starting with 500 p s f
at 2 feet below the top of the levee and increasing at 130 p s f per
foot of depth up to 12 feet below the top of the levee It is
anticipated that anchors need not be extended below a depth of 12
feet These allowable pressures are for the side facing the river
In addition there should be a setback distance equal to at least 5
times the anchor diameter or width measuring from the outer edge of
the anchor to the river side slope face An average skin friction of
150 p s f may be considered in addition to the dead weight of the
anchor to resist uplift component of cable forces
It is our understanding that the cables will have to be buried
a sufficient depth below the top of the levee to permit vehicle
movement We suggest that these cables be buried at least 18 inches

The construction of deadman anchor may consist of the following
1 Drill or excavate vertical shafts to desired depth We
believe that the holes should remain open without casing for
a short duration
2 excavate narrow trenches for the installation of cable
tendons
3 place cable tendons with suitable corrosion protective
coverings in the trenches with the anchoring end in the
excavated shaft carefully backfill the trenches and
4 cast concrete in the shaft
Tower Foundation Support
General
It is our understanding that the towers to support the suspension
cables will be situated on the level berm area at the toe of the
levee facing the river The foundation loading may be on the
order of 30 kips per tower It is further understood that
concrete spread footings similar to those existed before the
storm are desired to limit cost However these footings could
suffer damages in the future similar to those experienced in the
past
Our boring drilled at the levee berm disclosed compressible
materials at and below the bottom of levee fill It is assumed
that such compressible soil units may also exist at the tower
foundation area Therefore it is probable that some foundation
settlements may develop In addition a slow settlement should
be anticipated over a long period of time
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1278 00 Page 7
The settlements of new foundations would be different for various
types of foundations For spread footing foundations we
estimate that settlements of 4 to 6 inches may occur within the
first year and with additional long term settlements developing
over a period of several years For pile or caisson foundations
the settlements may be smaller However it is presumed that the
settlements of the aforementioned magnitude can be tolerated
Spread footings may be considered provided that the associated
higher risk of erosion and scouring can be accepted We believe
that if the footings are set back at least 20 feet from the river
bank and placed about 4 feet below the sea level these risks
would be much lower At least there will be some lead time
before the potential forthcoming danger is recognized and that
sone subsequent remedial actions can be taken
Spread Footings
For spread footing support a maximum allowable bearing pressure
of 1500 p s f may be used The footing bottom should be placed
at about Elevation 4 feet where the black medium sand is
anticipated The sand should be over excavated for another 2
feet and the over excavation replaced with coarse crushed rock
aggregate such as No 3 Fine type material to provide a firmer
base underwater for footing construction Tremie concrete may be
required

Cast In Place Pile Foundations
A more permanent foundation scheme which would provide better
resistance to scour and impact forces would be the cast in place
piles or caissons For the anticipated foundation load of 30
kips we believe that the use of 30 inch diameter cast in place
piles extending to a depth of 30 feet would provide adequate
support The caissons would need to be cased below water in
medium stiff organic silt and medium dense sands to prevent
caving of the drilled holes
The bottom and the sides of the holes should be cleaned and be
free of any loose or soft debris so that concrete will be in
contact with undisturbed natural deposit The withdrawing of
casing will be very critical to assure continuity and soundness
of the tremie concrete column particularly for the portion below

Driven pile foundations may also be desirable for the tower
foundation support We believe the pile can better withstand
the impact of floating debris and scouring From a construction
point of view the benefits of driven piles are that the
structural integrity of piles is better assured and the
supporting capacity can be better assessed during pile driving
The piles should consist of 12 inch square prestressed concrete
piles driven 25 to 30 feet below the existing grade to develop a
capacity of 15 tons each To resist lateral forces batter piles
should be considered

CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the footing excavations and backfill
operations or the caisson or pile installation be observed by a soils
engineer during construction to see that adequate bearing has been
obtained and the subsurface conditions are consistent with the
findings of our investigation Should soil conditions encountered
during construction be different from our field exploration
appropriate modifications to our recommendations would be needed
In particular the pile or caisson placement should be monitored
on a full time basis by a qualified soils engineer to verify that the
piles or caissons have been installed in accordance with our
recommendations
