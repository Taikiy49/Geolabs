In accordance with your request we have performed a geotechnical
engineering investigation for the above referenced project The
approximate location of the proposed site and its vicinity is shown on the

This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
The project involves the replacement of an existing wooden bridge
over Waimanalo Stream on the eastern flank of the station A new concrete
bridge is planned and it is our understanding that the concrete bridge
will span over a 90 foot distance without the need of a middle pier in the
stream

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our investigation is to explore the subsurface soil
and water level conditions to provide design parameters for the foundation
support of the structure
The scope of our investigation included drilling and sampling two
2 borings to 64 and 80 5 feet below the existing ground surface Also
included were laboratory testing of selected samples obtained engineering
analysis and evaluation of the field and laboratory data development of
the design recommendations and report preparation
Detailed description of our field exploration and laboratory test
results are presented in the appendices of this report
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the makai eastern side of the
Bellows Air Force Station and is close to the present day shoreline and
the mouth of the Waimanalo Stream Plate 1
The existing bridge is a two lane wooden structure about 90 feet
long and is supported by wooden piles along the stream crossing
The proposed replacement bridge and revised road alignment is
located immediately mauka west of the existing bridge Plate 2

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The two borings drilled indicate that in general about 42 to 60
feet of highly to moderately compressible layers of beach and or lagoonal
silty sand deposits exist below the upper 8 foot surficial crust of
gravelly silty fine sand The surficial crust is considered to he too
thin to allow the use of a footing type foundation for the abutments of
the proposed concrete bridge as significant settlements may occur due to
compression of the loose silty sand under the weights of the bridge
structure and its live loads
Therefore pile foundations are recommended to support the proposed
bridge structure
Approximately 15 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface a 10
to 12 feet thick layer of dense silty sand overlays 12 feet of soft sandy
silt This dense layer is not a suitable bearing stratum for piles
because of its limited and variable thickness Furthermore it may be
difficult to drive piles through this dense layer therefore
pre drilling through this cemented sand layer may be required
Detailed discussion and recommendations are presented below
Pile Foundations
The two borings one at each abutment encountered different soil
conditions and therefore two types of pile foundations are required A
hard coral layer suitable for end bearing was encountered in B 2 at the
north abutment Based on this boring high capacity end bearing piles are
recommended for this abutment The coral formation was not encountered in
B 2 at the south abutment therefore lower capacity friction piles are
recommended for this abutment

It should be noted that coral formations are often erratic
particularly near stream areas since the presence of fresh water will
inhibit the growth of the coral We recommend that additional probes be
drilled at about 10 foot spacing along each abutment to verify the
existence or non existence of the coral layer
General guidelines for the pile foundations are presented below
1 North Abutment A hard coral reef ledge approximately 17 feet
thick was encountered at a depth of about 40 feet below the
existing ground surface and is considered to be a suitable
bearing surface and stratum The average length of the
end bearing piles is estimated to be about 45 feet below the
existing ground surface Sixteen and one half inch octagonal
prestressed concrete piles with a 100 tons allowable pile
capacity could be used for the north abutment of the bridge
structure
2 South Abutment Suitable end bearing stratum was not
encountered to a depth of 80 feet below the existing ground
surface Therefore the piles would need to develop their load
carrying capacity primarily from frictional resistance between
the pile surface the medium dense silty sand and the very
stiff silty clay materials Some additional end bearing
resistance may be obtained from the very stiff silty clay which
the piles will be driven into
The average length of the friction piles is estimated to be
about 80 feet below the existing ground surface Sixteen and
one half inch octagonal prestressed concrete piles with 50 tons
allowable pile capacity could be used for the south abutment of
the bridge structure

Due to the variable soil conditions we recommend that a minimum
of 2 test piles for each abutment be first installed to
determine the drivability of the piles and to estimate the final
production pile casting lengths for the project The lengths of
these test piles should be about 50 feet and 80 feet for the
northern and southern abutments respectively
The piles should be driven with a hammer delivering a minimum of
about 30 000 foot pound of energy The type of hammer to be
used for production pile driving should be the same one used for
test pile installation
After the test piles are installed two piles should be selected
by the soils engineer for load testing to two times the design
loads of 100 and 50 tons respectively for the north and south
abutments in order to further verify the competency of the
bearing stratum
The 100 ton test piles should be driven to a final driving
resistance of about 15 blows per inch for the last two inches or
to about 100 blows for the last foot The 50 ton test piles
should be driven to a final driving resistance of 3 blows per
inch for the last 6 inches and to a minimum total penetration
depth of 80 feet below the existing ground surface or 70 feet
below the pile cap
The final pile driving criteria may be modified after the
completion of the test pile installation and the load test
program
Both the test and production pile driving should be monitored by
a geotechnical engineer to see that they are driven into
competent bearing formation

abutments has not been firmly established Assuming that the proposed

pressure The recommended lateral earth pressure for the two bridge

As the site is close to the ocean a high groundwater level of

Earthwork
Cuts up to about 10 feet may be required for the proposed bridge
abutments Relatively easy excavation is anticipated in the loose to
cemented silty sand
About 2 feet of fill may be required to raise the approach road
grade The site should be cleared of all debris old foundations and
asphaltic concrete pavements prior to filling
Select on site material such as the coral sand should be used as
fill and bridge abutment backfill The fill and backfill should be placed
in horizontal loose lifts of 8 inch maximum and compacted to a minimum of
90 of its maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557
All other fill and cushion materials should be approved by a soils
engineer prior to being used on the site
Pavements
Asphaltic concrete pavements may be utilized for the access ramp to
the bridge
The amount of on site excavation coral sand may not be sufficient
for pavement construction and imported fill materials may be required
For preliminary design the following pavement sections are recommended
assuming a subgrade CBR greater than 10

Design Review
The plans for foundations site grading and utility construction
should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written comments
prior to construction to see that the intent of our recommendations is
included
Construction Monitoring
The pile driving operations along with the predrilling of the pile
locations should be monitored on a full time basis by a qualified soils
technician under the direction of a soils engineer to verify that the
piles have been driven in accordance with our recommendations
It is recommended that excavation and backfill operations be
observed by the soils engineer during construction to see that adequate
compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface conditions are
consistent with the findings of our investigation
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

APPENDIX Cc
Laterally Loaded Pile Analysis
A A A A Ss Et tS en
Because of the anticipated lateral force acting on the piles a
laterally loaded pile analysis was performed The analysis was carried
out by using a finite difference program PILEDG which was derived from
the program COM624
The program adopts the P Y Curve approach and has the capability to
generate internally P Y curves given the soil data Since the lateral
soil modulus varies with the deflection experienced the computerized
analysis first assumes a modulus value then calculates the pile
deflection based on the assumed modulus The calculated deflection is
then used to determine the modulus from the P Y curves The calculated
modulus and the assumed modulus are then compared The procedure is
repeated until the new assumed and calculated moduli are essentially the
same
The results of the analysis showing the relationship of lateral
deflection at the top of the pile and the applied lateral force are
summarized on Plate C 1
