FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We understand that pipe bursting method will be used to install a new 14-inch 
diameter sewer line within the existing 8 and 10-inch sewer lines as part of the off-site 
improvements for the Cottages at Mauna Olu residential development. Detailed 
discussions and recommendations in support of the pipe bursting operation are 
presented in the following sections. 
Pipe Bursting 
Pipe bursting is a trenchless method that is widely used for the in-place 
replacement of existing pipes with a new pipe of the same or larger diameter because of 
deterioration of existing pipes and/or to increase the flow capacity. Pipe bursting is a 
pipe replacement alternative that usually reduces the impact to residents and/or 
surrounding areas when compared to open-cut rehabilitation techniques. The success 
of pipe bursting will be significantly influenced by the size of the new pipe, the invert 
depths of the existing sanitary sewer, presence of existing utilities and other 
improvements in close proximity to the existing sanitary sewer lines, and subsurface 
conditions along the sewer line alignment.   
The soil around the pipe (backfill and native soil) must be somewhat compressible 
to absorb the diameter expansion. Compressible soils are the ideal soils for pipe bursting 
because the outward ground displacements will be limited to an area surrounding the pipe 
alignment. Original backfill is the most suitable soil for bursting followed by (increasing 
difficulty) compressible clay, loose cobble, beach and running sand, densely compacted 
clay, and sandstone. Soils with long standup time allow the overcut (created by the 
expanded hole) to remain open for most of the bursting operation, thus reducing the 
friction force between the soil and the pipe. The overcut lowers the needed pulling forces

and consequently the axial stress on the new pipe during installation. Somewhat less 
favorable ground conditions for pipe bursting involve densely compacted soils and 
backfills, soils below the water table and expandable soils. Special soils such as highly 
expansive soils or collapsible soils will also cause problems. 
Pipe bursting is a method of pipe replacement that involves three main forces, 
named as burst, expansion, and drag, that must be overcome to accomplish installing a 
new pipe.  The first force is the force required to fracture or “burst” an existing pipe. 
Most types of pipes from 2 inches to more than 48 inches can be split or burst by pulling 
a hardened steel head through an existing pipe that is configured to focus energy on the 
pipe wall until it fails from the inside out.  
The second force is “expansion” which is the force required to expand the 
existing ground to allow insertion of the new pipe. In general, expansion of the existing 
hole (existing pipe inner diameter in inches) by 20 to 25 percent will be required to 
install the new pipe. Pipe bursting is the only pipeline rehabilitation method that allows 
the newly installed pipe to have the same or larger inside diameter (ID) than the existing 
pipe. 
This force will change depending on the ground conditions within the existing 
sewer trench. Because the pipe bursting process is replacing a pipe that was originally 
laid in a trench, the required information for properly estimating the force required to 
expand the soil to the required diameter needs to be obtained from the original trench 
design and construction. Therefore, the subsurface conditions within the existing sewer 
trench will be of significant value to a pipe bursting project.  Unfortunately, often times it 
is not practical to perform soil borings on top of an active sewer pipe due to the high risk 
damaging the existing sewer pipe, resulting in disruption to the sewer service to say the 
least, but more importantly the disastrous environmental impact of the leaked 
sewage.  However, it should be noted that borings drilled in close proximity to the 
existing sewer line should provide data that is comparative to those soils found inside 
the trench. 
The third force is referred to as “drag” and is the force of friction that is being 
exerted from the soil returning into contact with the new pipe as it is being installed. As 
the soil is expanded to allow installation of the new pipe, it is only in its expanded state 
for a short amount of time. Shortly after the expander head passes through the soil, the 
“relaxation” period starts which is putting the original soil back in contact with the new 
pipe. This contact will be the final state of the pipe in the ground as the 20 to 25 percent 
void is gone approximately 4 to 24 hours after the burst is complete, depending upon 
soil conditions.  
Each of the three forces can be managed and influenced through proper 
planning and understanding of the process. For example, the burst force can be directly 
influenced and reduced through varying burst head and tooling configuration. A vitrified 
clay pipe sewer line burst head is different from a cast iron burst head. An 
understanding of the tooling that is used during the burst is important and should be a

Retrieval and Insertion Pits 
The retrieval pits are for the removal of the pipe bursting pneumatic tool or hammer 
and are usually 10 feet long by 4 feet wide, located next to the manhole at the upstream 
side of the sewer line.  The insertion pits are generally 15 feet long by 3 feet wide at the 
pipe location and slope up at a gradient of three horizontal to one vertical (3H:1V) and are 
located on the downstream side of the manhole.

Site Grading 
We envision the majority of the grading work will involve excavation and backfilling 
of the retrieval and insertion pits. A Geolabs representative should monitor the site grading 
operations to observe the excavation and backfilling operations for conformance with the 
recommendations presented herein. 
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and 
backfilling of the retrieval and insertion pits. The contractor should determine the method 
and equipment to be used for pit excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal, 
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench 
shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the new sewer pipes for uniform 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of 
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill 
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the pit backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the 
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of compacted on-site soils (with a 
maximum particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be 
placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench 
backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
Because dense cobbles and boulders were encountered at relatively shallow 
depths during our field exploration, it appears that some of the pit excavation work within 
the Makaha Valley Plantation premises likely will involve excavation in the dense cobbles 
and boulders and may require hard ripping or the use of hoerams. The contractor must

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of about 20 and 30 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill equipment 
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual
