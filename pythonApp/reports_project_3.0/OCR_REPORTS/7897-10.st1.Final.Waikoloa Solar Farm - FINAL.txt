SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered volcanic ash and/or clinker materials 
underlain by basalt formation.  The thickness of the volcanic ash materials ranged from 
about 1.5 to 7 feet at the boring locations.  Volcanic ash materials generally consist of stiff 
sandy silts with some gravel.  In many areas, cobbles and boulders scattered at the 
ground surface were observed.  Therefore, we envision that the volcanic ash materials 
will likely contain various amounts of cobbles and boulders.  In some borings, volcanic 
ash materials were underlain by clinker materials with ash consisting of dense sandy 
gravel.   
Basalt formation was encountered from depths of about 1.5 to 12 feet below the 
ground surface and extended down to the maximum depth explored of about 17.5 feet 
below the ground surface.  Basalt formation encountered were generally slightly 
weathered and hard to very hard.  Based on the limited field exploration conducted at the 
site, it appears that the depth to basalt formation varies significantly across the project 
site.  It should be noted that cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should be 
expected during construction. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at 
the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new buildings planned. As an 
alternative, foundations for support of the new buildings planned also may consist of 
thickened-edge slab footings.  In general, the new structures should be constructed on 
similar foundation subgrade materials in order to reduce the potential for differential 
settlement and to provide for uniform support across the structures. In order to provide 
similar foundation subgrade materials for the new buildings, consideration should be given 
to over-excavating the subgrades within the new building pads by at least 2 feet. The 
over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted structural fill. 
Due to the presence of cobbles and boulders in the volcanic ash and/or clinker 
layers and basalt formation at relatively shallow depths, we envision that the traditional 
driven pile system may encounter difficult driving conditions in some areas.  Therefore, 
we recommend that the solar panel arrays be supported on a micropile system in areas 
where driving of the C-Pile or H-Pile sections are not feasible.  We recommend using a 
micropile system with an outside diameter of at least 5 inches to support the new solar 
arrays.  The micropiles should be drilled with an air-tracked drill rig or similar drill equipped 
with a drill bit of at least 5 inches in diameter.  For the solar array system, C-Pile or H-Pile 
sections, in lieu of central rebars, would serve as the reinforcement for the micropiles.

 GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Waikoloa Solar Farm Development project in the Waikoloa area on the 
Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, site grading, and pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
Project Considerations 
The proposed Waikoloa Solar Farm project is located off Waikoloa Road to the 
southeast of Waikoloa Village in the Waikoloa area on the Island of Hawaii.  We 
understand it is desired to develop a solar photovoltaic (PV) power plant at the site. The 
project site encompasses an area of over 500 acres of open, undeveloped land.  We 
envision that the project will consist of solar panels installed on ground mounted single 
axis tracker systems, various pads for electrical equipment such as switchgear, 
transformers, inverters, and overhead or underground electrical circuits.  
A preliminary exploration was previously conducted for the purpose of project 
planning and feasibility study. The purpose of this design-level field exploration was to 
further explore delineating the subsurface conditions encountered in the preliminary 
exploration. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that detailed foundation support 
for the solar array will be conducted by the vendor. Detailed information pertaining to the 
project layouts were not available at the time this report was prepared. However, we 
envision that structures will consist mainly of elevated solar panel arrays extending to 
about 6.5 to 7 feet above the existing grade with associated support facilities.

 SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both 
still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which 
makes up the north-central portion of the island. Based on available geologic information 
and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic 
lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were deposited 
during the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in Kohala and Mauna Kea, activity from 
the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over much of the island. This 
ash has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified by low in-situ densities 
and high field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field moisture content is 
sufficiently high, the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the soil temporarily loses 
strength when remolded and regains its strength after internal pore pressures dissipate). 
In more arid areas, the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even though field moisture 
contents can approach 100 percent of the dry unit weight. Our exploration indicates that 
the site is underlain by a surface mantle of this drier volcanic ash soil, which should 
transition to basalt formation and clinker at a deeper depth. 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe 
basalt type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava 
typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a 
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a 
denser and more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. 
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe

Site Description 
The project site is along the southern side of Waikoloa Road in the Waikoloa area 
of the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The project site encompasses an 
area of over 500 acres. 
The project site is generally an open, undeveloped land. Vegetation is sparse, 
mostly consisting of wild grasses and a few shrubs. A drainage gulch and power lines run 
along the center portion of the project site.  Unpaved access paths appear to have been 
cleared previously at some locations, including along the power line alignment. 
The surface materials generally consist of silty volcanic ash materials with varying 
amount of clinker. Boulders were also scattered around the ground surface in many 
areas. The ground surface is generally sloping down from east to west with local 
undulations. A topographic survey map was not available at the time this report was 
prepared.   
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, extending to depths of approximately 15.5 
to 17.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, six borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. P-1 through P-6, extending to a depth of approximately 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface were drilled for permeability testing. Two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were collected for laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR), R-value, Proctor, and thermal resistivity testing to 
evaluate the pavement support characteristics and heat transfer parameters of the 
near-surface soils. The approximate boring locations drilled and bulk samples collected 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

Building Foundations 
We understand that new one- to two-story buildings to house various equipment and 
appurtenances may be required for the project. Details of the new buildings and structural 
loading information were not available at the time this report was prepared. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new buildings planned. As an 
alternative, thickened-edge slab footings may also be considered for support of the new 
building structures planned.   
In general, the new structures should be constructed on similar foundation 
subgrade materials in order to reduce the potential for differential settlement and to 
provide for uniform support across the structures. In order to provide similar foundation 
subgrade materials for the new building structures, consideration should be given to 
over-excavating the subgrades within the new building pads by at least 2 feet. The 
over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted structural fill. 
Where the new structures will straddle varying subgrade conditions, special 
provisions should be made in these locations. A Geolabs representative should observe 
the footing subgrades to evaluate the need for special provisions.

An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on compacted structural fill, recompacted on-site 
materials or basalt rock formation. These bearing values are for supporting 
dead-plus-live-loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) when considering transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches 
below the lowest adjacent exterior grade. For footings bearing directly on the basalt rock 
formation, the footing bottom should be relatively level and clear of sharp points that may 
result in concentrated loading. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on slopes 
should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured from the 
outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is not less than 6 feet. 
Foundations located next to retaining walls, other foundations, utility trenches, or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the utility trench (or retaining wall). Alternatively, the footings 
should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines or bottom of the 
retaining wall. 
Due to the presence of volcanic ash soils, soft or pumping ground conditions may 
be encountered during construction, especially if the foundation excavations are exposed 
to inclement weather. Where soft or pumping ground conditions are encountered at the 
footing subgrades, the footing subgrades should be over-excavated until firm materials 
are encountered or to the maximum depth of about 2 feet and backfilled with compacted 
select borrow subbase materials (3-inch minus granular materials) wrapped on all sides 
with filter fabric. The select borrow subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. As an 
alternative, the footings may be extended down to bear directly on the firm materials.

recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than

Probing and Grouting 
As mentioned above, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the 
collapse of cavities below foundations, consideration may be given to implementing a 
program of probing and grouting of the building foundations during construction. 
Based on the relatively light structural loads anticipated for the new buildings, we 
believe the risk of potential collapse of cavities below the foundations would be low. 
Therefore, we believe probing and grouting may generally be omitted for the new 
structures planned at the site. However, if a higher degree of assurance against potential 
collapse of cavities below foundations is desired, a probing and grouting program below 
the foundations may be implemented. 
Where the probing and grouting program is implemented, we recommend drilling 
probe holes at 10-foot on centers for the continuous strip footings and/or thickened-edge 
slab footings. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each isolated spread footing

(or column) location. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should

If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation,

Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the site, we 
recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding. In 
addition, the probe drill should be available on-site until the probing and grouting 
operations are completed. The contractor also should be made aware that longer lag time 
between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the 
construction schedule. 
The probing and grouting program, where implemented, should be conducted 
under the observation of a Geolabs representative. This would allow our firm to monitor 
the presence of cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess 
grout take and/or changed conditions are observed. 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the 
new structures. For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. 
The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to 
serve as a capillary moisture break. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above 
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking.

We anticipate that the solar panel arrays will be supported on steel poles consisting

3.4.3 Micropile Foundation Settlements 
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression 
of the micropile member and subgrade response. We estimate the total settlement 
of the micropile-supported foundations to be 0.25 inches or less with differential 
settlements between columns supported on micropiles not exceeding about one-half 
of the total settlement. We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and 
should occur as the loads are applied.

3.5.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be

 Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Items of corrosion potential that are addressed in the subsequent 
subsections include the following: 
• Field Resistivity Surveys 
• Corrosivity Laboratory Testing 
• Thermal Resistivity Laboratory Testing 
3.10.1 Field Resistivity Surveys 
A total of 42 field resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum of about 40-foot 
intervals at six locations, designated as R-1 through R-6, using the in-situ Wenner 
4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson 
Electrical Laboratory, Inc. Locations of the resistivity tests are presented on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the 
average resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the underlying soil mass. The 
field resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average resistivity values of the 
soil mass at the approximate survey locations. Results of the field resistivity 
surveys are presented in Appendix D. It should be noted that the field resistivity 
survey may be influenced by temperature, weather conditions, soil moisture, 
existing underground utilities and other factors.

Based on the results of our field resistivity survey, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from about 20,000 to over 100,000 
ohm-cm, corresponding to a corrosion rating of 5 (mildly corrosive). It should be 
noted that the resistivity values of the subsurface soils varied with depth. 
Therefore, special attention should be given by the corrosion engineer when using 
the resistivity results provided in this report. 
3.10.2 Corrosivity Laboratory Testing 
Four sets of corrosivity testing, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), and 
Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) potential tests, were performed by our office and 
TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration.  A summary of the corrosivity tests is presented in Appendix B, Plate B-3. 
Based on the values of the chloride and sulfate content tested on the in-situ 
near-surface volcanic ash soils, we believe that either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) 
cement may be used for the concrete in contact with the ground planned. 
3.10.3 Thermal Resistivity Laboratory Testing 
We envision that electrical duct lines will be installed underground. Geotherm USA, 
our subcontractor, conducted laboratory soil thermal resistivity testing to evaluate the 
thermal characteristics of the in-situ near-surface volcanic ash soils encountered at 
the site. The sample was remolded to approximately 90 percent relative density 
according to ASTM D1557, Standard Test Methods for Laboratory Compaction 
Characteristics of Soil Using Modified Effort. Detailed discussions and test results are 
provided in the report entitled “Thermal Analysis of Native Soil Samples, Waikoloa 
Solar - Waikoloa, Hawaii (Project No. 7897-10),” dated December 3, 2019, as 
included in Appendix E.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling five 
boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, extending to depths of 
approximately 15.5 to 17.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  In addition, six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. P-1 through P-6, extending down to about 5 feet below the 
ground surface were drilled for the permeability testing. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-1. 
Five Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012) were performed on 
selected rock core samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the basalt rock 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-2. 
Four sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH Value (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), and 
Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) potential (SM 2580B) tests, were performed on selected 
soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate 
B-3. 
Two laboratory Resistance Value tests (AASHTO T 190) were performed on selected 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristic of 
the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the relationship between the moisture 
content and the dry density of the near-surface soils as fill materials.  The test results are 
presented on Plates B-8 and B-9.

Field Permeability Tests 
A total of six field permeability tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the 
permeability characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered.  Two constant head 
and four falling head tests were conducted at the site. The permeability tests were 
performed at locations as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The 
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to 
base calculation of the subsoil permeability. 
For the falling head test, the borehole was filled with water. The drops in water level 
with time were recorded. Results of the constant and falling head field permeability tests are 
presented on Plates C-1 through C-6.

Field Resistivity Surveys 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on November 7 and 8, 2019 for the field resistivity survey.  Forty-two soil resistivity 
surveys were conducted at six locations, designated as R-1 through R-6, using the in-situ 
Wenner 4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson 
Electrical Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to 
evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values 
of the soil mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, was assessed 
by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at the 
approximate survey locations shown on the site plan. The average soil resistance along the 
proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at 
equal spacing of generally 2.5, 5, 10, 15, 20, 30, and 40 feet. The depth of penetration of a 
resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (Nilsson Model 400) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the soil 
resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity (in ohm-
cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be computed. The 
evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented on Plate D-1. In 
addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test locations are presented 
in the graphs on Plate D-2. At the time of resistivity surveying, the temperature was about 
80º to 85º F. The existing ground surface was damp to moist. It should be noted that the soil 
resistivity may be influenced by temperature, weather conditions, existing underground 
utilities and other factors.
