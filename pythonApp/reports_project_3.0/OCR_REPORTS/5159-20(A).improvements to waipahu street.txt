GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING RECOMMENDATIONS
Retaining Structures
Based on the information provided we understand that a new retaining wall will be
constructed by cutting into the existing hillside to provide the necessary space and grade
adjustments for the roadway widening and improvement project The following general
guidelines may be used for preliminary design of the new retaining wall planned at the
project site
Wall Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration conducted
for the Leeward YMCA development we believe that the new retaining wall planned
for the project may be supported on shallow foundations such as continuous footings
An allowable bearing pressure up to about 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for design of shallow foundations bearing on the very stiff to hard in situ sandy
silts anticipated at the footing subgrade elevation This bearing value is for
as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general retaining wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In
addition wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum
depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For retaining wall
footings bearing on the on site materials the bottom of the footing excavations should
be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm
and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Soft
and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation may be
backfilled with the on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the bottom of footing may extend down to bear directly on the
underlying competent materials
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient
of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff on site soils Resistance
due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf assuming that soil around footings
is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations on
slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected

Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed directly around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the
walls discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site soils or select
granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill
should consist of low expansion impervious soils to reduce the potential for water
infiltration behind the walls In addition proper drainage and waterproofing of the
basement walls should be incorporated into the design and construction of this
project

cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious and other unsuitable

be properly disposed off site and or used in landscape areas where appropriate

Fill Materials
Fill materials behind the retaining wall should consist of non expansive select granular
material such as crushed coral or basalt The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell
of one percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Aggregate base and subbase materials required for the pavement section should
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Sections 30 and 31 of
the City and County of Honolulu Standard Specifications for Public Works
Construction September 1986 Imported fill materials should be tested by a qualified
geotechnical engineer for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery
to the project site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

Cut Slope
We understand that cut slope up to about 20 feet in vertical height is planned behind
the new retaining wall for the project construction Based on the very stiff to hard
subsoil conditions anticipated we believe that cut slopes planned for the project may
be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter In addition it is our opinion
that the 8 foot wide slope bench normally required for cut slopes greater than 15 feet
in vertical height may be omitted for this project
In general we recommend providing V ditches or earth berms at the top and bottom
of the cut slope In addition we also recommend placing erosion matting on the slope
to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the exposed slopes We recommend
immediately protecting cut slopes exposing the surface fill materials by appropriate
slope planting or other means to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the
exposed soils In the event that over cutting occurs consideration should be given to
leaving the slope as is rather than attempting to backfill the slope to the design grade
with sliver fills
