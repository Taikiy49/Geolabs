evaluation performed for the proposed new residence at 2525 Pali Highway in the
Nuuanu Valley area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations based on our site reconnaissance experience in the area and
literature research performed for the proposed residential project These preliminary
findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations are intended for the preparation
of the Conservation District Use Application CDUA only and are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of this transport of
sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly
sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base
levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits

The project site is located in Nuuanu Valley an amphitheater headed valley
carved by erosion into Koolau Basalt The base of this valley is filled with a thick
sequence of alluvium interbedded with deposits of lava and pyroclastics of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series

SLOPE STABILITY
We understand that many homes located along the hillsides in the valleys on the
eastern side of Oahu have experienced landslide movements These homes are
generally located on slopes underlain by highly plastic clay soils which is a major
component in the driving forces for slope failures According to the Soil Conservation
Services Soil Survey of the islands Molokai and Lanai State of
Hawaii the soil conditions at the project site are classified as a Kaena Series clay soil
which raises concerns regarding the potential for landslide or slope instability
First it should be noted that the landslide problems experienced by the many
homes in the valleys of Manoa and Palolo have occurred primarily
in the Lualualei Series clay which is different from the Kaena Series clay as classified
by the Soil Conservation Services for the subject site
Secondly it should be noted that the classifications presented in the Soil
Conservation Services publication generally apply to the near surface soils which is on
the order of about 3 to 5 feet thick Geologic maps of the area indicate that the
predominant soil deposit in the project area consists of Older Alluvium which is a stable
soil deposit An on site exploration of the subsurface soil conditions conducted in 1989
by our office confirmed the information provided by the Soil Conservation Service survey
and the geologic maps The field exploration disclosed that the project site was generally
underlain by a thin surface layer of clay about 1 to 3 feet thick over the more stable
alluvial soils and dense basalt rock formation at shallow depths

House Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we believe that
a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used to support the proposed new residence An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2 500 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the preliminary design of footings
bearing on the stiff alluvial soils or engineered fill materials This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
Previous experience with the surficial adobe clays indicate that the material
generally has a high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
especially if the soils have been remolded or compacted In order to reduce the effects of
the expansive clay soils resulting from moisture fluctuations we recommend that the
bottom of footings be embedded a minimum of 3 feet below the lowest adjacent finish
grade Where footings are located adjacent to or on slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 8 foot set back distance measured
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the slope face Continuous
foundations running parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped
footings
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete Excessive disturbance or over compaction of the adobe soils at the footing
subgrade level should be avoided to reduce the potential for subsequent heaving of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3914 00 Page 9
expansive clay soils If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of
footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underiying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent material If foundations are designed in
accordance with our recommendations we estimate total settlements to be less than
1 inch with differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings

Slab On Grade
The project site may be underlain by a surficial layer of adobe clay soils which is
considered to be highly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless
concrete slabs supported by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for
future distress to the lightly loaded slab on grade construction resulting from the shrinking
and swelling of these soils due to changes in moisture content To reduce the potential
for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on site expansive soils we
recommend that non expansive select capping fill material be provided for a minimum
depth of 24 inches below the bottom of the slab cushion The capping fill should also
extend laterally beyond the house slab perimeter a minimum of 24 inches
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3914 00 Page 10
The non expansive select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete The
select capping fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil above the
underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from
the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of
the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab due to
potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be incorporated below the concrete slab as a cushion
layer To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be
considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to
aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl
should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking The thickened
edges of slab adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches
below the lowest adjacent grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly
against the slab with non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water away from
the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations
Exterior concrete sidewalks around the house structure may be required for the
proposed project If sidewalks are planned around the house structure the capping fill
required under the house slab should also extend to the outside edge of the sidewalk
slabs The 24 inch capping layer recommended under the building slab may be reduced
to 12 inches for the sidewalks if some amount of slab cracking can be tolerated
Contraction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the sidewalk with
expansion joints at right angle intersections The subgrade soils beneath the sidewalks
should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report

Garage Slab and Concrete Driveway
For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slab and the driveway a 6 inch layer of base course over the 24 inches of non expansive
select granular capping fill is recommended for more uniform support The base course
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction We recommend that the concrete slab be designed by a structural
engineer for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a structural slab to
accommodate some amount of differential settlement For design of structural slabs a
modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the
compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the
slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The subgrade soils beneath the
garage and driveway slabs should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall

prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used

Bridge Foundations
We understand that a single span bridge is planned across Nuuanu Stream to
provide vehicular access to the proposed new residence Based on the subsurface
conditions anticipated at the project site and the potential for stream scour drilled shaft
foundations are recommended for support of the bridge abutments at the stream
crossing The drilled shaft foundations would derive support principally from adhesion
between the drilled shaft and the medium hard to hard basalt rock formation anticipated
at the project site For preliminary design purposes we recommend that drilled shafts
with a nominal diameter of approximately 2 to 3 feet and a minimum embedded length of
about 5 to 10 feet into the basalt rock formation be used for foundation support Based
on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the site we believe that an allowable
compressive load capacity of about 30 to 50 tons per shaft may be used for preliminary
design of the drilled shaft foundations A minimum center to center shaft spacing of
2 5 times the shaft diameter is recommended to avoid reduction in compressive load
capacity due to group action

Utility Trench
New utility connections will be required for the proposed new residence A
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the
pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for
the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement
of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select fill material The backfill
should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new residential structure should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation areas Excessive andscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
