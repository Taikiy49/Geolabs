performed for the Kunia Commercial Retail Center located in the Kunia area within the

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae
Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was
partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or
ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene
Epoch The dips of the lava beds generally are near horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees
from horizontal The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering
extending to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil
This in situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils
that grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty
material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks It should be noted
that spheroidally weathered boulders are typical in the Schofield Plateau The saprolite
grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth
2 2 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused solely by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes

3 1 Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the project
site we recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or
continuous footings be used for support of the new retail center buildings An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the
design of foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils and or compacted fills
This bearing value may be used to size the footings based on dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces In addition the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent ground level
The bottom of footing subgrades should be recompacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation the soft or and loose materials
should be over excavated to expose firm and unyielding materials and the excavation
should be backfilled with fill materials compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as

with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
In general footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a

3 2 Slabs On Grade
As mentioned previously the project site is underlain by a layer of residual soils
of about 5 to 14 feet thick Based on our laboratory test results the residual soil
materials exhibit a slight expansion potential for the materials near the existing ground
surface when subjected to moisture fluctuations The shrink swell potential of the in situ
materials increases to a moderate expansion potential with increasing depth below the
existing ground surface greater than about 6 to 8 feet below the existing ground level
To reduce the potential for future structural distress resulting from swelling of the
subgrade soils we recommend that the slab subgrades be properly prepared and
capped with select granular fill
In general we recommend that a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material be provided below the building slab areas The non expansive
select granular fill also should extend beyond the perimeter of the building a minimum of
24 inches or the select granular fill should extend to the edge of the concrete sidewalk
or concrete apron surrounding the building if applicable The non expansive select
granular fill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In addition we recommend that the subgrade soils be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement
of the select granular fill Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We also recommend that the building slabs on grades be reinforced with
reinforcing bars spaced at least 18 inches in each direction in lieu of the standard
welded wire fabric This recommendation is provided to reduce the potential for
structural distress due to differential movements of the slabs due to the nature of the
on site soils

Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed for
compaction of the fills
Cuts and fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to
one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against
sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face

3 5 Pavement Design
We understand that new parking areas are planned to serve the new retail
center In general we anticipate the vehicle loading for the parking lot of this project will
consist primarily of passenger vehicles light pick up trucks and handivans Based on
our field exploration and laboratory test results the near surface soils at the parking lot
appear to have CBR values ranging from about 3 to about 26 with expansion values of
about 0 4 and 0 3 percent Based on an average CBR value of 8 or greater we
recommend that the following flexible pavement section be used for preliminary design
purposes
Flexible Pavements for Parking Lot
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase
should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual
subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above
section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping
in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a
minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the subbase course layer to reduce
the potential for migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain
system be constructed adjacent to the paved landscaped areas

3 6 Underground Utilities
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be needed for the
project In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench
backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site clayey materials
or imported granular materials The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
3 7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the retail center structures should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the building structures and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling nine borings extending to depths of approximately 10 5 to 24 8 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
