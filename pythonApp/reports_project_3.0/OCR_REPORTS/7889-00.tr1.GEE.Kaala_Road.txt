SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Repair Kaala Road, Gate 3 to the 
Summit project. Based on our field exploration, the pavement subgrade soils from Gate 3 to 
Culvert 32 consist of fine-grained fills and residual soils with a minimum CBR value of about 
5 and the pavement subgrade soils from Culvert 32 to the summit consist of fine-grained 
fills and saprolite, clinker and basalt with a minimum CBR value of about 10.  
Most of the pavement distress for Kaala Road from Gate 3 to the summit appears 
to be due to delamination of the top overlay, which is about 0.5 to 2 inches in thickness. 
We anticipate areas with significant pavement distress will be spot fixed instead of 
reconstructing the entire roadway.  Based on these considerations, most of the pavement 
should be able to be repaired with a mill and overlay scheme consisting of milling off the 
delaminating overlay plus an additional 3/8 inches of asphaltic concrete. After the AC is 
milled and cleaned, a layer of GlasGrid or equivalent material should be tacked on before 
replacing the removed AC (min. 2”).

the surface to the subgrade (such as with alligator cracking), we recommend reconstructing 
the pavement using the following minimum flexible pavement section:

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Repair Kaala Road, Gate 3 to the Summit project, located in 
Mount Kaala, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of pavements 
and earthwork only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, we understand the government wants to 
rehabilitate Kaala Road, which is approximately 7 miles in length and connects Farrington 
Highway in Mokuleia (Elev. 20’) with the Kaala Air Force Station (AFS) at the summit of 
Mount Kaala (Elev. 4,025’). The road traverses both public and private land. We 
understand the project is to be split into a lower and upper section from near Culvert 42.  
1.2

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger Koolau 
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms 
the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas formed a 
broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials, which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series. 
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower, middle, and upper volcanic 
members. The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that 
built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano. The middle member consists of rock 
that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera. The upper member is a 
relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its 
history of evolution. In-situ chemical weathering of the lava flows has formed a mantle of 
residual soils, which grade to saprolite with depth. In general, saprolite is composed 
mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The 
saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth.  
The summit of Mt. Kaala, the highest point in the Waianae Mountain Range, is a 
relatively level plateau, approximately 1 mile across, and is held up by nearly horizontal, 
thick, unusually resistant lava beds of the upper member. Due to its flatness, the summit 
was historically a swamp due to little dissection and overall poor drainage characteristics. 
2.1 
Existing Site Conditions 
The lower section (from Gate 3 to Culvert 42) of Kaala Road is approximately 
3.2 miles in length and is paved with asphaltic concrete that has been overlaid at least 
twice. The lower section has experienced some delamination of the overlays and 
localized sections of light alligator cracking.

The upper section (from Culvert 42 to Summit) of Kaala Road is approximately

2.2 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 38 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 138, extending to depths of about 4.0 to 7.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. In addition, seven bulk samples of the near-surface 
soils, designated as Bulk Nos. 1 through 7, were collected for laboratory testing to 
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the material. The approximate boring 
and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The subsurface conditions along Kaala Road can be generalized into 
three different areas: Gate 3 to Culvert 32; Culvert 32 to Culvert 78; Culvert 78 to the 
Summit. 
2.2.1 Gate 3 to Culvert 32 
From Gate 3 to Culvert 32, our field exploration encountered an existing pavement 
section consisting of 3.5 to 5.5 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying 4.5 to 
7.5 inches of base course-like material. In this area, the pavement section overlaid 
fine-grained fills and residual soils of stiff to hard consistency. 
2.2.2 Culvert 32 to Culvert 78 
From Culvert 32 to Culvert 78, our field exploration encountered an existing 
pavement section consisting of 3 to 6 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying 4.5 to 
9 inches of base course-like material. In one boring, the pavement section was 
thinner with 2 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying 3 inches of ACB and 5 inches 
of base course-like material. From Culvert 32 to Culvert 78, the pavement section 
generally overlaid fine-grained fills and saprolite, clinker and basalt (rock 
formation).

2.2.3 Culvert 78 to Summit 
The roadway above Culvert 78 was explored with one boring. In addition, 
one boring was drilled at the helipad area.  
The existing pavement section in this area was 4.5 inches of asphaltic concrete 
overlying 5.5 inches of base course-like material. Beneath the pavement section 
was a thin layer of granular fill overlying medium stiff to stiff fine-grained residual 
soils.  
At the helipad, the pavement section consisted of 2 inches of heavily deteriorated 
asphaltic concrete overlying about 10 inches of granular fill. The granular fill was 
underlain by about 3 feet of fine-grained fill of medium stiff to stiff consistency. 
Beneath the fill was a soft fine-grained marsh deposit. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, 
time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Most of the pavement distress for Kaala Road from Gate 3 to the summit appears 
to be due to delamination of the top overlay, which may be caused by several factors 
including (but not limited to): 
1. 
Overlay is too thin; 
2. 
The bonding surface for the overlay was too heavily oxidized when placing 
the overlay; and 
3. 
There was an issue with the asphaltic concrete, such as a loose mixture or 
inadequate tack-coating.  
In general, the top overlays are about 0.5 to 2 inches in thickness. Based on the 
observed condition of the pavement, we anticipate areas with significant pavement 
distress will be spot fixed instead of reconstructing the entire roadway. Based on these 
considerations, most of the pavement should be able to be repaired with a mill and overlay 
scheme, as described below. Areas with visible alligator cracking (such as localized areas 
on the lower portion of Kaala Road) or where milling encounters the subgrade should be 
fully reconstructed.  
3.1 
Pavement Design 
The design procedures used in determining the new flexible and rigid pavement 
sections discussed herein are based on the design guidelines presented in 
UFC 3-201-01 Civil Engineering, UFC 3-250-01 Pavement Design for Roads and Parking 
Areas and UFC 3-260-02 Pavement Design for Airfields. We have assumed anticipated 
traffic for Kaala Road to consist of 12 heavy-duty pick-up trucks daily and two HS20 trucks 
(or similarly loaded large vehicles, such as a water truck) weekly over the life of the project 
(20 years).  
Pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 
95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of aggregate base and/or subbase 
course. It should be noted that the project site is located in a high rainfall environment 
throughout the year; therefore, the on-site soils likely will be constantly in a very moist to

wet condition. Therefore, aeration and drying of the pavement subgrade soils is likely 
impractical due to the high rainfall environment. In areas where high-moisture content 
soils are encountered and 95 percent relative compaction of the subgrade is impractical, 
we recommend the subgrade soils be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) 
drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to compact the exposed subgrade soils. The 
drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. We recommend 
proof-rolling the pavement subgrade without the use of vibration. Use of vibration during 
proof-rolling will induce a pumping condition of the subgrade soils with high in-situ 
moisture. 
If the subgrade is compacted as described above, it may be impractical to achieve 
100 percent relative compaction of the aggregate base course as required by the UFC. 
Therefore, we recommend the aggregate base course be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction, in this report, refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As a minimum, the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients 
should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage 
structures. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after 
construction.  In addition, aggregate base course for flexible pavements should conform 
to the material guidelines of UFC 3-250-01 and UFC 3-260-02, as applicable. 
3.1.1 Gate 3 to Culvert 32 
In general, the pavement condition is much better from Gate 3 to Culvert 32, 
compared to Culvert 32 to the summit. Pavement distress includes localized areas 
of pavement delamination and a few areas of light alligator cracking. Based on the 
available subsurface information and available laboratory test results, a CBR value 
of 5 was used in the pavement design analysis.  
Despite the relatively good condition of the pavement in this area, the pavement will 
likely not last for a design period of 20 years due to the thicknesses of the existing 
pavement section and relatively low subgrade CBR values. In general, the pavement

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations presented herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the borings drilled. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the drilled borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided in this 
report. 
The boring and bulk sample locations indicated in this report are approximate, 
having been taped from existing visible features at the project site. Elevations noted on 
the borings were obtained by locations from Google Earth. The boring and bulk sample 
locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the 
methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be 
noted that groundwater fluctuations may occur due to variation in rainfall, seepage, 
temperature, and other factors. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Kiewit Infrastructure West 
Co. for specific application to the design of the Repair Kaala Road, Gate 3 to the Summit 
project, located at Mount Kaala, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii in accordance with 
generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices. No warranty is 
expressed or implied. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil and/or rock conditions 
are encountered commonly during construction. Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such 
as soft deposits, hard layers, cavities, or perched groundwater, may occur in localized 
areas and may require additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
38 exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 138, extending to depths of 
about 4.0 to 7.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid stem augers. The borings were drilled 
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The 
Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the 
Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society 
for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-38. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented on the logs of borings have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seventeen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the samples tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) are indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-3. 
Seven Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on the 
bulk soil samples to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry 
density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through 
B-10. 
Seven California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on bulk soil 
samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-11 through B-17.

Road Preparation
Complete all crack sealing, pothole filling, base repairs, leveling 
course applications, etc.
Road Surface Condition
Surface must be:
• Dry
• Clean and dust-free
Asphalt pavement surface temperature must be between 
21°C and 60°C* (70°F and 140°F**). On asphalt surfaces less 
than 24 hours old, the surface temperature must be between 
21°C and 46°C (70°F and 110°F). GlasGrid TF contains a tack 
film designed to replace traditional tack coat and enhance the 
bond between the fresh pavement surface and the overlay 
asphalt. A light tack coat may be required over an aged, 
oxidized surface prior to installing the GlasGrid TF product..
Product Preparation
In order to preserve adhesive properties GlasGrid TF must 
be stored inside a dust-free environment and kept dry at the 
job site.
A GlasGrid System representative should be present during 
the initial installation of the GlasGrid TF.
Two Easy-to-Place Methods
	A.	Using a Fabric Tractor
1. Load roll onto front of tractor. Always load the 
red-colored end of the core on the right side of the 
installation vehicle to allow placement of mesh with 
the self-adhesive side down and the tack film on top.
2. Roll only with rubber-tired roller to activate adhesive. 
Roller must be kept clean.
3.	Perform paving operation.
	B.	 Manual Placement Procedure
1.  Load roll on back of pick-up truck. To ensure that the 
mesh will always unroll with the sticky side face 
down, load so that the red-colored end of the core is 
on the same side of the truck each time. Tack film should 
always be on top.
2. Secure the roll with locking device.
3. Secure start of roll on pavement as the truck slowly 
moves away.
4. For shorter pieces pull mesh off roller to desired length. 
Cut with utility knife and lay by hand.
5. Roll only with rubber tired roller. Roller must be 
kept clean.
6. Perform the paving operation.
Test for Proper Adhesion
1.	Cut 1 mC (approx. 1 sq yd) of GlasGrid TF.
2. Place on area to be paved.
3. Activate self-adhesive glue by rolling with a rubber tired 
roller or by walking on the sample.
4. Insert hook of spring balance through film and under 
center of GlasGrid TF piece.
5. Pull upwards until GlasGrid TF starts 
to pull from the surface.
6. Record results in kg (or lb).
7. If 9 kg (approx. 20 Ib) or more, OK to 
pave. Stop immediately if GlasGrid TF 
moves or ripples. 
	 If less than 9 kg (approx. 20 Ib), do 
not pave with the GlasGrid System 
without corrective action to addres 
the issue.
Important Issues
1.	To activate the tack film, asphalt must be placed at a 
minimum of 140°C (284°F) and the existing leveling 
surface temperature must be between 21°C and 60°C 
(70°F and 140°F). 
2. Because fiberglass is considered a skin irritant, workers 
should wear gloves while handling GlasGrid TF products.
3. To cut to desired length, lay GlasGrid TF over ironworks, 
roll, and then cut using a sharp utility knife.
4. GlasGrid TF must be applied without ripples. Sufficient  
tension during application will eliminate this problem.
5. Overlap at end of roll joints 75–150 mm (3 in.–6 in.). 
Ensure that the overlaps are shingled in the direction 
of paving and that these overlaps receive an application 
of tack.
6. Overlap longitudinal joints 25–50 mm (1 in.–2 in.).
7. Prior to paving, only construction and emergency 
vehicles shall be allowed to drive on the installed 
GlasGrid TF. If the GlasGrid/tack film becomes damaged, 
replace the material in this area.
*All metric values are nominal.
**All imperial values are approximate. 
†Preferred method is mechanical with tractor.
