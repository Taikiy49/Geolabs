INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Fuel Storage Tanks in Diego Garcia
Atoll The location of Diego Garcia is shown on the Area Map Plate 1

The general location of the site is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 2 The exploration was performed in general accordance with our
proposal dated August 21 1989
This report summarizes the findings from the supplemental exploration
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses based on the data obtained during the exploration These
recomendations are intended for design input and are not intended for
construction bidding estimation purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves the construction of five BBL above
ground steel POL storage tanks at the site Three 3 of the tanks will
be constructed initially with the remaining two 2 tanks scheduled for
construction at a later time The tanks will be about 150 feet in
diameter and 48 feet in height
The tanks will be enclosed by concrete containment dikes with a
lining system Also a 150 000 gallon concrete water tank concrete foam
pump house concrete oil water separator and concrete ocean outlet
outfall are included in the project
Site grading would involve about 8 feet of additional fill to raise
the finish grade to about elevation 12 to 13 feet MSL
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the current exploration was to provide supplemental
subsurface data to the previous 1988 exploration results by using
various types of drilling and sampling techniques
The previous exploration was conducted at the site in 1988 utilizing
the auger drilling method Based on the 1988 exploration data it
appeared that the subsoils consisted of thick deposits of loose to medium
dense coralline sands and gravels However there were some questions

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated on Diego Garcia an atoll of the Chagos
Archipelago in the Indian Ocean The atoll is a roughly footprint shaped
island approximately seventeen 17 miles in length with a central lagoon
of up to about seven 7 miles in width
The proposed fuel storage site is in the northern part of the western
arm of the atoll westerly of DG 1 Main Road The site encompasses
approximately eighteen 18 acres and is bounded by the existing POL
storage facility to the north DG 1 Main Road to the east the
ROICC Seabee compound Alpha Site to the south and DG 2 Coral Road to the
west
The topography of the site is nearly level with ground surface
elevations ranging between five 5 and seven 7 feet MSL The northern
and eastern portions of the site are vegetated The western portion of
the site is an open field with a thin cover of grasses The area is
believed to have been used as a construction staging yard

Tectonic Setting and Earthquakes
Diego Garcia is an atoll of the Chagos Arcipelago which is on the
crest of the Chagos Laccadive Rise near where the rise splits off from the
Mid Indian Ridge The activity of the region is evidenced by the Indian
Subcontinental Plate being thrust into the Asian Continental Plate forming
the Himalaya Mountain Range
Most seismic activity in the Indian Ocean occurs in two bands one
along a line from southern China through Burma Sumatera and Timor the
second along the mid oceanic ridge Diego Garcia is located east of the
seismically active mid ocean Central Indian Ridge

Geology
Diego Garcia is a footprint shaped atoll of the Chagos Archipelago
The atoll is about 850 miles south of India in the Indian Ocean
The origin of coral atolls was first postulated by Charles Darwin in
1842 His theory was that an atoll began as a fringing reef around a
volcanic island The island begins to subside tectonically at a slow rate
so that the coral growth maintains a reef line As the island subsides
and becomes smaller the reef grows larger and becomes a barrier reef
When the island finally disappears below sea level the upward growing
reef encloses only a lagoon and becomes an atoll
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1977 00 M Page 6
The subsurface conditions in a coral atoll vary substantially in both
the horizontal and vertical dimensions Much of the large scale vertical
variation is the result of worldwide glacioeustatic sea level changes that
occurred during the Pleistocene Ice Age One significant low stand of
the sea was a stand of relatively long duration at about sixty 60 feet
below present sea level Throughout the world many of the coral atolls
were eroded to this low stand and the materials above this level are the
result of rapid growth and accumulation of coralline deposits since the
mid to late Pleistocene The horizontal variation is largely dependent
upon the environment in which the coralline materials grew or were
deposited
Exploration Program
In general the exploration program consisted of drilling and
sampling twenty four 24 borings to depths ranging between 16 5 and 99 5
feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 and 4 Drilling sampling
and field testing techniques included triple tube coring HQ Wireline
standard penetration testing SPT and cone penetration testing CPT at
the tank sites
The previous exploration utilized auger drilling method with SPT
sampling at about 5 foot intervals and limited NX coring The SPT blow
counts indicated loose to medium consistency in the coralline materials
The NX coring also indicated the lack of structure in the coralline
materials
The purpose of the current supplementary exploration was to determine
whether the SPT blow counts accurately represent the structural
characteristics of the materials and whether the coralline materials have
a skeletal structure which would increase the strength of the in situ
material In order to accomplish this the field exploration program
included several different exploration methods as follows

Conventional auger rotary borings with SPT sampling were
conducted to correlate the blow count consistency with the
previous exploration data
Cone penetration testing CPT was conducted to obtain a
continuous profile of the consistency of the subsoils The CPT
was performed by hydraulically pushing an electric cone
penetrometer into the ground while continuously recording the tip
resistance and shaft friction
Continuous penetration resistance was measured adjacent to some
of the borings in order to obtain a continuous record of the blow
counts required to penetrate the ground without drilling
disturbances The continuous penetration was conducted by
driving a conical or blunt point attached to AW rods with a
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blows to drive the rods
every 6 inches were recorded
Triple tube coring was utilized in the exploration to attempt to
obtain continuous samples of the coralline materials to allow
visual examination of the structure of the materials The triple
tube system is superior to the conventional core barrel since the
inner tube greatly reduces disturbance from the drilling fluid as
the barrel is advanced and the disturbance due to sample removal
from the barrel
Borings were also drilled and sampled on the seaward and lagoon sides
of the proposed fuel storage area to provide a general overview of
subsurface conditions across the atoll
Four 4 borings were drilled within the existing fuel tank area
adjacent to the project site in order to correlate the subsoil conditions
with the reported performance of the tank structures during the recent
earthquakes

Subsoil Conditions
Based on the current exploration results the subsoils consist of a
surface fill layer of three 3 to eight 8 feet thick consisting of
dense tannish white coral sand with gravel overlying about ten 10 feet
of medium dense beach sand deposits
Below the beach deposits the borings encountered predominantly very
weak coral algal formation with sand filled open structures Although the
samples recovered by standard penetration tests SPT visually appeared to
be loose sand with coral gravel interpretation based on results from
other drilling and sampling techniques have indicated that some structure
is present and that the sand and silt are materials which have filled in
between the coral algal structures

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The previous interpretation of the data obtained in our original
1988 exploration was based on shallower borings drilling with auger
methods and sampled at greater intervals In the current exploration the
combination of different drilling and sampling techniques particularly
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1977 00 M Page 9
the use of triple tube coring have aided in our interpretation of the
exploration results and provided a better understanding of the subsurface
conditions at the site
Based on the supplemental exploration results it appears that the
coralline material has some structure Therefore the thickness of the
potential liquefiable loose sand layers interpreted from the previous
1988 drilling data may be reduced Accordingly the potential
tank ground settlements under static and dynamic loading conditions are
also reduced
Site Grading
In general the site grading would consist of six 6 to eight 8
feet of additional fill at the tank sites The finish elevation will be
about 12 to 13 feet above Mean Sea Level
It is our understanding that the required fill material will be
obtained from an existing dredged fill stockpile borrow site located
off site to the east of the runway The laboratory tests performed
during our 1988 exploration indicate that the borrow soils are classified
as SP SM according to the Unified Soil Classification System Based on
the laboratory test results and our visual observations at the site we
believe that these dredged coral sands are a good source of fill material
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading
limits should be cleared of vegetation debris rubbish and other
deleterious materials These materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1977 00 M Page 10
Fill areas should be scarified to a depth of six 6 inches moisture
conditioned and recompacted to 90 of its maximm dry density per
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Any loose pumping yielding or soft
spots encountered should be over excavated to firm ground and then
replaced with compacted non expansive select material
Fill Placement
a ae
Since up to eight 8 feet of fill is planned at each tank site we
believe a properly compacted structural fill pad may further reduce
the potential differential settlements experienced by a tank under
static and earthquake loadings
Due to the coralline nature of the fill material it is anticipated
that some re cementation effects will form within the compacted fill
if proper moisture content is achieved at the time of compaction We
recommend that the moisture content of the fill material should be
within 3 to 5 percent on the wet side of the optimm moisture content
at the time of compaction
Estimated static settlements due to the eight 8 feet of additional
fill are on the order of less than one 1 inch Because of the
sandy nature and relatively high permeability of the subsoils we
anticipate that the settlements would occur rapidly It is estimated
that 95 percent of the static settlements may occur within about one
1 month after fill placement
All fills should be placed in maximm ten 10 inch thick loose level
lifts and compacted to a minimm of 95 of its maximm dry density
per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78

Slopes
A slope ratio of 2 horizontal to 1 vertical 2H 1V is planned for
the perimeters of the embankment fills We believe that this slope
ratio is suitable for the proposed construction
The finished cut slope surfaces should be compacted to a minimm of
95 compaction per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 In order to achieve
the proper compaction we recommend that the slopes be constructed by
overfilling beyond the design slope limits and cutting back to the
design slope
The slopes should be vegetated as soon as practicable to minimize
erosion
Tank Foundation Design
Based on the current exploration results it appears that properly
designed shallow ring type of foundations may be used to support the
flexible steel tanks It is anticipated that the ring foundations will
bear on compacted structural fill An allowable bearing value up to 3000
p s f with a minimum footing embedment of 24 inches may be used for
foundation design The bearing value may be increased by one third when
temporary wind and seismic loads are considered
Estimated static settlements due to the storage tank loads are on the
order of less than one 1 inch total and one half 1 2 of an inch
differential provided that the fills are properly compacted
For an earthquake of Magnitude 7 6 on the Richter Scale such as the
1983 earthquake which occurred at about 50 kilometers from Diego Garcia
estimated dynamic ground settlements are on the order of one 1 to four
4 inches
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1977 00 M Page 12
We believe that the above settlements may be tolerable for the
proposed storage facility as long as some flexibility in the line
fittings connections to the tanks are provided so that necessary
maintenance may be performed should dynamic settlements occur during
earthquake loadings
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive
earth pressures developed against the buried vertical faces of the
foundation system and by frictional resistance between the bottom of the
tanks foundations and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 p c f This assumes that the soil around
the foundations is well compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may
be used
Containment System
The containment systems around each tank will consist of a concrete
berm or wall about 7 to 9 feet high and a enclosure lining between the
concrete berm and the tank structure
The concrete berm or wall may be designed for an allowable bearing
pressure of 2500 p s f on compacted fill The bottom of the berm footing
should be embedded a sufficient depth to provide an 8 foot minimm
set back distance between the outer edge of the footing and the nearest
adjacent slope face
A passive soil resistance of 350 p c f and a coefficient of friction
of 0 4 may be used to determine the lateral resistance of the structure
The passive soil resistance should be neglected for sloping areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1977 00 M Page 13
It is our understanding that two 2 systems are currently being
considered for the enclosure lining One system consists of an impervious
geotextile liner impregnated with bentonite clay The liner will be
installed first and wetted to let the bentonite clay swell A layer of
soil such as base course will be used on top of the clay The other
system consists of a thick bentonite clay layer overlain by base course
It is our opinion that the system of an impervious geotextile liner
with impregnated bentonite clay may be appropriate for the containment
purpose provided that the geotextile is protected from puncture during
installation The top soil layer should be compacted to a minimm of 90
compaction per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The design and field
installation should generally follow the manufactures specifications
The thick clay layer system may also be suitable provided that the
clay soil is placed properly and not allowed to dry out and develop
shrinkage cracks The clay soils should be moistened to about 3 to 5
above optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimm of 95 of the
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 We have some concern
that it may be difficult to prevent the clay from drying and shrinking
thereby increasing the permeability of the layer Therefore a minimum
clay layer thickness of 3 should be used

For the light pump house building and 150 000 gallon water tank
spread footing foundations may be used to support the structures Should
liquefaction occur during a strong earthquake loading it is anticipated
that the differential settlements to be experienced by the pump house and

embedded a minimm of 18 inches into the dense surface fill The bottom
of the footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of 95

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from the supplemental field borings
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The locations of the field borings were approximately determined by
taping from objects indicated on the topographic map undated by Control
Point Surveying Engineering Inc and the composite map provided
Elevations of the data points were interpreted from the topographic map
The physical locations of the field data points should be considered
accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field
data points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors

APPENDIX c
Cone Penetration Tests
a se ne A nn
Six 6 electric cone penetration tests were performed in the
coralline material near Boring 108 110 and 113
The cone penetrometer test consists of hydraulically pushing a steel
cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 10 square
centimeters connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 150 square
centimeters into the ground The tip and side friction resistance induce
during penetration are measured by strain gages within the instrument and
are recorded versus the depth of penetration
The values of the tip resistance and friction ratio sleeve friction
over tip resistance can be correlated to soil strength and
classification The results and the interpretation are shown on Plates
C 1 through C 6
