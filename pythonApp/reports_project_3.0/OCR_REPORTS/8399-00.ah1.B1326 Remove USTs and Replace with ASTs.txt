SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered medium dense/stiff fill materials 
overlying soft/loose lagoonal deposits and stiff alluvium extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 22 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was 
encountered at depths of about 7.2 and 7.7 feet below the existing ground surface. It 
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, 
storm surge conditions, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water 
runoff, and other factors. 
We recommend using a shallow foundation system to support the new above-
ground fuel storage tanks. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf may be 
used for the design of the footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils. The bottom 
of the footing excavations should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide 
a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel 
and concrete. 
The project site is generally underlain by near-surface sandy and/or clayey soils 
with low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To 
reduce moisture changes in the concrete slab subgrade soils, we recommend placing a 
minimum 12-inch thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill material (capping fill) 
below the concrete slabs. The non-expansive select granular fill should extend a 
minimum of 12 inches beyond the edges of the concrete slabs.  
We understand excavations and subsequent backfilling are required to remove 
the existing underground fuel storage tanks and to install new piping and conduits. 
General fill materials and/or non-expansive select granular fill materials should be 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction, as appropriate. The compaction requirement should be increased to at 
least 95 percent relative compaction within the areas subjected to vehicular traffic. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment with non-vibratory methods. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the B1326 Design/Construction to Remove USTs and Replace with ASTs 
project in the Naval Base area of Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, and site grading only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at the existing Navy Exchange (NEX) Building 1326 
(B1326) located in Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam (JBPHH) on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The project site is generally located along the east side of North Road between 
the intersections with Neches Street and Waterfront Street.  
Based on the information provided, it is proposed to remove the two existing 
10,000-gallon underground fuel storage tanks (USTs) and replace with two new 
12,000-gallon above-ground fuel storage tanks (ASTs) at the same location. We 
understand the existing UST system removal includes the two tanks, subsurface piping 
and connections, and electrical components. We understand that it is not desired to 
reconstruct the pavement areas surrounding the tanks and connecting utilities, and the 
asphaltic concrete patching is to match the existing pavement thickness. 
Based on our field observations and the plans provided within the RFP 
documents, the project site is relatively flat with ground surface elevations ranging 
between +8 and +10 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The existing USTs were partially 
above-ground, covered by concrete gunite.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex 
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level 
changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following 
the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were 
deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and 
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level 
during the late Pleistocene era, the eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, 
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff (locally known as “mudrock”) layers. 
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of volcanic tuff.  
During periods of insignificant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal 
sediments. During stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed distinct 
ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during 
that period. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. 
During the recent epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits. These deposits were 
subsequently covered with man-made fills placed during the middle portion of the 
twentieth century for land reclamation projects during the construction of the existing

Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two exploratory borings in

Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site will be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for 
the potential for soil liquefaction. For seismic design considerations, UFC 3-301-01, 
Structural Engineering, references the International Building Code, 2018 Edition 
(IBC 2018) and American Society of Civil Engineers 7-16, Minimum Design Loads and 
Associated Criteria for Buildings and Other Structures, 2016 Edition (ASCE 7-16).

Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore 
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. Soils 
most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and 
loose silts with little cohesion. 
Based on our analyses, it appears that the soft/loose lagoonal deposits below the 
surface fill generally have a factor of safety of less than 1.0 against soil liquefaction and 
is susceptible to potential soil liquefaction. Therefore, the project site could be subjected 
to appreciable seismically induced ground settlements (on the order of about 4 inches) 
in the event of soil liquefaction during a strong earthquake (M6+).  
Based on the subsurface materials encountered and our engineering analyses, 
the project site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils. Therefore, the project site 
would need to be classified as Site Class “F.” However, a site-specific response 
spectrum analysis is not required in accordance with Section A10 of the project RFP 
documents. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site and the 
geologic setting of the area, we believe the project site may be classified from a seismic 
analysis standpoint as a “Soft Clay Soil Profile.” Therefore, we believe the seismic 
design of the new ASTs may be based on a Site Class E soil profile in accordance with 
Chapter 20 of ASCE 7-16. 
Based on a Site Class E soil profile, the following seismic design parameters 
were estimated and may be used for the seismic design of the project.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered medium dense/stiff fill materials 
overlying soft/loose lagoonal deposits and stiff alluvium extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 22 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was 
encountered at depths of about 7.2 and 7.7 feet below the existing ground surface. It 
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, 
storm surge conditions, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water 
runoff, and other factors. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and the anticipated 
light structural loads, we envision the new ASTs may be supported on shallow 
foundations. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the project design are 
presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Shallow Foundations 
Based on the information provided, we understand the two new ASTs will be 
straddle supported by a strip footing at each end. Two footings of about 22 feet long, 6 feet 
wide and 18 inches deep will be constructed to support the two ASTs. Based on the 
subsurface conditions encountered and anticipated relatively light loads, an allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design 
of the footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils. The bearing value recommended 
is for dead plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such 
as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or 
on the sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings (base of 
footings) to the face of the slope. Foundations next to other subsurface structures, 
including but not limited to existing utility trenches and/or easements, should be embedded 
below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the 
subsurface structure, or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our experience within the 
project vicinity, the project site is generally underlain by near-surface sandy and/or 
clayey soils with low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture 
fluctuations. Therefore, we recommend placing a minimum 12-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill material (capping fill) below the slabs to reduce 
moisture changes in the slab subgrade soils. The non-expansive select granular fill 
should extend a minimum of 12 inches beyond the edges of the new structures. The 
non-expansive select granular fill should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
Prior to placing the non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend scarifying 
the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioning to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Compaction of the subgrade soils should be accomplished using non-
vibratory methods. The select granular fill and the underlying subgrade soils should be 
wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete. Where shrinkage 
cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend preparing the 
soils again as recommended. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed 
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of 
the soft areas and replacement with engineered fill.

3.3.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or 
in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated 
materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated 
to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be reused as fill materials 
and should be properly disposed of off-site or in landscaped areas, if appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture content is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density.  
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and 
replacing these areas with engineered fill. A Geolabs representative in the field 
should evaluate the need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil 
conditions.

3.3.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site soils above the groundwater table may be reused as a 
source of general fill material, provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. 
Imported fill materials should consist of select granular material, such as crushed 
basalt or coral. The select granular fill material should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or 
more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Where groundwater is encountered (within the excavations), backfill materials 
should consist of free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped on all sides with non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) to facilitate backfilling of excavations below the 
groundwater level. The free draining granular materials should be used up to a level 
of about 12 inches above the groundwater level to facilitate compaction of the 
overlying fill materials. 
To increase the subgrade stiffness of the UST excavations, we recommend the use 
of geogrids (Tensar TriAx Grid TX7 or equivalent) be considered. In general, the 
geogrids will interlock with the No. 3B Fine gravel, resulting in two benefits 
consisting of increasing the modulus of the free-draining granular materials by 
providing lateral confinement and enhancing the subgrade bearing capacity. 
3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials and non-expansive select granular fill materials should be 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction, as appropriate. Within the areas subjected to vehicular traffic, 
the compaction requirement should be increased to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory

3.5 
Corrosion Potential 
Chemical analyses, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride contents, and 
sulfate contents, were performed on a selected soil sample obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. The 
test results are summarized and presented on Plate B-7 of Appendix B. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as fuel lines or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  Resistivity 
is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics regarding the 
corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the resistivity, the 
greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. Conversely, the higher 
the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion of metallic objects. 
Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory testing, the subsurface soils at the 
project site exhibit minimum resistivity values of as low as about 1,600 ohm-cm,

indicating that the subsurface soils are very corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 2) to buried 
metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly designing metallic substructures 
(such as piping) for protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the 
soils. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate content tested 
on the in-situ materials, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used 
for the concrete in contact with the ground, provided that the appropriate minimum 
concrete cover over the reinforcing steel is maintained. It may be appropriate to consult 
with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection.

two exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about

classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils.  The test results are presented on Plate B-5. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content 
and the dry density of the near-surface soil as fill materials.  The test results are presented 
on Plate B-6. 
One set of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was 
performed by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil sample 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-7.
