SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on the boring information contained in the above mentioned report the
anticipated subsurface conditions at the site would likely consist of fill materials
extending to an elevation of about 1 to 1 foot Mean Sea Level MSL It should be
noted that man made debris such as Portland cement concrete was encountered in
the fill layer Therefore man made debris should be anticipated in the existing fill
material at the new elevator location
The fill materials were underlain by soft loose lagoonal deposits extending to an
elevation of about 30 to 34 feet MSL The lagoonal deposits consisted of silty
sand gravel and sandy silts Below the lagoonal deposits medium dense and stiff
alluvial deposits silty sands and sandy silts were encountered in the borings extending
to elevations of about 36 to 39 feet MSL The alluvial deposits were underlain by
medium dense to dense coralline deposits that extended to elevations of about 41 to
53 feet MSL Stiff alluvial deposits consisting of sandy silts were encountered below the
coralline deposits extending to elevations of about 60 to 63 feet MSL Medium dense
coralline detritus was encountered below the alluvial deposits and extended to the
maximum depth explored of 101 feet
We encountered groundwater at the project site at elevations ranging between 0 7
and 0 5 feet MSL It should be noted that groundwater levels could fluctuate depending
on tidal fluctuations due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean
seasonal precipitation groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the generally soft loose subsurface conditions encountered below the fill
layer we recommend using a deep foundation system to support the new elevator
planned for the Tiffany Co store Due to the limited space for the construction work
and noise considerations w e recommend supporting the new elevator on micropiles
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical recommendations for the project foundation
design is presented in the following sections
Micropiles
In general the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually
less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile
foundation typically is constructed by drilling a hole with or without casing placing
reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the hole Micropiles are desirable because they
can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and
ground conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal
disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we estimate that
a micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of 5 5 inches may be used to support the
elevator We recommend designing each micropile based on an allowable compressive
load capacity of 35 tons 70 kips The allowable compressive load capacity of the
micropiles is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be increased by up to one third
1 3 for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces

To provide an allowable compressive load capacity of 35 tons 70 kips we
recommend extending the micropiles through the lagoonal deposits and embedding the
grout bulb a minimum of 30 feet into the bearing layer consisting of stiff alluvial deposits
and medium dense to dense coralline deposits Based on the boring information we
estimate that the bearing layer starts at an elevation of approximately 35 feet MSL
therefore the tip elevation of the micropile would extend to approximately 65 feet MSL
The micropiles will derive its vertical support primarily from skin friction between the grout
and the surrounding alluvial and coralline deposits
To reduce the potential for caving in or necking of the lagoonal deposits which
would result in less grout cover around the steel reinforcement potential corrosion issue
we recommend using a cased micropile system permanent casing The outside
diameter OD of the permanent casing should be about 5 5 inches same as the grout
bulb size The permanent casing should extend 3 feet into the bearing layer or to
approximately 38 feet MSL
Lateral Load Resistance
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the
lateral load resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected Lateral
loads imposed on the new elevator may be resisted primarily by passive pressure
resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and by shear acting along the
sides of the pile cap Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the
pile cap may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per
square foot per foot of depth pcf above the groundwater level and 175 pcf below
the groundwater level Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by
shear resistance between the sides of the pile cap and the adjacent soils A side
shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only
Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the pile supported foundations to be 0 5 inches or less with differential
settlements not exceeding about one half of the total settlement We believe that
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

Micropile Proof Testing
We recommend performing a proof test pullout test on a selected production
micropile during construction to confirm the load carrying capacity of the installed
micropiles We understand that four production mircropiles are planned to support
the new elevator Therefore we recommend proof testing a minimum 1 production
micropile The proof test should consist of subjecting the micropile to at least
150 percent of the design load and the maximum test load should be held for at
least 10 or 60 minutes Proof tests on the micropiles also are integral parts of the
design of the micropile foundation system

In general the elevator pit walls should be designed for the at rest condition
because they are likely restrained or are not able to deflect by as much as 0 5 percent of
the wall height For walls that are free to deflect by as much as 0 5 percent of the wall
height an active condition may be used in the design These lateral earth pressures
above the groundwater level do not include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused
by groundwater trapped behind the walls
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a rectangular
distribution with a uniform pressure equal to 44 percent of the vertical surcharge
pressure acting on the entire height of the wall may be used to design the walls that are
restrained For walls that free to deflect cantilever a rectangular distribution equal to
28 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall
may be used for design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate
the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
Site Grading
Based on the existing site conditions we envision that majority of the site grading
work will consist of backfilling around the elevator pit At the on set of earthwork the area
within the contract grading limits should be cleared thoroughly Concrete slabs
construction debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be disposed of
properly off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation
quantities
The materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of general
fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension If required import fill should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed basalt or coral The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Geolabs should test imported fill
material for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site
for its intended use
Where groundwater is encountered within the excavation of the elevator pit
backfill materials should consist of free draining granular materials such as No 2 Rock
ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped on all sides with non woven filter fabric
Mirafi 180N or equivalent The free draining granular materials should be used up to a

LIMITATIONS
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based in part
upon information obtained from our review of the subsurface information contained in a
previous geotechnical investigation conducted for the original design of the
