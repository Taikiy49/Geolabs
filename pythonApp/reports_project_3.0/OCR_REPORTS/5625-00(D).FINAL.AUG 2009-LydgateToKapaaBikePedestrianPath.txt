1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Lydgate to Kapaa Bike Pedestrian Path project in the Kapaa area on
the Island of Kauai Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended
for the design of pavements foundations site grading and drywell only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The proposed project consists of constructing a new bike and pedestrian path
along the shoreline from Lydgate Park to Lihi Park We anticipate a 10 to 12 foot wide

near Kawaihau Road The spur alignment along Papaloa Road extends from

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late
Pliocene Epoch more than million years before present Following the cessation
of this main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the
extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the
concurrent deposition of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon
Volcanic Series These lavas consist of four distinct formations Napali Olokele
Haupu and Makaweli These formations are comprised of thin bedded a a and
pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with
the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level of Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenious sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

3 2 Shallow Spread Foundations
We understand that shallow spread foundations will be used for the elevated
walkway section on the hillside leading up to Gore Park between Kawaihau Road and
Iwaena Road Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
new elevated walkway structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds
per square foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the
stiff to very stiff residual soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
For footings bearing on the stiff on site soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and recompacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTMD 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with the on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the bottom of footing may be extended down to the underlying
competent materials
In general the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or
on sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 5 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of
the slope

dense beach sand deposit Based on the laboratory test results the beach sand has
severe corrosion potential Based on the structural loading and _ corrosion
considerations we recommend casing a minimum of 5 feet of the upper portion of the
anchor shaft with a 4 inch Schedule 40 PVC pipe The annular space between the steel
anchor shaft and the inside diameter of the PVC casing should be filled with cement
grout The cased shaft will provide a larger cross section for lateral load resistance and
additional corrosion resistance
The steel shaft should consist of a minimum 1 5 inch solid square bar with
minimum yield strength of 70 kips per square inch ksi The parts used for the steel bar
assembly should be galvanized per ASTM A153 We recommend that the lead section
of the helical anchor have a minimum length of 5 feet with 8 inch and 10 inch diameter
helices The cement grout should consist of a non shrink Portland cement grout with
minimum compressive strength of 4 000 pounds per square inch psi

method of construction and construction procedures Therefore we recommend
that a Geolabs representative is present to observe the installation of the helical
ground anchors during construction Construction of the ground anchors should be

Grouting of the cased portion of the helical anchors has a major impact on the

Ground Anchor Testing
In general we recommend conducting a minimum of one pre production
performance test on one sacrificial helical ground anchor prior to installation of
production anchors to confirm the contractor s installation methods and anchor
lateral resistance The load test should consist of a lateral load test on the sacrificial
anchor In general the lateral load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 3966 as modified herein The lateral load test should consist of subjecting
the helical ground anchor to at least 133 percent of the lateral design load and the
load should be held for at least 10 minutes
During the load test the helical anchor should sustain 100 and 133 percent of the
lateral design load with no more than 4 and 6 inches total lateral movement of the
anchor head as measured relative to the top of the anchor prior to the start of the
testing respectively

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Six One inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures These tests were run on
natural samples The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 3
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support The test results are presented on Plate B 4
One set of and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 were performed
on a bulk sample of near surface soils to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils
The test results are presented on Plate B 5

APPENDIX C
APPENDIX C
Permeability Tests
The permeability of the in situ soils was evaluated by performing one Constant
Head Permeability test and one Falling Head Permeability test at a selected depth
within Boring No 313 The tests were performed at a depth of about 11 5 feet below
the existing ground surface
The Constant Head Permeability test was performed by pumping water into the
borehole until the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient
period of time The water height and rate of pumping were then measured to provide
data upon which to base the calculation of subsoil permeability The field permeability
test results are presented on Plate C 1
The Falling Head Permeability test was performed by pumping water into the
borehole up to a reference level where the pumping of water was stopped The drop in
water height with time was then measured and used in the calculation of subsoil
permeability The field permeability test results are presented on Plate C 2
