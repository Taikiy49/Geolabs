REMARKS As requested we have performed a preliminary assessment of the drainage
and debris accumulation problem affecting the existing drainage system at
the Kaneohe Portal of the Wilson Tunnel in the District of Koolaupoko on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The approximate location of the project site is
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
An initial site reconnaissance was performed on with yourself
and members of HDOT HWY O in attendance A second site visit was
conducted on The second site visit was conducted to
observe the existing site conditions following another period of very high
rainfall and excessive runoff The following sections of this technical
memorandum present the findings from our site reconnaissance our
preliminary assessment of the problems and our preliminary geotechnical
recommendations for mitigation of the drainage and sedimentation
problems
Background
The project site is located above the Kaneohe Portal at the Wilson Tunnel
where an existing single drain inlet and concrete interception ditch swale
repeatedly clogs with soil and rock debris following high rainfall events The
drainage system was reported clogged and operating at reduced capacity
following a period of record setting rainfall and storm water runoff which
occurred during the month of March 2006 Recorded rainfall during the
month of March 2006 was approximately 35 inches at the Wilson Tunnel It
was reported that the debris clogged drainage system caused an undesired
diversion of storm water runoff which resulted in some cascading water onto
the highway at the in bound side of the Kaneohe Portal Following our July
7th site visit a contractor cleared approximately 4 cubic yards of soil and
rock debris from the area to restore functional drainage
On October 31 through November 2 a winter storm produced approximately
18 inches of rainfall with occasional rainfall burst rates exceeding about 2

Existing Site Conditions
As mentioned previously a concrete drainage chute directs intermittent
stream flow to an existing drainage catch basin inlet Based on our July 7
observations the existing drain inlet spanned by a steel grate with 1 inch
openings was completely covered by accumulations of silty and sandy soils
containing rock fragments consisting of gravel and cobbles which rendered
the catch basin non functional In addition an existing concrete drainage
swale traverses the width of the portal area parallel to the hillside and is
very gently sloped to drain toward the center at the single drain inlet The
swale was observed nearly filled with silty sediment material and appeared
to have limited drainage functionality The single drain inlet receives influent
from both the swale and the upstream stream channel Based on our
observations the clogged drain inlet and sediment filled drainage swale
were responsible for the drainage back up and the undesired diversion of
storm water runoff at the site
Storm water runoff flowing into the site appears to originate from the
surrounding forested slopes and from an unnamed drainage ravine which
extends further upslope The drainage basin feeding the ravine occupies
approximately 8 8 acres The ravine contains a natural stream channel with
exposed banks composed of mixed alluvial soils and loose rock and boulder

In addition to the accumulation of debris mentioned previously the existing
relatively flat ground surfaces between the toe of the cut slopes and the
existing drainage swale were observed to harbor variable accumulations
consisting of clayey and sandy alluvial soils with rock fragments consisting of
gravel and cobbles The debris accumulations are generally wedge shaped
in profile thickness and extend from the slope toe toward the drainage
swale The thickest part of the debris accumulation is estimated on the
order of about 3 to 4 feet in height adjacent to the toe of the cut slope
thinning to less than a few inches adjacent to the swale as depicted on
Plate 4 The debris appears to have accumulated over an extended period
of time evidenced by the growth of some trees and brush within the loose
deposits along the toe of the slopes Based on our observations the
existing loose soil and rocky deposits covering the drain inlet and filling the
concrete swale have significantly reduced the effectiveness of the existing
drainage system
The project site is located in a very high rainfall forest environment
containing steep weathered rock slopes with high surface runoff Therefore
control of the source of debris clearing of rocky debris and use of a
detention basin may not be practical due to limited space within the narrow
ravine The source of the rocky debris appears to be the unlined stream
channel and the adjacent steep slopes located within forest conservation
land Furthermore reconstruction of the existing drainage system to
increase the drainage capacity and provide additional drainage outlets may
not be practical due to the existing tunnel structure and lack of space to
contain new drainage system improvements
Based on our evaluation of the existing site conditions we believe that there
is generally inadequate control to segregate entrained sediment and rocks
from the stream flow emanating from off site Therefore debris
accumulations following larger rainfall events will continue to clog the
existing drainage system by depositing a mound of rocky material over the
inlet Based on our observations the clogging of the drain inlet will

Recommended Mitigation
With the exception of the rocky debris deposited on the drain inlet below the
drainage chute we believe the soft sediments accumulating in the
sedimentation buffer region may have accumulated for an extended period
of time perhaps many years The accumulated deposits are believed to
increase sedimentation within the swale especially when the drain inlet
becomes clogged
Based on our observation of the existing site conditions it appears that the
existing drainage system which generally consists of the concrete drainage
chute single drain inlet and relatively small capacity drainage swale may be
inefficient for high flow volume drainage interception considering the
potentially high volume of entrained debris which is periodically generated
at the site
Long Term Lower Maintenance Mitigation Alternatives
Based on our evaluation of the existing site conditions the drainage system
has limited means to control the introduction of rock debris gravel cobbles
and boulders and sediment from the unlined stream channel Possible
conceptual mitigation measures to control the introduction of debris and

Interim Higher Maintenance Mitigation
Because the long term lower maintenance mitigation alternatives may not be
feasible or practical to construct at this time we believe the following interim
measures could be undertaken to reduce the existing drainage and
sedimentation problems Regular inspection and maintenance of the debris
barriers recommended below will be required
1 Installation of several widely spaced steel bollards at the lined
channel inlet to minimize larger boulders from entering the drainage
system and causing a potential safety hazard
2 Installation of heavy duty chain link fencing 4 to 6 feet high along the
perimeter of the unlined stream channel to reduce the future influx of
rock debris from the adjacent slopes
3 Cleaning and removal of some of the existing loose rock debris from
the unlined stream channel upstream of the concrete drainage chute
to reduce scour of the loose materials
4 Installation of additional steel debris barrier screens baffles within
the existing concrete drainage chute similar to the single vertical
screen presently in place

believe the most effective method to maintain drainage is to inspect and
clear the drain inlet following larger storm events When debris
accumulation is observed the material should be promptly cleared and
disposed off site
