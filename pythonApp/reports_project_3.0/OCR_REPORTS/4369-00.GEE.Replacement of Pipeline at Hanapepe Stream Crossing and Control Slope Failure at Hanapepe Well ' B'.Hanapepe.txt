REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the extrusion
of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch more than
2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield building phase
there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea
Formation
GEOLABS INC
W O 4369 00 Page 5
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of dense
basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales
Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the
vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental glaciers
accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the oceanic
basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because more
water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these
changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations
resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the present sea
level of Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher
elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs
Most of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolite soils derived from the in situ
weathering of igneous rocks and recent stream deposits

Microtunneling
Microtunneling pipe jacking is a trenchless process for installing underground pipelines
ducts culverts and utilities The technique involves the use of powerful hydraulic jacks
that push or jack successive pipe sections through the ground behind a remotely
controlled excavating unit or cutting disk head simultaneously while the excavation is
taking place within the excavating unit
In order to install a pipeline using this technique jacking and receiving pits will need to
be constructed A concrete thrust block constructed behind the jacking frame within
the jacking pit provides the reaction to the hydraulic jacks In weak poor ground
conditions soil stabilization or other methods may need to be employed to increase the
reaction capability of the thrust block The hydraulic jacks pressing against the back
of the pipeline advance the disk cutting head into the soil formation To ensure that
the jacking forces are distributed around the circumference of a pipe being jacked a
thrust ring is normally provided with the design depending on the number of jacks being
GEOLABS INC
W O 4369 00 Page 9
used The jacks are interconnected hydraulically to ensure that the thrust from each
jack is the same The number of jacks used may vary depending on the pipe size the
strength of the jacking pipes the length to be installed and the anticipated frictional
resistance
The disk cutting head of the tunnel boring machine is controlled via cables from a cabin
on the surface where the operator monitors speed pressure and tunneling accuracy
At the face of the tunnel a rotating cutter head digs into the soil and rock and mixes it
with groundwater and water pumped from the surface Rocky material is reduced to
a maximum of inch size in a crushing chamber located directly behind the cutter
disk The excavated material is pumped out of the tunnel as slurry The water
introduced into the machine is matched in pressure and volume to the material being
displaced This prevents over excavation of the tunnel face thereby reducing the
potential for cave in of the soil surrounding the new casing The cutting disk speed and
direction of rotation the hydraulic jacking system as well as the slurry system
operations can be controlled from one control panel located remotely at the launch pit
The hydraulic steering cylinders located on the disk cutting head can be extended to
steer the head back on course should any deviation be detected A laser guidance
system provides information such as length of drive vertical and horizontal heading
vertical horizontal and roll deviation from the programmed course and steering
cylinder status data on a near continuous basis in order for adjustments to be made
continuously to keep the disk cutting head on course
It is our opinion that microtunnelling is not a viable method to install the pipeline due to
the stream deposits encountered The basalt boulders may have unconfined
compressive strengths of 5 000 to more than 10 000 pounds per square inch psi
Microtunneling machines are not effective in this type of formation Additionally
installation of the jacking pits would be problematic due to difficulty in installation of the
shoring and dewatering of the cobble and boulders

Horizontal Directional Drilling HDD
Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using a
multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface The
pilot hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical
cutting or a combination of both The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to
accommodate the pipe to be installed The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole
by the drilling machine Most HDD systems use a slurry to stabilize the walls of the
borehole and to reduce the frictional drag on the pipeline being installed Steering
corrections are made in the soils by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired
orientation and then advancing the shoe and string without rotation
In general the HDD hole would be drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit angles
limited to a maximum of about 14 degrees measured from horizontal The drill unit
should be positioned at an appropriate setback distance such that the required depth
starting from the entry point can be achieved In general the product pipe or utility line
should be installed in one continuous operation preferably without pause and should
protrude a sufficient distance beyond the entry and exit points to allow proper
termination or connection at a later time Shallow working pits may be required at each
end of the bore to allow placement of the drill pipe and allow transition to the
conventional pipe trench installation
The vertical and horizontal alignment of the drill hole is monitored from the surface
by a locator which tracks signals from the transmitter contained inside the drill head
housing
The feasibility of the HDD technique should be established based on ground
conditions diameter and length of the pipeline and the required achievable degree
of accuracy in both alignment and grade HDD is not currently practical for gravity
sewer lines which require precise line and grade tolerances HDD method is best
suited for soils with some cohesion i e clays However the method has been
quite successfully used in sandy soils by adding bentonite to the drilling fluid to
GEOLABS INC
W O 4369 00 Page 11
ensure borehole stability HDD method is not well suited for gravelly soils greater
than 50 percent gravel size particles due to difficulties in drilling the pilot hole and
maintaining the stability of the hole It should be noted that widely heterogeneous
formations such as alluvial soils with sands gravel cobbles and boulders present
difficult challenges to penetration and steering control Therefore use of the HDD
method in these ground conditions should be carefully considered
The technical feasibility of the HDD method depends on the characteristics of the
materials at the depth of the pipeline profile as well as the length of the drill path and
diameter of the pipe to be installed American Gas Association 1995 In general
gravel size material cannot be readily fluidized by the drilling fluid and may not be
stable enough to be cut and removed by the drilling fluid without caving in Therefore
subsoils containing a high percentage of gravel may present a serious limitation on the
utilization of the HDD method In addition it should be noted that obstructions such as
cobbles and boulders within the drill path may halt the advance of the drill bit reamer
or pipeline during pullback
Horizontal directional drilling HDD installation through the cobbles gravel and boulder
stream bed deposits would be problematic and expected to cause severe difficulty in
drilling and steering the pilot hole as well as hole stability problems such as caving in
of the pilot and reamed holes The caved in materials will obstruct or divert reaming
tools and or the pipe string during pipe pullback This will substantially increase the
pulling stresses on the pipe string and may impede pipe pulling or damage the
pipeline Deflection of the pilot drill bit may occur at the interface between the loose
and dense stream bed deposit formation due to an abrupt change in consistency This
may result in difficulties in steering during the pilot hole drilling and potential change in
the drill path alignment
Therefore it is our opinion that the horizontal directional drilling HDD method is not
a viable method for the proposed replacement of pipeline at the Hanapepe Stream
Crossing site

Control of Slope Failure at Hanapepe Well B
The field exploration and site observations indicate that the slope instability is a result
of improper placement of the fill during the original construction of the facility It appears that
the original sloping terrain was leveled by cutting into the hillside along the western boundary
GEOLABS INC
W O 4369 00 Page 15
and using this material to fill along the lower eastern boundary The fill may not have been
properly keyed and benched into the original ground and or not compacted properly
Stabilization could involve removal and proper replacement of the fill to restore the site
conditions This would require extensive grading and proper keying and benching into the
natural ground Also due to the limited distance to the stream a steep reinforced fill slope
would be required Alternatively a reinforced concrete or CRM retaining wall could be
constructed to support the fill and natural ground The required height of the retaining wall
would be about 6 feet if it were located near the existing control building
We understand that retaining walls are planned to retain the slope in order to
prevent damage to the facilities at the well site The following general guidelines may be
used for preliminary design of retaining structures
Wall Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we recommend
that a shallow foundation system consisting of continuous footings be used to
support the retaining wall structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to
may be used for the design of the retaining wall footings bearing on the
natural ground This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by
temporary wind and or seismic forces Wall footings should have a minimum width
of 18 inches
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of
reinforcing steel and or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

In general footings should be embedded about 2 feet below the original natural
grade Where footings are located adjacent to or on slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at
least 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge

A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated
GEOLABS INC

drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site soils or select
granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of
backfill should consist of the on site silty soils to reduce water infiltration behind the
walls
Pavements Design
We understand that a new reinforced concrete paved roadway is planned to extend
the existing concrete river ford crossing The length of the extension will be approximately
50 feet on the southeast side of river and 300 feet on the northwest side of the river with
a width of 10 feet Considering light traffic conditions on the proposed reinforced concrete
roadway we recommend that 6 inch thick reinforced pavement be used on the existing
boulder road
We also understand that the existing asphalt concrete pavement at the Hanapepe
Well B site will be reconstructed We anticipate that the new pavements will be subjected
to light vehicular traffic consisting primarily of passenger vehicles light pick up trucks and
occasional moderately loaded trucks We have made our pavement designs assuming the
pavement subgrade soil will generally consist of the near surface silty clay soils as
encountered during our field exploration If site grading exposes soils other than those
assumed additional tests should performed to confirm or revise the recommended
pavement sections for actual field conditions Based on the above assumptions the

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings to depths ranging from about 11 5 to 28 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
