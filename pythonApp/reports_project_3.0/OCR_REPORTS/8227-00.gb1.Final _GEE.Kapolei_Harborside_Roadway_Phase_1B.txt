SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the State Harbor Access Road Improvements, Kapolei Harborside – 
Phase 1B project in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
pavements, site preparation, sidewalks and concrete curbs and gutters, and underground 
utilities only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located west of Kalaeloa Boulevard and south of Kapolei 
Parkway in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  We understand that it is planned to 
continue the State Harbor Access Road from the intersection with Kapolei Parkway and 
extend the roadway in the southwesterly direction about 4,800 lineal feet.  For this phase, 
the State Harbor Access Road will terminate just before crossing the drainage channel.  
We understand that concrete sidewalks, underground utilities, and other related 
site improvements are planned for the roadway project. Based on the existing topography 
and planned improvements, the roadway will traverse two relatively deep existing 
detention basins that will need to be backfilled. 
We anticipate that general site grading work may consist of cuts and fills on the 
order of about 5 feet. Relatively thick fills on the order of about 18 to 22 feet will be 
required at the existing detention basins.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis for design of the new roadway and 
drainage structures and the geotechnical aspects of the roadway improvements project 
are the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the engineering properties 
of the materials encountered, and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the 
project site. Therefore, the following subsections provide a description of the geologic 
setting of the project site and the surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the 
site. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age. As volcanic activity in the 
Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded 
eastern slope forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project 
site is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by the erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu.  
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level.

The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials. Placement of man-made fills and commercial development of 
the area within the last 20 years have brought the project area to its present form. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located west of Kalaeloa Boulevard, below the Kapolei West 
development in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is approximately bounded 
by Kapolei Commons shopping center to the north, Hanua Street and the Kapolei 
Drainage Channel to the west, Malakole Street to the south, and the STG Casting Plant 
and Kapolei Business Park (Kalaeloa Boulevard) to the east. The approximate location 
of the site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field observations, the project site is relatively flat, sloping gently 
downward from northeast to southwest. Based on available information, existing ground 
surface elevations along the centerline of the proposed roadway range from about 
+22 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the south end of the site to about +45 feet MSL at the 
north end of the site.  
Two detention basins were observed at the north end of the site near the 
intersection with Kapolei Parkway.  The depths of the detention basins are about 18 and 
22 feet below the existing ground surface.  Based on our observations, the sidewalls 
exposed up to a 3-foot thick layer of clayey soils underlain by coralline deposits.  The 
thickness of the clayey soils appear to thin out at the southern detention basin.  Standing 
water was observed in the northern most detention basin.  It should be noted that since 
these basins are meant to temporarily detain stormwater for a period of time, the bottom 
of the detention basin could potentially have areas of soft soil that would need to be 
over-excavated prior to filling these detention basins.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
nine borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to depths of 
approximately 11.5 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, five bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, were obtained to 
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate 
boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the borings encountered fill material, coralline detritus, coral formation, 
coralline limestone and sandstone, and alluvium extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Boring No. 1, located at the south end of the roadway alignment, encountered a 
1.5-foot thick layer of medium dense basaltic gravel fill over medium dense silty coralline 
sand fill material extending to a depth of about 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
The fill was underlain by alluvium consisting of very stiff clayey soils extending to a depth 
of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Boring Nos. 2 through 8 encountered fill material up to about 3 feet thick. The fill 
material consisted of medium dense silty sand and stiff to very stiff clayey silt.  Below the 
fill material, the borings encountered coral formation, coralline sandstone and limestone, 
and coralline detritus.  The relative hardness of the coral formation and limestone ranged 
from medium hard to very hard.  In Boring Nos. 3, 5, and 7, the medium hard to very hard 
coral formation and limestone were encountered at shallow depths of about 2 to 3 feet

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered fill material, coralline detritus, coral 
formation, coralline limestone and sandstone, and alluvium extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration.  
The revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 by the State of Hawaii, 
Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research Branch 
normally requires the inclusion of an untreated permeable base course layer in the 
pavement section. However, we understand that future utility lateral tie-ins may be 
required as the area is developed in the future.  To reduce future construction difficulties 
such as raveling of untreated permeable base course during future utility installation work, 
we believe that aggregate base course may be used in lieu of untreated permeable base 
course in the pavement sections.  
We anticipate that the pavement subgrade soils near the Kapolei Parkway 
intersection will likely consist of clayey soils, which may be moderately to highly expansive 
when subjected to moisture fluctuations.  A minimum of 24 inches of base and subbase 
materials should be provided below the new pavements between Station (-)5+56.38 and 
Station (-)1+00.  The new pavements between Station (-)1+00 and the end of the project 
limits may be underlain by 12 inches of base and subbase materials since the subgrade 
soils in this section would consist of new fills or coralline materials. 
Based on the traffic data provided, the R-value of the subgrade soils, and the 
pavement and economic analyses performed, we believe that the rigid pavement sections 
would result in the most economical pavement section over the design life of the 
pavement.  The rigid pavement section should consist of 10.5 inches of Portland cement 
concrete (PCC) over 6 inches of untreated permeable base/aggregate base course 
underlain by 18 inches of aggregate subbase course between Station (-)5+56.38 and 
Station (-)1+00.  The rigid pavement section between Station (-)1+00 and the end of the 
project limits should consist of 10.5 inches of PCC over 6 inches of untreated permeable 
base/aggregate base course underlain by 6 inches of aggregate subbase course.

3.1.1 Methodology of Pavement Design 
Two types of pavement structural sections (flexible and rigid pavements) were 
considered in the pavement analyses. The flexible pavement sections presented 
herein were generally determined based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 
of the revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 prepared by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and 
Research Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem 
Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of 
Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections are generally based on the 
design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design 
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements”.

3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material,

Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
Based on the results of corrosivity testing, the subsurface soils at the project site 
exhibit an average minimum resistivity value of approximately 805 ohm-cm, indicating 
that the subsurface soils are extremely corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 1, based on the 
Board of Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion 
Control Standards, dated 1991) to buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend 
properly designing metallic substructures (such as piping) for protection against the 
potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the soil. 
Based on the Board of Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu Water System External 
Corrosion Control Standards, dated 1991, soils with a chloride content of more than 300 
parts per million (ppm), sulfate content of more than 2,000 ppm, and/or a pH of less than 
5.0 should be given a Corrosion Rating of 1 (extremely corrosive). Based on the results 
of our laboratory testing, one of the chloride content tests exceeded 300 ppm.  
It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review 
the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
nine borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to depths of about 
11.5 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface. Five bulk samples of the near-surface 
soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, were obtained to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-9. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test result is 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil sample 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic representations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two One-Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and Eurofin TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-4. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a selected 
bulk sample of the near-surface soil to determine the maximum dry density and optimum 
moisture of the on-site soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-5. 
Four laboratory Resistance (R) value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by 
Ninyo & Moore on selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-6 through B-9.
