INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Pago Pago Park Access Road and Bridge project in the
area of Pago Pago on the island of Tutuila American Samoa The general location of
the project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recom
mendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input and
may not be appropriate to form the basis for preparation of final construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Tutuila was built during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene age by
the extrusion of basaltic lavas and pyroclastic materials from five volcanoes Stearns
1944 Following the cessation of the main shield building activity there was a long
period of erosion accompanied by the growth of fringing coral reefs During the late
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3232 00 Page 7
Quaternary volcanism was renewed by eruptions of lava and pyroclastic materials at
several vents on the south side of the island forming the Tafuna Leone Plain
The main mountain mass of Tutuila was divided into seven formations The
rocks are thought to be of Pliocene Age consisting of thinly bedded to massive flows
of basalt andesitic basalt and andesite interbedded with cinder cones and tuff beds
with thrachytic dikes plugs and crater fills The rock units were erupted from as many
as five eruptive centers but the principal volcano was near the middle of the island
Pago Pago Harbor is believed to occupy part of the collapsed caldera of that principal
volcano Erosion along a major fault along the caldera wall formed the near vertical cliff
on the northeast side of the harbor
Prior to the eruption of the Leone Volcanics beach and lagoonal deposits
accumulated on and behind a submerged barrier reef at the present site of the Leone
Plain These deposits were later buried under several hundred feet of volcanic
materials These older beach and lagoonal deposits consist partially of sand and
coralline gravels
Three tuff cones and a thick sequence of lava flow form the plain between
Tafuna and Leone The rock is thought to be of Holocene Age possibly only a few
thousand years old as evidenced by geologic relationships in the area The lava flows
are mainly thinly bedded basalts with interbeds of tuff and breccia
Clays silts sands and gravels have been deposited on the many valley floors
located close to the ocean by alluvial processes In some valleys the alluvial deposits
may be on the order of more than 100 feet

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field exploration and laboratory testing programs the
proposed access road and bridge project traverses a significant thickness of soft
compressible lagoonal harbor deposits along the proposed roadway alignment The
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed access road and
bridge project include the following
consolidation of the underlying potentially compressible soils due to the
placement of the new embankment fills along the proposed access road
foundation support of the proposed bridge due to the presence of the thick
compressible soil deposits and
stability of the new fill embankment along the proposed access road
during and after construction
We understand that new fills up to on the order of 18 feet is anticipated along
the proposed roadway alignment In general loose sandy soils and very soft to
medium stiff clayey soils were encountered to depths of approximately 40 to 80 feet
below the proposed roadway embankment Based on our engineering analyses the
loose and soft soils below the surface should not adversely affect the stability of the
embankment fills of up to approximately 18 feet thick However placement of the new
fills will cause significant embankment settlements due to consolidation of the
underlying loose and soft lagoonal deposits
Because the thickness of the new fill varies along the road alignment the
settlements profile of embankment foundation soils will also vary accordingly Based
on our analyses settlements up to about 2 to 3 feet are estimated based on
GEOLABS HAWAII

embankment fills on the order of up to 18 feet thick We estimate that the majority of
the primary settlements would occur between about 6 to 9 months
Because of the significant settlements caused by the new embankment fills and
relatively long settlement period we recommend that the construction of the proposed
project be phased to accommodate the significant settlements anticipated Construction
of the proposed access road and bridge project should be separated into at least two
phases 1 construction of fill embankment and 2 construction of structures and
improvements After the completion of initial fill embankment a fill settlement waiting
period of at least 6 to 9 months shouid be provided before commencement of
constructing surface improvements structures such as the bridge concrete walls and
utility lines on or near the roadway embankment A program for monitoring the ground
settlements during the settlement period should be implemented

Settlement
As previously indicated settlement of the proposed access road embankment
will occur as a result of the embankment fill loads over the underlying compressible soil
deposits Therefore we recommend that ground surface settlement markers and
gauges be placed on the existing ground surface the completed fill embankment
surface and the completed MSERW structures to monitor the actual magnitude and
rates of settlements We estimate that a majority of the anticipated settlements would
occur in about 6 to 9 months Therefore we recommend that a settlement waiting
period of at least 9 months be provided following construction of the MSERW and fill
embankment Construction of the bridge structure the pavement structure and the
utility lines above the filled ground should be delayed as long as possible and after a
majority of the estimated primary settlements have occurred The estimated
magnitudes and rates of settlements of the MSERW structure and fill embankment on
the south and north sides of the bridge and recommendations for monitoring the
settlements are discussed below
South side of the bridge
Primary settlements on the order of 2 feet are estimated as a result of loads
imposed by the proposed 18 foot high MSERW structure on the foundation soils

Differential Settlements
Because the fills along the proposed access road vary significantly from 0 to
about 18 feet in height differential settlements along the face of the MSERW at
the south side of the bridge should be expected Based on our estimates we
believe that differential settlements on the order of 6 to 8 inches in 100 lineal feet

recommend that joints be provided at a maximum wall spacing of 50 feet along
the wall face to accommodate these potential differential settlements
Settlement Monitoring
It should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time
required for the filled ground to settle Settlement rates are generally affected
by small details of the subsoil structures and the history of the subsoil
deposition Therefore the actual settlement rate should be monitored during
construction and provisions should be available for potential delay to the project
construction schedule based on the estimated duration if a longer settlement
waiting period is actually required
To monitor the actual ground structure settlement rates we recommend that a
large number of settlement gauges be installed on the existing ground surface
prior to fill placement at the future fill areas A typical detail for settlement
gauges is presented on Plate 3 Settlement markers should also be installed on
the finished fill surface and MSERW surface to monitor the settlement rates

The settlement gauges and markers should be read by a professional land
surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs Hawaii for review
The readings should be transmitted on a weekly basis for the duration of the

settlement rates have stabilized
A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential
delay in the construction schedule and the possible extra costs that may be

Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls MSERW
As previous indicated retaining walls are planned on both sides of the proposed
access road from Station 13 00 to Station 19 00 Due to the relative thick soft harbor
deposit we believe that Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls MSERW in lieu
of conventional concrete retaining walis supported on piles may be a more effective and
economical design to support the new fills MSERW are composite wail systems which
utilize welded wire mats galvanized steel strips or other reinforcing elements to
reinforce the backfill zone and to improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone
These reinforcements are generally attached to precast concrete panels used for wall
facing MSERW are generally desirable due to their ability to tolerate significant
amount of total and differential settlements In addition the MSERW are also desirable
due to the wall flexibility ease of construction high load carrying capacity and
economy
Design of the MSERW will need to consider both external and internal stabilities
of the structure In evaluating external stability the MSERW structure must satisfy four
stability conditions 1 bearing failure 2 translational sliding 3 overturning stability
and 4 overail slope stability Geotechnical design parameters to evaluate these

parameters necessary to evaluate the internal stability of the MSERW are presented
in the Backfill Material subsection below The internal and external stabilities of the
MSERW will need to be evaluated by the design engineer We have evaluated the
overall slope stability for the planned MSERW and the results are summarized in the
Overall Slope Stability subsection
Foundation for MSERW
The near surface foundation soils for the MSERW on the south side of the bridge
consist generally of soft to medium stiff sandy silts and loose to medium dense
sandy gravels Groundwater levels were measured between 1 and 4 feet below
the existing ground surface in the borings along the existing roadway The
relatively shallow groundwater levels would present difficulties in achieving the
desired compaction of subgrade materials Therefore we recommend that the
subgrade be proof rolled with a smooth drum roller 10 ton minimum to detect
soft subgrade soils and to compact the existing ground surface If pumping
subgrade conditions are encountered during the proof rolling the pumping
subgrade materials should be removed to a maximum depth of 18 inches A
layer of filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be placed over the
resulting over excavation and backfilled with No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation The top of the No 3B Fine gravel should be proof rolled prior
to placement of the geotextile filter fabric below the embankment fill material
The geotextile fabric should have a minimum overlap of 2 feet and should extend
at least 2 feet beyond the edge of the improvement area
For the MSERW constructed on the prepared subgrade discussed an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 2 000 p s f may be used for dead plus live loads The

Lateral Load Resistance
Resistance to lateral loads imposed on the MSERW structure may be provided
by a combination of the following 1 friction between the reinforced soil volume
and the foundation soils and 2 passive resistance acting against the near
vertical faces of the embedded portion of the MSERW if embedded An
allowable friction coefficient of 0 3 may be used between the base of the
MSERW and the supporting subgrade soils For passive resistance an
equivalent fluid pressure of 200 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f
acting against the vertical faces of the MSERW embedded into the foundation
soils may be used A safety factor of 1 5 has been included in the friction
coefficient and passive resistance values presented above therefore a factor
of safety need not be included when evaluating sliding stability
Lateral Earth Pressures
The retained soil behind the MSERW structure will exert lateral earth pressures
on the MSERW structure Since a large portion of the MSERW structure will
retain the existing embankment fills we recommend that the MSERW be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3232 00 Page 17
designed with lateral earth pressures due to the embankment fills consisting of
select granular material On this basis we recommend that lateral earth
pressures equivalent to a fluid weighing 35 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f be used to design the internal and external stabilities of the
MSERW This value assumes a near level surface at the top of the wall and
does not include the effects of traffic and surcharge The MSERW should be
designed for all surcharge and traffic loads Lateral earth pressures due to
uniform surcharge loads should be taken as 30 percent of the vertical surcharge
pressure acting uniformly over the entire height of the MSERW structure
The lateral earth pressure value provided above does not include hydrostatic
pressures that may be caused by groundwater trapped behind the MSERW
structure Although the MSERW structure is composed of relatively free draining
materials we recommend that a drain be provided at the base of the MSERW
structure behind the reinforced soil volume to collect and discharge excess
water that may infiltrate through the wall structure Drains should consist of a
1 foot to 2 foot wide zone of permeable drainage material such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation with a perforated pipe with perforations
down at the base of the MSERW discharging to a proper discharge facility

term stabilities of the MSERW are at least 1 5 which is the minimum factor of
safety normally recommended

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy
of at least 50 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
level control such that pile driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled
at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which
could damage or cause cracking of the piles
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data to Geolabs Hawaii

the submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected
hammer Due to possible variation in the length of the driven piles unit prices
should be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception
of splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be
reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four hours and
the pile cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop within the pile
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3232 00 Page 24
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This can be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or
the more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing
capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the piles have
been driven to their full length or the blow counts steadily increase with depth
and reach 8 blow counts per inch for the last 6 inches of final pile penetration
or when total blow counts are at least 90 blows for the last foot It should be
noted that hard driving conditions may be encountered due to the presence of
boulders in the subsurface soils Therefore we recommend that the pile tips be
equipped with pile driving shoes to facilitate pile driving
Piles which do not meet the specified blow count requirements may be selected
by the project geotechnical engineer to be re tapped to evaluate its freezing
effect
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to
be driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required
energy to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs
Hawaii should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the
equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3 and on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is shown on Plate
B 5
One hydrometer analysis was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the fine particles of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate
B 6
Eight sieve analyses were performed on seiected sampies of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification The
test results are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 4 Graphic presentation of the
grain size distribution is presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
Four consolidation tests with time rates were performed on the potentially
compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 9 through
B 12
One unconsolidated undrained UU triaxial compression test was performed on
a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils The test
results are summarized on Plate B 13
Two direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
shear strength of the material Results of the direct shear tests are presented on
Plates B 14 and B 15
