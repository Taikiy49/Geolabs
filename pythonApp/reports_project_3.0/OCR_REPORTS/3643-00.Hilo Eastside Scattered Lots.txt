IONAL Y
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 7
The project site is situated on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa The lava
formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and
ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like
or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities voids
are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities voids were formed when
the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow
cavities voids and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along
the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalts with the exception of massive basalt flows are generally considered to
be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite readily in the horizontal and
vertical directions Water is normally transmitted through the porous rock matrix along
fractures and cavities voids and along clinker layers Therefore the permeability of the
basalt will be highly dependent on the presence of fractures cavities and clinker
layers

Increment 1
The sites are located in a residential neighborhood accessed by Nene Road in
Waiakea in the District of South Hilo on the island of Hawaii The sites currently exist
as undeveloped densely forested land surrounded by residential lots and accessed via
GEOLABS HAWAII

existing paved roadways Elevations were estimated to range between Mean Sea
Level MSL and about 20 feet MSL within the subject lots In general the lots appear
to have an elevation of about 15 feet MSL with local topographic highs and lows
Overall the ground surface of the vicinity is generally level with the exception of the
high localized relief consisting of troughs and hills Some of the topographic lows
intersect the groundwater table and form ponds filled by fresh to brackish water The
ground surface exists as pahoehoe lava with some thin soil cover
The topography of the scattered lots was observed to be highly variable with
high topographic relief within the individual lot due to the nature of the pahoehoe lava
flow Vegetation was observed to be very closely spaced allowing only difficult access
by foot Due to the dense vegetation and surface cover of organic forest debris the
ground surface was often obscured and difficult to negotiate Cavities consisting of
holes and troughs in the lava rock were observed at the ground surface Some of the
larger depressions in the lava surface have created ponds which consist of natural rock
formation These pond features were observed throughout the vicinity of the site
exposed on neighboring developed parcels of land Several adjacent occupied lots
have incorporated the rugged topography and ponds into the planned landscape The
water levels within the ponds were observed to vary depending on the tide and rainfall
conditions often rising or falling in the same day Based on this observation it is
assumed that the subsurface rock conditions may be highly permeable and transmit
subsurface water quickly in response to daily changes in the tides and rainfall run off
During our site visit it was observed that the weather ranges from light to
moderate rainfall on a daily basis Ponding of water during rainfall was observed to
occur in low lying areas especially in the lower elevations and closer to Kalanianaole
Avenue which parallels the nearby shoreline area

Surficial soil cover exists as a moist dark brown silt with some clay ML MH
The soil appears highly organic and is friable when dry The soil covering was
observed to exist as thin deposits restricted to flat and depressed areas within the site
Elevated or sloping rock surfaces were observed to be bare and contain no soil cover
During the drilling exploration the rock cores retrieved often contained some silty
soil deposits within the open fractures and within the near surface voids in the lava
formation The occurrence of these isolated buried soil deposits is believed to occur
from surface erosion by water rainfall transporting the loose soil into exposed fractures
and crevices where it is deposited
Due to the inaccessibility of the lots without extensive site clearing the borings
were drilled at the lot perimeters where some fills had been placed for the construction
of the existing roads and utilities As much as possible the boring locations were
selected to correspond with the elevation within the adjacent lot
A detailed reconnaissance of each of the lots was not made due to the difficult
access and often dangerous condition of the terrain and vegetation Reconnaissance
was limited to walking the perimeter of the sites and advancing inward where passage
was possible without trail clearing and where the ground surface was visibly exposed
It should be emphasized that the subsurface conditions within the lots may be
highly variable due to the nature of the lava flow deposits Lava tubes or other volcanic
cavities may occur at any location and at any depth and may not be discovered until the
site is cleared of vegetation or graded with heavy equipment The possibility exists that
large voids may be encountered during site grading which could cause damage to
equipment if the roof of a cavity should collapse under the weight of heavy machinery

Increment 3
The sites exist in the area of Kaumana and Ponahawai in the District of South
Hilo on the island of Hawaii The sites are accessed by Kaumana Drive and various
secondary residential streets The sites exist as undeveloped land which is densely
forested with fern brush and trees Rainfall at the site is moderate to high and occurs
frequently as steady drizzle to moderate rain
The sites are devoid of soil cover with pahoehoe basalt formation exposed at the
ground surface Some thin organic soils may exist in topographic depressions
although few soil deposits were observed at the ground surface The ground surface
exists as basalt rock formation with very high relief caused by the deposition of the
pahoehoe lava flows Numerous flow structures such as hummocks pressure ridges
and holes and troughs were observed at accessible portions of the sites making for
difficult and dangerous foot access Based on conversations with neighboring residents
and local surveyors the sites contain abundant large caves lava tubes holes and other
exposed and partially covered voids associated with the deposition of the relatively
recent lava flows

In general elevations at this site range on the order of about 1 040 feet MSL to
about 1 180 feet MSL Overall the ground surface slopes gently to the east but is
typically irregular and generally rugged The basalt formation exposed at the surface is
typically medium hard but grades with depth to hard rock formation The gradual
change in rock quality with depth reflects the decreasing amount of vesicles formed by
exsolving volcanic gases during deposition The upper lava flow surface typically
contains the highest concentration of vesicles giving the rock a pumice or scoria
texture which should allow for easier excavation of the near surface rock formation
Several of the borings drilled in the vicinity encountered a buried ash layer
sandwiched between lava flows of different age This ash layer may act as a hydraulic
confining layer affecting the flow of groundwater and seepage run off The depth of the
ash layer was located at about 12 to 15 feet below ground level and was noted to be
about 5 to 8 feet thick
It should be emphasized that due to the nature of the pahoehoe lava flows in the
vicinity of Kaumana the potential for encountering exposed or partially buried lava
tubes or cavities is believed to be high Caution should be exercised during site
clearing or grading with heavy equipment as the cavity structures may collapse
suddenly under heavy machine loads

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials such as the organic and or ash soil materials described in the

Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill material within the upper 4 feet
from the finish subgrades should consist of well graded materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension The on site cut materials generated from
excavations into the underlying basalt formation may be reused as a source of
general fill or backfill materials within the upper 4 feet from the finish subgrades
provided that they are screened of over sized materials greater than 6 inches in
largest dimension and processed to meet the above gradation requirements This
requirement is to facilitate future construction of building foundations and _ utility
trench excavations Over sized rock fragments may be used in deep fills or broken
down to smaller sized materials and incorporated into the fill material If the
excavated materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded
material off site borrow or on site rock crushing of large sized rock fragments or
boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and particle sizes
to develop a well graded material
On site materials consisting of boulders up to 4 feet in diameter may be used as
rock fill provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 4 feet below the
finish subgrades In general placement of boulders in fills should be performed in
accordance with the procedures recommended in the Boulder Disposal
subsection
If imported material is required the material should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 6 inches in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or
GEOLABS HAWAII

more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less Imported fill

Boulder Disposal
Over sized materials such as cobbles greater than 6 inches in dimension and
boulders rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size are anticipated

2 Boulders up to 4 feet in largest dimension may be placed at the bottom of

5 Boulders larger than 4 feet in maximum dimension should not be used as

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills 6 inch minus materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished
by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the

shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

Field Percolation Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was estimated by performing six constant head
percolation tests in selected boring locations at depths ranging from approximately 17 5 to
20 feet below the existing ground surface The test results are presented on Plates C 1
through C 6
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until
the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time
The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon
which to base calculation of the subsoil permeability
