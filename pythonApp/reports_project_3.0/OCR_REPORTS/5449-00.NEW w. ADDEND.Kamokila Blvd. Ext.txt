Dear Mr Hiu
In accordance with our fee proposal dated March 15 2005 we performed a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the Kamokila Boulevard Extension project in
the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This report summarizes the findings
from our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents our findings and
recommendations for the project design The findings and recommendations are
intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided it is desired to extend the southern side of the
existing Kamokila Boulevard in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The new
roadway will traverse approximately 700 feet to the south from the intersection of
Kamokila Boulevard and Kapolei Parkway towards the old railroad tracks The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The new roadway extension for Kamokila Boulevard measuring approximately
700 feet long will consist of two 30 foot wide travelways with an 8 foot wide sidewalk on
both sides separated by a 20 foot wide maximum 8 foot wide minimum median The
layout of the roadway extension alignment is shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Based on our field observations and the plan and profile drawings provided we anticipate
cuts and fills on the order of about 4 feet and 1 foot thick respectively will be required to
achieve the finished grades In addition new utilities such as water drain and sewer
lines are planned for the roadway extension project
A double cell new concrete box culvert measuring approximately 10 6 feet deep
and 32 7 feet wide in outside dimensions will be installed alongside the railroad tracks for
a total length of about 943 feet as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 2 The invert of the box
culvert is set at about 27 feet below the design finish grades Based on the information
provided the box culvert will be connected to an open concrete drainage channel for
about 1 320 lineal feet

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the surface and subsurface conditions at the site to formulate geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of pavements box culvert and site preparation
only To accomplish this we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following
tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of four borings extending to depths of about 21 5 to
31 5 feet below the existing ground surface for a total of approximately
106 lineal feet of field exploration
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our field
engineer
4 Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and
evaluating their engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data to formulate geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of pavements box
culvert and site preparation
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by our senior engineer
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by our
principal engineer
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and the Logs of Borings are
presented on Plates 3 through 7 of this report The results of the laboratory tests
performed on selected soil samples are presented on Plates 8 through 10

FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 21 5 to 31 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers A bulk sample designated as
Bulk 1 was obtained from the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics The approximate locations of the borings drilled and bulk sample
collected at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in our drilled borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our field representative who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 3
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 4 through 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at appropriate sample
depths

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the drilled borings the principal
geotechnical considerations for the project design include proper site preparation
requirements to take into consideration the potential presence of loose zones and or
cavities in the coralline deposits anticipated at the project site Detailed discussion of our
geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of pavements box culvert
structure and site preparation are presented in the following sections of this report
Pavement Design
Based on our field exploration the proposed new roadway alignment is generally
underlain by medium dense to dense coralline sands and gravel at the existing ground
surface Laboratory testing performed on the surface coralline materials indicated a
CBR value of over 60 and swell of less than 1 percent Based on the field exploration

Pavement Design
4 0 Inches Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base 91 Percent Relative Specific Gravity
6 0 Inches Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
16 0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and proof rolled with a minimum
20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to detect the presence of
potential near surface cavities or loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be
operated at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute If the proof rolling operations
detect near surface cavities or loose zones the area should be ripped open cleaned of
debris and then backfilled with compacted fill material or lean concrete
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basalt
aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to greater than 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with ASTM D 2041 The aggregate subbase should consist of
crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered
during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section The recommended
section assumes that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soils below the base and subbase course layers to reduce the potential for
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a
subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from the landscaping
irrigation For long term performance we recommend a subdrain system be considered
along the sides of the roadway

Concrete Box Culvert
Based on information provided we understand a new concrete box culvert
measuring approximately 10 6 feet deep and 32 7 feet wide in outside dimensions will
be installed alongside the existing railroad tracks at the south side of the site According
to the project civil engineer the invert elevations and finish grades along the concrete
box culvert alignment would be on the order of about 35 and 62 feet MSL
respectively Therefore approximately 18 feet of soil cover will need to be placed over
the new box culvert to achieve the design finish grades along the box culvert alignment
Based on subsurface conditions encountered along the box culvert alignment
B 3 and B 4 we believe the concrete box culvert structure may be designed in
accordance with recommendations presented in the Retaining Wall Foundations
subsection of this report The subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If coral formation is
exposed at the subgrade level it should be proof rolled with a vibratory drum roller
minimum 10 ton static weight about 6 to 8 passes to help detect and collapse
near surface cavities and or voids The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute The proof rolling operations should be performed in
the presence of a qualified geotechnical engineer or a Geolabs representative
Yielding areas which may be indicative of subsurface cavities and or voids
loose areas or cavities disclosed during the proof rolling operations should be
over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials The depth of
over excavation should be extended until dense underlying materials are exposed and
should be evaluated in the field by a qualified geotechnical engineer or a Geolabs
representative
We recommend an 8 inch thick gravel cushion layer be provided between the
new box culvert slab and the underlying subgrade soils The gravel cushion layer is
intended to provide for more uniform bearing support and should consist of free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation or similar material
The use of sand or S4C as backfill materials around the drainage structure should be
prohibited due to the potential for migration or loss of fines during heavy rains which
may result in potential ground settlement and distress to improvements above the
structure
Retaining Wall Foundations
In general we anticipate a shallow foundation system will be utilized to support the
planned retaining walls for the box culvert structure Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered in our field exploration we envision new retaining
structures would probably be supported on shallow foundations such as
continuous footings and or mat foundations An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of shallow foundations

For retaining wall footings bearing on the in situ materials the bottom of the footing

box culvert The stabilization layer should also extend beyond the sides of the box
culvert a minimum of 12 inches
Lateral loads acting on the box culvert structure may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the
foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing
on the in situ stiff soils or the stabilization layer over soft soils Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes the soil around
footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for
foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Drainage
For wing walls a typical drainage system may consist of a sack of permeable
material placed at each weephole location The sack of permeable material should
consist of about 1 to 2 cubic foot of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation material wrapped with non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent
The weepholes should consist of a minimum 4 inch diameter PVC pipe spaced not
more than 6 feet apart
Backfill immediately behind permeable drainage materials should consist of select
granular fill material with a maximum particle size of 3 inches Unless covered by
concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind
the walls

Utility Trenches
We anticipate utilities for the new roadway project will consist primarily of water
drain and sewer lines In general good construction practices should be utilized for the
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities The method and equipment
to be used for trench excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to
practical limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with
the applicable federal state and local safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears conventional excavation
methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site However some of
the utility trench excavations may encounter localized areas of hard to very hard coral
formation which will likely require the use of chipping with hoe rams
In general we recommend granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should
also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils with a maximum
particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
