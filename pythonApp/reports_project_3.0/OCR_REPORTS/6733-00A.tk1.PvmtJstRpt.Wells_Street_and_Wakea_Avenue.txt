SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed Wells Street rehabilitation project generally 
encountered hard clayey silts extending to a depth of approximately 5.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface.  For the Wakea Avenue rehabilitation project, our drilled 
borings generally encountered dense to very dense sands and silty sands extending to a 
depth of about 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  We did not encounter 
groundwater in the shallow borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Because the project will involve the reconstruction of the existing roadways, we 
have performed a detailed economic comparison involving both flexible and rigid 
pavement sections for the project in accordance with the latest guidelines provided by the 
State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and 
Research Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements will be constructed over the in-situ clayey and sandy soils in the 
shallow subsurface for the Wells Street and Wakea Avenue, respectively.  Based on the 
anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data provided for our pavement analyses, the 
economic analysis performed, and construction considerations, it is our opinion that new 
pavements for the roadway rehabilitation project may consist of (A) 3 inches of AC over 
10 inches of ACB on 6 inches of ASB for Wells Street extending from High Street to 
Waiale Road; and (B) 3 inches of AC over 4.5 inches of ACB on 4 inches of ASB for 
Wakea Avenue extending from Puunene Avenue to Hana Highway.  It should be noted 
that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils 
during construction to confirm that the above pavement design sections are adequate. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Wells Street and Wakea Avenue Pavement 
Rehabilitation project in Kahului on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project is along Wells Street from High Street to 
Waiale Road, and along Wakea Avenue from Puunene Avenue to Hana Highway for a 
total distance of 0.9 mile. The approximate limits of the roadway rehabilitation project 
are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.4. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to reconstruct 
the existing pavements with a new pavement structural section.  We understand that the 
proposed construction will be a federally funded project. Therefore, a pavement 
justification report conforming to the requirements and guidelines of the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation will be required. The new pavements for the 
project will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement Design 
Manual dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
portions of the roadways under consideration. The findings obtained from our field 
exploration and laboratory testing programs will be used as a basis to formulate 
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the pavement reconstruction for the 
project roadways.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom.  
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane.

Existing Site/Pavement Conditions along Wells Street 
The existing Wells Street within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway between High Street and Waiale Road except for the additional turn 
lanes at the intersection areas. The approximate project limits of the roadway are shown 
on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2.

grass, sidewalks, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils along the shoulder

damp condition. The existing Wells Street roadway alignment slopes down from west to 
east from the intersection of High Street to Kanoa Street, and transitions to a gentle 
slope between the limits of Kanoa Street and Waiale Road.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

3.4 
Design Pavement Sections 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the Wells Street 
alignment under consideration for this project is underlain by clayey soils.  We 
understand that placement of asphalt concrete base (ACB) or Portland Cement 
Concrete (PCC) directly on the clayey subgrade is not recommended.  Therefore, the 
pavement section should consist of an aggregate subbase (ASB) layer between the 
upper ACB or PCC and the underlying clayey subgrade soils. 
In addition, our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the Wakea 
Avenue alignment under consideration for this project is underlain by sandy soils with 
little or no cohesion.  As a result, the subgrade may not be able to compact into a dense 
mass to receive placement of the ACB or PCC layer.  Therefore, we recommend 
providing a 4-inch layer of aggregate subbase between the upper ACB or PCC layer 
and the underlying sandy subgrade soils.  The aggregate subbase will serve to provide 
confinement of the sandy soils and a compacted surface for the placement of the ACB 
or PCC. 
Due to the substantial number of roadway intersections and the long length of the 
roadway alignments, we envision that new pavements for the project would need to be 
constructed in phases.  Because of the poorly-graded nature of the permeable base 
material, we envision that the material could not be compacted into a dense and stable 
mass.  Our experience suggested that the permeable base material would tend to 
unravel or spill out of the completed pavement section when excavation is made for the 
adjacent section.   Therefore, incorporation of a permeable base layer for drainage

subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum 
10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse 
near-surface loose zones. The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of 
about 300 feet per minute. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or

Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignments by drilling 
and sampling seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7. The borings 
extended to depths of about 5 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.4. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
