In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 15 1992 we have drilled a boring
at the site of the proposed tower crane The following are our preliminary findings and
recommendations
Field Exploration
The boring encountered a surface layer approximately 14 5 foot thick of gravel
cobbles and boulders embedded in a soft loose matrix of clay silt and sand The
soft loose surface layer is underlain by generally competent basalt saprolite and extremely
to highly weathered basalt rock formation to the maximum boring depth of 86 feet below
the existing ground surface
Groundwater level was measured at approximately 5 5 to 7 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the time of our field exploration However no groundwater was noted
in a previous adjacent excavation which was approximately 10 feet deep during our initial
site visit
Crane Pad
It is our understanding that a pad foundation is desired to support the tower crane
We also understand that it is considered feasible to remove and replace the soft loose
surface layer below the pad bottom elevation and to dewater the excavation if necessary

Foundation analyses were thus performed on this basis Based on the subsoil
profile encountered we believe that a mat or pad foundation bearing on properly
compacted backfill material may be used to support the proposed tower crane provided
the following foundation subgrade preparation recommendations are closely followed
1 Excavate the soft loose surface soil layer completely to a minimum depth
of 15 feet below the existing ground surface and extend into the underlying
firm in situ saprolite soil The bottom of the excavation should then be
recompacted properly
2 Any soft loose pocket at the excavation bottom should also be removed
and backfilled with select granular material such as Select Borrow
compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
3 Backfill the excavation to the anticipated bottom elevation of the crane pad
at about five 5 feet below the existing ground surface with the Select
Borrow material compacted in maximum eight 8 inch loose level lifts to a
minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density in accordance with ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
If these foundation subgrade recommendations are properly followed we believe
that an allowable bearing capacity of 4 000 p s f may be used to design the crane pad
foundation A passive earth pressure of 200 p s f per foot of depth and a frictional
coefficient of 0 4 may be used to determine the lateral resistance of the pad foundation
Settlements of the pad under the design crane loads are estimated to be on the order of
one 1 inch total and one half 2 inch differential

Dewatering
Based on the measured groundwater levels groundwater should be anticipated
during excavation even though it could be perched groundwater table after heavy rainfall
We recommend that provisions be made to dewater the excavation if necessary
since it is essential to backfill the excavation in a dry condition so that the backfill material
may be properly compacted to reduce the potential of ground settlements under the

It is essential that the soft loose surface layer be completely removed below the
crane pad foundation and that the excavation and any over excavation for soft loose
pockets below the excavation be properly backfilled compacted and or recompacted

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the foundation design of a proposed tower crane to be built in front of the
in bound tunnel of the North Halawa tunnel at Interstate Highway H 3 which is currently
under construction The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report supplements and confirms the information and recommendations
presented in our letter dated May 29 1992
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The 122 foot high tower crane Liebherr model 200HC is expected to transfer high
loadings and bending moments to its foundation during operation
It is our understanding that a 60 foot long drilled pier foundation system was initially
designed for the crane but subsequently changed to a concrete pad foundation 20 feet
square and 4 5 feet thick Construction of the pad was stopped when soft subgrade soils
were encountered to depths of more than ten 10 feet below the design elevation of the
bottom of the concrete pad

During our initial site visit on May 14 1992 we observed many boulders and
cobbles embedded in a clayey silty matrix in the excavation for the tower crane pad
foundation
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this geotechnical exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the site in order to develop a generalized soil rock data set for
the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of
a feasible foundation system for the proposed tower crane giving consideration to the soft
subgrade conditions encountered
The scope of our exploration consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and drill crew to
and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of one 1 boring at the project site to approximately
86 feet below the existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the boring by a
Geologist from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and determining their
engineering properties

SUBSURFACE CONDITION
The exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one 1 boring to a
depth of 86 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate location of the
boring is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The boring encountered a surface layer approximately 14 5 feet thick of gravel
cobbles and boulders embedded in a soft loose matrix of clay silt and sand The
soft loose surface layer is underlain by generally competent basalt saprolite and a highly
to extremely weathered basalt rock formation to the maximum boring depth of 86 feet
below the existing ground surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Crane Pad
It is our understanding that a pad foundation is desired by the contractor to
support the tower crane We also understand that the contractor considers it to be
feasible to remove and replace the soft loose surface layer below the proposed concrete
pad bottom elevation and to dewater the excavation if necessary
These factors were given due consideration during our analysis and formulation
of our design recommendations Based on the subsoil materials encountered we believe
that a mat or pad foundation bearing on properly compacted backfill material may be
used to support the proposed tower crane provided the following foundation subgrade
preparation recommendations are closely followed
1 Excavate the soft loose surface soil layer completely to a minimum depth
of 15 feet below the existing ground surface down to the underlying firm in
situ saprolite soil
2 The bottom of the over excavation should then be backfilled with imported
Select Borrow material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of its

D 1557
3 Backfill the over excavation up to the design elevation of the bottom of the
concrete pad This is required to develop the requisite resistance to the
over turing moment and other structural considerations The backfill should
be placed in about nine inch loose lifts with each loose level lift compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent of its maximum dry density as indicated above
If the soft material below the pad foundation is completely removed and replaced

estimated to be on the order of one inch total and one half inch differential

Dewatering
Based on the measured groundwater levels in our boring groundwater should be
anticipated during excavation as a possible result of a perched water condition and or
the result of heavy rains
We recommend therefore that provisions be made to dewater the excavation if
necessary It is essential to backfill the excavation in a dry condition so that the backfill
material may be properly placed and compacted to reduce the potential of ground
settlement under the design crane loads Backfilling of over excavation under high water
levels will only be acceptable if dewatering of the excavation is continuously maintained
The dewatering method should be determined by the contractor giving due
consideration to the requirement that it will not affect the structural integrity of the
structural backfill and the underlying stiff saprolite subgrade soil
Construction Monitoring
It is critical that all soft loose subsurface materal is completely removed sufficiently
down to the competent formation below the crane pad foundation and that the over
excavation is properly backfilled with select granular material each lift to be compacted
to the specified level of compaction density in dry conditions inside the properly braced
excavation
Therefore we recommend that the project geotechnical engineer be retained to
observe the construction procedures and to monitor the over excavation below the pad
elevation to observe that soft loose material is completely removed and that the backfilling
and compaction of the backfill material is consistently carried out in the field The
recommendations given in this report are contingent upon these observations during
foundation construction

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based upon
information obtained from the boring Variations of conditions from those encountered
at the boring location may occur The nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will
be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The location of the boring was determined by taping from the portal of the inbound
tunnel indicated on a grading plan of the site The physical location of the boring should
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The water level at the boring was observed at the times given on the graphic
representation and or in the text of this report This information has been reviewed and
interpretations have been made in the formulation of this report However it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall temperature and other

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling one 1 boring to
a depth of 86 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate location shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 Truck mounted equipment was used to drill the boring
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard spilt spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches or for fraction of a foot where a full
foot of penetration was not practicable are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Moisture content determinations were performed on selected samples The results
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Log as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Log as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
