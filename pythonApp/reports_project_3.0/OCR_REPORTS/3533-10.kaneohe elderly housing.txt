REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and the soil materials are
transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes
become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3533 10 Page 7
general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows
transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of this transport of
sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly
sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base
levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In
addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older partially
consolidated deposits

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration at the proposed project site consisted of drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths ranging from
about 11 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered very stiff to hard clayey silts and silty
clays throughout the depths explored in the majority of the borings drilled Stiff to very
stiff sandy silt soils were encountered in Boring 4 and 7 at depths ranging from

below the existing ground At the lower portion of the site some of the soils encountered

considered moderately compressible when subjected to large areal loads such as new
fills
Groundwater was encountered in Boring Nos 1 through 4 and 7 during our field
exploration at depths of approximately 10 5 to 25 3 feet below the existing ground
surface These depths generally correspond to elevations of approximately 49 to
54 3 feet MSL It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered very stiff to hard clayey silts and silty
clays throughout the depths in the majority of the borings drilled at the subject site Some
of the borings drilled at the lower portion of the site encountered some soft to medium stiff
soils between depths of about 10 and 20 feet below the surface The soft to medium stiff
soils may be considered moderately compressible when subjected to large areal loads
such as the new fills planned at this portion of the site
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings
and the anticipated structural loads at the community building location we believe that
shallow spread and or continuous footings bearing on the in situ very stiff to hard clayey
soils may be used for support of the proposed community building Because of the
proximity of the apartment buildings to the drainage channel along the east side of the
project site and the planned shallow surface water run off disposal facilities in the area a
lower soil bearing pressure is recommended for the design of foundations for the
apartment buildings Because the foundations of the apartment building will be supported
both on native ground and on compacted general fill we recommend that a 12 inch thick
layer of non expansive select granular fill material be provided below the bottom of the
footings for more uniform bearing support The non expansive select granular fill should
extend laterally a minimum of 1 foot beyond the edges of the footings
Based on the current grading concept we understand that new fills of up to about
8 feet thick will be required to raise the existing ground surface at the lower portion east
side of the project site to achieve the design finish floor elevation of the proposed
apartment buildings Due to the potential presence of soft soils underlying the new fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3533 10 Page 10
areas at depths of about 10 to 20 feet we recommend that the new fills be placed as
soon as practical to induce the anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to the
construction of the foundations Therefore a settlement waiting period will be required
prior to construction of improvements such as building foundations in the fill
In order to reduce the settlement waiting period a temporary surcharge program
with settlement monitoring should be implemented to evaluate the magnitude and rate of

Slab On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface silty clay and clayey silt soils with a swelling potential ranging from slight to
moderate when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless floor slabs constructed above
the slightly to moderately expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for
future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of
these clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for
structural distress resulting from the swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that
the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material The non expansive select granular fill material
should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed building
structures
The non expansive select granular fill material should be placed soon after
moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils The prepared
non expansive select granular fill and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and
kept moist prior to final placement of concrete Subgrade soils should be prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this
report Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and non expansive select
granular fill material are also discussed in the same section of this report
For the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that
a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below the slabs to serve as
a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site clayey
soils or non expansive select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete
slabs the upper 24 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious
material such as general fill composed of the on site clayey soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls

Surcharge Program
As previously mentioned new fills up to about 8 feet thick will be required to raise
the existing ground surface at the lower portion east side of the project site to achieve
the design finish floor elevations for the proposed apartment buildings As previously
indicated some soft to medium stiff soils may be anticipated at depths of about 10 to
20 feet below the ground surface at the lower portion of the site Therefore some
ground settlements will occur when the new fills of up to about 8 feet thick are placed
over the potentially compressible soils
In general the settlement rates for the potentially compressible soils would be
much slower and would require longer settlement waiting periods to reduce the effects
of settlement on the improvements constructed in and on the fills The settlement
waiting period for embankment fills constructed over the soft to medium stiff soils may
take months depending on the nature and thickness of the soft soils Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered and the planned fill height we estimate potential
settlements on the order of about 3 to 4 inches at the lower portion east side of the
project site In addition we estimate that 80 to 90 percent of the primary settlement
would occur in about 4 to 6 months after the new fill is placed Therefore we believe
that the new fills should be placed as soon as practical to allow the anticipated ground
settlements to occur prior to construction of the footings on top of the fill
In order to shorten the 4 to 6 month settlement waiting period a temporary
surcharge program may be considered for the embankment fill placed over the soft
soils The surcharge program would aid in accelerating ground settlements thus
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3533 10 Page 22
reducing the settlement waiting period Based on a surcharge fill height of about 3 feet
above the design finished grades we believe that the settlement waiting period may be
reduced to about 2 to 3 months to achieve approximately 80 to 90 percent
consolidation
In general the excavated on site soils excess materials may be reused as a
source of surcharge fill materials A slope ratio of up to 1H 1V may be used for the
temporary surcharge fill material placed on top of the new fill In general the surcharge
fill may be placed in lifts of about 1 foot high and compacted with a sheepsfoot roller for
a minimum of 3 to 4 passes Adequate precautions must be taken to reduce the
potential for erosion of the surcharge fill slopes during construction and in the
settlement waiting period needed to attain the required consolidation
It should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the exact time
required for the filled ground to settle since the settlement rates will be affected by
variations of the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition For the soft
subsoils anticipated at the project site we believe that the estimated settlement period
could vary by as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual settlement period
Therefore the actual settlement rates should be monitored and a_ settlement
monitoring program should be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the
estimated settlements during the settlement waiting period prior to construction of the
foundations on the fills In addition provisions should be made for potential delays in
the construction schedule if a longer settlement waiting period is required
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that a minimum of
six settlement gauges be installed in areas where new fills are placed over the soft soils
lower portion of the site The settlement gauges should be installed in the lower
portion of the site as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 prior to the fill placement
operations A typical settlement gauge detail is presented on Plate 4 The settlement
gauges should be read optically by a qualified professional surveyor and the readings

recommend that two readings minimum 24 hours apart for each settlement gauge be
taken prior to site filling to establish a baseline Subsequent readings of the settlement

Surface Water Run Off Disposal System
Shallow surface water run off disposal facilities are planned at the proposed
housing site Three probe borings designated as Boring Nos P 1 through P 3 were
drilled to depths ranging from about 4 to 8 6 feet below the existing ground surface at the
proposed surface water run off disposal facility site The borings indicated that the
near surface silty clay and clayey silt soils in this area have low percolation rates
Percolation tests were conducted at various depths to evaluate the infiltration
characteristics of the subsoils to provide data for design of the facilities to be used for
disposal of storm water run off A total of six field percolation tests were performed at
depths of approximately 4 and 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the
six percolation tests conducted the permeability of the upper 8 feet of subsoils range
from about 10 to 10 feet per minute ft min The results of our percolation tests are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 3 of Appendix C Due to the variability of the subsoil
conditions the absorption capacity of the disposal system should be confirmed by
additional percolation tests during construction
The planned surface water run off disposal facilities should be located away from
the building foundations since water leaching into the ground from the disposal facilities
could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent loss of bearing strength of the
foundation soils resulting in foundation settlement and building distress

initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement procedures
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or imported
select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Seven ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and
graphically on Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
One consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on a sample
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 6
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
typical clayey soils encountered at the site The test results are presented on Plates B 9
and B 10

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The field percolation rates of the on site soils were estimated by performing six
falling head percolation tests in Boring Nos P 1 through P 3 The percolation tests were
performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to depths ranging from approximately 4 to
8 6 feet below the existing ground surface Six inches to 1 foot of gravel were then
placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect the bottom of the holes from scouring and
sediments The boreholes were then presoaked for at least 4 hours prior to testing
During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to approximately 2 feet
below the existing ground surface The time intervals and water drops were then
recorded to provide data upon which to base the calculation of the permeability values of
the subsoils The test results are presented on Plates C 1 through C 3
