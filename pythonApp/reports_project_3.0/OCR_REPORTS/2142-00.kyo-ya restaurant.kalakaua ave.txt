INTRODUCTION
In general accordance with our proposal to Hideto Horiike
Associates Inc dated August 31 1988 we have performed a geotechnical
engineering exploration for the proposed redevelopment of Kyo Ya
Restaurant in Waikiki on the island of Oahu Hawaii
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical
engineering recommendations derived from analyses based on field and
laboratory data obtained during our exploration
The discussions and recommendations presented herein are intended for
design input and may not contain sufficient data for construction cost
estimation or bidding purposes

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the Waikiki area of metropolitan
Honolulu near Fort DeRussy The general location of the project site and
its vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The site is situated on the southwest makai side of Kalakaua Avenue
near its intersection with Olohana Street The site is relatively level
and is presently occupied by the existing two story Kyo Ya Restaurant and
a two story commercial structure
The existing restaurant building is of wood frame construction and the
commercial building is constructed of masonry Several vertical and
horizontal cracks were noted on the outside wall of the commercial
building
The northern side of the site abuts the Holiday Inn Hotel parking
structure which has a half basement the bottom of which is 6 feet lower
than the existing ground surface on the project site
Except for the existing buildings the site is covered by asphaltic
concrete and Portland Cement concrete pavements which are used as parking
and loading areas It is believed that sewer water and other utility
lines are present below the existing ground surface
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is currently proposed to redevelop the site for the construction of
a new expanded Kyo Ya restaurant complex The present scheme is to stage
the redevelopment in two 2 phases

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early
Pleistocene by the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae
and Koolau Koolau is the younger of the two and reached the end of its
main shield building phase about 2 million years ago The project site is
located on the southwestern flank of Koolau
As the main shield building on the island Oahu drew to a close the
island began to subside slowly While the island was sinking fringing
coral falgal reefs began to grow on the seaward margins of the island
The project site is located on the Honolulu Coastal Plain a thick
sedimentary wedge composed of intercalated coral algal reef deposits

terrigenous and marine sediments and volcanics of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series In the early part of this century the area was low marshy
wetlands As the city of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was
urbanized man made fills were placed to reclaim the wetland areas
Soil Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and
sampling four 4 borings to depths of 41 5 to 51 5 feet below the
existing ground surface
In general the soil conditions encountered by the drilled borings
appear to be relatively uniform The four 4 borings encountered a
surface layer of man made fill about 8 feet thick This fill is generally
composed of medium dense sands and gravels and forms a crust over
underlying softer or looser materials
Below this fill layer the borings generally encountered very loose to
loose gray lagoonal deposits about 27 feet thick consisting of silt
sand and coral fragments and gravel down to a depth of about 35 feet
Underlying these lagoonal deposits our borings encountered generally
medium dense to dense coral sand and gravel which are detrital deposits
associated with coral algal reefs At the depth of about 40 feet it
appears that there was a denser coral layer about 1 to 2 feet thick A
generalized subsurface soil profile is presented on Plate 3
Groundwater was encountered in the borings during our field
exploration at a depth of about 4 feet below the existing ground surface
It is anticipated that due to the nature of the subsurface materials
encountered and the relative proximity of the ocean the groundwater
levels may fluctuate in response to tidal influences and storm surge
conditions

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration encountered a surface crust about 8 feet thick
of medium dense sand and gravel fill underlain by loose or very loose
lagoonal deposits This upper crust does not have sufficient bearing
capacity to support the anticipated 120 kip column loads which will be
imposed by the proposed structures without inducing significant foundation
settlements
Our preliminary estimates indicate footing settlements on the order of
5 to 6 inches Therefore we recommend that pile foundations be used to
support the proposed parking and restaurant structures
Pre drilling through the upper dense crust is recommended for the line
of piles located along the northern Holiday Inn Hotel boundary to minimize
pile driving vibration damage to the adjacent basement structure
Pile Foundations
Our field exploration did not encounter a distinct coral layer with
sufficient strength and thickness to develop refusal for end bearing
piles Therefore we recommend that piles developing frictional
resistance in the lower medium dense to dense coral sands and gravels be
used to support the proposed structures It is our opinion that 12 inch
square prestressed precast concrete piles driven into the coral sand and
gravel extending about 35 to 43 feet from the existing ground surface
should be capable of supporting an allowable design load of 40 tons each
Vertical Pile Capacity
Friction piles would develop their vertical load capacities primarily
from frictional resistance between the piles and the surrounding
soils Some capacity is also developed from the bearing of the pile
tip against the subsoils below the pile

Based on our exploration data and analyses friction piles for the
project driven to obtain a minimum of 43 foot embedment below the
existing ground surface would be able to develop an allowable pile
capacity of 40 tons each Actual pile lengths could vary between 35
to 43 feet below the ground surface However we believe a better
estimate of the production pile length required may be obtained by
implementing a test pile program as discussed below
The piles should be driven with a hammer delivering about 26 000 foot
pounds of energy per blow Some of the piles may stop at a depth of
less than about 43 feet if the coral ledge encountered at that depth
in the pile locations has sufficient thickness and or strength to
block the penetration of piles The recommended pile driving criteria
are as follows
1 Each pile should be cast in one continuous section
2 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for
each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity
modified when the driving process is interrupted for more than
four 4 hours
3 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft
soil the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled reduced
energy so that excessive tensile stresses are not transmitted to
the pile
4 The pile must be driven to the coral sand and gravel layer which
is about 35 feet below the existing ground surface The pile
driving may be terminated when the blow counts steadily increase
with depth and reach 8 blows per inch for each of the last 3
inches or when aggregated blow counts are 50 blows for the last
foot

These criteria are based on the use of a diesel driving hammer with a
rated energy of 26 000 foot pounds to drive a 12 inch square concrete pile
with an allowable design load capacity of 40 tons The driving criteria
should be modified according to the actual energy of the hammer used
Based on the above recommendations pile settlements are estimated to be
on the order of less than one half of an inch total
We recommend that both the test and production pile driving be
monitored by the project geotechnical engineer to observe that the
friction piles are driven continuously to the required depths and that
sufficient resistance has been developed
Based on our recent analysis the allowable lateral pile capacity is
sixteen 16 kips per pile under a lateral displacement of 0 25 inch
The piles may be considered to be fixed at about 18 foot depths from
the present grade Detailed descriptions of our lateral load analyses
are presented in Appendix Cc
Test Pile Program
A test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following main
objectives
1 To examine the drivability of piles with the use of one or more
selected hammers
2 To verify that pre drilling through the upper fill layer is not
required for piles located away from the adjacent Holiday Inn
Hotel site
3 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the driving criteria
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2142 00 Page 9
DK No 005
4 To more accurately determine the production pile order lengths
To achieve these objectives we recommend that about 10 percent of the
total piles be test driven at the site The test piles should be
about 45 feet long and may later be incorporated into the actual
foundation
Splice cans should be available at the site during test pile driving
Splice cans may be fabricated with 1 4 inch thick high grade steel and
should be at least 6 feet long
As a part of the test pile program a minimum of one pile load test is
recommended The selected test pile should be load tested to twice
the allowable load capacity in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1143
procedures
The test piles should be driven with the same hammer which is to be
used for production pile driving The test pile program should be
conducted under close and continuous observation of the geotechnical
engineer who will determine when piles may be stopped and to provide
guidance should additional measures be required due to unforeseen
driving conditions The geotechnical engineer should evaluate the
final load carrying pile capacity and production pile embedment
lengths based on the results of the test pile driving and the pile
load test
Site Grading
Based on the present plans site grading up to 2 to 3 feet high is
anticipated for the proposed construction It is important that fill
placement should be placed as soon as practicable to allow ground
settlements to occur first prior to pile installation and slab
construction to reduce the effect of potential ground settlements on these
construction

At the onset of earthwork the project area within the grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All demolition waste debris
A C pavements rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site to prevent contamination of the
excavated materials
After clearing the area should be scarified to a depth of at least 6
inches below the cleared or finished grade and proofrolled for a minimm
of six 6 passes with a heavy compactor Any soft yielding or pumping
areas detected during the proofrolling should be overexcavated to firm
ground and the resulting void backfilled with select non expansive
material compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density per ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78
The on site surface fill soils may be reused as a source of fill
and or backfill material Imported fill material should consist of
non expansive select granular materials less than 3 inches in maximum size
with less than 20 passing the 200 sieve
Again the required fill should be placed as soon as possible and in
maximum 8 inch thick horizontal lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 of
its maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Slabs on Grade
Slabs on grade may be used for the first floor of the proposed
structures We recommend that a minimm of 4 inches of base course
compacted to 95 of the ASTM D 1557 78 maximum density be placed as a slab
cushion The pouring of the slabs should be delayed as much as possible
to allow for fill settlements to occur first
An impervious moisture proofing membrane should be considered below
the slab of the restaurant building to protect the floor coverings from
damage by moisture infiltration

part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings and
probes Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur
and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were determined by taping from
structures indicated on the topographic survey map dated February 23
1988 provided by Community Planning Inc Elevations of the data points
were determined by estimation from elevation points on the same
topographic survey map The physical location and elevation of the field
data points should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by
the method used

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content determinations were performed on selected samples as
an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
Two 2 sieve analysis tests were also performed to aid in the soil
classification The test results are summarized on Plate B 1

APPENDIX C
Lateral Load Analysis of Piles
Lateral load analysis was performed using Program PILEDG which is a
microcomputer adaption of a finite difference laterally loaded pile
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The
program solves for deflection and bending moment along a pile under
lateral loads as a function of depth
The analysis was carried out with the use of internally generated
non linear P Y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection
was then computed using appropriate soil moduli at various depths The
analysis was performed for a laterally loaded 12 inch square pile
Since the soil modulus varies with the deflection experienced the
computerized analysis first assumes a modulus value then calculates the
pile deflection based on the assumed modulus The calculated deflection
is then used to determine the modulus from the non linear P Y curves The
calculated modulus and the assumed modulus are then compared The
procedure is repeated until the newly assumed and calculated moduli
converge
The results of the lateral load vs pile deflection analysis are
summarized on Plate C 1 for a 12 inch square pile
