SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of 
about 4 and 5 inches of AC over about 3 to 8 inches of the granular base material. 
Underlying the pavement structure, relatively stiff and medium dense fill followed by stiff 
to very stiff older alluvium was encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 
16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was not encountered in the 
drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on our field exploration and our experience in the project vicinity, we 
anticipate that the on-site near-surface clayey soils have a low to moderate expansion 
potential if maintained near its moisture content. Therefore, we recommend placing a 
minimum 12-inch thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill material (capping fill) 
below the planned concrete slabs-on-grade to reduce moisture changes in the slab 
subgrade soils. 
We understand that new catch basin structures will be constructed for the 
project. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used. To provide uniform 
bearing support for the new drainage structures, we recommend providing a minimum 
6-inch thick layer of No. 3 Fine gravel below the bottom of the pre-cast concrete catch 
basins. 
In general, the on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material, 
provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater 
than 3 inches in maximum dimension.  
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, 
and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. Based 
on the test results, we believe the project site should be classified as Corrosion 
Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) based on the updated Board of Water 
Supply External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021. It may be appropriate 
to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide 
detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Mahoe Street Improvements project in Waipahu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
concrete slabs-on-grade, underground utility structures, underground utilities, site 
grading, and corrosion potential only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. A separate Pavement 
Justification Report will be prepared by our office for the design of new pavements for

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano 
ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope 
forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is 
located on the southeastern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in-situ chemical weathering to 
depths of 50 to 150 feet and are characterized by red highlights in the soil.  This in-situ 
chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite over 
basalt rock formation with depth.  Based on radiometric measurements, the 
decomposition of these soils has taken place over approximate 1-2 million years. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. These sediments 
accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral/algal reef formations to 
form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero 
in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This 
wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over 
the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued 
from earlier geologic time through the present. 
Based on geologic maps, the project site is underlain by a basaltic rock formation 
on the northern portion of the site and alluvial deposits on the southern portion of the 
site. The geologic boundary falls approximately halfway between Huakai Street and 
Mahoe Place.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 4 
and 5 inches of AC over about 3 to 8 inches of the granular base material. Underlying 
the pavement structure, relatively stiff and medium dense fill followed by stiff to very stiff 
older alluvium was encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled 
borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our field exploration and our experience in the project vicinity, we 
anticipate that the on-site near-surface clayey soils have a low to moderate expansion 
potential if maintained near its natural moisture content. Therefore, we recommend 
placing a minimum 12-inch thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill material 
(capping fill) below the planned concrete slabs-on-grade to reduce moisture changes in 
the slab subgrade soils. 
In general, the on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material, 
provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater 
than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported fill materials should consist of crushed 
basalt or coral. Detailed discussions and recommendations for these items and other 
geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We understand that concrete slabs-on-grade may be utilized for the proposed 
bulb-outs, speed tables, and sidewalk reconstruction. Based on our field exploration and 
our experience in the project vicinity, we anticipate that the on-site near-surface clayey 
soils have a low to moderate expansion potential if maintained near its moisture 
content. Therefore, we recommend placing a minimum 12-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill material (capping fill) below the slabs to reduce 
moisture changes in the slab subgrade soils. The capping fill should be compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material such as 
coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in its largest dimension. The material should 
also contain between 10 and 30 percent fines (particles passing the No. 200 sieve). 
Select granular fill should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 
or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Prior to placing the non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend scarifying 
the subgrade soils to a depth of about 10 inches, moisture conditioning the soils to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacting to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The select granular fill and the underlying subgrade 
soils should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete. Where 
shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend 
preparing the soils again as recommended. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the 
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require 
over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with engineered fill. 
Control joints for the concrete slabs-on-grade should be provided at intervals 
equal to the width of the slab with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. The 
thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slab edges should be 
backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils. These 
areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the 
potential for water ponding around the slabs.

3.2.1 Underground Utility Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing pressure 
of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for the design of the 
underground utility structures bearing on the relatively stiff/dense fill and older 
alluvium at the project site. To provide uniform bearing support for the new 
drainage structures, we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick layer of 
No. 3 Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the bottom of the 
drainage structures. We anticipate the catch basins will be pre-cast concrete 
structures. 
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface 
prior to the placement of the No. 3 Fine gravel. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of the excavations should be over-excavated to 
expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled 
with select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  
Based on our engineering analyses, we believe that the net increase in bearing 
pressure on the underlying stiff/dense fill and older alluvium resulting from the 
drainage structure construction would be minimal. Settlements on the order of 
less than 1 inch are anticipated for the drainage structures.

3.3.4 Excavations 
Based on the anticipated grading, excavations up to about 12 feet below the 
existing ground surface will be required to install the new catch basins. In 
addition, trench excavations may be required to install new underground utilities. 
We anticipate these excavations may encounter the relatively stiff and medium 
dense fills and older alluvium encountered in our borings. It is anticipated that 
most of the material may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
However, cobbles and boulders are frequently encountered in older alluvium 
deposits. Therefore, excavations that encounter hard cobbles and boulders may 
require the use of hoerams and/or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on field data from the borings drilled at the site. Contractors should be 
encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make their 
own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
3.4 
Underground Utility Lines 
We anticipate that underground utilities may be required for the project. In 
general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling 
of the trenches for the new utilities. The contractor should determine the method and 
equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal, 
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench

In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above

3.5 
Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, 
and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. The 
test results are summarized and presented in Appendix B. Detailed results of the pH 
testing (EPA SW9045), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) 
performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. are presented in Appendix C. 
Based on the updated version of the Board of Water Supply, City and County of 
Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021, 
there are two soil corrosivity categories. Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) 
applies to soil meeting any one of the following conditions. Corrosion Category B 
(Negligibly to Mildly Corrosive) is applicable when none of the following conditions are 
met. The updated version of the standards is effective as of the publishing date for 
Oahu only. 
• Resistivity ≤ 5,000 ohm-cm 
• pH ≤ 6.5 
• Water-soluble chloride concentration ≥ 500 ppm

• Water-soluble sulfate concentration ≥ 1,000 ppm 
• Within fluctuating saltwater table 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, 
the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic 
structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to 
the corrosion of metallic objects. Results of the laboratory testing indicate that the 
on-site soils have a resistivity value of about 3,000 ohm-cm with a pH value of 6.36. 
Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered as Corrosion Category A 
(Moderately to Severely Corrosive) based on the updated Board of Water Supply 
External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021. 
In addition, chloride content and sulfate content testing were performed by 
TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils 
encountered. Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site 
soils, the test values are about 44 parts per million (ppm) for chloride content and 320 
ppm for sulfate content. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion 
engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion 
protection. Detailed results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300.0) testing, performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc., are presented in 
Appendix C.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling six exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to 
depths of about 6.5 and 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-3, were collected 
for laboratory R-value testing. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid stem augers. The approximate locations of the drilled 
borings and bulk samples are shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.6. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in our laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance 
with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering 
Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 
are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through 
A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth, and a 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected 
in-situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site soils. 
The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
The stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented on 
Plates B-2 through B-5. 
One set of Corrosion test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM 
G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed 
by our office and TestAmerica laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from 
our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-6. 
Three laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed on 
selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-9.
