SITE DESCRIPTION
The Ewa Villages project site is located in the Honouliuli area of the Ewa
District on the island of Oahu to the west of Fort Weaver Road The Ewa Villages site
encompasses approximately 150 acres in the vicinity of Ewa Town Renton Village
and Verona Village
Much of the site is currently used for the cultivation of sugar cane The existing
village areas are comprised of old plantation village style housing generally consisting
of single wall wood framed houses and community buildings The existing structures
are in varying states of repair Portions of the site in the vicinity of Ewa Mill were
previously used for mill and railroad operations
The topography is generally level with a very slight slope in the southerly
direction Existing ground surface elevations range from about 35 to 65 feet above
Mean Sea Level The general trend of surface drainage is in a southerly direction
with several existing drainage and irrigation ditches traversing the site
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two
shielded volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ended lava flows from
Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau
Weathering and erosion of this plateau generated sediments which were
transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south these
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 6
alluvial sediments accumulated along with and interbedded with marine sediments
and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness
from zero feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This
wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over
the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has
continued from earlier geologic time through the present

Subsurface Conditions
Based on our current field exploration the soils encountered at the project site
may be generalized as follows
Coral Algal Deposits
This unit is the oldest of the soils and geologic units encountered at the project
site and consists of fossil remnants of coral aigal reefs and their related detrital
debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant
portion of any coral rock assemblage
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 7
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral debris consisting
of gravel sand and silt sized clastic fragments Some of the coral fragments
were dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented and or
uncemented fine sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the Coral Algal Deposits and the
degree of cementation frequently present this formation generally has high in
situ strength characteristics Structurally the Coral Algal Deposits are capable
of supporting heavy foundation loads However in the current exploration
Coral Algal deposits were generally encountered only in the deepest portion
of Boring 104 and 106
Older Alluvium
The Older Alluvium is assigned the position of being the second oldest unit in
the stratigraphic column of the project area even though some of the
Coral Algal Deposits may be intercalated with the alluvial deposits
This unit generally overlies the Coral Algal Deposits and consists of terrigenous
sediments which have been transported by stream action from the upper
reaches of the streams The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard
brown silty clays and clayey silts Gravel lenses representing buried stream
channels are sometimes encountered Portions of the Older Alluvium unit
contain some weathered basalt boulders and cobbles Although no
groundwater was encountered during our field exploration these coarse
grained parts of the unit may also contain perched groundwater in isolated
areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 8
The Older Alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength values resulting in
relatively high bearing capacities Frequently these materials show a
moderate to high shrink swell potential Structurally the Older Alluvium
materials are considered to be sufficiently competent to support relatively
heavy structural loads with proper design
Older Alluvium deposits were encountered in most of the borings drilled and
sampled at the site The Older Alluvium consists primarily of stiff to hard dark
brown to brown clayey silts to silty clays with some sandy and gravelly
interbeds This unit is differentiated from the younger alluvial soils by exhibiting
a stiffer or harder consistency often accompanied with weak cementation
lower moisture contents and a higher proportion of granular interbeds
Alluvium
Recent to late Pleistocene alluvial deposits Alluvium were encountered in the
upper portions of some of the borings drilled and sampled at the subject site
These alluvial soils consist primarily of medium stiff to stiff dark brown to brown
silty clays and clayey silts These soils are distinguished from the Older
Alluvium by having a slightly softer consistency generally higher moisture
contents and very few interbeds of granular soils The unit also includes upper
soils which have been somewhat disturbed during deep tilling operations
In general most of the stiff to hard clay soils in both the Older Alluvium and
Alluvium units may be classified as having low to moderately high swelling expansion
potential when wetted However clays with high shrink swell potential have been
encountered at nearby locations and are anticipated to exist at the site

site in Boring Nos 107 and 108 at depths ranging from 10 5 to 18 3 feet below the
existing ground surface This appears to be a perched condition and the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the development project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view provided that our recommendations presented herein are
followed
In general our current field exploration indicates that the project site is
underlain by a surface layer of medium stiff to hard silty clays overlying medium
dense to dense coral gravel deposits The surface clay soils vary in thickness from
approximately 15 3 feet to the maximum depth explored of 26 5 feet in Boring No
107
Based on our laboratory test results the on site silty clays have swell potentials
that range from low to moderately high
It is our opinion that spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the proposed structures if the foundations and subgrades are properly
designed
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of earthwork the site should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed of the existing pavement vegetation and other deleterious
GEOLABS HAWAII

materials Abandoned utilities should be removed and the trenches filled with
engineered backfill as described in the Utilities section of this report Deleterious

Stripped asphaltic concrete paving material may be incorporated into general
fill materials provided that the material is reduced to less than three 3 inches in

If additional imported material is required the material should be non
expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock or cinder
sand The material should be well graded with no particles greater than

Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site and in its vicinity
indicate that generally the upper 15 feet of the site is underlain by medium stiff to
hard silty clays It is generally anticipated that excavation for infrastructure installation
and for the drainage channel along the southerly boundary of the site may be

Utilities
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility line trenching at the site
The subsurface materials encountered at the site are generally competent
However areas of loose materials may be encountered Trench shoring design and
installation should be the sole responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and
bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
Utilities should be placed on a six inch cushion of 3B Fine gravel ASTM C
33 No 67 gradation The self compacting granular backfill such as 3B Fine
should be used around the pipe and extend 12 inches above the top of the pipe It
is critical to use this self compacting material to minimize the potential of formation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 13
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides
of the pipes which could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The use of on site clayey soils as backfill around utility pipes is not acceptable
Trench backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches
in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent maximum dry density
per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 In roadway and structural areas the upper two
2 feet of backfill below finished subgrade should be compacted to 95 percent
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials
encountered at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be
used to attempt to compact the on site soils
Foundation Support and Slab on Grade
It is our understanding that a bus shelter is planned for this phase of the
project The structure will entail column footings and slab on grade construction
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
on site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select material be used
for a minimum depth of eighteen 18 inches below foundation or slab subgrade
This select material should be non expansive granular material such as crushed
coral mudrock or cinder The material should be well graded with no particles
greater than three 3 inches in size with a laboratory CBR swell value not exceeding
one 1 percent and CBR value of 20 or more It should not contain large
percentages of boulders organic or other deleterious material It should contain
sufficient fines so that there is a minimum of 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve
and should be capable of forming a competent stable mass when compacted
W O 2819 00 Page 14
Laboratory testing to evaluate the swelling potential of the upper fill soils is still
in progress Visual examination of the soils indicates that they generally have low to
moderate swelling potential
An allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f may be utilized for the design of
spread footing foundations bearing on subgrade soils prepared in accordance with
the recommendations presented in this report This value may be increased by
one third for temporary wind and seismic loads
The footings and the thickened edges for slabs on grade should be embedded
a minimum of eight 8 inches below the lowest adjacent subgrade The footing and
slab subgrade should be thoroughly moisture conditioned and kept moist prior to
concrete pour If the materials below the slab on grade are found to be expansive
a capping layer of non expansive select borrow with a minimum depth of eighteen
18 inches should be placed below the slab subgrade

Subdrainage
It has been our experience with projects in the Ewa area and with projects
having similar soil conditions to find that when a granular select material is used as
subbase course or as a capping over clay soils there is a tendency for water to
accumulate at the interface between the select material and the underlying clay soils
It appears that the granular select material is relatively permeable and that the
clay soils are effectively impermeable Precipitation and landscape irrigation water
infiltrate through the topsoil into the select material and form a perched water table
on the clay
Problems have occurred when this water reaches the subgrade of pavement
section prisms and when the select material daylights on top of the clay When the
water seeps into road subgrades it can soften the subgrade soils and weaken the
pavement section Where the seepage daylights continuously wet areas can
develop
To minimize the potential of accumulation of water along the select material
and clay soil interface we recommend that subdrains be installed to intercept water
and divert it to appropriate drainage locations such as drain inlets Generally the
worst problems have occurred where the water moves into road subgrades
Therefore we recommend that subdrains be installed along the sides of the road
prisms The subdrains could either be of the conventional type i e a perforated pipe
embedded in free draining gravel or one of the newer synthetic types e g MiraDrain
or ConTech 100

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit determinations were
performed on selected soil samples obtained The results are presented on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths The Atterberg Limits are summarized
on Plate B 1 thru B 3
Eight 8 CBR tests were performed on surface bulk sample to evaluate
subgrade soil strength for pavement design The test results are summarized on
Plates B 1 thru B 3
Seven 7 determinations of Maximum Dry Density were performed to aid in the
Classification of the near surface soils
Four 4 one inch ring swell tests were performed on undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in both natural and
compacted conditions The test results are summarized on Plate B 4
Two 2 direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed fine grained soil
samples to evaluate their compressibility and settlement characteristics The test
results are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
