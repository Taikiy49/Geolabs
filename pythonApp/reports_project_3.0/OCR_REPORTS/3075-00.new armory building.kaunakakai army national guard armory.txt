INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed New Kaunakakai Army National Guard Armory in the town
of Kaunakakai on the island of Molokai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3075 00 Page 2
A letter dated was issued by our office presenting our preliminary
recommendations for the subject project
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our design level geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for
design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Kaunakakai Army National Guard
Armory in the town of Kaunakakai on the island of Molokai Hawaii It is proposed to
demolish the existing armory building and construct a new armory at its location The
new armory will be a one story structure covering approximately 8 500 square feet in plan
dimension We understand that the new building will be a steel framed structure with
concrete slab on grade and perimeter masonry walls Based on the information provided
to us maximum column loads are estimated to be on the order of 20 kips per column
with wall loads of approximately two 2 kips per lineal foot Construction of new utility
lines and asphalt pavements for the parking areas are also anticipated for the project
construction
Based on the proposed finish floor elevations of 30 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
grading consisting of minor cuts and fills on the order of one foot 1 is anticipated to
provide a level building pad for the proposed construction Our previous experience in
the area suggest that the project site may be underlain by alluvial soils over decomposed
basalt material Special attention will be given to the potential swelling characteristics of
the near surface soils on the future floor slabs supported on grade

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the existing Kaunakakai Army National Guard
Armory site in the town of Kaunakakai on the island of Molokai Hawaii The existing
armory is situated approximately at the northeast corner of Homeolu and Kolapa Place
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The armory site is rectangular in shape and the property is bounded by a chain
linked fence An existing armory building and two smaller structures are located on the
western and northern sides of the site respectively
The terrain at the armory site is relatively level except at the northern portion where
the ground surface grades upward at a gradient of about ten percent 10 Based on

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three 3 borings
to depths ranging between 15 and 17 feet below the existing ground surface
In general our exploration encountered a very thin surface layer of clayey silt of
approximately one 1 to three feet 3 thick overlying in situ basaltic rock formation

year

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Our field exploration indicates that the site is underlain by stiff clayey silt soils over
hard basalt formation at shallow depths Based on the competent subsoil conditions
encountered at the project site it is our opinion that conventional shallow foundations
consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed armory building
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3075 00 Page 6
Laboratory tests performed on the on site clayey silt soils indicate that the material
generally has a moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture content
fluctuations Therefore we recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on
a layer of non expansive select borrow capping fill to reduce the potential effects of the
moderately expansive near surface soils Detailed recommendations are presented in the
following subsections
Shallow Foundations
We understand from conversations with the project structural engineer that light
structural column loads on the order of 20 kips per column are anticipated for the
proposed construction Because of the generally competent subsoil conditions
encountered at the site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may
be used for support of the proposed new armory building
An allowable bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be
used for design of footing foundations bearing on the stiff in situ soils encountered at
shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 4 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade We estimate the footing settlements under the anticipated design
loads for footings bearing on the stiff in situ soils to be less than 1 inch total and inch
differential
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3075 00 Page 7
All footings should either bear entirely on the in situ stiff soils or on the underlying
basaltic rock formation For footings that straddle across soil and rock formation the
footing subgrades should be over excavated to a depth of at least twelve inches 12 and
replaced with compacted select granular fill for more uniform footing bearing support
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff in situ soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footing excavations be compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over
excavation should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom be extended down to the compacted
firm in situ soil
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm foundation bearing
conditions and the embedment depths

Slab On Grade
The project site is underlain by a layer of clayey silt soils which are considered to
be moderately expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless slab supported
by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to lightly
loaded slab on grade construction resulting from the shrinking and swelling of these soils
due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress
resulting from the swelling of the on site moderately expansive soils we recommend that
the concrete slabs be supported on 12 inches of non expansive select fill material The
non expansive select fill should extend at least two feet 2 beyond the perimeter of the
proposed structure
The select fill material should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification
and recompaction of the existing subgrade soils Subgrade soils should be prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this
report The select fill would serve as a capping fill layer of moist soil above the underlying
potentially expansive subgrade soils and this capping fill would also reduce the potential
loss of moisture from the above prepared moist subgrade soils The reduction in future
subgrade saturation should reduce the potential swelling of the underlying moderately
expansive clayey soils Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and select fill are
discussed in the Site Grading section of this report
The prepared capping fill and the underlying subgrade soil should be wetted and
kept moist prior to final placement of concrete In accordance with the requirements of
D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61 dated a basaltic termite barrier
BTB will need to be incorporated into the design of new facilities Therefore concrete
floor slabs should be supported on a six inch 6 layer of Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB
above the underlying capping fill The BTB layer will serve as a slab cushion and termite
barrier To reduce potential moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3075 00 Page 9
coverings a moisture barrier such as a plastic membrane on top of the six inch 6
basaltic terminate barrier layer is recommended A two inch 2 layer of BTB should be
placed over the vapor barrier membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to
aid in curing of the slab concrete
Where slab is subjected to vehicular traffic a six inch 6 layer of base course
compacted to 95 percent relative compaction is recommended in lieu of the six inch 6
BTB layer Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints
which are subjected to vehicular traffic
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded
at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Site Grading
We anticipate that site grading for the proposed project will consist of minor cuts
and fills on the order of about one foot 1 to provide a level building pad for the
proposed construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Slabs and foundations of the existing structure that is to be demolished should be
completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted select granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with
horizontal lifts of compacted select granular fill material Utilities that are to be abandoned
in place should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3075 00 Page 10
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs and
pavement areas should be over excavated to a depth of at least 12 inches below the
future slab or pavement subgrade level such as bottom of BTB or base course If zones
of soft loose or saturated soils are encountered at the over excavated subgrade level
deeper over excavation may be required to expose firm material The resulting excavation
should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material The clayey material
resulting from the over excavation should be removed and properly disposed of off site
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation of the structure
we anticipate that a relatively small amount of over excavation of the surficial expansive
soils would be required
Areas to receive fills should be scarified to a depth of eight inches 8 moisture
conditioned to at least two percent 2 above optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Select granular fill material should
be placed in level lifts not exceeding ten inches 10 in loose thickness and compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
The select granular fill material should consist of imported granular material such
as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles greater than three inches 3 in largest dimension The
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher
and a swell potential of not more than one percent 1 when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain less than 20 percent passing the No
200 sieve Any imported fill material should be tested and approved for its intended use
by our firm prior to delivery to the project site

Pavement Design
Asphaltic concrete pavements for the access driveway and parking area are
anticipated to serve the new structure We anticipate the vehicle loading for this project
will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light army trucks We have made our
preliminary pavement designs assuming the pavement subgrade soil will consist of at
least 12 inches of imported select granular fill material Based on the above assumptions
we recommend the following pavement sections be used for preliminary design purposes
3 0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inches Base Course 95 percent compaction
9 0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on 12 Inches of
Compacted Select Granular Fill
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and density tests should be performed
on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy
of the above section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping
in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of two
inches 2 into the subgrade soil below the base course aggregate to reduce migration
of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we recommend a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to
paved landscape areas

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of six inches 6 of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
is recommended under the pipes Self compacting granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up
to about one foot 1 above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and
to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging
the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot 1 above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material
The backfill should be placed in maximum ten inch 10 level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper two feet
2 of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to 95
percent relative compaction
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it is our opinion that trench work for utility installation will involve excavation
in the basaltic rock formation and it will require hard ripping or hoerams Care must be
exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the
rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3075 00 Page 13
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations
Site Coefficient
Basalt rock formation was encountered at relatively shallow depths of about one
foot 1 at the site Based on this information the project site may be classified as being
a stiff soil or rock site corresponding to an S soil profile with a soil site coefficient of 1 0
Table No 23 J of the Uniform Building Code 1991

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
3 borings to depths ranging between 15 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
