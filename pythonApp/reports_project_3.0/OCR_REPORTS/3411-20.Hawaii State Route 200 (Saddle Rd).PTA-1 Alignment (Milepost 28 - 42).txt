SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field exploration program it appears that the majority
of the proposed Saddle Road PTA 1 alignment is predominantly underlain by weakly
cemented poorly graded sands and moderately cemented conglomerate materials that
overlie the underlying a a basalt rock formation Volcanic ash and pahoehoe basalt rock
formations are exposed at the ground surface near the eastern terminus of the roadway
alignment In general we believe that a large portion of the excavated materials may
be used as a source of fill materials for embankment construction provided that the
materials are properly processed and placed in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Grading section of this report
The near surface volcanic ash soils if used as a source of fill materials should
be blended with the cinder sands alluvial sands and or basalt rock fragments to
provide a relatively well graded material prior to placement as fills for the construction of
embankments Because the volcanic ash soils may be potentially thixotropic in nature
when wet significant strength may be temporarily lost when remolded and or
disturbed Furthermore the volcanic ash soils are highly susceptible to wind and or
surface water erosion Therefore appropriate soil erosion protection dust control
provisions and earthwork construction procedures should be observed and
implemented during the earthwork operations when volcanic ash soils are involved If it
is necessary to utilize the pure volcanic ash soils as fill materials the contractor should
be made aware that additional time and effort likely will be involved with the fill
placement operations In general we believe that cut slopes exposing the volcanic ash
deposits should be designed with a slope ratio of 3H 1V or flatter
The excavated cinder sand and alluvial sand materials encountered in our
exploration may be used as a source of fill materials or blended with volcanic ash soils
and basalt rock fragments to provide a relatively well graded fill material Based on our
experience the cinder sands and alluvial sands may experience a volume decrease of
about 10 to 20 percent when excavated processed and used as fill material In
general cut slopes exposing cinder sand and alluvial sand deposits may be designed
with a slope ratio of 2H 1V or flatter However it should be noted that the cinder sands
and alluvial sands are highly susceptible to erosion by wind and or surface water
runoff and should be adequately protected from erosion by appropriate means
In general cut materials generated from excavations into the surface
conglomerate and underlying basalt formation may be used as a source of fill and

However we understand that the County of Hawaii has continued to provide
maintenance work for Saddle Road up to this date

2 1 Regional Geology
The Hawaiian Archipelago consists of an elongated island chain comprising the
eight major islands of the State of Hawaii and hundreds of small islets and atolls
extending for more than 1 000 miles to the northwest of the Hawaiian Island of Niihau
The age of the islands in the Hawaiian Archipelago increases progressing from the
southeast toward the northwest direction
The Hawaiian Archipelago is believed to have formed as a result of plate tectonic
movement working in combination with a relatively deep stationary focal point of volcanic
heat and magma lava generation The Pacific Plate containing the upper earth s crust
underlying the Central Pacific Ocean continues to slowly move in a northwesterly
direction while the nearly stationary hot spot of magma generation continues to produce
eruptive emissions beneath the plate As a result a continuous chain of volcanic islands
formed and has slowly moved off in the northwesterly direction carried away from the
active center of volcanism by plate movement The Island of Hawaii is presently located
adjacent to the deep seated hot spot region The most distant volcanic island remnants
located far to the northwest of Hawaii have been reduced to submerged seamounts as a
result of a long history of land erosion and subsidence
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 030 square miles The island was formed by the coalescence of five
primary shield volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which
has had some activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are considered to be active Due to the
coalescence of the primary voicano fianks the oldest lower flanks have been
overlapped and buried deeply by more recent volcanic eruption of lava thus the
evolution of the broad dome shape of the Island of Hawaii that is visible today
As an illustration of volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii over the past
150 years or so Mauna Loa Volcano has averaged a caldera eruption approximately

has spanned continuously since about 1983 Therefore the southern and eastern

A sixth volcano Loihi which is presently an active submarine volcano lies

2 2 Local Site Geology
The proposed Saddle Road PTA 1 alignment is located in the vicinity of the
Department of Army s Pohakuloa Training Area PTA in the District of Hamakua on the
island of Hawaii The PTA is located on the western side of the Humuula Saddle which
forms the central upland portion of the Island of Hawaii located between the Mauna Kea
and Mauna Loa Volcanoes The Humuula Saddle is a broad relatively flat to gently
sloping plateau situated between the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes The
regional terrain rises from the east Hilo side and from the west Waikoloa side to crest
at the Humuula Saddle which is located just to the west of the intersection of the
existing Saddle Road and the Mauna Kea Observatory access road The Humuula
Saddle formed as a result of the coalescence of lava flows erupted from the Mauna Kea
and Mauna Loa Volcanoes
In general the proposed Saddle Road PTA 1 alignment traverses the foot of the
steeper south and southwest facing slopes comprising the upper mass of Mauna Kea
Volcano The south and southwest facing slopes of Mauna Kea rise abruptly from the
surrounding flatter terrain of the Humuula Saddle Several prominent cinder cone vents

The flatter terrain located at the foot of the Mauna Kea mountain slopes in the
vicinity of the PTA is underlain by surface alluvial and fluvial sediments that appear to
have been consolidated to form weakly to moderately cemented conglomerate
materials which may be classified as sedimentary rock The weakly to moderately
cemented sediments consist of well graded sands with embedded gravel cobble and
boulder clasts that exhibit a rounded shape which is characteristic of fluvial materials
that have been eroded and transported by the work of flowing water
Previous studies by H T Stearns have characterized the deposits as
fan glomerate owing to the apparent deposition as alluvial fan features It is believed
that the cemented character of the conglomerate deposits is due to the rapid fluid
transportation and quick deposition of the materials from extreme runoff events and
associated debris flows The large scale runoff events are hypothesized to have
occurred when volcanic eruptions rapidly melted portions of the historic Pleistocene age
glacial icecap atop Mauna Kea Volcano The melted water sent torrents of heated
debris flows containing rock and sediment down the mountain flanks and gullies to flood
the gentle slopes in the vicinity of the Humuula Saddle
The alluvial fan deposits appear to be thicker at the mouths of the larger gullies
carved into the southwestern facing slopes of Mauna Kea Volcano Two of the more
prominent gullies are the Pohakuloa and Waikahalulu Gulches which are located
northerly of the eastern portion of the project site There are a number of smaller
unnamed gullies along the southwestern facing slopes that are believed to have
contributed to the deposition of the alluvial materials located at the foot of the mountain
The alluvial fans may have coalesced to produce the gently sloping uniform
topographic character of the region Based on our observations the consolidated
sandy materials grade to very coarse conglomerate consisting of rounded fluvial
cobbles and boulders cemented in a matrix of sand and gravel as the mouths of the
larger gullies are approached

As mentioned previously the project site is located on the upland plateau located
along the foot of the steeper southwest facing slopes of Mauna Kea Volcano The
summit of Mauna Kea Volcano has an elevation of about 13 784 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL The neighboring Mauna Loa Volcano located approximately 25 miles toward
the south has a summit elevation of about 13 680 feet MSL The elevations of the
project site ranges from about 5 530 feet MSL at the western terminus of the alignment
to about 6 760 feet MSL at the eastern terminus
The length of the proposed new Saddle Road PTA 1 alignment is approximately
13 14 miles and generally traverses the northern limits of the PTA and portions of the
existing power transmission easement Most of the project site exists as undeveloped
land that has been used for army training maneuvers Despite the remote location of
the project site numerous existing jeep trails traverse the proposed Saddle Road PTA 1
alignment Some observed disturbance to the native ground conditions along the
proposed alignment include localized areas of ground surface grading flattening and
clearance of vegetation for the construction and maintenance of access trails and
mining of cinder cone and granular alluvial materials
The project site is generally sparsely to moderately vegetated with flora
consisting of grasses small shrubs and scattered trees depending on the time of the
year In general the vegetation coverage gradually increases toward the easterly
direction along the proposed road alignment The middle eastern half of the proposed
alignment traverses some older a a lava flows where a thicker growth of larger trees
was observed The far eastern portion of the proposed road alignment traverses land

on the northern through eastern flank of Mauna Kea Volcano Due to the higher
elevation of the project site cooler temperatures and lower humidity and location on
the south facing mountain slopes annual rainfall is generally less than about 30 inches
The typical rainfall occurs in the afternoon as infrequent light misting showers as clouds
and fog form and migrate up toward the Humuula Saddle from the eastern and
occasionally the western slopes Heavier rainfall may be anticipated during storm
weather conditions and shifts in the prevailing wind direction
The average daytime temperatures in Fahrenheit range between the low 60s
and 70s with abrupt changes occurring with the formation of afternoon clouds and fog
Night time temperatures average in the upper 40s and 50s under generally clear sky
conditions The low cloud and fog conditions may occur periodically however the
frequency of occurrence is generally lower than at lower elevations of Saddle Road
which are located toward the east and west of the project site The overall climate may
be characterized as dry cool and breezy with relatively low precipitation low humidity
and a high evaporation rate

3 2 Erosion Control
Implementation of an adequate erosion control program would be crucial to the
satisfactory performance of the planned cut slopes and fill embankments against
potential failure or raveling In general erosion of the existing slopes planned cut
slopes or new fill embankments are caused by surface water runoff General
guidelines such as establishment of vegetation and provision of drainage swales and
earth berms have been provided and discussed under the Cut Slopes and Fill
Slopes subsections of this report In areas where the establishment of vegetation at
the slope face is difficult and may require a long period of time other temporary erosion
control methods or applications such as flexible geosynthetic matting should be
considered as part of the erosion control program Flexible geosynthetic matting
provides a flexible ground armor that resists erosion while allowing natural vegetation to
establish Its primary and secondary functions include the following
Temporary Erosion Control
Mulching
Permanent Erosion Control
Reduced Surface Water Runoff Velocities
PON
Temporary Erosion Control
The flexibility weight web structure and porosity features of the flexible
geosynthetic matting make it particularly effective in temporary erosion control It
conforms easily to the ground surface and remains firmly in place as a stable
ground armor The mat shields the soil surface from the erosive forces of wind and
rain and prevents soil seed and fertilizer from washing away Finally it provides a
rough surface to retain sediment deposits during surface water runoff
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3 3
Mulching
The high porosity web structure of the flexible geosynthetic matting acts as a
non deteriorating mulch It holds soil seed and fertilizer in place and retains
sediment runoff as a medium for root growth It also retains moisture and heat
necessary for germination and allows uninhabited growth of grass and other
vegetation
Permanent Erosion Control
Once vegetation growth is established the flexible geosynthetic matting
continues to provide permanent erosion control as it becomes embedded in the
new stem and root system It protects new vegetation from washout and
reinforces and anchors the vegetation in place as a cohesive unit
Reduced Surface Water Runoff Velocities
Re vegetated slopes and ditches reinforced with the matting reduce water runoff
flow velocities which is an important advantage that cannot be achieved with
concrete lining or other rigid lining systems It reduces runoff flow volume and
concentrations and reduces the need for energy dissipation at the ditch outlet or
slope base It also promotes water percolation and groundwater recharge

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed Saddle Road PTA 1 Alignment
project were explored by drilling and sampling 24 borings designated as Boring Nos 1
through 15 and Boring Nos 101 through 109 The borings extended to depths ranging
from about 16 5 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface Nineteen bulk samples
the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics and the quality
of the materials for use as embankment fills Seven of the bulk samples Bulk 106
through Bulk 112 were obtained at the proposed culvert locations to evaluate the
corrosivity of the near surface soils for the design of the culverts
The approximate locations of the test borings drilled and bulk samples collected
Plate 3 The approximate locations of the test borings and near surface bulk samples
also are shown on the Site Plans Plates 4 1 through 4 12 The borings were drilled
using both truck mounted and skid mounted drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our project staff geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 24
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third Ginches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Appendix A Continued
Field Exploration
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its

high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation

APPENDIX B
Geophysical Survey
The subsurface conditions along the Saddle Road PTA 1 alignment also were
explored by the performance of 41 seismic refraction survey lines from June 19 through
The approximate locations of the seismic refraction surveys are
summarized on Table B 1 and are shown on the Site Plans through 4 12
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compression waves through geologic materials The seismic refraction surveys were
conducted using a 12 channel signal enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound
sledge hammer as a seismic energy source Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compression waves
P waves These waves are refracted at interfaces between materials with contrasting
densities The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded
by the operator
The recorded data is input into a desktop computer for processing which
involves the interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves After the
interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical calculations
that yield approximate depths or thickness of materials below the survey lines and
provide their approximate compression wave P wave velocities The velocities are
averaged along the travel path of the compression waves The calculated velocities
may be empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the materials The
following legend explains the terms shown on the Seismic Refraction Profiles presented
flow velocity inversion layer profile computations could not be performed using
seismic refraction at the locations of SR 40 and SR 41
Velocity Compression wave velocity in feet per second
Dip Dip of the top of the layer in degrees
Depth Depth below ground surface in feet

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Seven sets of Corrosion Tests including Minimum Resistivity
Chloride Content and Sulfate Content were
performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc on selected soil samples obtained
from some of the planned drainage structure locations during our field exploration The
results of these tests are summarized on Plate C 1
Two Specific Gravity tests ASTM D 854 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the specific gravity of the soils The test results are summarized on
Plate C 2
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate C 3
Thirty seven Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 including three
Hydrometer tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plates C 4 through C 11
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates C 12 through C 15
One Consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on a
sample of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are
presented on Plate C 16
Ten unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation
encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on
Plate C 17

Laboratory Testing
Twelve laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils and the quality of the materials for use as embankment fills
The test results are presented on Plates C 18 through C 29
Nine Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 30 through C 38
Seven laboratory Resistance R Value Tests AASHTO T 190 93 were
performed on selected bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement
support characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates C 39
through C 45

APPENDIX D
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing four constant
head and one falling head tests in selected borings Boring 108 and
109 at depths ranging from approximately 22 6 to 31 feet below the existing ground
surface
The constant head tests were performed by pumping relatively clean water into
the boreholes until the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a
sufficient period of time The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured
to provide data upon which to base calculation of the subsurface soil permeability
During the falling head testing the boreholes were carefully filled with relatively
clean water to the top of the boreholes The time intervals and water drops were then
recorded to provide data upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of
the subsoils The test results of the field permeability tests are presented on Plates D 1
through D 5
