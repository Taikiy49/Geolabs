INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our comprehensive office
research supplemented by limited field exploration work
performed at various stages for the above referenced
residential development as shown on Site Plan Plate 1
A preliminary soil investigation report was performed for
the overall Mariners Ridge developments by the former project
soil engineer their reference
W O No 182 dated
Site grading inspection and testing were also performed
by their W 0 363 during the period
1972 through 1974 Construction and grading operations
were discontinued in early 1975 by the original developer
and we understand that some of the lots may not have been
completed or graded to the planned grades It is now
desired to build on the remaining vacant lots in various
phases by contructing single family dwellings The
proposed residential structures include 1 1 and 2 story
buildings approximately 45 by 65 feet in plan dimensions
The new houses will be located on vacant lots adjacent
to existing paved streets and sidewalks

PURPOSE SCOPE
The purpose of the comprehensive office research was to
determine the general subsurface soil conditions at the
building lots by reviewing daily field inspection reports
correspondence and various soil reports prepared by
another soil engineer in order to
search for any potentially adverse conditions requiring
any additional exploration work at those lots Based
upon the office research supplemented by limited field
work of test pits and seismic refraction surveys we were
to provide a conclusion on the overall existing conditions
of the building lots to outline the building lots that
may require additional work for the intended construction and
to provide the necessary soil engineering services to
develop recommendations for foundations floor slab support
and site development for some building lots when requested
and authorized

GENERAL SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our research and observation of the exposed
materials in some test pits the filled lots are underlain
by fill soils consisting of silty clay with lava rock
fragments Grading operations in majority of the lots were
performed and tested under the observation of the previous
soils engineer in the years 1972 to 1974 Based on our
research into the available records and data it appears
that most of the lots were graded with compaction However
due to the lack of written certification of the adequacy
of these lots it is not possible to tell from the available
records to what grade or elevation the fills were properly
compacted and what additional work had to be performed
Those lots that were specifically noted in the daily field
notes have been investigated and reported under separate
reports The remaining lots were noted to be tested and
were not given specific comments either adverse or
favorable Therefore random seismic refraction surveys
were used to identify the unifomity or compactness of the
W O
P 7
underlying material These seismic surveys were intended
as a guide line to identify the lots that contain different
velocities Based on the velocities of known materials at
or near the site comparisons were made to assign a range
of velocities that are either loose moderately compacted
or well compacted The depths of the materials exhibiting
various velocities were also calculated The results of
the seismic surveys and their corresponding probable soil
conditions and depths are presented on Table II
It should be pointed out that these classifications are
approximate and are intended only as a guide in lieu of
the difficulty and high cost of performing drilling and
test pit excavations to obtain the detailed subsurface
soil conditions Therefore these results are only
approximations and should be accepted as such If higher
accuracies of the soil conditions are desired we would be
glad to discuss the cost and program for such a study
Based on the above described seismic survey studies it
appears that some lots are underlain by shallow loose
fill and some lots are well compacted and some lots
appear to be moderately compacted The loose fill and
probable moderately compacted fill soils should be
verified by test pit excavations

are founded on adequate bearing material

undisturbed materials or at least 12 inches below the

In order to provide increased stability for foundations
adjacent to sloping ground the footings should be deepened
so that a set back distance of at least 5 feet could be
maintained The set back is the distance from outside
edge of the footing at the bottom of the footing to the
slope face
Anticipated settlements are expected to be relatively small
on the order of one half inch
Resistance to lateral loads may be provided by a combination
of passive pressure and friction Passive pressure may be
taken as that exerted by a fluid of density equal to 250
p c f Friction may be taken as normal dead loads times
a coefficient of friction equal to 0 4 four tenth
A combination of friction and passive pressure may be used
provided that passive pressure component is reduced by one
third
Grading
Current final grading plans for all lots are not available
but it is anticipated that minor additional fill will be used
W O
P 13
All areas to receive fill and backfill should be stripped
of vegetation Benching and keying are required to properly
bond new fill to slopes
Fill and backfill should be placed in horizontal 9 inch
lifts and compacted to at least 90 percent of maximum
dry density obtained using ASTM Designation D 1557 70
modified Proctor standard
Slab On Grade
Slab on grade may be used with both compacted subgrade and
heterogeneous fill subgrade requirements for compaction
and base course will depend upon subgrade material and usage
Existing fill is coarse and contains many cobbles Excavation
which loosens the surface of the fill should be followed by
compaction of the subgrade to 90 percent modified Proctor
The slab for parking basement utility room may be supported
directly on grade
The slab for living areas should be underlain by a four inch
layer of 3 4 inch gravel or other select layer of base To
further protect against moisture and dampness an impermeable
moisture barrier may be incorporated within the base course
layer
