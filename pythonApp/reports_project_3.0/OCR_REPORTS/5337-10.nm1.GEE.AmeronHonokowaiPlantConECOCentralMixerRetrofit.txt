SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally disclosed that most of the site is underlain by about
2 to 3 feet of very dense gravelly sands with concrete fragments up to 18 inches that was
placed as fills when the old batch plant foundation slab was buried Below the old concrete
slab foundation elevation the soil materials consist of a very dense silty sand about 0 5 to
4 feet thick over a medium dense to very dense silty fine sand alluvial material
extending to a depth in excess of 8 2 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on the results of our exploration the site is generally underlain by competent
soil materials Therefore it is our opinion that the development is feasible from a
geotechnical engineering point of view provided that our recommendations are followed
We understand that the finish floor of the foundation slab will be level with the
existing ground surface Based on this we recommend an allowable bearing value of up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf in the foundation design for the new CON E CO central
mixer
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and
recommendations

SECTION 1 SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by excavating and
sampling three test pits and two trenches along the cross section of the proposed
foundation pad location The test pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 3
extended to depths of about 2 0 to 8 2 feet below the existing ground surface
Two lateral trenches were excavated to get a better understanding of the location of the
previous foundation pad from the original batch plant which was buried when the
existing batch plant was constructed approximately 25 years ago The top of the
concrete pad was encountered at 3 0 to 3 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Our field exploration generally encountered old fills consisting of granular soils
over alluvial deposits The alluvial deposits are most likely related to the neighboring
stream channel adjacent to the Honokowai Plant s property line The fill soils generally
consisted of very dense brown to gray gravelly sands which included some concrete
fragments of 12 to 18 inches in size The alluvial soils consisted of medium dense to
very dense dark brown silty fine sand
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater may occur or fluctuate
depending on seasonal precipitation and other factors including seepage along joints
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the test pits are presented on the
Logs of Test Pits in Appendix A Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected
soil samples are presented in Appendix B

SECTION 2 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration the site is generally underlain by
competent soil materials Therefore it is our opinion that the development is feasible
from a geotechnical engineering point of view provided that our recommendations are
followed
For design purposes we recommended an allowable bearing value up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf in the foundation design for the new CON E CO
central mixer pad
In general conventional earthwork and construction methods may be used for
the project however areas of hard excavation may be encountered where concrete
fragments are encountered at shallow depths The removal of the concrete fragments
may result in over excavation of the new foundation footings If this occurs the on site
granular soils consisting of maximum particle size less than 2 inches may be used as
backfill and placed in 8 inch loose lifts The footings should then be compacted to
achieve a relative density of 95 when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1557
2 1 Building Foundations
Based on the design concept and the subsoil conditions encountered at the site
we believe that spread or mat foundations may be used to support the structure
planned An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for design of
footing foundations bearing on the in situ fills This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff dense soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing
steel or concrete Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The

over excavation may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the
underlying competent materials Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finish grades
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills as
recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the
order of one half inch
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a Geolabs
representative prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
2 2 Site Preparation
Based on the grading plan provided we anticipate that the site preparation work
for the project will consist of very minor cuts and or fills to achieve the design finish
grades
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris
deleterious material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of
properly off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials with the cleared and grubbed spoils Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed off site

SECTION 2 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Aggregate subbase and base course
materials should be moisture conditioned to near the optimum moisture placed in level
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established
in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
A Geolabs representative should observe site grading operations It is important
that our representative observe the site grading operation to evaluate whether
undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification process and whether the
exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration
2 3 Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections will be required for the project In
general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the proposed utilities The method and equipment to be used for
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavation should comply with the applicable local state and
federal safety requirements The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring
design and installation Trench shoring and bracing should also conform to the
appropriate health and safety requirements
A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort on the backfill thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes

Hawaii California
SECTION 2 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Improper backfill material and placement could result in backfill settlement and pipe
damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts
and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas
the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from the field test pit exploration Variations of conditions between
and beyond the field test pits may occur and the nature and extent of these variations

Field Exploration

test pits and two lateral trenches The test pits designated as Test Pits Nos 1 through 3
extended to depths of about 2 0 to 8 2 feet below the existing ground surface The test pits
were excavated using an Ingersol Rand Rubber Tire Backhoe Excavator

textural examination in the field and monitored the excavation operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Test Pits Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained by hand collection of the excavated material and
transported back to our laboratory for analysis
