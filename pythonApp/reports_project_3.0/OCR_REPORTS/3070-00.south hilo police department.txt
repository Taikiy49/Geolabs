INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed South Hilo Police Department Warehouse and Radio Shop

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new Fire Department Building for the County of Hawaii
and a new South Hilo Police Warehouse in the district of Hilo on the island of Hawaii
It is our understanding that the Fire Department Building will consist of a three 3
story building with about 53 new parking stalls and a 120 by 120 feet helicopter pad
It is our understanding that the new Police Warehouse will consist of a warehouse
with ground and mezzanine levels and a single story radio shop During the preparation
of this report information pertinent to the site grading are not available Due to the
relatively flat terrain we anticipate minimal cut and fill work during the construction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3070 00 Page 5
The project site is underlain by Hamakua Volcanic Series which typically
composed of basaltic lave flows which are mantled by a surface layer of Pahala ash The
basaltic lava flows were laid down in the a a phase characterized by a clinkery surface
and a core of dense rock The Pahala Ash is an air laid deposit of volcanic ash from the
Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa mountain during the Pleistocene Epoch The
Pahala Ash has high in situ moisture contents and low in situ densities and is
characterized as having thixotropic properties i e it is susceptible to significant loss of
strength when remolded under equipment vehicular or seismic loads

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Footing Foundations
It is our opinion that the proposed projects are feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view The clinker basalt formation encountered at relatively shallow
depth below the surface of the project site should provide adequate foundation support
for the proposed buildings Shallow spread or continuous footings and slab on grade
construction may be used to support the proposed buildings bearing on the underlying
clinker basalt formation
An allowable bearing capacity of up to 4 500 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the underlying dense clinker
basalt formation Based on data from Boring and 5 soft and friable clinker
basalt is encountered down to a depth of approximately five feet 5 Therefore it
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3070 00 Page 7
appears that footing foundations may have to be embedded in the order of about five feet
5 below the existing ground surface The allowable bearing pressures are for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third vs for transient applications such as
temporary wind and or seismic loads
If soft loose pockets are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation they
should be over excavated to competent in situ basalt material and replaced with lean
concrete or structural fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D
1557 91 test procedures
If footings are bear on two different types of materials such as in situ hard basalt
and unconsolidated clinker we recommend that the portion of footing subgrade in loose
clinker be over excavated down to match the adjacent hard basalt The over excavation
should then be backfilled with lean concrete This will reduce the potential for differential
settlement across the footing span due to its bearing on two types of rock with different
bearing capacities
The individual footing excavation and preparation should be observed during
construction by representative of Geolabs Hawaii to evaluate if adequate bearing capacity
would be obtained prior to placement of reinforcement and concrete
It is our opinion that total settlement for footings bearing on the underlying
competent clinker basalt formation should be less than inch with differential settlements
not exceeding about half that amount

Slab on Grade
We recommended a minimum four inch 4 thick layer of cushion material such
as base course and select borrow material be installed below the slabs to provide uniform
support for the slabs Due to the anticipated seismic activities poorly graded backfill
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation are not
recommended
The above slab cushion layer can bear on the recompacted in situ clinkery
material It is recommended that after complete removal of the soft volcanic ash soil the
entire subgrade should be scarified a minimum of 10 inches and recompacted to 95
percent relative compaction to obtain a more uniform dense subgrade surface to receive
the overlying four inch 4 thick of cushion material
A moisture barrier membrane covered with a one inch 1 layer of sand is also
recommended between the cushion layer and the slabs
Lateral Resistance
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated with an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 p s f
per foot of depth This assumes that soils around footings are well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on
the in situ clinker basalt formation
Cavity Probing
In order to reduce the possibility of footing settlement resulting from collapse of a
unconsolidated clinkers cavities or voids in the underlying basalt rock formation we
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3070 00 Page 9
recommend that a cavity probing program be implemented The cavity probing program
may also be used to confirm the embedment depth of the footings
We recommend that all of the footing excavations be probed at approximately 10
feet on centers for continuous footings and with a minimum of one probe drilled at each
column footing location The probes should consist of a 3 inch minimum diameter hole
drilled to a minimum depth of 10 feet below the bottom of footing
If a cavity is located below a footing additional probe holes should be made to
delineate the horizontal extent of the cavity The probe holes should be grouted from the
bottom of the hole using low pressures The probing and grouting operations should be
observed by representatives of Geolabs Hawaii to see that these recommendations are
carried out properly and to locate potential voids near the footing excavations During the
grouting operation Geolabs Hawaii may recommend modifications to the grouting
procedures to reduce grout loss in cavities and to expedite the above construction
program

Design Subgrade California Bearing Ratio CBR
Laboratory CBR tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D 1883 test
method on bulk samples obtained from the subgrade soils of the subsoils during
our field exploration The test results indicated CBR values between 3 7 and 5 0
which are summarized on Plate B 1 of Appendix B The lowest CBR test value of
3 7 was selected for use in our flexible pavement analysis to account for some
localized soil variability
From the approximate relationship between CBR and modulus of subgrade
reaction a CBR value of 3 7 correlates approximately to a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 115 pounds per cubic inch pci Considering the contribution from a
well compacted base course an effective modulus of subgrade reaction of 150 pci
was used at top of the base course for design of rigid pavement

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of 11
borings to depths ranging between 5 and 20 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 The borings were
drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary core
drilling tools as appropriate
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the boring logs
at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based
on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample and dry density are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the
Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of the soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining
to the performance or suitability of a soil
California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883 tests were performed on selected samples
as an aid to evaluating their pavement support characteristics The results of these tests
are also summarized on Plate B 1
