SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration indicates that the project site is underlain by fill 
consisting of stiff silty clay over marsh and lagoonal deposits of medium dense sands and 
stiff silts.  Volcanic tuff was encountered in the deeper portion of some borings; however, it 
is believed to be below the excavation limits of the new waterline.  Similarly, groundwater 
was encountered near the termination depth of two of the borings but it is at a depth that 
most likely will not impact the project.  
It is anticipated that the new waterline will have invert depths on the order of about 
4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings generally indicate stiff clay soils 
within this zone. Based on the relatively competent subsurface soil conditions encountered 
within the proposed drainage alignment, we believe that conventional excavation methods 
may be used for installation of the waterline. 
An allowable lateral bearing capacity of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the reaction blocks for the new pipeline bearing against compacted 
on-site clays.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for design of the new water pipe alignment project.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
engineering analyses performed for Phase 1 of the replacement of the existing 
waterlines along Ohana Nui Circle within Joint Base Pearl Harbor Hickam on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based 
on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The 
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of trench excavation 
and backfilling only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the replacement of approximately 2,000 lineal feet of 8-inch 
waterline along Ohana Nui Circle in the existing Earhart Village family housing area. 
Based on the information provided, the existing waterline will be abandoned in place 
and new piping will be installed by the cut-and-cover method. 
1.3

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch.  The Koolau Volcanic 
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows. 
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials 
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor. The 
sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 
100 feet above the present sea level.   
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits.  Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have 
added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration. 
2.2

provided, the proposed waterline alignment starts at the southeastern part of Ohana Nui 
Circle near Nehe Street and proceeds to the north between the housing units from 
741 to 792 Ohana Nui Circle.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by fill 
consisting of stiff clay over marsh and lagoonal deposits of medium dense sands and 
stiff silts. Volcanic tuff was encountered in the deeper portion of some borings, however, 
it is believed to be below the excavation limits of the new waterline.  Similarly, we 
encountered groundwater near the termination depth of two of the borings, although it 
was at a depth that most likely would not impact the project.  
It is anticipated that the new waterline would have invert depths on the order of 
about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings generally indicate stiff 
clay soils within this zone. Based on the relatively competent subsurface soil conditions 
encountered, we believe that conventional excavation methods may be used for 
installation of the waterline. 
An allowable lateral bearing capacity of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for the design of the reaction blocks for the new pipeline bearing against 
compacted on-site clays.  
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of waterline installation, 
and other geotechnical aspects are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the new water main inverts would be on the order of about 4 to 
5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Installation of the proposed water mains will 
involve excavation into the underlying fills and marsh or lagoonal deposits.  It is 
anticipated that the fills and marsh or lagoonal deposits may be excavated with normal 
heavy excavation equipment.  However, based on previous experience in the area, 
there is a potential that boulders and cobbles may be encountered within the fills. 
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is 
based solely on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site and 
previous experience in the project vicinity.

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the ground 
surface will be required for the installation of the new waterline. Deeper excavations 
may be required where the water mains traverse existing underground utilities.  Where 
excavations greater than 5 feet in depth are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and 
benching should be used.  Based on the existing site conditions (i.e., an active roadway 
in a family housing area), we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations 
are not practical.  On this basis, trench walls would have to be cut vertically requiring 
the use of shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative (i.e., competent 
person) should be on-site at all times during excavation and construction work for the 
opportunity to promptly observe changing or unforeseen conditions, such as, high 
groundwater, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect 
the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential 
for excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such

3.3 
Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe.  Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration.  For this project, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding 
material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided 
below the pipe.  
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the 
pipe.  Prior to the placement of bedding material, a Geolabs representative should 
observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials are exposed at the 
bottom of the trench or whether the bedding should be extended deeper. 
3.4 
Trench Backfill 
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches 
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free draining granular material 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting 
stresses on the pipe.  In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce 
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in 
backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
Based on our field exploration, the existing subsurface soils at the site are of 
poor quality for use as trench backfill, within 18 inches under pavement areas. The 
trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 18 inches below pavement 
subgrade may consist of excavated on-site material less than 3 inches in size. Backfill

settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on 
Ohana Nui Circle. 
3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfills should be moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  The upper 2 feet below the pavement grade 
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.  Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the 
specified compaction.

3.6 
Pavement Design 
We envision that flexible asphalt concrete pavement will be used for this project.  
In general, we anticipate the vehicle loading for the pavement will primarily consist of 
residential use by passenger vehicles.  Based on the conditions encountered in our 
exploration, we recommend using the following pavement section: 
Flexible Pavements  
3.0-Inch Asphalt Concrete 
6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
9.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness Over Minimum 12 inches of  
    Non-Expansive Granular Material 
The aggregate base course materials should consist of crushed basalt aggregate 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.  Aggregate base course 
materials should meet the material requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course 
as specified in the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 
(2005). 
3.7 
Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes.  The thrust force acting on 
the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block.  We anticipate that the planned inverts would be about 4 to 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
Based on our field exploration, very stiff clayey soils and medium sands were 
encountered at shallow depths.  We recommend using lateral bearing pressures of 
2,000 psf for the design of thrust blocks bearing on stiff clay.  
3.8 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes.  The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert.  Based on our calculations, settlement on 
the order of less than 0.5-inch is anticipated for the project.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of conditions between and 
beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the “Replace Waterline at Ohana 
Nui Circle, Phase 1 Water Plans” undated, by the U.S. Naval Facilities Engineering 
Command.  Elevations of the borings were interpolated based on their relative position 
to the contours shown on the same plans. The field boring locations and elevations 
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, which were drilled to depths 
ranging from approximately 9.2 to 10.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig (CME-45) equipped with continuous flight augers. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our engineer/geologist, who monitored the drilling operations 
on a continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 
through A-5. Rock Cores were described in general accordance with the Rock 
Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch OD split-barrel sampler with a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and 
third 6 inches (a total of 12 inches) of an 18-inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
