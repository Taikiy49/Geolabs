SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered residual soils underlain by highly
weathered basalt. The residual soils encountered in the borings consist of stiff to very stiff
with some localized zones of medium stiff clayey silt and silty clay. The highly weathered
basalt was encountered at depths of about 4.5 to 26 feet below the existing ground
surface. The highly weathered basalt below the residual soils is generally very dense.
Clayey silt and silty clay soils were interbedded within the highly weathered basalt.
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of the field exploration.
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered, we recommend that
the water storage tank be supported on shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or
continuous footings. To provide uniform support across the water tank structure, we
recommend that the water tank floor and foundations be supported on 12 inches of
compacted aggregate subbase course. The aggregate subbase course should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The 12-inch thick aggregate
subbase course layer would also serve to provide a protective cover or working platform
over the underlying clayey subgrade soils due to the high rainfall environment at the
project site.
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 psf may be used for design of footings
bearing on the 12 inches of aggregate subbase course placed over the stiff residual soils.
This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. For design of the tank
floor slab, a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pci may be used for the compacted
aggregate subbase course material.

recommendations for design of the new water storage tank project.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Water Storage Tank Replacement project at the Kamole
Weir Water Treatment Facility (WTF) near Haliimaile in the Makawao area on the Island
of Maui, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations
resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed water storage tank replacement project only. These recommendations are
intended for the design of foundations, retaining structures, site grading, and pavements
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project site is located near the Kamole Weir Water Treatment Facility near
Haliimaile in the Makawao area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. We understand that a
new water storage tank is planned to be constructed at the north and northeast sides of
the existing water treatment facility.
Based on the information provided, the new water storage tank will be a
rectangular-shaped structure with plan dimensions of about 240 feet wide and 260 feet
long and will have a storage capacity of about 6.0 million gallons (MG). Based on the
existing ground surface elevations, we envision that the east and southeast sides of the
storage tank area will be excavated to depths on the order of about 10 feet below the
existing ground surface.
A perimeter road and a driveway are planned for the project. The new roads will
consist of asphaltic concrete pavements. The new driveway will begin from the existing
water treatment facility and head in a northeasterly direction. In addition, we
understand that a gravel-surfaced road is planned at the project site.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older West Maui
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene
Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these
two volcanoes. The project site is located near the northern end of the island and on
the lower northern slope of the East Maui Volcano (Haleakala).
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows. The
lower unit, the Honomanu Volcanic Series (late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch)
consists of thinly-bedded, basaltic pahoehoe and a’a lava. These lava tend to be very
permeable and carry basal water. Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series (early
Pleistocene Epoch), composed primarily of thicker alkalic a’a flows, which may contain
many inter-stratified, thin ash-soil layers. A long quiescent period followed, during
which time, canyons were carved in the volcano, although a few eruptions may have
occurred during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes
(higher and lower stands of the sea level). At the end of the rest period, copious flows
erupted along the southwest and east rifts. This is the third and upper rock unit, known
as the Hana Volcanic Series (Pleistocene Epoch).
Most of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolitic soils, derived from
the in-situ weathering of igneous rocks. In general, residual and saprolitic soils grade to
weathered basalt formations with depth. Agricultural developments within the last century
have brought the project site to its present conditions.

3.1. Water Storage Tank Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the project site, we
recommend that the water tank be supported on shallow foundations consisting of
spread and/or continuous footings. To provide uniform support across the water tank
structure, we recommend that the water tank floor and foundations be supported on
12 inches of aggregate subbase course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction. The 12-inch thick layer of aggregate subbase course below the water tank
foundations and floor would also serve as a protective cover or working platform over
the underlying clayey subgrade soils due to the high rainfall environment at the project
site.
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may
be used for design of footings bearing on the 12 inches of aggregate subbase course
placed over the stiff residual soils encountered in the borings. This bearing value is for
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such
as those caused by wind or seismic forces. For design of the tank floor slab, a modulus
of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be
used for the compacted aggregate subbase course material.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades. For footings bearing on the compacted aggregate
subbase course placed over the stiff soils, the bottom of the footing excavations (in the
native soils) should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of the aggregate
subbase course material. If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom
of the footing excavations, the soft and/or loose materials should be over-excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with
on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the footing
bottom may be extended down to bear directly on the underlying competent materials.
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility

At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be

and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed off-site or used in

areas exist in a relatively moist state with high moisture contents. Therefore, drying
or aeration of the excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as

effort along with thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified compaction may be
necessary and would generally reduce the rate of fill placement for this project.
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or
ponding should not be allowed to compact the on-site clayey soils.

optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the
maximum dry density.

3.3.4 Excavation
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by residual
soils overlying highly weathered basalt. Based on our borings, we anticipate that
excavations near the eastern corner of the water tank structure may likely
encounter very dense, highly weathered basalt. It is anticipated that the
near-surface residual soils and highly weathered basalt may be excavated quite
readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators. However,
some localized hard zones may be encountered in the highly weathered basalt
encountered at shallow depths, which may require the use of hoerams or chipping.
In general, the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor, subject to practical limits and safety considerations.
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our
visual observation of the existing site and field data from the borings drilled at the
site. We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this project be
encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own
interpretation.
3.3.5 Fill Slopes
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills
should be keyed and benched into existing slope to provide stability of the new fill
against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue
up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill placement
recommendations presented above. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling
and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face.

that most of the trenches for the pipelines will be excavated in the residual soils and

highly weathered basalt. In general, we recommend that a granular bedding layer
consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be

Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement
of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of excavated on-site soils with
a maximum particle size of 3 inches or well-graded granular materials less than
3 inches in maximum size. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are located in paved
areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction.
Trench excavations within the tank structure footprint should be backfilled with
concrete (or lean concrete) placed neat against the sides of the trench excavation up to
12 inches below the tank slab subgrade. The remaining 12 inches of the water line
trench backfill should consist of structural fill material compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction.

thrust force acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting
against the face of the thrust block. Based on our field exploration, clayey silt and silty
clay soils were encountered at shallow depths. We recommend that a lateral bearing

3.7 Pavements
We understand that a driveway and a perimeter road consisting of flexible
pavements will be constructed at the project site. In addition, we understand that a

The gravel-surfaced road planned at the project site should consist of an 8-inch
thick layer of aggregate subbase course over the pavement subgrade. We understand
that concrete headers extending into the subgrade soils will be constructed on both
sides of the gravel-surfaced roads. We believe that surface water infiltrating into the
gravel layer will likely be trapped at the pavement subgrade and between the concrete
headers. This will likely cause the clayey subgrades to soften, which would lead to
premature failure of the gravel road.
To reduce the potential for substantial water being trapped below the gravel road,
we recommend that a longitudinal drain be placed along the centerline of the
gravel-surfaced road. The typical longitudinal drain should consist of a 4-inch PVC
perforated pipe (with perforations facing down) surrounded by permeable backfill
material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation). The weed barrier
below the bottom of the gravel layer should extend around the sides and bottom of the
permeable backfill material.

3.8 Drainage
The finish grades outside the water tank structure should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding.
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration. In addition, drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run-off away
from slabs and foundations.

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8, extending to depths of
about 17 to 42.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using
a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight augers and coring tools. The
approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-8.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification. Results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on
Plate B-1.
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. Results of the direct shear tests
are presented on Plates B-2 through B-4.
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D 2166) was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics. Results of the
test are presented on Plate B-5.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils. Results of the tests are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on the
near-surface soils to determine the dry density and moisture content relationship. Results
of the test are presented on Plate B-8.
