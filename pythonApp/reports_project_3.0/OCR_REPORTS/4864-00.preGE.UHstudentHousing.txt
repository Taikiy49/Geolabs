In accordance with our fee proposal dated we have conducted
a preliminary geotechnical engineering study for the proposed University of Hawaii
Student Housing Frear Hall Redevelopment project This letter report summarizes our

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located on the Upper Makai Campus of the University of Hawaii
at Manoa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Frear Hall is located between the
International Gateway House Dormitory and the Hale Aloha Housing Complex
We understand that the existing Frear Hall building structures were constructed in
1952 The existing Frear Hall consists of three to four story and one story structures of
concrete and CMU construction Frear Hall includes 144 beds for female students in a
dormitory configuration Currently the Frear Hall building is not being used for student
housing It is proposed to demolish the existing structures and construct a new Frear Hall
housing complex with 360 beds in a 6 room suite configuration The new building complex
will consist of four story concrete structures along Dole Street and five story concrete
structures along the former Quarry area
The project consists of conducting a preliminary engineering study that will be used
in support of preparing a Phase 2 project development report for the Frear Hall
Redevelopment project Our study included research and review of available in house
sources of subsurface information and other pertinent documents In addition a
reconnaissance of the project site and observation of the surface soil conditions was
performed

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The Frear Hall property covers an area of approximately 81 600 square feet The
project site is bounded by the International Gateway House Dormitory and Dole Street on
the north Manoa Stream on the east the Hale Aloha Housing Complex on the south and
the former Quarry area to the west In general the site slopes down toward the south at
slope inclinations ranging from about 3 to 10 percent The ground surface at the site
ranges from about Elevations 70 to 80 feet Mean Sea Level MSL At the time of our
site reconnaissance the site was covered with grass and trees outside the existing
building area
An asphaltic concrete paved driveway of about 20 feet wide was noted between the
existing Frear Hall structures and Manoa Stream It appears that the existing ground
surface drops down by about 25 feet from the driveway level down to the level of Manoa
Stream In addition the existing ground surface sharply descends by about 50 feet to the
former Quarry level at the south and southwest boundary of the site Dense basalt rock
formation was noted along the cliff face
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The project site is
located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
northwest to southeast trending rift zones These lavas consist of interbedded
pahoehoe and a a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form A a is the
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flow typified by a rough jagged spinose clinkery
surface Pahoehoe is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth
billowy or ropy surfaces After the cessation of the main shield building phase of Koolau
Volcano there was a long period of erosion
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge along
the westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf
vents When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled
down the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor These lavas
ponded and filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley The ponding allowed the lava to
densify as it cooled forming dense massive rock The new lava flows from these vents
blocked Manoa Stream and forced the stream to the east between the East West
Center and Waahila Rise These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili District where

ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The anticipated subsurface conditions described herein were determined from a
review of geological maps and available subsurface information from past project
drawings and from observations of the surface soil conditions to obtain an indication of the
subsurface conditions at the project site
Based on geologic maps the project site is characterized by basalt rock formation
of the Honolulu Volcanic Series This basalt formation generally consists of dense a a
type flows with clinker layers Subsurface information was obtained from the logs of
borings for the adjacent International Gateway House Dormitory project and the northern
portion of the Hale Aloha Housing Complex project Based on the logs of borings the
project site is generally underlain by a surface layer of firm brown silty clay and sandy silt
with gravel and boulders ranging from about 1 5 to 11 feet in thickness The surface layer
was underlain by medium hard to hard basalt rock formation
The presence of groundwater was not indicated in the logs of borings reviewed
The borings extended down to about 46 feet below the existing ground surface at the site
PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
A desk top study was performed for the project and included a review of available
subsurface information at the site Based on this study preliminary geotechnical
recommendations pertaining to design of foundations and geotechnical engineering
considerations during design and construction are presented in the following sections
The preliminary recommendations and considerations are based on the results of the
desk top study and are intended for planning and preliminary design purposes only
Building Foundation
Based on the available subsurface information from the adjacent dormitory
structure the project site is generally underlain by a clayey surface layer extending to
depths of about 1 5 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface The surface layer was
underlain by a medium hard to hard basalt rock formation extending to a depth of about
46 feet the maximum depth of the borings The available borings did not indicate the
presence of groundwater within the depths drilled
Based on the available subsurface information we believe that a shallow
foundation system bearing on the firm surface layer may be used to support the new
building structures For preliminary design purposes allowable bearing pressures on the
order of about 3 000 to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of
footings bearing on the firm surface layer The allowable bearing pressures are for

In general we recommend that the footings for the building be embedded a
minimum depth of about 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade for the
footings design to bear on the surface soil layer If the higher bearing pressures based on
footings bearing on the basalt rock formation are desired the depths of embedment for
the new footings would need to be increased from the minimum of 2 feet to on the order of
about 4 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface for the footings to bear directly on the
basalt rock surface
In addition it should be noted that foundations located next to utility trenches or
easements should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench As an alternative the footings should be
extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
The bottom of the footings should bear on the firm surface layer or the basalt rock
formation Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation
should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with compacted fill materials
Because of the potential for future erosion that may occur near the tops of slopes
and for foundation support considerations we recommend that a building setback distance
of 30 feet measured from the tops of slopes be maintained adjacent to the former Quarry
cliff face at the south and southwest boundaries of the site For other areas where
footings may be constructed near the tops of slopes or on slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured from the outside
edge at the base of the footing to the face of the slope is not less than 8 feet
Foundation Probing
It should be noted that cavities and or voids may be encountered in the basalt rock
formation at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations be implemented during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
50 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center The probe holes should be at

Building Slabs On Grade
The available boring information indicates that the project site is underlain by a
layer of clayey soils at the surface Unless floor slabs are properly designed and
constructed there is a potential for future distress to the slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of these soils due to changes in the moisture content To reduce
the potential for structural distress resulting from shrinking and swelling of the subgrade
soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at
least 24 inches of select granular fill material The select granular fill below the
slabs on grade for the building structure should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally
beyond the edges of the slab or to the edges of the sidewalks or concrete aprons where
provided The select granular fill should consist of imported granular material such as
crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The subgrade soils below the select granular
fill should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches properly moisture conditioned and
compacted
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be incorporated below the building slabs to serve as a
cushion fill layer To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the
gravel cushion fill layer Two inches of sand may be considered above the moisture
barrier to provide for protection of the moisture barrier and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete Provision of the 2 inches of fine sand above the moisture barrier is optional and
will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer
We anticipate that exterior concrete flatwork and sidewalks will be constructed for
the proposed project Where exterior flatwork and sidewalks are planned we recommend
that the exterior flatwork and sidewalks be supported on at least 12 to 18 inches of select
granular fill The select granular fill required under the slabs should extend to the outside
edge of the exterior flatwork and sidewalk slabs

Additional Services
This preliminary geotechnical engineering report is prepared for planning and
preliminary engineering purposes only It is not intended for use in design and or
preparation of construction documents for the proposed project A design level

services for this project
