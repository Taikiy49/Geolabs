This technical memorandum presents the findings of our preliminary geotechnical 
assessment performed in support of the Department of Defense, Diamond Head Crater 
Repair of Various Tunnel Shotcrete Finishes project at Diamond Head Crater in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This technical memorandum applies to the 
Diamond Head Crater Mule Tunnel M0.2 site. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This technical memorandum summarizes our findings and presents our 
preliminary geotechnical recommendations based on our visual observation of the 
existing shotcrete conditions. The preliminary geotechnical recommendations presented 
herein are intended for the planning of the tunnel and portal shotcrete finish repairs 
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this technical memorandum. 
Project Considerations 
The various tunnels (five tunnels) comprising the project is on the hillside of 
Diamond Head Crater. Cracking and spalling of the existing shotcrete finishes have 
been observed at the tunnel portals and the two vehicular tunnel walls. In addition, it 
appears that some drainage conditions at the tunnel portals may have contributed to 
some of the cracking and spalling of the existing shotcrete finishes at the three Mule 
Tunnel (M0.2, M4, and M6) portals and the two vehicular tunnel (Kahala and Kapahulu) 
portals.  
We understand it is desired to repair the deteriorated/cracked shotcrete that may 
be considered a potential safety hazard. As-built drawings for the tunnels were not 
available, and a topographic survey was on-going at the time of our field observations 
and assessment. 
Existing Site Conditions 
In accordance with our fee proposal dated June 20, 2016, we conducted a 
preliminary visual assessment of the existing shotcrete finish conditions at the Diamond

Head Crater, Mule Tunnel M0.2 Portal. The location of the M0.2 portal site is shown on 
the Site Location Plan, Plate 2. 
The tunnel was constructed on the northeast side of the Diamond Head Crater 
during World War II. The portal entrance was developed by constructing a near-vertical 
cut slope through the weathered rock hillside. The site is underlain by highly to 
moderately fractured tuff formation exhibiting horizontal and adverse inclined bedding 
that is conducive to generating falling rock. The fractured sub-vertical rock slope 
surrounding the portal is faced with a thin layer of shotcrete. Based on our observations, 
the existing shotcrete facing generally ranges between about 0.25 and 1 inch thick with 
no visible wire-mesh or fiber reinforcing. Localized pockets appear to contain shotcrete 
ranging up to about 2 to 3 inches in thickness. An overview photograph of the 
M0.2 Tunnel portal is presented on Plate 3.1. 
Based on our observations, the existing shotcrete at the M0.2 tunnel portal is in 
relatively fair to locally poor condition, with some localized spalls, multiple open distress 
cracks, and widespread fine interconnected micro-fracture cracking. In addition, some 
holes and lifted shotcrete facing were observed related to shrubbery, root and stem 
penetrations, mainly along the perimeter and top of the tunnel crown shotcrete facing. 
Based on our observations, it appears that about 60 to 70 percent of the existing portal 
shotcrete surface may be in generally poor condition due to age and deterioration. 
Selected photographs of the existing portal shotcrete conditions are presented on 
Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
We observed some existing utility cables draped over the slope surface and 
entering the tunnel doorway. A steel portal door and concrete wall comprise the center 
portion of the “rear” portal face with shotcrete surroundings. No top of slope tunnel 
crown swale was observed; however, the left side of the shotcrete face contains a 
shallow trough developed in the shotcrete which appears to direct some surface water 
runoff along the left edge of the shotcrete facing. The shotcrete trough discharges onto 
an adjacent rock terrace surface devoid of shotcrete facing. 
Based on our observations, it appears that haole koa shrubbery growing along 
the top of tunnel crown and perimeter of the shotcrete facing is causing destructive 
ruptures, lifting and cracking along the shotcrete edges. The lifted and cracked 
shotcrete margins appear to allow surface water infiltration behind the shotcrete facing, 
causing additional cracks and spalls.  
Based on our observations, we believe that potential falling objects from spalled 
and/or broken shotcrete may present a potential hazard to persons accessing the tunnel 
entrance. Therefore, we recommend replacing the damaged shotcrete. As an interim 
precautionary measure, we recommend installing warning signs alerting the possibility 
of falling rock and objects surrounding the tunnel portal.

Recommendations 
Based on our preliminary geotechnical assessment, the existing widely scattered 
and interconnected areas of observed distress and damage suggest that complete 
removal and replacement of the existing shotcrete facing at the M0.2 tunnel portal is 
appropriate. Localized repair of the irregular crack patterns and transitions between new 
and existing shotcrete may not provide good continuity of repair for the aged facing. In 
addition, we believe full removal and replacement of the entire portal shotcrete is best 
for long-term safety and stability of the tunnel portal shotcrete. 
Based on our visual assessment and estimation, it appears the portal shotcrete 
recommended for replacement may encompass approximately 1,260 square feet of 
area. We envision the shotcrete removal by saw cut and chipping and replacement with 
new fiber reinforced shotcrete of about 1 to 2 inches in thickness applied to the 
prepared surface. Surface preparation may include vegetation removal and scaling of 
loose soil deposits and fractured rock material at the ground surface.

Shrubbery encroachment around the perimeter of the shotcrete appears to be a 
contributing factor to the shotcrete deterioration. We recommend thoroughly clearing, 
grubbing, and herbicidal poisoning of vegetation growing within 5 feet of the shotcrete

Finally, because infiltrating water is also a cause for the shotcrete deterioration,

to pass beneath the edge of the portal shotcrete facing.

are based in part upon information obtained from visual observation of the existing 
conditions. Variation of the conditions may occur between and beyond our observation 
points, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
additional field observations are conduced or construction is underway. If variations 
then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein.

This technical memorandum presents the findings of our preliminary geotechnical 
assessment performed in support of the Department of Defense, Diamond Head Crater 
Repair of Various Tunnel Shotcrete Finishes project at Diamond Head Crater in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This technical memorandum applies to the 
Diamond Head Crater Mule Tunnel M6 site. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This technical memorandum summarizes our findings and presents our 
preliminary geotechnical recommendations based on our visual observation of the 
existing shotcrete conditions. The preliminary geotechnical recommendations presented 
herein are intended for the planning of the tunnel and portal shotcrete finish repairs 
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this technical memorandum. 
Project Considerations 
The various tunnels (five tunnels) comprising the project is on the hillside of 
Diamond Head Crater. Cracking and spalling of the existing shotcrete finishes have 
been observed at the tunnel portals and the vehicular tunnel walls. In addition, it 
appears that drainage conditions at the tunnel portals may have contributed to some of 
the cracking and spalling of the existing shotcrete finishes at the three Mule Tunnel 
(M0.2, M4, and M6) portals and the two vehicular tunnel (Kahala and Kapahulu) portals. 
We understand it is desired to repair the deteriorated/cracked shotcrete that may 
be considered a potential safety hazard. As-built drawings for the tunnels were not 
available, and a topographic survey was on-going at the time of our field observations 
and assessment.

The tunnel was constructed on the northeast side of the Diamond Head Crater 
during World War II. The portal entrance was developed by constructing a near-vertical 
cut slope through the weathered rock hillside. The site is underlain by highly to 
moderately fractured tuff formation exhibiting horizontal and adverse inclined bedding 
that is conducive to generating falling rock. The fractured sub-vertical rock slope 
surrounding the portal is faced with shotcrete. Based on our observation, the existing 
shotcrete facing generally ranges between about 0.25 to 1 inch thick with no visible 
wire-mesh or fiber reinforcing. Localized areas appear to contain shotcrete ranging up 
to about 2 to 3 inches in thickness, with some pockets approaching about 6 inches in 
thickness on the left slope surface facing. 
For discussion purposes, the M6 tunnel portal shotcrete was divided into three 
sub-areas referred to as the left wall Area “A”, left slope surface Area “B”, right wall 
Area “C”, and rear wall Area “D”, as shown on Plate 3.1.  
Based on our observations, the existing shotcrete at the M6 tunnel portal is in 
relatively fair to locally poor condition with many localized spalls, multiple open distress 
cracks, and widespread fine interconnected micro-fracture cracking. In addition, some 
holes and lifted shotcrete panels were observed related to existing shrubbery root and 
stem penetrations, mainly along the edges and top of slope shotcrete facing. Based on 
our observations, it appears that about 50 to 70 percent of the existing shotcrete surface 
may be in generally fair to poor condition as a result of broken surfaces, spalls and 
irregular fracture patterns that are widely distributed across the shotcrete surface. 
Selected photographs of the existing conditions are presented on Plates 3.1 through 
3.5. 
In addition to the left wall comprising the portal, we observed shotcrete facing on 
the left flanking slope surface (Area “B”). The shotcrete surface on the left flanking slope 
is in poor condition and is comprised of lifted and broken panels of shotcrete with many 
holes and crack penetrations as a result of vegetation growth. Pocket areas of 
thickened shotcrete on the order of about 4 to 6 inches thick were observed filling 
topographic depressions on the left flanking slope surface. 
Observed shotcrete damage includes deteriorated edge terminations (spalled 
and lifted) and open distress fractures of horizontal and vertical orientation generally 
ranging between about 0.1 and 0.2 inches in crack width. We observed open horizontal 
cracks in the facing above the doorway overhang.

the top tunnel crown, shotcrete margins, and left flank slope surface is causing 
destructive perforation, lifting, and cracking of the shotcrete. The damaged shotcrete

Recommendations 
Based on our assessment, the existing widely scattered and interconnected 
areas of observed distress and damage suggest that complete removal and 
replacement of the existing shotcrete facing at the M6 tunnel portal is appropriate. 
Localized repair of the irregular crack patterns and transitions between new and existing 
shotcrete may not provide good continuity of repair for the aged facing. In addition, we 
believe full removal and replacement of the entire portal shotcrete is best for long-term 
safety and stability of the portal shotcrete. 
Based on our visual assessment and estimation, it appears the portal shotcrete 
recommended for replacement may encompass approximately 2,400 square feet of 
surface area. We envision the shotcrete removal by saw cut and chipping and 
replacement with new fiber reinforced shotcrete of about 1 to 2 inches in thickness 
applied to the prepared surface. Surface preparation may include vegetation removal 
and scaling of loose soil deposits and fractured rock material at the ground surface.

Recommendations 
Based on our assessment, there are enough scattered and interconnected 
damage areas to warrant complete removal and replacement of the existing shotcrete 
facing at the north and south portals and portions of the tunnel interior. Due to the many 
adjacent localized repair of irregular crack and spall patterns, and associated transitions 
to older existing shotcrete material, it is our opinion that localized repairs may not 
provide good continuity of repair for the aged facing and for the overall longer-term 
safety and stability of the portal shotcrete. 
Based on our visual assessment and estimation of surface area, it appears the 
portal and tunnel interior shotcrete recommended for replacement may encompass 
approximately 11,000 square feet of surface area. New shotcrete swales should be 
constructed along the top crown margin and perimeter of the portal shotcrete surfaces 
at both the north and south tunnel portals. We envision the old shotcrete removal by 
saw cut and chipping and replacement with new fiber reinforced shotcrete comprising 
about 1 to 2 inches in thickness applied to the prepared ground surface. 
Shrubbery encroachment around the perimeter of the shotcrete appears to be a 
contributing factor to the shotcrete deterioration. We recommend thoroughly clearing, 
grubbing, and herbicidal poisoning of vegetation growing within 5 feet of the shotcrete 
perimeter.

This technical memorandum presents the findings of our preliminary geotechnical 
assessment performed in support of the Department of Defense, Diamond Head Crater 
Repair of Various Tunnel Shotcrete Finishes project at Diamond Head Crater in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This technical memorandum applies to the 
Diamond Head Crater Kahala Tunnel site. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This technical memorandum summarizes our findings and presents our 
preliminary geotechnical recommendations based on our visual observation of the 
existing shotcrete conditions. The preliminary geotechnical recommendations presented 
herein are intended for the planning of the tunnel and portal shotcrete finish repairs 
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this technical memorandum. 
Project Considerations 
The various tunnels (five tunnels) comprising the project is on the hillside of 
Diamond Head Crater. Cracking and spalling of the existing shotcrete finishes have 
been observed at the tunnel portals and the vehicular tunnel walls. In addition, it 
appears that some drainage conditions at the tunnel portals may have contributed to 
some of the cracking and spalling of the existing shotcrete finishes at the three Mule 
Tunnel (M0.2, M4 and M6) portals and the two vehicular tunnel (Kahala and Kapahulu) 
portals.  
We understand it is desired to repair the deteriorated/cracked shotcrete that may 
be considered a potential safety hazard. As-built drawings for the tunnels were not 
available, and a topographic survey was on-going at the time of our field observations 
and assessment.

The Kahala vehicular access tunnel was constructed on the east side of 
Diamond Head Crater during World War II. The portal entrances were developed by 
constructing near-vertical cut slopes through the weathered rock hillside. The tunnel

conducive to generating falling rock. The Kahala Tunnel provides public access to the 
crater and is heavily used by pedestrians and vehicles, including larger transport trucks 
and buses. An existing steel framed and wire mesh covered protection structure is at 
the east tunnel entrance and serves to protect tunnel users from possible falling objects 
emanating from the steep slopes bounding the entrance.

Kahala Tunnel Interior 
Based on our observations, the tunnel interior is in generally fair to good 
condition with some zones of increased polygonal, interconnected, pattern cracking 
throughout the length of the tunnel interior. Visible groundwater seepage was not 
observed at the tunnel interior. In addition, existing shotcrete spalls and cracks wider 
than about 0.1 inches were not observed. We observed what appears to be randomly 
oriented, polygonal-shaped, fine interconnected cracking with crack boundary staining. 
The staining may be the result of ground moisture wicking and mobilization of 
mineralization bordering the crack, which results in light and dark colored staining on 
the shotcrete surface surrounding the crack orientation. Many of the cracks appear to 
be closed or “healed” by mineralization deposits along the crack aperture.  
Because there is no groundwater level present, it is our opinion the existing fine 
cracking may be the result of concrete shrinkage and aging as opposed to underlying 
structural deficiencies. The fine cracking appears to produce some very slight moisture 
wicking along the crack aperture. Selected photographs of the observed fine cracking 
conditions at the tunnel interior are presented on the attached Plates 3.5 through 3.10. 
Based on our assessment of the existing conditions and fine cracking of the 
Kahala Tunnel interior, we do not believe that complete shotcrete removal and 
replacement is necessary at this time. The appearance of the relatively minor cracking 
observed in the tunnel may be improved by the application of a colored sealant or other 
coating to improve the aesthetic appearance of the tunnel lining. Alternatively, a thin 
shotcrete flash coat on the order of about 0.25 inches may be applied to improve the 
appearance of the shotcrete surface.

Recommendations 
Based on our preliminary geotechnical assessment of the east portal, we 
recommend some limited repairs by removal and replacement of selected areas of 
shotcrete that exhibit distress fractures and edge deterioration. Consideration should be 
given to the redesign of the top edges of shotcrete to reduce the potential for surface 
water infiltration. The recommended localized repairs may encompass a surface area of 
approximately 2,700 square feet. 
Based on our preliminary geotechnical assessment of the tunnel interior, the 
shotcrete is in generally good to fair condition. Repairs are not necessary at this time; 
however, the application of surface coating may improve the appearance of the existing 
shotcrete surface. The coating could be a colored sealant or a flash coat of thin 
shotcrete to provide a more uniform appearance. 
Based on our preliminary assessment of the west portal, we recommend some 
limited repairs by removal and replacement of selected areas of shotcrete that exhibit 
distress fractures, vegetation penetration, and edge deterioration. Consideration should 
be given to the redesign of the top edges of shotcrete adjacent to the existing unlined 
swales to reduce the potential for surface water infiltration. The recommended localized 
repairs may encompass a surface area of approximately 3,700 square feet. 
We envision the old shotcrete removal by saw cut and chipping and replacement 
with new fiber reinforced shotcrete of about 1 to 2 inches in thickness applied to the 
prepared ground surface.  
Shrubbery encroachment around the perimeter of the shotcrete appears to be a 
contributing factor to the shotcrete deterioration. We recommend thoroughly clearing, 
grubbing, and herbicidal poisoning of vegetation growing within 5 feet of the shotcrete 
perimeter.
