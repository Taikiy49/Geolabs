INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering consultation
performed for the proposed streetlight and traffic signal light poles as part of the proposed
Kuhio Beach Expansion and Kalakaua Avenue Beautification Project The proposed pole
structures will be located on the makai side of Kalakaua Avenue in the Waikiki area in the
District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our engineering analyses based on recent and previous nearby field
explorations These recommendations are intended for the design of streetlight and traffic
signal light pole foundations only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided it is proposed to beautify Kuhio Beach and the
surrounding area As a part of this project new traffic signal lights and streetlights are
proposed in the current right makai travel lane as this lane will be eliminated and made a
part of the proposed development The project site is located along Kalakaua Avenue

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene
Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which
are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this
area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise
in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy wetlands
As the city of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area became urbanized man made fills were
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits
consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was deposited
during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which apparently smothered the coral
GEOLABS HAWAII

reef development Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor
quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads
Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping Kuhio Beach is generally
underlain by two major stratigraphic units These include the Recent Unconsolidated Marine
Calcareous Sediments and Recent Fill

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed light and traffic signal light pole locations
are generally underlain by very loose to dense silty sand and gravel deposits Based on the
materials encountered in our field explorations we believe that the proposed lights and traffic
signal light poles may be supported on drilled shaft type foundations to resist the generally
high ground line and torsional moment induced by loading of the poles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3936 10 Page 7
We understand that three types of drilled shaft foundation systems are planned for
supporting the poles These include
1 For centerline loading conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a single concrete
drilled shaft at grade
2 For eccentric loading anchor bolt onto a structural member that is connected
to a single concrete drilled shaft at grade
3 For a two shaft design conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a pile cap
Except for the base shear the loading and boundary conditions on the drilled shaft
for eccentric loading were adjusted based on a maximum 3 foot off center loading The
loading and boundary conditions on a two shaft design were adjusted assuming that the
loads would be equally distributed
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our analyses of the loading on
the foundations we recommend that drilled shafts be used to support the poles
Foundation analyses were conducted based on a combination of axial and lateral
loads in addition to ground line moments using the computer software program LPILE Plus
for Windows In addition the drilled shaft foundations were also checked for the torsional
moments induced by the poles The analyses were based on a free head condition and a
maximum lateral deflection of not more than one fourth 4 inch at the top of the shaft
For centerline and eccentric loads up to 3 feet off center we recommend 30 inch
diameter drilled shafts with lengths ranging from 6 to 13 feet below the ground surface be
used to support the proposed poles Axial loads of up to 10 5 kips and torsional moments
of up to 10 kip feet were used in the design of the drilled shafts In addition foundation
analyses for the drilled shafts were performed with lateral loads of up to 2 5 kips along with
ground line moments of up to 46 5 kip feet

Loading Conditions
Based on the information provided we understand that the proposed streetlights
and traffic signal lights will be supported by hollow 8 to 16 flute tapered cylindrical
galvanized cast iron and steel pole structures of approximately 10 to 30 feet in height
above the ground surface The greatest composite height of the proposed pole structures
is 34 feet The base of the poles will be about 24 inches in diameter The poles will be
anchored to the drilled shaft by means of four 1 inch diameter 36 inch long L type anchor
bolts to a 13 inch square anchor plate The traffic signal light mast arms consist of hollow
8 to 16 flute tapered cylindrical steel of approximately 20 feet in length The streetlight
cross arms consist of bent ductile galvanized cast iron and steel pipe of approximately
5 to 6 feet in lateral length The poles mast arms and cross arms will be relatively light
in weight about 1 kip but they will be subjected to high overturning moment torsional
moment and lateral forces at the foundation level These loading values are based ona
combined dead live and wind loads
In general there were about five types of poles and fixture combinations To
simplify construction we have limited our design to the following three types of poles
1 30 foot high light poles with one or two light fixture mast arms without a traffic
signal light mast arm
2 30 foot high light poles with two light fixtures with a 20 foot long traffic signal
light mast arm
3 10 to 14 foot high traffic signal light poles without any mast arm

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the loading conditions provided and the subsurface conditions
encountered we recommend that the poles for the streetlights and traffic signal lights be
supported by drilled shaft foundations As previously indicated three types of drilled shaft
foundation systems are planned for the poles These include
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3936 10 Page 12
1 For centerline loading conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a single
concrete drilled shaft at grade
2 For eccentric loading anchor bolt onto a structural member that is connected
to a single concrete drilled shaft at grade
3 For a two shaft design conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a pile cap
Based on the bottom diameter of the steel poles 24 inches at the base the
available auger core barrel sizes from the local drilling companies and the shaft lengths
anticipated drilled shaft diameters of 30 inches were considered in our foundation
analyses for the single pile foundations For two pile foundations drilled shaft diameters
of 18 inches were considered in our foundation analyses The major considerations in our
foundation analyses included the lateral loads and ground line moments induced in the
drilled shaft foundations
In general lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the shaft and
induced moment in the shaft Based on a maximum horizontal deflection of
one fourth inch at the top of the drilled shaft subjected to ground line overturning
moments and lateral forces we have evaluated the required drilled shaft lengths using the
computer software program LPILE Plus for Windows This program is a microcomputer
adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the
University of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment
along a drilled shaft under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried
out with the use of internally generated non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli
The lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various
depths The analyses were performed for a laterally loaded cast in place concrete drilled
shaft The results of our engineering analyses are summarized in the following table

Torsion Resistance
In general pole foundations will be subjected to torsional moments resulting in torsion
on the drilled shaft foundations For single pile foundations torsion may be resisted by the
side shear along the drilled shaft surface and adjacent soils For two pile foundations torsion
may be resisted in the same manner as for overturning moments and lateral loads In our
foundation analyses we have evaluated the drilled shafts subjected to the torsional moments
provided by the project structural engineer Based on our analyses we believe that the
recommended diameter drilled shafts extending to the lengths recommended should be able
to resist the torsional moments without significant movements We wish to emphasize that
the sides of the drilled holes should be relatively free of loose materials and the concrete for
the drilled shaft should have close contact to the in situ soils after the drilled shaft has been
constructed to provide the torsional resistance required
Uplift Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the pole structure
and drilled shaft foundation and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils The
combined dead load of the pole structure and drilled shaft foundation alone were determined
to be sufficient to resist the uplift loads without significant movements

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction construction procedures and workmanship As a result we
recommend that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the
installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may have a
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we anticipate that drilling through
loose soils will be required during construction of the majority of the drilled shaft
foundations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing
of the drilled holes will be needed during construction Installation of the casing may be by
vibration driving or twisting to advance the casing The contractor should be made aware
that temporary casing of the drilled holes would be needed In addition care should be
exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of
the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained
above the bottom of the casing and or groundwater level during removal of the casing
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and
in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all drilled shafts should be cleaned of
disturbed material prior to constructing the foundation The bottom should also be fairly level
Backfill against the shaft foundations should not be allowed
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids slurry is not recommended Groundwater
conditions are anticipated therefore placement of concrete by wet construction methods
using a tremie pipe will be required A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch
slump range should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the
surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from
the bottom of the drilled shaft
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3936 10 Page 16
Placement of concrete using a tremie pipe will likely cause some groundwater and soil
to discharge out from the top of the drilled shaft The Contractor should be made aware that
this fluid should be properly disposed of Since the water will be turbid from soil particles
disposal into storm drains will likely not be acceptable The Contractor should also be made
aware that a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be
necessary depending on the proposed method of water disposal The Contractor should
consult their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the
latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit application
