REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of
the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
erosion cut valleys deep into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay is located on Mokapu Peninsula which
was built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series These vents are
believed to be among the oldest of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows
from vents formed salients permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the
main body of the island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Ice Age lowering of the sea level in response to
continental glaciation sands from emerged coral reefs were blown inland by the
prevailing winds to form sand dunes along the northern portion of the airfield
An extensive deposit of unconsolidated calcareous marine sediments primarily
consisting of beach sand made up of water worn coral shells and organisms covers
the western tip of Mokapu Peninsula Man made fills consisting of marine sediments
dredged from the ocean floor were used to build up the area around the southern
and western shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the wash rack facility and along the tow way were
explored by drilling and sampling seven borings to depths of about 16 5 to 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Our field exploration disclosed that the area is
generally underlain by a variety of loose to medium dense coralline materials
consisting of sandy gravels and gravelly sands extending to the maximum depths
explored A relatively thin layer of soft lagoonal deposit approximately 2 feet thick and
consisting of light gray silty clay materials was encountered at depths between 5 and
10 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater was encountered in the
borings at depths of approximately 5 to 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface
These depths generally correspond to elevations of approximately 101 to 103 feet
Due to the proximity of the site to the ocean we anticipate that the groundwater level
would be affected by tidal fluctuations
The subsurface conditions at the rinse facility was explored by drilling and
sampling two borings to depths of about 12 and 14 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Our field exploration disclosed that the area is generally underlain by very stiff
to hard silty clays to a depth of about 6 feet below the existing ground surface The
silty clays were underlain by very stiff to hard sandy silts extending to the maximum
depths explored Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the existing wash rack and rinse facilities indicated that
the two sites are underlain by very different types of subsoil and groundwater
conditions Our field exploration at the wash rack facility disclosed that the area is
generally underlain by loose to medium dense coralline materials with a relatively
shallow groundwater table When these coralline materials are properly recompacted
they should provide adequate support for the proposed flexible and rigid pavements at
the new tow way and wash rack expansion However because of the relatively shallow
groundwater table encountered in the drilled borings significant over excavation and
recompaction of the subgrade soils will be difficult during construction Therefore we
recommend that soil reinforcements such as geogrids be incorporated into the flexible
pavement subgrades for stabilization purposes In addition special construction
procedures should be observed during construction to reduce the potential difficulties
with pumping subgrade conditions For the existing wash rack upgrade a minimum
4 inch thick fully bonded PCC overlay may be used to meet the new aircraft loading
conditions based on 75 percent of the design gear loads
Our field exploration at the rinse facility encountered very stiff to hard silty clays
over sandy silts Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time
of our field exploration Due to the potentially expansive nature of the clayey subsoils
encountered a transition layer consisting of 12 inches of select borrow is recommended
between the upper base course and the underlying clayey subgrade at the new load
bearing rigid pavement for the rinse facility Due to the relatively stiff subgrade soil
conditions use of reinforcing fabrics and geogrids would not be required at the rinse
facility expansion

Design Subgrade Conditions
The subgrade conditions encountered in our borings drilled at the wash rack and
rinse facilities are significantly different The following describes our assumptions for
the design subgrade conditions at each location
Wash Rack Facility
Our field exploration indicated that the subgrade soils at the wash rack facility
consisted of loose to medium dense sandy gravels and gravelly sands Based
on our field California Bearing Ratio CBR tests performed on similar type of
subsoil conditions at the adjacent sites a design CBR value of 7 may be used
for the subgrade soils in the flexible pavement analysis Laboratory CBR tests
were performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils obtained during our
field exploration for the wash rack facility Based on the test results shown on
Plates B 3 and B 4 CBR values of 65 and 76 were obtained for the
recompacted coralline materials Therefore it is our opinion that the granular
subgrade soils may be used for the subbase course in the flexible pavement
section provided that the material is properly compacted to the requirements
presented in the Subgrade Preparation and Subbase Preparation sections
For flexible pavement design purposes it is our opinion that a design CBR value
of 40 may be used for the subbase in the analysis
Based on past plate load test results performed on similar types of soil in the
vicinity of the project site the modulus of subgrade reaction values K values
were found to range typically from 200 to 400 pounds per square inch per inch
of deflection pci on the recompacted granular subgrade materials For rigid
pavement thickness design purposes a subgrade modulus of 200 pci may be
ysed provided that the subgrade is prepared in accordance with the recom
mendations presented in the Subgrade Preparation section

Rinse Facility
Our field exploration at the rinse facility disclosed that the area is generally

presents our recommendations for subgrade preparation
Wash Rack Facility
For the flexible pavement at the wash rack facility we recommend that the
subgrade be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned and
compacted to a minimum of 100 percent relative compaction The subgrade
compaction requirement may be reduced to 95 percent relative compaction for
Figid pavement design based on 75 percent design gear toad Retative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage

of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
To reduce the potential for subgrade pumping heavy or rubber tired equipment
should not be allowed on the excavated subgrade In addition vibratory action
of the compaction equipment should not be used in order to reduce potential
pumping of the subgrade If soft clayey spots are encountered at the subgrade

Base Preparation
The new flexible and rigid pavement sections planned for this project will consist
of asphalt concrete and Portland Cement Concrete pavements overlying an aggregate
base course The primary functions of the aggregate base course consist of the
following

1 To provide uniform support for the pavement surface course throughout
the design life
2 To improve load transfer across joints
3 To reduce potential pumping by providing drainage through the use of a

4 To provide a construction platform to expedite construction
5 To reduce the potential for volume change in the subgrade soils by

Portland Cement Concrete Overlay
Strengthening of a pavement will increase its load bearing capacity and is
accomplished with the use of an overlay of either asphalt concrete or Portland Cement
Concrete Fully bonded concrete overlays are used only where the base slab is in
relatively good condition and no structural distress is evident The new concrete
overlay would be placed directly on top of the base slab Prior to placing the overlay

the surface of the base slab must be properly prepared to ensure a full bond with the

Pavement Joints
Joints should be provided in rigid pavements for the following reasons
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused
by temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction
latest revision Joints Reinforcements and Mooring Eyes in Concrete Pavement
should be referred to for joint details and construction requirements
Joint sealants should be used to provide a seal to reduce potential infiltration of
water and to provide a compressible joint Joint sealing compounds used should be
jet fuel resistant and blast resistant and should follow the recommendations of Federal
Specification SS S 200 Type M Two Compound Cold Applied Sealing Compound

The geogrid should be unrolled in the expected primary traffic direction The
ends should be staked after pulling the roll out flat on an even surface of subgrade or
base course material and pulling taut The ends and sides of rolls should be
overlapped by a minimum of 24 inches Construction ruts should be filled with
compacted engineered fill prior to placement of geogrid Tracked vehicles should not
be operated directly on the geogrid a minimum lift thickness of 6 inches of subbase
course material should be placed over the geogrid prior to traffic by tracked vehicles

Guide Specifications for Aggregate Base Course
The aggregate base course should consist of a well graded crushed basalt rock
material with the gradation listed below The aggregate base course materials should
also meet the following requirements
1 Liquid Limit not exceeding 25 as determined by ASTM D 4318
2 Plasticity Index not exceeding 5 as determined by ASTM D 4318
3 Percentage of wear not to exceed 40 as determined by ASTM C 131
4 A minimum CBR value of 90 as determined by ASTM D 1883
5 Gradation as determined by ASTM C 136
