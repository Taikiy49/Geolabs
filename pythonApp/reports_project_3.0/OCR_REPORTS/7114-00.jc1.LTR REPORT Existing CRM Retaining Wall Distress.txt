PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at 2403 Kaululaau Street in the Papakolea subdivision of 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The existing CRM walls are about 115 feet long, 
supporting the roadway (Kaululaau Street).  The ground surface below the retaining wall 
slopes downhill for 10 to 20 feet.  Portions of the existing CRM retaining walls experienced 
up to 2.5 feet of horizontal movement.  The shifted retaining walls are about 60 feet long 
and 6 to 10 feet tall.  Based on our observation, we suspect that the shifting CRM retaining 
walls may be caused by excessive hydrostatic pressure behind the retaining wall and/or 
slope creep. 
It is desired to restrain the retaining walls from further movement.  Due to the 
existing retaining wall geometry, we believe that conventional retaining walls may not be 
feasible without extensive shoring to support the road in order to construct the retaining 
wall footings.  We envision construction of a soil nail wall to restrain/strengthen the 
existing CRM retaining wall.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene Epoch to early

Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the 
island.  The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the Koolau Volcano remained active.  
Therefore, the older Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank was partially buried below the 
younger Koolau Volcano lavas that banked against the Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank. 
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by a 
long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the 
dome-shaped Koolau Shield Volcano.  The erosional valleys grew larger by the 
processes of stream erosion and mass wasting (landslides and rockfall) emanating from 
the steepened valley sidewalls.  The deeper valley floors were filled with accumulations of 
alluvial sediments and colluvial landslide materials as the island eroded and slowly 
depressed beneath the weight of the new landmass. 
Approximately 850,000 to 1,000,000 years ago, some limited volcanic activity 
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents 
scattered across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu.  The eruptions occurred along 
the established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano.  Some of the most notable 
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu, such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head 
Craters, were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  In addition, some 
Honolulu Volcanic Series eruptions occurred in the Tantalus area that blanketed the 
Koolau mountainside and Makiki vicinity with cinders (Roundtop and Sugarloaf pyroclastic 
deposits).  The eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series continued over a period of 
several hundred thousand years, which might have ended as recently as approximately 
30,000 years ago. 
The project site is situated near the northeastern flank of Punchbowl Crater. 
Papakolea subdivision is located on the southerly facing slope of Kanaha Stream valley. 
The axis extends towards the southwesterly direction.  The geologic conditions underlying 
the site are the result of in-situ chemical weathering of basaltic lava flows.  The shallow 
soils are generally residual soils consisting of reddish to brown silty clays which grade 
with depth to silty sand saprolitic soils which, in turn, grade with depth to highly weathered 
basaltic rock. 
FIELD EXPLORATION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3.  The borings extended to about 
31 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous-flight solid stem augers and HQ casing.  The boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487,

Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with

Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples (if 
applicable) in the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, if applicable. 
Core samples of rock and/or hard materials encountered at the project site were 
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary drilling 
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.  The rock and/or hard 
material left in the hollow core of the bit are mechanically recovered for examination and 
description.  Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description 
System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2 of Appendix A. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of soil/rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) also is a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of 
the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the

LABORATORY TESTS 
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and 
classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii.  Selected samples were tested to 
evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered.  
The laboratory testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the 
moisture, density, plasticity, and gradation of the soils encountered. 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to 
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating 
the layers between the borings.  The moisture content tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D2216.  The unit weight tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D2937.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
One Atterberg Limit tests (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2.  
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength characteristics of the basalt 
formation encountered at the project site, Unconfined Compression tests were performed 
on the selected rock core samples in accordance with ASTM D7012.  Results of the 
laboratory tests indicated that the moderately weathered hard basalt rocks encountered in 
the borings have unconfined compressive strength ranging from about 700 to 
33,000 pounds per square inch (psi). The test results are presented on Plate B-3 in 
Appendix B.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings extending to a depth of approximately 31 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Borings were drilled on the roadway behind the existing rock retaining walls. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the project site is underlain by a layer of stiff/dense surface fills and/or 
residual soils over weathered basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth 
drilled of about 31 feet below the existing ground surface.  The weathered basalt formation 
generally consisted of interbedded hard lava rock, cemented and/or weakly cemented 
clinker materials. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate with 
variation in rainfall, surface water runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3 of Appendix A. 
SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
We understand that the project site may be subjected to seismic activity, and 
seismic design considerations will need to be addressed in accordance with Section 1613 
of the International Building Code (2006). Our field exploration generally encountered 
stiff/dense surface fills and/or residual soils overlying weathered basalt rock formation 
extending to the maximum depth of about 31 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and geologic 
setting of the site, the project site may be classified as a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock”, 
Site Class “C”. 
SOIL NAIL RETAINING WALLS 
As mentioned previously, the conventional retaining walls may not be a feasible 
option to replace the existing retaining walls without extensive shoring to support the 
road in order to construct the retaining wall footings.  To protect the public roadway, we 
recommend construction of a soil nail wall, to restrain/strengthen the existing CRM 
retaining wall from further horizontal movement.  We believe that the construction of the 
soil nail wall provides an economical remedial solution and minimizes impact to the 
public access during the construction. 
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope.  A shotcrete facing is 
generally applied to the face of the reinforced excavation to provide the appearance of a 
conventional concrete retaining wall.  The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile 
and shear strengths to the soil.  The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a

system is slightly battered at a slope inclination of about one horizontal to twelve vertical 
(1H:12V). 
Design of the soil nail retaining wall system will need to consider both the internal 
and external stability of the reinforced soil mass.  The design of the internal stability 
includes establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted reinforcing 
bars.  The external stability includes overall slope stability of the reinforced excavation.

stability of the soil nail retaining wall system are provided in the following table.

The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/soil 
interface contact, is variable depending on the type of soil and grout, the overburden 
stress, and the construction procedures used in installing these grouted reinforcing bars.  
For a rough estimate, an average bond stress of 1,000 and 2,000 pound per square foot 
(psf) may be used for the on-site stiff soil and weathered rock, respectively.  The bond 
stress used in the design will need to be confirmed in the field during construction. 
We recommend installing soil nail anchors with a minimum length of 17 feet to 
achieve sufficient internal and external stability for the static and seismic loading 
conditions.  The soil nail anchors should be extended at least 7 feet into the weathered 
basalt formation, to provide a pullout allowable capacity of 32 kips.  Actual lengths of the 
soil nail anchors should be verified in the field, to confirm adequate embedment in the 
weathered basalt rock formation.  Therefore, unit price of the soil nail anchor should be 
required in the contract bid documents. 
Based on our field observation, we envision that the existing CRM retaining walls 
experienced a combination of shallow toe sliding and rotational failure.  We recommend 
installing two rows of the soil nail anchors to provide sufficient internal stability.  The soil 
nail pullout capacity should be verified during construction by proof testing a minimum of 
10 percent or five of the production nails, whichever is greater, to 150 percent of the 
design capacity.

Based on our preliminary slope stability analyses, the soil nail retaining wall system 
with the above design parameters should have a factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the 
combined internal and external stability for the service loading condition.  For the seismic 
loading condition, a factor of safety of 1.0 or greater for the combined internal and external 
stability should be achieved. 
It should be noted that our preliminary soil nail retaining wall evaluation is based on 
the following assumptions. We recommend that the soil nails require some form of 
corrosion protection, such as a fully encapsulated double corrosion protection system or 
epoxy coating. 
Soil nail of 8-inch diameter with #10 reinforcing bar (11/4-inch diameter); 
Soil nail inclination of 15-degree from horizontal; 
Soil nail pattern with 5-foot vertical and horizontal spacing (center-to-
center); 
Design bar yield stress of 36 ksi and punching shear of 45 kips at bar head; 
First layer of soil nail at 3 feet below upper ground surface; 
Surcharge of 100 psf behind the retaining wall and earthquake loading 
condition under a horizontal acceleration of 0.20g. 
The design of the soil nail retaining wall system does not include hydrostatic 
pressures that might be caused by groundwater or water trapped behind the walls.  
Therefore, the soil nail retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures.  New weepholes should be installed near the bottom of the CRM 
walls.  The weephole may consist of 4-inch PVC pipes installed through the existing CRM 
wall and new shotcrete facing.  The end of the PVC pipe should be wrapped with 
nonwoven geotextile fabric for filtration, against the soil/rock behind the CRM wall.  The 
weepholes should be installed at a spacing of 5 feet center-to-center.  The ground in front 
of the weephole should be protected by GRP, to minimize potential erosion caused by 
runoff water. 
The upper ground surface should be sloped to divert water away from the CRM 
walls and to reduce water infiltration behind the walls.  In addition, the project civil 
engineer should verify that the soil nail construction has adequate clearance from the 
existing underground utilities within the roadway. 
As part of the special inspection in accordance with IBC 2006 Section 1704 – 
Level 1 Special Inspection, Geolabs should be retained as design geotechnical engineer 
of record for observation of the drilling, grouting and proof testing during soil nail retaining 
wall construction.  The proof testing nails should be selected by Geolabs after installation 
based on the drilling and grouting records.
