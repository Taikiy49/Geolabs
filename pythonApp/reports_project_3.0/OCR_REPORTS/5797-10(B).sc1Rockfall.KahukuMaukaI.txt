REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
The project site straddles the eroded north facing foothill of the Koolau Range and
is within the Hoolapa Gulch drainage basin as shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 The drainage basin contains an intermittent stream channel which passes
through the project site The project site interior contains some hills bordering
Hoolapa Gulch which are generally composed of scattered low relief basaltic rock
outcroppings mixed with residual soil materials Other taller slopes containing frequent
massive basalt rock outcroppings reside outside the project limits at the northwestern
corner of the site Based on our reconnaissance the hilly terrain within the project site and
vicinity is underlain by weathered volcanic rock and derived residual soils of the
Tertiary age Koolau Volcanic Series
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is within the lower elevation foothills of the Koolau Range in the
Kahuku area of the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is bounded by Kamehameha
Highway towards the north and the existing Charlie Road along the eastern perimeter
Charlie Road is an existing paved road that provides access to the nearby military
reservation and Kahuku Training Area Other military and privately owned lands extend
towards the west and south from the project site The project location and general vicinity
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The project site generally encompasses an open range vegetated with thick brush
and grasses with scattered trees An intermittent stream with confining banks typically on
the order of about 5 to 10 feet high occupies the drainage basin identified as
Hoolapa Gulch The stream channel passes through the project site and is highlighted on
the Site Plan Plate 2
The project site interior consists of hilly terrain composed of residual and saprolitic
soils with sporadic areas of generally low relief surface rock outcropping An oval shaped
hill with steep radiating side slopes resides outside of the development boundary near the
northwest corner of the project site The hill contains frequent massive rock outcroppings
lining the summit and side slopes

SITE RECONNAISSANCE
A geological reconnaissance of the project site was conducted to obtain a visual
overview of the existing site conditions with respect to the occurrence of potential natural
hazards such as existing slope instability past debris flow activity and potential rockfall
source areas A primary purpose of the field reconnaissance was to obtain an overview of
the general location size spatial distribution and setting of existing surface boulders and
rock outcroppings residing on sloping terrain within the project limits and bordering the
project periphery In addition the field reconnaissance was performed to evaluate
probable boulder rolling paths that could encroach on down slope development The field
information was used to support our computer simulation and statistical evaluation of
potential rockfall behavior using the CRSP analytical engineering tool
Based on our reconnaissance evidence of active slope instability or debris flow
deposits was not observed The existing slopes and drainage features appear generally
stable and overt visible signs of previous deep seated landslide earth movement or debris
flow activity were not encountered

subdivision lots appear to have some exposure to potential rockfall hazards The lots that
may have exposure to potential rockfall hazards include Lots 2 3 and 4 The affected lots
are within and bordering the hilly terrain at the western and central portions of the project
site

Site reconnaissance and a literature review was performed to assist in the
evaluation of the existing project site conditions with respect to natural hazards such as
rockfall potential slope stability and debris flow potential In addition to our
reconnaissance and literature review we performed some computer simulation and
statistical analysis of potential rockfall activity using the Colorado Rockfall Simulation

Rockfall Simulation Analysis CRSP
As previously discussed we performed a site reconnaissance to visually identify
and locate potential rockfall hazards such as loose surface boulders and unstable rock
outcroppings residing within the project site and the adjacent up slopes The site
reconnaissance also permitted us to evaluate probable rolling trajectories in relation to the
existing topography and proposed development boundaries Following the site
reconnaissance selected information recorded in the field was input to the CRSP to
validate the field observations
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input
parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project
were assigned based on the observations and measurements collected in the field The
program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on hill slope
topographic profiles and other specific input information such as rock size shape and
parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions Information obtained
from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity bouncing height
kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is useful to assist in the
site specific design of appropriate development setbacks and various rockfall mitigation
structures such as rockfall impact barriers and rock catchment ditches
Topographic information input to the CRSP was obtained from the project site
topographic map provided for our use The topographic survey is from an aerial survey
The topographic contour map provides 5 foot contour intervals As a result the model hill
slope profiles developed from the available topographic map provide a generalized
representation of the existing ground surface topography Two generalized slope profiles
identified as Slope Profile 1 and 2 on Plate 3 were used in our CRSP analyses to evaluate
the potential rockfall hazard conditions at the western side of Lots 3 and 4
Our CRSP analysis was performed using spherical shaped boulders
conservative scenario ranging from 2 to 12 feet in dimension initiating from specified
source area s identified during the field reconnaissance For each simulation run
1 000 source rocks were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results
Numerical input coefficients were used which approximated the typical ground surface
relief and roughness conditions
Our rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable interception and
catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the computer rockfall
simulation analysis CRSP The 90 percent catchment criteria is a target that is commonly
used in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified by a statistical
and probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences such as rockfall activity
Since the analyses are based on statistics and probability a 100 percent criteria is
impractical to achieve

Recommended Conceptual Rockfall Mitigation Program
Based on our study we recommend a mitigation program consisting of the
following components
e Dwelling Location Restrictions Lots 3 and 4
e Grading and Rock Removal Lots 2 and 3
The construction of other rockfall control and containment structures such as
rockfall impact barrier fencing net drapery systems and catchment ditches are less
desirable due to the high construction and the probable replacement costs the long term
maintenance requirements and access issues
Based on our study it is our professional opinion that an effective permanent and
low maintenance rockfall mitigation program for this project should consist of site grading
and the implementation of permanent dwelling location restrictions We recommend
excavation and grading of some of the existing hills to remove the existing potential
rockfall hazards from within Lots 2 and 3 In addition we recommend the establishment of
dwelling location restrictions at portions of Lots 3 and 4 westerly of Hoolapa Gulch
Dwelling Location Restrictions
The establishment of permanent dwelling location restrictions for rockfall protection
is appropriate where the site conditions terrain and available land area are
favorable for the natural dissipation of rolling rock kinetic energy within a designated
no build buffer land area Dwellings such as permanent residential structures
should not be permitted for construction within the designated rockfall buffer area
Grading and Rock Removal
Earth grading consisting of cut and or fill earthwork using heavy construction
machinery could be employed to reduce the potential rockfall hazard by altering
the existing terrain Hills containing potential rockfall hazards could be entirely
removed or graded to reduce the potential rockfall hazards and to achieve stable
graded slope settings In some cases engineered fills could be placed to develop a

The removal of surface rock outcroppings and boulders by the performance of a
boulder sweep and the manual scaling of loose rock from existing rock outcrops is
viable where the targeted rocks or slope areas are generally limited and or readily
defined Rock scaling is the selective removal of potential rockfall hazards from
steeper slopes using manual labor and hand tools to pry loose rock from the slope
surface and move the material to the slope bottom for collection and proper
disposal Rock scaling crews remove loose rocks in a methodical controlled
manner working from top to bottom on the slope Manual rock scaling is often
employed where access is limited and the use of heavy construction equipment is
not feasible due to the terrain The use of hydraulic rock splitters pneumatic tools
and small blasting charges may be used to break and remove larger rock units from
the slope Readily identifiable rock outcrops and boulders within a finite area could
be removed by heavy construction equipment if the access conditions are suitable

professional opinion that the recommended rockfall mitigation program is viable and
should meet our minimum 90 percent protection criteria The grading and dwelling location
restrictions should be permanent and require minimal maintenance
