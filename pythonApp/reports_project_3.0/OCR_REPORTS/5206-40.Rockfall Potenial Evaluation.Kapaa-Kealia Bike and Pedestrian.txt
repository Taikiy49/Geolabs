REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano built
by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 5 to
6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 2 million
years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion Following the cessation of this
main volcano shield building phase about 172 million years ago renewed volcanic activity
occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series
and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The basalt rock of the Koloa Volcanic Series is generally characterized by thick lava
flows of dense basaltic rock extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in
various locales on the eastern half of the Island of Kauai Associated with the Koloa
Volcanic Series lava flows are some localized volcanic deposits consisting of pyroclastic
materials volcanic ash and cinders which usually are encountered as surficial mantling
deposits and accumulations surrounding the cinder cone vents Rocks of the Koloa
Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project
site
The project site is along the coastline easterly of Kuhio Highway in the Kapaa and
Kealia areas of the Island of Kauai The project site is underlain by weathered basaltic rock
and derived residual and saprolitic soils of the Koloa Volcanic Series Coastal hill slopes
and some sea cliffs of about 20 to 30 feet in height border the makai side of the path
alignment The steeper coastal hill slopes extend inland and merge with gently sloping
bluff top terrain Due to the age and environmental exposure of the coastal slopes the
basaltic rock materials are generally highly to extremely weathered saprolitic with hard
basaltic rock occurring as localized hillside outcroppings and as rock outcroppings along
the shoreline In addition many erosional remnant hard basaltic rock boulders occur as
scattered rocks embedded within the near surface residual and saprolitic soils which
comprise the majority of the sloping ground surfaces upslope of the path alignment The
localized weathered basaltic rock outcroppings and remnant surface boulders residing on
sloping terrain above the bike path were identified as potential rockfall hazards

SITE RECONNAISSANCE
We performed a site reconnaissance from August 1 through 3 2007 to identify and
map the potential rockfall hazards residing on the upslope side of the path which are
believed to have some potential to affect the path alignment as rockfall hazards The
mapping effort was also performed to develop generalized descriptions of the various site
conditions and to map the approximate distribution of existing surface rock outcroppings
and surface boulders on the hillsides Discussion of the existing site conditions for each
potential rockfall hazard slope segments is presented in the Potential Rockfall Conditions
section below Condensed maps depicting the existing site conditions and relevant field
information are provided for reference on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 14 It should
be noted that the maps may not show all of the existing boulders and rock outcroppings
due to some access limitations and occurrence of generally localized ground cover of
vegetation
Based on our field reconnaissance the various slope segments A 1 through A 4
and B 1 through B 10 are classified with respect to the anticipated relative level of
potential rockfall hazard and risk The slopes were classified as low moderate or high
hazard based on the existing site conditions and potential for dangerous rockfall activity
Following are descriptions of the relative hazard level classification used in our evaluation
for this project
Low Potential Hazard
Includes hill slopes and potential rockfall hazards that appear to be generally stable
embedded settings or consists of small rock fragment size typically less than
about 18 inches in dimension with a generally low potential for rock movement
initiation and resultant danger to persons and structures The included hill slopes
are typically lower in height with flatter slope gradients containing mainly isolated
rockfall hazards or has significant buffering terrain to reduce the potential for

Moderate Potential Hazard
Includes hill slopes and potential rockfall hazards that appear to be potentially
unstable or consists of larger rock fragment size typically greater than about
18 inches in dimension with a moderate potential for rock movement initiation and
resultant danger to persons or structures The included hill slopes are typically

High Potential Hazard
Includes hill slopes and potential rockfall hazards that appear to be potentially
unstable or consists of many larger rock fragment sizes typically greater than
about 18 inches in dimension with a high potential for rock movement initiation and
resultant danger to persons or structures The included hill slopes are typically
greater than about 15 feet in height with inclinations steeper than about
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V and contain multiple rockfall hazards
consisting of partly embedded and loose surface rocks with minimal hillside
buffering protection to reduce the potential for encroachment on the path alignment
The classification also includes hillsides containing rockfall hazards encompassed
by a lower steep cut slope with upper natural slopes extending higher toward the
hill top

Potential Rockfall Simulation Analysis
As previously discussed we performed a site reconnaissance to visually identify
potential rockfall hazards such as surface boulders and unstable rock outcroppings
residing on sloping terrain that could affect public safety on the bike path The site
reconnaissance also permitted us to visually evaluate probable rock rolling trajectories in
relation to the topography and the bike path alignment Information from the field
reconnaissance was used in our computer simulation of potential rockfall conditions using
the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP
The purpose for our preliminary CRSP analyses is to facilitate the evaluation of
potential rockfall conditions which we identified by visual observation during our field
reconnaissance It is widely recognized in rockfall engineering that natural initiation of
rockfall movement on sloping terrain is generally retarded on hill slopes that are flatter than
about 3H 1V Therefore it was desired to perform some preliminary rockfall simulation
using a variety of hill slope inclinations commonly encountered throughout the project site
to supplement our opinions which are based in part on visual observations
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input
parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project
were assigned based on our observations recorded in the field The program provides a
statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on hill slope topographic profiles
and other specific input information such as rock size shape and parameters used to
quantify the typical ground surface conditions Information obtained from the CRSP
analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity bouncing height kinetic energy and
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
Akinaka Associates Ltd Page 15
W O 5206 40
roll out distance The output information is useful to assist in evaluating potential rockfall
inundation zones and the design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall
impact barriers rock catchment ditches or other rockfall containment systems
Topographic information input to the CRSP was obtained from the available project
site topographic map The topographic map provides 2 foot contour intervals and shows
the path alignment in relation to the surrounding hill slope topography Therefore the
model hill slope profiles developed from the available topographic map provide a
generalized representation of the existing ground surface topography
Six selected hill slope model profiles identified as Slope Profiles 1 through 6 were
developed to support our CRSP analysis The approximate locations of the slope profiles
used in our analysis are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 14 The graphic
representations of the model slope profiles produced from the available topographic

Our CRSP analysis was performed using spherical shaped boulders
conservative scenario ranging in size from 2 to 5 feet in dimension initiating from
specified source area s identified during the field reconnaissance For each simulation
run 1 000 source rocks were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results
Numerical input coefficients were used which approximated the typical ground surface
relief and roughness conditions Our rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable
interception and catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the
computer rockfall simulation analysis CRSP The 90 percent catchment criteria is a target
that is commonly used in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified
by a statistical and probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences such as
rockfall activity Since the analyses are based on statistics and probability a 100 percent
criteria is impractical to achieve

Option 1 Rock Slope Scaling
Rock slope scaling is typically performed to reduce the potential for rockfall activity
on slopes composed mainly of rock material Rock slope scaling is performed to
remove loose fractured rock material from the slope face The work is typically
performed manually by skilled scaling crews who may be suspended on the
hillside using climbing gear Hand tools such as pry bars are most commonly used
to scale the loose rock material from the slope face The work is performed in a
methodical controlled manner from top to bottom to move the scaled material
toward the bottom of the slope for collection and disposal
Rock slope scaling may be performed as a stand alone mitigation scheme or it may
be performed to prepare a slope for the installation of wire mesh drapery or
anchored wire mesh Rock slope scaling may need to be repeated as the rock
material continues to weather which may produce additional loose rock as time
progresses Temporary protection of existing down slope structures and persons is
required during the scaling work It is our opinion that rock slope scaling could be a
viable rockfall mitigation option for most of the Area A slopes Slope Segments

Option 4 Rockfall Impact Barrier Fence
Rockfall impact barriers are commonly used to intercept and retain rockfall on or
below sloping areas containing numerous widely scattered potential rockfall
hazards that cannot feasibly be stabilized in place or removed Rockfall impact
barriers are specially designed continuous flexible fences consisting of steel posts
connected to wire rope support cables from which wire mesh panels and steel ring
nets or cable nets are hung The barrier is designed to flex and absorb rockfall
impact energy while retaining the fallen material In this very corrosive coastal
environment heavier gauge sacrificial steel materials may be necessary to
prolong the life of the steel barrier system Rockfall barriers require periodic
inspection and some maintenance to maintain the desired level of rockfall
protection over time Following a large and damaging rockfall event the debris
must be removed from the nets and some components of the system may require
replacement Eventual replacement of the barrier system may be required due to
cumulative environmental corrosion and degradation The rockfall barrier could be
constructed along the top of cut slopes or along the toe of flatter slopes where
rockfall trajectory would not overtop the barrier
It is our opinion that low to medium capacity rockfall impact barriers could be a
viable rockfall mitigation scheme which may be stand alone in some cases or
combined with other rockfall mitigation such as rock slope scaling boulder removal
slope flattening or construction of anchored wire mesh for slope stabilization
where necessary It is our opinion that low to medium capacity rockfall impact
barriers of about 6 to 8 feet in height may be a viable rockfall mitigation option for
hazards cannot feasibly be removed and where favorable barrier site topographic

Option 5 Wire Mesh Slope Drapery
The use of wire mesh drapery could be a viable option to reduce rockfall initiation
from sloping terrain and to control the fall of rocks beneath the mesh material on
steeper slopes The method involves the draping and connection of overlapping
wire mesh panels supporting cables and ground anchors on the slope surface
The galvanized and zinc coated double twisted wire mesh can be manufactured
with a colored PVC coating to help protect against abrasion and environmental
corrosion The slope must be prepared for the installation with the removal of most
woody stem shrubbery from the slope face to permit a close fit on the hill An
intensive rock scaling effort must be performed to remove loose fractured rocks
debris and overhanging material from the hillside The mesh panels could be
placed using a helicopter or a crane to unfurl the mesh material on the hillside
Periodic inspection and maintenance of the system is required as well as
occasional removal of entrapped debris Eventual replacement of the major system
components may be necessary due to the highly corrosive coastal environment
The mesh is installed by establishing a top line of grouted anchor bars and wire
rope support cables The overlapped and clipped mesh panels are hung from the
top support rope The mesh is typically not pinned to the slope face and the bottom
of the drapery is left open to allow discharge of fallen material into a limited width
catchment zone Alternatively for spot treatment the wire mesh could be placed
locally over generally isolated problem areas using an appropriate pattern of drilled
and grouted ground anchors to pin the mesh and underlying loose rock material
Wire mesh is generally useful for stabilizing and controlling blocks of rock that are
less than about 3 feet in dimension The stabilization and control of larger blocks of
rock require the use of heavier steel ring nets or cable nets that are more resistant
to tearing
It is our opinion that wire mesh drapery could be a viable rockfall mitigation scheme
as a stand alone method in some cases or combined with other rockfall mitigation
schemes such as an upslope rockfall impact barrier or individual boulder removal
where necessary It is our opinion that wire mesh drapery is an appropriate rockfall
mitigation option for Slope Segments A 2 through A 4 A typical sketch for wire
mesh drapery is presented on Plate D 5

Option 6 Anchored Wire Mesh
Anchored wire mesh was developed as a stronger wire mesh material that can be
used to stabilize steep slopes composed of unconsolidated materials and rocks
The wire mesh is composed of high tensile steel wire mesh that may be anchored
to the slope face using a pattern of commercially available rock anchors nails on
the slope Once the mesh is fastened to the slope by the rock nails it is
pre stressed with a defined force against the nails utilizing special spike plates
Reacting to the tension of the nails the mesh presses on the slope surface and
acts to minimize deformation and slippage to prevent breakouts from occurring For
this project we recommend preparing the steep cut slopes by cutting them to a
slightly flatter inclination such as 1H 1V and removing the existing slope face
irregularities to promote a close mesh fit on the terrain The anchored wire mesh
could also be used to spot treat unstable rock outcrops that are difficult and too
complex to remove
We believe the anchored wire mesh system could be a viable option to reduce
falling rock from the cut slope and to improve the stability of the cut slopes against
shallow slope failures Erosion control mats may be installed in conjunction with the
anchored wire mesh to facilitate greening of the slope and to help protect the
exposed slope face from surface erosion temporarily until the permanent ground
cover is re established
It is our opinion that anchored wire mesh could be a viable rockfall mitigation
scheme combined with other rockfall mitigation such as an upslope rockfall impact
barrier or individual boulder removal where necessary It is our opinion that
anchored wire mesh is an appropriate rockfall mitigation option for Slope Segments
B 1 through B 5 A typical sketch anchored wire mesh is shown on Plate D 6
