REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised
deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
GEOLABS INC
W O 4489 00 Page 6
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments at correspondingly higher elevations in areas protected by fringing reefs and
beach strands During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward
The project site is underlain by alluvial soil deposits Agricultural land development
and other projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and the foundation loads anticipated we recommend
that deep foundations consisting of drilled shafts or piles be used for foundation support
of the proposed 36 inch waterline at the stream crossings The drilled shaft or pile
foundations would derive support principally from adhesion between the drilled shaft or pile
and the medium stiff silty clay and the very stiff to hard alluvium encountered in our
borings Based on the foundation loads anticipated we recommend that a shaft diameter
of 30 inches and embedment depths of at least 50 feet below the bottom of footing be used
for the drilled shaft foundations We recommend that 16 5 inch octagonal concrete piles with
a minimum length of 50 feet below the pile cap be used for the pile foundations
It should be noted that potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered
during installation of the drilled shafts due to the presence of very soft silty clay soils and
groundwater levels encountered in the borings Therefore temporary casing of the drilled
shaft holes may be required to reduce the potential for caving in of the holes during drilling
GEOLABS INC
W O 4489 00 Page 9
In addition placement of concrete by tremie methods will be required for the drilled shafts
since groundwater is expected within the depths of the drilled shafts
Based on the generally poor subsoil conditions encountered at the location of the
proposed valve vaults we recommend that a stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of
clean granular fill material wrapped on all sides with a layer of non woven filter fabric such
as Mirafi 180 N or equivalent be provided below the valve vaults An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 1 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used on the stabilization layer
Due to the shallow groundwater table encountered in our boring we believe that interlocking
steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing may be required for temporary shoring purposes
especially where dewatering of the excavations will be necessary Detailed discussions and
recommendations for design of the structures are presented in the following sections of this
report
Drilled Shaft Foundations
We understand that a bridge crossing is one of the options considered for this project
Details of the structural loading were not available at the time this report was prepared
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads anticipated
we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of four 30 inch diameter
cast in place drilled shafts be used at each abutment location to support the proposed
36 inch waterline crossing Waimanalo and Kahawai Streams The drilled shafts should

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be present to
observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the
following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
Due to the relatively shallow groundwater and soft loose soils encountered in our
borings cave in of the material will likely occur during the drilling operation
Therefore to reduce the potential for caving in of the holes we recommend that
temporary casing be required to facilitate installation of the drilled shafts Care should
be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for
necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of concrete above
the bottom of the casing or adequate concrete head to counter hydrostatic
pressures should be maintained during removal of the casing In addition the
anticipated relatively shallow groundwater levels may also pose construction
difficulties since proper observation of the sides and bottom of the shafts may not be
possible
Although dense gravels were not encountered in the borings drilled at the proposed
bridge crossing foundation locations during our field exploration there exists the
potential for presence of remnant gravels or boulders embedded in the alluvium soil
horizon Therefore the drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate
equipment and tools to drill through these obstructions where encountered
Appropriate measures will also be needed to avoid dislodging boulders into the drilled
shaft hole during the drilling and shaft installation process
Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
GEOLABS INC
W O 4489 00 Page 12
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended Because
of the groundwater table anticipated within the depths of the drilled shaft
excavations concrete placement by tremie method will be required during
construction of the drilled shafts under such conditions The concrete should be
placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the
bottom of the drilled hole A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 7 to 9 inch
slump range should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and
the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to
reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix A
representative from Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of drilled
shafts during construction

Predrilling
Our borings at the site encountered very dense surface fill materials Therefore we
recommend that the pile locations be predrilled to approximately 10 feet below the
design finished pile cap grades to reduce the potential for hard driving conditions
near the ground surface during pile installation In addition predrilling the pile
locations will also aid in reducing the vibrations generated during the pile driving
operations on the adjacent existing structures The diameter of the predrilled holes
should be limited to approximately 20 inches for the 16 5 inch octagonal piles to
provide the pile with sufficient soil contact for lateral load resistance If an
over sized predrilled hole is encountered during construction the annular space
between the piles and predrilled holes should be backfilled by jetting clean sand into
the void to provide the recommended lateral load capacity
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 50 000 foot pounds per blow The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the soft soils may be controlled at a lower energy
to reduce tensile stress development within the piles which could cause damage to
the piles

pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of installing
the piles to the recommended capacity

Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete
piles pile driving may be terminated when the aggregated blow counts are at least
3 blows per inch for the last 12 inches or 8 blows per inch for the last 4 inches The
preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and or modified after selection of the
pile driving equipment and performance of a wave equation analysis In addition
the pile driving criteria may be modified during pile driving to accommodate
variations of soil conditions in the field
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for
more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft
driving such as when the pile tip is within soft soils the hammer stroke or energy
should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop
within the pile

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Following clearing
and grubbing subgrades to receive fill should be scarified to a minimum depth of
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of new fill
material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density determined by ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing and grubbing operations
should be over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade should
be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices
for over excavation and fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site surface fills may be re used as a source of general
fill and backfill materials provided that they contain no lumps or fragments greater
than 3 inches in maximum dimension The soft and wet clayey and silty soils
generated from the deeper excavations should not be re used as fill and backfill
materials and should be disposed of properly off site
Where imported fill materials are required as general fill and backfill materials the
imported fill and or backfill materials should consist of well graded granular

materials with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or less The materials should
have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition the materials should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should
be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery
to the project site for the intended use

The fill and backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content

Excavation Support
In order to install the new waterline and valve vault boxes we anticipate that an
excavation on the order of about 5 to 10 feet below the ground surface will be required for
the project construction Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we anticipate
that conventional excavation equipment may be used for the proposed excavation The
method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated material should be
stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from
the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings and the depths of
the excavations anticipated we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavation with
bracing will be required to support the excavation and to aid in dewatering Excavations
shoring support and construction works should conform to all applicable Federal State
and other safety requirements
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements Because of the depths of the proposed excavations variable
subsurface soil conditions and shallow groundwater level a qualified geotechnical and or
structural engineer should be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring
system used The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to be
present on site during excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe any changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen
subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or
techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
Even with proper construction procedures some movement of the shoring system
and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Additionally based on the soft soils encountered at the site new fills planned
at the site would induce ground settlements as a result of consolidation of the underlying
GEOLABS INC
W O 4489 00 Page 22
soft loose soils Therefore it is difficult to estimate accurately the magnitude of ground
movements during construction The magnitude of ground movements also depends
greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring supports are
installed the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent ground should be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with
instruments
Our field exploration generally indicates that soft subsoil conditions should be
expected near the invert elevations of the waterlines and valve vault boxes Therefore we
recommend that a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of 18 to 24 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent be provided at the bottom of the excavations exposing soft soil conditions The
stabilization layer should extend to the edges of the excavation shoring supports The
purpose of the stabilization layer is to provide a working platform at the bottom of the
excavation in order for personnel to work within the excavation The stabilization layer
would also aid in the dewatering operations for the excavation
Dewatering
In general we envision that dewatering will be required for the excavation at the site
Groundwater was encountered at depths ranging from about 0 5 to 10 feet below the existing
ground surface in the borings drilled during our field explorations Based on the groundwater
levels anticipated and the nature of the soils to be encountered during the excavations
dewatering will be required for this project Therefore dewatering provisions will need to be
included in the contract documents for the proposed construction Since the excavation will
involve discharge of groundwater a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System
NPDES permit may be necessary The contractor should consult their independent
consultant for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit application
GEOLABS INC
W O 4489 00 Page 23
Based on the anticipated depths of the excavations and the relatively shallow
groundwater table dewatering with cut off wails such as inter locking sheet piles may be
considered However sumps will be needed to collect water that percolates up into the
base of the excavation or infiltrates through the sheet piles The sheet piles should be
driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to
reduce the amount of dewatering within the excavations in areas underlain by soft subsoils
Use of an inter locking sheet pile shoring support system with dewatering with tremie
concrete plug is relatively watertight which should allow the groundwater levels outside the
excavations to be maintained close to the original pre construction levels Therefore some
type of groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring
support such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations should
be carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
Dewatering Considerations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of the
proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may
include but are not limited to sheet piling tremie plugs and sump pumping We
suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
GEOLABS INC
W O 4489 00 Page 24
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing buildings roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the
contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and
to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations granular subsoils may be
susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should be
carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed
without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with the shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a
blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the soft compressible soils Therefore the use of
GEOLABS INC

a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level
should not be allowed
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected
to the excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater drawdown
resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other existing
adjacent structures

block Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf above the groundwater table
and 100 pcf below the groundwater for thrust blocks embedded in the in situ soil To estimate
the frictional resistance between the thrust block and surrounding soils a coefficient of
friction of 0 2 may be used These values assume that the soil around the block is
well compacted and the top of the thrust block is embedded a minimum of 3 feet below the
ground surface

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test results
is provided on Plate B 1
Four sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 and ASTM D 1140 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 2
Two unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strengths of the underlying clayey soil
The test results are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and
B 6
