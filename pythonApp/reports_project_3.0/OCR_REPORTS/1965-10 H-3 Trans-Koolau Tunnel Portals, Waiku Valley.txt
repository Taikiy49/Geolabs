SUMMARY
A geotechnical exploration program was carried out to provide
pertinent soil and rock mass classification data to aid in the design and
construction of the H Tunnel portal buildings in Haiku
Valley Kaneohe Oahu Hawaii
The program consists of drilling eight 8 borings installing two
2 piezometers performing five 5 field permeability tests and
conducting borehole orientations and laboratory testing
Results of the subsurface exploration indicate that the tunnel
portals will be constructed predominantly in highly to moderately
weathered basalt of the Koolau formation This formation consists of aa
and pahoehoe flows that are layers of moderately to closely jointed dense
to vesicular basalt and are interbedded with layers of weathered clinkers
basalt fragments
Groundwater levels recorded in open holes and piezometers during the
monitoring period January to April 1989 varied between E1 760 and 824
at the borehole locations

2 0 PROJECT AND SITE DESCRIPTION
The H Tunnel is located in the upper Haiku Valley
area of Kaneohe and is within the Koolaupoko District of Oahu Plates 1
and 2 When completed the H 3 Highway will alleviate the increasing
traffic flow difficulties experienced by the existing Likelike and Pali
Highways The proposed project is jointly supported by the State of
Hawaii and the U S Department of Transportation
The proposed tunnel portals are situated at the foot of the Pali
near Honolulu Hawaii The topography and various surface features along
the alignment are shown on Plates 1 and 2
The proposed H 3 tunnel traverses the foothills of basaltic lava
flow ridges The Haiku portal area presently varies in surface elevation
from approximately 780 to 885 feet above Mean Sea Level

4 1 Geologic Setting
The entire Hawaiian Archipelago forms a southeast trending arc of
volcanic islands 1491 miles long The major Hawaiian Islands are located
along a 404 mile arc at the southeasterly end of the chain Oahu the
third largest of the eight 8 major islands of the Hawaiian Archipelago
covers a land area of approximately 604 square miles The island was
formed by the volcanic activity of two 2 large shield shaped volcanoes
4 1
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1965 10
The island of Oahu is divided into four 4 main areas Waianae
Range Koolau Range Schofield Plateau and Coastal Plains
The chief topographic features of the island of Oahu are the two
elongated basaltic domes the Waianae Range on the west side and the
Koolau Range on the east side The Schofield Plateau is located where
the two 2 ranges merge and was formed by ponded lavas of the Koolau
Volcano The Coastal Plains adjacent to the ocean are nearly level and
are formed by intercalated marine and terrigenous sediments The
Honolulu Plain extends easterly from Pearl Harbor and is mostly occupied
by the City of Honolulu
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene periods by the accumulation of thinly bedded basaltic
lava flows erupted from numerous vents along a fissure line over 30 miles
long that roughly parallels the mountain trends No evidence of a
general break in the deposition of these flows has been found The main
Koolau Shield building activity ended approximately 2 5 million years
ago
The project site is located within the Koolaupoko District which
lies about the midrange flank of the Koolau Range that forms the central
eastern portion of Oahu Plate 1
Basalt Flows of the Koolau Formation
The Haiku Valley tunnel portals will be excavated in basaltic lava
flows of the Koolau volcanics Two types of lava flows are recognized
aa and pahoehoe Aa is the Hawaiian term for lava flow typified by a
4 2
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1965 10
rough jagged spinose clinkery surface and pahoehoe is the Hawaiian
term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth billowy or ropy
surface The flows generally average 10 feet or less in thickness
particularly the aa flows The entire rock mass shows exceptional
petrographic uniformity Wentworth 1951 Although pahoehoe and aa lava
flows are common in the Koolau Formation there is a preponderance of aa
flows on the lower flanks of the mass Aa flows include both clinker
basalt fragments and dense interior components Nearly all the Koolau
lava flows are vesicular The pahoehoe flows have a rather regular
distribution of vesicles which often shows marked flow lines The aa
dense masses have almost no vesicles in some places but more commonly
have a few large irregular vesicles or vugs The contacts between lava
flows are irregular due to the broken character of the pre existing
surface formed by the chilling and increase in viscosity of the advancing
lava
Lava tubes are generally encountered in any extensive tunnel
excavated through the Koolau Formation Wentworth 1951 Commonly lava
tubes are 1 or 2 feet in width however some 20 foot wide tubes have
been reported by Wentworth 1951 Lava tubes may form in aa flows but
far greater numbers occur in pahoehoe flows MacDonald et al 1983
An important characteristic of the Koolau lava flow formation is its
generally high permeability This is due to contraction or shrinkage
joints normal to lava flow surfaces open spaces along most of the
contacts between flows lava tubes and the open structure of many of the
clinker layers Field permeability tests are believed to under predict
observed rock mass permeability

The chemical composition of the Koolau basalts varies from normal
basalts to olivine basalt or hypersthene basalt from studies of rock
thin sections by previous investigators see Data Sources The
variation in lithology between flows a few feet apart laterally or
vertically may be as great as the variation between flows widely
separated
Fresh groundwater is impounded by dikes in a northwest trending rift
zone at and near the Koolau crest amd for some distance downgradient of
the crest The fresh groundwater floats on saline groundwater as lenses
outside the rift zones The downgradient extent of the dike impounded
reservoir is difficult to define The eastern flank windward slope of
the Koolau range has not been eroded as extensively as the leeward side
Dikes were noted near the study area The existence of dikes is believed
to diminish significantly to the west of the Koolau crest
The project site is located in the marginal dike zone where the
dikes number fewer than 100 per mile and constitute less than five
percent 5 of the total rock volume Takahashi and Mink 1982 The
general alignment of some dikes and delineation of the dike complex and
marginal dike zone are shown on Plate 3
The marginal dike zone yields more dike impounded water than the
dike complex because the dike complex acts as a deterrent to water flow
in all directions whereas the marginal dike zone generally affects flow
in the direction normal to the dikes
The location of water levels during construction will depend largely
on the number of dike intersections and how much the trend of dikes
deviate from the direction of groundwater flow Perched water reservoirs
may also be encountered

Weathered Basalt Clinker
Clinker zones encountered during the field exploration were commonly
weathered in situ The degree of weathering generally varies from
moderately to highly weathered
Most clinker layers encountered in the borings are less than 3 feet
thick Zones of high core loss may consist of filled lava tubes or soil
materials or both The thick clinker layers may represent a succession
of thin lava flows comprised of thin dense interiors and overlapping
Clinker that make it difficult to differentiate individual flow units
Clinkery materials recovered by coring mostly contain cobble gravel
to sand sized basalt fragments which are cemented together The apparent
lack of fines in some cored clinkery material and the occasionally high
core losses may be a result of material being washed out by drilling
fluids
Residual Material
The presence of residual soil at the Haiku site is limited due to
the narrow ridges and steep sideslopes The soil on the ridge forms a
perched cap that is thicker at the top and overlaps down the sides to a
point where erosion keeps pace with weathering in the drainageways
Because of vigorous plant growth the top soil layer may be gray or
black Beneath the soil layer is a zone of weathered rock This rock is
soft in places and exhibits the characteristic structures of lava flows
The weathered Koolau rock presents a complete range from slightly
weathered rock through deeply weathered rock to structureless residual
soils

Recent Sediments
Colluvium is the mass of detrital mantle rock that overlies the
basalt bedrock and consists of soil and rock detritus that has moved by
gravity from its original position Lower down on the side hills the
mantle may not exist and bedrock outcrops are exposed In some places
the mantle rock is fairly strongly cemented and moderately compacted
Shallow avalanches of colluvium usually only a few feet thick often
occur after heavy rains
The Alluvium unit is composed of terrestrial sediments deposited by
the movement of water and is mostly silts and clay with gravel and
cobbles Some gravels are uniformly graded and contain cobbles Partly
because of the dominance of chemical weathering the fine grain size of
the parent rocks the steep slopes and short streams these sediments
are predominantly fine grained and poorly sorted
In the active stream channels the loose bouldery and pebbly
detritus is classified as recent alluvium

Boring Logs Equipment and Sampling Details
Subsurface explorations for the proposed H Portals at
Haiku Valley were conducted by drilling eight 8 borings ranging in
length from 50 to 140 feet The borings were drilled with Diedrich D 25
or skid mounted drill rigs that were airlifted to the
drill sites by helicopters a Hughes 500 C and a Bell 204
The soil portions colluviun of the borings were sampled by using a
2 inch split spoon Four inch 4 diameter steel casing was used to
clean out the borings after each split spoon sampling interval
Rock coring was carried out using the Longyear HQ3 triple tube
wireline rock coring system for all of the borings The wireline system
was used to obtain rock core samples in highly weathered basalt lava
flows and in most clinker layers The occasionally very high core loss
experienced in some borings may be a result of the friable and silty
nature of the clinker matrix
To facilitate better recovery of the soil rock samples a mixture of
air fresh water foaming agent airquick foamer and a co polymer clear
mud was used for the drilling fluid system to conserve water and to
lessen the erosive action on the sample while coring
Two 2 single level piezometers were installed in boreholes HV 5
and Hv 8 to allow long term monitoring of groundwater levels Graphical
presentations of each piezometer installed including depths and
different types of backfill materials are presented on Piezometer
Details in Appendix A Periodic readings of the piezometric levels were
made and the readings are summarized on Plate C 1 in Appendix C
The two angle holes HV 1 and HV 3 were surveyed with a PAJARI
directional borehole instrument to measure the inclination dip and
azimuth horizontal direction of the borehole at different depths
These readings are summarized on Plates E 1 and E 2 in Appendix E
Materials encountered in the field explorations were described and
classified in the field by an engineering geologist The classifications
were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the
laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the
materials encountered is presented on the Boring Iogs Plates A 1 1
through A 8 5

Weathering
Degree of weathering as defined here is physical disintegration due
to chemical alteration of the minerals in the rock Terms and
abbreviations used to describe weathering are
EW
UW
Extremely Weathered The original minerals of the rock have
been almost entirely altered to secondary minerals even though
the original fabric may be intact saprolite
Highly Weathered The rock is weakened to such an extent that a
2 inch diameter core can be broken readily by hand across the
rock fabric saprolite
Moderately Weathered Rock is discolored and noticeably
weakened but a 2 inch diameter core cannot usually be broken by
hand across the rock fabric
Slightly Weathered Rock is slightly discolored but not
noticeably lower in strength than fresh rock
Unweathered Fresh Rock Rock shows no discoloration loss of
strength or any other effect of weathering

boreholes to allow long term monitoring of groundwater levels The

slotted section Backfill around the slotted section consists of either
silica sand No 12 basalt manufactured sand or coral sand The sand
long slotted piece A bentonite seal was placed on top of the sand layer
and the hole was filled with a cement slurry grout up to the ground
surface
A graphical presentation of the piezometer details which includes
the depths and different types of backfill materials is shown on

taken for all the piezometers and vertical open boreholes to April 14

APPENDIX D
Laboratory Testing
The moisture content unit weight and gradation were determined on
selected samples in general accordance with ASIM standards as an aid in
the classification and evaluation of soil properties Bulk specific
gravity tests were performed on the core pieces in accordance with ASIM
D 1188 This method was deemed more appropriate than ASTM C 97 since the
bulk specific gravity would reflect differences in the vesicularity of
the cores
Fifty nine 59 unconfined compression tests were performed on
selected rock core samples in general accordance with ASIM standard
D 2938 79 The ends of the rock core samples were cut with a diamond saw
parallel to each other and at right angles to the longitudinal axis of
the core The axial lengths of the samples were 2 to 2 5 times its
diameter The load was applied continuously at a strain rate of 0 05
inches minutes until failure occurred The stress at failure and the
failure mode are recorded in the summaries on Plates D 1 through D 17
Sketches of the failures are available on the original laboratory
worksheets
The Unified Soil Classification system has been used to classify the
laboratory tested soil samples The grain size distribution of the
samples vary but the residual materials and saprolite when disaggregated
generally classify as highly plastic silts MH grading to silty sands
SM The results of grain size distribution or sieve tests are highly
dependent on the length of time the samples are tested When the sieves
are vibrated longer more disaggregation takes place and therefore a
greater percent of particles in the clay and silt size result

APPENDIX E
Directional Borehole Survey
A survey to verify the Azimuth horizontal angle and inclination
dip of the two 2 angle holes HV 1 and HV 3 was done using a PAJIRI
directional borehole instrument The readings were taken at selected
depths usually at 20 to 25 foot intervals using the HQ wireline
techniques Non magnetic rods are attached to the inner tube to pass the
instrument through the drill bit beyond the magnetic influence of bit and
Grill rods
The survey data are summarized on Plates E 1 and E 2 The calculated
borehole location based on the inclination and azimuth measurements are
presented on Plate E 3
