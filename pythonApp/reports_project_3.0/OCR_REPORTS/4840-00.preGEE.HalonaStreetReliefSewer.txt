2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present level
Locally the fringing coral algal reefs continued to grow during the high stands of the
sea leaving fossil reefs and coralline sediments which are now exposed above sea level
Interbedded with these coral algal materials are alluvial sediments which were transported
from the nearby eroding Koolau Range As the sea level continued to rise the off shore
fringing reef continued to build and created a lagoon behind it which was later filled with
loose sediments As the lagoon became shallower soft organic sediments were
deposited in a swampy marsh environment
Contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level changes thickly bedded lava
flows from the Honolulu Volcanic Series Makuku vent covered portions of the site
Subsequently as the forces of weathering and erosion continued to erode the adjacent
Koolau land mass to the east alluvial deposits accumulated forming a thin mantle over
most of the site In more recent years the lowlying marsh areas as well as other areas at
the site appear to have been filled with nearby alluvial soils and coralline sediments to the
present elevations

The Cured In Place Pipe CIPP system can be used to eliminate or reduce the
inflow of storm water infiltration of groundwater exfiltration of pollutants surface

irregularities defects joints in the sewer collection systems The CIPP techniques is
normally preferred over other alternatives to rehabilitate the existing pipelines under the
following conditions
e The pipeline has adequate flow capacity during the expected life of the liner
The pipe diameter ranging from 4 to 108 inches and length up to 1 000 to

e The host pipe is not severely damaged broken or deformed There are no

CIPP is a liquid thermosetting resin saturated material that is inserted into the
existing pipeline through the existing manholes by hydrostatic inversion air inversion or
by mechanically pulling with a winch and cable The water inversion process utilizes water
pressure to expand the lining against the walls of the host pipe The air inversion process
employs air pressure to invert the lining With the mechanical winching process the lining
is winched into place and expanded using a calibration hose inverted into the lining under
water pressure After insertion the resin saturated material cures by application of hot
water or steam to form a new pipe of slightly smaller inside diameter but of the same
general shape as the original pipe The liner is designed to fit snugly against the wall of
the host pipe
There are several advantages of the CIPP method over other methods of
trenchless pipeline rehabilitation Grouting is not normally required due to the tight fit of
the CIPP against the existing pipe The CIPP has no joints and has a very smooth interior
surface which typically improves flow capacity despite the slight decrease in diameter
Non circular shapes can be accommodated often without a decrease in flow capacity The
lining is capable of accommodating bends and some existing pipe deformations Entry is
possible via existing manholes or through minor excavations and the total installation can

3 2 Pipe Bursting
The pipebursting method utilizes a bursting tool to break the existing pipe internally
and compacts pipe fragments into the surrounding soils As the bursting tool advances it
sets a new pipe of equal or larger diameter into the original alignment In this process the
pipebursting head bursts through the existing pipe as it is winched with a cable through the
original pipe The bursting tool is typically a standard pneumatic mole with two
hydraulically operated breaker arms in front It is powered with compressed air and
operated remotely Attached to the head is the new pipe which simply replaces the old
pipe in its place The debris from the old pipe remains in the ground around the new pipe
and serves as a stabilizer for the surrounding soils The existing pipe can be replaced with
High Density Polyethylene HDPE SDR21 to SDR11 Pipe and the normal range for
pipebursting is from 3 inches to 36 inches in diameter
The main advantage of this method is that the primary hydraulic capacity is not lost
The capacity either improves with the installation of a large diameter pipe or remains the
same with the installation of a similar size pipe Accurate line and grade tolerance can be
maintained and installation is fast There is minimal excavation and restoration of the
construction site and disruption to the surrounding traffic is reduced compared to the
open trench replacement method
Depending on the type and consistency of the in situ soils there is a risk of
damaging the surrounding utilities and other structures because of the vibrations
emanating from the bursting tool In some instances buried utilities are not disturbed by
the pipebursting operation because the surrounding soils absorb the vibrations of the

pneumatic heads However the existing sewage flow needs to be diverted thus
interrupting sewer service In addition visual inspection of the installation is difficult since
much of the work done is hidden below ground

Alternatively considerations may be given to the use of ground improvement
techniques such as jet grouting to improve the bearing support capacity of the underlying
soft loose alluvial lagoonal deposits Jet grouting is a ground modification method in which
thin high pressure jets of cement grout are discharged sideways into the borehole wall to
simultaneously excavate and then mix with the soil The result is a column of modified soil
with low permeability and improved strength This technique is effective across a wide
range of soil types including granular soils such as sands and gravel and cohesive soils
such as silts and clays The jet grouted soil mass would also serve as a dewatering plug
to reduce the amount of groundwater to remove from the trench excavation during
construction

Based on the information provided we understand that pipe ramming is being

tool a pneumatic system and first pipe section are lowered into the starting pit and placed
on adjustable bearing stands The pipe is then accurately aligned and the tool firmly
secured to the pipe by means of chains and straps to ensure that full impact is transmitted
to the pipe The pipe ram is connected to a conventional air compressor and the section

Ground behavior for soft ground tunneling may be described based on six
categories as summarized on Plate 4 Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground
Tunnels Loose sandy soils can be characterized as firm running or flowing ground
depending on the degree of cementation and on the groundwater conditions Above the
groundwater level these soils will not stand unsupported but will tend to ravel or run until a
stable slope is formed that buttresses the tunnel face If there are sufficient fines present
to give the soil some apparent cohesion these soils may be able to stand unsupported in
the heading for short periods of time Below the groundwater level these soils may
behave as flowing ground unless the cohesion or cementation of the soil is high enough to
resist the seepage forces Both running or flowing ground behavior indicates potentially
unstable ground conditions with low stand up time Squeezing ground conditions may
occur where the soil is observed to advance slowly into the tunnel excavation without any
signs of fracturing Squeezing occurs without an increase in the water content or a volume
change in the soil and is governed by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden
pressure Squeezing ground may include soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on
the overburden pressure at the tunnel depth

Microtunneling is a trenchless process for installing underground pipelines ducts
culverts and utilities The technique involves the use of powerful hydraulic jacks that push
or jack successive pipe sections through the ground behind a remotely controlled
excavating unit or cutting disk head at the same time as the excavation is taking place
within the excavating unit The following briefly describe the advantages and methods of
construction for microtunneling
In general use of microtunneling techniques may offer the following major
advantages or benefits as compared to the conventional open trench method of
construction
1 Reduced disturbance and potential damage to adjacent utilities existing
roads and surrounding structures
2 Improved public and job site safety
3 Reduced surface disruption to vehicular and pedestrian traffic
4 Reduced disturbance to local residents and businesses
5 Reduced air and noise pollution and vibration
6 Greater flexibility in alignment selection and reduced need for utility
relocations
7 Reduced need for extensive shoring system
8 Reduced need for significant dewatering
9 Smaller volumes of spoils that require transportation and disposal
10 Reduced potential for treatment of contaminated soil and groundwater
In general the tunneling methods selected would depend on the stability of the
ground Where unstable ground conditions are likely to be encountered the face of the
tunnel must be made stable to prevent ground loss and to enable mining to take place
safely This may be achieved by the following methods
1 Dewatering by well points or deep wells
2 Grouting by the use of cement cement bentonite or chemicals

3 Ground freezing
4 Using specialized tunneling methods such as the following
a compressed air
b full face earth pressure balancing machines
Cc slurry soft ground tunnel boring machines
The choice of process is a function of the nature of the ground the water content
and in particular the particle size analysis of the subsoils encountered In order to install a
pipeline using this technique jacking and receiving pits will need to be constructed A
concrete thrust block constructed behind the jacking frame within the jacking pit provides
the reaction to the hydraulic jacks In weak poor ground conditions piling or other
arrangements ground improvement may need to be employed to increase the reaction
capability of the thrust block The hydraulic jacks pressing against the back of the
pipeline advance the disk cutting head into the soil formation To ensure that the jacking
forces are distributed around the circumference of a pipe being jacked a thrust ring is
normally provided with the design depending on the number of jacks being used The
jacks are inter connected hydraulically to ensure that the thrust from each jack is the
same The number of jacks used may vary depending on the pipe size the strength of the
jacking pipes the length to be installed and the anticipated frictional resistance
The disk cutting head of the tunnel boring machine is controlled via cables from a
cabin on the surface where the operator monitors speed pressure and tunneling
accuracy At the face of the tunnel a rotating cutter head digs into the soil and rock and
mixes it with groundwater and water pumped from the surface Rocky material is reduced
to a maximum inch size in a crushing chamber located directly behind the cutter disk
The excavated material is pumped out of the tunnel as slurry The water introduced into
the machine is matched in pressure and volume to the material being displaced This
prevents over excavation of the tunnel face thereby reducing the potential for cave in of
the soil surrounding the new casing
The cutting disk speed and direction of rotation the hydraulic jacking system as
well as the slurry system operations can be controlled from one control panel located
remotely at the launch pit The hydraulic steering cylinders located on the disk cutting

CATEGORIES OF GROUND CONDITIONS FOR SOFT GROUND TUNNELS
After Terzaghi 1950
Firm Ground A heading may be advanced several feet or more without immediate
support Hard clays and cemented sand or gravel generally fall into this category
Raveling Ground After excavation material above the tunnel or in the upper part of the
working face tends to flake off and fall into the heading Slightly cohesive sands silts and
fine sands gaining their strength from apparent cohesion typically exhibit this type of
behavior Very stiff fissured clays may be raveling materials also
Running Ground Cohesionless dry soils run from any unsupported vertical face until a
stable slope forms at the natural angle of repose i e approximately 3 degrees to
35 degrees Running ground consists of dry cohesionless materials such as clean loose
sand or gravel
Flowing Ground If seepage develops at the working face raveling or running ground is
transformed to flowing ground which advances like a viscous fluid into the heading Silt
sand or gravel below the water table without a high enough clay content to develop
significant cohesion will be flowing type soils
Swelling Ground A condition where the ground absorbs water increases in volume and
expands slowly into the tunnel This may occur in highly over consolidated clays that
exhibit high volume change characteristics upon wetting
Squeezing Ground Squeezing ground conditions are analogous to plastic flow and the
soil is observed to advance slowly into the tunnel excavation without any signs of
fracturing Squeezing occurs without an increase in the water content or a volume change
in the soil and is governed by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden pressure
Squeezing ground may include soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on the
overburden pressure at the tunnel depth

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two hydrometer tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plate B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the shear strength parameters of the soils encountered in the borings The test
results are presented on Plate B 3
One consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on a sample
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
