SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Paumalu Teleport project in Haleiwa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and recommendations resulting from our
field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These recommendations
are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade and site grading only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project consists of the construction of two new 16 meter antennas adjacent
to the existing control building at the comsat facility at end of Comsat Access Road near
Sunset Beach in Haleiwa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project also involves the
construction of an equipment shelter approximately 17 by 30 feet in plan dimension and
a new concrete utility box
Based on the information provided we envision that a shallow foundation system
may be used to support the new antennas Details of the structural loads were not
available at the time this report was prepared

geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of foundations
slabs on grade and site grading The work was performed in general accordance with
our fee proposal dated March 8 2007 Our scope of work included the following tasks
and work efforts

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is largely composed of the weathered remnants of the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that
Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below the Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general
saprolite saprolitic soils is mainly composed of silty and clayey materials and is typical
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with increased depth
The project site is in a high elevation along the coastal areas of northern Oahu
above Sunset Beach Based on our review of available geological map information we
believe that the soil conditions at the project site consist of fill materials underlain by
saprolite and weathered basalt formation
2 2 Site Description
The project site is situated in a high elevation above Sunset Beach on the north
shore of the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is within the existing
Paumalu Teleport communication facility adjacent to a private ranch The Paumalu
Teleport facility is accessed through a private roadway off Kamehameha Highway The
location of the proposed 16 meter antennas is a gently sloping terrace and lawn grass
area adjacent to the control building A flag pole exists in the middle of the site and a
concrete walkway lies between the building and pole

Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings extending
to depths of approximately 10 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site is underlain by about 5 to 9 feet of very stiff to hard fill

overlying extremely weathered basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth
drilled of about 40 feet below the existing ground surface
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may occur or

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of fill material overlying stiff
saprolite and weathered basalt formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of
approximately 40 feet below the existing ground surface We did not encounter
groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on the relatively competent subsurface soil conditions encountered at the
project site and the anticipated mat foundation to be used for support of the new
antennas we recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds
per square foot psf to design the foundations bearing on the compacted on site clayey
soil
We also recommend using slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings
to support the new equipment shelter building To reduce the potential for appreciable slab
cracking resulting from the expansive near surface clayey soil we recommend reinforcing
the slab on grade with No 3 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center in both
directions To further reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slab on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the surface clayey soils the
subgrade of the slab on grade should be properly prepared and capped with 12 inches of
structural fill
Detailed discussion and recommendations for the design of foundations
slabs on grade site grading and other geotechnical aspects of the project are
presented in the following sections herein

We recommend embedding the footings 24 inches below the lowest adjacent
exterior grade The bottom of foundations should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below an
imaginary 45 degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
This assumes that the soils around the foundations are well compacted minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
A Geolabs representative should monitor the foundation excavations prior to
placing the reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions
and the required embedment depths
3 2 Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade foundations with thicken edge footings
will be used for the new equipment shelter building constructed at or near the existing
grades Based on the laboratory test results the clayey soils encountered at the project
site exhibit a moderate to high potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to
moisture fluctuations
In general we believe that the slab on grade foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations contained in the Foundations section herein

Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The thickened edge footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and be
embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade We
recommend using reinforced slabs on grade for the new equipment shelter to reduce
the potential for appreciable slab cracking resulting from expansion of the near surface
clayey soils The reinforced slabs on grade should be a minimum of 5 inches thick and
reinforced with No 3 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center in both directions
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the surface clayey soils the subgrade should be
prepared properly and capped with a 12 inch thick structural fill consisting of
well graded granular materials To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration
and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer
Prior to placing the cushion granular fill we recommend scarifying the clayey
subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioning the soils to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacting to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
We emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly
against the slab edges with low expansion relatively impervious soils These areas
should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the potential
for water ponding around the slabs and foundations

3 5 Underground Utilities
We envision that new utility lines and connections may be needed In general we
recommend placing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the pipes Free draining granular materials such
as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe
damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site clayey materials or
imported granular materials To reduce the potential for future ground subsidence the
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about
8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative
compaction Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent
relative compaction
3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new antenna pads and equipment shelter
building should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to reduce the
potential for ponding It is also advised to install gutter systems around the building
structures and to divert the discharge away from the foundation and pavement areas
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils

These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings extending to depths of approximately 10 to 40 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We drilled
the borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
laboratory testing Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples to aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test results
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The Direct Shear test
Four one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on samples of the relative
undisturbed soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test
results are presented on Plate B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the bearing characteristics of the soils
and the quality of the materials for use as embankment fills The test results are
presented on Plate B 4
