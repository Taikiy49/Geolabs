SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered residual and saprolitic soils extending 
to the maximum explored depth of about 36.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  A 
thin layer of surface fill about 1.5 feet thick consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey silt was 
encountered in three of the drilled borings.  The residual soils generally consisted of stiff 
to hard silty clay and clayey silt. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings 
at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are 
subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, 
and other factors. 
Based on our field exploration, we envision that installation of the proposed 
waterline will involve excavations into the stiff to hard clayey residual and saprolitic soils. 
It is anticipated that the stiff to hard clayey soils may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment, such as a large backhoe excavator, near the ground surface. 
However, it should be noted that cobbles and boulders are frequently encountered in the 
residual and saprolitic soils underlying the Koa Ridge Development. Therefore, 
excavations that encounter cobbles and boulders may require the use of hoerams and/or 
chipping. 
In general, the subsurface soils encountered during our field exploration may be 
used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade, provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, organic materials, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension.  
Based on the laboratory testing, the corrosion potential of the subsurface soils at 
the project site meets the Corrosion Category A criteria (moderately to severely corrosive) 
based on the updated version of the Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu 
Water System External Corrosion Control Standards. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the installation of the new waterlines.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Waiahole Ditch Undergrounding project at the Koa Ridge Development 
in Waipio on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of site 
grading, trench excavation, backfilling, underground utility structures, thrust block/vault 
design, and corrosion potential only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Koa Ridge development site is located between Mililani Town and Waipio 
Gentry in the Waipio area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. It is approximately bordered by 
Ka Uka Boulevard on the south, Interstate Route H-2 to the east and north, and Kipapa 
Gulch on the west. 
As part of the development, undergrounding of the Waiahole Ditch at the northern 
end of the development is planned. We understand that the water from the Waiahole 
Ditch will be placed underground in 48-inch diameter reinforced concrete pipes (RCP) for 
a distance of about 1,256 linear feet. In addition, five manhole structures up to about 
30 feet below the existing ground surface are planned.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that site grading work for the 
project will primarily consist of excavating and backfilling trenches about 14 to 32 feet in 
depth to install the proposed 48-inch diameter reinforced concrete pipe (RCP) waterline.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below the Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas 
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The project site is located within the Schofield Plateau. The Schofield Plateau was 
formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes 
of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips of the lava beds are near 
horizontal (between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal). The lava flows on the plateau have 
undergone in-situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the 
reddish color of the soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade 
to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material, while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical 
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to a 
basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Spheroidally weathered boulders within the 
residual and saprolitic soils are typical in the Schofield Plateau.

Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to 
expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be reused as fill materials 
and should be properly disposed of off-site or in landscaped areas, if appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and 
poor drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and replacing these 
areas with engineered fill. A Geolabs field representative should evaluate the need 
for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material, 
provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported fill materials should consist 
of crushed basalt or coral.  
Non-expansive, select granular fill material should be well-graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883.

General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture

 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it is anticipated 
that cut slopes would expose soils consisting of very stiff to hard silty/clayey 
residual soil. In general, we believe that cut slopes into the soil material may be 
cut at an inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Slope 
benches are recommended at maximum 20-foot vertical height intervals. Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V (such as at the gullies) should be keyed and 
benched into the existing slope to provide stability for the new fill against sliding. 
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations.   
Fill slopes more than 5 feet in height should be constructed by overfilling and 
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. 
Track-rolling of slopes more than 5 feet in height should not be accepted. If over-
cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching requirements should be

3.2 
Trench Excavation 
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. Based on the 
information provided, we anticipate that the inverts of the 48-inch diameter RCP waterline 
will be about 14 to 32 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our field 
exploration, we envision that installation of the proposed waterline will involve excavations 
into the very stiff to hard clayey residual and saprolitic soils. It is anticipated that the very 
stiff to hard clayey soils may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such 
as a large backhoe excavator, near the ground surface. However, it should be noted that 
cobbles and boulders are frequently encountered in the residual and saprolitic soils 
underlying the Koa Ridge Development. Therefore, excavations that encounter cobbles 
and boulders may require the use of hoerams and/or chipping.  
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our experience in the project vicinity and the field and laboratory data from the 
borings drilled at the project site. Contractors should be encouraged to examine the site 
conditions and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent 
interpretation. 
The excavated soils should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance 
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the excavation 
in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement. In addition, the 
excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground utilities to 
reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the 
underground utility lines.

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable

It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude 
of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how 
quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
It is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed properly 
and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously monitored for 
cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with instruments until the waterline 
excavation is finally backfilled. It should be noted that during and after the installation of 
the shoring system, minor settlements may be possible. Therefore, it is recommended 
that the contractor retains a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the 
shoring system used.

3.4 
48-Inch Diameter RCP Waterline 
We understand that a 48-inch diameter RCP waterline will be used for the 
Waiahole Ditch Undergrounding project. In general, we recommend providing granular 
bedding consisting of 12 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) 
under the pipes for uniform support. Free-draining granular materials, such as 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial 
trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around 
the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for the 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site soils or 
select granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose 
lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce 
the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. The upper 3 feet below the 
finished grade in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be compacted to a minimum 
of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density 
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined 
in accordance with ASTM D1557.

 Underground Utility Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing pressure 
of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for design of the 
underground utility structures bearing on the very stiff to hard residual and saprolitic 
soils at the project site. To provide uniform bearing support for the new structures, 
we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick layer of No. 3 Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the bottom of the manhole structures. We 
anticipate the manholes will be pre-cast concrete structures.  
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface 
prior to the placement of the No. 3 Fine gravel. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of the excavations should be over-excavated to expose 
the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled with select 
granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Based on our engineering analyses, we believe that the net increase in bearing 
pressure on the underlying stiff to hard residual soils and compacted general fills 
resulting from the underground utility structure construction would be minimal. 
Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are anticipated for the drainage 
structures.

 Backfilling Underground Utility Structures 
The underground utility structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled to 
reduce the potential for subsidence at the ground surface. The excavated on-site 
soils or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and 
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension may 
be used as backfill up to the finished subgrades.  
Underground utility structure backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. Where structures are located below future pavement areas, the upper

3.6 
Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the 
pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block.  
Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that most of the trench excavations 
will likely encounter residual soils consisting of stiff to hard silty clay. Therefore, we 
recommend using lateral bearing pressures of up to 1,500 psf for the design of thrust 
blocks bearing against the stiff residual soils underlying the site. 
3.7 
Utility Line Settlement 
Primary settlement of the waterlines is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause the settlement of 
the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, the 
settlement on the order of less than 1/2 inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
3.8 
Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, and 
sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our field exploration 
to evaluate the corrosivity of the in-situ soils near the planned invert elevations of the new 
RCP waterline. The test results are summarized and presented in Appendix B. Detailed

results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) performed 
by Eurofins TestAmerica are presented in Appendix C. 
Based on the updated version of the Board of Water Supply, City and County of 
Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021, there 
are two soil corrosivity categories. Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) applies 
to soil meeting any one of the following conditions. Corrosion Category B (Negligibly to 
Mildly Corrosive) is applicable when none of the following conditions are met. The 
updated version of the standards is effective as of the publishing date for Oahu only. 
• Resistivity ≤ 5,000 ohm-cm 
• pH ≤ 6.5 
• Water-soluble chloride concentration ≥ 500 ppm 
• Water-soluble sulfate concentration ≥ 1,000 ppm 
• Within fluctuating saltwater table 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Results of the laboratory testing indicate that the on-site soils have 
resistivity values of about 4,200 to 6,600 ohm-cm with pH values of about 4.87 to 5.44. 
Therefore, the on-site soils near the planned invert elevations of the new waterline may 
be considered as Corrosion Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) based on the 
updated Board of Water Supply External Corrosion Control Standards dated 
July 28, 2021. 
In addition, chloride content and sulfate content tests were performed by Eurofins 
TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. 
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the test 
values are generally relatively low. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional 
corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for 
corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling seven borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 16.5 to 36.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers. In addition, two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System) as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Eight Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Nine Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected 
in-situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey 
soils. The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Individual stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented on 
Plates B-2 through B-10. 
Four Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of 
the on-site clayey soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were 
used as the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-11 through B-14. 
Two sets of corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57) performed by our office and Chloride (EPA 300) and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300) tests performed by Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc., were performed 
on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-15. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-16 and B-17.
