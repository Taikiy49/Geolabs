INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Makiki District Park Site Improvements project in the Makiki
area in the District of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our ffindings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations for the
proposed light poles and retaining wall and design of basketball court and walkway
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of Koolau on the eastern end
of the Punchbowl Crater Late stage volcanic eruptions known as the Honolulu Volcanic
Series form landmarks on Oahu such as Diamond Head Punchbowl Tantalus and Salt
Lake craters The subsurface conditions in the Makiki area are dominated by residual
soils near the surface followed by cinder sand and volcanic tuff cemented volcanic ash
deposits from these late stage volcanic eruptions The residual soils weathered from
volcanic ash and cinders from these late stage volcanic eruptions

Light Pole Construction Considerations
The performance of shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s method
of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend that a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the installation of
excavated and drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may
have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the shaft foundations
The load carrying capacity of shafts depends to a large extent on the contact
between the shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling or
excavating the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all
shafts should be cleaned of any loose or disturbed material prior to constructing
the foundation The bottom should also be fairly level The concrete should be
poured neatly against the excavation side walls Backfill against the shaft
foundations should not be allowed
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended
Groundwater conditions are not anticipated therefore placement of concrete by
dry construction methods may be used A low shrink concrete mix with high slump
6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide close contact between the
shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix

Retaining Walls
An allowable bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized for the design of retaining wall foundations bearing on the on site soils or properly
compacted fill material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent
grade and deep enough to provide a horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured
from the outside edge of footings to the slope face Footings should have a minimum
width of 18 inches and footings parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed
in stepped footings
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
it should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
should be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction or concrete In lieu of filling the over excavation the
footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements it should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf Resistance due to friction may be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3948 00 Page 14
estimated using a coefficient of friction of 0 4 These values assume that the concrete for
footings is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should
be neglected
Free standing retaining walls with level backfill should be designed to resist an
equivalent fluid earth pressure of 40 pcf For sloped backfill conditions the equivalent
fluid earth pressure should be increased to 50 pcf Free standing walls are defined as
walls that are allowed to rotate about 0 5 percent of the wall height If the wall is not a
free standing type design equivalent fluid earth pressure should be increased by 20 pcf
These values do not account for surcharge or hydrostatic loads Surcharge stresses due
to areal surcharges live loads and point loads should be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 40 percent of the vertical surcharge
pressure acting on the entire height of the wall which is free to deflect cantilever may
be used for design For walls that are restrained a rectangular distribution equal to
55 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may
be used for design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the
surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
Backfill behind retaining walls should consist of the on site soils or select granular
fill material compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around
the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to an
appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3948 00 Page 15
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall Unless covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill
should consist of on site sandy silt or clayey silt material to reduce water infiltration behind
the walls

In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfill material such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining
granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of
on site soils as backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
Soft or loose soils encountered at the bottom of trenches should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material or to a maximum 12 inches and backfilled
with select granular fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade below the existing pavement section or sidewalk section should consist of
the on site soils or fill
The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the road pavement subgrade should
be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The road pavement
section material should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project
site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill
material
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of
backfilling observations and soil density tests should be performed by Geolabs Hawaii
during excavation and backfilling operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the

adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the proposed basketball court and retaining wall
should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the
potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should be avoided Planters next to foundations and slabs should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation and slab recommendations since ponded water could cause
subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slabs immediately after
setting the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away
from slabs and foundations
