SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills over beach deposits and 
lagoonal deposits followed by weathered limestone formation at the Dehumidified 
Supply Storage Warehouse location. The surface fills generally consisted of medium 
dense to dense sandy gravel and gravelly sands extending down to about 1.8 meters 
below the surface. Beach deposits consisting of medium dense to dense sands with 
occasional loose zones were encountered directly below the surface fills. Lagoonal 
deposits consisting of loose silty sands and gravel with occasional cemented zones 
were encountered below the beach deposits. The lagoonal deposits generally were 
underlain by weathered limestone formation extending down to the maximum depth 
explored of about 28.5 meters below the ground surface. The weathered limestone 
formation encountered was generally soft to medium hard. The consistency and quality 
of the weathered limestone formation encountered varied significantly with depth. 
Groundwater was encountered in our borings at about 2.3 to 2.6 meters below the 
ground surface at the time of our field exploration corresponding to about Elevation +0.6 
to +0.9 meters. 
Generally, we recommend using 406-mm square precast prestressed concrete 
piles with an allowable compressive load capacity of 670 kN (150 kips) per pile to 
support the proposed warehouse structure. To develop the recommended pile capacity 
of 670 kN, a pile embedment of approximately 26 meters (85 feet) below the existing 
ground surface may be required. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we 
anticipate some minor predrilling of the surface materials may be needed to assist in 
setting the piles. Therefore, we recommend predrilling the pile locations to a depth not 
deeper than 6 meters to facilitate setting of the piles only. 
In general, we recommend the test pile program consist of conducting a dynamic 
load test using a Pile Driving Analyzer (PDA) on at least 16 test piles to monitor the 
hammer and drive system performance, assess pile installation stresses and integrity, 
as well as to evaluate pile capacity. The test piles may be incorporated into the actual 
foundation system upon evaluation of the dynamic load test results. Test piles should be 
cast at least 1.5 meters longer than the estimated pile length to account for potential 
variation in the subsurface conditions. After initial driving of the test piles to the 
approximate tip elevation, re-striking of the test piles should be conducted no earlier 
than 72 hours after the initial driving. The text of this report should be referred to for 
detailed discussion and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Dehumidified Supply Storage Warehouse facility project 
(FY14 MCON Project P-637) at Polaris Point within the Apra Harbor Naval Base in 
Guam. The project location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, floor slabs, lift station, earthwork, pavements, and storm water 
management systems only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed design-build project is located at Polaris Point within the Naval Base 
in Apra Harbor, Guam. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. The project consists of construction of a Dehumidified Supply 
Storage Warehouse facility. The project site is adjacent to the Submarine Learning 
Center/Submarine Squadron 15 Headquarter (Project P-465). 
Based on the project documents, the new Dehumidified Supply Storage 
Warehouse will consist of a one-story warehouse building measuring approximately 
3,716 square meters in area. The Dehumidified Supply Storage Warehouse will have a 
finished floor elevation of about +3.4 meters. Based on the relatively level ground 
surface conditions at the site, we envision only minor site grading consisting of cuts and 
fills of less than about 0.5 meters will be required to achieve the design finished grades. 
In addition, there will be a paved driveway around the warehouse building and a 
paved parking lot to service the warehouse facility. Underground utilities, such as 
sewer, drainage, water, and electrical lines, will be provided to service the facility. In 
addition, two infiltration basins will be constructed for storm water management systems 
as required by the Request for Proposal (RFP) documents.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island 
archipelago, which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming 
the Mariana Trench. The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches extending from 
Japan to the Palau Islands including the Japan Trench, Mariana Trench, Yap Trench, 
and Palau Trench. 
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces: 
the southern mountains, the central mountains, and the northern plateau. The island 
formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas. 
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic 
rocks and conglomerates, tuffs, and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene Epochs. 
Lava flows and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority 
of the southern one-third of the island. The plateaus of the northern portion of the island 
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level. During the 
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs, a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an 
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits. Tectonic activity has lifted these 
coral-algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau. 
The central portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic 
materials of the southern mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau. 
The project site at Apra Harbor is located in the south-central portion of the island. The 
local geology is dominated by the volcanic materials and associated materials of the 
southern mountains. Due to surface drainage courses of the southern mountain terrain, 
the near surface soils in the area consist of Holocene age alluvium and associated 
deposits, such as coralline detritus, alluvium, and lagoonal deposits. These younger 
unconsolidated soil deposits generally are more susceptible to liquefaction and slope 
stability issues, especially around the shoreline areas.

2.3.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Liquefaction is a phenomenon during which loose, saturated, cohesionless soils 
(such as sands and non-plastic silts) temporarily lose their shear strengths due to 
the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress 
applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated 
sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil 
tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up 
of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is 
reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could 
equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to 
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil 
acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and 
if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Significant factors known to affect the liquefaction potential of soils are the 
characteristics of the materials such as grain size distribution and relative 
density; the plasticity of the fine-grained soils, the degree of saturation; the initial 
stresses (overburden pressures) acting on the soils; and the characteristics of 
the earthquakes such as the intensity and duration of ground shaking. The major 
factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of soil deposits are as follows: 
1. 
Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more 
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands. 
2. 
Initial Relative Density - Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to 
liquefaction. Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative 
density. 
3. 
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration - Liquefaction potential is directly 
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake.

Because the project site will be subjected to seismic activity, we have evaluated the 
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site based on a peak ground acceleration 
of 0.9g (corresponding to the Maximum Considered Earthquake with a return 
period of 2,475 years). The subsurface conditions at the site generally consist of 
surface fills over beach deposits, lagoonal deposits, and weathered limestone 
formation. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the lagoonal deposits 
encountered at the project site (between depths of about 3 meters and 15 meters 
except for some more resistant zones with some cementation of the subsurface 
materials) generally are susceptible to liquefaction when subjected to a peak 
ground acceleration of 0.9g. In the event of soil liquefaction caused by a peak 
ground acceleration of 0.9g, the estimated seismically induced ground settlement 
due to soil liquefaction ranged from about 150 to 350 mm with an average of about 
300 mm.  
The other soil deposits (surface fills, beach deposits, and weathered limestone 
formation) generally are not susceptible to soil liquefaction. The weathered 
limestone encountered generally consists of soft to medium hard formation with 
pockets of sandy and silty soils. The sandy and silty soils encountered in the 
limestone formation generally occur in small pockets and generally do not appear 
as continuously layered materials. Therefore, we believe the limestone formation 
with pockets of the sandy and silty soils also are not susceptible to liquefaction.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills over beach deposits and 
lagoonal deposits followed by weathered limestone formation at the Dehumidified 
Supply Storage Warehouse location. The surface fills generally consisted of medium 
dense to dense sandy gravel and gravelly sands extending down to about 1.8 meters 
below the surface. Beach deposits consisting of medium dense to dense sands with 
occasional loose zones were encountered directly below the surface fills. Lagoonal 
deposits consisting of loose silty sands and gravel with occasional cemented zones 
were encountered below the beach deposits. The loose lagoonal deposits are 
susceptible to soil liquefaction when subjected to a peak ground acceleration of 0.9g 
corresponding to the Maximum Considered Earthquake (2,475-Year return period). 
The lagoonal deposits generally were underlain by weathered limestone 
formation beginning at a depth of about 13.4 meters extending down to the maximum 
depth explored of about 28.5 meters below the ground surface. The weathered 
limestone formation encountered was generally soft to medium hard. The consistency 
and quality of the weathered limestone formation encountered varied with depth. 
Groundwater was encountered in our boring at about 2.3 to 2.6 meters below the 
ground surface at the time of our field exploration corresponding to about Elevation +0.6 
to +0.9 meters. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings and the 
presence of potentially liquefiable lagoonal deposits, we recommend using 406-mm 
square precast prestressed concrete piles with an allowable compressive load capacity 
of 670 kN per pile to support the proposed warehouse structure. To develop the 
recommended pile capacity of 670 kN, a pile embedment of approximately 26 meters 
below the existing ground surface may be required. In addition, we anticipate subjecting 
each of the piles to a lateral load of approximately 135 kN with a corresponding moment 
demand of about 244 kN-meters due to seismic loading. Therefore, the piles will need to 
be structurally reinforced to provide the proper bending strength to withstand the lateral 
load and moment demands acting on the foundation elements.

Pile Foundations 
The Dehumidified Supply Storage Warehouse will consist of a one-story 
warehouse structure with a finished floor elevation of about +3.4 meters. Based on the 
moderately heavy column loads and the presence of potentially liquefiable, loose 
lagoonal deposits, we recommend using driven pile foundations deriving support from a 
combination of skin friction and/or end-bearing in the weathered limestone formation to 
support the warehouse structure. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, piles 
extending through the upper fill soils, beach deposits, and potentially liquefiable 
lagoonal deposits and embedded in the weathered limestone formation to a depth of 
approximately 26 meters below the ground surface will be required to develop the 
recommended capacity to support the warehouse structure. 
3.1.1 Vertical Pile Capacity 
Generally, we recommend using 406-mm square precast prestressed concrete 
piles with an allowable compressive load capacity of 670 kN per pile to support 
the warehouse building structure. The allowable compressive load capacity is for 
supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
The piles would derive load supporting capacity from friction and/or end-bearing 
in the weathered limestone formation. To develop the recommended pile 
capacity of 670 kN, a pile embedment of approximately 26 meters below the 
existing ground surface may be required. The recommended embedment depth 
correlates to an estimated pile tip of about Elevation -23 meters. A minimum pile 
spacing of 2.5 times the pile width center-to-center is recommended to avoid

load capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads. The 
capacity of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project 
structural engineer. 
As indicated previously, the loose lagoonal deposits at the project site are 
susceptible to liquefaction during a seismic event at the project site. Due to the 
potential for soil liquefaction of the loose lagoonal deposits at the project site, 
settlement of the loose lagoonal deposits will result in downdrag loading of the deep 
foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable materials. Therefore, the 
allowable compressive load capacity of the driven piles has been reduced by the 
expected downdrag load in the pile foundation analyses. Based on our foundation 
analyses, we estimate the downdrag load imposed on the vertical driven piles due 
to the potential for liquefaction of the lagoonal deposits may be on the order of 
about 265 kN per pile. We believe the downdrag load imposed on the pile 
foundations due to soil liquefaction may be considered a transient load.

3.1.6 Pile Driving Criteria

termination criteria when the pile tip is within the weathered limestone formation.

actual hammer used for the project. The acceptability of piles reaching refusal 
above the design tip elevations will need to be assessed on an individual basis 
during the pile driving.

interrupted for more than four hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required 
depth and/or meeting the pile driving criteria. During soft driving, i.e., when the pile 
tip is within loose and/or soft soils, the hammer stroke (or energy) should be 
carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop within the 
pile.

In general, the excavated on-site granular soils may be re-used as a source of fill

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 104, extending to depths of about 
20.9 to 28.5 meters below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 200-mm hollow stem augers and rotary-wash 
methods. In addition, two shallow borings (designated as P-101 and P-102) were drilled to 
evaluate and determine the infiltration characteristics of the near-surface materials for 
design of storm water management systems. The borings were drilled at the approximate 
locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 thru A-6. 
The majority of the soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 50-mm (2-inch) OD standard penetration sampler using a 63.5-kg (140-pound) 
hammer falling 460 mm (30 inches). In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples 
were obtained in general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of 
Soils, by driving a 75-mm (3-inch) OD modified California sampler with the same hammer 
and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 152 mm 
(6 inches) of a 460-mm (18-inch) drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on 
the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type 
used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test 
(SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiency of the safety hammer used for this project was 
measured recently. Hammer calibration tests were performed on the Mobile B-61 
automatic trip safety hammer in March 2014 for another nearby project. The energy data 
was analyzed, and the results indicate an energy transfer ratio of 88% (average of 87.1% 
and 89.2%) may be used for the automatic trip safety hammer assembly.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Twenty-one Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. The fines content of the sample tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) is indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B-2 through B-6. 
Five fines content tests (ASTM D1140) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the percent of particles passing the No. 200 sieve and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the sample tested is indicated on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths and summarized on Plate B-7. We also included the 
fines content from the twenty-one sieve analysis tests on Plate B-7 for ease of 
reference.  
One laboratory compaction test was performed on a bulk sample obtained to 
evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry density of the 
near-surface soils. The Modified Proctor test was performed in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. The test results are presented on Plate B-8. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils collected to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-9.
