SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration suggests that the project site is generally underlain by 
surface fills, coralline detritus, and very loose to loose lagoonal deposits overlying coral 
formation, extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 52 feet below the 
ground surface. Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 7.8 feet below the 
ground surface, generally corresponding to about Elevation +2.2 feet MSL. It should be 
noted that water levels may vary with tidal fluctuations, storm surge conditions, 
seasonal rainfall, time of year, and other factors. 
Based on the loose granular deposits encountered, we believe that the project 
site may have a factor of safety of less than one against liquefaction. Therefore, we 
believe the project site could be subjected to potential ground settlements (on the order 
of about 2 to 5 inches) in the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+). 
However, we believe lateral spreading associated with liquefaction would not be a 
significant design consideration based on the relatively flat topography across the site 
and the considerable distance to the potential seismic source. 
Provided that the estimated liquefaction settlements are acceptable and based 
on the relatively light weight of the planned pre-engineering metal building system, we 
believe that shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new 
mezzanine structure may be used.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf 
may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted on-site 
soils. The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 
percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior 
to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or concrete. If the estimated liquefaction 
settlements are not acceptable for the project, we recommend utilizing a deep 
foundation system bearing into the underlying coral formation. 
The design of metallic substructures, such as metallic piping, should consider the 
effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Based on the results of the 
laboratory corrosivity tests, the subsurface soils at the project site exhibit a minimum 
resistivity value of about 1,600 ohm-cm, indicating that the subsurface soils are 
moderately to severely corrosive (Corrosion Category A) to buried metallic structures. 
Therefore, we recommend properly designing metallic substructures (such as piping) for 
protection against the potential for corrosion.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity at the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from 
the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming the Schofield 
Plateau. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene Epoch and early 
Pleistocene Epoch by the extrusion of successive thin bedded lava flows. The main 
shield-building stage ceased approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from historic 
drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet 
since the cessation of the early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). During 
the period of island subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow along the southern 
coast of Oahu, forming embayment protected by barrier reefs. A series of lagoons 
formed behind the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments 
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the oceans fell because there was less 
water available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded (melted), 
global sea levels rose because the volume of water increased. The land mass 
comprising the Island of Oahu remained essentially stable during these water level 
changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations 
resulted in the stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present 
sea level on the Island of Oahu.  
The higher sea level stands caused landform changes, including the 
accumulation of deltas and alluvial fans composed of terrigenous sediments in the 
heads of the old bays, the growth of reef deposits at correspondingly higher elevations,

overlying the marine-lagoonal sediments.  
The FedEx facility is along the Southern Oahu Coastal Plain. In general, the 
subsurface materials underlying the site consist of man-made fills placed over lagoonal 
and alluvial sediments underlain by coral reef and coral-algal reef limestone at greater 
depths. Calcareous marine sediments containing coralline detritus materials are also 
embedded discontinuously with alluvial deposits.

2.4 
Seismic Piezocone Penetration Testing 
Seismic piezocone penetration testing (SCPTu) was performed at three locations 
(CPT-1 through CPT-3) using seismic piezocone testing equipment, also referred to as 
Seismic CPT (SCPTu). The test procedures are discussed in more detail in Appendix B. 
Generally, conducting SCPTu involves the use of a steel cone connected to a friction 
sleeve that is hydraulically pushed into the soil. The tip resistance, sleeve friction, and 
piezometric pore pressures are measured, providing information that can be correlated 
to the soil type and behavior in a virtually continuous profile with depth. SCPTu can be 
performed only to the depths to which the cone tip can be hydraulically pushed with our 
truck-mounted drill rig set up. 
Three seismic piezocone penetration tests were performed at selected locations, 
designated as CPT-1 through CPT-3. At the locations tested, the SCPTu were 
terminated at depths ranging from about 23 to 25 feet below the existing grade due to 
refusal conditions. Due to early refusal conditions near the existing ground surface, the 
upper portion of each CPT location was predrilled to extend the CPT to deeper depths. 
The seismic cone tip contains a geophone sensor. At each depth interval 
(approximately 1 to 2 meters or 3.3 to 6.6 feet), the advancement of the cone tip was 
halted, a seismic pulse was generated at the ground surface by striking a loaded plate 
with a switched hammer, and the resulting seismic shear wave was detected at the 
geophone. The distance from the source and the wave arrival time allows for the 
determination of the average shear wave velocity of the soil material over a depth 
interval. The seismic piezocone penetration test records are presented in Appendix B.

2.6 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2018 Edition (IBC 2018), the project 
site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to 
be addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and soil 
profile type for seismic design. 
2.6.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are 
so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some 
of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few causes minor to 
moderate damage.

In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa 
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in 
Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic 
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from 
the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous 
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not 
confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the 
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An 
earthquake of Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 
1948 on the Island of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store 
windows, ruptured building walls, and broken underground water mains.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration suggests that the project site is generally underlain by 
surface fill, coralline detritus, and lagoonal deposits overlying coral formation, extending 
to the maximum depth explored of approximately 52 feet below the ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 7.8 feet below the existing ground 
surface, generally corresponding to about Elevation +2.2 feet MSL. It should be noted 
that water levels may vary with tidal fluctuations, storm surge conditions, seasonal 
rainfall, time of year, and other factors. 
Based on the very loose to loose granular deposits encountered, we believe that 
the project site could be subjected to liquefaction ground settlements on the order of 
about 2 to 5 inches. Provided the estimated liquefaction settlements are acceptable, the 
relatively light metal building structure may be supported on shallow foundations. If the 
estimated liquefaction settlements are not acceptable for the project, we recommend 
utilizing a deep foundation system bearing into the underlying coral formation. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for the design of foundations and 
other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections.  
3.1 
Building Foundations 
Provided that the estimated liquefaction settlements described previously are 
acceptable, we believe that the new mezzanine structure with relatively light structural 
loads may be supported on shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous 
strip footings.  
Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at the foundation subgrades, we 
recommend providing a stabilization layer (minimum 24 inches thick) below the footings 
to provide uniform support for the foundations. The stabilization layer also should 
extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the edges of the footing. The stabilization 
layer should consist of 24 inches of 3B Fine Gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) 
wrapped on all sides in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent).

An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf may be used for the design of 
footings bearing on the recompacted on-site materials (minimum 90 percent relative

one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. The

If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated static design loads for footings 
bearing on the recompacted on-site soils to be on the order of about 1 inch or less. 
Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials should 
be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less (static loading only). 
Lateral loads acting on the mezzanine structure supported on shallow 
foundations may be resisted by a combination of friction between the base of the 
foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure developed against 
the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of the footings. A coefficient of friction of 
0.3 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils. Resistance due 
to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for footings embedded in the on-site

3.2 
Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared.  After clearing, existing structures and pavements that are to be 
demolished should be completely removed. Over-excavations resulting from demolition 
should be backfilled with compacted general fill materials. Existing utilities to be 
abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation should be properly 
backfilled with general fill material placed in 8-inch loose lifts and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Utilities to be abandoned in-place under the 
proposed improvements should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete (or Controlled 
Low Strength Materials) under low pressure. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over-excavated 
to expose the underlying firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or organic soils 
should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where 
appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing, the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of the soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as 
determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Imported materials required for site filling should consist of select granular fill 
materials, such as crushed coral and/or basaltic gravel. The materials should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in the largest 
dimension. In addition, the materials also should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883.

test results are summarized and presented in Appendix D. Detailed results of the

Based on the updated version of the Board of Water Supply, City and County of 
Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021, 
there are two soil corrosivity categories. Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) 
applies to soil meeting any one of the following conditions. Corrosion Category B 
(Negligibly to Mildly Corrosive) is applicable when none of the following conditions are 
met. The updated version of the standards is effective as of the publishing date for 
Oahu only. 
• Resistivity ≤ 5,000 ohm-cm 
• pH ≤ 6.5 
• Water-soluble chloride concentration ≥ 500 ppm 
• Water-soluble sulfate concentration ≥ 1,000 ppm 
• Within fluctuating saltwater table 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, 
the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic 
structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to 
the corrosion of metallic objects. Results of the laboratory testing indicate that the 
on-site soils have a resistivity value of about 1,600 ohm-cm with pH value of about 8.01. 
Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered as Corrosion Category A 
(Moderately to Severely Corrosive) based on the updated Board of Water Supply 
External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021. 
In addition, chloride content and sulfate content testing were performed by Cerco 
Analytical to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. Based on the 
chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the test values are 
about 80 parts per million (ppm) for chloride content and 220 ppm for sulfate content. It 
may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test 
results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection. Detailed results 
of the Chloride Content (ASTM D4327) and Sulfate Content (ASTM D4327) testing, 
performed by Cerco Analytical, are presented in Appendix F.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one borehole, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of approximately 
52 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. The boring was advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight 
solid-stem augers (SSA) and PQ-size wireline coring methods to advance the PQ-size 
casing from the ground surface. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-1.1 and A-1.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Boring indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques, consisting of a PQ wireline coring system 
with double tube core barrel (core size of 3.35 inches), in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary 
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left

The coring rates indicated on the Logs of Boring refer to the time required to complete

Recovery (REC) is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a 
coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Boring as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the total core run in rock 
that is sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, 
discounting any drilling, mechanical, and handling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 
feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Boring as RQD = 50%. It should be 
noted that RQD does not apply to coral material. However, the RQD values of the coral 
material are included in the boring logs for rough characterization of the coral material.

Seismic Piezocone Penetration Tests 
Geolabs, Inc. conducted cone penetration tests using the seismic piezocone 
penetration testing equipment at three probe locations, designated as CPT-1 through 
CPT-3, as shown on the Boring Location Plan, Plate 2. 
The tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D5778 – Standard 
Test Method for Performing Electronic Friction Cone and Piezocone Penetration Testing 
of Soils. The probes extended to depths ranging from about 23 to 25 feet below the 
existing pavement surface using seismic piezocone penetration testing equipment 
(SCPTU) before refusal conditions to our system were encountered. Due to early refusal 
conditions, upper portion of each CPT location was predrilled to extend the CPT to 
deeper depths. After predrilling, the hole was backfilled, and the cone was pushed back 
down. It should be noted that the cone recorded near zero side friction values within the 
predrilled zone at each CPT location. 
The seismic piezocone penetration test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone (Serial Number DSG0824), manufactured by Vertek 
with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1.55 square inches (10 square 
centimeters) connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 23.25 square inches 
(150 square centimeters), into the ground at a rate of approximately 20 mm/second 
(+5 mm/second) using a CME-75D truck-mounted drill rig. 
The tip and side friction resistance and pore pressure induced immediately 
behind the cone tip (designated as the U2 parameter) during penetration are measured 
by strain gauges within the instrument and are recorded versus the depth of penetration 
at about 2-inch (50-millimeter) intervals. When the material encountered was too hard 
for further penetration by the SCPTU equipment, which might cause potential equipment 
damage, penetration was halted, and the cone was withdrawn. 
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone with an embedded inclinometer 
to detect the arrival of a shear wave propagated from the ground surface. The seismic 
measurements are made when the SCPTU has stopped, and a seismic shear wave is 
sent into the subsurface. A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded 
plate in opposite directions with a switched hammer at approximately 1-meter (3.28 
feet) to 2-meter (6.56 feet) intervals. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer 
blow to the cone was measured at each depth interval. The vector difference of these 
depths divided by the time difference provided the average seismic shear wave velocity 
of the subsurface material over the depth interval. 
The field data collected was subsequently processed in the office using the 
computer software program – CPT Coneplot developed by Vertek. Graphic 
representations of the seismic piezocone penetration test data are presented on 
Plates B-1, B-2, and B-3. In general, the plots include cone tip resistance, friction ratio

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was performed 
using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. Seismic shear wave 
velocity testing using seismic cone penetration testing equipment was performed at one 
boring location, designated as Boring No. 1, as shown on the Boring Location Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at approximately 3.3 to 
6.6-foot intervals extending to depths of about 52 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. In order to conduct the seismic shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test 
boring generally was advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of 
the boring. Logs of the materials encountered in the boring are presented on the Logs of 
Boring in Appendix A. After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the 
borehole, the bored hole was backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter time-release bentonite 
pellets. The temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to 
place the bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward. When the 
bentonite pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the boring, the pellets start to 
hydrate slowly. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell and soften. Backfilling above 
the static groundwater table was performed in stages by adding water to a limited 
amount of bentonite chips to ensure hydration before additional bentonite chips were 
placed in the boring. The probe was then pushed through the softened bentonite 
extending to a depth of about 52 feet below the existing pavement surface using the 
seismic cone penetration testing equipment (SCPT). 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone into the bentonite backfilled boring. The cone has an 
apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected surface area of 1.55 square inches 
(10 square centimeters) equipped with an electronic depth sensor attached to the cone 
rod. The seismic cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a 
shear wave generated and propagated from the ground surface. Seismic 
measurements are made when the SCPT has stopped, and a shear wave is sent into 
the subsurface. A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with 
a switched hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the 
cone was measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these 
depths divided by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths 
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The seismic shear wave velocities measured for the full depth and the average 
seismic shear wave velocity calculated for the top 52 feet of the soil profile at the boring 
location are presented on Plates C-1.1 and C-1.2. The weighted average seismic shear 
wave velocity for the upper 52 feet of the soil profile was calculated based on the 
average shear wave velocity method described in Chapter 20 of the American Society

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depth, and the 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate D-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate D-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plate D-3. 
Two Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected intact core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the tuff 
and coral formations encountered. The test results are presented on Plate D-4. 
One set of Corrosion test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (ASTM D4327), and Sulfate Content (ASTM D4327), 
was performed by our office and Cerco Analytical on a selected soil sample obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate D-5.
