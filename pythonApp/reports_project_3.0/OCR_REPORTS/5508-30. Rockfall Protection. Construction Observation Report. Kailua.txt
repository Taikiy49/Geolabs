Project Considerations
The Olomana Dey Agricultural Subdivision project site is on the south side of
the Old Kalanianaole Highway in the Kailua area of the Island of Oahu Hawaii as
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The overall subdivision development
consists of 23 agricultural lots As a result of a hill slope assessment and rockfall hazard
evaluation required by the City and County of Honolulu Lot Nos 1 and 2 were observed
to contain potential rockfall hazards requiring mitigation to protect downslope
development Therefore rockfall protection improvements consisting of a rockfall impact
barrier system was constructed at the project site to reduce the potential for rockfall
activity to affect downslope development
The final rockfall mitigation design accepted by the City County of Honolulu
was completed by Geolabs in April 2008 for Sound Investments LLC A period of time
elapsed where the project ownership changed from Sound Investments LLC to
The new owner requested a minor shift in the alignment of a portion
of the barrier system to accommodate space for future home site development at Lot
No 1 Geolabs concluded that the requested alignment shift would not have an adverse
impact on the efficiency of the protection system The alignment shift made during
construction at Barrier Segment A is shown on the Rockfall Barrier As Built Plan

Rockfall Protection Construction
We observed construction of the rockfall protection improvements on a part time
basis between and at which time the project was
considered substantially complete by means of a pre final site inspection The prime
contractor Goodfellow Brothers Inc provided a trail for hillside access developed a
working slope bench for the barrier construction and provided the field survey for the
barrier foundation locations The field layout survey was based on a Barrier
Segment A realignment provided by Geolabs Inc and the alignment of Barrier
Segment B as shown on the construction plans
The rockfall specialty subcontractor Prometheus Construction Inc performed
the rockfall barrier installation work which included the construction of micropile and
concrete block foundations installation of the wire rope ground anchors and the
assembly of the barrier system in general accordance with the manufacturer s product
and installation manual

Barrier Post Foundations
The Type and Type Il barrier post foundations were constructed in general
accordance with the Type foundations shown on Sheet G 7 of the construction plans
prepared by Geolabs Inc In general the foundations were excavated to the required
depth and the battered micropiles extending approximately 15 feet below the block
foundation were drilled and grouted in place Based on our observation of the
foundation excavation and micropile drilling stiff clayey residual and saprolitic soils
overlying generally hard weathered basalt rock materials were encountered
Following grouting of the micropiles concrete was poured to complete the block
foundation encasing the micropiles The concrete mix was delivered by Hawaiian
Cement and pumped to the work site using a large concrete boom pump truck operated
by Ohana Concrete Pumping Co Approximately 48 cubic yards 6 trucks of concrete
were used for the concrete block foundation construction
Two base plate anchor bolts for each foundation were inserted into the fresh
concrete using temporary steel templates for proper bolt spacing Following the
concrete placement some small annular gaps between the anchor bolt and concrete
were observed at the top of some of the foundations The gaps were brought to the
contractor s attention for correction The contractor sealed the observed bolt annulus
using a low viscosity injection resin combined with a surface coating of an elastomeric
waterproofing material Proof testing of five random selected anchor bolts was
performed to verify the acceptable pull out capacity of the corrected bolts The actual
foundation locations are shown on the As Built Plan Plate 3
Wire Rope Ground Anchors
The wire rope ground anchors were installed in general accordance with the
details shown on Sheet G8 of the construction plans prepared by
restraining anchors are not required for the Type II Barrier GBE 500AR Thus the
number of upslope post restraining anchors for the project was reduced by 12 The
vertical barrier posts employ extra steel bracing to preclude the need for the upslope
restraining anchors
All wire rope anchors were installed in 6 inch diameter drilled boreholes which
were drilled to the minimum required depth shown on the plans The boreholes were
drilled using an air percussion hammer mounted on a track excavator machine Based
on our observations hard rock formation generally was encountered between depths of
about 5 and 9 feet below the existing ground surfaces
Based on our observations the double strand wire rope ground anchors consist
of 1 inch diameter galvanized 6x19 construction IWRC wire rope fitted with a thimble
end The anchors were suspended in the open boreholes using two 2 PVC borehole

Anchor Proof Testing
The contractor performed the anchor proof testing work which consisted of
testing 100 percent of the lateral anchors and 50 percent of the upslope restraining
anchors in general accordance with the contract documents The testing was
performed in general accordance with requirements on Section 662 Technical
Specifications Pages 662 8 and 662 9 The design pull out capacity was 26 tons
52 kips for the lateral anchors and 19 tons 38 kips for the upslope restraining
anchors
A total of twelve 12 lateral anchors and seven 7 upslope restraining anchors
were satisfactorily proof tested by the contractor under the observation of our
engineer geologist A complete list of all wire rope anchor installations is provided in

Barrier Maintenance Considerations
Rockfall impact barriers are designed for limited maintenance requirements
following construction However maintenance of the barrier will likely be needed
following large rock impacts or when long term corrosion of the steel components may
compromise the structural integrity of the system Although regular maintenance needs
are generally low rockfall impact barriers do require routine non technical visual
inspection to ensure the desired performance of the system Routine visual inspection
should help to identify early visual signs of steel corrosion due to environmental
exposure loose hardware connections and possible system damage due to events
such as large rock impacts wildfire or vandalism
The responsible person or agency owner should inspect the rockfall impact
barrier structure at least once per year or following a large rock impact for visible signs
of rock impact debris accumulation in the net or deterioration and corrosion of primary
system components such as the wire mesh steel posts and ground anchor wire ropes
lf a significant volume of rock material consisting of a large boulder s multiple
boulders or deep accumulations of rock and other debris is encountered a rockfall
specialty contractor should be contracted to properly remove the debris and restore
system function In addition if components are visibly broken loose and sagging or
excessively corroded a rockfall specialty contractor should be contracted to perform
inspection and repairs
Barrier system maintenance such as the repair or replacement of worn broken
components i e the braking elements net panels and connecting hardware may be

required following a larger rockfall impact The frequency of barrier maintenance
typically depends on the size and frequency of rockfall events
As mentioned previously rockfall impact barriers are designed to require minimal
routine maintenance However even where a low incidence of rockfall activity is
experienced periodic more thorough inspection of the system components by a
qualified person should be conducted at scheduled intervals of about 5 to 10 years In
corrosive environments and where rockfall activity is relatively moderate qualified
persons should perform the thorough inspections at intervals of about 5 years
Due to the often difficult hillside access condition of some barrier installations
some owners may elect to establish a contract with a local rockfall specialty contractor
to perform routine inspections large volume rock debris removal and other system
maintenance as needed The rockfall specialty contractor typically maintains the proper
equipment and trained personnel to conduct work on difficult access slopes For these
reasons the rockfall specialty contractor should be contracted to perform the removal of
large rockfall debris and to perform barrier system repair The rockfall mitigation
specialty contractor typically utilizes portable construction equipment which is
transported onto the slope to perform the potentially hazardous rock removal and barrier
system repair work Furthermore the rockfall specialty contractor typically would employ
temporary safety controls to protect down slope persons and property during the
performance of the potentially hazardous maintenance work
It is important to note the need for maintenance on rockfall impact barriers should
be minimal because the barriers are designed to withstand multiple small and medium
magnitude rock impacts without being compromised Therefore it is generally not
necessary to clean the barrier if there is only a light accumulation of rocks at the bottom
of the net and the net has maintained its original capture height
The manufacturers GBE Barrier System maintenance manual is presented in
Appendix D for reference The manual provides general information pertaining to
inspection frequency methods for debris removal safety issues when conducting
repairs and instruction for replacement of damaged barrier system components
Finally we suggest the performance of routine trimming and clearing of
vegetation along the barrier alignment Vegetation such as wild grasses and shrubbery
should be cleared or trimmed periodically to reduce excessive and dense vegetation
overgrowth that may cover the barrier Woody stem vegetation such as trees and
mature shrubbery should be cleared from the barrier alignment and not be allowed to
become established The primary reasons for the recommended vegetation
maintenance include the following
1 To maintain access to the length of the barrier alignment for routine
inspection and future barrier maintenance when necessary

maintenance budget should be developed to accommodate such work
Lot Development Considerations
The purpose for the installation of the rockfall impact barrier system is to provide
protection to reduce the potential for falling rock hazards to encroach on the region
down slope of the barrier alignment which spans across the hillside within Lot Nos 1
and 2 We understand the development scheme for Lot Nos 1 and 2 was not defined
beyond the establishment of the lot driveway locations Therefore we wish to provide
some considerations to guide future development of the lots within the constraints of the
established rockfall impact barrier system
1 Permanent dwelling structure construction should not encroach within
40 linear feet down slope of the barrier alignment The purpose is to avoid
future dwelling structure encroachment within a buffer zone that
accommodates the potential down slope deflection of the barrier nets
under potential high velocity rock impact and net loading
2 Future earthwork grading or grade alteration must be avoided within the
existing barrier alignment Any future grading adjacent to the existing
barrier alignment should be carefully evaluated and approved by a
qualified professional engineer familiar with the design of rockfall impact
barriers
2 Future earthwork or grading immediately surrounding the rockfall impact
barrier alignment should be carefully assessed by a qualified professional
engineer to limit the potential for adversely compromising the existing
barrier foundations and ground anchor installations
4 Permanent dwelling construction should be avoided in the unprotected
region upslope from the existing rockfall impact barrier alignment Future

development above the barrier must be carefully evaluated by a qualified
professional engineer Additional rockfall hazard controls may be required
to protect development made upslope from the barrier alignment

V PRODUCT LIABILITY
Rockfall landslides mudflows and avalanches are sporadic and unpredictable Their causes may be human
construction etc or environmental weather earthquakes etc The various sources of such events make it
impossible to implement a scientific process to guarantee the safety of people and property
Nevertheless the application of appropriate engineering calculations using predictable parameters and the
subsequent generation of flawless protection measures in specific risk areas plays a considerable role in reducing
the risk of injury and the loss of property
The monitoring and maintenance of such systems is absolutely vital in order to ensure that the required level of
safety is sustained The level of safety can also be impaired by events natural catastrophes unsuitable
dimensioning parameters or the failure to use standard components systems and original parts as well as by
corrosion caused by pollution other man made factors and other external influences
In contrast to 1 1 rockfall tests which despite testing a very rigorous load case only demonstrate a standardized
situation field applications can give rise an extremely wide range of barrier configurations and designs due to the
different topological conditions It is not always possible to precisely determine the impact of such modifications
and adjustments Critical aspects include the distance between the posts changes in direction the installation
angle of the rope anchors the direction of impact and the speed of impact
When it comes to assessing the impact of larger scale deviations and special situations Geobrugg is able to offer
support and propose feasible solutions However Geobrugg cannot guarantee the same level of performance as
the 1 1 rockfall tests In critical cases we recommend using individual components that have been reinforced
from those used in the standard barriers

ll FUNCTIONAL EFFICIENCY OF THE BARRIER SYSTEMS
The functional efficiency of the system is based on one to one rockfall tests carried out and tested in accordance
with the guidelines for approval of rockfall protection nets ETAG 027 The one to one rockfall tests are carried out
by dropping a block vertically into the middle field of a three field barrier The distance between posts is 10 m and
an impact velocity of 25 m s is reached The full scale test were approved by a notified test body and the Europe
an Technical Approval ETA has the number ETA 09 0262

V PRODUCT LIABILITY
Rockfall landslides debris flows or avalanches are sporadic and unpredictable The cause is human buildings
etc for example or forces beyond human control weather earthquakes etc The multiplicity of factors that
may trigger such events means that guaranteeing the safety of persons and property is not an exact science
However the risks of injury and loss of property can be substantially reduced by appropriate calculations that ap
ply good engineering practices and by using predictable parameters along with the corresponding implementa
tion of flawless protective measures in identified risk areas
Monitoring and maintenance of such systems are an absolute requirement to ensure the desired safety level
System safety can also be diminished through events natural disasters inadequate dimensioning or failure to
use standard components systems and original parts but also through corrosion caused by environmental pollu
tion or other man made factors as well as other external influences
In contrast to the one to one rockfall tests which indeed test an extreme load case but still only demonstrate a
standardized situation in the field the layout and design of a protection system can vary greatly because of the
topography The influence of such alterations and adaptations cannot always be determined exactly Critical
points are for example post spacing changes in direction placement angle of the rope anchor and the direction
and velocity of impact
Geobrugg can assist with estimating the influence of larger deviations and special situations and can offer rec
ommendations for feasible solutions Geobrugg cannot however guarantee the same behavior as in the one to
one rockfall tests In critical cases it is advisable to reinforce particular components as compared with the stand
ard barrier

Subject Basic Rockfall Barrier Inspection Plan
In general the components in a rockfall protection barrier do not require any maintenance as long as
there is no rockfall into the barrier and no extensive corrosion which affects the prospective barrier
lifetime However regular inspections are recommended in order to maintain the performance of the
barrier which can be affected by rocks lying in the net and also to have the chance to discover damage
in the system due to smaller impacts or corrosion An inspection is always required after any rockfall
impact For a less than design energy impact minimal maintenance should be required larger repairs
may be necessary if rock impacts with kinetic energy beyond the design load rating are experienced
The frequency of inspection is ultimately determined based on the frequency of rockfall and other
influences such as weather snow plowing etc that may occur at a barrier installation For further
guidelines on maintenance and inspection intervals refer Geobrugg maintenance manuals Our
manuals include detailed guidelines for the working life of the rockfall barrier inspection guidelines and
repair and application of damaged components
Basic Inspection
1 Rock and debris behind barrier In order to maintain the barrier design height debris should
not be allowed to accumulate more than 1 3 of the net height Also rocks that have been
stopped but are lying in the barrier or on support ropes need to be removed
o Removal of the rocks can be accomplished with small equipment that can be driven
behind the barrier This requires loosening the upslope retaining ropes and moving
them temporarily Another option is to lay down the nets to gain access from the front
o Inthe case that large rocks are unable to be moved by standard equipment the rocks
can be mechanically broke into smaller more manageable pieces by breaking them apart
using expansive chemicals or other pneumatic means
o Another option is to use a crane or long armed excavator to lift the rocks over the
barrier
2 Support ropes lateral anchor ropes upslope anchor ropes The support ropes were
inspected for tightness wear abrasion brittleness severe buckling and other significant
damage
3 Brake rings elements Brake rings are design to absorb multiple impacts without replacement
assuming they have additional capacity The criteria for determining the remaining capacity is
the percentage that a brake ring has been activated If the activation is greater than 50
approximately 3 ft the brake ring should be replaced
1
4 Wire rope anchors Wire ropes anchors need to be replaced if they are damaged severely
corroded or if the anchors are pulled out of the ground greater than 2 inches
5 Ring Nets Ring nets are designed to deform elastically and withstand high energy impacts If
they are deformed plastically or if greater than 10 or more rings per panel are squeezed
severely deformed or if the wire is fractured the entire net should be replaced
6 Secondary Wire Mesh The secondary wire mesh functions to prevent small rocks from
penetrating the larger opening of the ring nets If this secondary net is damaged pulled down
or not performing its intended function in any way it should be remedied
7 Posts connection devices ground plate The posts and ground plate serve the function of
static support of the rockfall barrier components If the posts ground plates or anchors become
corroded or if they are bent severely greater than 15 degrees they should be replaced
8 Misc items Any other conditions that appear incorrect broken or improper should be
investigated and repaired

V PRODUCT LIABILITY
Rockfall landslides debris flows or avalanches are sporadic and unpredictable The cause may be human
buildings etc for example or forces beyond human control weather earthquakes etc The multiplicity of
factors that may trigger such events means that guaranteeing the safety of persons and property is not an exact
science
However the risks of injury and loss of property can be substantially reduced by appropriate calculations that
apply good engineering practices and by using predictable parameters along with the corresponding
implementation of flawless protective measures in identified risk areas
The monitoring and maintenance of such systems is an absolute requirement to ensure the desired safety level
System safety can also be compromised through events natural disasters inadequate dimensioning or failure to
use standard components systems and original parts but also through corrosion caused by environmental
pollution or other man made factors as well as other external influences
In contrast to the 1 1 rockfall tests which indeed test an extreme load case but still only demonstrate a
standardized situation in the field the layout and design of a protection system can vary greatly because of the
topography The influence of such alterations and adaptations cannot always be determined exactly Critical
points are for example post spacing changes in direction placement angle of the rope anchors and the
direction and velocity of impact
Geobrugg can assist with estimating the influence of larger deviations and special situations and can offer
recommendations for feasible solutions Geobrugg cannot however guarantee the same behavior as in the 1 1
rockfall tests In critical cases it is advisable to reinforce particular components as compared with the standard
barrier

i Any debris that accumulates in the barrier should not exceed a maximum of a third of the usable height

3 2 Remaining usable height following an incident
The remaining usable height of the barrier following an incident is an initial indicator of the level of damage that
has occurred Clear sagging of the support rope or the net and a significant change in the angle of the posts
indicate an elongation of one or more U brakes which may need to be replaced
3 3 U brakes
In the course of the EOTA certification tests depending on their installation position the U brakes became
elongated to varying degrees Accordingly following several minor incidents their energy absorption capacity for a
subsequent maximum incident varies
For the sake of simplicity for the U 150 an elongation of 30 cm has been set and for the various U 300 modes
the elongation of 60 cm is set as a maximum initial elongation If the initial elongation exceeds this value the
brake must be replaced Once the U brakes have become elongated the usable height of the barrier has
decreased By re tightening the support ropes the usable height can be restored to its original value

Corrosion
