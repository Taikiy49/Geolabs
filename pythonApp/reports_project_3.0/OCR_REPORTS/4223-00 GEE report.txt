REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred
to as the Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
material which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series The
Waianae Volcanic is divided into lower middle and upper volcanic members The lower
member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that built the main mass
of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock that accumulated
and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a relatively thin
capping layer which covers the entire top of the shield volcano late in its history of
evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of Oahu occurred which produced the large valleys of the
western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were gradually filled with
enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined result of
stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the island mass Over
time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and colluvial
deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation formed relatively dense
deposits However the current surficial deposits above these older deposits are typically
found to be unconsolidated

Overflow Structure Design
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow footings may be used for support of the proposed overflow structure
An allowable bearing pressure of may be utilized for the design of the overflow
structure bearing on the on site dense clayey basalt gravel very stiff silty clay or properly
compacted select granular fill material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade Footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum
horizontal setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footing to the
slope face
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing bottom they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be deepened to the underlying firm materials
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pcf Resistance due to friction may be estimated using a coefficient of
friction of 0 35
The overflow structure should be designed to resist an equivalent fluid earth
pressure of 55 pcf This value assumes that the structure is under a restrained
condition not able to rotate more than 0 5 percent of the wall height This value does
not account for surcharge or hydrostatic loads Surcharge stresses due to areal
surcharges live loads and point loads should be considered in the design
GEOLABS INC
W O 4223 00 Page 10
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the overflow
structure a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 45 percent of the
vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall may be used in the
design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads
Backfill behind the overflow structure should consist of concrete or CLSM low
strength concrete or flowable fill If it is desired to allow possible future excavation
with normal construction equipment then CLSM with a compressive strength of 50 to
250 psi should be utilized
Due to the difficulty in achieving compaction on a sloping ground with a thin soil
cover over the utility line we recommend that the 24 inch diameter overflow line located
beneath the liner be encased above and below pipe with either concrete or CLSM low
strength concrete or flowable fill This should also reduce the potential of damaging the
utility lines If it is desired to allow possible future excavation with normal construction
equipment then CLSM with a compressive strength of 50 to 250 psi should be utilized
Note that the weight of the pipe should be greater than the bouyant force to prevent
movement of the pipe during backfill placement
We recommend that overflow structure excavations be observed by a
representative of Geolabs Inc prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to
confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area to receive the select borrow subbase material
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris
construction debris and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site or in a designated area
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with properly compacted select granular fill The soft soils
should not be reused as fill material
The subgrade should be scarified a minimum depth of 8_ inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or prepared
again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted
engineered fill
Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill
material provided the largest rock fragments in the material is less than 3 inches in
maximum dimension and that the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious
materials In addition the on site materials to be used as general fill should be

relatively well graded and should have a minimum of 10 percent passing the

Select granular fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded

for use as select granular fill
Select borrow subbase materials should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and
should conform to the City and County of Honolulu Department of Public Works
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction dated September 1986
Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Inc for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use

classification The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 3

depths Graphic representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 4
