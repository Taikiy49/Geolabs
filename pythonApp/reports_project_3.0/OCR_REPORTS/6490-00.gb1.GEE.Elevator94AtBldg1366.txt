Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years
ago
The geologic conditions underlying the Pearl Harbor area and the project site are
the result of a complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by
glacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the
Pleistocene Epoch Following the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano
alluvial soil materials were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the
Pearl Harbor area
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level During the Recent Epoch as sea
level rose to its current stand the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits These deposits were subsequently covered by man made fills placed during
the middle portion of this century for land reclamation projects during construction of the
existing Pearl Harbor area

Liquefaction
Based on the International Building Code the project site may be subjected to
seismic activity and should be evaluated for soil liquefaction potential
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing
an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength
of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up

W O 6490 00

Page 7

could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil
acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and
if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to
relative density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is
directly proportional to the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the boring indicates that the
site is underlain by loose to very loose lagoonal deposits overlying stiff alluvial
deposits The lagoonal deposits consisted of silty sand and extended to about
45 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our analyses the lagoonal
deposits are potentially liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6 with an
associated peak ground acceleration of 0 24g
It appears that the loose to very loose lagoonal deposits below have a factor of
safety of 0 4 to 1 0 against liquefaction Therefore it is our opinion that the project
site could be subject to appreciable ground settlements on the order of about
6 inches or greater in the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake
M6 The effects of potential liquefaction at the site should be taken into
consideration when designing the proposed new structure However we believe

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the available information the existing building and elevator shaft is
supported on pile foundations Since the existing structures are supported on pile
foundations we do not recommend supporting the new walls of the elevator shaft on
shallow foundations since there is a greater potential for significant structural distress
due to differential settlement between the existing and new structures We recommend
supporting the new walls of the elevator shaft on a deep foundation system to reduce
the potential for significant structural distress caused by differential settlement between
the existing and new structures Considering the space constraints it is not practical to
use driven pile or cast in place drilled shafts due to the relatively large installation
equipment required for these foundation systems In addition significant amount of
noise and vibrations would be generated from a driven pile foundation during
construction on the adjacent building structure therefore we believe that cased
micropile foundations may be used to support the new structure due to the relatively
smaller equipment required for installation
The cased micropile foundation system should have a permanent casing and
grout bulb diameters of at least 7 5 inches The permanent casing unbonded zone
should extend to the top of the stiff alluvial deposits encountered at a depth of about
100 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered the micropiles would derive its vertical support primarily in the bonded
zone from skin friction between the grout and the surrounding stiff alluvial deposits We
recommend using an allowable compressive load capacity of 45 tons Based on our

Micropile Foundations
As previously mentioned the existing building and elevator shaft is supported on
pile foundations We do not recommend supporting the new walls of the elevator shaft
on shallow foundations since the potential for significant structural distress due to
differential settlement is increased when shallow and deep foundations are used
simultaneously to support a structure Therefore we recommend supporting the new
walls of the elevator shaft on a deep foundation system to reduce the potential for
significant structural distress caused by differential settlement between the existing and
new structures
Considering the space constraints we believe it is not practical to support the
new walls on driven piles or cast in place drilled shafts In addition installation of a
driven pile foundation will generate a significant amount of noise and vibration The
noise and vibrations generated from a driven pile foundation during construction will
have an effect on the adjacent building structure therefore we believe that cased
micropile foundations should be used to support the planned new structure
In general the cased micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter
usually less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The
micropile foundation typically is constructed by drilling a hole with or without casing
placing reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the hole Micropiles are desirable
because they can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and in
numerous soil types and ground conditions In addition the micropile installation
generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the
environment
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend utilizing a micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of 7 5 inches to
support the proposed new elevator shaft walls The permanent casing for the cased
micropile system should have a minimum outside diameter OD of about 7 5 inches
The permanent casing unbonded zone should extend to the top of the stiff alluvial
deposits encountered at a depth of about 100 feet below the existing ground surface

Downdrag Loads
The lagoonal deposits at the project site may be susceptible to liquefaction during a
seismic event In the event of seismically induced liquefaction settlement of the
lagoonal deposits will occur at the project site When the lagoonal deposits move
downward relative to the stationary micropile the downward movement will result in
negative skin friction forces generally known as downdrag forces acting on the
micropile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of
the liquefiable layer relative density of the potentially liquefiable lagoonal deposits
and the micropile perimeter
Due to the potential for liquefaction of the very loose to loose lagoonal deposits at
the project site settlement of the lagoonal deposits will result in downdrag loading

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by shear along the grout
bulb bonded zone and the adjacent soils The recommended uplift load capacity
for the micropiles is 100 kips per pile The uplift load capacity provided is intended
for transient load applications only Therefore the uplift load capacity should not be
further increased for transient load applications For sustained uplift loads we
recommend reducing the uplift load capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist
sustained uplift loads The project structural engineer should check the capacity of
the micropile member in tension
Lateral Load Resistance
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the
lateral load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected
Lateral loads imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily
by passive earth pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and
grade beams and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade
beams
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per
square foot per foot of depth pcf above the groundwater level and 140 pcf below
the groundwater level Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by
shear resistance between the sides of the pile caps and grade beams and the
adjacent soils A side shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square feet psf
may be used for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only

Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the micropile supported foundations to be on then order of about
1 inch or less with differential settlements of about 0 5 inch or less We believe that
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the compressive and uplift load capacities of the micropiles
is highly dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed
by the contractor to install the micropile Therefore the compressive and uplift load
capacities of the micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and
micropile foundation systems In order to determine whether the contractor s
methods of micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate
compressive load capacity we recommend performing a pre production
compressive load test at the site In general the purpose of the pre production
load test on a micropile is to fulfill the following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
To evaluate the degree of long term settlements of the micropile
In general the pre production compressive load test should be performed in
accordance with ASTM D 1143 Based on experience we believe that the load
tests should be conducted at least seven days after completion of the micropile
installation to allow the grout adequate time to cure
For the compressive load test two to four additional micropiles may be used for
reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre production load test
micropile The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load
test micropile to provide adequate reaction in uplift to be determined by the
contractor

used Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads
Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up of
hydrostatic pressures behind the retaining structure A typical drainage system for
the retaining structures should consist of about 1 cubic foot of permeable material
such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped with
non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent at each of the weep hole
locations The weep holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart
The backfill from the bottom of the retaining structure to the bottom of the weephole
should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration into the subsurface In addition the upper 12 inches of
the retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure
unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface

compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by mechanical rollers or other types of

Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one boring
designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of about 145 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We
used a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring
tools to drill the boring
Our geologist observed the drilling operations on a near continuous basis and
classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and textural examination in
the field These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plate A 1
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
