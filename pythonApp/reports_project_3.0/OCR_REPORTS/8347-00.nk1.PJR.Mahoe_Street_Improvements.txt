SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed for the Mahoe Street Improvements project. 
Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that the majority of the pavement subgrade 
soils likely will consist of near-surface fill material with an R-value of about 22. 
Typically, new Department of Transportation (DOT) pavements include a 
permeable base drainage layer to facilitate drainage and increase the pavement life. 
However, since the existing pavement structures along Mahoe Street does not have a 
drainage layer, only pavement structural sections without a permeable base layer were 
considered in our pavement analyses. 
Based on the estimated traffic design parameters, the R-value of the subgrade soils 
and the economic analyses performed, a rigid pavement section was the most 
economical. For rigid pavement construction, roadway closures will be required to allow 
time for the concrete pavement to cure and harden. Although a rigid pavement section 
was the most economical, we recommend using a flexible pavement structural section for 
the new pavements for the project to reduce the amount of road closures for pavement 
installation. The recommended pavement structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC 
on 4.0 inches of ACB over 12.0 inches of ABC should be used for the design of 
pavements for the new Mahoe Street improvements project. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement 
surface should be adequately sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.  
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed 
discussion and specific pavement design recommendations for the new roundabout 
project.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed 
for the design of new pavements for the Mahoe Street Improvements project in 
Waipahu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located along Mahoe Street between Waipahu Street and 
Hinaea Street in Waipahu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based on the project drawings 
provided, we understand that new bulb-outs and speed tables will be constructed to 
improve safety along the roadway. The bulb-outs and speed tables will be located at the 
cross streets along Mahoe Street.  
In addition to the safety improvements, we understand that a number of new 
catch basins will be installed near the improved intersections. Based on the information 
provided, we understand that the invert depths of the new catch basins will range from 
about 4 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface.  
A grading plan was not available at the time this report was prepared. However, 
we envision that general site grading will be minimal to construct the bulb-outs and 
speed tables. Excavations up to about 12 feet below the existing ground surface may be 
required to install the new catch basins.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano 
ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope 
forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is 
located on the southeastern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in-situ chemical weathering to 
depths of 50 to 150 feet and are characterized by red highlights in the soil.  This in-situ 
chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite over 
basalt rock formation with depth.  Based on radiometric measurements, the 
decomposition of these soils has taken place over approximate 1-2 million years. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. These sediments 
accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral/algal reef formations to 
form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero 
in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This 
wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over 
the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued 
from earlier geologic time through the present. 
Based on geologic maps, the project site is underlain by a basaltic rock formation 
on the northern portion of the site and alluvial deposits on the southern portion of the 
site. The geologic boundary falls approximately halfway between Huakai Street and 
Mahoe Place.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed for the Mahoe Street Improvements project. 
Typically, new Department of Transportation (DOT) pavements include a permeable 
base drainage layer to facilitate drainage and increase the pavement life. However, 
since the existing pavement structures along Mahoe Street do not have a permeable 
base drainage layer, only pavement structural sections without a permeable base layer 
were considered in our pavement analyses. 
Based on our economic analysis, a rigid pavement structural section would be 
the more economical option. Although the rigid pavement section is the most 
economical option, we recommend using the flexible pavement structural section to 
reduce the amount of road closures for the project. 
3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The flexible pavement sections presented in this report were generally 
determined based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement 
Design Manual dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the 
State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and 
Research Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem 
Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of 
Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections are generally based on 
the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design 
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements.”

3.3 
Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on our field exploration and the current design concept, we anticipate that 
the pavement subgrade soils may consist of the near-surface fill material, consisting of 
stiff to very stiff clayey silt and medium dense silty sand/gravel, encountered in our 
borings.  
Laboratory Resistance (R) Value laboratory testing was performed on three bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils. The results ranged from 21 to 24. Based on the 
laboratory data, an R-value of 22 has been adopted for the subgrade soils in our 
pavement design analyses for this project. The results of the laboratory tests are 
presented in Appendix B. If site grading exposes soils other than those assumed in the 
pavement design, additional tests should be performed to confirm and/or revise the 
recommended pavement section for actual field conditions. 
3.4 
Design Pavement Section 
The revised Pavement Design Manual requires a highly permeable base 
drainage layer below the pavements unless the site has a very low annual rainfall or the 
site consists of pavement subgrades that are free-draining (permeability greater than 
100 feet per day). Because the project site is not located in a low rainfall environment 
and the pavement subgrade soils are not free-draining, a permeable base layer should 
be incorporated into the pavement structural section. 
However, since the existing pavement section along Mahoe Street does not have 
a permeable base drainage layer, only pavement structural sections without a 
permeable base layer were considered in our pavement analyses. 
Based on the traffic volumes anticipated for the new pavements, asphalt 
concrete base (ACB) should be used as the base material in flexible pavement sections 
for the project. Based on the assumptions presented above, we considered flexible and

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
We anticipate that the new pavement for the roadway improvements project will 
be constructed at or near the existing roadway elevation. In general, the area within the 
contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on-set of 
earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials

should be removed and properly disposed of off-site or disposed of in a designated area 
to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate subbase (ASB) materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, 
where necessary, to the pavement subgrade level (i.e., bottom of the AB or ASB layer).  
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend scarifying and re-preparing the soils as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit 
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities.

3.7 
Compaction Requirements 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Aggregate base course materials (a.k.a. aggregate for untreated base) 
required for the new pavements should conform to the requirements stipulated in 
Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction, 2005 (HSS). The aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and 
compacted to no less than 95 percent of the maximum density according to 
AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). 
Aggregate subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to 
the requirements stipulated in Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard 
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, 2005 (HSS). The aggregate subbase 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 
8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to no less than 95 percent of the maximum 
density according to AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basaltic 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined 
in accordance with AASHTO T209 (ASTM D2041). 
Asphaltic concrete (AC) material should be constructed in general accordance 
with Section 401 – Hot Mix Asphalt Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard 
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005) and subsequent amendments.

3.8 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
As previously mentioned, a permeable drainage layer was not incorporated into 
our pavement design due to the adjacent sections of Mahoe Street lacking a permeable 
base drainage layer. Therefore, it is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately 
and to maintain positive drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface 
water may be carried off the pavement quickly for proper discharge. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS

information obtained from the field borings and bulk samples. Variations of the 
subsurface conditions between and beyond the field borings and bulk samples may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to

the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods 
used.

depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. This data has 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it 
must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in seasonal rainfall, surface 
water runoff, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling six exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to 
depths of about 6.5 and 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-3, were collected 
for laboratory R-value testing. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid stem augers and PQ coring tools. The approximate 
locations of the drilled borings and bulk samples are shown on the Overall Site Plan, 
Plate 2, and the individual Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.6. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in our laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance 
with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering 
Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 
are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through 
A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth, and a 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected 
in-situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site soils. 
The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
The stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented on 
Plates B-2 through B-5. 
One set of Corrosion test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM 
G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed 
by our office and TestAmerica laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from 
our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-6. 
Three laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed on 
selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-9.
