INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Puuopae Replacement Bridge project located to the west of
the intersection of Puuopae Road and Kipapa Road in the area of South Olohena on the
island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed replacement bridge project These recommendations are
intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project involves the construction of a new single span bridge
supporting a two lane roadway across Kalama Stream as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 It is our understanding that the new bridge will replace the existing steel bridge
along Puuopae Road spanning across Kalama Stream The new bridge will have a
concrete deck supported at each end on seat type abutments The planned bridge will be
approximately 50 to 70 feet long 37 feet wide and approximately 12 feet above the

engineer structural loads on the order of approximately 30 to 55 kips per lineal foot for

footings will be at approximately 311 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Therefore we

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai and are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded from
groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with the
vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Kauai
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 3384 00 Page 6
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
In the area of the project alluvial soils have accumulated on the basalt rock
formation along Kalama Stream The alluvial deposits generally consist of clayey silts
sands gravels with some cobbles and boulders Some fills appeared to have been
placed in the area for the development of Puuopae Road

STREAM BED MATERIAL FOR SCOUR ANALYSIS
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying away
material from the bed and banks of streams Total Scour generally consists of three
components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local
Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as shape and
dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity of approach flow size and
gradation of stream bed material bed configuration etc
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristics of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material Ds is used to
calculate the depth of flow the flow velocity and fall velocity of the bed material In order
to evaluate the size and gradation of the stream bed material for scour depth analysis at
the project site a bulk sample of the stream bed material was retrieved for our laboratory
tests The stream bed material generally consists of dark brown silty sand with gravel A
sieve analysis with hydrometer test was performed on the retrieved stream bed soil
sample for grain size distribution of the material Based on the results of our laboratory
tests the median diameter of the stream bed material Ds is approximately
The results of our laboratory tests on the stream bed material are presented on Plate B 1
in Appendix B of this report and were provided to the project civil engineer for the scour
depth analysis
Based on the information provided to us a scour depth analysis was performed by
the project civil engineer for a 500 year flood Based on their analyses total scour depths
of 25 1 feet and 16 4 feet were obtained for the left east and right west abutments of
the Puuopae Replacement Bridge respectively The two scour depths correspond to
elevations of 278 9 and 287 6 feet MSL for the east and west abutments respectively

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our exploration the estimated scour depth and the structural loads
provided we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of 30 inch diameter
cast in place drilled shafts be used to support the proposed new bridge The drilled
shafts would extend through the estimated scour elevation and be embedded into the
dense basalt rock formation
We envision that site grading would consist of minor cuts and fills on the order of
less than 2 feet relative to the existing ground surface for the proposed project In
addition we anticipate that excavations on the order of 4 to 6 feet and subsequent
backfilling will be needed for construction of the new abutment walls

Drilled Shaft Foundations
As previously discussed a deep foundation system consisting of 30 inch diameter
drilled shafts extending through the estimated scour elevation into the basalt rock
formation may be used to support the proposed new bridge The cast in place drilled
shafts would derive vertical support only from adhesion between the drilled shaft and the
surrounding dense basalt The end bearing component of the drilled shafts has been
discounted due to the potential for caving in of the loose soft soils which would preclude
obtaining a clean bottom during construction
We recommend that all drilled shafts be embedded a minimum of 8 feet into the
basalt formation to derive the vertical support from adhesion between the drilled shaft and
the dense basalt An allowable compression load capacity of 200 kips per shaft may be
used for design of the drilled shafts based on an 8 foot basalt rock socket This value is
for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces Based on the data obtained from our field
exploration the top of the dense basalt formation is estimated at approximately 291 to
292 5 feet MSL Therefore a preliminary drilled shaft tip elevation of about 283 feet
MSL is recommended to develop the required compressive and lateral load capacity for
the subject shafts Drilled shafts should have a minimum center to center spacing of
three times the diameter to avoid capacity reduction due to group action
Uplift loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
dead load of the drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent
basalt For intermittent uplift forces an ultimate uplift capacity of 150 kips per shaft may
be used to resist uplift loads The structural capacity of the drilled shaft in tension should
be checked by the project structural engineer

Lateral Load Resistance
In general lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a
combination of the following 1 resistance of the drilled shaft soil system to deflect and
2 passive resistance of soils against the vertical faces of footing caps where applicable
Because of the anticipated depth of scour passive resistance of soil against the face of
footings should be neglected due to scour conditions

surrounding soil rock the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the shaft
and induced moment in the shaft Based on the subsoil conditions encountered we have
evaluated the lateral load resistance of the shafts

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will be due to compression of the drilled
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the dense basalt formation
Total drilled shaft settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch with
differential settlements between the abutments estimated to be less than inch We
believe a significant portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are
applied Post construction foundation settlements should be approximately 4 inch or
less
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures The load bearing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3384 00 Page 13
capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact between the shaft
and the surrounding soil rock Therefore proper construction techniques are important
The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into
the holes The subsoils at the bridge site consist predominantly of alluvial soils with
cobbles and boulders near the ground surface Drilling through the cobbles and boulders
may be slow and may require special tools In addition the drilled shafts are designed to
be embedded into the dense basalt formation therefore coring into the dense basalt
formation will be necessary
Due to the relatively shallow groundwater and sandy soils encountered cave in of
the material may occur during the drilling operation Therefore to reduce the potential for
caving in of the holes temporary casing may be needed The anticipated high
groundwater levels may also pose further construction difficulties since proper
observation of the sides and bottom of the shaft may not be possible Therefore a
representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to observe the drilling and installation
of drilled shafts during construction Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not
recommended because use of the drilling fluids may reduce the friction between the
drilled shaft and surrounding dense basalt
A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range concrete mix should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils and basalt
formation The concrete should be placed promptly after drilling within 24 hours to
reduce the potential for caving in The concrete should also be placed in a suitable
manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix Since
groundwater will likely be encountered in the drilled holes placement of concrete by
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3384 00 Page 14
tremie methods is recommended During concrete placement a minimum of 5 feet of
concrete head should be maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe

adjacent soils and any surcharge effects caused by loads adjacent to the abutments

In general areas that are to receive fills should be cleared of all vegetation and
deleterious materials The resulting grub spoil material should be disposed of properly
off site Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be filled with
engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction After clearing
and grubbing all subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 Test Methods Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The on site materials may be reused as a source of fill and backfill materials
provided that they are free of organic materials and contain no lumps or fragments
greater than 3 inches in greatest dimension Cobbles and boulders if encountered
should not be used as backfill material Where used as fill material the on site soils
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Backfills behind abutment walls should be compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction since the backfill is intended to support the
roadway

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 2 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings ranged from about 32 to 36 8 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis with hydrometer test ASTM D 422 was performed on the
stream bed material to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One unconfined compression test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate its strength characteristics Results of the test are
summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate its drained shear strength characteristics Results of the test are
presented on Plate B 2
One set of corrosion tests pH ASTM D 4972 and minimum Resistivity
ASTM G 57 was performed on a selected sample obtained during our field exploration
The test results are presented on Plate B 3
