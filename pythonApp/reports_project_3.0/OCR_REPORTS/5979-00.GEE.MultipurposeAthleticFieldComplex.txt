REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the
two prominent volcanoes the Wai anae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two the Wai anae
Volcano is the older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The
Ko olau Volcano rose above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the
younger of the two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly bedded
a a and pahoehoe lava flows which erupted from elongate and linear trending volcanic
vents associated with the prominent rift Zones of each of the volcanoes The Ko olau lavas
banked against the lava flows of the Waianae Volcano to form the Schofield Plateau of
the central interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains which form the western third
of the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt rock formations on the Island of Oahu The
Ko olau Mountains form the eastern two thirds of the island and include the rock layers
exposed along the extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of
Wai anae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet
under the tremendous weight of the rock In addition to the subsidence of the island the
force of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and slumping of
portions of the land mass to form the topography of today Erosion by streams and ocean
waves has continued to work on the exposed land surfaces to form the topography of the
present day
The coastal area of Laie lies within the Coastal Plain of Oahu and is to the
northeast of the Ko olau Mountain As a result much of the generally flat land area is
underlain by unconsolidated coastal sediments coralline silts and sands with pockets of
hard cemented sand dunes sandstone and coral limestone rock formation Progressing
toward the hills of the Ko olau Mountains the subsurface conditions gradually change to
reflect an increase in thickness of terrestrial sediments such as the alluvial soils derived
from the hills and valleys located to the southwest of the coastline The alluvial soils may
overlie the buried coral and sand deposits in the subsurface
The coral and limestone rock encountered buried in the subsurface often contain
locally occurring void and cavity structures originating during the deposition of the
calcareous deposits in shallow marine environments In addition some of the voids
develop following the coral and limestone deposition as a result of the slow percolation of

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 extending to depths of approximately 10 to
25 5 feet below the existing ground surface Generally Boring 3 4 and 10 were
drilled at the western portion of the site over the existing softball field while the remaining
borings were drilled over the rugby field The approximate boring locations are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on the Log Legend Plate 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests
are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
In general our field exploration encountered a surface fill layer on the order of
about 2 5 to 8 feet thick consisting of medium stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts
Over the existing softball area the fill layer was underlain by alluvium The alluvium
consists of silty clays and clayey silts extending to the maximum depth of the borings at
25 5 feet below the surface The alluvium is generally medium stiff to hard except in
Boring No 4 where soft alluvium was encountered from 14 to 21 feet below the surface

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography we anticipate that the grading work will generally
consist of minor cuts and fills of less than 2 feet thick The following grading items are
addressed in the succeeding subsections 1 Site Preparation 2 Fills and Backfills and
3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils shall be
properly disposed off site and or used in landscaping areas where appropriate
Prior to filling and backfilling the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of
12 inches moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For synthetic turf
utility field subgrades the minimum relative compaction should be 95 percent
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above Saturation
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and
poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill The excavated soft soils should be disposed properly off site
and or used in landscaping areas where appropriate Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Fills and Backfills
Based on the topography at the project site we anticipate minor cuts or fills may be
required for the proposed facility
General fill material if required should consist of low expansive materials less than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12 or higher and a swell potential of less than
1 percent in accordance with ASTM D 1883 The fill material should also be free of
vegetation and deleterious materials It should be noted that the general fill
requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Fill materials under the
building slabs and synthetic turf utility field should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork Geolabs should conduct observations and soil density
tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree
of compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of
moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be
noted that the moisture requirement of the fills and subgrades about 2 percent
above the optimum moisture is an important requirement for the use of on site
clayey soils
Building Foundation
We understand a 2 660 square foot field house will be constructed between the
proposed synthetic turf utility field and natural grass utility field It is designed to
accommodate restrooms locker rooms concessions and an option to integrate
bleacher seating for up to 250 Based on our field exploration the current location of the
proposed field house foundation has 2 5 feet of very stiff clay underlain by 8 feet of
medium dense coralline sand A very soft clay layer about 8 5 feet thick was
encountered below the sand layer
Based on the soil condition at the project site we believe that shallow spread
and or continuous footings may be used for support the proposed field house An
allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design of footings
bearing on the medium dense coralline sand This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces
Foundations should have a minimum width of 12 inches and should be
embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Footings
constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be embedded deep

Foundations next to utility trenches and or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or embedded as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Soft loose materials encountered at the slab or footing subgrade level should be
over excavated to expose the underlying dense firm materials and replaced with
engineered fill material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 44 may be used for footings bearing on the coralline sand Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around
the foundations are well compacted or undisturbed
We anticipate that a concrete slabs on grade will be used for the field house To
reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling of the subsoils we
recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to fill placement as discussed in
the Site Preparation section above and placing a minimum of 12 inches of
non expansive structural fill capping fill material below the building slab areas
In addition we recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill
consisting of No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slab to serve
as a Capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slab To reduce the
potential for moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the No 3 Fine gravel Flexible
floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can
better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended that the interior wall design
incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible ground
movements
Prior to the placement of the No 3 Fine gravel cushion fill the slab subgrades

moisture conditioning to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a

Settlement Monitoring Program
The project site might be subject to minor cut or fill of up to 2 feet Due to the
presence of soft clay pockets underlying the project site some ground settlements are
anticipated when the new fills are placed over the compressible soil deposits Therefore
we recommend placing the new fills as soon as practical to allow the anticipated ground
settlements to occur prior to construction of the footings on top of the fill
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend installing a minimum of
two settlement gauges at the field house location A qualified professional surveyor should
optically read the settlement gauges and transmit the readings to Geolabs for review in a
timely manner We recommend taking two readings minimum 24 hours apart for each
settlement gauge prior to site filling to establish a baseline Subsequent settlement gauge
readings should be taken on a weekly basis
Light Pole Design Consideration
Based on the information provided we understand the light poles might be installed
around the new complex field Based on our field exploration and laboratory analyses the
site is generally underlain by surface fill over alluvium and sandstone We recommend
using a_ single cast in place concrete drilled shaft to support each light pole
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support from friction
between the concrete shaft and the surrounding soils and minor contribution from the end
bearing
The drilled shaft will also provide lateral resistance Lateral load resistance is a
function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflection at the top of the shaft and the induced moment in the shaft The lateral load
analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded deep foundation program
originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection
and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral loads as a function of depth
