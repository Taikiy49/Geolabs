SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the New Reservoir situated at Waiaha in North Kona on the
Island of Hawaii This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses for the proposed reservoir project These recommendations are
intended for the design of foundations site grading and pavements only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report
1 2 Project Considerations
It is proposed to construct a water reservoir for potable water The
reservoir will be approximately 103 feet in inside diameter and approximately 36 feet in
height with a concrete floor slab walls and roof In addition a control building will be built
at the southern side of the new tank and a new asphalt concrete paved service road will
be provided The new water reservoir will replace two abandoned steel water tanks and
will be built at the northern end of the site almost at the same footprint of one of the tanks
to be demolished
The proposed reservoir tank project site is located at the Waiaha Well and
Reservoir Tank Site on the eastern side of the Old Mamalahoa Highway situated at
Waiaha in North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The general project area is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
We anticipate that the present site grade will be maintained therefore minimal
cuts will be required to attain the required finished grades for the proposed reservoir
and pertinent structures Based on the project plans provided the existing ground
surface elevation at the reservoir site is approximately 1542 0 feet above Mean Sea
Level MSL The finish floor elevation for the new water reservoir is anticipated to be
at approximately 1542 0 feet above mean sea level

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central part of the island and northwest of Mauna Loa The lava
formation encountered at the site appears to consist of pahoehoe flow which spread and
ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or
billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten
state and represent both intra flow cavities lava tubes and inter flow cavities blisters and
pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the
flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the fractures and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features

3 1 Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the on site basalt rock formation would provide suitable foundation support for the
proposed reservoir structure To provide more uniform support for the shallow
foundation system we recommend that the subsurface materials below the bottom of
the tank floor and foundations be over excavated and be replaced with at least
12 inches of base course The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
For design of foundations bearing on the 12 inch base course placed over basalt
rock formation we recommend that an allowable bearing pressure of up to
6 000 pounds per square foot psf be used This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces For design of the tank floor a modulus of subgrade reaction
of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
base course
Foundations and thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade If
foundations are located adjacent to a slope sloping down away from the foundations the
foundations should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 8 foot setback
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the foundations to the slope face
Where foundations are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
foundations should extend to a depth below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure
Due to the potential for presence of cavities in the basaltic lava flows in the vicinity
we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the foundation locations be
implemented during construction A representative from Geolabs Inc should observe
the probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the vicinity cavities and or voids may be present in the
basaltic lava flows To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from
collapse of the cavities below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity
probing and grouting at the foundation locations be conducted during construction The
cavity probing may be eliminated for the project if it was done during the construction of
the existing reservoirs
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and at 10 foot on center along the perimeter wall foundation The
probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet
below the planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be
drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity
and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with

Cut and Fill Slopes
We anticipate that the proposed cut slopes would generally expose the medium
hard to hard basalt formations encountered in the borings In general we believe
that cut slopes exposing the medium hard to hard basalt formations may be
designed with a slope inclination of or flatter Cut slopes should be
protected by appropriate slope planting or other means to reduce the potential for
erosion of the exposed materials as soon as possible after the slope faces are
exposed
Permanent fill slopes constructed using structural fill materials or imported fill
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design
slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In addition slope planting
should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the
finished slopes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 40 and 41 5 feet below
the existing ground surface respectively The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with rotary wash method of drilling coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
