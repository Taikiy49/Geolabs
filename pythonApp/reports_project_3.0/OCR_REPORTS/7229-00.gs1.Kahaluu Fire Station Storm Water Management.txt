SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Kahaluu Fire Station Storm Water Management project in 
Kahaluu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations 
resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These 
recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the storm water management 
improvements. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is on the southwest corner of the intersection of Waihee Road 
and Ahilama Road in Kahaluu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  We understand that the 
project consists of the following improvements:  
Installation of two seepage wells 
Installation of two underground infiltration basins 
Installation of one bio-retention basin and

130 cubic feet and a bio-retention basin with a minimum storage volume of 110 cubic 
feet are planned for the site.  The infiltration basins are near the southeast and 
northwest corners of the site.  The bio-retention basin is at the front of the site adjacent

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls, which are then transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium.  Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium.  In general, 
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit 
large volumes of water for very short durations.  Because of this, transport of sediments is 
intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture 
of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines.  When the erosion base levels change, 
these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes 
begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the 
material. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the 
coarse-grained portion of the sediment into a poorly-consolidated sedimentary rock, or 
conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors 
and upstream in headwaters.  This continued erosion generates material which is 
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits.  Depending on the local 
base level and rate of transport, these new sediments are generally transient in terms of 
geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of 
older, partially consolidated deposits.

The project site is situated near the base of the Koolau Pali, a high cliff that 
extends along most of the windward side of the Koolau Mountain Range on the Island of 
Oahu.  The climate along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average annual 
rainfall.  Following the main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch, stream erosion, landslide and deep weathering of 
the basalt rock became the dominant agents affecting the morphology of the area. 
The project site is generally underlain by alluvial soils derived from large-scale 
erosion and landslide along the base of the Koolau forming colluvial aprons that were 
subsequently transported by runoff to create the alluvial fans generally forming the 
present day topography of the region.  
Based on the USDA Soil Conservation Service and the University of Hawaii Soil 
Survey of the Islands of Kawai, Oahu, Maui, Molokai and Lanai (1972), the project site 
lies in an area classified under the Lolekaa Series (LoB). This series consists of 
well-drained soils on fans and terraces on the windward side of the island of Oahu. 
These soils developed in old, gravelly colluvium and alluvium.  Permeability in this type 
of soil is described in the survey to be moderately rapid. Runoff is slow and the erosion 
hazard is slight. Soft weathered gravel is common in the subsoil.

Existing Site Conditions

area of approximately 18,220 square feet. About 11,030 square feet of the project site

stands at the corner of the property where Waihee Road and Ahilama Road intersect.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff older alluvial deposits 
consisting of silty clays and clayey silts with some sand extending to about 11.2 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled 
borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Seepage wells, underground infiltration basins, and a bio-retention basin are 
planned for the project.  Based on the results of the field infiltration testing, it appears 
that these storm water management systems are feasible for the project site.  
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design 
recommendations are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Subsurface Infiltration 
We understand that the project includes several retention and disposal systems 
for the on-site management of storm water runoff.  These systems include seepage 
wells, underground infiltration basins, and a bio-retention basin.  In order to obtain 
subsurface infiltration information in support of the design of these systems, we 
conducted falling head infiltration tests in each of the borings shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
These infiltration tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009).  These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering borings to selected test 
depths between 5 about 11.2 feet below the existing ground surface for the infiltration 
basins and seepage wells.  Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid 
casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the soil 
exposed on the bottom of the boring.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test

Based on the infiltration testing, the infiltration rates ranged from about 1.2 to

We anticipate that the seepage wells will be constructed by drilling a hole down 
to the design bottom elevation.  Due to the potential for smearing of the sidewalls of the

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Borings B-1 through B-3, extending to depths of 5 to 
11.2 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made to the soil classification 
in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key, 
Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed

Infiltration Tests 
In order to provide information on the surface infiltration rates of the on-site soils, 
we conducted three falling head infiltration tests at the boring locations shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  These tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The procedure used for the infiltration consisted of augering boreholes to depths 
of about 5 to 11.2 feet below the existing ground surface.  After reaching the test depth, 
a solid PVC pipe, or well casing, was installed in the borehole flush with the bottom so 
that water could only infiltrate into the soil through the exposed bottom soil. 
Water was placed into the open pipe and allowed to pre-saturate the soil prior to 
conducting the infiltration test.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with approximately 24 inches of water and 
taking periodic readings over a one hour trial period.
