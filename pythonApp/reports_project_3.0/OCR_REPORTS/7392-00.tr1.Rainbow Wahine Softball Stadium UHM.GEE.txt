SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Rainbow Wahine Softball Stadium Phase 2A project located 
at the University of Hawaii at Manoa on Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of pole 
foundations, utilities, localized low areas, and earthwork only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
Based on provided information, the Phase 2A project will include new foundation 
supports for safety netting, the relocation of an existing 48-inch drain line, and 
correction of low areas in the outfield. 
We understand that an existing 48-inch drain line is located below the bleachers 
along the third base line.  It is planned to install a new 48-inch drain line along Kalele 
Road that will bypass the existing drain line below the bleachers.  The grade within the 
softball field is about 4 to 5 feet higher than the area adjacent to the existing roadway. 
We understand that there are two areas in the outfield (left and center fields) that 
contain low spots that appear to be sinking.  It is desired to determine the cause of the 
low areas and to provide remedial measures.

following tasks and efforts:

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The 
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years 
ago. 
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
northwest to southeast trending rift zones. These lavas consist of interbedded 
pahoehoe and a’a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form. “A’a” is the 
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flow typified by a rough, jagged, spinose, clinkery 
surface. “Pahoehoe” is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth, 
billowy or ropy surfaces. After the cessation of the main shield-building phase of 
Koolau Volcano, there was a long period of erosion. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Along the crest of the ridge along the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley, there are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. 
When these vents were active, one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down 
the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor. The lava flows ponded 
and filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley. The ponding allowed the lava to densify as 
it cooled forming dense massive rock. The new lava flows from these vents blocked 
Manoa Stream and forced the stream to the east between the East-West Center and 
Waahila Rise. These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili District, where the basalt 
rocks were quarried in the past. Reef limestone related to the Waimanalo stand of the 
sea underlies the basalt at the former Moiliili Quarry. Man-made fills were placed in the 
area after termination of operations at the Moiliili Quarry and brought the project site to 
its current conditions.

2.1 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located at the softball stadium at the University of Hawaii at 
Manoa’s (UHM) lower campus on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The softball stadium is 
bounded by the Dillingham Tennis Courts to the north, Kalele Road to the southwest, 
and a service road to the southeast as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. In the early- to 
mid-part of the 20th century, the area was used for a quarry. As a result, vertical cut rock 
faces of about 50 feet are located in the northern vicinity of the project site.   
Based on the topographic information provided, the softball field has existing 
ground surface elevations of about +16 to +19 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The ground 
surfaces surrounding the softball field range from about +14 to +15 feet MSL. 
2.2 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths 
ranging from 10.0 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, the 
subsurface conditions at the low areas were explored by probing four locations, 
designated as Probing Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 8.6 to 10.8 feet 
below existing grades. A bulk sample of the near surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, at 
Boring No. 6 was obtained to evaluate the characteristics of the near-surface soils. The 
approximate boring, probe, and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The borings within the softball stadium (Boring Nos. 1 through 5) generally 
encountered fill overlying alluvium. The fill was encountered at the surface extending to 
a maximum depth of about 8.5 feet below existing grades and consisted of medium 
dense to very dense silty sands and gravels, and stiff to hard silty clay. It should be 
noted the probes encountered 6 to 12 inches of soft fill material at the surface in the 
localized low areas. Beneath the fill, the subsurface exploration encountered alluvium to 
the maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet beneath the ground surface. The 
alluvium consisted of cobbly boulders, stiff to very stiff silts and clays, and loose to very 
dense clayey gravel.

3.1.1 Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the drilled shaft, allowable deflection at the top of 
the drilled shaft, and the induced moment in the drilled shaft. The lateral load 
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program 
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear 
“p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed 
using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.

conditions at the site, the results of our analyses are summarized in the following 
table. The project structural engineer should verify the drilled shaft structural

Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered

3.1.3 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
In general, the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the 
contractor's method of installation and construction procedures. The following 
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled 
shaft foundations. 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on the 
friction between the shaft and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper construction 
techniques especially during the drilling operations are important. The contractor 
should exercise care in drilling the shaft hole and in placing concrete into the drilled 
hole. 
It should be noted that cobbles and boulders were encountered in the drilled 
borings within the depths of the drilled shafts. Therefore, some difficult drilling 
conditions should be anticipated. The drilled shaft subcontractor should have the 
appropriate equipment and tools to drill through these types of natural obstructions, 
where encountered. The drilled shaft subcontractor should demonstrate that the 
proposed drilling equipment will be capable of installing the drilled shaft to the 
recommended depth and dimension. 
Based on the estimated length of the drilled shafts, groundwater may be 
encountered in the drilled holes during the shaft installation. Therefore, concrete 
placement by tremie methods is recommended during construction of the drilled 
shafts in lieu of free fall methods.  The concrete should be placed in a suitable 
manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled 
hole. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should be used 
to provide close contact between the drilled shaft and the surrounding soils. The 
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for 
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix. In addition, the concrete 
should be placed promptly after drilling (within 24 hours after drilling of the holes) to 
reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled hole.

Localized Low Areas 
We were unable to determine the exact cause of the localized low areas on the

3.3.3 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site soils may be used as a source of general fill 
materials, provided that deleterious materials such as vegetation are removed and 
over-sized materials greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension are screened. In 
the event that imported fill materials are needed for the project construction, 
imported fill materials should consist of select granular fill material, such as crushed 
coralline and/or basaltic materials. The materials should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
3.3.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Select 
granular fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Mechanical 
compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials. 
Compaction efforts by water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed. 
3.4 
Utility Trenches 
The project includes relocation of an existing 48-inch drain line. In general, good 
construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the 
trenches for the proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the method and 
equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. The excavation should comply with the applicable local, state, and 
federal safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring 
design and installation. Trench shoring and bracing also should conform to the 
appropriate health and safety requirements.

In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. 
Free-draining granular materials such as No. 3B Fine gravel also should be used for the 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support 
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the 
potential for pipe damage. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the 
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material and placement could result 
in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of approved on-site soils 
(maximum particle size of 3 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be 
placed in maximum 8-inch level loose thickness, and mechanically compacted to not 
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future 
ground subsidence.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
six exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending down to depths 
of about 10.0 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with 
a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid-stem augers and coring tools. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
O.D. standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. 
modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that

uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Core Recovery may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Core Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as Core Recovery = 
74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs 
of Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth, and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate C-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
The fines content of the sample tested (percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve) is 
indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of 
the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C-2. 
Two Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
rock core samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the underlying 
basalt formation.  The test results are presented on Plate C-3. 
One laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed 
on a bulk soil sample to evaluate the support characteristics of the soils. The test results 
are presented on Plate C-4.
