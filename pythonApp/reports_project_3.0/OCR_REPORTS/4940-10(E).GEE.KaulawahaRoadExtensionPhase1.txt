The project site is located in the Makaha Valley area in the District of Waianae on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed roadway extension generally traverses over
open land extending from the intersection with Kaulawaha Road near the Board of Water
Supply property on the west to Ala Hema Street on the southeast as shown on the Site

elevations within the new roadway extension alignment generally range between about
10 and 26 feet Mean Sea Level MSL

Selected soil samples from the field explorations were tested in the laboratory with
an emphasis on evaluating the moisture density swelling potential and strength
characteristics The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples
obtained from the field explorations
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937

presented on Plates 11 through 13 of this report
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
In general our field explorations disclosed that the project site may be underlain by
highly expansive clays over coralline sands and gravel at shallow depths Based on a
review of the geology and our experience in the area we anticipate that the subsoils
underlying the proposed roadway alignment could range from Mokuleia clay consisting of
recent alluvium of high plasticity to coral deposits and consolidated terrestrial sediments in
a very dense state with cementation Laboratory CBR tests conducted on the Mokuleia
clays indicate the soils exhibit high swelling potential with relatively low CBR values when
subjected to moisture fluctuations

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of
pavements and site preparation are presented in the following sections of this report
Pavement Design
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing performed for the project
we believe that the majority of the proposed roadway alignment connecting
Kaulawaha Road and Ala Hema Street is underlain by highly expansive clayey soils
with localized areas exposing coralline materials Laboratory testing performed on the
clayey soils generally indicated a relatively low CBR value of less than 5 and a swell of
over 10 percent Based on the field explorations and laboratory tests the following
pavement sections are recommended
Pavement Design Non Expansive Granular Subgrade Soils
2 5 Inches Asphalt Concrete
8 0 Inches Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
10 5 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
Pavement Design Expansive Clayey Subgrade Soils
2 5 Inches Asphalt Concrete
8 0 Inches Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
18 0 Inches Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
28 5 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
Where expansive clayey soils are encountered at or near the bottom of the
base course layer the pavement subgrade should be over excavated to the coral layer to
provide the 18 inch aggregate subbase layer as recommended above The
over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If coralline deposits are exposed at or near
the bottom of the base course layer the subgrade should be proof rolled with a minimum
10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight passes to help detect near surface cavities
and or loose zones prior to the pavement construction The aggregate base course and
aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades for the
proposed roadway improvements we anticipate that site grading work consisting of cuts
and fills up to about 6 feet thick will be required for the project construction In general
the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at
the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious material and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future
improvements should be over excavated to expose firm and or stiff material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Existing structures and pavements that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled
with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be
removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with granular fill
material moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch
level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition future pavement areas should be
excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the base
course or subbase layer In areas where expansive clay soils are encountered at the
pavement subgrade level the pavement subgrade should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density If coralline deposits are exposed at the pavement subgrade
level the subgrade should be proof rolled by a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a
minimum of eight passes to help detect near surface cavities and or loose zones prior to
pavement construction
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately
25 square feet of subgrade area

Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be scarified and re prepared as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should meet the
requirements of Sections 30 and 31 of the City and County Standard Specifications
The materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Imported fill materials below the subbase and base course layers if required
should consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or
basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger
than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition the material should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or
less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The materials
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It
is important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding
should not be allowed anywhere on the site during or after construction Where
concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we
recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below
the base and subbase course layers to reduce migration of landscape water into the
pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect
