INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a preliminary geotechnical
engineering investigation performed for the subject project The general
location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
soil conditions along the proposed causeway alignment and to develop
soil parameters for the foundation design of the 4480 foot long bridge
structure This investigation also included the related approach roads
realignment of Saratoga Boulevard and abutments at Ford Island and
Halawa
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1247 00 Page 2
The scope of the investigation which was performed in general
accordance with our revised proposal dated January 27th and our proposal
for additional investigation dated May 2 1984 included a review of
available geologic and soil survey literature and soil reports of the
general area field reconnaissance seismic reflection surveys electric
cone penetrometer testing drilling of thirteen 13 onshore and nine 9
offshore borings laboratory soils testing engineering analysis of the
field and laboratory data and report preparation
The main objectives of the investigation are
1 to determine the thickness of soft material stiff underlying
alluvium and rock formation along the bridge alignment area to
evaluate potential bearing materials pile capacity and range
of pile lengths
2 to assess the shear strength and settlement characteristics of
both the soft harbor mud and the stiff alluvium as they would
govern the pile capacity
3 to evaluate possible under consolidation of the soft harbor
muds and potential pile downdrag
4 to determine allowable lateral pile capacities
W O 1247 00 Page 3
5 to determine if competent coral or tuff deposits would be
encountered near the shoreline areas and thereby shorten the
length of piles and possibly increase the pile capacity
6 to evaluate the characteristics of the subsoils to determine if
special splices or pile driving procedures would be required
7 to determine where competent bearing soils adequate for
abutment support are located at the Ford Island and Halawa
Abutment sites and
8 to evaluate the soil conditions for grading design of the
approacn roads

METHODOLOGY OF INVESTIGATION
General
Available topographical plans geological and soil literature and
relevant existing technical reports were used in a preliminary office
study to attempt to identify pertinent geological features and to assist
in the planning of the subsequent field investigation
Field investigation included seismic reflection surveys in the
areas within the general offshore alignment area electric cone
penetrometer testing and drilling and sampling of borings

Laboratory tests were carried out to classify and estimate the
engineering properties of the various soil strata These data were
assessed quantitatively and applied to subsequent foundation
recommendations in order to determine the bearing support of the
underlying soils
Drilling and Sampling
Borings were drilled along the proposed alignment to provide
pertinent geotechnical data for the approach roads bridge and abutment
foundation design recommendations
The land borings drilled for the approach and abutments on both the
Halawa and Ford Island ends of the causeway were drilled with
truck mounted drilling rigs utilizing auger rotary wash and rock coring
methods Photograph 2
The offshore borings were drilled with either a skid rig mounted on
a wooden platform raft utilizing 55 gallon drums for flotation or a
truck mounted drill rig on a steel shelled landing craft Photographs 3
and 4 The offshore drilling was coordinated with the Pearl Harbor Port
Services to minimize interference with the many watercrafts utilizing the
harbor
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1247 00 Page 6
During the course of the investigation it became apparent that the
thickness of the very soft harbor sediments would be a major controlling
factor in the bridge foundation design Therefore additional borings
were drilled within the thick and soft harbor sediment area to further
define the horizontal and vertical profiles as well as soil engineering
properties of this layer
Detailed description on drilling and sampling are presented in
Appendix A of this report
Electric Cone Penetrometer Testing
It was very difficult to obtain high quality undisturbed soil
samples from the upper harbor sediments due to its very soft consistency
Therefore electric cone penetrometer testing was conducted in the harbor
sediments to measure its in situ strength
Detailed description on the electric cone penetrometer testing
procedure and the results are presented in Appendix B
Geophysical Exploration
Seismic reflection surveys were conducted along the offshore
portions of the alignment to complement the data obtained by the offshore
borings The surveys were conducted after the first few borings were
drilled in order to provide a reference profile which could be correlated
to the seismic reflection survey readings
W O 1247 00 Page 7
It was hoped that the survey could provide subsurface information
in order to optimize the location of the remaining borings by locating
critical soil layers and abnormalities
The survey was somewhat successful in locating the underlying
basalt and very stiff alluvium layers However it was unable to provide
information within the deep harbor sediment area since the acoustic wave
could not penetrate through the organic harbor sediments
The seismic reflection survey was performed by ECO Systems
Management Associates Inc of San Diego California under the
observation of our geophysicist Details regarding the survey and the
interpretation of the data are presented in Appendix C
GEOLOGIC SETTING OF PEARL HARBOR
The origin of Pearl Harbor has been discussed in almost every
treatise involving the geology of the Island of Oahu Various authors
have attributed its origin to a multitude of different processes
Stearns and Vaksvik believed that the Pearl Harbor Lochs are drowned
river valleys as did Macdonald and Abbott This belief was further
supported by a masters degree thesis by K Munro J Pollack felt that
Pearl Harbor is wholly of marine origin and that the lochs are the result
of a lack of growth of coral materials due to inhibition of fresh water
entering a lagoonal environment Findings from our investigation appear
to confirm that the Pearl Harbor Lochs are drowned valleys which were
previously cut through older terrigenous and marine sediments
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1247 00 Page 8
The stratigraphic history of Pearl Harbor is quite complex due to
its large areal extent the large number of streams and drainageways
entering it and a complex series of sea level changes The base of the
stratigraphic profile is Koolau Basalt which underlies the project area
in the Pearl Harbor vicinity
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main
shield building activity ceased approximately 2 5 million years ago
Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has
subsided up to 1200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow
on the southern coast of Oahu forming a broad bay with a barrier reef
across its mouth The growth of the reef essentially kept pace with the
rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind the barrier reef
and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many eustatic sea
level changes as a result of wide spread glaciation in the continental
areas of the world As the great continental glaciers accumulated the
level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the
oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted the sea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1247 00 Page 9
level rose because more water was available The land masses remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were
eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in
stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level
The sequence and ages of these fluctuations are quite complex and
not fully known The estimated significant sea level stands are
tabulated on Plate 3 and the postulated fluctuations with geological
time are plotted on Plate 4
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments in the head of the old bay accumulation of
reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may affect
the deposits of limestone and maris within the predominantly lagoonal
sedimentary unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys through the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials
and lagoonal deposits and induration of the reef deposits In addition
the terrestrial erosion deposited the alluvial soil units under relatively
high energy conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1247 00 Page 10
During periods of no significant sea level changes the continued
stream action extended alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited
alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
The geologic history of the Pearl Harbor area has been further
complicated by the deposition of more recent explosive pyroclastic
Materials from vents of the Salt Lake Volcanics of the Honolulu Series
These deposits originated from post erosional volcanic events which were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
The eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the
interaction of groundwater with the rising magma which resulted in steam
explosions that expelled large quantities of pyroclastic materials such
as ash and cinder These deposits of pyroclastic materials have
consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as mudrock The oldest
of those eruptions at Aliamanu caused a diversion of Halawa Stream
towards the north and into an area of what are now the southeast lochs
during the Kaena 95 feet high stand of the sea
The remaining two events Salt Lake and Makalapa were essentially
simultaneous and have been assigned to the stand of the sea which was at
between 40 and 60 feet below present
Those last two eruptions caused further diversion of Halawa Stream
and deposited a mantle of tuff over much of the Shipyard and Submarine
Base areas of Pearl Harbor
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 11
As shown on Plate 4 a relatively rapid rise in sea level is
postulated 15 000 years ago During that process the deep valleys in
the Pearl Harbor were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so sea level
has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in the low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environment of Pearl Harbor resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor
sediments in the lochs and channel areas
Land reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Pearl Harbor area to its present form A great many of those
projects were done in 1940 s to handle fleet activities during World War
II For those projects expediency took precedence over quality and many
of the resulting fills are of poor quality

subsurface materials consisted mainly of thick alluvial and lagoonal soil
deposits that are underlain by Koolau basalt rock at depths ranging from

stratigraphic units with minor sub units within The engineering
characteristics of tnese units appear to be reasonably consistent for

The basalt rock which was encountered in Boring 4 at 219 feet
and Boring 11 at 325 feet below MLLW consisted of moderately
weathered vesicular basalt with close spacing of joints It is
anticipated that various types of volcanic rock components
such as clinker layers and lava tubes could be encountered
Between these two boring locations the depths to basalt

reflection survey data conducted over the water The data

the top of the basalt encountered in Borings 4 and 11 Based

The Koolau basalt is the aquifer material of the Pearl Harbor
Artesian Basin which is one of the major developed groundwater
bodies supplying fresh water for the Island of Oahu A
fresh water artesian head of about 17 feet above MLLW was
encountered in both Borings 4 and 11 at about the top of Koolau
basalt unit
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1247 00 Page 14
The aquifer is the permeable lava rock while the upper
confining member caprock is the relatively impermeable
alluvial and lagoonal deposits There is no lower confining
member as is the usual case in a basal fresh water lens At
the bottom the fresh water level is confined by salt water
according to the Ghyben Herzberg Lens Theory which is based on
the principle that fresh water floats on salt water in coastal
regions This effect of the ratio of densities of fresh and
salt water causes the salt water to be pushed downward about 40
feet below sea level for every foot that the fresh basal water
table rises above sea level Therefore the sub sea level
fresh water body may be more than 600 feet below sea level
Structurally the Koolau basalt is the strongest of all the
soil units encountered within the study area However its use
for foundation support in the Pearl Harbor area is severly
limited by the considerable depths to reach the top of the
basalt and the artesian conditions expected at these depths
These conditions will increase the difficulty in construction
and consequently the project costs Although the potential
impact of breaching the caprock seal may only be a temporary
condition the long term effects would have to be studied
further

Old Lagoonal Deposits
The old lagoonal deposit unit consists of marine or lagoonal
sediments which have been desiccated and consolidated into very
stiff to hard gray clayey silts In the project area this
unit overlies the top of the old alluvial unit and in cross
section has a shape that resembles a wedge along the causeway
alignment The thickness of this unit tapers from about 120
feet at the Ford Island abutment area to about 40 feet at the
Halawa abutment area
The lower boundary of this unit rests on the undulating
terraced erosional surface of the old alluvium The upper
boundary of the old lagoonal unit appears to form a relatively
flat horizon along the westerly two thirds of the offshore
area However the upper boundary of this unit varies
significantly in the easterly one third of the offshore area
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1247 00 Page 17
The relatively level upper boundary probably represents the
corresponding stand of sea level when this unit was deposited
The variations along the easterly side probably represent the
results of stream erosion closer to the mountain area during
subsequent lower stands of the sea
The old lagoonal unit consists almost exclusively of clayey
silts of rather uniform texture and grain size with occasional
shell fragments Various shades of gray colors in this unit
may be indicative of a chemically reducing environment The
upper portion of this unit is generally brown or mottled brown
gray suggesting that the lagoonal soil may have undergone
oxidation process from desiccation and invasion of alluvium
into desiccated cracks The lower zones of this unit
particularly at 160 to 200 foot depths below MLLW in Borings
8 through 11 are a very dark gray color indicating that the
reducing environment continued where the groundwater may have
stagnated even though the sea level might have dropped 350 feet
below the present sea level during the Kahipa Mamala low stand

Young Alluvium
The young alluvium as identified in this report is different
from the old alluvium by its generally higher sand contents
In addition this unit is located above and separated from the
older alluvium unit by the old desiccated and over consolidated
lagoonal soil unit
The young alluvium consists mainly of stiff to hard brown to
dark brown clayey silt with varying amounts of sand and
infrequent pockets of gravel and cobbles due to a higher energy
depositional environment The sand or gravel particles in this
alluvium unit area generally decomposed into brownish saprolite
particles from the oxidization process in the subaerial
depositional environment
Included in this unit are subunits of localized thin layers
about 10 feet of lagoonal marine sediments consisting mostly
of very stiff to hard gray clayey silts These gray silts are
identified as lagoonal facies subunits of the young alluvial
unit It is also possible that these lagoonal facies reflect a
brief stand of the sea at each corresponding level The
primary gray color in this subunit is probably due to its
chemically reducing depositional environment

Coral Algal Deposits
The coral reef and related deposits encountered at shallow
depths are probably the youngest units in the stratigraphic
assemblage in the project area and have continued to accumulate
even through the present This unit consists of fossil
remnants of coral algal reefs and their related detrital debris
deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the
dominant portion of any coral rock assemblage
The coral algal unit was encountered in Borings 15 through 18
on the Ford Island land area at relatively shallow depths of 4
to 10 feet These coral rocks are cemented coral debris
consisting of gravel sand and silt sized clastic fragments

dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented and or
uncemented fine sands and silts The coral algal unit on Ford

underlain by old alluvium and lagoonal deposits as well as
older coral algal units at depth

youngest of the natural materials in the project area They
consist of volcanic ash which has consolidated or hardened to
form a medium hard rock called tuff that resembles siltstone or
sandstone locally called mudrock The tuff rocks are
usually found overlying the coralline deposits or young
alluvium units Although not encountered in the borings this
rock unit can be observed along Kamehameha Highway at the
eastern side ot Pearl Harbor

may be relatively thin or missing at the site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The main feature of the project is the bridge crossing from Ford
Island to Halawa landing as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The bridge
will be supported on piers spaced at 140 feet on centers with pier caps
at the water line
The approacn on the Ford Island side will be an on grade roadway to
the abutment while the approach on the Halawa landing side will be
structurally supported
Based on our findings the main soil condition affecting the
causeway bridge structure is the deep soft harbor sediment in the eastern
half of the channel as shown on the Generalized Soil Profile Plate 5
These soft sediments offer only very limited resistance to
transient and short lived loading such as wind or seismic force and
cannot be relied on to provide resistance to long term loading The
combination of tnis soft soil condition and the relatively long
unsupported height of the pile from the mudline up to the pile cap up to

APPENDIX B
Electric Cone Penetration Tests
The cone penetrometer test consists of hydraulically pushing
a steel cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 10 square
centimeters connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 150 square
centimeters into the ground
The tip resistance and side friction resistance required for
penetration are measured by strain gages within the instrument and are
recorded versus the depth of penetration
The values of the tip resistance and friction ratio sleeve
friction over tip resistance can be correlated to soil strength and
classification
The electric cone penetration tests were performed in the
offshore borings 20 and 21
The results and interpretation are plotted on Plates B 1
through B 4

A INTRODUCTION
Geophysical surveys along the proposed bridge route between the
Naval Reserve and Ford Island were performed by ECOsystems Management
Associates Inc ECO M under contract to Geolabs Honolulu Hawaii
Results from these surveys are presented in this report
Mobilization field work and demobilization were accomplished
during 1984 During this period weather and sea
conditions were considered moderate except for occasional rain
showers and gusty winds The vessel used for this project was a 23
foot utility boat owned and operated by Sea Engineering
The area surveyed is approximately 0 5 x and lies between
the Halawa Stream and Ford Island within the East Loch see Plates l
and 2 The seismic reflection equipment utilized for this survey
included a 1000 Joule Boomer system with a towed hydrophone array and
a sub bottom profiler The two systems were operated
independently over two separate grids The failed during
preliminary tests and another transceiver had to be obtained from the
mainland thus delaying its operation Both the Boomer and
recordings were made with an EPC model 3200 recorder at 1 2 and
sweep rates respectively
Survey positioning was accomplished with a Motorola Mini Ranger
III The stated accuracy of this system is 3m The navigation pre
plots provided by ECO M were not usable because the shore station MON
USN 134 was obstructed by a row of trees therefore the profiles were
collected by running compass courses Navigation fixes were taken at
30 sec intervals for both the Boomer and the survey
Several unforeseen problems arose during field operations which
should be noted The electronic navigation was adversely affected by
reflections from large ships moored at the OIL refueling dock 74
of the 475 total shotpoints had to be revised or deleted Another
problem was created by traffic from ferries and tour boats crossing
the survey area Numerous lines had to be aborted or restarted
Engine and propeller noise from these craft saturated the hydrophones
which masked the seismic signals 200 500 foot sections of record
were affected upon each passage Also an intermittent electrical
short in the preamp section of the hydrophone streamer resulted in
_ periodic data loss
However in spite of these difficulties a sufficient number of
survey lines were obtained to provide adequate coverage within the
survey area
And finally the most significant factor affecting data
acquisition was geologic in nature and was discovered during early
testing of the Boomer system Sub bottom penetration over large
portions of the survey area were found to be masked by the near
surface sediments An areal view of the masked areas are shown on
Plate 1 One possible explanation for this phenomenon is that a
gaseous section exists within the recent lagoon muds The character
of these seismic recordings is typical of the gas charged sediments
found along the deltas bordering the Gulf of Mexico It seems
plausible that similar stratigraphic conditions could exist within
Pearl Harbor in that local streams are capable of providing abundant
quantities of organic material from the lush mountaineous regions of
the island The masking effect is again discussed at the end of
Section B
B GEOLOGICAL AND GEOPHYSICAL INTERPRETATION
The seismic records show a relatively smooth flat seafloor
except across dredging channels and over areas adjacent to the exposed
reef complex Data from 7 indicate that the surficial
sediments within the channel consist of soft grey clayey silt Below
this lagoon mud the logs indicate a complex history of alternating
alluvial and lagoonal sedimentary deposition to depths greater than
300 feet This suggests that the embayment has been subjected to the
dynamic geologic processes that accompany rapid changes in sea level
The stratigraphic horizons delineated on profiles A A and B B
Figs 1 2 are prominent seismic events that appeared to correlate
with major changes in lithology presented on the boring logs
Primarily these horizons represent transitions to alluvial materials
with higher sand and gravel content A point to bear in mind when
interpreting the Boomer data is that a stratigraphic unit must have
fairly uniform elastic properties over a thickness of at least 3 feet
to be detected
The surficial sediment section is shown to vary in thickness from
100 160 feet however the base of this section should not be
interpreted as the bottom of the recent lagoon mud The appearance of
seismic layering within this section is good evidence that the entire
unit is not homogeneous The top of the Old Alluvium Unit 1 is
interpreted to consist of mostly gravel materials This horizon as
well as the volcanic basement interface appears to be an erosional
unconformity with the overlying sections having been deposited as a
sequence of onlapping beds
The Isopach map shown on Plate 1 depicts the thickness of
sediments between the seafloor and volcanic basement The contours
indicate that the overburden increases gradually from northeast to
southwest where the thicknesses range from respectively
All depth measurements were based on an average velocity of 5250
for the entire sediment column This value was
derived from velocity measurements made on core samples from Halawa
B 4
The survey lines are shown on Plate 3 Sub bottom
information depicted on these recording is generally limited to one
shallow reflective horizon below a nearly transparent surface layer
The thicknesses of these most recent lagoonal muds vary from
with little or no mud over areas where the Boomer showed good
penetration There appears to be a direct correlation between the
presence of this mud layer and the masked zones that are evident on the
Boomer records Also the areal shape of this boundary is perplexing
in that it seems to be more closely related to an easterly extension of
the shoaled reef complex than to channel topography In conclusion
there doesn t appear to be a definitive answer to the masking
phenomenon however these relationships suggest that the
transmission of sound through these sediments is somehow controlled
by lateral variations in composition and structure

APPENDIX D
Laboratory Testing
The triaxial and direct shear strength results for the stiff
underlying soil are summarized on Plates D 1 through D 3 along with the
design Mohr failure envelope selected for our analysis
Moisture content Atterberg Limit and unit weight
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the
classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of the
moisture content and unit weight tests are presented on the log of
borings at the appropriate sample depths The Atterberg Limits are
summarized on Plates D 4 through D 10
Five laboratory CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the suitability of the soil for
pavement support The test results are summarized on Plates D 4 through
D 10
Two moisture density tests modified Proctor were performed
on bulk samples of the soils to determine their compaction characteris
tics The test results are summarized on Plates D 4 through D 10
Four specific gravity tests were performed on typical samples
of the underlying soil The test results are presented on Plates D 1l
through D 10
Eight sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of the
underlying highly weathered tuff to evaluate its grain size distribution
The test results are summarized on Plates D 4 through D 10
Twenty eight triaxial compression tests including
consolidated undrained with pore pressure measurement and unconsolidated
undrained were performed on undisturbed samples of the underlying soils
to determine the shear strength parameters of the soils The test
results are plotted on Plates D ll through D 38
Thirty four direct shear tests were performed on saturated
samples of the various soil types to evaluate the shear strength
parameters of the soils The test results are plotted on Plates D 39
through D 72

APPENDIX E
Lateral Pile Analysis
Lateral pile analyses for this investigation were mainly
performed utilizing the LPILE computer program and were cross checked by
utilizing the COM622 program suggested in DM 7 2 Both programs were run
by an in house IBM PC micro computer
Input from both soil and structural considerations are
required The structural input includes
1 Pile dimensions
2 material properties of pile and
3 structural loadings e g axial and lateral
loading and or bending moments
The soil input consists of representative p y curves at
selected depths along the pile The p y curves represent the
relationship between the lateral loading on and lateral displacement of
the soil The relationship is non linear usually given as
P Ct y 804
where C is a constant at a chosen depth and is a function of soil stress
strain parameters is a positive real number between 0 and 1
Our analysis was carried out for piles embedment in soil
conditions represented by the 4 soil catagories in the harbor area and
those at the Ford Island and Halawa Abutment area The magnitude of the
lateral loadings was varied in order to study its effect on the amount of
lateral pile deflection A typical computer print out is presented on
Plates E 1 through E 5
The curves of lateral deflection at the top of pile versus
lateral loading applied at the same location are presented on Plates E 6
through E 16
Curves indicating the calculated pile deflection versus
embedment depth for various size piles for each soil category are
presented on Plates E 17 through E 25

APPENDIX F
m3 Vertical Pile Capacity Analysis
The vertical capacity of a pile is given by the sum of the
frictional resistance between the soil and pile shaft and the base
resistance generated at the tip of the pile
L For the evaluation of the shaft friction the method
represented by the following equation was adopted
Ca X Su
where Ca is the shaft friction per unit area
Su is the undrained shear strength of soil and
2 o is a dimensionless parameter dependent on Su
In general X varies between 0 5 to 1 0 and its value
Ls usually increases with decrease of Su
a For the evaluation of the base resistance the equation used
i is represented as follows
q Nc D Ng PYN
Where Nc Ng and N_ are functions of the friction angle
and their values can be determined from Charts by Terzaghi Peck and
ry Meyerhof
C is the cohesion of the soil
DY is the overburden pressure and
B is the diameter of the pile
A computer program based on the above two equations was
written to evaluate the vertical pile capacity Plates F 1 through F 6
Ld presents the vertical pile capacity with pile tip elevation for various
size piles for each soil category
i The anticipated settlement of the pile groups under the
design vertical load of 1600 kips plus the weight of the pile cap were
calculated for various pile configurations The results of the
settlement analysis are plotted on Plates F 7 through F 10

APPENDIX G
Wave Equation Analysis
The wave equation analysis for pile driving is based on using
a finite element analysis with input of the stress wave generated during
the impact of the hammer onto the pile
The primary objectives of the analysis are
1 To analyze the driving stresses in the pile
2 to determine the suitable driving system to be used for
the pile driving and
3 to predict the dynamic ultimate capacity of the pile
The wave equation analyses for this investigation were
performed by utilizing the computer program WEAPG which was originally
written for the Federal Highway Administration by Goble Associates
under the name WEAP WEAPG is the adopted version of WEAP which runs on
IBM PC and compatibles
Our analysis assumed the use of 54 inch diameter cylindrical
prestressed concrete pipe pile with no splice required The capblock was
assumed to be made of aluminum and micarta and a plywood cushion was to
be used Two main cases both of which were based on the more critical
Group 3 soil profile were analyzed
Case A Pile tip at the bottom of the soft clay at 120
feet depth Maximm tensile stresses will occur
if hammer strokes are not controlled Actual
range of driving resistance is low between 2 to
30 blows per foot
Case B Pile tip in the very stiff clayey silt at 140
feet depth Maximum compressive stresses will
occur Actual driving resistance is more than
100 blows per foot
Based on the above two main cases a parametric study was
_then carried out to investigate the influence due to the change of the
following factors

As shown on Plate G 6 hammers with maximum rated energy of

During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft

As shown on Plate G 8 the change of soil quake and damping
condition affect to quite a large extent the blow count especially
during hard driving But as shown on Plate G 9 its effect on the
maximum stresses are less distinct on the order of 0 to 10 Table 1
on Plate G 3 also indicates that the effects of change of soil quake and
damping are not significant during easy driving
Much of the suggested quake and damping values for each type
of soil are crude and empirical Therefore it is our opinion that
dynamic measurements of force and velocity by the use of a pile analyzer
during test pile testing should be carried out in order to reveal a more
realistic set soil parameters to be input into the analysis
Cushion Thickness
Cushion thickness has little effect on blow counts and
maximim tensile stress as shown on Plate G 3 Table 2 Plates G 10 and
G 11 However the use of a thicker cushion would help to reduce
maximum compressive stress as shown on Plate G 3 Table 2 and Plate G ll
During pile driving the cushion is continuously compressed
resulting in decrease of thickness and hence increase of maximum
stresses on the pile Therefore old cushions should be replaced when
their thickness becomes too small Based on the results presented on
Plates G 10 and G 11 an optimum cushion thickness is about 6 inches
