INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the existing Building F within the Kalaheo High School campus in the Kailua area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for a Structural Study of the settlement that
has occurred at Building F only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the Kalaheo High School campus along Mokapu
Boulevard in the Kailua area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Building F is located at the
eastern portion of the school campus just south of the existing Building E as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 Cracks in the CMU walls and sagging beams and window frames were
observed along the walls of Building F These signs of distress are more noticeable at the
rear south side of the building Manholes for drainpipes that traverse the building are
present at the front of the building to catch surface run off
The existing Building F is a two story concrete structure approximately 36 by 200 feet
in plan dimensions Based on the building plans the concrete building frames are supported
on 7 foot square footings spaced about 15 and 34 5 feet apart in the longitudinal and
transverse directions respectively Smaller footings are utilized at the stairways at the ends
of the building The building floors are constructed of 4 inch thick concrete slabs Concrete
grade beams have been constructed along the southern longitudinal wall line The grade
beams span between the footings and are constructed on top of the spread footings Some
of the classrooms on each floor are separated by CMU walls

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the glaciers
melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to
above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials
caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
GEOLABS INC
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered the
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes consisting of
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered the natural condition
of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of valley
alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as
alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older
alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial
deposits Urban development has since altered the natural formation of the area with
man made fills placed near the existing ground surface

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings extending to depths ranging from about 16 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface around the perimeter of Building F In addition a test pit extending to a depth
of about 3 5 feet below the existing ground surface was excavated near an existing footing
The approximate locations of the borings and test pit are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings generally encountered a surface fill layer approximately 2 5 to 15 feet in
thickness consisting of stiff to hard clays with varying amounts of silt sand gravel and
organic materials and medium dense sandy gravel with silt The surface fill was underlain

Observed Building Distress
There are several signs of structural distress throughout Building F on both the first
and second floor levels The distress appears to be most evident on the southern portion of
the building between Grid Lines 6 and 9 on both the upper and lower floors as shown on the
Observed Building Distress Plan Plate 3
The first floor level appears to exhibit the most distress The window sill along the
southern portion of the building appears to have settled about 3 inches near Grid Line 8
Wooden boards have been added on top of the original window sill to extend the window sill
to accommodate the settlement that has occurred Sagging of the window sill of about 3 8 to
1 2 inch vertical drop within 2 feet was measured The concrete slab in the distress area has
also settled Several isolated cracks were noted in both the perimeter and adjoining CMU
walls of the classrooms The cracks in the CMU walls were generally diagonal cracks
through the CMU block joints At the northern side of the building separation between the
northern perimeter wall and the walls separating the classrooms was observed The
separation increased in width from the floor to the ceiling indicating that some tilting of the
CMU walls has occurred

Similar distress location and characteristics were observed on the second floor level
except where the window sill repair extended to another adjoining classroom Also tilting of
the CMU walls was not observed at the northern side of the building
Findings From Historical Plans
A search was conducted to obtain previous construction plans in the project area
Based on a Master Plan for Kailua Mokapu Intermediate School First Increment dated
December 1964 an old drainageway crosses Building F near the central and western
portions of the building as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The existing topography of the
1964 Master Plan indicate that the building was partially constructed on cut and partially on
fill In general it appears that the northern portion of the building was constructed on cut and
the southern portion of the building was constructed on fill It appears that cuts of up to about
4 feet and fills of up to about 10 feet were needed to grade the area for the construction of
Building F
Potential Causes of Distress
Based on the results of our field exploration and review of historical plans we believe
that the following may be potential causes of the structural distress observed at Building F
e Consolidation of clay with organic material layers beneath the footing
e Softening of the footing subgrade caused by surface water accumulation
e Differential settlement of the building constructed partially on cut and partially on
fill
Fill materials consisting of clays with organic material were encountered below the
existing footing level in the boring and test pit performed near the south central portion of the
building The organic materials generally tend to be highly compressible with a long
consolidation period when loaded These clays with organic material may have undergone
long term settlement under the existing building load

W O 4552 00 Page 9
At the southern side of the building water from the downspout of the building gutter
system discharges onto the ground surface immediately adjacent to the building foundation
Near Grid Line 8 a significant ground depression was noted with indication of water ponding
up to about 4 5 feet deep We believe that the underlying soil beneath the footing may have
softened due to the accumulation of excess surface water
Based on the old topographic plan it appears that Boring No 1 is located within the
cut area and Boring and 4 are situated within the fill area The results of our field
exploration indicates that the upper soils within Boring No 1 are generally stiffer than the
upper soils encountered in Boring and 4 In addition the depth of fill encountered
is deeper in Boring and 4 as compared to Boring No 1 Therefore differential
settlement of the building may have occurred because of the different stiffnesses of the
underlying soils
Our laboratory test results indicate that the underlying clay soils are moderately to
highly expansive The moisture content of the clay soils was generally low and ranged from
about 17 to 28 percent Because of the relatively low moisture content of the underlying clay
soils and the observed building distress we believe that the building distress was not caused
by the heaving of the expansive clay soils beneath the building However these relatively
dry clay soils have the potential to swell when wetted
Recommended Remedial Measures
Several remedial alternatives were considered for Building F These remedial
alternatives included the following
1 Underpinning the footings and slabs using vertical piers
2 Underpinning the footing and slabs using helical piers
3 Ground stabilization by grouting
4 Surface water drainage improvements
GEOLABS INC
W O 4552 00 Page 10
Based on our borings the underlying soils are generally stiff to hard with standard
penetration resistance from 23 to 54 blows per foot Because of the anticipated difficulties
in installing the helical piers within these stiff soils this alternative was omitted In addition
because of the high cost and uncertainties associated with stabilizing the underlying soils by
grouting this alternative was also omitted
In order to reduce the potential for the observed distress from recurring it is our
opinion that a combination of Alternative Nos 1 and 4 be utilized
Underpinning the Footings and Slabs Using Vertical Piers
Most of the observed building distress was noted on the southern side of the building
between Grid Lines 6 and 9 Therefore it is recommended that the footings and slabs within
this area be underpinned and supported on vertical piers to reduce the potential for additional
settlement to occur The vertical piers for the footings and slabs should consist of 4 5 inch
and 3 5 inch diameter galvanized steel pipes respectively The vertical load on the piers will
be resisted by side resistance developed at the perimeter of the pier and by end bearing
resistance at the tip of the pier For the 4 5 inch diameter piers an allowable vertical load
of up to 30 kips may be used for a 38 foot long pier driven into the stiff alluvial material An
allowable vertical load of up to 20 kips may be used for a 3 5 inch diameter by 38 foot long
pier driven into the stiff alluvial material A minimum pier spacing of about 2 feet should be
used
The piers are connected to the existing footing by load transfer brackets and to the
existing slab by slab lifting assemblies Each pier should be load tested to their allowable
vertical load
As an alternative to underpinning the slabs the use of high density polyurethane foam
injected beneath the slab may be considered The high density foam should be specifically
designed for lifting realigning undersealing and void filling concrete slabs which bear
directly on soil The high density foam should have a compressive strength of at least
85 pounds per square inch

Surface Water Drainage Improvements
Positive drainage away from the building should be re established This will help
reduce the potential for ponding of water which results in bearing strength loss of the
foundation soils due to saturation Therefore we recommend that the following be
implemented at the subject site as early as possible
1 The finished grades of the lawn area along the southern side of the building
should be sloped to shed water away from the building walls and foundations
and to reduce the potential for ponding
2 The existing discharge end of the gutter should be extended a minimum of 10
feet away from the building to divert discharge away from the walls and
foundations
3 Excessive watering near the walls and foundations should be avoided
4 Since water taps are located at the southern side of the building care should
be exercised in using the hose bib to avoid flooding the existing ground below
the hose bib

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings extending to depths ranging from about 16 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface In addition a test pit extending to a depth of about 3 5 feet below the
existing ground surface was excavated The approximate locations of the borings drilled and
the test pit excavated are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The test pit was excavated
manually with a shovel
The materials encountered in the borings and test pit were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist or engineer who monitored the drilling and
excavation operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 4 and the Log of Test Pit Plate A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings and the Log of Test Pit at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings and on the
Log of Test Pit at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 2
