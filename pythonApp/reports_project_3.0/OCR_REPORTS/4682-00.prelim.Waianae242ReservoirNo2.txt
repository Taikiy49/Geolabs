Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded
lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower middle and upper volcanic
members The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that
built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock
that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a
relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its
history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred which produced the large
valleys of the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were gradually
filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined
result of stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the island
mass Over time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and
colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation formed
relatively dense deposits However the current surface soil deposits above these older
deposits are typically found to be unconsolidated

As previously mentioned the Site G reservoir tank site is located along Paheehee
Ridge in the Waianae area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is located
approximately 500 feet to the north from the end of Kuwale Road The project site is
generally sloping from north to south at a slope inclination of about four horizontal to one

Based on our site reconnaissance the majority of the project site is covered with

cobbles A few kiawe trees were also observed at the project site

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling one boring designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of about
121 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate focation of the boring
drilled for the project is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the boring drilled for the project the project site is generally underlain by
colluvium and conglomerate deposits over basalt formation at greater depths Our boring
encountered colluvium consisting of cobbles and boulders in a sandy silt matrix from the
ground surface extending to a depth of about 11 feet below the ground level Below the
colluvium layer conglomerate consisting of cobbles and boulders in a cemented soil
matrix was encountered from a depth of about 11 feet extending to about 26 5 feet below
the ground surface It should be noted that the cementation of the conglomerate ranged
from weak to moderate The conglomerate was underlain by another deposit of colluvium
consisting of loose silty sands with some cobbles unconsolidated Basalt formation was
encountered below the second colluvium layer from about 52 feet below the ground level
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 121 5 feet below the ground surface
The basalt formation encountered was generally slightly to highly weathered and ranged
from medium hard to very hard
Groundwater was not encountered in the boring at the time of our field exploration
However groundwater levels may fluctuate depending on seasonal precipitation
groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors Detailed descriptions of the
materials encountered during our field exploration are presented on ihe Logs of Boring
selected samples are presented in Appendix B

Excavations
Consiruction of the proposed reservoir will likely require cuts of over 70 feet in
depth Based on our field exploration at the site the project site is underlain by colluvial
soil deposits slightly to moderately cemented conglomerate and basalt formation ranging
from medium hard to very hard In general we envision that the on site materials may be
readily excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as backhoe excavators
and large bulldozers However some of the deeper excavations into the dense basalt
formation may require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and the limited field data
obtained from the boring performed at the site We recommend that additional field
exploration be conducted at the site to evaluate the site conditions with respect to the
proposed excavation
Cut Slopes
Based on available subsurface information it appears that the cut slopes at the site
would expose the colluvial soi deposits conglomerate and basalt formation Cut slopes
exposing the well cemented conglomerate and basalt formation may be designed based
on a slope inclination of or flatter However cut slopes exposing the
unconsolidated soil deposits such as the loose to medium dense colluvium and the
weakly cemented conglomerate should be designed based on a slope inclination of 2H 1V
or flatter Based on the limited field exploration performed at the site we believe that the
upper 60 feet of the 90 foot high cut slope should be designed based on a slope inclination
of 2H 1V The remaining lower 30 feet of the 90 foot high cut slope may be designed
based on a slope inclination of or flatter
Because excavation of the cut slopes would be a significant cost item for
construction of the proposed reservoir we recommend that additional borings be
conducted during the design phase of the project to further delineate the extent of the
loose colluvium and weakly cemented conglomerate that would require a flatter slope
inclination In addition a representative from Geolabs should observe and map the cut
slopes during construction to evaluate whether the exposed cut slope materials are
consistent with our assumptions
Rockfall Protection
As previously mentioned cut slopes of over 90 feet in height may be required for
construction of the proposed reservoir in general structures situated below a hillside
slope have an inherent potential hazard from boulders rolling down the hillside Because
the natural slopes at the site are generally flatter than 1H 1V the boulders tend to roll
along the ground surface and most of the boulders are stopped by trees other boulders

or come to rest on flatter areas Man made structures such as fences or ditches may be
installed to stop the occasional boulders that may continue down slope
it should be noted that steep rock cuts generally increase the hazard from rockfall
Pieces of rock formation may break loose from the cut slope face and may roll or bounce
downslope depending on the steepness of the slope Extensive research by the U S
Department of Transportation Federal Highway Administration FHWA indicates that
boulders tend to roll down slopes flatter than 1H 1V bounce on the slope face for ratios of
about to 1H 1V and fall straight to the bottom for steeper slopes
Due to the high cut slopes planned and the proximity of the reservoir structure the
potential for rockfall hazard at the site is a significant design issue for the project Rockfall
hazard in the basalt formations is generally more severe in weathered basalt and
formations with thick clinker layers which are susceptible to erosion and loosening of the
boulders over time In addition the rockfall hazard in the weakly cemented conglomerate
is also more substantial due to the potential for erosion of the weakly cemented soil matrix
and loosening of the boulders over time Several measures may be considered to reduce
the potential hazard from rockfall at the projeci site
1 In general the finished slope face should be carefully excavated to avoid
loosening of the intact rock and undercutting portions of the slope which
may creaie future instability Loose pieces of rock should be removed from
the slope as excavation of the cut slope progresses If the coniractor is
unable to make a relatively smooth cut surface the use of chemical
expanders installed in predrilled holes may be considered
2 Mid slope benches should be avoided as recent research by FHWA has
indicated that the benches tend to accumulate debris and form launching
ramps for falling rocks which increase the hazard for potential rockfall
3 A rockfall catchment ditch should be provided at the toe of the cut slope to
collect falling rocks and debris Based on guidelines from FHWA the
catchment ditch should be about 20 feet wide for a slope height of about
60 to 100 feet
A Structures should be set back from the slope as much as practicable to
provide for an additional safety zone
5 Lined surface water interceptor ditches should be constructed along the tops
of the cut slopes The fined interceptor ditches should be designed to
intercept and divert surface water runoff to appropriate drainage areas thus
reducing the amount of surface water that could percolate into the ground
and discharge as seepage from the cut slope face

Reservoir Foundations
As previously mentioned we understand that the proposed reservoir tank planned
at the site will have a diameter of about 200 feet We envision that the reservoir tank
structure will likely be supported on a concrete floor slab with a thickened edge perimeter
footing Based on the preliminary grading plan provided the entire structure will be
situated above a cut condition In general the mauka side of the reservoir tank will
require an excavation of over 70 feet below the existing ground surface The makai
side of the reservoir tank will be excavated to less than 5 feet below the ground surface
Based on the limited subsurface exploration conducted we believe that the
proposed reservoir will likely straddle across the colluvium and basalt formation
transition at the reservoir subgrade elevation Therefore we recommend that the
reservoir tank subgrade be over excavated and replaced with structural fill materials in
order to provide a uniform bearing surface for the reservoir foundations Due to the
limited field exploration conducted at this time we recommend that additional borings
be drilled to further evaluate the transition of the various subsurface materials across
the footprint of the proposed reservoir tank location
Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present in the
basaltic lava flows at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from collapse of cavities below foundations a program of cavity probing
and grouting of the foundation locations for the reservoir will likely be required during
construction
In general we recommend that the probeholes be drilled at each column footing
and at 10 foot on centers for the thickened edge perimeter footing In addition probeholes
should be drilled within the tank floor area at about 10 foot on centers starting at the center
of the tank and radiating out at 10 foot radial increments The probeholes should be at
least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom
of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probeholes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void
