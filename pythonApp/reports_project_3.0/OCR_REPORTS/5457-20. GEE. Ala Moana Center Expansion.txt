SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the International Building Code the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed
for the project The following sections provide discussions on the potential for liquefaction
and the soil profile for seismic design at the project site
Liquefaction Potential
Based on the IBC 2003 the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and
should be evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered and a design peak ground acceleration of 0 15g liquefaction may
occur resulting in appreciable settlements at the ground surface Therefore the effects of
potential liquefaction should be taken into consideration in the design of the proposed
retail connector
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the
build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is
reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the
overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to
behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit
both horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant
deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion Generally it is acknowledged that liquefaction
will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the ground surface In
deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent
liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a
soil deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
a Magnitude _and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake

FOUNDATION SUPPORT SYSTEM EVALUATION
As mentioned previously the project area generally is underlain by surface fills
over soft and or loose lagoonal deposits at relatively shallow depths Based on the
moderately high column loads anticipated for the proposed retail connector we believe
that the existing subsurface soils would not provide adequate foundation support for the
proposed structure without appreciable settlements under the structural loads In
addition the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground settlement on the
order of 3 to 4 inches in the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake M6 as
described previously and the structure supported on shallow foundations may sustain
appreciable damage that would disrupt the accessibility of the retail development
Therefore we recommend that a deep foundation system be used for support of the
proposed retail connector The deep foundation support system would consist of either
driven precast prestressed concrete piles or augered cast in place ACIP piles
We also evaluated the use of drilled shaft foundations for support of the new
structure planned for the project Based on the soft and or loose subsoil conditions
anticipated at the project site we envision that the drilled shaft foundations would derive
vertical support primarily from skin friction between the drilled shaft and the coral ledges
and coralline detritus starting at depths of about 30 feet below the existing ground
surface
One of the primary advantages of the drilled shaft foundation system is that
construction of the drilled shafts would not generate the noise and vibrations normally
associated with other deep foundation system such as driven pile foundations Drilled
shaft foundations are highly effective in soil formations that can stay open after drilling
until concrete placement However the loose sands and silts below the groundwater
level at the project site likely will not stay open following drilling Therefore temporary
casing and or the use of drilling fluids mineral and or polymer slurry likely will be
necessary to maintain the integrity of the drilled hole during drilled shaft installation This
would increase the construction difficulty and costs for the foundations
In addition the anticipated shallow groundwater level at the project site may pose
some construction difficulties because proper observation of the sides and bottoms of the
drilled shafts may not be possible Finally a drilled shaft foundation system would be
highly desirable and strongly recommended for structures where significant foundation
structural loading is required Based on the magnitude of the structural loads on the order
of about 500 kips per column we believe that use of a drilled shaft foundation system
may not be cost effective for the new structure planned at the site
In general we envision that 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete
piles may be used for the foundation support of the proposed retail connector The driven
piles will develop its load bearing capacity primarily from end bearing on the upper dense
coral ledges or embedded in the underlying medium dense coralline detritus materials
underlying the project site at greater depths Based on our experience in the vicinity we

Pile Hammer Selection
As with most project sites underlain by coral ledges and coral formation selection
of the pile hammer for use in driving the piie foundations will be of critical consideration In
general we recommend that the piles be driven with a hammer capable of delivering
energies in the range of about 25 000 to 35 000 foot pounds in the field Hammers with
higher energies such as hammers with energies greater than 50 000 foot pounds are not
recommended for this project The hammer should be equipped with an energy level
control such that driving within the soft and or loose soils may be controlled at a lower
energy to reduce the potential for tensile stress development which could damage or
cause cracking of the piles
Selection of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven
This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the
pile and also reducing the potential for damage to the piles Geolabs should review the
pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of installing
the piles to the recommended capacity The contractor should forward pile and driving
equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to construction for evaluation Geolabs should
review the submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected
hammer

ACIP Pile Construction Considerations
Proper installation of the ACIP piles is dependent upon the procedures and
equipment used by the foundation subcontractor Therefore the foundation
subcontractor s personnel on the project site who will be responsible for the installation
should have relevant experience with the augering and pumping equipment experience
with placing ACIP piles under similar subsurface conditions as well as experience with
handling the special grout mixes admixtures etc
The importance of grouting pressure varies with soil type and other factors It is
important that a sufficient grout pressure is maintained to assure a constant flow of grout
to the tip of the auger This will allow adequate grout quantities to be built up on and

around the auger flight The grout build up or head of grout is necessary to insure an
adequate grout supply to replace the volume of soil removed when withdrawing the auger
during installation and to maintain adequate pressure to prevent collapsing of the

installation control used during installation of the ACIP piles Therefore the foundation
subcontractor and the on site inspector need to continuously monitor the grout volume

In order to assure that the ACIP piles are installed in accordance with the project

Static Load Tests
As part of the pre construction activities we recommend that a static load test be
conducted for the project The load test should be conducted on the 18 inch diameter
ACIP pile extending to a depth of about 60 to 65 feet below the ground surface The
results of the load test will be used to confirm or modify the estimated tip elevations of the
production ACIP piles The load test shaft should be structurally reinforced and
instrumented with embedment strain gages for load testing purposes As a minimum one
embedment strain gage should be placed at each level starting at about 5 feet above the
tip of the ACIP pile and subsequently at about 10 foot intervals
The ACIP pile load test should be performed in general accordance with the Quick
Load Test Method of ASTM Test Designation D 1143 The load test pile should be loaded
to at least 250 percent of the allowable compressive load capacity of the ACIP pile to
evaluate the side shear resistance and end bearing components of the ACIP pile
Installation and presentation of the load test data should be performed by a professional
experienced in these types of load testing procedures The load test pile should be loaded
at increments of about 20 kips and should be held for a minimum of 4 hours at each load
at 300 kip 450 kip and 600 kip load increments to evaluate the potential for creep effects

adjacent exterior grade Due to the presence of shallow groundwater conditions at the

SLABS ON GRADE
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new retail
connector structure The borings drilled encountered loose lagoonal deposits near the
existing ground surface at the project site It should be noted that pumping subgrade
conditions have been experienced near the existing ground elevations during construction
on adjacent projects in the past Therefore construction documents should contain
provisions such as unit prices and budget to over excavate the slab and pavement
subgrades where necessary and backfill the over excavation with compacted fill
Where soft soils are encountered at the slab subgrade level we recommend that a
stabilization layer be provided below the concrete slabs to reduce the potential for
structural distress to the future improvements The stabilization layer should consist of at
least 18 to 30 inches of No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped on all sides in
a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent The actual thickness of the
stabilization layer will need to be determined in the field during construction based on the
exposed soil conditions and the depth below finished subgrades The need for over
excavation of the soft soils below the concrete slabs on grade and backfilling with a select
granular fill materials should be evaluated by a representative from Geolabs in the field
during construction
In general the subgrades for concrete slabs on grades should be scarified to a
depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction For the interior building slabs
which will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a minimum 4 inch
thick layer of cushion fill be provided for uniform support in addition to the stabilization
layer where required based on the exposed soil conditions The cushion fill should
consist of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation and would serve as a
capillary moisture break It should be noted that the provision for cushion fill is in addition
to the stabilization layer where required
To reduce the potential for excessive moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier minimum 10 mil thickness is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Placement of 2 inches of moist fine
sand above the plastic membrane may be considered to provide for protection of the

W O 5457 20
membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist
fine sand above the moisture barrier is optional and will be left up to the discretion of the
project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor
slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as trucks and or
forklifts we recommend that a 6 inch layer of select borrow subbase in addition to the
stabilization layer where required be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4 inch thick
gravel cushion layer The 2 inch sand and moisture barrier may also be omitted for these
slabs The select borrow subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural
slabs supported on 6 inches of select borrow subbase a modulus of subgrade reaction of
about 150 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the slab
resting on the compacted select borrow subbase Where slabs are intended to function
as rigid pavements for trucks a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for
preliminary design purposes In addition provisions should be made for proper load
transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the slabs with
expansion joints at right angle intersections The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to
unpaved areas should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly
against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils well graded on site
granular soils These areas also should be graded to divert water away from the slabs
and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs
