2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which had activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern and western flanks of Hualalai
Mountain and the western and northwestern flanks of Mauna Kea which makes up the
west central part of the island The lava formations encountered at the site appear to be
of a a and pahoehoe type lava flows which spread and ponded as they approached the
ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders
Typically a denser and more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow
core
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface with an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in
pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state
and they represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and or pockets
inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Blisters and or pockets are generally smaller in horizontal
extent
Basalt formations are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can
transmit water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general
water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

2 2 Climatic Conditions
The Island of Hawaii is located in the tropics in the Pacific Ocean Hawaii is
characterized by mild temperatures throughout the year moderate humidity persistent
northeasterly trade winds significant differences in rainfall within short distances and
infrequent severe storms
The temperature on the Island of Hawaii typically ranges between 65 degrees
Fahrenheit and 85 degrees Fahrenheit The maximum temperature recorded on the
Island of Hawaii was approximately 100 degrees Fahrenheit at Pahala Elevation
870 feet Mean Sea Level MSL on and the lowest temperature recorded
was approximately 12 degrees Fahrenheit on Mauna Kea Elevation 13 770 feet MSL
on May 17 1979
The annual rainfall on the Island of Hawaii averages about 25 to 30 inches The
rainfall may be as much as fifteen times the average annual rainfall over the upper
mountain slopes and crests on the windward side and may be less than one third the
average annual rainfall in the leeward lowland areas The leeward areas obtain their
rainfall mainly from a few winter storms Therefore the rainfall is usually seasonal and the
summers are dry In the wetter regions the rainfall comes from both winter storms and
tradewind showers
The project site is located in the Districts of North Kona and South Kohala on the
Island of Hawaii The average annual rainfall near the project site ranges between 20 and
30 inches The precipitation at the project site may be considered low to moderate

3 3 Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our field exploration the pavement subgrade along the locations where it
is planned to reconstruct the pavements consist of granular fills clinker materials and
basalt rock formation The granular fills consist of basaltic and coralline boulders cobbles
gravel and sand A Laboratory Resistance R Value test was performed on the
near surface granular fills obtained from our field exploration
Based on the laboratory test the granular fills have an R value of 79 The
laboratory test results are presented on Plate B 1 of Appendix B In general we have
performed the pavement designs assuming that the pavement subgrade soils will consist
of compacted granular fills clinker material and basalt rock formation The laboratory
tests indicate that the granular fills exhibit an R value greater than that of aggregate
subbase material Based on our experience we believe that the basalt rock formation and
the clinker materials would also exhibit an R value equivalent to or greater than that of
aggregate subbase material Therefore a design R value of 60 subbase material was
adopted in our pavement analyses for subgrade materials consisting of granular fills
clinker materials and basalt rock formation

conditions

After clearing the future reconstructed pavement areas should be excavated
where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the asphalt concrete
base layer The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
In areas where the pavement subgrades expose basalt rock formation the
subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory roller for a minimum
of eight passes in lieu of scarification to help detect and collapse near surface cavities
Loose areas or cavities disclosed during proof rolling operations should be backfilled
with granular fill materials 6 inch minus The vibratory drum roller should travel at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute Due to the potential for presence of cavities lava
tubes blisters pockets etc and soft and or wet soils contract documents should
include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities
Fill Materials
Fill materials should consist of well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in
largest dimension The excavated on site materials may be re used as fill or backfill
materials provided that the materials are processed and reduced to a relatively
well graded select granular material with a maximum particle size of about 6 inches
The maximum particle size of the fills and backfills should be limited to a maximum
of 3 inches for fills and backfills to be placed in confined locations Confined locations are
defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less than 10 tons in
static weight In addition if the contractor elects to use smaller sized compaction
equipment compaction equipment less than 10 tons in static weight the maximum
particle size of the fill and backfill materials should be limited to 3 inches in dimension

3 9 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the aggregate subbase and or subgrade soils The granular fills clinker
materials and basalt rock formation in this area are permeable based on the field
permeability tests conducted during our field exploration Considering the permeable
nature of the subgrade materials granular fill materials clinker materials or basalt rock
pavement drainage for this project is not as critical as in other areas on the Island of
Hawaii As a minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients
should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage
structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction Where landscaping is planned adjacent to the pavement areas we
recommend that a subdrain system be constructed to collect excess water from
landscaping irrigation and to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape
water into the pavement section
Subdrains should be provided in areas where seepage is observed during and after
grading or as recommended by the Engineer during construction Subdrains should
conform to the requirements of Section 605 Underdrains of the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction
minimum the subdrain pipes should consist of 6 inch diameter perforated pipes
conforming to Subsection 706 12 of the HSS with the perforations facing down The
subdrains should be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of
about 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools The
approximate locations of the borings for the project are shown on the Enlarged Site
Plans Plates 3 1 and 3 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer dropping
18 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches
of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
