REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
two primary shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older Waianae Volcano is 
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is 
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic 
inactivity, during which erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic 
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  
These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is located in Manoa Valley on land mapped by others as being 
partially underlain by relatively young volcanic products belonging to the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series. The volcanic products include localized hard rock lavas and localized 
volcanic vent deposits that are believed to have erupted from late-stage vents in the 
Tantalus and Manoa areas. The near-surface deposits may include dense basalt rock 
and cinder materials with derived soils.

Atherton House Slope Site 
The subject rock slope spans discontinuously for a distance of about 150 linear feet 
along the western property boundary between the Atherton House and an adjacent church 
property. An existing chain link fence separates the properties and the majority of the rock 
outcrop resides within the MPI property. It should be noted that the existing fence line 
appears to be within the Atherton House property and not directly along the property 
boundary.  
The rock slope spanning along the property line traverses from the Atherton House 
front yard to the side yard, adjacent to the house, and consists of three distinct “mounds” of 
rock outcropping. The rock material comprising the rock slope is generally very blocky, 
slightly to moderately weathered and hard to very hard. Poor quality rock material, such as 
basalt clinker rubble and embedded soil seams, were not observed comprising the exposed 
surfaces of the rock outcroppings.  
For discussion purposes, the length of the Atherton House rock slope was divided 
into three distinct rock outcropping masses, identified as 1) the Driveway Outcrop, 2) the 
Observatory Outcrop, and 3) the Garden Outcrop.  
As discussed in our Phase I report, based on our visual assessment, there appears 
to be a very limited potential for hazardous rockfall conditions at the existing Driveway 
Outcrop and Observatory Outcrop sites. However, as previously noted, a loose cobble 
approximately 18 inches in largest diameter was observed along the Observatory Outcrop 
stairway. Although not a significant rockfall hazard, the cobble has the potential to loosen 
overtime and roll down the stairway, potentially causing physical harm. The observed loose 
cobble is presented on Photo No. 1 on Plate A-1. 
Based on our Phase I study, two existing rock outcrop undercut voids were 
documented at the Garden Outcrop. The observed voids in the lower portion of the outcrop 
have the potential to deteriorate with the passage of time and may produce a potential 
rockfall hazard condition. Due to heavy vegetative ground cover at the Garden Outcrop, an 
inspection of the entire rock mass was not completed at the time of our Phase I study. The 
vegetation coverage was removed following our Phase I recommendations. The ground 
exposure allowed for a complete visual evaluation of the outcrop to be conducted during our 
Phase II study. 
Based on our Phase II observations, two outcrop undercut voids previously 
documented in our Phase I reporting on the Garden Outcrop were reassessed and 
determined to pose a potential rockfall hazard in need of stabilization for long-term stability.  
The locations of the undercut voids are shown on the Site Plan 1, Plate 3.1, and depicted in 
Photograph Nos. 2 and 3 on Plates A-1 and A-2, respectively. Following the complete 
vegetation removal, we did not observe other potential outcrop instability requiring rock 
stabilization at this time.

Parker Place Slope Site 
As discussed in our Phase I report, the Parker Place slope site is approximately 
140 linear feet in total length and was divided into two slope segments composed of differing 
rock character. The western slope segment extends approximately 80 linear feet along the 
driveway and contains the steeper and taller rock cut slope. The cut slope extends to heights 
of about 11 to 19 feet above the adjacent ground surface fronting the slope. The eastern 
slope segment extends about 60 feet in length and consists of an elongated pile of individual 
nested boulders which tapers from a height of about 8 feet to about 3 feet at the eastern end 
of the slope. The location of the slope site in relation to the existing driveway and parking 
stalls is shown on the Site Plan 2, Plate 3.2. 
An initial Phase II site reconnaissance was conducted on May 10, 2019 to record and 
identify the probable locations for rock bolt installation and the application of localized 
shotcrete slope facing.  In addition, the existing loose boulders were identified and quantified 
for complete removal from the site.  
In general, the existing exposed soil slope surfaces showing some potential for 
additional ground loss and surface erosion with the passage of time were identified to 
receive a fiber reinforced shotcrete slope facing.  The design approach was developed to 
stabilize the critical or key block components (boulders) comprising the cut rock slope. The 
critical boulders were determined to be the individual rock masses that either had the 
potential to create a rockfall hazard directly or that may actively support adjacent rock 
masses that may have the potential to develop into potential rockfall hazards should the 
identified critical boulder shift or loosen in position.  
Our Phase I study proposed the concept of utilizing a broad pattern layout of rock 
anchor bolts across the length of the rock slope face. Based on our additional Phase II site 
evaluation, we refined the rock bolting design to reduce the number of ground anchors by 
using a targeted approach consisting of placing the rock bolt anchors at selected locations 
to stabilize the identified key rock block components comprising the slope face. The effort 
substantially reduced the number of rock anchors needed to stabilize the slope. 
Based on our current field assessment, we determined that the slope stabilization 
design at Parker Place should consist of two (2) separate shotcrete slope areas, twelve (12) 
rock bolt locations, and removal of approximately 18 cubic yards of rock material (boulders) 
for the rockfall hazard mitigation scheme. The location of the shotcrete slope areas, rock 
bolts, and rock material to be removed are discussed in the following section. 
Another site reconnaissance was performed on June 5, 2019 after the ground 
surface vegetation was cleared from the Parker Place slope. The purpose of the additional 
reconnaissance was to evaluate the slope areas previously obscured by dense vegetative 
cover and to estimate the size and locations of reinforced shotcrete pedestals needed to 
provide rock block support. Based on our field assessment, one additional rock bolt is 
necessary (for a total of 13 rock anchor bolts). Undercut voids, wide-open rock fissures, and 
overhanging rock block features were evaluated and measured to facilitate the design of the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our Phase II design level study included additional field observations and 
assessment of the rock outcroppings to support  the final design of the rockfall hazard 
mitigation improvements presented in our Phase I conceptual study. In general, the rockfall 
hazard mitigation improvements at the Atherton House and Parker Place slope sites include 
vegetation clearing, rock slope scaling, targeted rock bolting, shotcrete slope protection, and 
reinforced shotcrete support pedestals. It is our professional opinion that the recommended 
rockfall mitigation program is a cost-effective scheme that should aid in reducing the 
potential for dangerous falling rock hazards at the project sites. 
Atherton House – Observatory and Garden Outcrops 
As previously noted, a single loose cobble size rock was observed on the 
Observatory Outcrop adjacent to the existing outcrop access stairway. Because the cobble 
has the potential to cause physical harm, we recommend securing the cobble in place using 
lean cement mortar. 
At the Garden Outcrop, two rock outcrop undercut voids were observed and may 
pose a potential rockfall hazard. The primary mechanism for rockfall activity resulting from 
the undercut voids and existing limited rock support is the possible weathering, deterioration 
and expansion of rock fractures within the overhanging rock material with the passage of 
time. Therefore, we recommend installing shotcrete support pedestals within the existing 
undercut void areas to provide basal support for the overhanging material. 
At the onset of construction, we recommend cutting and removing the existing 
vegetation within the identified undercut voids and surrounding the rock outcropping. In 
addition, loose debris, deleterious and other unsuitable or yielding materials should be 
removed from the undercut voids and properly disposed of before commencing with the 
shotcrete pedestal installation work. 
The shotcrete support pedestal should consist of a reinforced shotcrete mass that 
will completely infill the undercut void area. We recommend utilizing a 6x6 – W2.9xW2.9 
Welded Wire Fabric (WWF) reinforcing with a minimum shotcrete cover of 8 inches. To 
secure the pedestal in place within the void, we recommend providing 0.5-inch diameter 
stainless steel grouted anchors with centralizers.  The anchors should be spaced 24-inches 
on-center, each way along the top and bottom of the void. The grouted anchors should be 
embedded a minimum of 6 inches in competent/basalt material and protrude a minimum of 
6 inches into the shotcrete. The shotcrete pedestal should be well-drained to reduce the

Rock Scaling 
Rock slope scaling activity typically consists of experienced scaling crews employing 
manual labor and hand tools to remove loose rocks and precariously positioned rock 
fragments from the slope face. The scaling crews performing the rock slope scaling 
should be experienced in the performance of rock slope scaling by rappelling and/or 
working from crane baskets.  Rock slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting 
from the top of the slope and working downward in a controlled and orderly manner. 
In general, rock slope scaling involves the careful removal of existing loose rocks and 
potential hazardous debris from an exposed and vegetation-free  slope face. 
However, due to the nature of the Parker Place slope, the removal of some key rock 
blocks could cause new slope instability encompassing the surrounding rock 
material. Therefore, we selected and mapped the specific slope areas and individual 
boulders for careful removal. The locations where rock scaling should be performed, 
and the associated photograph number for each location in Appendix A, are 
described in the following table.

If additional loose boulders are discovered during the vegetation removal and rock 
scaling process, or if additional unforeseen slope instability is caused by the rock 
scaling process, Geolabs should be contacted immediately to evaluate the slope 
conditions and course of action on a case-by-case basis. 
In general, each rock slope scaling crew typically consists of a scaling supervisor, 
who should be a specialist in rock slope scaling, and one to three experienced rock 
slope scalers. The scaling supervisor should have a minimum of five years of high 
slope scaling experience with a minimum of 2,000 hours of demonstrated experience 
supervising slope scaling. The scaling supervisor should have completed the 
American Red Cross “basic first aid course”, or equivalent, and should have 
experience or training in the use of emergency remote rescue techniques. 
Each slope scaler is an individual who is engaged in accessing the slope face and 
removing loose rock and materials from the slope face using a variety of hand tools 
such as pry bars at locations that require modified rock climbing techniques for the 
safe execution of the work. Each slope scaler should have a minimum two years of 
experience with a minimum of 1,000 hours of demonstrated experience performing 
similar slope scaling work. 
The scaling crews are typically supported by an ancillary ground crew that is 
responsible for the cleanup of the materials and debris deposited at the base of the 
slope. 
The rock slope scaling will require complete closure of the adjacent parking stalls and 
roadway during the time that rock is being scaled or removed from the slope. We 
recommend all rock slope scaling work be conducted during daylight hours only.  
It should be noted that rock slope scaling work may be considered “hazardous” work 
and should be classified as a “high risk” activity. Visibility is the most essential

Shotcrete Slope Protection 
Based on our field evaluation, we observed two soil surface slopes at the Parker 
Place slope site that may be susceptible to future erosion and ground loss. The two 
soil slope areas are located between approximately Sta. 0+00 to 0+27 and at 
Sta. 0+67. If left unprotected, the soil slopes may erode and ravel over time, exposing 
new loose material and possibly undercutting the stability of adjacent boulders or 
blocks of rock. Therefore, following vegetation removal and slope scaling, we 
recommend installing reinforced shotcrete over the selected soil slope faces. The 
approximate location and recommended extents of the shotcrete slope protection are 
depicted in Photo Nos. 4, 5, and 11 in Appendix A. 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete 
be constructed for inspection.  
The shotcrete slope protection should be well-drained to reduce the potential build-up 
of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system for the shotcrete slope protection 
would consist of 2-foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage product, such as 
Miradrain or Enkadrain, placed on the face of the slope at a maximum horizontal 
spacing of 5 feet on-center. The prefabricated drainage product should extend from 
the top to the base of the slope and should be hydraulically connected to a weephole 
near the toe of the slope. Details of the shotcrete slope protection are presented on 
the Typical Shotcrete Slope Protection Detail, Plate 4.3.

Based on our evaluation of the slope, we recommend that the rock anchor bolts be 
a minimum of 8 feet long and should anchor the target surface boulder or block to 
underlying competent rock formation. The drilled hole for the rock bolt should be a 
minimum of 4 inches in diameter. The grout should have a minimum 28-day

28-day unconfined compressive strength of the grout, the rock bolt should be 
post-tensioned to a lock-off load of at least 6 kips. To preserve the natural look of the

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations of potential rockfall activity only. Variation 
in the surface and subsurface conditions between our observations may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings 
and recommendations provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, 
and erosion conditions.  Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting from 
natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), the 
potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time.  Therefore, the 
findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only within a reasonable time 
from the date of issuance of this letter report.  Land use, site conditions, and/or other factors 
may change with the passage of time.  Therefore, additional work to evaluate the 
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report 
due to changes with the passage of time will be required. 
Finally, there are no guarantees in the professional engineering and architectural 
design fields with respect to potential rockfall hazards due in large part to the unpredictable 
nature of rockfall activity, which is affected by many external variables including natural and
