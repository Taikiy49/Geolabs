Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Waianae Volcano became
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried
below the Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch
The dips of the lava beds are generally near horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees from
horizontal The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering extending
to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil
Along the drain line alignment in situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed
a mantle of residual soils that grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is
composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic
rocks It should be noted that spheroidally weathered boulders are typical in the Schofield
Plateau The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth

Further discussions on the general characteristics of the geologic units
encountered in the borings are presented in the following subsections
Older Alluvium
Older alluvial deposits are generally very stiff to hard silty and clayey soils that
originally were deposited in alluvial environments buried over long periods of time
by more recent deposits to become consolidated and then subsequently uncovered
at the ground surface by erosion or other geologic processes This unit generally
overlies the residual soil saprolitic soils and basalt formation Older alluvium
consists of terrigenous sediments which have been transported by stream action
from the upper reaches of the streams

Portions of the older alluvium unit contain some weathered basaltic cobbles and

Residual Soils
Residual soils are completely weathered volcanic rocks that have lost all visible rock
texture characteristics The residual soils at the project site are typically brown to
reddish brown in color The residual soils may be mottled with various colors
Residual soils are comprised mainly of clayey materials with stiff to very stiff
consistency Typically residual soils occur overlying saprolitic soils or weathered
basalt formation It should be noted that relict boulders of hard rock may be
encountered in residual soils In addition there is potential for encountering hard
basalt rock ledges in residual soils because the volcanic rock may not have
weathered uniformly throughout the rock formation Some relict boulders of hard rock
occasionally may be encountered in residual soils
Saprolitic Soils
Extremely weathered volcanic rock is referred to as saprolitic soils or saprolites
Saprolitic soils represent rock that has been reduced by weathering to soil like
components with weathered rock fragments The soil material retains the remnant
rock texture such as layering vesicularity and some fracture patterns Saprolitic soils
are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and are composed of mainly
silty materials The coloring scheme of saprolitic soils depends on the type of parent
volcanic rock from which the soils are derived and the degree of chemical weathering
that the material has experienced Because the parent material does not weather
uniformly throughout the formation relict boulders of hard rock and or hard rock
ledges may be occasionally encountered within saprolitic soils

Basalt Formation
Basalt formations include a broad range of volcanic basaltic rock The basalt rock
material was extruded as sequential layered lava flows from volcanic vents The
basalt rock which frequently contains interbedded porous clinker materials range
from very hard to soft rock Weathering decomposition ranges between highly
weathered and slightly weathered Fracture discontinuities within basalt rock
formations range from severely fractured shattered and broken to relatively
unfractured and massive in character

tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the

Liquefaction Potential
The project site may be subject to seismic activity and the potential for soil
liquefaction will need to be evaluated
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by
earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected
to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease
in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the
pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows

Design of metallic substructures such as electrical conduits or other metallic

Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion
of metallic objects
Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory testing the subsurface soils at the
project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of approximately 1 600 ohm cm indicating
that the subsurface soils are very corrosive Corrosion Rating of 2 based on the Board of

properly designing metallic substructures such as piping for protection against the
potential for corrosion
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and
structures is dependent on the pH chloride content and sulfate content found in the soil
In general soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million ppm sulfate
content of less than 2 000 ppm and a pH of greater than 5 0 may be considered
non corrosive to underground concrete pipelines and structures based on the above

We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling

for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on a

by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results
is provided on Plate B 1
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey soils two
Unconfined Compression tests were performed on selected in situ samples in
accordance with ASTM D2166 Individual stress strain curves of the unconfined
compression tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D2850 were
performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of
the materials The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures were used as the
applied confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil samples The test results
and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two Uniaxial Compression tests ASTM D7012 Method C were performed on
selected rock cores to evaluate their uniaxial compressive strength of the rock formation
encountered Results of the uniaxial compression tests are presented on Plate B 6
One set of Corrosion tests including Minimum Resistivity ASTM
G57 Chloride Content and Sulfate Content were performed
by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories Inc on a selected soil sample obtained from
our field exploration The test results are summarized on Plate B 7
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
