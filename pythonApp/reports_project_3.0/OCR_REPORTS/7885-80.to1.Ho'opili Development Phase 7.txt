SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings within the planned 
development. Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surficial fill 
overlying medium stiff to hard and medium dense to dense alluvial deposits extending to the 
depths explored of about 20.3 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate to 
high shrink-swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. 
Considering the lightly loaded structures planned for the subdivision development, we 
believe that structural distresses could occur to the new residential buildings and pavements 
due to the moderate to high shrink-swell potential of the on-site soils unless special 
subgrade preparations are implemented during mass grading construction. Therefore, 
special attention should be given to the site grading preparation and foundation design to 
reduce the risk of structural distresses due to the moderately to highly expansive soil 
conditions. 
We recommend implementing the following site grading preparation procedures to 
create a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits at the project site. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near-surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These soils 
should be disposed of properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, if 
appropriate. 
2. 
Over-excavate and remove an additional 12 inches of soil and moisture 
condition to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture content for 
use as general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials. 
3. 
Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as general fill, the bottom of 
the over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. 
The intent of the above site preparation is to create a 2-foot thick reworked soil 
layer within the entire grading limits. The reworked soil serves to close-up the shrinkage 
cracks observed throughout the site. It would reduce the future soil swelling/heaving 
potential by preparing the clayey soils with elevated moisture contents and reduced 
compaction requirements. In addition, it decreases the affinity of the clayey soil for water 
and creates a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil. It

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope, forming a broad 
plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments that were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of Interstate Route H-1 Highway to over 
1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining 
formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. The 
deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic times through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is generally situated near the northwestern portion of the Ho`opili 
Development in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bordered by 
Farrington Highway to the northwest and Kualakai Parkway to the southwest. 
At the time of our field exploration, the site was being used as a cultivated agricultural 
land by Aloun Farms. Several unpaved access roads traverse across the project site. The 
newly built elevated rail track passes through the middle of the project site from the 
southwest to the northeast toward the Ho’opili Rail Station. 
Based on our site observations and the provided plans, the project site generally 
slopes gently down from north to south. Ground surface elevations range from about +142 
to +162 feet Mean Sea Level at the southern to northern portions of the project site, 
respectively.

3.1.2 Site Perimeter Cut-Off Trench 
Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa/Kapolei 
area during prolonged dry weather conditions and were observed at the existing 
ground at the time of our field exploration. Since shrinkage cracks tend to be a conduit 
for water infiltration into the subsurface soils, we recommend implementing special 
site preparation procedures consisting of installing a cut-off trench along the 
perimeter of the grading limits at the project site. We recommend a cut-off trench at 
least 3 feet deep and 5 feet wide consisting of the on-site clayey soils placed and 
compacted with elevated moisture contents, as illustrated on Plates 5.1 and 5.2. As 
an alternative, the cut-off trench may be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength 
Material. 
The on-site soils used for this cut-off trench should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The intent of this cut-off trench is to create a barrier to reduce water 
infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil from the shrinkage cracks on the 
adjacent properties.

3.2 
Building Foundations 
Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked soil 
layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a 
post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. This proprietary system utilizes a series 
of recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes,” which are placed with the open side down on 
the soil. The Waffleboxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings throughout the floor 
system, creating a very stiff foundation. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically 
poured over and between the boxes. The empty space beneath the boxes allows for soil 
expansion, reducing the uplift of the slab. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
may be used for the design of the Wafflemat foundations bearing on compacted reworked 
soil layer or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations presented 
herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Generally, minimum subgrade preparation is required for the Wafflemat foundation 
system. Considering the moderate to high expansive soil conditions, we recommend 
preparing the Wafflemat subgrade in accordance with Section 3.1.1 – Site Preparation 
herein.

3.6.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
The 18-inch capping layer below the pavement sections should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent of particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent 
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material 
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in 
Sections 31 and 30, respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
3.6.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirement for the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least

95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course 
should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional effort for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the 
aggregate base course (for flexible pavement section) or the aggregate subbase 
course (for rigid pavement section) materials using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with 
a gross vehicle weight of at least 30,000 pounds, such as a fully-loaded water 
truck. The equipment used for proof-rolling should be operated at a speed of about 
300 feet per minute (about 3 miles per hour) and make at least two passes over 
each area designated for proof-rolling. Proof-rolling should also be performed on 
successive lifts of aggregate base course or subbase course materials. Areas with 
excessive rutting and/or pumping should be over-excavated to expose firm 
material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with either 
well-compacted fill or aggregate base/subbase material. 
3.6.8 Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas, which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be

3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH value, minimum resistivity, chloride 
content, and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our 
field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. 
The test results are summarized and presented on Plate B-25 of Appendix B. Detailed 
results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests 
performed by Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. are presented in Appendix D. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from approximately 110 to 1,500 ohm-cm and 
pH values of between 6.8 and 8.1 within the upper 5 feet of soils, corresponding to a 
corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) to 2 (Very Corrosive) based on the guidelines 
provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply. Therefore, we 
recommend properly designing near-surface metallic substructures (such as piping) for 
protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent, in part, on the chloride content and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate content tested 
on the in-situ materials, we believe that the near-surface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used 
for the concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult with a

3.8 
Utility Trenches 
It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed development project will consist 
primarily of electrical, water, drain, and sewer lines. In general, good construction 
practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the 
proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The excavation 
should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements. The 
contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation. Trench 
shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining 
backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from 
compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce 
the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site clayey soils as backfill 
directly around the utility pipes is not recommended and should not be allowed. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill material. 
The granular or on-site soil backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content or about 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
respectively, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench backfill below 
the pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
For the deep manhole structures, we recommend the backfill compaction be 
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for

3.9 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed water 
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. Surface 
water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It is highly 
recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert discharge away 
from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations 
and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. Planters next to 
foundations should also be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the 
potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting the 
concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface. In 
addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be 
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 108, extending to depths drilled of 
about 20.3 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The subsurface of the proposed 
elementary school location was explored by drilling and sampling two borings designated 
as Boring Nos. 201 and 202, extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Four bulk samples of the near-surface soils were 
obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. In 
addition, six boreholes, designated as I-101 through I-105 and I-201, were drilled to a depth 
of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface for infiltration testing. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-16.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

A P P E N D I X   B 
Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Eight Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Eight Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Nine one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and/or 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-4. 
Three One-Dimensional Swell or Settlement Potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is remolded 
to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to simulate the 
anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle until it has 
stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil sample to evaluate 
its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-5 through B-7. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, seven Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil 
samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-8 through B-14. 
Two Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D2435) were performed on 
selected samples of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility 
characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are 
presented on Plates B-15 and B-16. 
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-17 through B-20.
