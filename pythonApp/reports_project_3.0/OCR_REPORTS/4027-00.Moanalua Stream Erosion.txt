This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses of the subsurface conditions at the project site located in Moanalua Valley in
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Our findings are based on a site reconnaissance
and the evaluation of three soil samples collected from the existing slope using a portable
soil sampler driven into the ground by a hand hammer These recommendations are
intended for the design of the proposed CRM retaining wall The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Approximate
sampling locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Project Considerations
The project site is located at the southwestern side of Moanalua Golf Course Club
House in Moanalua Valley in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to
construct a concrete reinforced masonry CRM retaining wall approximately 120 feet
long along the northeast embankment of the stream to protect the existing slope from
erosion We understand that the proposed CRM retaining wall will be approximately
2 feet thick with a 6 inch gunite bed and the bottom of the key will be embedded at a
2 foot minimum below the scour line In addition it is proposed to construct a concrete
cap on the top of the wall The streambed is approximately 10 feet below the existing
ground surface

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located at the southwestern side of Moanalua Golf Course Club
House in Moanalua Valley in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 A site visit was conducted by a field engineer from our
office in June 1998 At the time of our visit the water elevation in the stream was
approximately 1 foot in depth In addition silt with scattered cobbles and boulders as
well as tall grass and frogs were observed in the streambed The existing northeast
embankment to be protected appears to have a slope inclination ranging from near
vertical to approximately one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V and is currently covered
with tall grass and a few small Hale Koa trees A vertical CRM retaining wail
approximately 8 feet high has been constructed along the southwest side of the stream
Based on the information provided the elevations at the site range from about
90 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the top of the embankment in the upstream area to
approximately 75 feet at the bottom of the slope at the edge of the stream in the
downstream area of the project limits The stream width varies from approximately 25
feet upstream to approximately 10 feet in the downstream area of the project limits
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by performing a site
reconnaissance and collecting three samples by hand designated as Sample Nos 1
through 3 at depths of about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface along the
exposed stream embankment The approximate sample locations are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
Based on our field exploration and site reconnaissance the ground surface is
covered with grass and approximately 3 to 6 inches of clayey silty soils topsoil
Underlying the surficial soils the existing stream embankment generally consists of
alluvium including clayey silt with sand gravel cobbles and boulders In general the
particle size increased with depth i e gravel grades to cobbles grades to boulders
The water level in the stream was approximately one foot at the time of our site
visit and the streambed approximately 10 feet below the existing ground surface
However it should be noted that waier levels in the stream may be influenced by heavy
rainfall and seasonal precipitation Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered
during sampling are presented in the Sampling Summary in Appendix A

Retaining Walls
Based on the materials sampled the existing stream embankment consists of
alluvial materials including clayey silt with sand gravel cobbles and boulders For the
design of the proposed CRM retaining wall the following guidelines may be used
1 An allowable bearing pressure of 2000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for wall footings bearing on the in situ silty soils This bearing value
may be increased by up to one third for considerations of seismic loads
wind loads and other transient loads The footing embedment should be a
minimum of 24 inches below the stream scour line
2 The bottom of the footing excavation should be firm and unyielding prior to
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The resulting
over excavation should be backfilled with the on site soil properly
moisture conditioned and compacted
3 Resistance to lateral loads may be provided by frictional resistance between
the footing base and the soil subgrade A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may
be used to evaluate the frictional resistance developed between the
footings and the bearing material
4 We recommend that an equivalent fluid pressure of 150 pounds per cubic
foot pcf be used to evaluate the passive earth pressure developed against
the vertical face of foundation However that value may be reduced if
scour occurs
5 The recommended lateral earth pressures for the design of retaining walls
expressed in equivalent fluid pressures are presented in the following table

Based on the proposed design it is assumed that the wall will not be
restrained therefore the walls should be designed for an active condition
These lateral earth pressures do not include hydrostatic pressures that
might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the walls
6 We understand that the retaining walls will be constructed neatly against the
in situ soils with minimal excavation to achieve the desired slope inclination
in addition it is proposed to construct a concrete cap on the top of the wall
In order to minimize water infiltration behind the walls we recommend that
the cap extend a minimum of 42 inches from the face of the wall for a
minimum of 12 inches from the back of the gunite layer of the proposed
wall
7 Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures Therefore based on the proposed design we
recommend that weep holes be installed at a minimum of 10 foot spacing
along the proposed CRM retaining wall for additional drainage
8 Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads
within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the
loaded side of the wall a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure
equal to 35 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire
height of the wall may be used in the design for walls that are free to
deflect Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the
surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
We recommend that foundation excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of the reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Stability Analysis
Based on the proposed sloping design of the CRM retaining wall a stability
analysis was performed on a typical section through the wall The stability analysis was
performed using the computer program XSTABL originally developed by Purdue
University at West Lafayette Indiana In performing the analysis it was assumed that the
on site silty soils would be used behind the wall The computer analysis results indicated
the proposed new CRM wall should have factors of safety greater than 1 5 against slope
stability failure for the four proposed slope inclinations In general a minimum factor of
safety of 1 5 is desired for slope stability

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that Geolabs Hawaii be retained to provide geotechnical
engineering services during construction of the proposed CRM retaining wall The items

recommendations and to expedite suggestions for design changes that may be required
in the event that subsurface conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this letter
report was prepared The recommendations provided in this report are contingent upon
such observations
if actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are
different from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications
to the design should be made
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based in
part upon information obtained from field samples Variations of subsoil conditions
between the sample locations may occur and the nature and extent of these variations
may not become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident
it will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report
