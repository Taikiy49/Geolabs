Geotechnical Engineering Evaluation
Lot No 54 Queen Emma s Bluffs
In accordance with your request this letter report presents our findings of the
geotechnical engineering evaluation for the residence located at Lot No 54 of the Queen
Emma s Bluffs Subdivision Our geotechnical engineering evaluation of the subject
residence is based on our field observations the subsurface information obtained and the
laboratory tests conducted on the soils encountered at the site Our evaluation was
conducted in general accordance with our fee proposal dated October 14
Project Considerations
The subject lot is located at i Place within a residential subdivision in
Princeville on the island of Kauai Hawaii The building was constructed in the early
1990 s following mass grading and development of the subdivision Subsequently the
home has remained unoccupied to this date
The existing house is a 2 story structure of wood framing and continuous footing
construction Based on the available grading plans the building may be constructed on a
cut ground surface with some small fills located on the northern and northwestern
portions of the now level lot Prior to the development of the site the area was utilized
primarily for pasture land

Recent observations of the interior of the home have disclosed signs of foundation
settlement evidenced by floor deflections cracking of the gypboard walls and door
framing being out of square Because the house has been unoccupied for a long period
of time it is uncertain when the deformation actually occurred and whether it may be
continuing It is currently desired to evaluate the site for subsurface conditions that may
be influencing the settlement observed within the home and to estimate the potential for
possible future settlement
Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering evaluation was to obtain an overview
of the subsurface conditions at the project site to develop a generalized soil rock data set
for the formulation of geotechnical engineering opinions regarding the cause s and
potential future movements of the house Since it was desired to avoid damage to the
interior of the house our explorations were conducted along the outside perimeter of the
house In order to accomplish these objectives we have conducted an exploration
generally consisting of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Literature research and review of the history of the project site including the
available construction plans reports and other documents etc
2 Excavation of four shallow hand dug test pits or hand auger borings along
the perimeter of the house Princeville Corporation provided the labor
assistance for the excavation work
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the test pits borings bya
Project Geologist from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data to develop geotechnical
engineering opinions pertinent to the evaluation of possible cause s of the
movements and an assessment of potential future movements of the
house
6 Preparation of four copies of this letter report summarizing our work on the
project and presenting our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations
7 Coordination of our work on the project by a Project Geologist of our firm

Evaluation Findings
A total of four test pits were excavated around the perimeter of the house to
evaluate the soils encountered and to collect undisturbed samples of the materials for
laboratory testing The approximate locations of the test pits are shown on the Site Plan
Sketch provided as Plate 1 of this report
Subsurface Exploration
Two test pits were located close to areas known to have experienced settlement
Additionally two test pits were located at other perimeter areas for comparison of the
subsoil conditions The field exploration was conducted on October 15 under dry
weather conditions The test pits were excavated to depths ranging from 2 to 3 feet
below the ground surface adjacent to the wall footings Relatively undisturbed samples
were collected representing both the fill soils and the underlying native soil deposits For
reference the Logs of Test Pit Excavations are provided in Attachment A and the Results
of Laboratory Tests are provided in Attachment B of this report
In general the soils encountered were a reddish brown clayey silt and dark brown
clayey silt in a moist to very moist condition The soils ranged from medium stiff at the
ground surface grading to stiff to very stiff at depths generally below about 1 foot below
the ground surface Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the reddish
brown soils encountered appear to represent fill soils whereas the darker brown soils
appear to represent the native residual and alluvial soil of the site Based on the
laboratory test results in general the soil moisture appears to be relatively uniform
throughout the depths explored at the site
It should be noted that particular areas of slow water seepage were observed
mainly contained within a thin gravel layer generally encountered just below the buried
wall footing at depths ranging from 11 to 22 inches below ground level In addition some
seepage of water was observed in Test Pit No 1 at a depth of about 1 feet below the
ground surface generally confined to the area located along the fire place wall The
observed seepage water may be a result of excess irrigation water or roof gutter
discharge accumulating or moving through the more permeable gravel subsoil conditions

Site Drainage
Based on our observation of the site drainage it appears that the existing ground
surface topography around the building perimeter especially at the location of roof
downspouts may be causing storm runoff and excess irrigation water to pond against the
foundations The roof downspouts were observed to discharge water directly to
topographic low spots adjacent to the building foundation Some of the downspout bases
were observed blocked by vegetation and root growth creating poor drainage conditions
It is important to note that the more significant settlements appear to be occurring in the
vicinity of these downspout outlets The gutter down spouts may be contributing to
increased soil moisture beneath the foundation
We recommend that the drainage around the perimeter of the house be improved
by extending the outlets for the gutter down spouts to drain away from the foundation to
reduce ponding and saturation of the soils next to the building In addition it is advisable
to evaluate the present sprinkler irrigation system and implement changes to reduce any
irrigation water which may be impacting the house or ponding around the perimeter
Settlement
Based on our review of the subsurface data and laboratory test results it is our
opinion that the majority of the settlement probably occurred shortly after construction of
the house and that most of the settlement may have already occurred The settlement
could have been caused by the consolidation of the very moist native residual and alluvial
soils beneath the foundation Also it is possible the design or the construction of the
foundation system may be undersized for the building load and soil conditions existing at
the site
It is our understanding from the structural engineer that 12 inch wide continuous
footing foundations were utilized to support the house using allowable bearing values of
footing with a bearing pressure of 2500 p s f would be 1 to 2 inches Estimated
settlements for other bearing pressures would be in proportion to the settlements for the
Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field data points such as hand probings Variations of
conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until improvements are made to the property If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations
given in this report
