1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located on the eastern corner of the intersection of South 
Beretania Street and Fort Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  We 
understand that the project includes the installation of two seepage wells and shallow 
trench drains. 
Two seepage wells are planned within the driveway and parking area behind the 
fire station.  Shallow trench drains will be installed along the driveway entrance from 
Fort Street.  Storm water from the building downspouts and the shallow trench drains 
will be directed to the seepage wells.  We understand that the bottom of the wells will be 
about 7 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Based on the topographic survey plan provided, the site slopes down slightly 
towards the north with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about +27 to 
+25.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from 
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older Waianae volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age.  The project site is situated at the intersection of Pauoa and Nuuanu 
Valleys as they open onto the southeastern Oahu Coastal Plain.  The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern third of the 
Island of Oahu.  The project site is located on an accumulation of alluvium, derived from 
erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep 
valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows 
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series.  The Honolulu Volcanic Series, which began less than a 
million years ago (MacDonald and Abbott, 1970), produced numerous cinder and tuff 
cones, and basalt flows, which became inter-layered with the coastal plain deposits.  
The nearby Punchbowl Hill (Puowaina) is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built 
against the end of a spur of the Koolau Range.  The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized 
vitric ash and lapili with scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt.  
Most of the coastal plain developed during the Pleistocene Epoch when the sea 
level experienced fluctuations related to the glacial stages.  As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess 
of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.  
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.  
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations.

The advances and retreats of the sea level produced reef deposits at varying

materials to the east.  A surficial layer of man-made fill was placed over these deposits

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The Central Fire Station is located on a trapezoidal shaped lot encompassing an 
area of approximately 11,445 square feet. About 93 percent of the study area 
(10,635 square feet) is considered impervious by current improvements such as 
concrete slabs and pavement. 
The front and side along Fort Street and South Beretania Street are covered with 
landscape.  At the rear of the station building, a driveway and parking area surfaced 
with concrete pavement were noted. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of 10 and 10.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  We explored the shallow near-surface infiltration conditions by 
performing an infiltration test in each of the two borings.  The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer about 1.5 to 6 feet 
thick underlain by volcanic cinder and coralline detritus extending to the maximum depth 
explored at 10.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The volcanic cinder was not 
encountered in one of the boring. 
The surface fill generally consisted of loose to very dense gravels, sands, and 
cobbles.  The volcanic cinder was composed of loose to medium dense silty sand and 
sand.  The coralline detritus consisted of dense to very dense silty sand with some 
gravel.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a loose to very dense surface fill 
layer underlain by loose to medium dense volcanic cinder, and dense to very dense 
coralline deposits extending to about 10.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Two seepage wells and shallow trench drains are planned for the project. Based 
on the results of the field infiltration testing, it appears that these storm water 
management systems are feasible for the project site.  
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design 
recommendations are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Subsurface Infiltration   
We understand that the project includes a retention and disposal system 
consisting of seepage wells for the on-site management of storm water run-off.  In order 
to obtain subsurface infiltration information in support of the design of the seepage 
wells, we conducted falling head infiltration tests in each of the borings shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
These infiltration tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009).  These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering borings to selected test 
depths about 10 and 10.5 feet below the existing ground surface for the seepage wells.  
Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom 
of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the 
boring.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the infiltration rates of 
the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test consisted of four trials of filling the

Based on the infiltration testing, the infiltration rates ranged from about 8.5 to 
9.5 inches per hour.  Based on these infiltration rates, it is our opinion that the planned 
storm water management improvements are feasible.  It should be noted that the 
infiltration tests were conducted through a 4-inch diameter borehole, which may not 
represent the actual infiltration conditions within the finished seepage well. The results 
of the infiltration tests at the above-specified test locations indicate that an 
infiltration/detention system and similar methods of storm water management would be 
feasible for this project. Due to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the infiltration 
capacity of the disposal system should be confirmed by conducting additional infiltration 
tests during construction. 
3.2 
Construction Considerations  
We anticipate that the seepage wells will be constructed by drilling a hole down 
to the design bottom elevation.  Due to the potential for smearing of the sidewalls of the 
hole during the drilling, we recommend raking that the walls of the well excavation rough 
to minimize the sidewall smearing which reduces the infiltration capacity of the well.  
The bottom of the seepage well should be leveled and not compacted.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analysis and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and infiltration testing.  Variations of 
conditions between and beyond the field borings and infiltration tests may occur, and 
the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway.  If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to reevaluate the

borings were estimated by interpolation from the spot elevations on the same maps.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of 10 to 10.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock cores 
were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on 
the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Infiltration Tests 
In order to provide information on the surface infiltration rates of the on-site soils, 
we conducted two falling head infiltration tests at the boring locations shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2.  These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures 
in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with 
other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The procedure used for the infiltration consisted of augering boreholes to depths 
of about 10 and 10.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  After reaching the test 
depth, a solid PVC pipe, or well casing, was installed in the borehole flush with the 
bottom so that water could only infiltrate into the soil through the exposed bottom soil. 
Water was placed into the open pipe and allowed to pre-saturate the soil prior to 
conducting the infiltration test.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with approximately 24 inches of water and 
taking periodic readings over a one hour trial period.
