SITE DESCRIPTION
The entire project encompasses an area generally bounded by Kokea
Street North School Street and H 1 Freeway The Phase II area is
situated in the northern portion of the site and Phase III area is
situated in the central portion bounded by Palama Street to the
southeast
In general the topography slopes down gently to the southwest with
ground elevations ranging from approximately 34 feet above Mean Sea Level
MSL near School Street to 8 feet near H 1 Freeway
Most of the streets where the proposed improvements are planned are
generally narrow fairly flat and presently paved with asphaltic
concrete
The existing asphaltic concrete pavements are in fair to poor
condition

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas
from two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Waianae the older of
the two reached the end of its activity before Koolau and is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age Koolau however is estimated to
have ceased activity in the early Pleistocene
As volcanic activity on the Island of Oahu drew to a close the
island began to subside slowly While the island was sinking fringing
coral algal reefs began to grow on the seaward margins of the island
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell
in response to the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew the
global sea level fell below the existing level and as the glaciers melted
and retreated the sea level rose to heights above the current stand of
the sea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2043 00 A Page 5
Locally the fringing coral algal reefs continued to grow during the
high stands of the sea leaving fossil reefs and coralline sediments which
are now exposed above sea level Interbedded with these coral algal
materials are alluvial sediments which were transported from the nearby
eroding Koolau Range
As the sea level continued to rise the off shore fringing reef
continued to build and created a lagoon behind it which was later filled
with loose sediments As the lagoon became shallower soft organic
sediments were deposited in a swampy marsh environment
Contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level changes thickly
bedded lava flows from the Honolulu Volcanic Series Makuku vent covered
portions of the southeastern corner of the site
Subsequently as the forces of weathering and erosion continued to
erode the adjacent Koolau land mass to the east alluvial deposits
accumulated forming a thin mantle over most of the site
In more recent years the lowlying marsh areas as well as other
areas at the site appear to have been filled with nearby alluvial soils
and coralline sediments to the present elevations
Subsurface Soil Conditions
Based upon our field exploration the subsurface conditions may be
categorized into five 5 general types of material as described below
1 Fill A surface layer of fill was encountered at various
locations throughout the site and generally consists of loose to
dense silty sands gravels and cobbles intermixed with medium
stiff to stiff clays and clayey silts This fill was probably
derived from nearby sources during the early development of the
site

2 Alluvium Stiff alluvial soils primarily consisting of mottled
dark brown to gray clay with many boulders These clay soils
have high expansion potential
3 Basalt Dense rock formation was encountered below the surface
clay in the southeastern portion of the site
4 Marsh Deposits Very soft organic soils consisting of dark
gray to gray clayey silts and silty clays with root fibres and
humus These soils have generally been capped with a surface
layer of fill
5 Coralline Deposits Loose to very dense tannish white to
tannish brown coralline sands and gravels were encountered in
the western central portion of the site Locally these
sediments are interbedded with loose lagoonal silty sands and
stiff alluvial clays

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration encountered stiff alluvial soils over dense
rock formation in the eastern corner of the site stiff alluvial soils
with many boulders in the northern and central portions and loose to
very dense coralline sediments as well as soft marsh deposits in the
western portion
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2043 00 A Page 7
The near surface stiff alluvial soils and fills generally have high
expansion potential and therefore relatively thick pavement sections
are recommended
In general relatively easy excavation is anticipated for utility
trenches planned in areas underlain by soft marsh deposits stiff
alluvial soils and coralline sediments However a significant amount of
boulders should be anticipated within the alluvial soils and due to the
variability of coralline deposits localized areas of hard reefrock
should also be anticipated Hard excavation is anticipated for the
deeper portions of the trenches in the southeastern corner where rock
formation may be encountered
Soft organic silts and clays were encountered in the western corner
of the site Adequate shoring and bracing are critical in this area to
minimize damage to the existing structures due to lateral ground
movements or failure of the trench walls Groundwater was also
encountered in this area which may require careful dewatering of the
excavations
The existing roadway pavements have deteriorated and in general
are in poor condition In order to provide uniform support for the new
pavement sections the existing pavements should be removed down to the
new pavement section level and reconstructed
The broken up asphaltic concrete could be recycled or stockpiled
near the site if space available for reuse as borrow for trench
backfill or fill The base course layer could also be stockpiled for
future use as a source of select borrow or borrow However care should
be exercised not to contaminate the granular material with the underlying
clayey subgrade soils New base course should be used in the pavement
section

Pavement Design
Based on the results of our field exploration previous experience
and laboratory test results the recommended pavement section is
presented below
2 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 Inch Base Course
18 Inch Select Borrow Subbase
26 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
Note Select borrow subbase course may be omitted where coral sand
and gravel are present at subgrade level
The base course should be crushed rock or other granular material
capable of developing a field CBR value of 85 or more and conforming
with Section 31 Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction
The select borrow should be crusher run or coral with less than 15
passing the 200 sieve and compacted to 95 of its maximum density
The top 6 inches of the subgrade soil should be scarified and
recompacted wet of optimum moisture to 95 compaction The subgrade
soil should not be allowed to become dry and to develop shrinkage cracks
prior to the completion of the pavement placement
The subgrade and pavement sections should be shaped to drain
subsurface water away from the pavement and into suitable drain outlets
We recommend that weepholes be installed in the catch basins at the base
course level in order to allow drainage of accumulated subsurface water

New Utility Lines
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench
the size of the pipe the unit weight of backfill material and the
frictional resistance between the backfill material and the trench
side walls To compute the vertical loads on the pipes a soil unit
weight of 120 p c f and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 is
recommended Earth forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with the
width of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should be
kept to a minimum
Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The underlying stiff alluvium rock formation and coralline
sediments generally have good bearing capacity Therefore minimal
settlements are anticipated for structures supported on these
layers
The new utility line construction in general will not materially
increase the vertical loading upon these underlying soils if no
additional fills are placed upon the existing ground surface The
net difference in loading between the weights of the excavated
material and backfill will be essentially small Therefore
significant settlements are not anticipated if proper construction
techniques are utilized
In soft or loose soil areas some amount of surface settlements may
occur due to disturbance of the subsoils dewatering operations and
increase in unit weight of the trench backfill The amount of
settlement to be experienced depends on the contractor s
construction methods
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2043 00 A Page 10
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great
effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore
the pipe bedding is an important design consideration
For pipes bearing on the rock formation stiff alluvial soils and
dense coralline sediments at the invert level a minimum of six
inches of compacted granular bedding material should be placed below
the pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides
a minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should
extend half way up the pipe sides The bedding material should
consist of clean granular material such as 3B Fine gravel
Subgrade Preparation
Where soft or loose soil conditions are encountered in the trench
bottom western corner of the site stabilization of the soft soils
is required prior to pipe installation To provide uniform support
and a working platform an 18 foot layer of granular material such
as 3B Fine gravel over a geotextile should be provided below the
utility pipe and the bedding layer
Joints and Connections
Due to the various soil conditions which will be crossed by the
pipe flexible pipe joints are recommended to allow for some amount
of differential settlement These flexible connections are
particularly important in the soft ground areas

Trench Excavation
The anticipated depth of excavation is 6 to 8 feet The method and
equipment used for excavation should be the choice of the
contractor within practical limits
Relatively easy excavation is anticipated through the stiff alluvial
soils and loose to dense coralline sediments However a
significant amount of boulders should be anticipated in the alluvial
soils Hard excavation is anticipated in the underlying rock layer
in the southeastern corner of the site and in localized areas
underlain by coralline sediments where harder reefrock may be
encountered
Easy excavation is anticipated in areas underlain by soft marsh
subsoils Sheeting and bracing is required for this portion to
prevent a cave in of the trench walls Due to the low strengths and
high water contents of the soils and the proximity to existing
structures special care must be exercised to prevent a failure of
the trench walls or upheaval of the trench bottom due to the
excavation and dewatering operations Sheetpile or other support
system may be required to obtain trench stability If practical
the excavation should be made in short sections and backfilled as
soon as possible
The excavation should be provided with proper bracing in accordance
with the anticipated loading conditions traffic and construction
materials stockpile and equipment and to satisfy the applicable
safety requirements The design and safety of the trench supports
should be the responsibility of the contractor
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2043 00 A Page 12
The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should be
continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of
movements and possible sloughing of the trench walls Such
monitoring is particularly critical in areas where existing
structures and utilities are adjacent to the trench excavation
Dewatering
Groundwater was encountered in the western portion of the site at
depths ranging between 5 9 and 10 0 feet below the existing ground
surface
The method of dewatering should be left open to the contractor
provided adequate stability of the excavation is maintained The
marsh deposits encountered in the borings in the western portion
consisted mostly of organic clays and silts with some silty sand
The project dewatering operation should be done without pumping out
the soil fines pumping clear water only It should be coordinated
with the excavation support system so that the stability of the
excavation is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out
without creating a quick condition or softening the bottom of the
excavation
Another alternative to dewatering would be to place the pipe ina
wet trench condition
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be in accordance with the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction September
1986
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2043 00 A Page 13
Granular material such as sand should be used for the backfill up
to about one foot above the pipe to reduce the compactive effort
required and possible damage to the pipe
Most of the excavated material will consist of clay soils and
boulders which may not be suitable for reuse as backfill
Therefore imported borrow material may be required Backfill
should be mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum
dary density established by ASTM D 1557 Below roadway areas the
backfill within 2 feet of the finished grade should consist of
select borrow and be compacted to 95 of its maximum dry density
If practicable the trenches should be kept free of water during
backfilling
Manhole Structures
The manhole structures could bear on the hard rock formation dense
coralline sediments or stiff alluvium Structures supported on the
underlying rock formation or dense coralline sediments may be designed
for an allowable bearing value of 5000 p s f Structures supported on
stiff alluvium may be designed for 3000 p s f Minimal settlements are
anticipated for these structures
Where it is necessary to place the manhole structures on the soft
marsh soil or loose sandy soils an 18 inch layer of gravel 3B Fine
over a geotextile should be provided below the structure to provide
uniform support An allowable bearing value of 1500 p s f may be used
on the 18 inch gravel layer The increase in load on the subsoils due to
the manhole structures is small Therefore large settlements are not
anticipated if proper shoring and dewatering techniques are utilized
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2043 00 A Page 14
The lateral earth pressure acting on the underground structures will
depend on the type of backfill used the extent of the backfill material
and the compactive effort required on the material behind the structure
walls Granular material is recommended for the backfill adjacent to the
structures to reduce the compactive effort required and to minimize the
build up of additional lateral earth pressures
For the design of manhole structures the following lateral earth
pressures are recommended
Backfill above water 50 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
Backfill below water 25 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
plus water pressure

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs
at the appropriate sample depths
Six 6 one inch ring swell tests were performed on undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the natural soils
The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Four 4 Atterberg Limit tests were performed to aid in the
classification of the soils The test results are summarized on Plates
B 2 and B 3
Three 3 sieve analyses were performed on samples of the underlying
sandy soils to determine their grain size distributions to evaluate the
general permeability of the soils The test results are summarized on
Plates B 2 and B 3
One 1 California Bearing Ratio CBR test was performed on samples
of the near surface soils to evaluate their suitability for road subgrade
support The test results are summarized on Plate B 3
