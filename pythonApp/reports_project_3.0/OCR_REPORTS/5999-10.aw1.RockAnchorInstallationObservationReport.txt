PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The new Information Technology Center project at the University of Hawaii at 
Manoa is located in the District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. It is bounded by 
Bilger Hall to the north, the Bilger Hall Addition to the west, the Physical Sciences Building 
to the east, and Correa Road to the south. The new building will be seven stories high with 
a footprint of up to 12,500 square feet at the Data Center level. It will house the 
system-wide data and telecommunications infrastructure, IT public services facilities, and 
administrative and staff office spaces. Along the south side of the exterior of the building, 
there will be a canopy supported on columns. 
Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, 
the structural demand for uplift at the canopy support columns, we recommend installing a 
foundation system consisting of rock anchors to support the new canopy supports planned 
for the project. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the new Information Technology Center project site 
and generally consisted of a surface fill layer, consisting of about 3 to 9 feet of very stiff to 
hard silty clays. Below the surface fill soils is a generally massive and hard basaltic rock 
formation which extended to the maximum depth explored as noted in the referenced 
Geolabs, Inc. Report dated April 21, 2008. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to seasonal precipitation, runoff, and other factors. 
ROCK ANCHOR INSTALLATION 
Rock anchors were used to provide the necessary uplift load capacity for the 
canopy supports during construction of the new Information Technology Center project. 
Geotechnical recommendations for the rock anchor foundations were presented in our 
Addendum dated May 25, 2010 prepared for the project. The purpose of our geotechnical 
engineering observation and consultation services was to observe the rock anchor 
foundation installation for general conformance with our recommendations. 
The rock anchor subcontractor for the project was Pacific Ground Systems, Inc. 
The rock anchors were installed by using hollow drill rods with 5 or 8-inch diameter drill bits 
with side discharge holes for water and grout discharge. Each rock anchor hole was 
advanced by rotary percussion using a top hammer mounted on a Casagrande C6 
hydraulic crawler. Drilling fluids consisting of a neat-cement grout mixture and/or clean 
water were used to flush the soil cuttings from the hole. Once the subcontractor has 
reached the design depth for the rock anchor, the subcontractor reduced the water cement 
ratio of the grout mixture to the proper 0.4 to 0.5 range and continued to pump the grout 
through the hollow drill rods to create a grout bulb in the drilled hole. Grouting of the

The following deviations from the construction documents were observed and 
noted during the course of the rock anchor installation process: 
Due to the limited capability of the drilling equipment, the subcontractor has 
requested to reduce the diameter of Rock Anchor Nos. RA-5 and RA-6 from 
8 inches to 5 inches.  Based on our engineering analyses, we recommend 
that the anchor length for these two anchors be extended by an additional 
4 feet for a total of 17 feet below the footing bottom in order to develop the 
design uplift load capacity of 30 kips (15 tons) per rock anchor. 
The planned Rock Anchor No. RA-7 at gridline A/3.95 was eliminated due to 
the presence of an existing utility line running directly beneath the footing 
location. 
Based on our field observations, we believe the rock anchors installed and 
accepted for the project have been constructed in general conformance with the intent of 
the project plans and specifications and within the limits of generally accepted construction 
practices. We believe the rock anchors for the project have been installed to the required 
diameter and depth to provide the design uplift load capacity of 30 kips per rock anchor.
