INTRODUCTION
In accordance with our fee proposal of July 27th and the Underground Injection
Control U I C permit application No UH 1922 we have completed our visual
observations during construction of 5 of the 13 new drywells planned for the Kailua
Landfill Closure Phase 3 Construction In addition we have performed five injection
tests for the new drywells This engineering report has been prepared in support of
Underground Injection Control permit application No UH 1922 The approximate
location of the project site and vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field observations and testing of a portion
of the new drywells at the project site In addition the as built depths of the 13 project
drywells are summarized and provided with this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Kailua Landfill project site is located in Kealakehe North Kona Hawaii The
site is a municipal landfill operated by the County of Hawaii Department of Public
Works Solid Waste Division The Kailua Landfill Closure Phase 3 Construction
includes construction of a final cover and site access road over the top portion of the
landfill construction of surface water ditches swales drain dips and drywells
installation of chain link fencing and gates installation of vertical gas extraction wells
and construction of the gas collection and monitoring systems
In general the final cover system was constructed on the top portion of the
landfill and covered approximately 3 5 acres of the 18 acre landfill site The final cover
system involved subgrade preparation and installation of a geomembrane liner
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3491 00 Page 2
12 inches of drainage soil and 12 inches of topsoil hydro seeded with grass The
drainage system improvements consist of the installation of surface water drainage
ditches and 13 drywells
As stated in the Keahole to Kailua Development Plan runoff generated within
each area will be disposed of within each area by means of infiltration into the
ground Sheet flow should be used and concentration of runoff should be avoided
Therefore the County of Hawaii requires that new development create no net increase
in discharge of surface or storm water runoff The general practice on the island of
Hawaii is to utilize drywells as a method of disposal of surface runoff
As part of the Phase 3 construction a total of 13 new drywells have been
constructed for disposal of storm water runoff under UIC permit application No UH
1922 Under the conditions set forth in the approval to construct the new drywells the
Department of Health DOH had required that five of the drywell excavations be logged
during construction for geologic lithology Also the five drywells were required to
undergo short term injection testing to evaluate their operational status after
construction A total of five drywells were injection tested

GENERAL INFORMATION
The project site is located approximately 3 miles north of Kailua Town in the
District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii The landfill footprint limits of refuse
occupies approximately 18 acres of the 27 acre landfill facility located east of Queen
Ka ahumanu Highway The Kailua Police Station is located immediately adjacent to the
landfill northeast of the site The drywells are situated on the parcel identified by Tax
Map Key 3rd Division Por 16 The drywells are located surrounding the
perimeter of the closed landfill The approximate location of the 13 new drywells are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 3
Based on information obtained from the State Department of Land and Natural
Resources Commission on Water Resource Management there are no known
extraction wells used as sources of potable water within mile of the landfill site
Drinking water in this portion of the island of Hawaii is obtained from wells tapping the
basal aquifers in Hualalai Basalts The new drywells were installed in a surficial basalt
formation that appears to be Hualalai Basalt

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The site is located in Kealakehe District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii
The climate of the area is mild with an average annual temperature of about 74 F and
annual rainfall ranging from about 20 to about 40 inches per year Rainfall at the site
typically occurs as daily light afternoon showers except during periods of stormy
weather where heavier rainfall can be expected
The site is situated on the lower western flank of Mount Hualalai The
topography is relatively gentle to moderate sloping down toward the ocean westward
from the central portion of the island Elevations across the site range from about 100
to about 230 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The site had been a municipal landfill which was recently closed Formerly the
land had been undeveloped rocky land covered with brush and scattered small trees
At the time of the drywell logging and injection testing the Phase 3 construction was
nearing conclusion The five drywells which were injection tested were completed and
are in a functional state
Regional Geology
The island of Hawaii is composed of five basalt shield volcanoes built by the
extrusion of lavas during the Pleistocene and the Holocene Epochs The site is located
on the western flank of Mount Hualalai a now dormant volcano which last erupted
between 1801 to 1803 This mountain is thought to be of an intermediate age between
Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa its larger neighboring mountains Hualalai appears to
have entered the late stage of the eruptive cycle Rocks of Hualalai Volcano cover a
portion of the western side of the island of Hawaii Most of its lavas are alkalic olivine
basalt and physically are composed of both pahoehoe and Aa flow types

Earthquake Conditions
The island of Hawaii is designated in the 1994 Uniform Building Code as
Seismic Zone 3 Seismic Zone 3 is considered to be an active zone of earthquake
occurrence Most of the earthquakes that occur on the island of Hawaii are related to
subsurface magma movement and are termed volcanic earthquakes
The drywells were constructed in a relatively competent layered rock formation
with some rock fill The drywells generally consist of simple drilled holes which are
unlined except for the upper portions of the drywell constructed in fill material These
potentially less stable zones were lined with a single perforated precast concrete ring
of approximately 6 feet in diameter Due to the below grade construction of the
drywells and placement of a precast concrete ring in potentially less stable zones of the
excavation seismic conditions are not considered significant No buildings or other
structures were observed located near the constructed drywells
Flood and Tsunami
Based on the grading plans site grading to provide positive drainage of the site
has been provided In addition the site is located inland of and at a higher elevation
than the expected tsunami inundation zone for this portion of the island of Hawaii
Therefore we believe that tsunami flooding should not adversely affect the operation
of the drywells
A system of drainage ditches culverts and berms have been constructed to
discharge runoff to the drywells for subsurface disposal The drainage culverts were
designed to discharge runoff below the perimeter roadway for disposal to the drywells

Land Use
The site is situated on land which is below the Underground Injection Control
Line for this portion of the island of Hawaii Solid waste had been disposed of at the
Kailua Landfill since about the mid 1970s ending in early 1995 The original grade at
the site was generally level to gently sloping and refuse was placed over the original
grade with little excavation of the native materials Lava tubes are known to exist
below the landfill at varying depths Parametrix 1995 The site is in a suburban area
zoned for commercial development The landfill site is permanently closed to refuse
disposal therefore construction and use of drywells at the site is considered to be
consistent with this use
SYSTEM OPERATION
It is proposed to use the new drywells for disposal of storm water runoff flowing
from the covered landfill surface and paved access roadway Use of the drywells will
be intermittent and variable and will be dependent upon weather conditions and storm
water runoff quantities
The influent will not be treated prior to disposal and the volume and rate of
injection will vary according to the intensity of rainfall Based on a 50 year storm event
Q the design maximum capacity is 6 cubic feet per second per drywell For the
drywell system consisting of 13 drywells the maximum design capacity is 78 cubic
feet per second Injection will be under gravity pressure i e the head pressure that
builds up within the depth of the drywell Since the new drywells are intended for
intermittent disposal of storm water runoff only there is no additional standby system
nor are monitoring wells planned

The complete drywell system consists of 13 drywells around the perimeter of the
landfill site Stormwater is intercepted by a system of ditches which divert the runoff
to the drywells for disposal Culverts have been constructed to transmit the runoff
beneath the access roadways to the drywells The 13 drywells for this project are
located down gradient of the Underground Injection Control Line
GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The site is centrally located at Latitude North and Longitude 156 00
21 West The ground surface elevations at the site range from approximately 100
to about 220 feet MSL Subsurface conditions encountered were basalt rock
formation generally consisting of interbedded clinker and massive to fractured
pahoehoe basalt rock Groundwater was not observed in the drywells selected for
observation or testing during our field evaluation
The regional characteristic of groundwater occurrence in the area is fresh to
brackish water in a basal volcanic aquifer of relatively high permeability Published
data indicates that the groundwater table slopes towards the ocean at a gradient of
about one foot per mile Regional discharge is generally along the coastline
The lithologies of the 5 logged drywell excavations are presented in Appendix
A of this report All depths reported in the logs were measured from the existing
ground surface to the bottom of the drywell after construction
WELL CONSTRUCTION
Generally the drywells encountered interbedded layers of hard Pahoehoe and
medium hard A a clinker basalt of the Hualalai Volcanic Series Some small voids were
encountered by the drywell excavation In general the drywells were constructed by
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3491 00 Page 9
drilling an approximately 6 foot diameter core and excavating the rock core or fractured
rock materials The drywells were constructed by Tri S Inc from September 1st
through the 29th The sidewall of the drywells were generally smooth although some
irregularity of the diameter exists within the clinker zones of the excavation
The drilling method of drywell construction was observed to have produced some
residual fine waste material which was deposited in the natural fractures of the rock
formation during drilling These fine deposits may act to reduce the permeability of the
formation until it becomes washed out by regular use of the drywell An increase in the
permeability may be observed at that time
Based on our observations of the five selected drywells the entire depth of the
drywells were unlined except for the placement of a single precast perforated concrete
ring at the top of the drywell immediately below the concrete drywell cover The rings
were placed in drywells where the upper portion of the drywell was constructed in fill
material The rings were placed to provide stability for the excavation and the concrete
cover A mixture of crushed rock and cobble material along with some cinder sand
and gravel were used as backfill behind the concrete rings The 13 completed drywells
range in depth from about 19 6 feet to 21 0 feet below the finished ground surface
The as built constructed depths of the total 13 drywells are provided in
Appendix C This information was provided by following the
completion of drywell construction
INJECTION TESTING
The subsurface permeability at the site was tested by the injection of clear fresh
water from a water tank truck with a maximum capacity of approximately 4 000 gallons
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3491 00 Page 10
Injection rates were measured by use of an in line paddle wheel flow sensor During
testing clean water was injected at averaged rates of about 180 to 190 gallons per
minute for a period of about 16 to 19 minutes depending on the flow rate for each
injection test During the injection of water the variations of the water level in the
drywells were recorded with respect to time and rate of injection Following the
conclusion of the test the time lapse for the drywell to completely drain the water was
also recorded Groundwater was not observed in any of the excavated drywells prior
to the beginning of the injection testing
These tests indicate that at an average injection rate of about 190 gallons per
minute the basalt formation has an approximate coefficient of permeability k with a
range on the order of approximately 230 to 1 280 feet per day The measured head
level rise in response to injection ranged from zero to about 82 inches from the bottom
of the drywell The high variability of the calculated subsurface permeabilities may be
due in part by the method of drywell construction used and the nature of the subsurface
materials The core drilling of the drywell bores provided a smooth equi dimensional
excavation in the massive rock formation however the coring process appeared to
have generated an irregular excavation surface when intersecting clinker layers
throughout the drywell The results of the injection tests are presented in Appendix B
