PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Ewa Beach Sewers Section 4 Identification ID No 278 project is located on
the eastern and northern sides of Fort Weaver Road within an existing residential
subdivision in the Ewa Beach area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Currently the existing
subdivision is serviced by cesspool systems and it is proposed to replace the cesspool
systems with a new gravity sewer system
Based on the prefinal project drawings the proposed sewer lines will be installed
with invert depths up to 10 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Based on the borings performed
for the project we anticipate that the new sewer lines will be installed within the underlying
coral formation and coralline materials which are typically highly permeable materials
Therefore difficult dewatering conditions may be encountered while installing the sewer
lines below the groundwater table within these deposits
We understand it is desired to evaluate the use of trenchless installation methods
for the project In particular the use of pilot tube microtunneling

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located within an existing residential subdivision The
subdivision consists of one to two story single family homes relatively narrow roadways
paved with asphaltic concrete AC pavement and numerous cul de sacs Apart from
North Road both sides of the residential roadways are used for street parking All the
residential roads have one lane of traffic in each direction However due to the width of
some of the streets and the parked cars on either side some of the roadways are
effectively one lane
Based on our field reconnaissance observations the condition of the existing
AC pavement ranged from poor to good Main thoroughfares such as North Road
showed little signs of potholes or cracking and generally looked better maintained than
some of the smaller side streets Residential streets farther away from the main roads
such as Hanaloa Street exhibited signs of distress such as previously filled potholes
longitudinal cracking and alligator cracking
In general the site gently slopes down from north to south with existing ground
elevations of approximately 20 to 8 feet MSL respectively

TRENCHLESS INSTALLATION METHODS
Trenchless installation methods consisting of Horizontal Directional Drilling HDD
microtunneling and pilot tube microtunneling were evaluated for the project Previously
HDD was evaluated for a low pressure sewer line installation and presented in our report

dated Further evaluation was performed for the planned gravity sewer

Although tighter tolerance for line and grade can be achieved with current HDD
installations we believe that utilizing the HDD method for the gravity sewer line
installation would not be feasible due to the numerous lateral connections to the houses
that need to be made An excavation pit would be needed for each of the lateral
connections to the main sewer line Due to the short distance to the houses we envision
that conventional trenching would be required for the laterals
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine TBM capable of installing
underground pipes at relatively accurate lines and grades Jacking and receiving pits are
required to accommodate the TBM and other microtunneling equipment As the TBM
excavates the soils the pipe behind the TBM is being pushed simultaneously by hydraulic
jacks The excavated material is removed during the microtunneling process by a mixture
of water and slurry which is disposed either through a pipe or placed on a screw conveyor
system
The microtunneling method of pipe installation is the most costly compared to the
other trenchless methods being considered The jacking and receiving pits required for
microtunneling are larger in size and more costly compared to the pilot tube
microtunneling method Due to the numerous lateral connections the number of pits
required would be significant Therefore we believe that this trenchless method of
installation is not feasible for the project
Pilot Tube Microtunneling PTMT employs the pipe jacking and guidance system
used for microtunneling the steering method used for horizontal directional drilling and
the cuttings removal system used in auger boring for the installation of pipes up to
30 inches in diameter This method of pipe installation is capable of installing the pipe to
the current standard of line and grade requirements for gravity sewer lines The maximum
drive length using this method is about 400 feet and will depend on the ground conditions
and PTMT operator s skill
The PTMT method typically consists of drilling a pilot hole about 4 inches in
diameter A target with electric light emitting diodes is mounted in the steering head and
the head position is monitored using a camera mounted theodolite or laser and TV
monitor The slanted surface of the drill head is used for steering After completion of the
pilot hole the borehole is enlarged by a reamer and auger casing with augers
We believe that the PTMT method is the preferred trenchless method compared
to HDD and microtunneling methods However this trenchless method may be costly
compared to the conventional trenching method due to the numerous lateral connections
The PTMT method may be considered for difficult crossings using a conventional
trenching method such as below large or deep existing structures

PILOT TUBE MICROTUNNELING
In general the sections of utility lines that would benefit the most from trenchless
installations would be utility lines with deep invert elevations below the groundwater table
utility lines crossing underneath large drainage culverts and sections without any laterals
and or chimney connections Pilot tube microtunneling installation considerations are
provided below
Anticipated Tunneling Conditions
Evaluation of the subsurface conditions is important in assessing the viability and
feasibility of PTMT techniques and in determining appropriate construction techniques
For the design and cost estimation pertaining to PTMT special attention should be given
to the characteristics of the soils that are likely to be encountered Some of the important
factors relating to the subsurface soil conditions that may significantly influence
construction costs include face or heading stability settlement potential and magnitudes
allowable passive pressures for providing thrust block resistance groundwater control
muck characteristics and or special disposal requirements
Based on our previous field explorations the new sewer alignments will likely
traverse through the medium hard to hard coral formation and medium dense to dense
coralline detritus encountered in our drilled borings Localized sections of very soft to
loose coralline materials may be encountered especially near the intersection of North
Road and Haiamu Street
Potential Tunneling Difficulties
An assessment of the ground conditions likely to be encountered along the PTMT
excavations is important for evaluating the face stability of the excavations and to provide
information on the selection of appropriate equipment and methods for the tunnel and
shaft construction The ground conditions should be assessed to reduce the potential for
loss of ground surface settlement and damage to existing utilities buildings roadways
and other improvements as a result of the planned tunneling operations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our previous field explorations
we believe that key geotechnical engineering considerations for the PTMT construction
method would be difficult drilling through the hard coral formation and localized very soft
to loose coralline materials For tunneling conditions in the loose coralline materials below
the groundwater table significant loss of ground resulting in potential surface settlement
could occur unless appropriate guided boring equipment is used
Surface settlement is usually considered to be the primary source of damage to
adjacent streets utilities and other surface facilities during tunneling operations
Empirical methods have been developed for estimating surface settlement due to soft
ground tunneling by the study of observed settlements on past projects The typical
settlement pattern that develops above a soft ground tunnel is a trough shaped
depression resembling an inverted normal probability curve with the maximum settlement

Jacking and Receiving Pits
The presence of hard coral formation may pose difficulties during construction of
the jacking and receiving pits Based on the subsurface conditions at the site we believe
that the use of interlocking steel sheet piles for shoring of the jacking and receiving pits
would be appropriate for this site Considerations may also be given to the use of concrete
caissons for the jacking and receiving pits Due to the subsurface conditions at the site
we recommend that the sheet pile locations for excavation support be predrilled in order
to achieve the required penetration below the bottom of the excavation
Due to the potentially variable subsurface conditions anticipated along the new
sewer line alignments each jacking and receiving pit should be designed individually by
the contractor for the appropriate lateral loads and the subsurface conditions In addition
it should be noted that care should be exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility
lines during the installation of the steel sheet piles
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the materials from within the jacking and receiving pits near the ground surface based on
the subsurface conditions encountered However it should be noted that there is the
potential to encounter hard to very hard coral formation and deeper excavations may
require predrilling or chipping and hoeraming
It should be emphasized that the excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer
than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the
excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement toward the
excavation

Thrust Block Resistance
The thrust block at the jacking pit provides the reaction for the main jacks when
pushing the pipe forward The allowable passive pressure resistance of the soil which is
a function of the strength of the soil will control the thrust resistance that can be
developed It may seem reasonable to allow the thrust block to develop the full passive
pressure resistance because this is a temporary construction loading condition However
it must be recognized that relatively large strains will be required in order to develop the
maximum passive pressure resistance At the jacking loads that impose stresses
approaching the maximum passive pressure resistance there may be large movements
of the thrust block in response to the strains required to develop full passive pressure
resistance
In addition large movements of the thrust block are not desirable for pipe jacking
because the movements are usually not uniform and it becomes difficult to maintain the
thrust block perpendicular to the pipe alignment If the thrust block does not remain
square the force applied to the pipe may not be uniform resulting in eccentric loading
that could damage the pipe
Movements of the thrust block can also be controlled by limiting the allowable
passive pressure resistance to about one half of the maximum passive pressure
resistance This corresponds to a much lower level of strain and smaller soil movements
strain compatible design If the project requires higher jacking forces than can be safely
transferred to the soil mass behind the thrust block then the soil mass should be improved
or stabilized
Other Considerations
Excavation and construction works should conform to Federal State and other
safety requirements The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with
all applicable safety requirements It is recommended that the contractor retain a qualified
geotechnical engineer to evaluate and design the shoring system used The adequacy
and safety of the shoring installation is the responsibility of the contractor His her
representative who should be required to be continuously present on site during the
excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to promptly observe any
changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsurface conditions
unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or
techniques etc which may adversely affect shoring stability
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring
system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
the excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to estimate
accurately the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground movements
also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring
supports are installed the subsurface conditions the size of the excavation and the rate
of excavation

DEWATERING
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation Groundwater was encountered at
depths ranging between about 5 5 to 18 5 feet below the existing ground surface
corresponding to elevations of about 0 5 to 3 0 feet MSL Based on the groundwater
levels encountered dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract
documents for the proposed construction Since the excavation may involve discharge
of groundwater a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may
be required for this discharge The contractor should consult their independent consultant
for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the shoring support
such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations should be
carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
