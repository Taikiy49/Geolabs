PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves design and reconstruction of the existing Nuuanu Stream 
channel wall along the River Street Mall between the pedestrian bridge and North Kukui 
Street. The existing channel wall was constructed in the 1930’s of quarried basalt blocks 
and has undergone lateral displacement resulting in sidewalk settlement behind wall. 
Based on the as-built plan provided, we understand that the bottom of the existing 
cement rubble masonry (CRM) wall was founded at an elevation of approximately -4 feet 
with sheet piling on the stream side extending to an elevation of -24 feet. We understand 
an approximate 183-foot long section of the CRM wall is required to be stabilized. Due to 
historical channel wall feature, replacement of the damaged wall is not desirable. 
Based on the topographic map provided, the existing ground elevations along this 
portion of the River Street Mall range from +7 to +8 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
Consideration was given to characterize the subsurface compressibility for design of CRM 
wall stabilization repair.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is largely composed of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae

Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is 
partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its geomorphology 
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea 
levels during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The 
subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age 
coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits (laid during high stands of the sea) that are 
intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in this area 
is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as 
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers melted, global sea levels rose because 
more water was available. The landmass of Oahu remained essentially stable during these 
changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations 
resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea 
level on Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, growth of reef deposits at 
corresponding higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal 
sediments may also have resulted in deposits of coral/algal limestone and marls within 
the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary unit. 
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused 
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of 
the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high-energy 
conditions. During periods of insignificant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal 
sediments. 
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the 
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of 
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series, including Aliamanu-Salt Lake-Makalapa, 
Kamanaiki, Punchbowl, Roundtop-Tantalus-Sugarloaf and other minor vents. These 
post-erosional events following the cessation of shield building were contemporaneous 
with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations. 
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of 
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions, which expelled large 
quantities of pyroclastic material - predominantly ash and cinder. Some of the eruptions

About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that 
rise, the deep valleys in the project vicinity were drowned. In the last 10,000 years, the 
sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have 
continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in 
thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly 
valleys and drainageways. 
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have 
brought the Honolulu area to its present form. A great many of those projects were 
originally constructed for commercial, residential, and/or military development. Many of 
the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting structural loads.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the as-built drawings, the existing CRM wall has a retaining height of 
about 12 feet. Weepholes are shown on the as-built drawing, but not observed on the 
existing CRM wall. Due to the historical structure, we understand that replacement of 
the existing CRM wall is not feasible. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered in the borings and our field 
observation, we believe that the excessive lateral earth pressure with hydrostatic 
pressure caused damage/distress to the existing CRM channel wall. To reduce lateral 
earth pressure on the existing CRM retaining wall, we recommend that a flexible wall 
system, such as Geotextile Fabric Reinforcing Fills (GFRF), be designed and installed 
behind the existing CRM wall. To minimize the hydrostatic pressure, we recommend 
installing a subdrain system near the bottom of the GFRF. The following contains our 
recommendations for the GFRF retaining structure planned at the project site. 
The geotextile fabric reinforcing fills system is a composite wall system, which 
utilizes woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi PET 70/70 or equivalent, to reinforce the 
backfill zone and to improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone.  This composite 
system essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to carry appreciable 
structural loads.  In addition, GFRF is desirable for ease of construction, high load carrying 
capacity, and economy. The flexible retaining system is capable of accommodating ground 
movements. 
In general, the reinforced zone of the GFRF should extend laterally behind the wall 
face to a distance of approximately 10 feet to provide adequate factor of safety for global 
slope stability.  In addition, the geotextile fabric should be placed at a maximum vertical 
spacing of 18 inches.  The front face of the geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 
3 feet from the face of the wall.  We recommend placing 12-inch wide 3B Fine Gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) between the GFRF and the existing CRM wall. The 12-inch 
3B Fine Gravel zone should be capped with 18-inch impervious backfill. A typical section 
of the GFRF system is presented on Plate 3. 
The bottom of the excavation should be stabilized with 18-inch, open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) wrapped by non-woven geotextile fabric, such as 
Mirafi 180N or equivalent.  
The backfill within the reinforced zone may consist of the select granular fills 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  A unit weight of 125 pounds 
per cubic foot (pcf) may be used for the compacted select granular fills for design 
purposes.  The reinforced fill material should have an angle of internal friction of at least 
34 degrees when compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  A coefficient of 
friction of 0.45 may be used to evaluate the resistance to sliding. 
We recommend installing a 2-foot wide strip of prefabricate drainage product, such 
as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, placed on the face of the cut slope.  The prefabricated

optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction in accordance with ASTM D1557 due to the soft characteristic of the 
subgrade material anticipated. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

evaluate the compaction quality of the fill material. 
Excavation 
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment used for 
excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. Based on our field 
exploration and the available information, the surface soils and underlying soft 
lagoonal/estuarine deposits encountered in the borings may be excavated with 
conventional earthmoving equipment. However, it should be noted that some larger 
boulders may be embedded in the estuarine deposits, especially near the stream bed. 
Excavation in these materials may require the use of heavy excavation equipment. 
Due to the soft compressible deposit encountered in the borings, the excavated 
soils should not be stockpiled on site, to reduce the potential of appreciable ground 
settlement and/or movement. In addition, the compressible lagoonal/estuarine deposits 
may have organic contents, which are unsuitable for fills. Therefore, we recommend 
hauling the excavated soils off site. 
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 8 to 10 feet may be required for 
the project construction. Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered in the 
borings, the contractor should carefully evaluate the shoring system accordingly, to 
protect personnel working in the excavations. In addition, the existing cracked CRM wall 
sections should be protected from falling during excavation. 
The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the 
shoring installation. His/her representative, who should be required to be continuously 
present on site during excavation and construction work, will have the best opportunity 
to promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such as unforeseen

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 52 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made 
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits to aid in soil classifications. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two Consolidation tests (ASTM D2435) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on-site soils.  The test 
results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
