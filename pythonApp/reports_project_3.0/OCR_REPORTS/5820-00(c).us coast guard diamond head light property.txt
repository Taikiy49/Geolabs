PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is within the existing United States Coast Guard Diamond Head
Light property adjacent to Diamond Head Road in the Honolulu area of the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The site is on hillside terrain and is bounded by Diamond Head Road toward the
north and the shoreline toward the south Deep drainage ravines also border the property
on the eastern and western ends The hillside is composed of cemented calcareous
conglomerate and some volcanic tuff materials
Portions of the existing CRM walls adjacent to existing structures at the upper
portion of the property are experiencing some ground erosion and undermining of the
retaining wall footings In addition coastal erosion along the base of the hillside at the
shoreline has developed some slope undercutting conditions and localized collapse of
earth materials Therefore a geotechnical engineering evaluation of the existing erosion
conditions at the central and coastal portions of the site was performed to provide
recommendations for probable conceptual mitigation options

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu

During the later Pleistocene Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the
oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea levels rose because more
water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable during these
changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations
resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to present sea
level on Oahu
The project site is adjacent to Diamond Head Road on the lower southerly facing
slopes of Diamond Head Crater as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Diamond
Head is a remnant volcanic cone generally composed of layered volcanic tuff mudrock
and some cemented conglomerate materials The volcanic tuff cone was created by
cemented ash and cinder fallout which was explosively emitted from the crater during late
stage hydromagmatic interaction of water with magma explosions that ended
approximately 700 000 years ago Because the vent had blasted through older underlying
layers of basaltic rock and reef limestone the ash and cinders comprising the tuff contain
an appreciable amount of entrained fragments of basalt rock and coralline reef materials
Compositional and grain size layering is commonly visible in exposed profiles of the tuff
materials Exposure to rainfall and percolating groundwater is responsible for some
decomposition of the tuff cementing agent which often leads to raveling of exposed loose
materials and the development of post depositional fracturing along weakened surfaces
within the rock mass
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is bounded by Diamond Head Road toward the north and the
ocean toward the south The project site encompasses a rectangular shaped property on a
southerly facing slope which is bounded by deep gulches at the eastern and western
ends The upper half of the property is generally level and contains the lighthouse living
structures various parking areas and a landscaped garden The lower half of the property
contains vacant moderate to steeply sloping land extending down toward the shoreline
and narrow beach below An existing CRM retaining wall extends across the property
along the southern limit of the improved portion of the property containing the structures

The second area of reported concern encompasses an approximate 160 lineal foot
long section of coastline near the southeastern corner of the property where shoreline
slope undercutting is occurring Within this section of shoreline slope an approximately
40 foot long section of the shoreline slope has experienced a recent failure involving about
a dozen blocks of soft volcanic tuff rock which have broken from the steepened slope just
above the narrow sandy beach The approximate area of existing shoreline slope
undercutting and fallen rock is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The generally rectangular and equal dimensional fallen blocks of rock range in size
between about 4 and 8 feet in length by about 3 to 4 feet wide The fallen blocks of rock
are composed of brown volcanic tuff which may be characterized as a relatively soft rock
material A light tan cemented calcareous conglomerate material forms a distinct
horizontal bedding contact with the underlying tuff formation The calcareous
conglomerate appears to extend higher up towards the existing structures The blocks of
volcanic tuff appear to have fallen from about 3 to 5 feet above the beach level The block
failures appear to be a result of an active naturally occurring undercutting condition along
the base of the slope The undercut condition is caused by the abrasion and movement of
sand and ocean waves that encroach during periods of higher tide and surf conditions
The unsupported mass of the overhanging volcanic tuff apparently caused the material to
break and topple along a generally planar failure surface coincident with the depth of the
undercut
Based on our observations the undercut appears to extend approximately 3 to
4 feet deep under the face of the slope The height of the wedge shaped undercut spans
about 1 to 2 feet on average between the transient sand beach substratum and the roof of
the undercut The undercut wedge appears to terminate where loose beach sand fills the
void It is anticipated that the measurable undercut height and depth may vary depending
on the movement of the loose beach sand in response to tides near shore currents and
wave action Based on our observations and probing of the ground a hard
sandstone coralline limestone layer is believed to exist below the loose surface beach

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our observation of the existing site conditions the following discussions
and concept level recommendations are presented to address the observed surface
erosion conditions Our discussions and recommendations are based on the belief that the
shoreline slope erosion and rock deposition conditions are within the Coast Guard
Diamond Head Light property as stated in the previous Project Considerations section of
this report
Wall Footing Erosion
Based on our observations the erosion of soil along localized portions of the
existing CRM retaining wall footing appears to be relatively minor and may be corrected
using a surface treatment without the need for excavation and structural wall repair It
appears that the outside base of the wall was originally covered by some soil backfill
which over time washed away leaving the existing localized wall footing exposures We
estimate that approximately 6 to 18 inches of soil covering the outside base of the wall has
been eroded The areas of greater soil loss have exposed portions of the wall footing We
believe the erosion condition may be corrected by the application of shotcrete to fill the
undermined wall condition and cover the existing gently sloping ground surface
immediately fronting the length of the wall The shotcrete surface treatment would serve to
backfill the localized voids below the footing and prevent the further loss of surface soils

Undercut Shoreline Slope
Based on our observations the existing undercut slope condition does not appear
to pose a real risk for an extensive landslide type slope failure and resultant destabilization
of the existing upslope structures Large scale landslide failure is not anticipated due to the
generally consolidated character of the earth materials existing slope inclinations and the
lack of visible discontinuity surfaces such as inclined bedding planes or open fractures on
which large scale slope failure may be predisposed However the slope undercutting is
responsible for the local block type failures due to the loss of support occurring near the
high water mark The block failure is anticipated to occur as a rapid break and fall of blocks
of earth material from a relatively low height above the beach elevation
Global sea levels are believed to be very slowly rising and the coastal effects have
been documented on portions of the Islands of Oahu and Maui It is our opinion that if the
existing undercut condition continues unabated the undercut could deepen and additional
block failures involving the undercut slope are anticipated Because the site is along a
popular shoreline access future block failures could pose a safety hazard for persons
traversing the beach or loitering in close proximity of the slope face

Past experience with shoreline hardening performed with the intent to preserve
coastal property has been problematic often resulting in unintended consequences
fronting the shoreline improvements The unintended consequences often migrate laterally
along the adjacent coastline The loss of natural beach sand cover and resulting higher
encroachment of the water surface has commonly been reported following some shoreline
hardening projects Therefore we believe it is essential to consult a qualified coastal
engineer for detailed site evaluation and slope stabilization design assistance The history
of shoreline changes at the property could be researched to determine if the area is
experiencing a long term shoreline transgression If natural shoreline transgression is
found to be occurring remedial hardening of the shoreline could result in the undesired
reduction of the sandy beach fronting the slope
We believe that many slope stabilization options for this site are affected by the
relatively remote shoreline site location and difficult access for mechanized equipment and
construction materials Based on our review of the available maps the majority of the
slope undercut and resulting block failures appear to encompass the hill slope located
within the Coast Guard property and jurisdiction
We reviewed several options for remedial slope stabilization We believe these
options are not viable due to 1 difficult site access 2 the geological character of the soft
to medium hard earth materials comprising the slope and 3 higher construction costs
Options reviewed include
e Soil nail or rock anchor bolting
e Anchored wire mesh slope cover
e Undercut removal by excavation and slope flattening
e Placement of revetment erosion control structures fronting the slope
e Retaining wall
To reduce the potential for future block failures and rockfall hazards to affect the
public mitigation and hazard reduction options could encompass the following concepts
Option 1 Backfill of Slope Undercut Condition
Stabilization of natural shoreline erosion is a difficult and costly task due to many
factors Provided that shoreline access land ownership and shoreline construction
permitting issues are not an impediment we believe that backfilling of the existing slope
undercut by keying the backfill material into the underlying hard sandstone and coralline
reef material could be a viable option to stabilize the existing shoreline slope erosion and
undercut conditions The backfill material should support the overhanging earth materials
and prevent future block type failures along the section of slope experiencing ocean wave
undercutting Backfill material may consist of the application of shotcrete which is believed

Option 2 Maintain Natural Condition and Post Warning Signage
This option entails keeping the site in the existing natural condition by leaving the
fallen blocks in place along the slope face undercut The fallen blocks may help to stabilize
the coastal sediment flux and reduce the ocean wave energy attack which is believed to
be responsible for the development of the undercut condition In addition the fallen blocks
may assist in deterring the public from entering beneath the undercut feature and loitering
in front of and on top of the affected slope area Appropriate warning signage indicating
the potential for hazardous rockfall activity and undercut collapse should be posted The
intent of the warning signage is to alert shoreline users of the potentially hazardous rockfall
conditions The warning signage should also alert the public to stay off of the hill slope
along the slope undercut area
