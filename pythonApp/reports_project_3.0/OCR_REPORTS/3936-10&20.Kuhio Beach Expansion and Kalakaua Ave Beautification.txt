performed for the Kuhio Beach Expansion and Kalakaua Avenue Beautification Project
located along Kuhio Beach and on the makai side of Kalakaua Avenue in the Waikiki area
in the District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field explorations laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining structures site
grading slabs on grade and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise
in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy wetlands
As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits
consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was deposited
during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which apparently smothered the coral
reef development Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years

Based on the results of our explorations we believe that the proposed structures
such as the new concessions comfort stations police substation and retaining walls may
be supported on shallow foundations bearing on the on site medium dense to very dense
sand and gravel deposits hard sandy clay material and or well compacted fill material
Based on our explorations loose to very loose sand and gravel deposits may be
encountered at the proposed retaining wall foundation locations If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of the foundation excavations the soft and or loose

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill
material provided that the largest rock fragments in the material is less than 3 inches
in maximum dimension and that the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious

site were also explored by drilling and sampling four borings in 1998 for the Anti Crime
Streetlighting Improvements Waikiki designated as Boring Nos 7 through 10 The
approximate locations of the borings drilled for other projects are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger
equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler and a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler with
a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of these
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Seven sets of minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 78 and and pH ASTM G
51 and EPA 9045 tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the corrosion
potential of the soil The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 1
Six sets of sulfate content and chloride content tests were
performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific on selected samples to evaluate the
corrosion potential of the soil The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 2
Eight sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification The
results of these tests are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics The test results and shear stress versus normal
stress plots are presented on Plate B 6

APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
The falling head permeability testing was performed in general accordance with the
Auger Hole method This method is a variation of the borehole method in which water is
added to fill an auger hole at a given level and then the fall of the water level is observed
with time The permeability is based on the assumption that a unit hydraulic head gradient
is assumed for the flow through the bottom and side of the hole This method is expected
to give only a very approximate indication of the actual permeability of the soil
The rising head permeability testing was performed in general accordance with the
borehole bailing method This method involves removal of water by means of bailing to a
given level and then the rise of the water level is observed with respect to time The
permeability is based on the assumption that a unit hydraulic head gradient is assumed for
the flow through the bottom and side of the hole This method is expected to give a better
indication of the actual permeability of the soil than the Auger Hole method
