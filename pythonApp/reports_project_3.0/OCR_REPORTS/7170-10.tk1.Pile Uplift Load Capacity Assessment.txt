Dear Ms. Tanaka: 
As requested, we have evaluated the uplift load capacities of the waterside piles 
at the proposed crane stowage tie-down locations along Pier 53 and Pier 53 Extension 
on Sand Island in Honolulu Harbor on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  This letter report 
summarizes the findings from our assessment and presents our geotechnical 
engineering recommendations derived from our engineering analyses and evaluation. 
These recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
Matson, Inc. (formerly known as Matson Navigation Company) has container 
operations at Piers 51B, 52, and 53 on Sand Island in Honolulu Harbor on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. These piers were constructed in the 1980’s and early 1990’s and are 
supported by 16.5-inch octagonal, precast prestressed concrete piles. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that four new crane stowage 
locations are planned along Pier 53 and Pier 53 Extension.  Each crane stowage 
location will consist of two tie-downs for a total of eight tie-downs.  The approximate 
locations of the proposed tie-downs, designated as Tie-Downs A through H, are shown 
on the Tie-Down Location Plan, Plate 2. 
Each tie-down will consist of ten existing waterside piles that will be subjected to 
uplift forces (in tension) or totaling 80 piles for the eight tie-downs.  The 80 existing 
waterside piles that will be subjected to uplift forces are shown on the Tie-Down 
Location Plan, Plate 2.  According to the information provided by Lifttech Consultants 
Inc., we understand that an average ultimate uplift load capacity of 20 kips per pile is 
desired at each of the tie-down locations to resist the crane tie-down force.  Therefore, it 
is desired to evaluate the uplift load capacities of these 80 waterside piles in support of 
the design of the proposed tie-downs at the new crane stowage locations.

Existing Pile Driving Records 
In order to evaluate the uplift load capacities of the 80 waterside piles at the 
tie-down locations, it would be crucial to determine the embedment of the driven pile (for 
single pile) or the embedment of the splice can (for spliced pile) below the mudline based 
on the information contained in the pile driving records.  The embedment length could then 
be used to evaluate the uplift load capacity of the driven pile based on an assumed 
subsurface soil profile.   
A generalized subsurface soil profile across the wharf site was developed using the 
pertinent soil boring information contained in the report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering 
Exploration, Construction of Pier 53 Extension, CY-6 & CY-9, Sand Island, Honolulu, Oahu, 
Hawaii, Job H.C. 1693” dated December 28, 1990 by Geolabs-Hawaii. The interpreted 
subsurface conditions are provided on the Generalized Subsoil Profile Across Wharf Site, 
Plate 3.  The logs of borings used in developing the generalized subsoil profile are provided 
in Appendix A for ease of reference. 
We understand that efforts by Matson Terminals, Inc. and the Department of 
Transportation, Harbors Division to locate the pile driving records for these 80 waterside 
piles have been unsuccessful.  Based on our archive research for the Pier 53 Extension 
project, we were able to locate 26 pile driving records or information that belonged to this 
group of 80 existing waterside piles. The existing waterside piles at the tie-down areas with 
pile driving records or information for our engineering analyses are shown on the Tie-Down 
Location Plan, Plate 2. 
Non-Destructive Pile Integrity Testing 
Due to the lack of a complete set of pile driving records as mentioned above, we 
have recommended that a geophysical survey consisting of pile integrity testing be 
performed in an attempt to determine the single pile and/or splice can embedment.  The 
non-destructive pile integrity test would serve to provide an estimation of the pile length 
or spliced depth, which could be used to evaluate the upload load capacity of the driven 
pile based on an assumed subsurface soil profile as mentioned above. 
The non-destructive pile integrity testing was performed by our subcontractor, 
Global Geophysics, from November 10 to 14, 2015 with assistance from our office. Due 
to inaccessibility issues, non-destructive pile integrity testing could only be performed on 
47 piles out of the 80 piles at the tie-down locations.  The locations of these tested piles 
are shown on the Tie-Down Location Plan, Plate 2.  It should be noted that one of the 
tested piles, designated as Pile No. H11, was not part of the 80 piles subjected to uplift 
forces and therefore, not included in our engineering analyses.  Detailed discussions of 
the methodology and results are presented in the report entitled “Report on the 
Geophysical Survey at Pier 53 Extension, Honolulu, HI” dated November 30, 2015 by 
Global Geophysics.  A copy of the report is provided in Appendix B for ease of 
reference.

Discussions of Findings and Recommendations 
The pile tip and/or splice can embedment obtained from the pile driving records and 
non-destructive pile integrity testing are presented on the Summary of Pile Tip/Spice Can 
Embedment, Plates 4.1 and 4.2. Where both the pile driving record existed and 
non-destructive testing was performed on a particular pile, the information contained in the 
pile driving record was used in our engineering analyses.  Based on the embedment depth 
and the generalized subsoil profile as discussed above, we have evaluated the ultimate 
uplift load capacity for the driven pile and the results are shown on the Summary of Uplift 
Load Capacity, Plates 5.1 through 5.3.  These individual uplift load capacities were then 
used to formulate the average ultimate uplift load capacity per pile at each of the tie-down 
locations.  The results are shown on Plates 5.1 through 5.3 and summarized in the 
following table.

As shown on Plates 4.1 and 4.2, the pile tip and/or splice can embedment obtained 
from the pile driving records are substantially deeper than those interpreted from the non-
destructive pile integrity test data.  Our field observations also indicated that additional piles 
have been driven at some of the tie-down areas, providing additional uplift load resistance. 
 Therefore, we believe that the actual average ultimate uplift load capacity at the tie-down 
locations could be even higher than those indicated in the above table, especially at Tie-
Downs E through F, where the results are solely based on the data collected from the non-
destructive pile integrity tests. 
Based on our engineering analyses using the data contained in the pile driving 
records and/or collected by the non-destructive pile integrity testing, it is our opinion that 
each group of the 10 existing waterside piles at Tie-Down Location A through H should be 
able to develop a minimum average ultimate uplift load capacity of 20 kips per pile.

Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from drilled borings, pile driving data by others, and 
non-destructive pile integrity test results. Variations of subsoil conditions between and 
beyond the field data points may occur, and the non-destructive pile integrity test data 
may not disclose pile conditions that may affect the data interpretation.

METHODOLOGY AND INSTRUMENTATION 
Pile Integrity Test (PIT) 
A pile integrity test (also known as low strain dynamic test, sonic echo test, and low strain 
integrity test) is one of the methods for assessing the condition of piles or shafts. It is cost 
effective and not very time consuming. 
The test is based on wave propagation theory. The name "low strain dynamic test" stems 
from the fact that when a light impact is applied to a pile it produces a low strain. The impact 
produces a compression wave that travels down the pile at a constant wave speed (similarly 
to what happens in high strain dynamic testing). Changes in cross sectional area - such as a 
reduction in diameter - or material - such as a void in concrete and bottom of the pile - 
produce wave reflections. 
This procedure is performed with a hand held hammer to generate an impact, an 
accelerometer or accelerometer placed on the side of the pile to be tested to measure the 
response to the hammer impact, and a data acquisition and interpretation electronic 
instrument. 
The test works well in concrete or timber foundations that are not excessively slender. 
Usually the method is applied to recently constructed piles that are not yet connected to a

Global geophysics services are conducted in a manner consistent with the level of care and
