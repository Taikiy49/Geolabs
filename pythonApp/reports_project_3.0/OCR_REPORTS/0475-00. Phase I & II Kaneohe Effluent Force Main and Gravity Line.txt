Gentlemen
This report presents the results of our preliminary soils
engineering and geologic investigation for the proposed Kahaluu
Kaneohe Effluent Force Main and gravity line between Stations
15 00 to 316 00 as shown on the attached plot plan
The study consisted of subsurface borings geologic reconnaissance
laboratory testing and engineering analyses as outlined in
Phase I of our proposal dated May 8 1973 and in general accord
ance with our contract dated May 18 1973 The purpose of our
investigation was to determine the general subsurface conditions
and develop pertinent soil and foundation recommendations for
the design and construction of the proposed drain

DESCRIPTION
The proposed portion Station 15 00 to Station 316 00 of the
24 inch diameter concrete main extends approximately 30 100 feet
from the junction of Kamehameha Highway and Kahekili Highway to
the Kaneohe Sewage Treatment Plant Between Station 15 00 and
190 00 the line will be a force main and gravity fall between
Station 190 00 and 316 00
In general the alignment heads southeast along the Kahekili
Highway then following along Keaahala Road Anoi Road Paleka
Road and Waikalua Road The alignment then crosses Kaneohe
Stream and ends on the north side of Kaneohe WWIP The pre
liminary plan and profile of the line are shown on Figures 1
and 2
FIELD EXPLORATION
The field exploration consisted of 16 borings ranging in depth
from 10 to 52 feet and geologic reconnaissance The drilling
was accomplished using a truck mounted rotary drilling equip
ment with continuous flight auger Borings 1 to 3 were drilled
using a 9 hollow stem auger while the remainder were drilled
using 6 solid stem augers The drilling operations were conti
nuously supervised by an engineer from our firm The approximate
boring locations are shown on Figure 1 and the boring logs are
Page 3

August 13 1973
presented in Appendix A
In all borings either Standard Penetration Test or 2 4 inch
I D ring samples were taken at selected depth intervals
The Standard Penetration Test consists of driving a 2 inch
0O D split spoon sampler 18 inches with a 140 pound hammer
free falling a distance of 30 inches The number of blows
required to drive the sampler the last 12 inches is termed the
Standard Penetration Resistance N and is an approximate
measure of the relative density or consistency of soil These
resistance values are plotted on the boring logs Samples
obtained were visually classified in the field and representa
tive material was placed in air tight containers and returned
to our laboratory for inspection and testing

General
The sewer alignment is located on the northeast side
of the elongated Koolau Volcanic Range which forms the
southeast portion of Oahu Hawaii
After the formation of the Koolau Volcanic Range and
the associated Kailua Volcanic Series in the Kaneohe
Kahaluu area the constituent volcanoes were deeply
dissected by stream erosion The clastic debris
Older Alluvium boulders through clay size eroded
from the mountains was redeposited along the mountain
flanks and coastal plains of Oahu Several subsequent
periods of emergence and submergence of the area followed
The concomitant changes of relative sea level gave rise
to varying configurations of Kaneohe Bay Sediment in
filling of minor embayments around the Bay s periphery
has produced swamps along the proposed sewer route
Subsequent parts of uplift and or an eustatic lowering
of sea level and erosion has exposed many of the older
deposits Recent deposits along the sewer line route

fills placed for Kahekili Highway

ENGINEERING RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Relatively good subsurface conditions were encountered along
the alignment with the exception of the soft bay mud deposits
in Borings 1 through 3 near the Kaneohe Stream and sheet
pile supports would be required in this area Kaneohe
stream may be crossed by tunnelling and jacking of the
pipe under the stream Relatively soft materials were also
encountered in Borings 11 through 16 Boring 14 was a shallow
boring and did not reach soft material and relatively thicker
bedding of granular material will be required
If localized very soft areas are encountered during excava
tion of the pipe trenches a base course of gravel may have
to be used to stabilize the trench bottom
Design and construction of the proposed effluent force main
should be relatively straight forward for the rest of the
alignment Dewatering trench support uplift pressure
etc will have to be considered in the Kaneohe Stream area

Pipe Bedding
Distribution of the stresses against the bottom of a pipe
has a great effect on the load supporting capacity of the
pipe Pipe bedding the contact between the foundation and
the pipe is therefore an important design consideration
We recommend Class B Bedding which can be achieved by two
methods
1 Shaped Bottom with Tamped Backfill The bottom of the
trench excavation should be shaped to a cylindrical
surface with a radius 2 inches greater than the radius
to the outside of the pipe and sufficient to allow
60 percent of the width of the pipe to be bedded in a
sand fill placed in the shaped excavation Carefully
compacted backfill shall be placed at the sides of the
pipe to a thickness of at least 12 inches above the
top of the pipe
2 Compacted Granular Sand Bedding with Tamped Backfill
The pipe should be bedded in compacted granular material
placed on a flat trench bottom The granular bedding
should have a minimum width of one fourth the outside
pipe diameter on each side and should extend halfway
Page 14
W O 475 00
August 13 1973
up the pipe at the sides The remainder of the side
fills and a minimum depth of 12 inches over the top of
the pipe shall be filled with carefully compacted
material See Plate 4
For controlling the pipe gradient and reducing the differen
tial settlement it is advisable to overexcavate at least
6 inches and backfill with sandy material compacted to a
90 percent relative compaction according to ASTM D 1557 70
This overexcavation should be increased to 12 inches in the
area from Kamehameha Highway to the Valley of the Temples
Approximately Station 15 00 to Station 108 00 In this
area soil conditions are highly variable due to the fact
that the highway is built on old stream and also due to
presence of swampy materials If localized soft spots are
encountered then gravel should be used to stabilize the trench
bottom The gravel layer should be between 6 and 12 inches
thick

This section of the alignment is in the Kaneohe Stream area

concrete piles driven to the dense gravelly sand should be
capable of supporting an allowable design load of about
30 tons each The following guidelines should be used for
design and construction of pile supports

Kaneohe Stream Crossing
Pipe crossing across the Kaneohe Stream may be accomplished
by tunnelling and jacking In this method a steel casing
about 4 in diameter is advanced by tunnelling and jacking
The pipe is then advanced resting on two wooden blocks or
sleepers per pipe section and the volume between pipe and
casing is filled with slurry sand or very lean concrete
The casing may be left in place or may be pulled out If
this method is used strict control on alignment may be
rather difficult
Sheeting and Bracing
Sheeting and bracing to prevent cave in of the trench wall
is recommended in the area where soft swampy materials
and bay muds were encountered Kaneohe Stream Area and
between Kamehameha Highway and Valley of the Temples
In areas of firm clayey silty soil the trench walls may
be sloped at 1 1 slope or braced In areas where hard
basalt is encountered the trench wall may be excavated
vertically Trench excavation sheeting and bracing must

Backfill
Backfill should be so placed that the pipe will not be
displaced or damaged Backfill material to a depth of one
foot over the crown of the pipe should consist of selected
free draining well graded sandy material and be placed by
hand or suitable equipment and compacted to a density
consistent with design assumptions
Above the select material on site materials may be used for
backfill and should be compacted to a minimum of 90 of
maximum dry density Open areas may be compacted to 85
The on site materials are suitable for backfilling provided
that compressible organic materials rubbish debris and
other objectionable material are first removed Rock
greater than 9 inches in diameter should not be permitted
in the backfill
In areas to be paved the upper zone of backfill should be

Floatation of Pipe
In areas where groundwater is encountered before and during
the backfilling operation precautions should be taken to
prevent floatation of pipelines due to the entry of the
large quantities of water into the trench Eighteen to
twenty four inches of backfill over the pipe would prevent
floatation of the pipe upon submergence
Settlements
Total settlements of the pipe should be minimal provided
good construction practices are followed However flexi
bility in the pipe should be provided at the junction of
different ground condition such as rock and soil cut and
fill firm soil and ground underlain by swampy materials
pipes supported on piles etc Where the pipe passes be
tween rock and soil the bedding should be increased to
12 inch thickness thinning out to 6 inches thickness in
a distance of about 25 feet on both sides Settlements of
ground adjacent to trenches can be avoided by preventing
water ground losses oversteepened slopes and insufficient
Page 21

August 13 1973
shoring of vertical trenches
In the area between Kamehameha Highway and Valley of the
Temples the recently constructed Kahekili Highway is built
over swampy and old stream beds and may undergo differential
settlements due to underlying highly variable and soft
swampy materials If the pipe in this area is rigidly
supported such as on piles then excessive loads may be
imposed on pipe due to settlements of adjacent ground Due
to this reason pile foundation is not recommended in this
area and some flexibility should be provided in the pipe
to take differential movements

LABORATORY TESTING
A laboratory testing program was developed to establish the
engineering properties of the soils and rocks encountered at
the project site The tests where applicable to the subject
project are included in this Appendix
1 Classification Tests
a
b
c
Visual Classification All soil samples obtained from
the borings were brought to our laboratory where they
were visually reclassified to confirm or modify the
field classification prior to finalizing the Logs of
Borings
Water Content Determinations In addition to visual
classification typical samples were tested for natural
water content as an aid in soil classification and in
evaluating soil properties The water content values
are based upon the dry weight of the soil
Atterberg Limits Atterberg limit tests were performed
on selected clay and silt samples obtained from the
borings for the purposes of identification and correla
tion of soils Standard procedures ASTM D 424 for the
plastic limit and ASTM D 423 for the liquid limit were
used in the performance of these tests
1
2
d
Grain Size Analysis Sieve analysis were performed an
granular soils For fine grained soils the hydrometer
test was performed to determine the distribution of
the grain sizes beyond the No 200 sieve
Shear Strength Tests
a
b
Unconfined Compression Tests Intact core and split
spoon soil samples which apparently exhibited high
plasticity were tested for unconfined strength Length
to diameter ratio of 2 was maintained throughout the
tests to avoid scatter of test results and to facilitate
evaluation of shear strength values for design purposes
Direct Shear Test In general soil samples were tested
for shear strength using a 2 41 I D direct shear box
Each set consisted of at least two tests each test run
under different normal loads The results are plotted
as Normal Stress vs Shear Strength with a failure envelope
drawn to determine values of cohesion c and angle of
frictional resistance
Consolidation Test Consolidation tests were performed on
selected undisturbed ring samples of the compressible
soils to provide basic data for making settlement calculations
Porous stones were placed on the top and bottom of the samples
to allow drainage Vertical loads were applied in increments
with each load increment being allowed to consolidate prior
2
to adding the next increment Measurements of the time
and consolidation were obtained during each load increment
and rebound was measured during the unloading portion
Consolidation test results are plotted in terms of percent
settlement versus applied loads
Swell Test The ring sample was placed between porous
stones and a 60 psf load was applied to the sample for a
24 hour period The difference between the initial and
final sample heights is the amount of swell which is expressed
as a percentage of the initial height of the sample
Maximum Density Test Samples of soils are compacted in a
mold of given size with a 10 pound rammer dropped 18 inches
to determine the relationship between moisture content and
density of the soil At least three trial points at dif
ferent moisture content are run to determine the maximum
density and optimum moisture content The test is run in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 70

FIELD EXPLORATIONS
This investigation consisted of drilling two 100 ft deep borings
and taking limited representative Standard Penetration Test samples
at about 10 foot interval The attached plot plan indicates the
approximate Boring locations
The Standard Penetration Test consists of driving a 2 inch O D
split sppon sampler 18 inches with a 140 pound hammer free falling
a distance of 30 inches The number of blows require to drive
the samples the last 12 inches is termed the Standard Penetration

Resistance N and is an approximate measure of the relative
density or consistency of a soil These resistance values are
plotted on the attached boring logs Samples obtained were
visually classified in the field
SOIL CONDITIONS
The boring logs for the two test holes generally indicate a stiff
surface layer of about 4 to 10 feet underlain by soft swampy
materials to at least 45 foot depth Stiffer clayey silt materials
start from about 55 feet and extends to the limits of our borings
See the individual boring logs for a more complete description
of the soil layers In general materials in Boring 1 appears to
be relatively stiffer than in Boring 2
CONCLUSION
The subsurface soil conditions encountered at this site preclude
the use of conventional spread or continuous footings for
foundation support of heavy structures and structures that cannot
withstand relatively excessive settlements since the surface
crust is rather thin However for very light structures and
structures that can withstand relatively large settlements
individual proposed locations should be investigated in detail
to determine if shallow footings may be used
