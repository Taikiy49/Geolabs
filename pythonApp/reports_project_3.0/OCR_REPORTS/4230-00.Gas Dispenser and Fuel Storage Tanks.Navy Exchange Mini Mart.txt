INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed gas dispenser and fuel storage tanks at the Navy Exchange Mini Mart
located in Pearl City Peninsula on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade and site
preparation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
it is proposed to construct two new gasoline dispensers one island with associated
fuel storage tanks and controls located at the Navy Exchange Mini Mart in Pearl City
Peninsula on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided we understand
that the fuel storage system will consist of two above ground storage tanks with capacities
of 6 000 and 10 000 gallons each The two above ground storage tanks will be supported
on a concrete pad We also understand that the gas dispensers will be credit card type
dispensers with a lighting system for night use The gas dispensers will be open and will not
be covered by a canopy A new concrete slab will be constructed around the gas dispensers
for vehicular traffic
Based on the information provided we envision that the proposed improvements will
be constructed near the existing grades with minimal site grading In general the existing
asphalt concrete pavements at the site will be retained and re used as much as possible
In addition we understand that new asphalt concrete curbs will be constructed in the area
of the existing parking stalls as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located in the middle of Pearl City Peninsula in the Pearl Harbor
area on the south central coastal plain of Oahu The geologic conditions underlying the
Pearl Harbor Lochs were the result of a complex series of depositional and erosional events
due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosion volcanic activities during the
Pleistocene Epoch
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano
alluvial materials were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pear
Harbor The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising
about 95 feet above the present sea level As the island of Oahu submerged coral reefs
grew around the shoreline keeping pace with the subsidence of the isiand
During the Recent Age as the sea level rose to its current stand the Middle Loch
area was covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of cohesive soils with
localized granular soil lenses over the underlying alluvium Land development and
GEOLABS HAWAII

reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have added man made fills along the
shoreline bringing the site to its present configuration

corner of Lehua and Waipuna Avenues The project site is currently occupied by the parking

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As discussed above the proposed project site is generally underlain by stiff surficial
fill materials overlying silty alluvial soil deposits The surficial fill materials generally consist
of stiff clayey silts and silty clays and the underlying alluvial soil deposits are comprised of
sandy silts The surficial fills and the underlying alluvial soil deposits are generally stiff to
hard in consistency and should provide good foundation support for the proposed
improvements Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing we believe that a
shallow foundation system bearing on the recompacted surficial fills may be used to support
the proposed fuel storage tanks
Based on our laboratory test results the surficial fill soils exhibit a slight potential for
shrink swell when subjected to changes in the moisture content Therefore we recommend
that slabs on grade at the site such as the slabs for the gas dispensers be supported on at
least 12 inches of select granular fill material In addition special attention should be given
to the subgrade preparation requirements presented in the Slabs On Grade section of this
report to reduce the potential shrink swell effects on the concrete slabs
Detailed discussions and specific design recommendations pertaining to the design
of foundations slabs on grade and site preparation are presented in the following sections
of this report

Foundations
Based on our field exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of mat foundations or slab on grade with thickened edges may be used to
support the proposed above ground fuel storage tanks An allowable bearing pressure of
up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on the dense and or stiff surficial fills encountered at the site The allowable bearing
pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 4 3 for transient
loads such as wind and or seismic forces Foundations should be embedded a minimum
of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior
to placement of reinforcing steel Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Foundation
subgrades should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If shrinkage cracks appear
in the foundation excavations the soil should be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks
and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete placement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that the total foundation settlements under the anticipated
design loads to be on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order
of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the foundations and the supporting fill
materials Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf These values assume
GEOLABS HAWAII

that the concrete for the foundations is placed directly against the foundation excavations
and that the soils around the foundations are well compacted Unless covered by pavements
or Slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be
neglected
We recommend that foundation excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and or concrete to confirm the
competency of the bearing materials and embedment depths If unsuitable materials are
encountered in the footing excavations these materials should be over excavated to expose
firm soils and replaced with well compacted general fill controlled fill
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed gas
dispensers and trash enclosure The surficial fills encountered at the proposed project site
consist of stiff to very stiff silty and or clayey soils which exhibit a slight expansion potential
when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we recommend that the subgrade soils
be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of non expansive select granular
fill material
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should
be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks or be prepared again Saturation and subsequent yielding of
the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional
cost to the owner
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4230 00 Page 9
Concrete for the slabs gas dispensers may be placed directly on the compacted
select granular fill material The select granular fill materials should be compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction The subgrade below the select granular fill should be
scarified a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per square inch
per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted select granular fill material
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slabs with relatively impervious soils on site silty and clayey
soils and graded to divert water away from the slabs to reduce the potential for water
ponding around the slabs
Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish asphalt concrete pavements and
other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and disposed of
properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted general fill
controlled fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of
off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation
quantities
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4230 00 Page 10
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with the excavated on site fill materials The on site
fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content piaced
in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
After clearing and demolition areas beneath the future slabs on grade should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick capping layer of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab Prior to placement of the capping
fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned
to above the optinum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction In areas where the finished grades will be subjected to vehicular traffic such
as the gas dispenser areas the compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill
The select granular fill recommended below slabs and imported fill materials where
required should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral basalt
gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and shouid contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii

Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections will be required for the
proposed project The method and equipment used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should
comply with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring and
bracing should also conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes The initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should also
consist of free draining backfill such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use this free draining
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
We understand that sand may be the preferred backfill material around the piping
network at the site Therefore in lieu of the free draining backfill materials recommended
above consideration may be given to the use of sand such as S4C material for the bedding
and initial trench backfill materials Where sand is used for bedding and initial trench backfill
the sand should be moistened and densified with a vibratory plate or similar compaction
equipment

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils clayey silts
and silty clays or select granular fill materials The backfill should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the dispenser slab areas and the fuel
storage tank pad foundations should be sloped to shed water away from the pavements and
pads to reduce the potential for ponding Surface water ponding should not be allowed on
the site during or after construction These drainage requirements are essential for the
proper performance of the pavements and pad foundations since ponded water could cause
subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The edges of the tank
pad excavations should be properly backfilled to reduce water infiltration In addition
drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain
surface water runoff away from the pavements and pads

selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on natural samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
