SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration encountered 3 to 8 inches of asphaltic concrete 
pavement underlain by a thin granular fill layer overlying residual soil extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Majority of 
the asphaltic concrete pavement encountered is about 3 to 4 inches thick. The granular fill 
layer generally consisted of 2 to 8 inches of medium dense to dense silty sand with gravel 
to sandy gravel with a little silt. The residual soil consisting of soft to hard clayey silts and 
silty clays was encountered below the granular fill layer. Dense boulder (core stone of 
residual soil) was encountered in one of the drilled borings at a depth of about 3.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface.  
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration.  
It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

and Kokee Road generally exhibit low to moderate severity pavement distresses with 
localized areas exhibit failed pavement sections with high severity pavement distresses.

be rehabilitated with an AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.0 inches of the existing

Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed 
for the design of the pavement rehabilitation and added pavement shoulders for the 
proposed Waimea Canyon Drive/Kokee Road Improvements, Phase 2 project located in 
Waimea on the Island of Kaua‘i, Hawai‘i. The project location and general site vicinity are

This report summarizes our findings and presents our pavement design 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report.  It should be noted that our findings and 
recommendations for the road stabilization in the vicinity of Mileposts (MP) 9.3 and 9.4 of

September 11, 2013, and addendum dated December 1, 2021, under separate covers.

Project Considerations 
Waimea Canyon Drive/Kokee Road is a winding, narrow, two-lane undivided road. 
The project involves improvements on the existing pavement along Waimea Canyon 
Drive/Kokee Road from MP 4.6 to MP 14.0.  Planned improvements involve widening the 
existing roadway to provide a minimum 2-foot wide new pavement shoulder. 
In addition, improvements consisting of milling the existing pavement and installing 
a 2-inch asphaltic concrete (AC) overlay will be implemented for Waimea Canyon Drive 
from about MP 4.6 to MP 6.0, and Kokee Road from about MP 8.0 to MP 14.0. Localized 
severely distressed areas will be reconstructed with new pavement sections. It is our 
understanding that the resurfacing of Waimea Canyon Drive/Kokee Road, between about 
MP 6.0 and MP 8.0, was performed under a separate project. 
This pavement justification report has been prepared in support of the design of 
the proposed pavement improvements.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kaua‘i is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano built 
by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 
5 to 6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 
2½ million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the 
cessation of this main volcano shield-building phase, about 1½ million years ago, 
renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the 
post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial 
sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon 
Volcanic Series. These lavas consist of four distinct formations: Napali, Olokele, Haupu, 
and Makaweli. These formations are comprised of thin-bedded a`a and pahoehoe flows 
to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben. The project site is within the 
Napali Formation of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the 
vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels 
rose because more water was available. The land mass of Kauai remained essentially 
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative 
to the present sea level of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at

The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic 
Series is generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with 
interbedded thin clinker layers. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual 
soils which grade to saprolite with depth. In general, saprolite is composed of mainly silty 
material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades 
to a basaltic rock formation with depth. 
The surface soils underlying the project site are described by the U.S. Soil 
Conservation Service in their publication "Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, Oahu, Maui, 
Molokai and Lanai, State of Hawaii" (1972). The predominant surface soil type in the 
general area of the site is described as follows: 
Oli Silt Loam (OME), 10 to 30 Percent Slopes 
This soil developed in volcanic ash deposited over basic igneous rock and occupied 
uplands dissected by many small gulches. In a representative profile, the surface 
layer and subsoil is dark brown silt loam that has prismatic and subangular blocky 
structure. The substratum is slightly weathered hard rock. The slope range of this soil 
is 10 to 30 percent. Permeability is moderately rapid, runoff is medium, and the 
erosion hazard is moderate to severe. This soil is easily erodible because it is very 
friable and powdery. 
Mahana Silt Loam (MaD), 12 to 20 Percent Slopes 
This soil developed in volcanic ash and is gently sloping very steep. These soils are 
geographically associated with Oli and Puu Opae soils on Kauai and Kolekole soils 
on Oahu. In a representative profile, the surface layer and subsoil is dusky red to 
dark reddish-brown silt loam that has a subangular blocky structure. The substratum 
is compact silty clay loam. The slope range of this soil is 12 to 20 percent. Runoff is 
medium, and the erosion hazard is moderate.

2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is along the Waimea Canyon Drive/Kokee Road, which is a 
winding, narrow, two-lane undivided road along the western side of Waimea Canyon on 
the Island of Kauai, Hawaii.  Based on our field exploration and the grading plans 
provided, the existing Waimea Canyon Drive/Kokee Road within the project limits goes 
uphill northerly with elevations rising from about +1,560 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) to 
+3,644 feet MSL with some winding turns and rolling ups and downs.  The pavements 
along Waimea Canyon Drive/Kokee Road generally exhibited low to moderate severity 
pavement distresses with localized failed areas observed with high severity alligator 
cracks.  Majority of the roadway has 2 to 6 feet wide unpaved shoulders covered with 
grass, dirt, or gravel.

Subsurface Conditions

designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 21, extending to depths of about 4 and 5 feet below

the pavement support characteristics of the materials. The approximate boring and bulk 
sample locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.46. 
In general, our field exploration encountered 3 to 8 inches of asphaltic concrete 
pavement underlain by a thin granular fill layer overlying residual soil extending to the

fill layer generally consisted of 2 to 8 inches of medium dense to dense silty sand with

silts and silty clays was encountered below the granular fill layer. Dense boulder (core

3.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the type and severity of the distress exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed from an engineering point of view. Existing pavements with minor distress 
and severity may be rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing. However, 
reconstruction of the existing pavements would be prudent for those areas where the 
observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or 
subgrade materials. The following information would be required to provide input design 
parameters for the pavement rehabilitation/reconstruction design and analyses: 
Traffic Index 
Condition of the Existing AC Layer 
Resistance (R) Value of the Subgrade Materials 
It is our understanding that a pavement condition survey conducted by Wilson 
Okamoto Corporation indicated the majority of the pavements under consideration for this 
project appear to exist in fair condition. Based on the project scope of work and our field 
observation, the project would not require reconstruction for the entire roadway within the 
project limits in accordance with the guidelines presented in the Pavement Design 
Manual, prepared by the State of Hawai‘i, Department of Transportation, Highways 
Division, Material Testing and Research Branch.  
Since severely distressed pavements along localized portions of Waimea Canyon 
Drive/Kokee Road within the project limits amount to less than 15 percent of the total 
pavement area, these severely distressed pavements should be reconstructed with a new 
pavement structural section designed based on a 10-year design life in accordance with 
the guidelines presented in the above pavement design manual. 
Since the project consists of resurfacing, pavement reconstruction and adding 
paved shoulders, only flexible pavements were considered in the pavement analyses. 
Based on the traffic volumes anticipated for the roadways, we believe that the asphaltic 
concrete base (ACB) should be used as the base material for the project.

3.6 
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared 
and grubbed at the on-set of earthworks. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and properly disposed of off-site or in a 
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. 
After removal of the existing AC and base layers to accommodate the new 
pavement structural sections, the exposed subgrade soils should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to compact the 
exposed subgrade soils. The drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet 
per minute. 
If subgrade soils with relatively high in-situ moisture are exposed, we recommend 
proof-rolling the pavement subgrade without the use of vibration. The use of vibration 
during proof-rolling will induce a pumping condition of the subgrade soils with high in-situ 
moisture.  
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend scarifying and re-preparing the soils as recommended above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices 
for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities.

3.7 
Fill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches 
may be reused as a source of general fill material.  In general, the on-site materials exhibit 
high natural water content, and drying should be anticipated prior to their use. Imported 
fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select granular material, such 
as crushed coralline or basaltic materials. The materials should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less.  Imported fill materials should be tested for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use and conformance to the State of Hawai‘i, Standard Specifications for Road 
and Bridge Construction (2005) and subsequent amendments. 
3.8 
Compaction Requirements 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Due to the high natural 
water content of the subgrade soils, the required compaction for the subgrade was 
reduced from 95 percent to 90 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base and 
subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to not less than 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphalt concrete (AC)

3.9 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. It should be noted that the 
clayey subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
It is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately and to maintain positive 
drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface water may be carried off 
the pavement quickly for proper discharge. Surface water ponding should not be allowed 
on-site during or after construction.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
21 exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 21, extending to depths of 
about 4 and 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. The borings were drilled 
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.46. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-21. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Eleven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-3. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Eight laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed on 
selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 through B-13.
