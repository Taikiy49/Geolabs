INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
analysis performed for the proposed Kilauea Replacement Bridge project The project
site is located southeast of the junction of Kolo Road and Liliuokalani Street in the Kilauea
area on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations based on our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the proposed replacement bridge project These recommendations are
intended for design of foundations site grading and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 5
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai and are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded from
groups of vents aligned in north south trends at various locales Associated with the
vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
In the area of the project alluvial deposits have accumulated on the basalt rock
formation along Kilauea Stream The alluvial deposits generally consist of silty clays
clayey silts sands and gravels with cobbles and boulders In addition some fills
appeared to have been placed in the area for the development of Kolo Road

Liliuokalani Street in the Kilauea area on the island of Kauai Hawaii A new bridge will

STREAM BED MATERIAL FOR SCOUR ANALYSIS
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying away
material from the bed and banks of streams Total scour generally consists of three
components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local Scour
The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as shape and dimensions of a
pier or abutment depth of flow velocity of approach flow size and gradation of stream
bed material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristics of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material D in
conjunction with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate fall velocity of
stream bed materials in scour depth analysis In order to evaluate the size and gradation
of the stream bed material for scour depth analysis at the project site a bulk sample of
stream bed material was retrieved for our laboratory tests The stream bed material
generally consists of brown silty sand with gravel A sieve analysis with hydrometer test
was performed on the stream bed soil sample for grain size distribution of the material
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the median diameter of the stream bed
material D is approximately The results of our laboratory tests on the stream
bed materials are presented on Plate B 1 in Appendix B of this report and were provided
to the project Civil Engineer for the scour depth analysis
A scour analysis was performed by the project Civil Engineer for the 500 year
storm event Based on their analyses total scour depths of 53 46 and 56 62 feet were
obtained for the east and west abutments of the Kilauea Replacement Bridge
respectively The two scour depths correspond to elevations of approximately
210 5 and 207 4 feet MSL for the east and west abutments respectively Due to the
relatively deep scour depths anticipated we understand that rip rap will be provided at the
abutments along the stream as a counter measure against scour in lieu of allowing the
structures to withstand scour Therefore the bridge foundations should not be affected
by scour

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the relatively heavy
structural loads we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of 36 inch
diameter cast in place drilled shafts be used to support the proposed replacement bridge
The drilled shafts should be embedded a minimum of 10 feet into the basalt rock
formation to derive the necessary support for design loads Based on the top of basalt
rock formation encountered in our borings we estimate that the drilled shafts will need to
be about 55 feet long measured from the bottom of the footing elevation
We envision that site grading would consist only of minor cuts and fills on the order
of less than 2 feet relative to the existing ground surface for the proposed project In
addition we anticipate excavations up to about 33 feet below the ground surface will be
needed for constructing the new abutment walls and footings followed by backfilling
These excavations should be adequately shored and braced to provide a safe working
environment in order to construct the abutment footings
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundations abutment
walls site grading pavements and other geotechnical aspects of the project are
presented in the following sections

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The load bearing
capacities of drilled shafts depend on the friction resistance between the shaft and the
surrounding soil rock Therefore the Contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft
holes and in placing concrete into the holes The subsoils near the bottom of abutment
footings consist of alluvial soils with boulders and basalt fragments Drilling through the
boulders and basalt fragments may be slow and may require special tools In addition
the drilled shafts are designed to be embedded at least 10 feet into the basalt rock
formation therefore coring into the hard basalt formation will likely be required
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 13
Due to the relatively shallow groundwater and granular soils encountered cave in
of the material may occur during the drilling operation To reduce the potential for
caving in of the holes temporary casing may be needed The anticipated high
groundwater levels may also pose further construction difficulties since proper
observation of the sides and bottom of the drilled shafts would be difficult Therefore a
representative of Geolabs Hawaii should be present at the site to observe the drilling and
installation of drilled shafts during construction Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids
is not recommended because use of these fluids may reduce the friction between the
concrete shaft and surrounding soils rock
A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range concrete mix should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts surrounding soils and basalt formation
The concrete should be placed promptly after the completion of drilling within 24 hours
to reduce the potential for caving in The concrete should also be placed in a suitable
manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix Since
groundwater will likely be encountered in the drilled holes placement of concrete by
tremie methods is recommended During concrete placement a minimum of 10 feet of
concrete head should be maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe at all times

Site Grading
The extent of grading required to construct the proposed replacement bridge will
generally consist of site preparation and minor grading to achieve final design grades In
addition relatively deep excavations followed by backfilling will be required to construct
the abutment wall footings currently set at elevation 264 feet MSL In general all
grading work should conform to Section 200 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 and the site specific
recommendations contained in this report
Site grading operations should be observed by a qualified Geotechnical Engineer
It is important that the Engineer observe the site grading operation to evaluate whether
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification process
and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our
exploration
In general areas to receive fills should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious
materials The resulting grub spoil material should be disposed of properly off site Soft
weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be over excavated to
expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be filled with engineered fill
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction After clearing and grubbing
all subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings were about 16 5 to 110 feet below the
existing ground surface or bridge deck The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
