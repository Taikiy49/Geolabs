2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years
ago
The geologic conditions underlying the Pearl Harbor area and the project site are
the result of a complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by
glacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the
Pleistocene Epoch Following the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano
alluvial soil materials were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the
Pearl Harbor area
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level During the Recent Epoch as sea
level rose to its current stand the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits These deposits were subsequently covered by man made fills placed during
the middle portion of this century for land reclamation projects during construction of the
existing Pearl Harbor area

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most are so small that they could only be detected by sensitive instruments
However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause
minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of high seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example several small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
In addition some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly
damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
Over the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu We understand
that an earthquake of Magnitude 4 8 to 5 0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault

We performed shear wave velocity profiling using Cone Penetrometer Testing
CPT equipment in an effort to more closely analyze the subsurface conditions for
seismic design considerations at the site The CPT method involves inserting a
sensitive probe into the soil at a constant speed Various sensors embedded in the
probe continuously measure the tip resistance side friction and induced pore
pressures as the probe is systematically pushed through the soil In general
subsurface explorations consisting of CPT yields more accurate results for highly
sensitive materials such as the soft estuarine deposits because the data is obtained
directly from the in situ soils Therefore CPT with seismic shear wave velocity
profiling is considered to be the industry standard for higher order analysis such as
the detailed seismic analyses especially in these subsurface conditions
Shear wave velocity tests and piezocone penetration tests were performed to
depths of about 101 0 to 140 4 feet below the ground surface using seismic
piezocone SCPTU testing equipment at the approximate locations shown on
Site Plan Plate 2 Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field
exploration the average shear wave velocities for the materials within the upper
100 feet of the soil profile were between 380 and 420 feet per second

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the 2003 International Building Code IBC the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for the potential for soil
liquefaction Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and a design peak
ground acceleration of 0 18g liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable
settlements at the ground surface Therefore the effects of potential liquefaction
should be taken into consideration when designing the new building structures
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration
such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly
enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress
internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During
liquefaction the soil acquires mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and
vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows

1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more

3 2 Lightweight Fill
In areas where fills greater than 1 foot are required to achieve the design finished
grades such as the area north and northeast of the existing theaters consideration
should be given to using lightweight fill consisting of Expanded Polystyrene EPS
blocks also known as Geofoam The lightweight fill will impose less weight on the
estuarine deposits reducing the amount of additional settlement that will occur
EPS blocks typically have a unit weight of about 2 pounds per cubic foot
Because the site is located in a flood zone area measures should be taken to
counteract the uplift buoyant force imposed on the EPS blocks Typically an adequate
amount of soil fill cover should be provided to result in a minimum factor of safety of 1 3
against uplift buoyant force Since we recommended limiting the amount of soil fills to
1 foot to reduce ground settlement at the site providing an adequate amount of soil fill
cover is not practical therefore other restraint devices should be used to counteract
uplift buoyant force
To counteract the uplift buoyant force acting on the EPS blocks planned at the
existing theater area we recommend strapping the EPS blocks to the foundations of the
existing theater In other areas where EPS blocks are planned we recommend using
other restraint devices such as ground anchors or concrete blocks The EPS blocks
may be strapped down to the ground anchors or concrete blocks that are placed on
either side of the EPS fill An allowable uplift capacity of 10 kips per ground anchor that
extends to a minimum depth of 10 feet below the ground surface may be used for
design
Prior to placement of the EPS blocks we recommend placing a filter fabric and a
thin sand layer at the bottom of the geofoam blocks to assist in block alignment and
provide a more uniform bearing surface

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
two borings extending to depths of about 143 0 and 203 0 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with augers and rotary coring
equipment
The geologist classified materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are

APPENDIX B
Seismic Piezocone Testing
Due to the nature of the subsurface soil conditions at the site we performed
seismic shear wave velocity profiling using Cone Penetrometer Testing CPT in an
effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the site Shear
wave velocity tests and cone penetration tests were performed at three probe locations
designated as CPT 1 through CPT 3 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The probes
extended to depths of about 101 0 and 140 4 feet below the ground surface using
seismic piezocone testing equipment SCPTU
The seismic piezocone penetration test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone manufactured by with an apex
angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1 55 square inches 10 square centimeters
connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 23 25 square inches 150 square
centimeters into the ground The tip and side friction resistance and pore pressure
induced immediately behind the cone tip designated as the U2 parameter during
penetration are measured by strain gages within the instrument and are recorded versus
the depth of penetration in about 2 inch 5 centimeter intervals
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear
wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are made when
the SCPTU has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A shear wave is
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
Logs of the seismic cone penetration tests are presented on Plates B 1 through
B 3 in Appendix B In general the plots include cone tip resistance friction ratio sleeve
friction over tip resistance pore pressure differential pore pressure ratio shear wave
velocity soil behavior based on the R zone and interpreted soil description The values
of the tip resistance friction ratio and pore pressure may be correlated to soil strength
and classification The center of the friction sleeve is approximately 4 inches 10 cm
behind the cone tip To calculate the friction ratio the average friction resistance and tip
resistance are compared at the same depth This involves an offset of the friction
resistance by the physical distance of 4 inches

pore pressure ratio is calculated as a ratio of excess pore pressure to cone tip resistance
expressed as a percentage The excess pore pressure was calculated by subtracting
estimated equilibrium water pressure from the measured pore pressure The equilibrium

and the density of water The soil behavior is determined using the average tip resistance

samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test results
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C 1
Four Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plate C 2
Three Consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 with time rates were performed on soil
samples to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the encountered materials
The test results are presented on Plates C 3 through C 5
Four unconsolidated undrained Triaxial Compression test ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils
The test results are presented on Plates C 6 through C 9
