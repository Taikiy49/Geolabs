GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION 
NEW MIKI WATERLINE 
ISLAND OF LANAI, HAWAII 
Dear Ms. Nishikawa: 
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the New Miki Waterline project on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the 
project. These recommendations are intended for the design of the pipeline foundations 
and site preparation only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is proposed to install a 
new 16-inch diameter waterline extending from Miki Road near the MECO facility to an 
existing pump station near Hii Flats for a total of about 12,900 linear feet (about 
2.44 miles).  The alignment of the new waterline generally follows an existing 10-inch 
diameter cast-iron waterline, within the Palawai Basin south of Lanai City, as shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
We envision that the new waterline will be installed at a relatively shallow depth. 
Based on available geological topographic map, ground elevations along the new 
waterline alignment varies from about +1,220 feet at Miki Road to about +1,120 feet near 
the low depression of the Palawai Basin, and rising to about +1,320 feet near the existing 
pump station.  An existing water tank is above the pump station at the peak of the Hii 
Flats, at an elevation of about +1,640 feet.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is the 
result of building on the southwest rift zone. The summit of the shield collapsed to form a 
caldera from which a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends 
south-southeastward toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff indicate 
that this Manele Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of the caldera, Miki Basin 
(with an average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the 
ridge between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. At 
the south, the floor of the Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, 
where the most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the 
volcano. The project site is located within the Palawai Basin. 
Site Description 
The project is south of Lanai City within a depressed basin (Palawai Basin), that 
was cultivated previously.  It is bounded by Miki Road to the west and Kaumalapau 
Highway to the north, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The new waterline traverses in 
an east-west direction beginning at Miki Road and intersects Manele Road ending at the 
pump station beneath the existing water tank near the mouth of Kapohaku Gulch. 
Ground surface along the new waterline was covered with shrubs and grass.  
Surface soil was very dry at the time of our field exploration. The existing 10-inch waterline 
was partially exposed above the ground surface at several locations, indicating some soil 
erosion occurred. 
Subsurface Condition 
The field exploration at the project site consisted of excavating and sampling 
20 test pits, designated as TP-301 through TP-320. The test pits were excavated to a 
depth of about 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, we encountered a thin surface layer of agricultural soil on the order of 
1 to 2.5 feet thick with extensive agricultural roots and debris. The agricultural soil layer

Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend 
using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) for the 
design of the valve vault foundations. We also recommend using an allowable lateral 
bearing capacity of 1,500 psf for the design of the thrust blocks. 
Based on the laboratory resistivity testing conducted on the near-surface soil, the 
on-site soils have resistivity ranging from 1,000 to 6,000 ohm-cm with chloride contents of 
8 to 20 parts per million (ppm) and sulfate contents of 10 to 35 ppm, which may be 
considered as corrosive to very corrosive. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site grading 
are presented in the following sections. 
Valve Vault Foundations 
We envision that concrete valve structures may be required for the proposed 
waterline. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 psf. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by 
seismic forces.  For design of the valve vaults mat foundation, a modulus of subgrade 
reaction of 150 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used on the 
recompacted on-site soil. 
If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the valve structure 
excavations, they should be removed to expose the underlying firm and/or dense 
materials.  The over-excavation may be backfilled with the excavated on-site soils 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

Soil Resistivity 
Eight sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including pH and minimum resistivity 
were performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-1 of Appendix B. The resistivity was determined in 
general accordance with ASTM G57. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity is, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure will have. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil 
will contribute to corrosion to metallic objects. Results of the resistivity testing indicate 
that the on-site soils have resistivity ranging from 1,000 to 6,000 ohm-cm with pH values 
varying from 4.9 to 7.0. Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered 
corrosive to very corrosive. 
In addition, three sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including chloride content and 
sulfate content, were performed by TestAmerica to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site 
soils encountered. The test results are also summarized on Plate B-1 of Appendix B. 
Details of the test results are included in Appendix C for reference. Based on the 
chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the test values are 
generally relatively low. Therefore, we believe that either Type I or Type II cement may 
be used for the concrete in contact with the ground planned for the project. It may be 
appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results 
and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection. 
Site Grading 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill 
materials. 
In general, the on-site materials generated from the excavations may be re-used as 
a source of general fill and backfill materials, provided that the materials are screened of 
over-sized materials (greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension) and are free of 
deleterious materials.  As mentioned previously, extensive agricultural roots were 
observed in the near-surface soil. The surface soil with vegetation roots should be 
removed during the clearing and grubbing and not be used as the general fill and backfill.

Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the new waterline would be on the order of about 3 feet below 
the existing ground surface.  Installation of the proposed waterline will involve excavation 
into the underlying near-surface agriculture soil and stiff residual/alluvium soils.  It is 
anticipated that the agriculture soil and residual/alluvium soils may be excavated with 
normal heavy excavation equipment.  However, based on previous experience in the area, 
there is a potential that boulders and cobbles may be encountered within the alluvium 
materials. 
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is based 
solely on anticipated surface conditions at the project site and previous experience in the 
project vicinity. 
Trench Excavation Support 
We envision that excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the ground 
surface will be required for the installation of the new waterline.  Where excavations 
greater than 5 feet in depth are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and benching 
should be used. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative (i.e., competent person) 
should be on-site at all times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity 
to promptly observe changing or unforeseen conditions, such as, high groundwater, 
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring 
stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to 
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due

Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe.  Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration.  A minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipe. 
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the 
pipe.  Prior to the placement of bedding material, a Geolabs representative should observe 
the excavated trench bottom to evaluate whether firm materials are exposed at the bottom 
of the trench or whether the bedding should be extended deeper. 
Trench Backfill 
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches 
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free-draining granular material 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting 
stresses on the pipe.  In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce 
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement 
and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on-site soils (with a maximum 
particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in 
maximum 8-inch loose level lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent 
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. 
Where trenches are located below pavement areas, the upper 18 inches of the trench 
backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.

be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry

moisture content.  Where compaction is less than required, additional compaction effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the 
specified compaction.
