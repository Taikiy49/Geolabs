Atterberg Limits ASTM D4318
Atterberg Limits test is used to determine the plasticity of the fraction of a soil
material finer that the 40 sieve For the sample tested the fines were found to be
non plastic This conforms to the specifications for both base course and subbase
Modified Proctor ASTM D 1557 C
The Modified Proctor test is used to determine the maximum relative dry density of
a soil and its optimum moisture content percent by dry weight achieving for that
maximum density For the sample tested the maximum relative dry density was
136 0 pounds per cubic foot and the optimum moisture content was 5 0 percent There is

California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883
The California Bearing Ratio CBR test is used to evaluate the support
characteristics of a soil material A sample is compacted in the laboratory to 90 to
100 percent relative dry density and then saturated After saturation the sample is
penetrated with a ram and the corresponding stress is recorded to yield the CBR value at
0 1 and 0 2 inches of penetration The sample tested in our laboratory yielded a
base course material has a CBR value of 85 or more
Flat and Elongated Particles ASTM D 4791
This test is used to estimate the behavior of an aggregate material under repeated
loading Generally flat or elongated aggregate particles will have a tendency to break
down under cyclic loading causing base or subbase material to degrade over time
Generally a flat and elongated particles content of less than 25 percent is considered
acceptable The sample submitted to our lab had results of 13 16 and 10 percent of the
material retained on the ¾ ½ and 3 8 sieves respectively
Los Angeles Abrasion ASTM C 131
This test is used to estimate the behavior of an aggregate material under repeated
loading Generally aggregate particles will have a tendency to abrade and break down
under cyclic loading causing base or subbase material to degrade over time This test
simulates this case by tumbling a sample of the aggregate material with a number of steel
spheres for 500 revolutions In most cases an aggregate loss of less than 40 to
50 percent is considered to be acceptable The sample submitted to our lab had a loss of
24 percent
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Although the sample submitted to our laboratory had test results that generally fell
in the acceptable range for base course material the lack of fine material is a serious
shortcoming in the recycled asphalt base material As stated earlier the fines fraction of
a base material serves to act as a binder Without the fines the material may not
stabilize and there is a potential for it to unravel In addition the CBR value obtained by
testing the sample is low On the basis of these two items we can not recommend the
use of the material as base course
However it is our opinion that the material is acceptable for use as subbase
course and that it exceeds the general requirements for select fill material A principal
obstacle to the use of the material will be the acceptance by the end user and this
obstacle is primarily one of the end user s perception and their sense of aesthetics
