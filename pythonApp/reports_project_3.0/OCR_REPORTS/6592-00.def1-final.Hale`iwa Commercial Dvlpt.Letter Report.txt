are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the plans provided, the proposed wastewater treatment and disposal 
system for the Hale`iwa Commercial Development has been planned as an aerobic 
treatment system followed by a disposal component consisting of six 45-foot by 75-foot 
absorption beds. 
In order to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils and the disposal 
capacities of the proposed leach field site for wastewater disposal, field percolation tests 
were conducted within the proposed disposal area in general accordance with the State 
of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater 
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards. 
PURPOSE AND SCOPE 
The purpose of our field exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface 
conditions at the proposed disposal component location and to perform field percolation 
tests to evaluate the subsoil infiltration characteristics to formulate geotechnical 
recommendations for the design of the disposal components of the on-site sewage 
disposal system. The work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal 
dated October 24, 2011. Our scope of work included the following tasks and work 
efforts:

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program consisted of the excavation of four relatively shallow 
test pits to depths ranging from about 2.6 to 5.0 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Two of the test pits were excavated to determine the local depth to groundwater and 
colocated pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 and 2, were excavated for the percolation 
tests.  The approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the test pits encountered a surface layer of fill, consisting of brown 
clayey silt with some sand and construction debris, extending to a depth of about 1.5 to 
2.0 feet below the existing ground surface. Below this surface fill, the excavations 
encountered generally soft to medium stiff alluvial materials, consisting of brown clayey silt 
with trace of sand and some cobbles, to the maximum depth explored of 5.0 feet below the 
existing ground surface. This alluvial horizon graded with more cobbles and some 
boulders as the depth increased in Test Pit No. 1. It should be noted that the brown clayey

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS 
We understand that on-site sewage treatment and disposal are proposed for the 
future commercial development.  Therefore, we have conducted field percolation tests to 
obtain data pertaining to the design of the sewage disposal system. 
Field Percolation Testing 
Field percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of 
Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater 
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the 
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The percolation tests were performed by the 
excavation of approximately 2-foot by 4-foot by 3-foot (W x L x D) test pits. The pits were 
presoaked for a minimum of 4 hours prior to the percolation testing. 
Approximately 1 inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the pits to protect 
the bottom of the holes from scouring. The holes were carefully filled with water to a 
minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack. The time intervals and water drops 
were then recorded to provide data upon which calculation of the percolation tests were 
based.

and between depths of about 2.6 and 3.6 feet (Test Pit No. 2) below the existing ground 
surface at the proposed disposal component location. Field percolation test results are

Based on the test results obtained, the percolation rate at the site exceeds 
60 minutes per inch, which is restrictive for conventional in-ground disposal of 
wastewater effluent.  We believe the percolation characteristics are not favorable for the 
construction of conventional absorption beds in the on-site alluvial soils.  
Alternate Disposal System 
After review of the Addendum to Preliminary Engineering Report – Wastewater 
dated August 2011, prepared by Group 70 International, it is our opinion that an 
alternate disposal system may be developed by combining the concepts of an 
absorption bed and a mound disposal system.  Based on the topographic plan provided, 
it appears that approximately 5 feet of fill or more will be required to raise the grade of 
the northern side of the development to provide parking for the shops and restaurants. 
We recommend mass grading the site using a relatively permeable material, 
such as recycled asphalt pavement (RAP), to an elevation equivalent to about 12 inches 
below final pavement subgrade.  Preliminary percolation testing at another site where 
RAP material was used as fill indicates that this material has infiltration rates on the 
order of about 12 to 15 minutes per inch. 
The fill material should be compacted to a minimum of 90% relative density.  
Infiltration chambers, similar to those manufactured by Infiltrator Systems, Inc., 
Hancor, Inc. or Cultec, Inc. should then be installed in the mass graded fill area.  This 
would require excavation of the RAP fill.  However, the material may be reused if it is 
not contaminated by the underlying clayey soils. 
The chambers should be underlain by about 6 inches of No. 3B Fine aggregate. 
The chamber beds should be backfilled with No. 3B Fine to pavement subgrade level 
and then covered with geogrid material, such as Tensar Triax TX140. It is highly 
recommended to underlie the entire parking area with the geogrids.  The area may then 
be brought to final subgrade level using the excavated base course material compacted 
to 95% relative density. 
Since it is anticipated that the wastewater system’s peak loading will be during 
daylight hours, this system would provide additional storage for infiltration of the effluent 
during non-peak hours and would provide additional filtration of the effluent as it 
percolates into the subsurface. 
The chambers selected for this installation should be traffic rated to support the 
wheel loads of vehicle that will traverse the parking area. Preliminary research indicated 
that Infiltrator Systems provides a product that has an H-20 rating and Hancor provides 
chambers with an H-10 rating.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information 
obtained from the field test pit observations and percolation tests. Variations of subsoil

reductions of 34%* in Vermont with

University laboratory research and extended field 
evaluation has verified the infiltrative efficiency
advantage of the open surface architecture provided
by chamber systems over old-fashioned stone and
pipe systems.  The advantage lies in the absence of
solid bodies in the leachfield surface architecture –
the matrix of combined gravel, soil and biomat at the
infiltrative surface.  
The stones embed into the soil surface and occupy
about 60% of the potential infiltrative area. This
forces the water to travel through the limited pores in
the soil- filled gaps between the stones.  An
obstructing layer of biomat forms in all leachfields
treating septic tank effluent.  As the biomat develops,
the infiltrative capacity of that remaining soil area 
is reduced to a point where ponding occurs in 
the leachfield.  
The advantage of the open surface architecture is
that the entire area is available for infiltration instead
of the 40% remaining area between the stones. The
open surface architecture of chambers over old-fash-
ioned stone trenches is expressed as: 
100% open area / 40% open area = 
2.5 more infiltration potential.  
With an added safety factor, a 34% smaller 
footprint will provide equal or better 
infiltration than a stone and pipe trench.

Preparing the Site
1. Review site plans to determine the height of the seasonal
high water table or other limiting factors.
2. Calculate the number of sand lifts necessary. 
3. Confirm that the sand used to build the mound meets
Section 1-517 (c) requirements. If no specifications are avail-
able, Infiltrator Systems recommends sand fill that meets
the current state specifications.
4. Install sedimentation and erosion control measures.
5. Cut trees flush to the ground. Above ground vegetation
should be closely cut and removed. Do not remove surface
boulders. 
6. Refer to the engineer’s plan for preparing subgrade.
Prepare existing ground surface per section 1-517. 
Note: Topsoil and subsoil may never be removed from
beneath a mound wastewater disposal area.
Placing the Sand
1. Use a dozer or tracked
vehicle to evenly spread a
one-foot lift of specified fill
material over  required
area.
2. Each sand lift must be
tracked in by making 
several passes back and
forth to consolidate the fill.
The contractor may 
determine the means and methods necessary to 
stabilize fill. Add water if necessary to obtain appropriate
moisture content. 
Note: Stabilizing the fill is critical to prevent settling and will
not have a significant effect on permeability of clean, sandy fill.
3. To stabilize the fill, a tracked vehicle can be driven over
the entire fill/mound. After first tracks are made across the
fill/mound, move across the fill/mound at increments equal
to the width of the wheels/tracks. 
Note: Check local regulations to determine if wheeled 
vehicles are allowed on fill systems. 
4. Place consecutive lifts following Steps 1 and 2 until
design elevation is achieved (desired elevation is the infil-
trative surface). Lifts should not exceed a 12 inch height.
5. Lightly drag a landscape rake over the final infiltrative 
surface to scarify the top 1⁄2-inch of the sand. Check bed 
elevation to be sure it is level.
