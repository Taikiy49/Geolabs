INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Replacement of Oshiro Bridge project in the Glenwood area
of the District of Puna on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an area
_ of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of the Kilauea Shield Volcano
which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
materials These flows are believed to be late Pleistocene to Holocene in age and belong
to the Puna Volcanic Series The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow
which is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure
of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3976 00 A Page 5
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

Our field exploration at the bridge replacement site generally encountered basalt
rock formation near the existing ground surface extending to the maximum depth
explored of approximately 40 feet below the existing pavement surface Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered we believe that a shallow foundation system bearing
on the medium hard to hard basalt rock formation may be used to support the proposed
new abutments and wing walls To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting be implemented at the foundations for the abutments and
wing walls during the project construction

Based on the design criteria provided by the County of Hawaii and State of
Hawaii Department of Transportation the Replacement of Oshiro Bridge project will

Scour Potential
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying
away material from the bed and banks of the stream Total scour over a period of time
generally consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation
2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of
factors such as the shape and dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity
of approach flow size and gradation of stream bed material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristic of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material Ds in
conjunction with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate the fall velocity
of stream bed materials in scour depth analysis Based on the materials encountered
in the two borings drilled for the project it is anticipated that high quality basalt rock
formation will be encountered at the proposed bottom of footing elevations of the
replacement bridge The basalt rock formation may be considered a non erodible

Bridge Foundations
Based on the design criteria provided by the County of Hawaii the replacement
bridge foundations will be designed based on the Working Stress Design WSD
method Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a
shallow foundation system bearing on the hard slightly to moderately weathered
slightly to closely fractured basalt rock formation be used for support of the planned
bridge abutment and wing wall structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to
15 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on the basalt rock formation This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
In general footings should be embedded deep enough to bear directly on the
hard slightly to closely fractured basalt rock formation or extended to the depth of the
existing bridge foundations whichever is deeper Based on our exploratory borings we
anticipate that bottom of footings will need to be located about 8 to 10 feet below the
existing pavement grade in order to bear directly on the high quality basalt rock surface
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying hard basalt rock formation The
GEOLABS HAWAII

over excavation should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete or the bottom of
footing may be extended deeper to bear on the basalt rock surface
It should be noted that the foundations for the replacement bridge should be
placed directly on the basalt rock formation Soil type backfills such as a subbase or
leveling course should not be used below the foundations to avoid undermining and
scour due to the anticipated flowing water conditions at the bridge location Therefore we
recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending

lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may exist in the basalt
rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend
that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the abutment and wing
wall areas The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at
least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be
drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity
and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for
grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of granular materials generally
less than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill material should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 12 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it appears that the trench work for utility installation will likely involve
excavation into the basaltic rock formation and will require hard ripping or hoerams Care
must be exercised to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the rock
formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 each extending to a depth of
about 40 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers and coring tools The approximate locations of the borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 2 2
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Two unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the underlying
basalt rock formation The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic representation of the grain size distribution is provided on Piate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture content of the soil
Compaction of the sample was performed by dropping a 10 pound hammer from a height
of 18 inches The sample was compacted in 5 equal layers with each layer receiving
10 blows of energy The sample was soaked for 4 days prior to the penetration test The
results of the test are presented on Plate B 2
