SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As required by the City and County of Honolulu a slope assessment and rockfall
evaluation was conducted for the proposed Waianae Coast YMCA development in an
effort to study and provide mitigation recommendations pertaining to the potential rockfall
hazards present at the property Our ground surface reconnaissance at the project site
consisted of identifying the sources of potential rockfall on the hillside and mapping the
general distribution of surface boulders that appear to have rolled down to the lower
elevations of the hillside In addition rockfall simulation using computer modeling was
performed to estimate the rockfall behavior emanating from the identified source areas
and to assist in the selection and design of an appropriate rockfall hazard mitigation
scheme for the proposed development
The basalt rock formation comprising the mountainside at the project site consists
of layers of lava flow that have eroded over time to form a stepped slope profile The
differential erosion occurring between the various layers and rock splitting along
pre existing fractures are responsible for the production of falling rocks from the hillside
Based on the results of our rockfall hazard analyses of the existing hillside we estimate
that about 27 to 44 percent of the boulders that may roll down the mountainside during a
rockfall event would pass through the toe of the existing hillside and encroach into the
development site Therefore we believe that there are rockfall hazards present at the
property that are in need of mitigation for the development of the new Waianae Coast
YMCA Facility
Our evaluation of the existing conditions at the property suggest that the
performance of slope scaling and installation of draped anchored wire mesh may not be
feasible practical or effective in mitigating the rockfall hazards present at the property
Based on our rockfall hazard analyses we believe that 1 the installation of a
high impact rockfall barrier upslope of the existing concrete drainage interceptor ditch with
a chain link fence along the toe of the hillside or 2 the construction of a rockfall
catchment ditch with a low impact rockfall barrier along the toe of the hillside should
provide a relatively high level of protection for the proposed development from potential
rockfall hazards upslope of the project site
The rockfall impact barriers proposed for construction at the project site consists of
a specialized barrier fence structure which is designed to absorb the energy from rockfall
impact and retain the fallen material Based on our engineering analysis for the project
site the proposed high impact rockfall impact barrier should consist of a 12 foot high
barrier with a rated energy absorption capacity encompassing about 184 foot tons The

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes identified as Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of
the island The younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its
eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank
These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield
Plateau
The Waianae Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and some
pyroclastic materials which are collectively grouped and classified as belonging to the
Waianae Volcanic Series The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower
middle and upper volcanic members The lower member is comprised of lava flows
and associated pyroclastic rocks that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield
Volcano The middle member consists of rock that accumulated and gradually filled the
vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a relatively thin capping layer that covered
the entire top of the shield volcano late in its history of evolution
Once the majority of the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep
erosion landslide activity sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu
occurred producing the large amphitheater valleys located on the west side of the
Waianae Shield Volcano The deep erosional valleys were carved by down cutting
streams and landslide mass transfer processes which gradually cut back the valley
walls and filled the valley floors with large accumulations of alluvial and colluvial
materials Over the passage of time and as a result of multiple sea level fluctuations
some of the valley filling alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through varying
degrees of consolidation and cementation formed relatively dense conglomerate
materials These consolidated and or semi consolidated older alluvial and colluvial

Based on our field observations the basalt rock formation comprising the
mountainside consists of sequential layers of lava flow that have eroded over time to
form a stepped topographic slope profile The stepped rock slope profile is a typical
result of erosion and weathering processes acting upon the rock layers which have
variable resistance to decomposition and erosion Therefore the hillside is composed
of sequential hard erosion resistant layers dense basalt rock which tend to form ledge
rock outcrops and more erosive layers of softer rock material clinker and thinly bedded
lava which tend to form flatter slope segments containing some thin soil development
The flatter interslope segments typically harbor loose boulders and broken rock
fragments that have fallen from upslope sources
Some of the individual rock ledge features observed upslope from the project site
are sub vertical in inclination discontinuous in length and range from about 25 to
35 feet in height However the majority of the rock ledges are generally less than about

and the splitting of larger rock components comprising the rock ledge features along
existing fracture planes and joint surfaces likely are responsible for the production of
falling rock from the hillside In addition smaller boulders and rock fragments residing
on the interslope segments may be re mobilized as rockfall due to impact from other
falling rock heavy storm water runoff and disturbance by human activity
It should be noted that periodic wild fires which destroy the grasses and
shrubbery that provides ground cover and resistance to erosion have in the past

foot traffic and ground disturbance produced by firefighters traversing the slope may
cause instability of the existing loose boulder deposits residing on the hillside

3 1 Rockfall Mechanisms
Rockfall involves the detachment and fall of rock material from the slope face
Rockslide is another term that may be used to describe the detachment from a slope
and fall of a mass of rock material Rockfall is a more general term used to describe the
process of falling rock from a hillslope Rockfall may encompass the fall of a single
boulder or rock fragment or a shower of boulders or rock fragments Some of the
applicable mechanisms generating rockfall events at the site are described below

Loosening of rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of causes
including water pressure behind the rock differential weathering or erosion along
geologic contacts faults or shear zones weakening of rock due to temperature and
moisture cyclic fluctuations loosening by animals trees wind and ground vibration due
to earthquake seismic forces construction activity and vibration from loud sound
waves
A frequent cause of rockfalls is by invasive tree roots prying on the existing
cracks located in the rock slope surface Tree roots frequently develop and grow larger
in the discontinuities of rock materials The added force of wind action on tree branches
may create leveraged forces that are transferred to the roots lodged within the
discontinuity Over time the repetitive leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock
and cause it to fall
Inter layered rock such as lava flows with variable strength and rates of
weathering commonly exhibit accelerated erosion and rockfall The weaker layers
clinker tend to erode and undercut the more weathering resistant rock located above
This may lead to failure of undercut blocks of rock as support from the underlying
weaker layers is removed It is an especially important failure mechanism where thick
layers of altered clinker are bounded by very dense fine grained basalt rock The
contrast in rock strength is great and the dense rock layers act to retard groundwater
percolation thus forcing groundwater to build up and seep from the more permeable
clinker horizons
The construction of cut slopes create external disturbance to the slopes by
opening and widening joints in the rock and loosening the weaker clinker layers In
addition poor quality rock blasting techniques may create a network of secondary
cracks and stress induced crack patterns in addition to the natural joint patterns Over
the passage of time open face exposure of rock cut slopes allows the development of
cracks as a result of the relief of stress from the rock body

The daily cyclic temperature change from alternating exposure to sunlight and
shade as well as air temperature changes can accelerate erosion and weathering to
produce rockfall and landslide activity In the open area of the leeward coast the effect
of rock surface exposure to sunlight does cause a significant fluctuation of temperature
on the rock surface
Large trees that are dead or weakened by disease can come loose and fall from
the slope When the entire tree falls from a steep slope a potentially large amount of
entrained soil or rock material may accompany the root system The tree fall and
entrained earth material produces substantial debris that may reach the road In
addition to the fall of the tree the movement of the tree downslope may trigger
additional slope instability and cause additional fall of loose rock and debris
Furthermore the portion of the steep slope that the tree once occupied may be left in a
state of instability The slope instability may consist of a pocket or void space and the
loss of the pre existing root network may upset the equilibrium of stability that existed on
the hillside
Groundwater seepage from a slope and high pore water pressure in a slope
reduces the shear strength of soil and rock materials leading to potential slope failure
In addition water pressure acting upon tension cracks in rock formation may lead to
rockfall Groundwater seepage and pore water pressure build up are related to the
regional and localized groundwater conditions Groundwater seepage and pore water
pressure build up are also related to precipitation therefore there may be seasonal
variation in groundwater levels The seasonal groundwater variation may compound
the effects of normal cyclic ground moisture fluctuations from precipitation events

3 2 Rockfall Hazard Analyses
The Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP is a computer program that is
a widely accepted engineering tool used by experienced personnel to estimate potential
rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input parameters that are
selected on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project were selected
based on the observations and measurements collected in the field Basically the
program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on the
hillslope topographic profile and other input information such as rock size rock shape
and other parameters used to characterize the ground surface irregularities
Information obtained from the CRSP analysis included the predicted falling rock
velocity bounce height kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is
useful to assist in the site specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as
rockfall impact barriers or other rockfall containment systems
The CRSP was run utilizing a selected slope profile located above the project
site as indicated on the Site Plan Plate 2 The slope profile was created by combining
the regional United States Geological Survey U S G S map for the upper hillside and

3 3 Rockfall Impact Barrier
As previously mentioned rockfall impact barriers are used worldwide as an
effective rockfall mitigation and protection system Rockfall impact barriers are
constructed commonly on hillsides where protection of downslope areas from falling rock
is necessary Rockfall impact barriers are considered a viable rockfall protection system
where the source of rockfall cannot be readily identified and stabilized or where the source
area encompasses extensive land area that makes other mitigation measures impractical
to implement
W O INC Page 16
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The rockfall impact barrier proposed for construction within the project site consists
of a specialized fence structure which is designed to absorb the energy from rockfall
impact and retain the rolling boulders The rockfall impact barrier consists of cable wire
rope ring nets spanning between support posts that are held in place by anchor plates
and lateral restraint cables The barrier is designed to absorb the energy of a rock impact
and dissipate the energy through the use of flexible nets which act as a braking element
The rocks are thereby caught in the barrier as opposed to bouncing or striking with enough
force to topple the retention system The barrier may be designed for varying load impacts
that are estimated based on the existing site conditions such as boulder size slope
inclination and composition computed rolling velocities and possible boulder bounce
heights
The barriers are commonly used along highways and other areas where structures
located along lengthy slope segments must be protected from rockfall The barriers are
suitable for sites where rockfall sources are widely scattered and where it may be
generally impractical to locate and stabilize the individual hazardous blocks of rock
because of the quantity of hazards difficult slope access and other restrictions The
barriers would require some periodic maintenance to check the performance of the system
and remove accumulated rock material behind the barrier
The primary advantage of the rockfall impact barrier is that it is a comprehensive
rockfall protection system which should provide a high level of protection against most of
the potential rockfall hazards on the slopes In addition man induced disturbance to the
slope and natural vegetation would be limited to the immediate area surrounding the
rockfall impact barrier installation
The main components of the rockfall impact barrier include the support posts wire
ring nets anchorage ground plates wire retaining ropes braking rings and the spiral rope
anchors The steel components are typically hot dipped galvanized and the wire ropes are
DIN 2078 coated A special protective zinc aluminum coating may be applied where
environmental corrosion is of concern Under normal field conditions the lifespan of the
steel material components is estimated to range on the order of about 50 years Because
W O INC Page 17
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
of the higher corrosiveness of the environment in Hawaii primarily due to the oceanic
environment warm air temperature and higher humidity the lifespan of the steel
materials may be estimated on the order of about 20 years It is recommended that some
periodic inspection occurring on the order of about 5 to 10 year intervals be implemented
to check the condition of the rockfall impact barrier system for possible signs of corrosion
It should be noted that the ring nets which span between the posts may be
temporarily disconnected in order to remove boulders that may be caught by the rockfall
impact barrier Larger boulder impacts may require that some net panels be replaced to
maintain the integrity of the system It is difficult if not impossible to predict the frequency
of possible boulder impacts on the barrier It may be roughly estimated that a large
boulder impact under normal conditions may occur on the order of about once per 5 to
15 years
The effectiveness of a rockfall impact barrier as a rockfall mitigation measure for the
proposed development was evaluated by placing a rockfall impact barrier either
1 upslope of the existing concrete drainage interceptor ditch or 2 along the toe of the
existing hillside Based on our evaluation we believe that the installation of a rockfall
impact barrier upslope of the existing concrete drainage interceptor ditch would be the
more desirable location for the rockfall barrier alignment In general the safety goal for the
mitigation measure performance is to capture and retain 90 percent of the potential rockfall
based on the existing site conditions As indicated on Plate B 6 of Appendix B with the
installation of a high impact rockfall barrier upslope of the existing concrete drainage
interceptor ditch we estimate that only about 2 to 3 percent of the boulders that may roll
down the mountainside during a rockfall event would pass through the toe area of the
existing hillside and encroach into the development area

capacity with a rated capacity encompassing about 184 foot tons The maximum spacing
between the fence posts should be limited to about 20 to 25 feet Support cables and
