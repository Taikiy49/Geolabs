This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration for 
the Puuhonua O Waianae Mauka Development project at 85-908 Waianae Valley Road 
in Waianae on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory

the project. These recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of the 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, leaching field, site grading, and pavements only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Puuhonua O Waianae Mauka Development project site is located at 
85-908 Waianae Valley Road in Waianae on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
area extends north from Waianae Valley Road for about 0.3 miles and encompasses 
approximately 20 acres of land.  
Based on the provided concept plans, we understand the development will 
include an event space, 200 housing units, 11 common amenity buildings, several 
support buildings, an agricultural warehouse and base yard, and paved and gravel 
roads. We understand the planned buildings will be single-story and anticipate relatively 
light loading.  
Based on the topographic survey map provided and our field observations, the 
project site is generally on a ridge with a ravine traversing the southern portion of the 
site. The highest point of the site (near the Pilokea Street entrance) has an existing

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site consisted of conducting Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer (DCP) testing at eight locations, designated as DCP-1 through DCP-8. 
When a DCP test encountered refusal subsurface conditions within the upper 10 feet, 
an additional DCP test (denoted with an “A” suffix) was performed at an offset location. 
A total of 16 DCP tests were performed at the project site, extending to depths of about 
0.7 and 3.7 feet below the existing ground surface. Five bulk samples, designated as 
Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, were collected from the near-surface soils for laboratory testing 
as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their engineering properties. In 
addition, a test hole (designated as P-1) was excavated to a depth of about 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface for percolation testing access. The approximate locations of 
the DCP testing, bulk samples, and percolation testing are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration and the regional geology in the project vicinity, the 
subsurface conditions at the project site generally consisted of stiff/dense fills overlying 
alluvium/colluvium materials at greater depth. We anticipate the fill materials underlying 
the project site were undocumented and, therefore, highly variable in locations and 
depths. It should be noted that coralline boulders and basaltic cobbles were observed at 
the ground surface throughout the project site. We anticipate the above cobbles and 
boulders may be embedded below the ground surface resulting in the DCP testing 
refusal conditions. 
We did not encounter groundwater at the testing locations at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
Portable DCP testing provides a measure of in-situ material’s relative stiffness.  It 
was used for probing by driving an 8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg 
(17.6-lb) hammer falling 575-mm (22.6-inch) in vertical height. The penetration depth 
was recorded per blow and converted to standard penetration resistance (SPT, ASTM 
D1586) using a correlation between Penetration Index (PI) and SPT, developed by 
Sowers and Hedges and later Livneh and Ishai. 
Results of the DCP testing are presented on the Logs of DCP in Appendix A, 
Plates A-1 through A-16. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples are presented in Appendix B, Plates B-1 through B-16.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered highly variable stiff/dense fill materials 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. We did not encounter groundwater at the testing and bulk sample locations at the 
time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are 
subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, 
and other factors. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and the anticipated 
light structural loads for the structures, we envision the proposed buildings may be 
supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread, continuous strip, and 
slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge footings. Detailed discussions and recommendations 
for the project design are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision 
that the proposed single-story structures with relatively light structural loads (less than 
30 kips) may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or 
continuous strip footings. As an alternative, slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge footings 
may also be used. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 1,500 psf may be used for the 
design of footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills 
(minimum 90 percent relative compaction) placed to achieve the design finished grades. 
This bearing value is for dead plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.  
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes 
or on the sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 
horizontal setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings 
(base of footings) to the face of the slope. Foundations next to other subsurface 
structures, including but not limited to existing utility trenches and/or easements, should 
be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom 
edge of the subsurface structure, or the proposed footings should extend to a depth as 
deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid 
surcharging adjacent subsurface structures with additional structural loads and to 
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
The bottom of the footing excavations should be scarified to a depth of at least 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
and recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm 
and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with

If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing 
excavations, soft and/or loose materials should be over-excavated until dense and/or

should be backfilled with general fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent

If foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, the total settlement of the foundations is estimated 
to be on the order of about 1 inch or less with differential settlements on the order of 
about 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the 
base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure 
developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations. A 
coefficient of friction of 0.25 may be used for footings bearing directly on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills. Resistance due to passive earth 
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pounds per square 
foot per foot of depth (pcf), assuming that the soils around the footings are 
well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure 
resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. For 
footings adjacent to sloping ground, the passive resistance should be reduced or 
neglected. 
Slabs-On-Grade 
Generally, the project site is underlain by moderate to highly expansive clayey 
soils. Concrete slabs-on-grade constructed above these expansive soils should be 
properly designed and constructed to reduce the potential for future distress to the 
lightly loaded conventional concrete slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and 
swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content.  
We recommend incorporating the slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge footings 
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent well-compacted grade. We also 
recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils by scarifying to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioning to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacting to between 85 and 90 percent relative compaction, as specified in the 
“Site Preparation” subsection provided herein.  
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery 
vibration), we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch-thick layer of cushion fill consisting 
of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support to the slabs and serve as a capillary 
moisture break. To reduce the potential for appreciable future moisture infiltration

Retaining Structures 
We envision new retaining structures such as drainage headwalls and wingwalls 
may be required at the project site. In general, retaining structures should be designed 
to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, the designs for the 
retaining walls should be based on the parameters presented in the following 
subsections. 
Wall Foundations 
In general, we believe retaining wall foundations may be designed in accordance 
with the recommendations and parameters presented in the “Shallow Foundations” 
section. In addition, retaining wall foundations should be at least 18 inches wide 
and the bottom should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grades.

In general, the at-rest condition should be used for retaining structures where the

active condition should be used only for gravity retaining walls and retaining 
structures that are free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of the wall height. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the retaining structures should be 
considered in the design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded 
side of the retaining structure, a rectangular distribution with a uniform pressure

33 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the 
structure may be used for the design. 
Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or to weep holes. As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
connected hydraulically to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
A typical drainage system for site retaining walls would consist of 1 cubic foot of 
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of the weep hole locations. The 
weep holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weep hole or drainage 
layer should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of 
the retaining wall backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce 
the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure unless 
covered by concrete slabs at the surface.

The field percolation test was performed in general accordance with the 
provisions of the State of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 
11-62, “Wastewater Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate 
the percolation characteristics of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The test 
for P-1 was performed by excavating a hole of about 36 inches in diameter and 
extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Due to the low 
percolation of the first 12 inches of added water, the test hole was presoaked for at least 
4 hours. A 1-inch-thick layer of sand was placed at the bottom of the hole prior to the 
percolation testing. 
The field test consisted of multiple trials of carefully filling the hole with water to a 
depth of about 6 inches above the sand layer until successive trials did not vary by more 
than one-sixteenth of an inch. The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to 
provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation rate was based. Based on the 
field percolation test located at P-1, the percolation rate was achieved at approximately 
27 minutes per inch. It should be noted that percolation rates in excess of 60 minutes 
per inch are considered unsuitable for leach fields.  
Based on the results of the percolation test, a wastewater disposal system like a 
leach field would likely be feasible for this project site. Results of the percolation test are 
presented on the Site Evaluation/Percolation Test form, Plate C-1.

Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or in 
a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated 
materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to 
expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-
compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be reused as fill materials and 
should be properly disposed of off-site or in landscaped areas, if appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, moisture conditioned to 2 to 
4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to 90 percent 
relative compaction. The compaction requirements for finished subgrades 
subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the pavement subgrade, should be increased 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Where shrinkage cracks are observed at the ground surface after compaction of 
the subgrade, we recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended 
above. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to 
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas 
and replacement with well-compacted fill. A Geolabs representative should 
evaluate the need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
General fill may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum particle size of 6 inches 
and should be free of deleterious material. In general, the excavated on-site soils 
may be reused as a source of general fill material, provided they are free of 
vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The 
non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction (or 95 percent relative 
compaction for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic).  
Aggregate base course and subbase course materials should be moisture 
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
It should be noted that our recommendations require elevated moisture contents to 
reduce the potential swelling/heaving of the clayey soils encountered at the site. 
Therefore, we wish to emphasize that moisture conditioning of the fill materials is an 
integral and critical part of the earthwork recommendations provided herein. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Where moisture and/or compaction are less than required, additional moisture 
conditioning and compactive effort should be applied with adjustment as necessary. 
Where the reduced compaction requirements are applicable, it may be necessary 
to scarify the soil and recompact it to the proper range of relative compaction, if the 
required range of compaction is exceeded.

Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision the cut slopes at the project site generally will expose the stiff/dense 
clayey and sandy soils with varying amounts of gravel, cobbles and boulders as 
encountered during our field exploration. In general, cut slopes and permanent fill 
slopes constructed of the on-site soils may be designed with a slope inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 
5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of 
the new fill embankment against sliding. The filling operations should start at the 
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations.  
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Surface water should be diverted away 
from the tops of slopes, and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible 
to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the 
curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil to reduce the potential for migration 
of excessive landscape water into the pavement section. 
Corrosion Potential 
Chemical analyses, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride content, and 
sulfate content, were performed on selected soil samples obtained during our field 
exploration for corrosivity evaluation. The test results are presented on Plate B-16 of 
Appendix B. 
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. 
Generally, a lower resistivity value results in a greater potential for corrosion of buried 
metallic objects. Conversely, a higher resistivity value means the soil is less likely to 
contribute to the corrosion of metallic objects. Based on the results of the laboratory 
testing, the subsurface soils at the project site exhibit minimum resistivity values of 
about 970 and 1,100 ohm-cm, indicating that the subsurface soils are extremely to very 
corrosive (Corrosion Ratings of 1 and 2, respectively) based on the guidelines provided 
by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply (1991). 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 300 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the results 
of the laboratory testing, we believe the near-surface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive”. Therefore, we believe that either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) 
cement may be used for reinforced concrete substructures in contact with the ground 
planned for the project, provided that the appropriate minimum concrete cover over the 
reinforcing steel is maintained. 
It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review 
the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored using Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer (DCP) testing at eight locations, designated as DCP-1 through DCP-8. 
When a DCP test encountered refusal subsurface conditions within the upper 10 feet, 
an additional DCP test (denoted with an “A” suffix) was performed at an offset location. 
A total of 16 DCP tests ranging between about 0.7 and 3.7 feet below the existing 
ground surface were performed at the project site. The approximate DCP locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Portable DCP was used for probing by driving an 8-mm diameter 60° steel cone 
tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. The penetration depth was 
recorded per blow and converted to standard penetration resistance (SPT, ASTM 
D1586) using a correlation between Penetration Index (PI) and SPT, developed by 
Sowers and Hedges and later by Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and SPT blow counts are 
presented on the Logs of DCP at the appropriate depths, presented on the Logs of 
DCP, Plates A-1 through A-16.

Laboratory Tests 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected bulk 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) were performed on 
selected bulk samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on remolded soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Eight laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-12. 
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 C) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship. The test results are presented on Plates B-13 through B-15. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected bulk 
samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-16.

Field Percolation Test 
One field percolation test was performed at a selected location, designated as 
P-1, in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix C of the State of Hawaii, 
Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater 
Systems” to evaluate the percolation characteristics of the subsurface soils for 
wastewater disposal. The approximate field percolation test location is shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  
The percolation test was performed in the subsoil material by excavating an 
approximate 36-inch diameter hole to the specified depth of about 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Prior to testing, the percolation test hole was prepared by 
removing the smeared and loose materials from the sidewalls and bottom of the 
borehole, respectively, and by placing about 1-inch thick layer of coarse sand at the 
bottom of the borehole. During testing, the hole was carefully filled with clean water to a 
minimum depth of 6 inches above the bottom of the hole. The time intervals and water 
level drops were recorded to provide field data upon which calculation of the percolation 
rate was based. The percolation test results are presented on the Site 
Evaluation/Percolation Test form, Plate C-1.
