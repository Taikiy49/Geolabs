REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk
of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
The project site is located at the base of the northeastern slope of Puu Papaa
which is essentially an erosional remnant of caldera filling lava flows of the Koolau
Volcano After the Koolau Volcano ceased its activity it was followed by a period of
erosion and subsequent submergence during which time most of the eastern flank of
the volcano was removed Later glacio eustatic sea level changes during the
Pleistocene Epoch resulted in the accumulation of several hundred feet of marine and
non marine sediments
Our field exploration at the project site indicate that the eastern portion of the site
is underlain by highly weathered basalt and saprolite soils derived from the Koolau
Volcanic flows while the remaining portions of the site are underlain by alluvium over
coral reef detritus with pockets of beach sand deposits The alluvium adobe generally
consist of silty clays with high expansion potential and low shear strength when
saturated

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration encountered fills and alluvium consisting of silty clays and
m the surface to depths of about 3 to 14 feet below the existing ground
surface It appears that the clayey fill and alluvium decreases in thickness toward the
east Below the fill materials and alluvial soils coral formation consisting of sands and
sandy coral gravels and or weathered basalt formation were generally encountered
extending to the maximum depths drilled
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing the significant geotechnical
engineering consideration is the engineering properties of the near surface clayey soils
In general the near surface clayey soils have a high shrink swell potential when
subjected to fluctuations in moisture contents In addition it will lose significant
strength when saturated and will shrink when subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage
cracks and potential slope instability Due to climatic changes shrinking and swelling
of the near surface clayey soils should be expected since the near surface soils are
exposed to the elements rain and sunshine To reduce the potential effects of the
highly expansive soils on the perimeter wall we recommend that a drilled pier
foundation system be considered for support of the proposed perimeter wall A shallow
foundation system may be used to support the eastern portion of the perimeter wall
beyond of Wall No 2 due to the thinner near surface expansive soils

Expansive Soils
Based on our laboratory test results the near surface clayey fill and alluvial soils
generally have a high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the
moisture content In general the mechanism of shrinking swelling in expansive clays
is a complex phenomenon and is influenced by a number of factors which include soil
characteristics and environmental factors
Soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering properties such
as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the expansive potential of soils In general the
soil plasticity reflects the capacity of clay minerals to retain large amounts of water
while maintaining a coherent structure Increased soil density through compaction or
natural depositional history such as desiccation generally have greater swelling
potential and higher swelling pressure
Environmental factors comprise of initial moisture content and moisture varia
tions The initial moisture content generaily impact the potential of a soil to absorb or
expel water from the soil system in general a dry soil will have a higher capacity to
absorb water while a wet soil will lose water more readily on exposure to dry conditions
and shrink more Moisture variations can generally be caused by climate surface
drainage vegetation and permeability It should be noted that over watering of the
expansive soils caused by these environmental factors may result in significant
reduction in the shear strength and bearing capacity This is of particular concern for
soils with surficial fissures and cracks that allow for faster water infiltration resulting in
higher swelling potential
As discussed in the Site Description section surficial cracks and other distress
caused by the shrink swell cycles of the near surface clayey soils were observed
Based on our laboratory test results the on site clayey soils may be classified as highly

Slope Stability
As previously discussed the existing slopes below the perimeter wall site range
in inclinations from about 4H 1V to about 2H 1V In general the site slopes down
toward the treatment plant to the north with ground surface elevations ranging from 36
to 20 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Based on our field observation cracks parallel to
the traffic direction were noted on the asphalt concrete pavement of Kaneohe Bay Drive
fronting the proposed perimeter wall In addition shrinkage cracks were also noted
along the slope face below the proposed perimeter wall
Our stability analyses of the existing slope configurations and generalized
subsurface soil conditions indicate that the existing slopes have factors of safety
between about 1 5 and 1 9 for short term stability and factors of safety between
approximately 1 2 and 1 3 for long term stability Generally a minimum factor of safety
of 1 5 is recommended for overall slope stability Therefore it should be noted that
creep movements will likely occur and should be expected along the slope as
evidenced by the tilting chain link fence
In general the cracks noted along the roadway and slope face are indications
of potential instability of the existing slope Based on our observations we believe that
during dry periods the soil cover on the slope desiccated and began to crack forming
fissures for water to enter and accumulate Long periods of rainfall generally allow for

Drilled Pier Foundations
The drilled pier for the perimeter wall would derive vertical support primarily from
skin friction between the pier shaft and the underlying coral and or weathered
basalt formation For estimating purposes we have assumed that the upper
8 feet of soils below the existing ground surface at the perimeter wall location
consist of the highly expansive soils The frictional supporting capacity of the
piers in the highly expansive soils has been neglected
Based on our evaluation we recommend that a drilled pier with a minimum
diameter of 18 inches embedded a minimum of 9 feet below the highly
expansive soils be used to resist the uplift This would result in a drilled pier of
approximately 14 feet below the bottom of the pier cap which is about 3 feet
below the existing ground surface We recommend that the 14 foot drilled pier
be designed with an allowable compression load capacity of 15 kips for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 for transient loads such
as wind and seismic forces A minimum pier spacing of 3 times the pier
diameter center to center is also recommended to avoid reduction in capacity
due to group action

Grade Beam
Based on the existing design concept the grade beam is designed to support
and transfer the distributed stress due to the vertical and lateral loads to the pier
foundations To reduce the potential for damage by expansion of the on site
clayey soils we recommend that grade beams be placed at least 6 inches above
the ground surface to assure a minimum 6 inch gap between the bottom of the
grade beam and the ground surface The 6 inch gap is intended to isolate the
grade beam from the soil and to reduce the potential for the swelling of the
highly expansive soils from applying uplift pressure on the grade beams The
gap also aids in concentrating the structural loads on the drilled piers to resist
uplift forces To facilitate the placement of concrete for the grade beam we
recommend that the gap be filled with a highly compressible material such as
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2651 90 Page 19
Geofoam styrofoam or equivalent The styrofoam should not have a high
crushing strength to reduce the potential for transmitting uplift loads to the grade
beam
Other alternatives include placement of grade beams on other suitable compres
sible materials removable spacer bottom forms or use of special cardboard
soffit forms In the above cases provisions must be made to prevent surface
water from flowing under the foundation If it is not desirable to raise the grade
beam above the ground surface or to prevent contact with the underlying clayey
soils as discussed above the beam should be designed to resist an uplift
pressure of at least 2 500 p s f acting on the bottom of the grade beam The
total uplift loads may be reduced by designing the grade beam as narrow as
structurally practical
Alternatively in lieu of creating a 6 inch gap between the grade beam and the
subgrade we recommend that the subgrade soils below the grade beams be
over excavated by a minimum of 18 inches and replaced with non expansive fill
material to reduce the effects of the highly expansive on site soils The imported
non expansive fill material should be compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction A sketch of the wall section indicating the zone of over

be made to minimize surface water from flowing under the foundation
Drainage
The on site clayey soils have high shrink swell potential wnen subjected to
moisture fluctuations As a result water should be diverted away from the tops of
slopes and adequate slope planting should be done as soon as possible to reduce the
potential for erosion A drainage swale may be provided in front of the proposed
GEOLABS HAWAI

perimeter wall to drain away surface run off water in the shoulder area This will
reduce the shrink swell potential of the underlying soil and prevent over saturation of
the soils which will reduce their shear strength and cause instability of the existing
slope Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to
drain all surface run off away from the foundations
The finish grades outside the wall should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering

In general these drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance
of the foundations constructed on expansive soils since ponded water could cause
subsurface soil saturation and subsequent loss of strength or heaving of the near
surface soils The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the
walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water
infiltration
Site Grading
We understand that the proposed site grading will consist of minor cuts and fills
on the order of about 3 feet relative to the existing ground surface As discussed in the
Slope Stability section the long term stability of the existing slope has a factor of
safety of between 1 2 and 1 3 We understand that regrading of the existing slope or
other remedial measures to increase the stability of the existing slope are considered
cost prohibitive for this phase of the project Therefore it should be noted that the
following recommendations are presented to provide guidelines for cosmetic grading
of the surface at the project site

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation trees debris rubbish
construction wastes and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site to reduce potential contamination of the excavated
materials
The exposed surface following clearing and grubbing should be scarified to a
depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil ex
pressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Fill and Backfill Materials
In general fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Any boulders or cobbles larger than 3 inches in maximum dimension should be
separated from the excavated material and disposed of off site Over
compaction of the retaining wall backfill would exert additional earth pressures
on the walls and should be avoided
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2651 90 Page 22
General and structural fills should consist of imported non expansive or low
expansion materials Import fill materials should consist of 3 inch minus
granular materials such as crushed coral basalt crusher waste or cinder and
should not contain organic or other deleterious materials In general the import
fill should be well graded with 10 to 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve and
should have a laboratory CBR swell value not exceeding 1 percent when
prepared near the optimum moisture Import fill should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction Import fill materials should be tested and
approved for its intended use prior to hauling to the project site
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on our exploration a cut slope ratio of 2 H 1V or flatter may be used
Cut slopes exposing the expansive clayey soils should be protected from drying
to reduce the potential for instability due to shrinkage cracks Therefore the
slope face should be adequately protected from development of shrinkage
cracks It may be prudent to flatten cut slopes to 3H 1V or flatter In the event
that over cutting occurs consideration should be given to leaving the slope
rather than attempting to backfill the slope to the design grade with sliver fills
If backfilling of an over cut slope is necessary keying and benching require
ments should be implemented Alternatively cut slopes may be buttressed with
fill slopes constructed of non expansive or low expansion fills
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the compacted non expansive materials may
be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Where the existing
ground is steeper than 5H 1V keying and benching will be required to properly
bond the new fill to the existing slope

W O 2651 90 Page 23
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above recommendations for
fill placement Fill slopes should be constructed by over filling beyond the design
slope limits and cutting back to the design slope to provide adequate compaction
at the edge of the slope
We also recommend that V ditches be provided at the top of slopes and
geotextile fabrics be placed on the slope to reduce the potential for erosion
