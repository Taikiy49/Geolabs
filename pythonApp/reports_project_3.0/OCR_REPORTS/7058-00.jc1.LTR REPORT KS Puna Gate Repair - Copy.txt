PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Puna gate serves as a secondary school access on the southeastern side of 
the Kamehameha Schools along the eastern bank of an existing gulch. Existing Concrete 
Rubble Masonry (CRM) retaining walls are located parallel to and support the access 
road. The CRM wall has a net retaining height of about up to 6.5 feet. 
The slope below the CRM walls is about one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) with 
moderate brush/tree coverage, extending down to the gulch. No weephole was observed 
on the CRM walls. Conditions of the existing CRM walls were moderately fair except a 
1-inch wide vertical crack between the higher CRM wall and the lower CRM wall. Based 
on our observation, we suspect that the vertical crack may be caused by excessive 
hydrostatic pressure behind the lower CRM wall and/or slope creep.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned 
with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series.

The project site is situated on the southerly facing slope of an intra-valley ridge on 
the southwestern facing flank of Koolau.  The geologic conditions underlying the site are 
the result of in-situ chemical weathering of basaltic lava flows.  The shallow soils are 
generally residual soils consisting of reddish to brown silty clays which grade with depth 
to silty sand saprolitic soils which, in turn, grade with depth to highly weathered basaltic 
rock. 
FIELD EXPLORATION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3. The borings extended to about 
20 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous-flight solid stem augers and PQ casing. The boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1 of Appendix A. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations, such as 
groundwater levels, are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3 of 
Appendix A. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of 
Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer 
falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 
6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples (if 
applicable) in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, if applicable. 
Core samples of rock and/or hard materials encountered at the project site were 
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary drilling 
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.  The rock and/or hard 
material left in the hollow core of the bit are mechanically recovered for examination and 
description. Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description 
System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2 of Appendix A.

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of soil/rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) also is a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of 
the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the 
coral/rock, its relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics.  A dense rock 
formation with a high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require 
more arduous methods of excavation. 
LABORATORY TESTS 
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and 
classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected samples were tested to evaluate 
the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered. The 
laboratory testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture, 
density, plasticity, and gradation of the soils encountered. 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to 
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating 
the layers between the borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D2216. The unit weight tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D2937. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength characteristics of the basalt 
formation encountered at the project site, Unconfined Compression tests were performed 
on the selected rock core samples in accordance with ASTM D7012. Results of the

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings extending to a depth of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Borings were drilled on the roadway behind the existing rock retaining walls. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the project site is underlain by a thin layer of surface fill over highly 
weathered soft basalt rock about 5 feet thick and moderately weathered hard basalt rock 
formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 20 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate with 
variation in rainfall, surface water runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3 of Appendix A. 
SEISMIC DESIGN CONDIERATIONS 
We understand that the project site may be subjected to seismic activity, and 
seismic design considerations will need to be addressed in accordance with Section 1613 
of the International Building Code (2006). Our field exploration generally encountered 
very stiff and/or dense extremely to highly weathered rock overlying moderately 
weathered hard basalt formation extending to the maximum depth of about 20 feet below 
the existing ground surface. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and geology 
setting of the site, the project site may be classified as a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock”, 
Site Class “C”. 
SOIL NAIL RETAINING WALLS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and the condition 
of the existing CRM walls observed, we recommend constructing a soil nail retaining wall, 
to strengthen the existing CRM walls. We believe that the construction of the soil nail wall 
provides an economical remedial solution and minimizes potential risk of excessive 
ground disturbance from hard rock excavation, which may cause damage to adjacent 
private property next to the Puna Gate. 
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope.  A shotcrete facing is

and shear strengths to the soil.  The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a 
gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing.  In general, the face of the soil nail retaining wall 
system is slightly battered at a slope inclination of about 1H:12V. 
Design of the soil nail wall system will need to consider both the internal and 
external stability of the reinforced soil mass. The design of the internal stability includes 
establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted reinforcing bars. The 
external stability includes overall slope stability of the earth retaining system. The 
geotechnical design parameters needed for evaluating the internal and external stability of 
the soil nail retaining wall system are provided in the following table.

The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/soil 
interface contact, is variable depending on the type of soil and grout, the overburden 
stress, and the construction procedures used in installing these grouted reinforcing bars. 
For a rough estimate, an average bond stress of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for the weathered basalt rock formation. The bond stress used in the design will 
need to be confirmed in the field during construction. 
We recommend installing soil nail anchors about 15 feet long to achieve sufficient 
internal and external stability for the static loading and seismic loading conditions. The soil 
nails should be installed in both horizontal and vertical spacing of 5 feet and at 15 degree 
angle from horizontal. We also recommend corrosion protection for the soil nails, such as 
a fully encapsulated double corrosion protection system. To achieve a pull-out design 
capacity of 32 kips in the weathered rock, we recommend installing soil nails in 8-inch 
diameter drilled hole with #10 reinforcing bar (11/4-inch diameter). The soil nail pull-out 
capacity should be verified during construction by proof testing a minimum of 
two production nails to 150 percent of the design capacity.

As part of the special inspection in accordance with IBC 2006 Section 1704 – 
Level 1 Special Inspection, we recommend that Geolabs be retained as design 
geotechnical engineer of record for observation of the drilling, grouting and proof testing 
during soil nail retaining wall construction. The proof testing nails should be selected by 
Geolabs after installation based on the drilling and grouting records. 
As mentioned previously, we suspected that the vertical crack may be caused by 
excessive hydrostatic pressures that might by induced by groundwater or water trapped 
behind the walls. Therefore, new weepholes should be installed near the bottom of the 
CRM walls. The weephole may consist of 4-inch PVC pipes installed through the existing 
CRM wall and new shotcrete facing. The end of PVC pipe should be wrapped with 
nonwoven geotextile fabric for filtration, against the soil/rock behind the CRM wall. The 
weepholes should be installed at a spacing of 5 feet center-to-center. The ground in front 
of the weephole should be protected by GRP, to minimize potential erosion caused by 
runoff water. 
In addition, the upper ground surface should sloped be to divert water away from 
the CRM walls and to reduce water infiltration behind the walls.
