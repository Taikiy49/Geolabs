This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Royal Kunia II – Phase 1 project in the Kunia area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project site is generally located east of Kunia Road and north of the 
existing Kunia Village subdivision. The project location and general vicinity are shown on 
the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration and laboratory testing for the project. These findings and 
geotechnical recommendations are intended to evaluate the suitability of the stockpiled 
soils for use as general fill material only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

Lot 3 of the Royal Kunia Phase II, Increment I subdivision in the near future. The

observations and aerial views from Google Earth Pro, we estimate Stockpiles 1 and 2 
are approximately 8.5 and 10 acres in land area, respectively, with heights exceeding 
10 feet above the natural grade.

for the future development to meet the drainage requirements for Low Impact

STOCKPILE EXPLORATION 
Our field exploration consisted of excavating and sampling 7 test pits in support 
of the Royal Kunia II – Phase 1 project. The test pits were excavated by Royal 
Contracting Company Ltd. using a Komatsu PC200 excavator with 24-inch bucket. In 
addition to the test pits excavated, bulk samples of the stockpiled materials were 
collected for laboratory testing. The approximate test pit and bulk sample locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Five of the test pits, designated as TP-1 through TP-5, were excavated to depths 
of about 9.5 to 11 feet below the existing stockpile surface to evaluate the stockpiled 
materials and collect bulk samples. The stockpiled materials generally consisted of silty 
clay with coralline cobbles extending to depths ranging between about 7.5 and 10.5 feet 
below the existing stockpile surface. Underlying the stockpiles, our test pits encountered 
natural ground conditions consisting of clayey residual soils extending to the maximum 
depth explored of 11 feet below the existing stockpile surface. It should be noted that 
the surfaces of the two stockpiles were heavily vegetated at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Two of the test pits, designated as LID-1 and LID-2, were excavated in the 
natural ground to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface to perform 
modified infiltration testing in support of the planning of a stormwater runoff disposal 
system. Based on the results of the two LID test pits, the project site is generally 
underlain by residual soil consisting of silty clay extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to 
rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology and graphic 
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs 
of Borings in Appendix A. We performed laboratory tests on selected soil samples 
obtained during our field exploration, and the test results are presented in Appendix B.  
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the laboratory testing, the California Bearing Ratio (CBR) values of 
Stockpiles 1 and 2 generally range between about 3.9 and 5.6 with swell values ranging 
between about 1.2 and 2.6 percent. In addition, cobbles were observed in the stockpiled

material. Based on the above, we believe the onsite stockpiled materials may be 
feasible used as general fills to be capped under at least 2 feet of non-expansive select 
granular fill below the finish grade, provided a maximum particle size of 6 inches and 
free of deleterious material, for the future development. 
The onsite stockpiled materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 
inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM 
D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to 
the maximum dry density. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
INFILTRATION TESTING 
We envision the project may require on-site storm water runoff management, 
such as detention basins or bioswales, in order to meet the drainage requirements for 
Low Impact Development (LID). In order to obtain subsurface infiltration information in 
support of the planning of such systems, we conducted falling head infiltration tests at 
Test Pits LID-1 and LID-2 to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface 
materials encountered at the southwest and northeast ends of the stockpiles, 
respectively. This test was performed in general accordance with the procedures in 
Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009) with the modifications to test in the test 
pits. The procedures provided in the “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by excavating approximately 2 feet 
wide by 7 feet long test pits to a selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, the bottom of the trench was leveled for 
even infiltration. The falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the 
average infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted of 
four trials of filling the test pit with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a 
one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is then calculated based on the result of the 
fourth and last trial. The calculated infiltration rates at each test location are summarized 
in the table below. Detailed infiltration testing results are presented in Appendix C.

CORROSION POTENTIAL 
Chemical analyses, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride contents, and 
sulfate contents, were performed on selected soil samples obtained during our field 
exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. The 
test results are summarized on Plate B-10 of Appendix B. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory testing, the subsurface soils at the 
project site exhibit minimum resistivity values of as low as about 700 ohm-cm, indicating 
that the subsurface soils are extremely corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 1) to buried 
metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly designing metallic substructures 
(such as piping) for protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the 
laboratory tested values of pH and sulfate content of the in-situ soils, the near-surface 
soils at the project site may be considered “non-corrosive.”

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the test pits excavated and bulk samples collected. Variations of 
subsoil conditions beyond the field data point may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until excavation and hauling of the stockpile 
material and/or construction of the development are underway. If variations then appear 
evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The test pit and bulk sample locations indicated in this report are approximate, 
having been staked out in the field using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) 
device. Elevations noted on the test pits and approximate extents of the two stockpiles 
shown herein were estimated based on Google Earth Pro, Image dated June 10, 2019. 
The physical location and elevation of the test pits should be considered accurate only to 
the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the test pits depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in 
seasonal rainfall, and other factors. These data have been reviewed and interpretations 
made in the formulation of this report.

Laboratory Tests 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate 
the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed 
on bulk samples obtained from the existing stockpiles to evaluate the suitability of the 
stockpiled materials for fill material.  The test results are presented on Plates B-5 
through B-7. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method A) were 
performed on selected bulk samples obtained from the existing stockpiles to evaluate 
the dry density and moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-8 and B-9. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests, 
were performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected 
bulk samples obtained from the existing stockpiles obtained from our field exploration. 
The test results are summarized on Plate B-10. It should be noted that the chloride and 
sulfate content results were still pending at the time this report was prepared.

Field Infiltration Tests 
Two field infiltration tests were conducted within two test pit locations, designated 
as Test Pits LID-1 and LID-2, at the site to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the 
subsurface materials encountered at the southwest and northeast ends of the 
stockpiles, respectively. The approximate field infiltration test locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. These tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009) with the exception of the 
modifications for testing within excavated test pits and single-day testing. The 
procedures provided in the “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by excavating approximately 2 feet 
wide by 7 feet long test pits to a selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, the bottom of the trench was leveled for 
even infiltration. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the 
infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials 
of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings until the water 
drained out or up to one hour. The infiltration rate is then calculated based on the result 
of the fourth and last trial. The test results are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
