SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration encountered alluvial deposits that consisted of soft to very stiff 
clay, and extended to the maximum depth drilled of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in our borings at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend 
utilizing shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings to 
support the new structures. For design of the shallow foundations, an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for foundations bearing on the re-compacted 
in-situ soils or new compacted fills. The bearing pressure provided above is for supporting 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by up to one-third (1/3) when considering 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, footings 
should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. Detailed 
discussions and recommendations for design of building foundations, slabs-on-grade, site 
grading, and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following 
sections. 
Based on our laboratory test results, the soils encountered at the site have a high 
expansion potential when subjected to moisture-fluctuations. To reduce the potential for 
future distress to the lightly loaded concrete slabs resulting from shrinking and swelling of 
the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content, we recommend supporting 
concrete slabs on at least 36 inches of non-expansive, select granular fill material. The text 
of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the OTW Cable Hub Facility project in the Waimanalo District on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses. 
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations, slabs-on-grade, 
pavement and earthwork. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the construction of an OTW Cable Hub facility at 
41-515 Poalima Street in Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The building will 
have a footprint of approximately 1,500 to 2,000 square feet and is anticipated to be a 
single-story CMU structure with wood truss roof framing. The building is to house OTW 
equipment with 24/7 AC and power availability. Other site improvements will include 
driveway and curbs, and water and sewer connectors. Structure details were not 
available at the time this report was prepared. 
Based on the information provided and our field exploration, we anticipate that 
site grading, footing excavation, utility installations and site drainage will be required for 
the construction. The project layout is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the 
two extinct Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The Waianae and Koolau Volcanic 
Shields were built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs by thinly 
bedded basaltic lava flows. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island. The main shield-building activities ceased approximately 2.5 to 
3.5 million years ago. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still 
active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded and melted, global sea 
levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher 
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal 
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral-algal limestone and marls within 
the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units. 
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused 
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.  In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of

Existing Site Conditions

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin surface fill layer of dense 
sandy gravel over alluvium, consisting of soft to very stiff clay/silty clay.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels may fluctuate 
depending on seasonal precipitation, and other factors. Detailed descriptions of the 
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1

tests, four unconfined compression tests and one consolidation test were performed on 
selected soil samples obtained, and the test results are presented on Plates B-1 
through B-11.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered fill materials overlying silty alluvial 
soil. Based on the results of our exploration, we believe the proposed new facility 
structures may be supported on shallow foundations. The shallow foundations should 
be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade and bear on 
properly compacted fill or subgrade soils. 
Our laboratory tests of the soil samples retrieved from our field exploration 
indicated a high swell potential. To reduce the potential for structural distress to the 
future slabs-on-grade caused by the near-surface soil expansion, we recommend 
properly preparing and cappping slabs-on-grade with at least 36 inches of 
non-expansive structural fill materials.  
3.1 
Foundations 
We understand that the OTW Cable Hub Facility project will consist of 
constructing a single-story CMU structure and other site related improvements. Details for 
the proposed structures were not available at the time this geotechnical engineering 
exploration report was prepared. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at 
the project site, we recommend utilizing shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or 
continuous strip footings to support the new structure at the site. For design of the 
shallow foundations, an allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per square foot 
(psf) may be used for foundations bearing on the re-compacted in-situ soils or new 
compacted fills. The bearing pressure provided above is for supporting dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one third (1/3) for supporting transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Spread footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches, and the bottom of 
footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade. For footings bearing on the re-compacted in-situ soils or new compacted 
fills, the bottom of the footing excavations should be re-compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete.

Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations 
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm and/or dense materials. The 
over-excavation may be backfilled with on-site granular fills compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction, or the footing bottom may be extended down to the 
underlying competent materials. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing materials, and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.30 may be used for footings bearing on the re-compacted in-situ soils or 
new compacted fills. Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for 
footings embedded in the well-compacted structural fills or in-situ soils.  This value 
assumes that the concrete for footings is placed directly against the well-compacted 
structural fill materials or in-situ soils.  Unless covered by pavement or slab, the passive 
resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected. 
3.2 
Slabs-On Grade 
As mentioned earlier, the on-site soils have high swelling potential. To reduce the 
potential for structural distress resulting from the shrinking and swelling of the subgrade 
soils due to changes in the moisture content, we recommend properly preparing and 
capping the subgrade with at least 36 inches of non-expansive structural fill material. 
This capping fill should also extend beyond the new slabs-on-grade footprint a minimum 
of 24 inches.  
The non-expansive structural fill should be placed as soon as possible after the 
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the 
“Site Preparation” section herein. The prepared capping fill and the underlying subgrade 
soils should be kept moist until final placement of concrete.  
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at 
least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Areas adjacent to the slab 
should be backfilled with soil compacted tightly against the slab face. Those areas

3.3.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, debris, and other unsuitable materials should be 
removed and disposed properly off-site to reduce the potential for contamination of 
the excavated materials designated to be re-used as fill and/or backfill. If soft or wet 
soils are encountered during clearing, over-excavation may be required to remove 
the soft or wet materials to expose firm soils. The resulting over-excavation should 
be backfilled with compacted granular fill material. 
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation 
should be properly backfilled with granular fill material placed in 8-inch loose lifts 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Due to the high variablitiy of recently placed fills beneath the proposed project site, 
finish subgrades in cut areas and areas to receive fills should be scarified to a 
minimum depth of 6 inches and proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) 
vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. A Geolabs representative 
should observe the site preparation and proof-rolling operations. Yielding areas, 
loose areas, or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof-rolling operations 
should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials. 
After demolition, site clearing, and proof-rolling the exposed subgrade should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a

3.3.2 Fills and Backfills 
General fill materials used as fill or backfill may consist of the excavated on-site 
soils, provided that they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock 
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. 
Select granular fill and imported fill should consist of non-expansive granular 
material, such as crushed coral, mudrock, basalt or cinder sand.  The material 
should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in 
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing 
the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more 
and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D 1883.  Geolabs should test imported fill materials for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its 
intended use. 
3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.3.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it appears that cut 
slopes might expose previously placed fills. In general, we believe that cut slopes in 
fill material may be cut at an inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or 
flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations.

3.4 
Pavement Design 
Based on the general plan provided, we anticipate that the vehicle loading will 
consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light trucks. Our preliminary pavement 
design assumes that the pavement subgrade soil will consist of medium stiff to stiff silty 
clay as encountered in the borings during our field exploration. Based on the above 
assumptions, we recommend using the following flexible pavement section for 
preliminary design purposes: 
Flexible Pavements (parking lot area) 
  2.5-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
14.5-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The pavement materials should be constructed in general accordance with the 
sections of Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, 2005 (HSS) and 
subsequent amendments. CBR and field density tests should be performed on the 
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to evaluate the adequacy of the 
above pavement section. 
Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off the site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site 
during or after construction. Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or 
adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 
2 inches into the soils below the aggregate subbase layer to reduce the potential for 
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section. Alternatively, a 
subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from landscaping 
irrigation. For long-term performance, we recommend constructing a subdrain system 
adjacent to the paved/landscaped areas.

Utility Trenches 
It is anticipated that utilities will be constructed and/or relocated for the proposed 
project. The contractor should determine the methods and equipment to be used for 
excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The excavation should 
comply with all applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements. The contractor 
should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation. Trench shoring and

In general, for support of the utility lines, we recommend using granular bedding 
consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the 
pipes. The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of 
free-draining backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging 
the pipes from compaction of the backfill.  It is critical to use a free-draining granular 
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and 
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site soils as 
backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to 
the finish subgrade should consist of the approved on-site materials or select granular 
material. The backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface at the 
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with auger equipment. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous 
basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by laboratory testing. Soils 
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as 
shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are provided on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM  D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer tests results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D 2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under different surcharge 
pressures. The test results are summarized on Plate B-1. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth and are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Four Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 2166) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soil formation 
encountered. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-6. 
Two California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on samples of 
the near-surface soils and potential borrow materials to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. CBR test results were also used to evaluate the quality of the 
materials tested for use as fills. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Two Modified Proctor Compaction tests (ASTM D 1557 A) were performed on 
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-9 and B-10. 
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D 2435) was performed on a sample 
of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of 
materials encountered. Result of the consolidation test is presented on Plate B-11.
