Site Description
The project site is located in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
existing antenna facility is located within the Wahiawa Police Station near the intersection
of Koa Street and Club Street The tower is secured by gated chain link fences to the west
north and east A CMU equipment building is located south of the tower The tower can be
accessed from the paved parking area on the west then through a well manicured grassy
area At the time of our field exploration a brown colored container was also observed on
the west side of the antenna tower The project location and general vicinity are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
The existing 66 foot antenna tower is supported by three legs We understand a
new 140 foot tall antenna also supported by three legs is planned to replace the existing
antenna Based on the plan provided the new tower will be installed approximately 15 feet
east of the existing antenna tower Based on the available topographic survey the site is
relatively level with ground surface elevations ranging between about 941 to 942 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL We anticipate the site grading work will consist of minor cuts and
fills of about 1 foot or less
Subsurface Condition
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling one boring
designated as Boring No 1 extending to approximately 51 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operation on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate 3 Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Log of Boring Plate 4
Based on the boring drilled the subsurface conditions at the site generally
consist of a thin fill layer about 1 to 2 feet thick underlain by residual soils The residual
soils generally consist of very stiff clayey silt The residual soils grade to saprolite soils
at approximately 15 feet below the existing ground surface The saprolite soils are
generally very moist and grades from very stiff to medium stiff The saprolite soils
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 51 5 feet below the existing ground
surface
We did not encounter groundwater in the boring at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to rainfall time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other
factors

Based on the International Building Code 2006 Edition the project site may be
subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed
The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity soil profile type for seismic
design and the potential for liquefaction at the project site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic activity
therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic
eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground movement of magma
that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences
thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that they can only be detected
by sensitive instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt
and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of
Hawaii Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent are related to
volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with
increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced
numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not
confined only to the Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in
the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and
Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small landslides occurred on the
Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of Some houses on the
Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871
M7
In the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu An earthquake of
Magnitude 4 8 to 5 0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island of
Oahu The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows ruptured building walls and
broken underground water mains

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the International Building Code 2006 Edition the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will
need to be evaluated
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up
of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under
sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a
viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both
horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant
deformations

Antenna Tower Foundation
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf for design of a mat
foundation bearing on the properly compacted fill or stiff on site soils This bearing value is
as those caused by wind or seismic forces
A modulus of subgrade reaction of 100 pounds per square inch per inch of
deflection pci may be used for the mat foundation bearing on the stiff clayey silt The
subgrade modulus is the modulus of vertical subgrade reaction for a 1 foot square test
plate
The bottom of the mat foundation should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finish grade Deeper embedment may be required to provide
resistance to uplift and moment reactions at the base of the tower
The dead weight of the structure and the mat foundation may be used to provide
the required uplift load capacity Additionally the weight of the backfill material placed
above the foundations may be used to provide resistance to uplift loads if applicable A
unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot pcf may be used for imported select granular fill
material
The bottom of foundation excavation should be firm and unyielding prior to
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Soft and or loose materials encountered at the
bottom of the foundation excavation should be over excavated to expose the underlying
dense materials Concrete should be used to backfill the resulting over excavation
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than
1 inch Differential settlements between the center and the corner of the mat may be on
the order of about 0 5 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to passive
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 200 pcf This
assumes that the soil around the footings is relatively undisturbed or well compacted
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected A coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used for footings bearing
on properly compacted fill or on site stiff clayey silt material

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography we envision that the site grading work will
generally consist of minor cuts and fills At the on set of earthwork the area within the
contract grading limits should be cleared thoroughly Debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements
should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices
for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
The materials generated from cut areas may be reused as a source of general fill
material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock fragments
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension If required import fill should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed basalt or coral The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent Geolabs should test imported fill material for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in areas
subjected to vehicular traffic Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment Compaction by water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed for the on site soils
