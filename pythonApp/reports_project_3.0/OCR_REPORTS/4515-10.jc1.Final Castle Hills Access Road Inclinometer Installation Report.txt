PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
A gabion retaining wall will be constructed along one side of the downstream end of
the existing drainage outlet Due to past ground settlement and slope movement it is
desired to establish an instrumentation monitoring program to track potential ground
movements prior to during and after construction in order to differentiate between the
baseline movement and that induced during construction The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The instrumentation consisting of inclinometers and ground settlement plates
were installed around the project site adjacent to the existing private road private
properties City roadways and State streets Geolabs installed five inclinometers for
monitoring lateral ground movement Installation for the twenty nine ground settlement
points were conducted and monitored by others
The inclinometer monitoring is divided into two phases consisting of
inclinometer monitoring will be used as a baseline of documentation for potential ground
movement This report presents the inclinometer monitoring results conducted prior to
construction Stage 1

FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling five borings designated as
Boring Nos I 1 through I 5 extending to depths of approximately 87 to 100 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were used for the purpose of installing slope
inclinometers The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered and other observations such as
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Standard Practice for Thick Wall Ring Lined
Spilt Barrel Drive Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD modified California sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from
the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Standard Test Method for
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft soil formation were obtained by pushing a
3 inch OD thin walled stainless steel Shelby tube into the soil formation in general
accordance with ASTM D 1587 Standard Practice for Thin Walled Tube Sampling of Soils
for Geotechnical Purposes
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer tests provide an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques consisting of a PQ wireline coring system with core
barrel core size of 3 35 inches in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core
Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit
to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were described in
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log
Legend Plate A 0 2 The Rock Description System is based on the publication
Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses by
International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977

Recovery REC may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses where appropriate Recovery is defined as the actual
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt
For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
mechanical and handling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the
relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of

LABORATORY TESTS
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and classified
in our Honolulu laboratory Selected soil samples were tested to evaluate the physical
characteristics and engineering properties The laboratory testing program was formulated
with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture density plasticity gradation and shear
strength of the soils encountered
The laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural
moisture content Atterberg Limits sieve analysis direct shear and triaxial compression
Results of the tests performed are presented on Plates B 1 through B 12 Detailed
descriptions of the various tests performed for this exploration are presented below
Moisture and Density
Moisture Content and Dry Density tests were performed on selected soil samples to
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating
the layers between test borings The moisture content tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM D 2216 The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
In general our field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by
medium stiff to stiff surface fill material consisting of clayey soil extending to depths of 5 to
14 feet below the ground surface The surface fill material was underlain by a layer of very
soft marsh deposit with varied thickness of about 7 to 12 feet and alluvium deposit with
highly variable consistency from soft loose to stiff dense Highly to moderately weathered
volcanic tuff rock was encountered at depths of about 70 to 77 feet and extended to the
maximum depth drilled of approximately 100 feet below the ground surface We
encountered groundwater at depths varying from about 3 to 12 feet below the ground
surface at the time of our field exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels are
expected to vary with seasonal rainfall storm surge conditions time of the year and other
factors

INCLINOMETER INSTALLATION AND MONITORING
Geotechnical instrumentation consisting of slope inclinometer casing was installed
in the drilled borings to allow the continued monitoring on a real time basis The slope
inclinometers manufactured by Slope Indicator Company were used in this project The
inclinometer casings made of ABS semi rigid plastic with an outside diameter of
2 75 inches were installed in five borings to depths of 87 to 100 feet The purpose of the
inclinometer casing is to allow the lowering of the slope inclinometer device into the hole to
measure the lateral deflection of the soil at various depths in order to determine where the
earth movements are taking place as well as how much movement has occurred
In general the inclinometers recorded lateral earth movements perpendicular
A Axis and parallel B Axis to the adjacent streets Kupohu Street or Pilina Place as
shown on Plates 3 through 7 The inclinometers were read at approximately one month
interval to measure the rate of ground movement The cumulative displacements
measured by the inclinometers are presented on Plates 3 through 7
Based on our review of the inclinometer data recorded within the 14 month
monitoring period it appeared that Inclinometer Nos 1 through 5 currently have not
indicated significant subsurface ground lateral movement within the 14 month
monitoring period
It should be noted that Inclinometer No 4 measured some localized relative
movements up to 0 5 inches at depths between 15 and 25 feet as shown on Plate 6
This inclinometer was installed in front of Lot 17 near the end of Pilina Place Based on
our field observation and data review we believe that this movement is associated with
a problem of the inclinometer casing joint A small gap was identified at the depth of
20 feet while inclinometer reading The casing joint at the depth of 20 feet may not be
flush causing slight disengagement between the upper and lower casings It is our
opinion that this movement is not an indication of ground movement
