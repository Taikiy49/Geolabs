Additional Soils and Foundation Investigation
Proposed H W Foods Cold Storage Facilities
Honolulu Oahu Hawaii
In accordance with your request we have performed additional field
investigation and analyses to supplement our preliminary soils and
foundation investigation for the above referenced project The
approximate location of the project site is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1 Our work was performed in general accordance with our
proposal of
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
Project Consideration
It is proposed to construct a cold storage facility with
approximately 25 000 square feet of freezer and storage area on the
W O 1478 20 Page 2
vacant lot The facility will include freezers a chiller a dry storage
area a loading zone and an equipment room An employee parking zone is
also planned
The building construction is anticipated to consist of pre cast
tilt up walls or CMU walls with metal siding and roof
Fill of about 3 to 4 feet is planned to raise most of the site to
accommodate a loading dock at the entrance of the building Estimated
design wall load of 2 to 3 kips per foot are anticipated Also live
load due to food storage of up to 300 p s f is expected
Purpose and Scope
A preliminary investigation was previously conducted at the site
to evaluate the suitability of the lot for the proposed cold storage
construction The results of our preliminary investigation were
presented in our report of
The purpose of this investigation was to perform additional field
exploration to supplement the previous limited boring data in order to
develop soil data for the design of the site grading foundation and
pavement sections for the proposed structure

The scope of investigation included drilling and sampling three 3

Detailed description of our field exploration and laboratory test
results are presented in the appendices of this report
Site Description
The site is located on the western makai side of Nimitz Highway
in the Iwilei district of Honolulu
The area is generally flat and presently paved with asphaltic
concrete The surrounding properties are occupied by warehouse type
structures and storage yards

Discussion and Recommendations
General
The project site is underlain by a layer of compressible lagoonal
harbor deposit approximately 20 feet thick This layer varies from
loose coral gravel with a clay matrix in the mauka or western portion of
the site to a soft clay in the makai or eastern portion
Due to the soft ground conditions settlements are anticipated due
to the weight of the new fill and building loads
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 5
To induce ground settlements prior to foundation construction we
recommend that the required new fill and additional surcharge fill be
placed as scon as possible
If sufficient surcharge fill can be placed over the site it is our
opinion that a large percentage of the anticipated future ground settle
ments primary settlements due to the 3 5 feet of fill and the 300 psf
floor load could be eliminated However some amount of secondary
settlements may still occur over the life of the structure
Ideally the building construction should be delayed until most of
the settlement due to site filling have cccurred Then spread footing
foundations may be used for the building support with allowance for some
future settlements due to the structural and live loads
Alternatively a pile foundation in conjunction with a structural
flcor slab in order to eliminate the fill may be used
Our recommendations are discussed in detail and presented in the
following sections
Settlements
Based on the findings we believe that ground settlements will
occur due to 1 the weight of the new fill 2 the building load and 3
additional settlements that may develop if a heavy floor leading is
applied
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 6
Due to the variation in soil characteristics in the mauka makai
direction we anticipate that the settlement experienced will also vary
Generally less settlement and shorter waiting time are anticipated for
the mauka areas which are underlain by gravelly soils
The anticipated settlement due to 41 inches of new fill is
estimated to be on the order of 3 to 6 inches Based on the laboratory
test results we estimate that the time required for 90 of the
settlements to occur would be about 2 to 6 months In order to
accelerate the settlements and reduce the waiting time a temporary
surcharge fill may be utilized over the permanent fill as described in
the subsequent section
For the anticipated wall loads of about 3 kips per linear foot
estimated additional settlements after surcharging are on the order of 1
to 1 5 inches
Substantial live loads due to food storage may occur Calculated
settlements under a floor loading of 300 p s f is on the order of about
2 to 4 inches Surcharge fill may be used to minimize the live load
settlements
Surcharge Fill
We believe a surcharge program should be considered to minimize
ground settlements due to the new fill and floor loads
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1478 20 Page 7
The length of the surcharge period will depend on the amount of
surcharge used The total height of fill and surcharge should not exceed
10 feet to minimize the possibility of bearing or slope failure in the
soft soils If a 7 5 foot high surcharge is used our estimated
surcharge time would be on the order of 3 months To minimize the amount
of material required the surcharge fill height may be reduced to 5 feet
over the mauka portion of the site due to the faster settling time
anticipated there
The surcharge may be placed at a temporary slope of 1 1 H V
however the toe of slope should be set back a few feet away from the
property line to acconmodate some sloughing and erosion Ground
settlements caused by the surcharge fill may extend beyond the toe of
slope and into the adjacent properties The actual zone of influence of
the surcharge fill is governed by the height of fill and the thickness of
soft material below the site Based on the soil data we estimate that
significant zone of ground settlements may occur within about 25 feet of
the toe of the slope Therefore repairs to pavements and utilities may
be required if they are adversely affected by the surcharge fill
settlements
Pericdic optical survey readings of the installed settlement
markers would be used to monitor the actual settlement The readings
should be taken by the project surveyor and transmitted to the soils
engineer to determine the actual surcharge time required

It should be understood that although surcharge fill might help to
minimize ground settlement due to the new fill and floor loads footing
settlements due to building leads may still occur
Foundation Design
Shallow Spread or Continuous Footing
ns i ME Ss 8 a SN NY Ss eM ER a ee
After completion of the filling and surcharging program shallow
spread or continuous footing foundation may be used
In general spread footings should be placed at a level as high as
possible to reduce the footing load applied over the underlying
compressible deposits We believe that a maxinum net allowable
soil bearing pressure of 2000 p s f may be used for the design
Foundations should not be placed below about elevation 3 feet
otherwise special design may be required due to the close proximity
of the soft subsoils and water level
The anticipated footing settlement under 3 kips per foot of wall
leading applied after the new fill and surcharge have been placed
and allowed to settle is estimated to range between 1 to 1 5
inches Approximately 70 of these settlement will occur slowly
probably over a number of years after completion of the project
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1478 20 Page 9
We recommend that all footing bottems be embedded at least 12
inches below the lowest adjacent grade Lateral loads acting on
the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure developed
against the buried vertical faces of the foundation system and by
friction resistance developed between the bottom of the foundations
and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p c f This assumes that the soil
around the foundation is well compacted The frictional resistance
may be calculated by using a coefficient of friction of 0 4 The
above resistance values are maximum values and do not contain a
factor of safety
Pile Foundation
se am a me se
If the amount of settlement predicted are not tolerable then pile
foundations may be utilized The piles should be used to support
the building loads in conjunction with a structural slab to
eliminate the fill required and potential downdrag on the piles
The piles would develop their load capacity primarily fram
frictional resistance or adhesion between the piles and the medium
stiff to stiff alluvial soil encountered below about 25 feet depth
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1478 20 Page 10
Twelve inch square pre stressed concrete piles are reconmended
The piles should extend into the medium stiff to stiff soil
encountered at about 20 feet below the existing ground surface and
be driven to a total depth of 100 feet
The piles should be driven with a hanmer delivering a minimum of
about 24 000 foot pounds of energy and to a resistance of 3 blows
per inch for the last 6 inches
For the above recommendations an allowable pile capacity of 40
tons per pile may be used Pile settlements on the order of less
than one half of an inch are anticipated
Depending on the total number of piles required it may be
desirable to have a number of test piles installed at various pile
groups to evaluate the actual pile driving behavior and to
determine the length of the production piles to be ordered
To resist the lateral loads the passive earth pressure around the
pile caps and tie beams may be used We recommend an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
The backfill against the pile caps or tie beams should be properly
backfilled with select materials compacted to a dry density of at
least 95 of the maximum density as determined by ASTM D 1557 78
test method
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 11
The pile driving should be monitored by a geotechnical engineer to
see that they are driven into competent bearing formation
It appears that pile foundations have several significant
advantages over a spread footing and surcharge fill system If a
structural flcor system is used and the 3 5 feet of fill can be
eliminated long term secondary settlements can be virtually
eliminated Additionally the time and expense of the surcharge
fill plus repair of damages to adjacent properties due to
settlement can be avoided thus minimizing construction delays
Finally the open space below the freezer slab and the existing
ground surface can provide an effective deterrent to the formation
of ice lenses due to capillary action in the soil
It should be noted that the friction piles could lose some of their
load carrying capacity if the adjacent properties are filled
thereby causing ground settlements and downdrag on the piles
Freezer Unit
Due to the proximity of the water level provisions for a capillary
water break are an important consideration to minimize the possibility of
frost heave under the freezer units
Frost action occurs when there is a combination of freezing
temperature high water level and subgrade soils which have high
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1478 20 Page 12
capillary rise such as very fine sands and silts When the surface of
the soil is below freezing temperature upward moisture migration from
the high water level occurs due to capillary action and condensation
This water collects and freezes to form lenses of ice in the upper part
of the ground which displaces the soil and causes heaving The formation
of the ice lenses can continue indefinitely as long as suitable
conditions exist
The existing upper fill soils consist of silty sands which is
highly susceptible to frost action In order to reduce the potential for
frost action under the freezer a capillary break layer consisting of a
minimum of 2 feet of clean gravel such as No 3 Fine gravel over 12
inches of impervious soil is recommended under the freezer floor slab in
order to minimize capillary rise

Utility Lines
The underground utility lines should be placed as late as possible
after the completion of site grading to minimize post installation
settlements In addition flexible joints should be considered

W O 1478 20 Page 15
particularly the connections at building should have a higher degree of
flexibility to allow for differential settlements
Trench excavations would require proper shoring particularly if
patches of soft organic clays or silts are encountered Since dewatering
may affect adjacent structures a soils engineer should be consulted if
the water level is to be lowered
The materials and compaction requirements for the backfilling of
the trench should be consistent with those recommended under the Fill
Placement section
Generally for backfilling the utility trenches the compacted
density of the backfill from the pipe bed up to 12 inches above the pipe
should be a minimum of 85 of ASTM D 1557 78 The remaining portion
should be 90 If trenches are located under roadways the top 24 inches
of the backfill from the finished grade should be compacted to 95
minimum

Construction Monitoring
The earthwork operation including excavation fill placement and
compaction and surcharge should be cbhserved by the soils engineer during
construction to see that adequate compaction has been obtained and that
the subsurface conditions are consistent with the findings of our
investigation
We also recommend that the footing excavations be inspected by the
soils engineer prior to placement of concrete or reinforcing steel to
check that adequate bearing has been obtained
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to havling to the site
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

Laboratory Testing

Moisture content and unit weight determinations were
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented
on the Log of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four consolidation tests were performed on selected
undisturbed samples to evaluate the settlement characteristics of
subsoils The test results are presented on Plates B 1 through B 4
One direct shear test and five unconsolidated undrained
triaxial tests were performed on undisturbed samples to evaluate the
shear strength parameters of the deeper soils The test results are
plotted in Plates B 4 through B 10
