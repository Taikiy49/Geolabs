PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located in the Manele District within the existing quarry facility on 
the Island of Lanai, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. Based on the 
information provided, we understand that it is desired to expand the existing quarry facility, 
generating approximately 500,000 cubic yards of quality aggregate to support the Lanai 
hotel, housing, and other infrastructure development. 
The existing Manele quarry facility was not in operation at the time of our field 
exploration. Bottom of the quarry walls were partially filled with excessive materials 
generated from the construction site, as shown in the photograph below. It is desired to 
evaluate the rock quality for generating the aggregate base course and/or aggregate 
subbase course in support of the construction demands. 
Details of the expansion plans were not available at the time this report was 
prepared. We envision the quarry operation may be extended along the north and east 
quarry walls into the native lava rock formation. Based on the topographic map provided, 
the ground elevations at the top of the northern quarry wall varied from +620 to +630 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL), while sloping down along the eastern quarry wall from +610 to 
+510 feet MSL, as shown on Site Plan, Plate 2.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 
50.25 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by surface 
soils and weathered basalt formation with interbedded clinker extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 50.25 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
surface soils generally consisted of fills and/or alluvial soils about 11 feet thick at the top 
of the northern quarry wall and 2 to 6 feet thick along the top of the eastern quarry wall. 
Below the surface fills and alluvial soils, we encountered weathered basalt formation, 
generally slightly to severely fractured and slightly to severely weathered, with 
interbedded clinker consisting of loose to medium dense basaltic gravel. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff and other 
factors.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered surface soils and weathered basalt 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored about 50.25 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The thickness of the surface soils is highly variable consisting of fills 
and/or alluvial soils up to about 11 feet thick. The weathered basalt formation encountered 
below the surface soils was slightly to severely fractured, slightly to highly weathered, and 
soft to hard. It should be noted that interbedded clinkers consisting of loose to medium 
dense basaltic gravel were encountered within the weathered basalt formation.

In general, the LA Abrasion and Soundness measure the resistance for 
degradation and weathering action, where the Unconfined Compression Strength and 
Specific Gravity provide the aggregate hardness and relative density. Typical values of 
LA Abrasion and Soundness for the aggregate base course material meeting the local 
construction standard are no more than 50% and 10%. 
Based on the results of the laboratory testing, the typical compressive strength of 
the core samples ranges between 1,000 and 7,000 psi, which is defined as very weak to 
weak rock. This rating is consistent with the specific gravity value ranging between 1.8 
and 2.1, which is much lower than the typical aggregate value of 2.4 to 3.0. 
The mechanical degradation resistance as measured by LA Abrasion ranges 
between 35 and 47 percent, which meets the industrial standard of no more than 
50 percent. However, the chemical wearing resistance as measured by Soundness is 
between 13 and 100 percent, significantly exceeding the industrial standard of no more 
than 10 percent. 
Based on the weak rock hardness rating and high soundness value, the basaltic 
aggregate generated from the Manele Quarry site will unlikely meet the aggregate base 
course requirements. Consideration may be given to producing aggregate subbase 
course material from the expanded quarry site. We believe the aggregate subbase 
course material may be utilized to support future pavement construction without needing 
the aggregate base course material.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 31.5 to 50.25 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-5.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM  D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.

recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to 
describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Thirteen Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-1. 
Five L.A. Abrasion tests (ASTM C131) were performed by placing about 
5,000 grams of prepared samples into the Los Angeles Abrasion Machine for 
500 revolutions impact testing. The percent weight loss then was measured by passing the 
test samples through 1.70-mm (No. 12 sieve). The test results are presented on Plates 
B-2 through B-6. 
Five Soundness tests (ASTM C88) were performed by immersing prepared 
samples in the sodium sulfate solution for 16 to 18 hours. The stone samples then were 
dried in an oven at about 230°F after removal from the sodium sulfate solution. A minimum 
of two cycles of immersing and oven drying were conducted on each sample. The percent 
weight loss was measured by weighing the samples after completely washing out the 
sodium sulfate solution in warm water. The stones were also observed for distress, such 
as splitting, crumbling, cracking, flaking, etc. The test results are presented on Plate B-7. 
Ten specific gravity tests (ASTM C127) were performed on core runs to evaluate 
the average density of a quantity of coarse aggregate, relative density, and the absorption 
of the coarse aggregate. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.
