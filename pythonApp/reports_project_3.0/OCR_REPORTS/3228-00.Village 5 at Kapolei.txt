SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3228 00 Page 5
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to
the south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet
at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation
or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of sediments
has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work
have brought the Villages of Kapolei area to its present form

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
On the basis of our field exploration the project site is underlain by a surface layer
of very stiff to hard silty clay This silty clay layer is capable of supporting the proposed
residential structures on a shallow foundation system However the silty clay exhibits
moderate to high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations of moisture
content As a result we recommend that all slabs on grade should bear on a layer of
non expansive select capping fill material In addition special attention should be given
to the recommended procedures of subgrade preparation of slabs and pavements to
reduce the potential shrink swell effects of the near surface soils
With a layer of non expansive select capping fill material Slabs on grade with
thickened edge footings may be used to support the residential buildings
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of site
grading residential housing foundations and pavements are further discussed in the
following sections
Site Grading
For the purpose of definition building areas as referred to in the remainder of
this report are areas within the proposed building limits including exterior slabs as well
as the zones extending 5 feet laterally beyond the edges of the slab

Prior to commencement of earthwork the site should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed All existing surface vegetation old pavements construction
wastes and deleterious materials within the grading limit should be removed
and disposed of properly off site
Any zones of wet saturated or yielding areas encountered during the
clearing process should be over excavated to expose firm ground and the
resulting excavations should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill The need for over excavation should be evaluated by a representative
of Geolabs Hawaii in the field We recommend that contract documents
should include provisions for additive and deductive unit prices to account
for over excavation and engineered fill placement due to unanticipated over
excavation quantities
Upon completion of clearing and grubbing areas at grade or designated to
receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches moisture conditioned
to between 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91

prior to use Because site grading will consist only of minor cuts and up to

this project

Slab On Grade Foundations
We understand that the structures proposed for the residential development will be
supported by slab on grade with thickened edge footings Our exploration indicates that
the site is generally underlain by moderately to highly expansive clayey soils Unless slab
on grade foundations supported by these expansive soils are properly designed there
GECLABS HAWAII
W O 3228 00 Page 10
is potential for future distress to residential structures resulting from wetting and
subsequent swelling of these expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on
site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select capping fill material be
used for a minimum depth of 12 inches below the slab subgrade The thickened edges
of the slabs should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
exterior grade The base of the thickened edge should have a minimum width of 12
inches A graphic illustration of this foundation type is presented on Plate 3 Schematic
Section Slab on Grade on 1 foot of Select Material
Select Capping Fill Material
The select capping fill material should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral basalt crusher waste or cinder Select capping fill material
should be well graded with no particles greater than 3 inches in size and should
have a laboratory CBR swell value not exceeding percent when prepared at near
optimum moisture Select capping fill material should not contain boulders organic
or other deleterious material The select capping fill should be moisture
conditioned to near optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90
percent relative Compaction
The select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification
and compaction of the underlying subgrade soils The select capping fill material
should provide a capping layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially
expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from the clayey
subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the
subgrade soils should reduce the impact on the slab due to potential heaving of
the underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted

Slab Subgrade Preparation
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of concrete To
reduce the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce the costs of
form construction we recommend that the slab edges be cast neat against the
soils The slab edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and bottom
longitudinal steel reinforcement The top and bottom steel reinforcement bars
should be connected by stir ups The ends of the stir ups should extend into the
floor slab area and should be tied to the welded wire mesh A structural engineer
should be consulted for design details of this thickened edge which is intended to
function both as a moisture barrier and as a perimeter bearing wall footing
The welded wire mesh in the slab on grade should be embedded at the proper
height in the concrete to provide adequate reinforcement Blocks or saddles may
be used to raise the wire mesh A stiff grade of wire mesh is thus recommended
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be incorporated below the concrete slab as a cushion layer
The slab subgrade should be thoroughly wetted prior to concrete placement to
allow the select capping fill layer to remain in a moist condition To further reduce
moisture infiltration a plastic vapor barrier may be considered below the slab A
2 inch thick moist fine sand should be placed above the plastic sheet to aid in the
curing of the concrete
For minor unoccupied structures such as bus stop shelters and electrical
transformer pads a 12 inch thick layer of select capping fill layer should be used
above moist subgrade soil
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3228 00 Page 12
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce
the potential of water ponding around the foundations
An allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds per square foot p s f may be
utilized for the design of foundations This value may be increased by one third when
temporary wind and seismic loads are considered
Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since
they can better mask any minor slab cracking It is also recommended that interior walls
be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate small amount of possible
ground movements
If foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we estimate
total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of 14 inch
Retaining Wall
We anticipate that retaining walls may be required to provide grading separation
at the project site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 p s f may be used for
the design of retaining wall The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loadings such as wind or seismic forces
For footings located near the top of slopes or on slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance from the outside edge at the
base of the footing to the edge of the slope is not less than 8 feet
Assuming that granular material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation or equivalent are used for backfill a lateral earth pressures of 40 pounds per
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3228 00 Page 13
cubic foot p c f equivalent fluid pressure may be used to design walls with level backfill
in active condition
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressure A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations down at the base
of the wall discharging to a discharge facility A filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or
equivalent should be placed between the drainage material and the compacted backfill
material As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material with filter fabric The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the height of the wall should be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a rectangular
distribution with uniform pressure equal to 33 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure
acting on the entire height of the wall which is unrestrained at the top may be used in
design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads
If retaining walls are designed in accordance with our recommendations we
estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the
order of inch

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads may be resisted by a combination of passive earth pressure exerted
against near vertical foundation faces and soil friction between the base of footings slabs
and the supporting subgrade For lateral resistance design a passive earth resistance
of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 18 inches of soil should be neglected A frictional
coefficient of 0 3 may be used to evaluate sliding resistance developed between the wall
footing and the underlying soil
Soil Resistivity Test
We understand that ductile iron pipes will be used for the waterline system and that
the waterlines will be buried at a depth of about 4 to 6 feet below finished grades As
required by the Board of Water Supply City and County of Honolulu soil resistivity tests
were performed on 12 samples obtained from our borings
Except at Boring No 5 the laboratory test results generally indicate that the soils
near the proposed depth of the waterline have soil resistivity values on the order of 2 200
ohm cm At Boring No 5 a minimum soil resistivity value of 950 ohm cm was obtained
for the soil sample retrieved near the proposed waterline depth A summary of the soil
resistivity test results are presented on Plate B 4
Since fill placement on the order of 6 feet is anticipated at the project site we
recommend that additional soil resistivity survey be performed after the mass grading
phase to further evaluate the soil resistivity value

Utility Trench
It is anticipated that utilities for the development will consist primarily of water
drainage and sewer In general good construction practices should be utilized for the
installation and trench backfill of the proposed utilities
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for shallow utility line trenching at the
site At greater depth dense to very dense coral formation may be encountered and
would required heavy equipment for excavation Trench shoring design and installation
should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should
conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
Utilities should be placed on a 6 inch cushion of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation Self compacting granular backfill such as No 3B Fine gravel should
be used around the pipe and extend 12 inches above the top of the pipe It is critical that
a Self compacting material be used to reduce the potential of formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use
of on site clayey soils as backfill around utility pipes is not acceptable
Trench backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill
materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to
compact the on site clayey soils

Roadway Subdrain
It has been our experience with projects in the Ewa district and with projects having
similar soil conditions to find that when a granular select material is used as subbase
course or aS a capping layer over clayey soils there is a tendency for water to
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3228 00 Page 18
accumulate at the interface between the select borrow material and the underlying clayey
soils
It appears that the granular select borrow material is relatively permeable and that
the clayey soils are relatively impermeable Precipitation and landscape irrigation water
tend to infiltrate through the topsoil into the select borrow material and form a perched
water table on the underlying clay horizon
Premature pavement failures may occur when this water reaches the subgrade of
pavement section and when the select borrow material daylights on top of the clay
When the water seeps into road subgrades it can soften the subgrade soils and weaken
the pavement section Where the seepage daylights continuously wet areas can develop
To reduce the potential of accumulation of water along the select borrow material
and clayey soil interface we recommend that subdrains be installed to intercept potential
subgrade seepage water and divert it to appropriate drainage locations such as drainage
catch basins Generally the worst cases have occurred when the infiltrated seepage
water moved into road subgrades Therefore we recommend that subdrains be installed
along the sides of the main roadway prisms at about 2 to 3 feet behind the roadway curb
The subdrains should extend to a minimum depth of about 4 inches below the road
subgrade level The subdrains could either be of the conventional type such as a
perforated pipe embedded in free draining gravel or one of the newer synthetic types
such as MiraDrain or ConTech 100

Field Exploration
A a
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of 12
borings to depths of about 10 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory tests were performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil materials sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Designation D 2216 80 and Unit Weight
Determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain
properties such as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage
of the dry weight of the tested sample and dry unit weight are shown at the appropriate
sample depths on the Logs of Boring
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Designation D 4318 84 are used to evaluate the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Liquid Limit and Plasticity index of the
material are used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The
results of these tests are shown on the appropriate sample depths on the Logs of Boring
and are summarized on Plate B 5
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of the soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining
to the performance or suitability of a soil
CBR tests ASTM Test Designation D 1883 73 were performed on near surface
bulk samples to evaluate the soil strength for preliminary pavement design The test
results are summarized on Plate B 1
Expansion tests were performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils These
tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support and fill A total of 16
one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed or remolded samples
of the clay soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in natural and remolded
conditions respectively The test results are summarized on Plates B 2 and B 3
Corrosive Property Tests
Twelve sets of corrosion tests were performed on selected samples obtained
during our field exploration Each set of corrosion test includes the determination of pH
value and minimum resistivity The test results are summarized on Plate B 4
