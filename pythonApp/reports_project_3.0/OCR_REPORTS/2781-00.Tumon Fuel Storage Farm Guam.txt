INTRODUCTION
We have conducted a geotechnical engineering reconnaissance to aid in the
evaluation of the potential stability of the steep slopes on the westerly side of the project
site which were steepened by development of the adjacent properties at the base of the
slopes
This report summarizes our work on the project and presents our findings pertinent
to the stability of the site The work was performed in general accordance with our
revised fee proposal to GMP Associates Incorporated dated August 27 1991
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Tumon Fuel Tank Farm is situated above Tumon on the island of Guam
Mariana Islands The facility consists of seven barrel capacity underground
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2781 00 Page 2
fuel storage tanks which were constructed in two phases The first phase was circa 1950
and the second phase was around 1961
This portion of the island of Guam is composed of emerged Oligocene and
Miocene coral limestones which were tectonically uplifted The island remains seismically
active through the present Along the coastline wave action and other forces have
eroded the limestones into steep slopes ranging from about two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V to near vertical cliffs
The site is situated at the top of one of these steep slopes at an elevation of about
250 feet above Mean Sea Level overlooking Tumon Bay In the recent past off site hotel
and residential development has resulted in excavation along the toe of the cliff This off
site earthwork and the proximity of the resort and residential structures have raised
concerns regarding the potential instability of the tank farm and the slope particularly
during seismic activity
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
Our original proposal offered a geotechnical engineering exploration consisting of
the drilling and sampling of three 3 borings to depths of approximately 75 to 100 feet
below the existing ground surface for a total of 250 L F of drilling
The main objectives of the exploration would have been to
1 Identify the bearing layers of the tank and earth berm foundations and their
probable foundation behavior under seismic conditions
2 Evaluate the general stability of the soils and rock below the tanks

The project site is located near the northern end of Tumon Bay on the western side
of the northern Guam Plateau In this area the plateau steps down towards the ocean
in a series of wide benches The site is situated along the outer edge of one of these
benches
It appears that prior to development the site was nearly level Grading during
construction of the tank farm created a rolling terrain by the construction of mounded
earth berms around and over the storage tanks These berms are covered with grass
Along the western and northern boundaries off site development has steepened
the original slope which was about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V to a vertical cliff

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
Guam is the southernmost of the Mariana Island archipelago which is an island arc
parallelling the trend of the subduction zone forming the Mariana Trench
The base of the island was formed during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of
basaltic rhyolite lavas forming a volcano which is now the southern portion of the island
On the northern flank of this volcanic massif during the Oligocene and Miocene Epochs
a complex of coral and algal reefs built an elongated platform of limestones and related
deposits Tectonic activity has lifted these coral algal limestones above sea level to form
the northern Guam Plateau
Weathering and solution working of the limestones has partially recrystallized the
calcium carbonate of the limestones forming massive limestones in some areas and
poorly to well cemented detrital limestones in others Typically the detrital limestones are
highly variable in strength characteristics and are frequently karstic or cavernous in
nature
The project site is situated on this limestone plateau adjacent to a steep slope
which was created by wave erosion Resort and residential development along the base
of the cliff has steepened the toe of the slope to vertical
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2781 00 Page 6
The island of Guam is tectonically active and has experienced approximately 20
earthquakes exceeding 7 0 since 1902 Of these events 15 occurred prior to 1950 and
5 have occurred subsequent to that date It is reported that the 1902 earthquake had a
Modified Morcalli intensity of 9 i e an earthquake in which damage is considerable even
in well designed and well constructed buildings

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our reconnaissance and preliminary analyses it is our opinion that the
steep slope adjacent to the site is generally stable under static conditions However
since some instability may be induced by large seismic events Tank No 6 appears to be
the most susceptible to damage due to the proximity to the top of slope large height of
slope and the existing cavity within the slope Several alternatives are presented to
address the potential slope instability
Brief History of Site
The first three 3 tanks were constructed at the facility in about 1950 Plans for
this construction were not available for review Four 4 additional tanks 6 and
7 were constructed in the early 1960 s A review of the grading plans for this project
indicate that the grading consisted of fills up to about 15 to 20 feet to create berms
around the tank structures Comparison of the existing site conditions with the
topographic survey on the grading plan indicates that additional fills were placed between
Tank Nos 6 and 7 and along the north side of Tank No 6
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2781 00 Page 8
The topographic map indicates that the ground surface adjacent to the western
boundary next to Tank and 7 of the tank farm facility sloped down at a ratio
of about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V at the time of the construction of the
additional tanks
This adjacent sloping ground was steepened to near vertical a few years ago by
the adjacent property owner in order to create sufficient level area to construct two mid
rise hotels and a condominium building The existing slope ratio varies from about one
horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V near the top of the slope to slightly concave in some
areas near the bottom The existing slope height varies from about 40 feet along the
northern boundary of the site to about 70 feet in portions of the western area
Discussions with the adjacent property owner indicates that subsequent to the
steepening of the cut slope some sloughing of the slope material was experienced on the
northern side of the site adjacent to Tank No 6 It was reported that this sloughed
material consisted of miscellaneous fill material previously placed at the top of slope
during the installation of the additional tanks It was also reported that the surface
drainage from the tank farm used to drain over the northern boundary of the site behind
the new hotel building The surface drainage was subsequently prevented from exiting
over the slope by the construction of a berm at the top of slope It appears that the
surface drainage now is disposed by percolation through the on site coral materials
Tank Structures
The existing tank structures are about 130 feet in diameter by about 24 feet high
The bottom 10 feet of the tank is buried below the ground surface while the upper 14 feet
is covered by an earth berm at a slope of about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V
The top of the tank is covered by about 6 feet of soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2781 00 Page 9
The tank structures are constructed of steel and placed on a 15 inch thick concrete
slab foundation
According to on site personnel the tanks are empty majority of the time When
in use the tanks contain jet fuel products
Seismic Activity
Seismic activity includes earthquakes and faulting Stresses within the earth
causes subterranean rocks to strain to the breaking point When these stresses are
released by the breaking of the rock the resulting vibrations cause an earthquake
When there is displacement of the rocks along the plane of breakage a fault
results In strike slip faults the displacement is horizontal along the fault and in dip slip
faults the displacement is vertically along the fault
Unless a structure is situated directly on the fault line damage to the structure
results when the stresses induced in the structure by acceleration resulting from the
vibrations of an earthquake exceed the strength of the structure
According to the tri services technical manual Seismic Design for Buildings
February 1982 the island of Guam has been designated as Seismic Zone 3 which
indicates a relatively significant potential for strong ground motion generated by seismic
events The manual also establishes minimum seismic design criteria for any structures
constructed in such a zone for resistance to deformation and damage resulting from such
strong ground motion

Slope Stability Assessment
An assessment of the stability of the existing near vertical slope is a critical factor
in the project The stability of a slope depends on several parameters including 1 the
slope ratio 2 drainage or seepage conditions 3 the height of the slope 4 the strength
of the material comprising the slope 5 additional structural or man made surcharge loads
on the slope and 6 additional transient loads primarily earthquake loads
Slope Ratio
The existing cut slope varies from about one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V near
the top of the slope to a negative ratio concave in portions of the lower slope
An overall average slope ratio of 0 5 horizontal to one vertical appears
to be suitable for the slope stability analysis
Drainage Seepage Conditions
The build up of water adds additional weight and lateral loads to the slope which
increases the destabilizing forces The coralline material which comprises the
slope appears to have good drainage properties This is based on observations
of the granular nature of the material and the lack of water ponding on the ground
surface at the site This assumption is also reinforced by observation of other
Slopes and drainage patterns in the general area Therefore it anpears reasonable
to assume that there will not be additional loads on the slope due to the build up
of hydrostatic or seepage pressure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2781 00 Page 11
Height of Slope
The height of the existing slopes vary from about 40 to 70 feet and can be
obtained from the existing plans for the tank farm and the adjacent building
developments
Strength of the Material
The strength of the material is the most difficult of the parameters to determine
The coralline formation is generally heterogeneous and composed of silt sand
gravel and cobble size fragments with varying degrees of cementation
Additionally these formations typically experience casing hardening i e
cementation of the near surface material due to solution and deposition of the
carbonate minerals in the coral caused by the wetting and drying of rain water on
the exposed surface materials
Due to the relatively high strength of the coralline materials it appears that the
formation would behave more like a weak to moderately strong rock than a soil
However conventional rock mechanics analysis may not be entirely applicable due
to the lack of a regular pattern of joints or bedding planes Therefore more
quantative data regarding the strength parameters of the formation and possible
joints bedding planes or faults is necessary for a rigorous analysis of the stability
of the existing slope
Additional Structural or Man Made Loads
The existing tank structures add a relatively small load on the slope due to the fact
that it is buried and the weight of the stored fluids is much less than the unit weight
of the soil

Additional Earthquake Loads
Earthquakes can add a significant destabilizing lateral load on slopes Guam is
situated within a highly active seismic region and experiences many large
earthquakes As illustrated on Plate 6 there have been 20 recorded earthquakes
of magnitude 7 or larger since 1900 An earthquake of magnitude 7 is capable of
causing sever damage to structures
For preliminary slope stability analysis a pseudo static method is normally used
where the effect of an earthquake is represented by an acceleration seismic
coefficient times gravity acting at the centroid of the sliding mass The seismic
coefficient is determined on the basis of strong motion records near the site and
the geologic profile below the site Unfortunately strong motion records are
unavailable for Guam Although the USGS seismograph station on Guam is
equipped to record distant earthquakes it goes off scale during nearby moderate
to strong events Additionally the geologic profile at the tank farm site has not yet
been determined by borings
Based on the above there is presently insufficient data to accurately define the
earthquake force which should be used in the analysis however estimates of 0 25
to 0 35 appear to be reasonable based on preliminary data from the Naval Civil
Engineering Laboratory
it is apparent from the above discussion that there is insufficient soil and geologic
data to make an accurate determination of the slope stability based on rigorous
theoretical principles However a general assessment can be made based on visual
observations the information from the geophysical surveys and conditions of existing
slope in similar materials in other portions of Guam
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2781 00 Page 13
Visual observations of the soils rock exposed on the slope surface indicate that
the formation is moderately cemented and similar in composition to other steeply cut
slopes in the general area
The geophysical surveys indicated that the strength of the coralline formation
generally increases with depth and can be considered to be a moderately hard rock
There are many slopes which have been cut to near vertical throughout much of
Guam Almost all of them appear to be stable although some ravelling and undercutting
were observed in some It is our understanding that many of these slopes were cut tens
of years ago and therefore have been subjected to significant earthquake loads in the
past
Although the present data is insufficient to provide a rigorous analysis some
qualitative conclusions may be drawn
1 The present cut may be stable under static conditions due to the relatively
high internal strength and good drainage of the formation Some localized
ravelling may occur and result in deposition of material at the bottom of the
slope
2 Portions of the slope may become unstable if subjected to very high
earthquake loads The geometry of the slope alignment and the proximity
of Tank No 6 to the top of siope indicate that this tank may be the most
susceptible to potential damage Tank Nos 3 and 7 appear to be situated
far enough away from the top of slope that a significant portion of the slope
would have to fail prior to jeopardizing the tank Therefore some warning
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2781 00 Page 14
signs would probably develop prior to large failure of the ground next to the
tank
3 The strength of the formation appears to increase with depth so the upper
portions of the slope would be more susceptible to failure
4 The probable mode of failure would be splitting and toppling of blocks of
coral starting at or near the top of slope Isolated cases of this type of
failure were observed in some existing slopes on the island Toppling
following undercutting of the bottom portion of the slope may also be a
mode of failure Fortunately in these types of failure some warning signs
are visible prior to the actual failure i e cracks in the ground on gradual
undercutting of the slope
Possible Alternatives
The data obtained in this reconnaissance indicate that several alternatives may be
considered for the future of the tank farm
Do Nothing
In this alternative there may be a moderate risk that some slope instability may
occur near Tank No 6 which may result in possible damage to the tank and
potential release of fuel stored in the tank into the ground which could
contaminate groundwater and potentially impact water quality in Tumon Bay
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2781 00 Page 15
Move or Abandon Tank No 6
Since this tank is the most susceptible to damage removal or abandonment of this
tank would greatly reduce the potential damage and release of fuel
Construct a Caisson Wall to Support Tank
Construction of a conventional retaining wall to support the steep slope and tank
is considered not feasible due to the extremely large height of slope and
inaccessibility of the site Similarly in situ reinforcement of the slope by grouting
or installation of anchors or tiebacks would be extremely difficult for the same
reasons
It may be feasible to construct a caisson wall around Tank No 6 to increase the
strength of the formation and to develop additional lateral resistance against slope
instability The caissons could consist of drilled holes 24 to 28 inches in diameter
filled with concrete and reinforcing bars The depth of the caissons would be
about 100 feet below the existing ground surface Additional information on the
strength of the in situ coralline formation would be required in order to further
evaluate the feasibility of the caisson wall and to determine the actual sizes and
lengths required
Additional Exploration
Due to budget constraints this reduced scope reconnaissance work was
performed in lieu of the originally proposed level of exploration Our field reconnaissance
was performed to provide preliminary information for the initial assessment of the stability
of the slopes below the tank farm
Therefore it is recommended that additional exploration and geotechnical
engineering analyses be performed to provide additional borings and core samples to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2781 00 Page 16
develop in situ data for more detailed analyses Of particular interest in such additional
exploration work would be the determination of the probable range of strength parameters
for the coral materials in order to allow refinement of recommendations pertaining to the
site response and behavior of the subsoils and rocks underlying the site under extreme
seismic loading conditions

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the performance of four 4
geophysical surveys at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
geophysical refraction surveys were conducted using a 12 channel signal enhancement
seismograph with a 10 pound sledge hammer as a seismic energy source
Refraction and shear seismography are based on the transmission of elastic
energy compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves These
waves are refracted at interfaces between materials
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by
the seismograph The recorded data is downloaded to a portable computer either for
processing in the field or for storage and later processing The processing involves the
interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves
After the interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical
calculations which will yield approximate depths or thicknesses of materials below the
survey and give their approximate compressional wave velocities The velocities are
averaged along the travel path of the compressional waves The calculated velocities may
be empirically related to the strength characteristics of the materials The following legend
explains the terms shown on the Seismic Refraction Survey Results Plates A 1 through
A 8
Velocity Compressional wave velocity in feet per second
Dip Dip of the top of the layer in degrees
Depth Depth below ground surface in feet
In addition to the geophysical surveys the subsurface conditions at the site were
explored by the visual observation of the materials exposed in the cut slopes below the
tank farm site
The materials observed were examined and classified in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 2488 84 Description and Identification of Soils Visual Manual
Procedure by an engineer and a geologist from our firm
