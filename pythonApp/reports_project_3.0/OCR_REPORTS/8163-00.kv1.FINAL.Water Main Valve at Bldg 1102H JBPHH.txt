INSTALL NEW WATER MAIN VALVE AT BUILDING 1102H

Dear Mr. Kaya: 
This letter report presents our findings from our geotechnical engineering 
exploration conducted in support of the proposed installation of a new water main valve 
at Building 1102H at Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam (JBPHH) in Honolulu on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations 
based on our field exploration and laboratory testing. These findings and geotechnical 
recommendations are intended to support the new water main valve design and 
construction. The findings and geotechnical recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves replacing the existing main waterline from E Street to Building 
1102 at Hickam base at JBPHH, Hawaii. The project site is bounded by Hangar Avenue 
to the south and Vickers Avenue to the north. The existing Building 1102 is a three-story, 
14-wing historical structure covering an area of about 550,000 square feet. 
As a design-built project, the project requires the contractor to retain a professional 
geotechnical engineer to conduct a detailed subsurface investigation and testing, and 
develop geotechnical requirements and design criteria in support of the project design 
and construction. 
REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late

Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which 
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. The Koolau Volcanic 
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows. 
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials 
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the Pearl Harbor area. The sea 
level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 100 feet 
above the present sea level.   
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits. Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have 
added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 16.5 feet below the ground surface. 
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Based on our field 
exploration, the project site is generally underlain by thin fill material over medium dense 
coralline reef and weathered volcanic tuff. 
We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths of about 8 to 12 feet below 
the ground surface at the time of our exploration. Due to the proximity of the project site 
to the Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations and storm 
events. It should be noted that water levels may also vary with seasonal rainfall, time of 
the year, and other factors. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings drilled, and seismic 
shear wave velocity testing conducted in the project vicinity, we believe that the project 
site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a “Very Dense Soil and 
Soft Rock” site corresponding to a Site Class C soil profile type based on the Unified 
Facilities Criteria “Seismic Design of Buildings” (UFC 3-310-04), which references the 
UFC Structural Engineering (UFC 3-301-01), 2015 International Building Code (IBC 
2015), and American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) 7-10, Minimum Design Loads for 
Buildings and Other Structures. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the regional geology setting, 
the phenomenon of soil liquefaction is not a design consideration for this project site. The 
risk for potential liquefaction is non-existent based on the subsurface conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We anticipate that the majority of the water main inverts will be on the order of 
about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Installation of the proposed water 
mains will involve excavation into the underlying fills and coralline/tuffaceous materials. 
In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignment 
may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open-cut 
excavation are not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically 
necessitating the use of shoring during construction.  
Detailed discussions for design of the water main project, including trench 
excavation, trench excavation support, backfilling, thrust block/vault design, corrosion 
protection, and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following 
sections.  
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that majority of the pipe inverts will be on the order of about 
4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Installation of the proposed water main will 
involve excavation into the underlying fills and coralline/tuffaceous materials. In general, 
we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignment may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Although it is anticipated that majority of the coralline/tuffaceous materials may be 
excavated with normal heavy equipment, such as a large backhoe excavator, coral and/or 
volcanic tuff formation typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones. Therefore, 
some difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas during excavation 
construction. It is possible that the excavation for a portion of the water main trench will 
require the use of hoerams or chipping. The contractor must exercise care to avoid 
over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the coral/tuff formation, resulting in a 
potential loss of bearing strength. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. In addition, the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 
15 feet away from existing underground utilities to reduce the potential for appreciable 
ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the existing underground utility 
lines.

Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the ground surface 
will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Where deeper excavations 
(greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and benching 
should be implemented for the trench excavations. We believe that temporary cut slopes 
for open-cut excavations are not practical at the site; therefore, trench walls may have to 
be cut vertically necessitating the use of shoring during construction.  
It is important to install adequate shoring support prior to the excavation and to 
maintain it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The 
properly braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the 
adjacent ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress 
to adjacent structures or other improvements, such as roads or utilities.  
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative, 
who should be required to continuously present on-site during excavation and 
construction works, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing 
conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high 
groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may 
affect shoring stability.  
However, it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and 
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such 
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsoil 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. In addition, it should be 
noted that settlement of the existing ground may occur as a result of the vibrations 
generated during the extraction of the sheet pile shoring. Therefore, the contractor should 
give special attention during the sheet pile removal process to reduce the potential for 
appreciable ground settlement. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical, if necessary, and that the adjacent ground should be 
continuously monitored for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with 
instruments.

Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the load 
supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the medium dense sandy coralline/tuffaceous materials, 
a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation), should be provided below the pipe. If soft and/or loose soils 
are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an 
additional 12 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the cushion 
layer for uniform support.  
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (¼) the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater, and should 
extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a Geolabs 
representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials 
are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should be provided 
below the cushion layer.  
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench, 
the unit weight of trench backfill material, and the friction resistance between the backfill 
material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried pipes, we 
recommend that an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic feet (pcf) for the trench 
backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3 could be used. Earth forces acting upon 
the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the 
trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be 
considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas.  
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water System 
Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the cushion 
material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select free-draining 
granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required 
and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material 
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage.  
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to the 
pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site surface fill soils or imported fill 
materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious/organic materials, and clay lumps and rock 
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be noted that the fill 
requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based 
on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials.

Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have 
a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test the 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations.  
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for applying 
compaction effort, Lightweight Cellular Concrete (LWCC) may be used to backfill the 
trenches. The LWCC shall be Class III and have an in-place density between 65 and 85 pcf, 
and strength between 150 and 250 pounds per square inch (psi). The use of LWCC would 
also reduce the potential for future settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel 
loading from traffic on the roadway. 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the 
pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not 
be allowed to compact the excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials.  
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture 
content.  
Valve Vault Foundations 
Concrete valve structures may be required for the water main valve. Based on the 
subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 1000 pounds per square foot (psf). This bearing value is for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by 
seismic forces.

Thrust Blocks 
Thrust blocks may be required to provide resistance to the thrust forces due to 
pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth 
pressure acting against the face of the thrust block. 
We anticipate that the majority of the planned inverts would be about 4 to 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Based on our field exploration, variable materials ranging from 
dense fills to medium dense sandy coralline materials, and/or volcanic tuff/cinder materials 
were encountered within depths explored. We recommend using lateral bearing pressures 
of up to 1,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for the design of thrust blocks bearing on medium 
dense coralline/tuffaceous materials.  
Settlement 
The primary settlement of the water main is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill material 
placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying 
subsoils below the trench invert. Settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated 
for the project.  
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
Corrosion Potential 
One set of laboratory corrosivity test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was 
performed by our laboratory and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity 
of the on-site soils on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-4 of Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion will have on the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils have a 
resistivity value of about 6,200 ohm-cm. Therefore, the on-site soils may be considered 
moderately corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 4) based on the Board of Water Supply, 
City & County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards, dated 1991.  
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride, and sulfate contents found in the soil. Results 
of the chloride and sulfate content testings indicate that the on-site soils have a chloride 
content of about 8 mg/kg and sulfate content of about 10 mg/kg. Therefore, the on-site soils

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring No. 1 and Boring No. 2, extending to a depth of about 
16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The test result is presented on Plate B-1. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test result is presented 
on Plate B-2. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test result is presented on Plate B-3. 
One set of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4.
