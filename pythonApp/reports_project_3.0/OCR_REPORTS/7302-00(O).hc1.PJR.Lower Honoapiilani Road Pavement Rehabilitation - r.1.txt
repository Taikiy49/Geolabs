SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway rehabilitation project encountered a 
pavement section consisting of approximately 3 to 9 inches of AC over 4 to 8 inches of 
aggregate base course materials. Below the existing pavement section, the borings 
generally encountered fill and/or alluvium underlain by lagoonal deposits, residual soils 
and/or saprolite (extremely weathered basalt formation) extending to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The fill 
encountered was about 0 to 5.5 feet thick and generally consisted of medium stiff to hard 
silty clay and sandy silt.  The alluvium encountered was about 0 to 5.5 feet thick and 
generally consisted of very soft to stiff sandy silt and clayey silt. Lagoonal deposit was 
encountered in two borings drilled and consisted of loose to very loose silty sand. Residual 
soils and/or saprolite were encountered in four borings drilled and generally consisted of 
hard sandy silt and loose to very dense silty sand.   
Groundwater was encountered in one of the drilled borings at a depth of about 4.8 
feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The 
groundwater level measured generally corresponds to about Elevation +0.7 feet MSL. It 
should be noted that groundwater level may vary with tidal fluctuations, seasonal rainfall, 
time of the year, and other factors. 
Our field survey indicated that the existing pavements of Lower Honoapiilani Road 
from Honoapiilani Highway to Hoohui Road under consideration for this project appear to 
exist in fair to poor conditions. Based on our engineering evaluation and construction 
considerations, we believe that the areas in fair condition may be rehabilitated with a 
regular AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of the existing pavements and 
replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
We observed pavement failures along various portions of Lower Honoapiilani Road 
within the project limits. These highly distressed pavements should be reconstructed with a 
new pavement structural section designed based on a 30-year pavement life, in 
accordance with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual prepared by the 
State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and 
Research Branch.  
In general, the existing highly distressed pavements along Lower Honoapiilani 
Road located between Honoapiilani Highway and Haku Hale Place may be reconstructed 
with a new pavement structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC on 5.5 inches of 
ACB over the recompacted base, subbase or subgrade. For Lower Honoapiilani Road 
between Haku Hale Place and Hoohui Road, the existing highly distressed pavements

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Lower Honoapiilani Road Pavement 
Rehabilitation project in Lahaina on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project consists of pavements along Lower 
Honoapiilani Road, from Honoapiilani Highway to Hoohui Road, for a total distance of 
approximately 1.9 miles. The approximate limits of the roadway rehabilitation project are 
shown on Site Plan-1 through Site Plan-9, Plates 2.1 to 2.9. 
In general, the existing pavements showing minor distresses, and severity will be 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an asphaltic concrete (AC) overlay. Reconstruction 
of the existing pavements will be performed for those areas where the observed 
distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or 
subgrade materials. We understand that the proposed pavement rehabilitation project 
will be federally funded. Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to the 
requirements and guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will 
be required. The new pavements will be designed based on the methodology described 
in the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002, prepared by the State of Hawaii - 
Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older Tertiary Epoch 
(West Maui) and the more recent Pleistocene Epoch (East Maui), also known as 
Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain 
located between these two volcanoes.  The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows 
from Haleakala Volcano ponding against West Maui Volcano.  The gently sloping plains 
were subsequently filled with alluvium washed down from the slopes of West Maui and 
East Maui (Haleakala) Volcanoes.  The erosional processes are dominated by the 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are 
transported downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium.  Once 
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes 
become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
The project site is located on the western flank of the West Maui Volcano, which 
forms the west side of the Island of Maui.  The West Maui Mountains have been deeply 
incised by erosion depositing large quantities of recent unconsolidated sediments along 
the coastal regions. The basalt formations underlying the project site are part of the 
Wailuku Volcanic Series, which consist of a thick series of thinly-bedded a’a and 
pahoehoe lava flows dipping seaward at angles of about 10 to 20 degrees. These lava 
flows are known to be highly permeable and can readily dispose of surface water runoff 
in dry well applications. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Lower Honoapiilani Road within the project limits is generally a 
two-lane undivided roadway except for the additional turn lanes at the intersection 
areas.  The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of cut grass 
and concrete sidewalks with curb and gutter, traffic signs, utility posts, and drainage 
inlets. The surface soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist 
of brown clayey silt with sand and are generally in a damp to moist condition. The 
existing Lower Honoapiilani Road alignment is generally in a North-South orientation 
with rolling terrains.  Based on our field observation and the topographic survey map

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the majority of the roadway with the existing 
pavements under consideration falls into this category and appears to be generally 
showing minor signs of structural defects. However, there were several isolated 
areas of greater pavement distress which are covered in Section 2.2.2. Overall 
pavement deterioration is restricted to light cracking of low to moderate severity, 
mainly within the tire lanes, as well as both transverse and longitudinal cracking. 
The ride characteristic of the pavement was generally minor to moderate bumpy 
with the exception of the isolated structural failed areas.  
2.2.2 Areas with Structural Defects 
In general, isolated areas of the Lower Honoapiilani Road within the project limits 
fell under this category and were observed to be in poor to very poor conditions 
with moderately to highly distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking 
were observed to be widespread and generally moderate to high in severity, 
especially within the tire lanes. The poor areas consist of elongated, severely 
distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of 1/2 to 3/4 inches 
and rutting and some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes with 
depressions varying from 1 to 5 inches in depth. Numerous potholes and 
subsurface utility trenches patched with AC material were observed at various 
locations along the alignment. The ride characteristics of the roadway within these 
subject areas were observed to be generally irregular and bumpy in the areas of

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed. Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  The procedures used in 
determining the new pavement section were generally determined based on the 
methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated 
March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii - 
Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans).

3.4 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our evaluation, it 
is our opinion that pavement areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an 
AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of the existing pavements and 
replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up 
by the cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not 
preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the contractor 
should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing begins.  Finally, 
vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed immediately prior 
to paving.

3.6 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain, and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling eleven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 11, extending to depths of 
about 5.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on Site Plan-1 through Site Plan-9, Plates 2.1 to 2.9. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Six laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by 
Ninyo & Moore on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through 
B-8.
