PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new transmission line from the Iwilei
and Liliha Substations to the proposed Archer Lane Substation at the
Hawaiian Electric Company Ward Avenue facilities The preferred corridor
for the transmission line was defined by others in the initial planning
stages of the project
The transmission line will be a 138 kV system with the transmission
cables in underground duct lines It is anticipated that the duct lines
Will be installed in trenches generally constructed by the cut and cover
method
This preliminary report was prepared for project planning purposes
and therefore may not contain sufficient data nor appropriate
recommendations for detailed design of the transmission line
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our reconnaissance was to map and provide a
preliminary assessment of geologic and near surface soil conditions within
the proposed transmission line corridors to provide preliminary

General Site Description
The preferred corridor begins at the existing Iwilei and Liliha
Substations and follows Dillingham Boulevard and Liliha Street to the
intersection of Liliha Street and Vineyard Street At this intersection
the corridor turns and follows Vineyard Street to its intersection with
Lusitana Street It follows Lusitana Street to Alapai Street Alapai
Street to King Street and King Street to Archer Lane where it will
terminate at the proposed Archer Substation

The topographic relief along the proposed corridor is generally low

proposed corridor is urban and or suburban
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of
two extinct shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano
Waianae dated as late Pliocene in age Wentworth 1951 forms the bulk
of the western third of the island The younger shield Koolau dated as
early Pleistocene in age Wentworth 1951 forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas
in central Oahu
After the main shield building of Koolau there was a long period of
erosion followed by a renewal of volcanic activity during the Pleistocene
Macdonald and Abbott 1970 This renewed activity is known as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series and includes the vents which formed Punchbowl and
Tantalus along with lavas which filled the floors of Kalihi Nuuanu and
Manoa Valleys Wentworth 1951
Also during the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of
the world As the great continental glaciers accumulated the level of
the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the oceanic
basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic
in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the
sea which were both higher and lower relative to present sea level on
Oahu

The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments and the accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments
and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition
renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited
terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream
action deposited alluvial materials graded to the current base level
This alluvium blankets the older rocks and sediments
The geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the proposed
transmission line corridor are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the eruptions of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
Some of the Honolulu Volcanic Series eruptions such as Punchbowl
were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction of groundwater
with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder These
deposits of pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally
referred to as mudrock
The events have been described as
fire fountain eruptions which laid a blanket of medium sized cinder sand
over most of the Honolulu area locally referred to as black sand

The subsurface conditions along the majority of the route are
dominated by alluvial deposits and pyroclastic deposits such as black
sand In many areas these formations are mantled by a layer of alluvial
clay which is frequently an expansive clay locally referred to as adobe
or by man made fills placed as the area was urbanized
Within the past one hundred years or so the shape of the land has
been modified by the urbanization of the area Fills were placed in low
areas and higher areas have been graded
Subsurface Conditions
The research conducted for the project indicates that the subsurface
materials consisted mainly of alluvial soil deposits that are underlain by
basalts or pyroclastics of the Honolulu Volcanic Series It is
anticipated that there may be isolated areas of coral algal limestone and
older alluvium within the proposed alignment

Detailed descriptions and discussions of the major stratigraphic
units are presented in the following sections
1
Older Alluvium Qa
The older alluvium is assigned the position of being the oldest
unit in the stratigraphic column of the project area even though
some of the coral algal reef deposits and portions of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series may be intercalated with the alluvial
deposits
This unit generally consists of terrigenous sediments which have
been transported by stream action from the upper reaches of the
streams The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard
brown clayey silts and silty clays Gravel lenses representing
buried stream channels are sometimes encountered Portions of
the older alluvium unit contain some weathered basalt boulders
and cobbles These coarse grained parts of the unit may also
contain artesian water of the basal fresh water system or as a
sub aquifer
The older alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength
values which leads to a relatively high bearing capacity
Frequently these materials show a moderate to high shrink swell
potential however this is not considered a constraint for this
project Structurally the older alluvium materials are
considered to be sufficiently competent to generally allow
unsupported excavation to moderate depths

There are no known surface exposures of this unit within the
proposed transmission line alignment However it may be
encountered in some areas depending upon the depth of excavation
Coral Algal Deposits Q1s
This unit consist of fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and
their related detrital debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital
deposits tend to be the dominant portion of any coral rock
assemblage
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral
debris consisting of gravel sand and silt sized clastic
fragments Some of the coral fragments were dissolved and the
solution cavities filled with cemented and or uncemented fine
sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal
deposits and the degree of cementation frequently present this
formation generally has high in situ strength characteristics
Structurally the coral algal reef deposits generally allow
unsupported excavation although localized sloughing may occur in
areas of uncemented granular materials Frequently the
coral algal deposits are hard enough or cemented to the extent
that hard excavation may be encountered
Groundwater may be encountered in this formation if excavation
depths approach sea level This formation generally has a high
permeability which may make dewatering difficult
Honolulu Basalt Qhb
The Honolulu Basalts generally consist of interbedded pahoehoe
and a a flows of basalt lavas and may have plastic alluvial clays
with cobbles and boulders overlying the top of the rock

It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock
sub components such as clinker layers and lava tubes could also
be encountered
The basalt frequently acts either as a perching or confining
member for groundwater in the Honolulu Aquifer system
Therefore groundwater may be encountered in excavations located
within this unit
Structurally the basalt is the strongest of all the units
encountered within the study area Therefore the basalt makes
an excellent bearing formation due to its high strength
characteristics in an undisturbed state However it generally
requires hard excavation techniques such as hoerams for
trenching The presence of groundwater may soften the overlying
clay soils to the extent that shoring may be required to support
the portions of trench walls which are cut through them These
conditions could increase the difficulty in construction and
consequently project costs
4
Volcanic Tuff Qht
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consist of
volcanic ash which consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard
rock that resembles siltstone or sandstone locally called
mudrock As previously discussed the majority of the tuff in
the project area originated from a volcanic vent in Punchbowl
Crater and its occurrence is generally limited to that vicinity
The tuff is usually found overlying coralline deposits or
alluvium units
Since the formation is a medium hard rock it has very high
strength characteristics Structurally the tuff is very
competent and is generally capable of supporting open excavations
without shoring

This formation has relatively low permeability and groundwater is
generally not encountered in this formation When it does occur
it is usually in the form of seepage along joints
Recent alluvium Ra
The recent alluvium consists mainly of very soft to medium stiff
brown to dark gray clayey silt and silty clays with varying
amounts of sand and pockets of boulders cobbles and gravel
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in low
energy environments Frequently these soils assigned to this
unit accumulated very rapidly under water and have never been
exposed Therefore there is a tendency to encounter
underconsolidated sediments with very low strength
characteristics
Included in this unit are subunits of localized thin layers
about 10 feet of lagoonal marine sediments consisting mostly of
very soft to medium stiff gray clayey silts These gray silts
are identified as lagoonal facies subunits of the young alluvial
unit It is also possible that these lagoonal facies reflect a
brief stand of the sea at each corresponding level
The soils assigned to this unit are generally considered to be
poorly to very poorly suited for use as support for foundation
loads due to their low strength characteristics and high
plasticity Shoring and bracing is frequently required for
excavation in these soils
Groundwater is frequently encountered in excavations located in
this unit The groundwater occurs as a basal water table in low
elevations and as seepage in higher elevations particularly near
streams

Black Sand Rtsp
These soils were developed from volcanic cinder sands laid down
by eruptions of the vents of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series They generally form a relatively thin
less than 10 feet mantle over coral tuff or basalt and consist
of a surface layer a few feet thick of brown sandy silt silty
sand underlain by relatively unweathered cinder sand The sand
is usually of medium dense to dense consistency and is frequently
weakly to moderately cemented
These soils in general may be excavated using conventional
techniques and are a good source of backfill material The
cementation generally allows for minimal shoring in trenches
Groundwater is usually encountered as a basal water table in low
elevations The cinder sands generally have rapid permeability
and therefore dewatering may be difficult
Man Made Fill Rf
This mapping unit represents low areas which were filled during
urbanization The majority of these low areas are underlain at
relatively shallow depths by Recent Alluvium and were originally
marshy areas A small part of the filled lands are underlain by
coral
The quality and composition of the fill is variable These fills
were frequently placed without control Therefore it is
difficult to make any statement regarding trench construction in
these areas
Groundwater in the fill areas is generally expected to occur as a
basal water table near sea level However higher perched water
tables may be encountered

Ewa EmA A well drained soil derived from alluvium which is

Tantalus TCC A well drained silty soil derived from the

has a pH in the range of 6 1 to 7 3 and is moderately corrosive
to steel and has low corrosivity to concrete

Additional Exploration
This reconnaissance was performed on the basis of a literature search

variations may not become evident until construction or until a detailed
