Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling four borings to
depths of approximately 9 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface at the locations

Laboratory Testing
Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from the field
exploration Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed as an aid in

Evaluation and Discussions
In our geotechnical engineering report dated prepared for the subject
site we recommended that the expansive clays be over excavated down to
non expansive coral or to a maximum depth of 2 feet below the footings and 3 feet below
the slabs in order to reduce the soil heaving effects on the building structure resulting from
the swelling effects of the expansive clay soils underlying the project site Based on the
borings drilled at the subject site it is our opinion that the expansive clay was adequately
over excavated and replaced with low expansion fill materials
In the same geotechnical report we addressed the importance of prevention of
water ponding adjacent to the foundations We understand that the area adjacent to the
building had been flooded after heavy rains last year Water had been ponding around the
building for a period of time Based on our field observations and laboratory testing it
appears that the observed distresses may be attributed to moisture infiltration into the
subsurface soils which resulted from the water ponding near the building The water
infiltration or excess moisture once trapped in the subsoils underneath the building
structure are not able to evaporate as a result of the protective cover provided by the
concrete slab Due to the clayey nature of the subsoils at the subject site the clayey soils
exhibit swelling and softening upon wetting and likely resulted in lifting of the concrete
slabs causing the observed distresses described above The foundation drawings indicate
that the wall footing separating the kitchen from the dining area was connected to the slab
in the dining area but not to the slab in the kitchen Therefore the heaving of the dining
area slab could cause lifting of the footing and columns causing the cracks observed
The potential for movements are greater in the southern half of the building
because the northern half is believed to be underlain primarily by coral formation with
minimal amounts of expansive clay

Remedial Recommendations
Our exploration indicates that the observed slab and column distresses are caused
by heaving of the expansive clay soils probably initiated by infiltration of water from outside
the building Several alternative methods to reduce the future swelling movements of the
building were considered and are presented below Regardless of the method selected
improvements to the drainage adjacent to the building perimeters should be implemented
Alternative No 1 Removal and Replacement of the Clay Soils
This type of recommendation has worked well on similar expansive soils in the past
provided the water is not allowed to pond next to the building The beneficial effects of the
non expansive capping layer are the additional weight to resist swelling pressures a
uniform bearing layer for the foundations and increased depth that water must travel to
saturate the expansive clay soils The potential for swelling of the clay soils decreases
directly with the increase in thickness of the capping fill However it is our opinion that a
capping thickness of about 5 feet may be an economical limit for this site Therefore this
alternative consists of removal of the existing capping fill and expansive clay to a depth of
about 5 feet below the slab cushion layer and replacement with non expansive capping fill
The clays soils should be moistened to about 3 percent above optimum moisture content
prior to placement of the capping fill The slab and wall foundations may then be
reconstructed over the capping fill layer
The disadvantages of this method are the relatively high costs significant
earthwork and potential that the concrete slabs may experience some heaving if the
underlying expansive clay is saturated
Alternative No 2 Slab Replacement with Structural Slab on Micro Piles
The laboratory testing indicates that the underlying clay has a high potential for
heaving When the clay heaves the slab on grade is forced to move with the soil
Therefore slab distresses and cracks are a result of the low tolerance of the concrete
slab for movements We recommend replacing the existing slab on grade with a
structural reinforced concrete slab to allow 4 to 6 inches of space between the surface
of the soil and the bottom of the slab The space between the soil and slab will allow
future swelling of the underlying clay soil up to 4 to 6 inches without affecting the slab
The structural slab may be supported by a micro pile foundation system In
general the micro pile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually less than
8 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micro pile foundation is
typically constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in the hole and
grouting the borehole Micro piles are desirable because they can be readily installed in
access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In

structures the adjacent soils and the environment
We estimate that a micro pile system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of
5 to 7 inches may be used to support the structural slab loads For preliminary design
purposes we recommend that each micro pile be designed based on an allowable
compressive load capacity of 50 kips The allowable compressive load capacity for the
piles is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads
such as wind or seismic forces We recommend that the micro piles extend into the coral
formation below the site The borings from this exploration encountered clay soils to
depths of about 10 feet the maximum depth drilled However the previous borings drilled
for design of the building indicate that coral formation may be encountered at greater
depths For preliminary estimating purposes we have assumed that coral formation may
be encountered at depths of about 30 to 40 feet
To achieve the allowable compressive load capacity of 50 kips with a factor of
safety of 2 we estimate that each micro pile would need to extend to a minimum depth of
about 40 feet Additional deep borings should be drilled to finalize the design of the
micro piles
Settlements of the micro pile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the pile supported foundations to be less than 0 5 inches We believe that
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
The advantage of this system is that the future movements of the clay soils up to
6 inches will not affect the concrete slab The footing excavation and embedment is
greatly reduced since the micro piles may be used to resist the heaving pressures and
vertical loading The grading is less than Alternative No 1 since the existing soil under the
concrete slabs may remain in place
The disadvantage of this system is the relatively high costs for the structural slab
system
Alternative No 3 Chemical Stabilization of Expansive Soils
We believe that chemical stabilization using the Electrochemical Soil Stabilizer
EcSS 3000 may be a cost effective way of coping with the moderately to highly
expansive nature of the on site soils to reduce the potential for the observed distress from
recurring In general the EcSS 3000 chemical stabilization involves the injection of a
diluted chemical diluted in water into the ground to chemically stabilize the expansive
nature of the soils The chemical injection reacts with the clay particles in the soil to form a
soil mass that is less susceptible to shrink swell effects when subjected to moisture
fluctuations

Based on our evaluations we believe that the locally available EcSS 3000 for
chemical stabilization may be a technically and cost effective alternative to reduce the
shrink swell effects of the on site clayey soils For preliminary estimating purposes we
believe that the chemical stabilization would need to be injected to a minimum depth of
about 8 feet below the existing floor elevation In addition the spacing of the injection
holes should not be further than 3 feet on center in both directions The chemical
stabilization should also extend a minimum of 5 feet beyond the edge of the building
envelope
After injection of the chemical stabilizer a minimum curing time of 72 hours
should be allowed prior to post treatment sampling and testing for evaluation of the
treated product The post treatment sampling and testing would include drilling and
sampling about six borings extending to depths of about 10 feet to obtain relatively
undisturbed samples of the treated zone Four relatively undisturbed samples of the
treated zone should be obtained from each boring The relatively undisturbed samples
of the treated soils should be subjected to one dimensional swell tests conducted in
accordance with ASTM D 4546 Method B For acceptance purposes the average
swell in each of the post treatment borings should be 1 percent or less In addition the
results of the swell test of each treated soil sample should not exceed 2 percent
The advantages of this system are the relatively low cost and limited earthwork
needed The chemical can be injected through the existing concrete slab and does not
require removal and replacement of the soils below the slab The construction time for this
method is also much less than the others
The disadvantage of this method is the higher risk involved This is a relatively new
procedure with limited experience in Hawaiian soils Additional movements may occur if
the penetration of the chemical is prevented or the swell potential is not reduced
sufficiently

The bottoms of the continuous footings should be over excavated for a minimum
depth of 3 feet and backfilled with compacted structural fill material The compacted
structural fill will serve to provide uniform bearing support for the continuous footing Prior
to placement of the structural fill the bottom of the footing over excavations should be
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 3 percent above
the optimum moisture content and recompacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative
compaction Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the structural fill
layer should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials
The structural fill required under the footing foundations should consist of select
granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Compacted structural fill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by
frictional resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation
system A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted
structural fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Drainage Measurements
In addition to the above alternatives we recommend that positive drainage away
from the building structure be re established This will help to reduce the potential for
swelling and loss of bearing strength of the underlying clayey soils due to saturation
Therefore we recommend that the following measures be implemented at the subject site
as soon as possible preferably prior to the cosmetic repairs of the existing building
distresses
1 Excessive landscape watering from automatic sprinkle system near the
foundations should be avoided in order to reduce the potential for water
ponding around the building and foundations
2 The finished grades of the landscaping areas outside the building should be
regraded and sloped to shed water away from the building foundations It is
essential for the proper performance of the foundation support system since
ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent
heaving
We believe that the potential for structural distress to the building structure should
be significantly reduced if these measures are properly implemented and maintained
