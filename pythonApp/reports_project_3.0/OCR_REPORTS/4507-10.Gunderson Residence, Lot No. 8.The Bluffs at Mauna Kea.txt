The Gunderson Residence project at Lot No 8 of The Bluffs at Mauna Kea subdivision
is situated west of the Mauna Kea Volcano The site is also located adjacent to the
northwest facing coastline of the Island of Hawaii The lava formation at the site appears to
be of a a type flow which spreads downslope from vents further up on the mountain flank A a
lavas are typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a denser
and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core
Cavities such as lava tubes open fractures or other void spaces are commonly
encountered buried within the a a lavas however more predominantly within the pahoehoe
lavas Many of these cavities were formed when the still molten lava created the structures
by means of flowage and ponding of the lava The cavities represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally
smaller in horizontal extent
The a a pahoehoe basalt lava rock and clinker encountered at the site are generally
considered to be relatively permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both the
horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along
joints fractures inter flow contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers The
permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the primary water
transmission features

We understand that several site retaining structures will be required for the project The
following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of the retaining structures
planned at the site
Retaining Structure Foundations
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site we believe that
shallow continuous footings may be used to support the retaining walls planned at the
site In general foundations may be designed based on an allowable bearing pressure
of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf for footings bearing on 12 inches of
aggregate subbase select borrow subbase This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces
In general the footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In addition
foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions the footing
should be embedded deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Footings
oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge
of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
lf soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades below the
12 inch aggregate subbase the material should be over excavated and replaced with

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Since the project site is located on the Island of Hawaii seismically active dynamic
lateral earth pressures should be considered in the design of retaining structures
Dynamic lateral forces due to seismic loading may be estimated by 10H
pounds per lineal foot of wall for level backfill where H is the height of the wall in feet
It should be noted that the dynamic lateral forces provided assume that the wall will be
allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in the event of an earthquake
lf this amount of lateral movement is not acceptable the retaining wall should be
designed with significantly higher dynamic lateral forces for a restrained condition
The dynamic lateral earth pressures presented above are in addition to the static lateral
earth pressures An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic
lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the retaining structure

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed grading limits should be
excavated where necessary to the design finished subgrades The excavated
materials may be re used as a source of fill materials provided that the materials meet
the gradation requirements presented in the Fill and Backfill Materials subsection of
this report Loose and or soft spots encountered at the excavated subgrade levels
should be removed and replaced with structural fill materials compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction
In areas to receive fill or finished subgrades in cuts the subgrade should be
proof rolled with heavy equipment such as a 10 ton vibratory drum roller or a
Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting and possibly
collapsing near surface cavities
The proof rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a representative
from Geolabs Inc Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing
and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with compacted fill materials Due to
the potential for presence of cavities and or voids contract documents should include
additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills for the project should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement for the upper 8 inches
of fills below the pavements should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Where fills are composed of cobbles conventional compaction testing may not be
practicable Therefore a testing program to evaluate the number of passes of a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted at
the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a representative
from Geolabs Inc On the basis of this testing program the number of passes may
then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
Excavation
Based on the available information the project site is underlain by near surface fills and

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions permanent cut slopes exposing the
fill materials and basalt formation may be designed with a slope inclination of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Permanent fill slopes may also be designed
with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fills to be placed on existing slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against
sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to
the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Water should be diverted
away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as
possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
