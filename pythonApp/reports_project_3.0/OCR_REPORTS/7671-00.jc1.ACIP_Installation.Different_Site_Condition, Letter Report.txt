Dear Mr. Castell: 
In accordance with the contract documents, we provided geotechnical 
engineering, quality control services for Auger-Cast-In-Place (ACIP) pile installation. 
Based 
on 
our 
observation 
during 
the 
ACIP 
pile 
installation 
at 
the 
Communications/Electronics (Comm./Elec.) Facility site, the contractor encountered 
difficult drilling conditions resulting in construction delay and loss of drilling tools. As 
requested, we reviewed the Pile Installation Logs and the Boring Logs included in the 
RFP documents. This letter presents the results of our evaluation. 
There are three borings (BA-4, BA-5 and BA-6) drilled by others within the 
Comm./Elec. Facility building footprint that were extended to depths of about 80 to 102 
feet below the ground surface. Although the boring method was not specified in the logs, 
we believe that the borings were advanced to the depths using a solid-stem auger and/or 
wash boring method without core barrel. This condition indicated that the standard ACIP 
drilling method is adequate to penetrate the subsurface soil/rock conditions suggested by 
the Boring Logs. Boring Logs presented on Plan Sheets BB004 through BB007 are 
included in the Appendix for ease of reference. 
The ACIP piles for the Comm./Elec. Facility building were installed with 24-inch 
continuous-flight auger mounted on Bauer BG-28, that has a torque capacity of about 
199,142-pound-foot. Auger drilling resistance was recorded automatically by a Pile 
Installation Recorder (PIR). Each pile was drilled to a depth of about 75 feet below the 
cutoff elevation, as specified in the contract documents. 
Because the less powerful boring rig was capable of advancing boreholes up to 
102 feet below the ground surface, it was anticipated that the larger Bauer BG-28 drill rig 
(having a much higher torque capacity) would advance the continuous-flight auger to a 
depth of about 75 feet without much difficulty. However, extensive hard drilling conditions 
were encountered in some of the ACIP pile construction, causing construction delay and

pile drilling are much harder than the materials encountered in the borings and are 
different from the materials disclosed in the contract documents. 
To validate the above conclusion, we evaluated the ACIP pile drilling resistance 
during the installation, to compare the boring resistance in SPT blow counts. The 
evaluation was conducted in the vicinity of three areas where three borings were located, 
as shown on the Plan Sheet S1-101, also included in the Appendix. It should be noted 
that the SPT blow counts were normalized for correction of sampling methods and rod 
lengths. 
It should be noted that drilling of ACIP Piles 142B and 143B (located near 
Boring BA-6) was advanced to the specified depth without extensive hard resistance, 
which is consistent to the Boring Logs. Therefore, the average drilling resistances in 
Piles 142B and 143B, shown on Plate 1 in dash green line, may be considered as a typical 
drilling resistance baseline. The normalized SPT blow counts are also plotted in dash blue 
line for comparison. 
Two areas (Area-1 and Area-2) in the vicinity of Borings BA-4 and BA-5 were 
evaluated for the ACIP drilling resistance. In Area-1, the average drilling resistance for 
Piles 127B and 128B are plotted in a solid green line along the depth with the normalized 
SPT blow counts in dash blue line as shown on Plate 2. In Area-2, the average drilling 
resistances for Piles 055B, 032B and 033B are shown in Plate 3. For comparison, the 
typical drilling resistance baseline is also plotted in dash green line on Plates 2 and 3. 
Extensive drilling resistances encountered in Area-1 and Area-2 were as high as 
2.5 to 3 times of the typical drilling resistance baseline, as illustrated on Plates 2 and 3. 
The magnitude of the drilling resistances is much higher than what the Boring Logs were 
suggesting. We believe that the extensive drilling resistance encountered in the ACIP pile 
installation is a different site condition.
