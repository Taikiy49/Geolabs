This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Kalaeloa Barbers Point Harbor Eroded Shoreline project in the Kapolei

This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our site reconnaissance and laboratory testing for the project. These findings and 
geotechnical recommendations are intended for the temporary remedial repair of the 
eroded shoreline only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The eroded shoreline area is located at the east corner of the harbor extension 
area at Kalaeloa Barbers Point Harbor in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
We understand that surface runoff from heavy storm events has eroded the 
corner area of the harbor up to about 6 feet deep and up to about 4.5 feet wide and has 
created a small gully toward the harbor.  We understand that permanent repairs to the 
harbor shoreline will be performed in the near future and it is desired to provide 
temporary repair of the eroded area at this time. In addition, we understand that the 
temporary remedial measure needs to be located above the high-water level due to 
permitting issues. Furthermore, we understand that site grading work beyond the 
eroded gully to improve surface water drainage is not desired at this time.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano 
ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope 
forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau.  The project site is located 
on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by the erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the sedimentary 
wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Freeway to over 1,000 feet 
at Ewa Beach.  This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation, or

continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet 
waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys 
in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous 
reef materials.  Placement of man-made fills and commercial development of the area 
within the last 20 years have brought the project area to its present form. 
SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
A site reconnaissance was performed to observe the surface conditions of the 
eroded gully walls and the adjacent areas. One bulk sample of the near-surface soils, 
designated as Bulk-1, was collected to evaluate the engineering properties of the 
near-surface alluvial deposits. The approximate bulk sample location is shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field observations, the eroded shoreline area is generally covered 
with near-surface fill underlain by alluvial deposits. Near the water’s edge, coral formation 
was observed. In general, we observed near-surface fill materials consisting of coralline 
silty sands and gravels extending to a depth of about 2 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The near-surface fill was underlain by alluvial deposits consisting of stiff to very 
stiff sandy clay with some gravel extending to the bottom of the eroded gully about 6 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  
Based on our field observations and the topographic plan, the eroded gully ranges 
from about one and one-half horizontal to one vertical (1.5H:1V) to 1H:1V inclination as 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The bottom of the eroded gully was dry during our site 
visit.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our site visit, we observed that the project site is generally covered with 
near-surface fill underlain by alluvial deposits extending to the bottom of the eroded gully 
about 6 feet below the existing ground surface. Coral formation was observed at the end 
of the eroded gully near the harbor water’s edge. 
The probable cause of the erosion observed and recommended temporary 
remedial measures are provided below. 
Probable Cause of Erosion 
Surface erosion channels were observed at the top of the eroded gully.  Based 
on conversations with State of Hawaii, Department of Transportation, Harbor Division 
personnel, storm water runoff leading to the eroded gully was observed coming from the 
access road above and adjacent areas since the eroded gully was the low point in the 
area. A significant amount of surface runoff water was observed flowing to the eroded 
gully area during heavy rainfall events.  
Therefore, we believe that the erosive force of the surface runoff water and the 
erodible soils at the site may be the potential cause of the erosion observed at the east 
corner of the harbor extension area. 
Temporary Remedial Measures 
Based on our field observations and results of the laboratory testing, temporary 
remedial measures were developed to reduce the potential for additional erosion at the 
project site. The temporary remedial measures consist of utilizing dumped and grouted 
riprap, and geotextile. 
We understand that surface runoff from heavy storm events has eroded the 
corner area of the harbor and has created the small gully toward the harbor. To reduce 
the surface runoff water velocity and erosion potential, we recommend placing dumped 
riprap consisting of 12 to 24-inch diameter stones within the eroded gully area. Prior to 
placing the dumped riprap, a geotextile (Mirafi FW700 or equal) should be placed along 
the sides and bottom of the gully to provide separation from the underlying native soil. 
The geotextile should be installed per the manufacturer’s recommendations. Prior to 
placing the geotextile, existing vegetation and protrusions should be removed. The 
dumped riprap should be carefully placed to prevent damaging the filter fabric. 
To provide stability for the dumped riprap placed within the eroded gully, we 
recommend constructing a keyway at the lower end of the placed dumped riprap. The 
keyway should be a minimum of 2 feet wide and extend a minimum of 3 feet below the

resistance for the dumped riprap placed above the keyway.  
The geotextile (Mirafi FW700 or equal) should be placed along the sides of the

Based on our observations, hard coral formation may be encountered within the 
keyway excavation.  Therefore, hard excavation using hoeram and/or chipping should 
be anticipated. 
The area above the keyway should be backfilled with dumped riprap. The 
temporary backfill slope constructed of the dumped riprap material may be designed 
with a slope inclination of 1H:1V or flatter. To provide resistance to wave action above 
the high water level, the dumped riprap 3 feet above the top of the keyway and 3 feet 
behind the keyway should be grouted. 
To provide a relatively smooth and level surface at the top of the dumped riprap, 
the top of the dumped riprap may be grouted or concreted. The dumped riprap slope 
should not be grouted except for the lower 3 feet to facilitate drainage of water within 
the dumped riprap backfill. If grouting of the dumped riprap slope is needed, weep holes 
at about 2-foot spacing near the bottom of the dumped riprap slope face should be 
provided to drain collected water within the dumped riprap backfill.

extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If 
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations 
provided herein.
