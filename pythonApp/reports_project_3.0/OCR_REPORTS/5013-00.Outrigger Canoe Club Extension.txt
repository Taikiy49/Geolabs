2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae Volcano forms the
bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano The
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the project site are directly related to the
glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis
of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are
dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid
during high stands of the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The
base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in
sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft and or loose sediments along the coast in
the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy wetlands
As the city of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal
deposits consisting of silty coralline sands with coral fragments The lower clayey

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they could only be detected by

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC current code adopted by the City
and County of Honolulu the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and
should be evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction Soil liquefaction is a
condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the ground surface
undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore water
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an
increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength
of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up
could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil
acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and
if not confined will result in significant deformations

Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to
relative density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and a design peak ground
acceleration of 0 15g the likelihood of liquefaction at the project site appears to be
low to moderate for the soil layer located at depths of about 20 to 30 feet below the
ground surface Therefore we believe that the potential for liquefaction at the site
should not have an adverse impact on the project due to the depth of the potentially
liquefiable soil layer about 10 feet or more below bottom of foundations

weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

3 2 Uplift Load Resistance
As mentioned previously the basement structure may be subjected to buoyant
uplift forces due to the high flood elevations at the site about 10 feet MSL Based on

1 7 feet MSL Considering the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean
groundwater levels at the site are expected to vary with the tides and other factors In
general we believe that the static groundwater level at the site would vary between about
Elevation O and 3 feet MSL Therefore we believe that a static groundwater level of
3 feet MSL may be used in the design of the basement mat foundation and walls
In general resistance to uplift loads imposed on the mat foundation may be
mobilized by the dead weight of the structure and the mat foundation In addition the
weight of soils placed above the mat foundation system may also be used to resist uplift
loads Contribution of dead weight from compacted soils above the mat foundation may
be estimated using a unit weight of 110 pounds per cubic foot pcf
Based on the flood elevation of 10 feet MSL significant uplift loads may be
imposed on the mat foundations resulting from hydrostatic pressures due to the elevated
groundwater condition Therefore consideration may be given to the use of tie down
anchors to resist the buoyant uplift forces acting on the basement structure of the project
For design purposes we believe that low to moderate capacity tie down anchors may be
used effectively to resist the uplift loads We believe that the low to moderate capacity
tie down anchors may achieve uplift load capacities on the order of about 70 to 80 kips

12 inches drilled and grouted anchor with steel reinforcing The tie down anchor is
typically constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in the hole and
grouting the borehole Tie down anchors are desirable because they can be readily
installed in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that some of the ground floor slabs above the basement level for
the project will consist of concrete slabs on grade Based on the existing topography and
the anticipated finished floor elevation we envision that the slabs on grade for the
proposed building will be supported either on new compacted backfills or on the existing
ground conditions Based on the granular nature of the on site soils we believe that the
concrete slabs on grade may be supported on the granular on site soils
For the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill Consisting of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the slabs The open graded gravel
cushion fill would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform
support of the slabs To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab
and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is

In order to construct the new basement level we anticipate that excavations of

construction Based on the subsurface conditions and presence of existing structures
directly adjacent to the excavation we envision that temporary shoring of the
excavations and or underpinning of the existing footings will likely be required for the
project construction
Our field exploration at the subject site encountered a surface layer of man made
fill consisting of medium dense to dense silty sands Fill materials were generally
underlain by medium dense sandy and gravelly soils coralline detritus with pockets of
very loose materials Therefore we anticipate that conventional excavation techniques

may be used for the planned excavations In general we believe that interlocking steel
sheet piling may be used for temporary shoring purposes especially if some dewatering

liquefaction of the loose granular materials contained within the coralline detritus
Because disturbance to the surrounding soils due to construction activities may
trigger settlements of the existing foundations we recommend that the existing footings

appreciable settlement of the existing footings Some of the more common types of

presented previously

After clearing finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills

compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
AASHTO T 180 ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Yielding areas and
soft loose areas disclosed during clearing and scarification operations should be

In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general
fill material provided that the materials are free of vegetation deleterious materials and
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension Imported fill

well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension

materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use

facilitate compaction of fill materials above

thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 The borings were advanced to a
maximum depth of about 50 3 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled using a portable drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on
Plate B 2
