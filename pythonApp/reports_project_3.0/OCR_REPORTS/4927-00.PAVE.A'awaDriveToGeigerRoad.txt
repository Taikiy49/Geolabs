SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be required for the new Fort Weaver Road Route 76
Widening Aawa Drive to Geiger Road project Due to the clayey nature of the subgrade
soils encountered at the project site we believe that a permeable drainage layer should be
incorporated into the pavement design for the new roadway lanes Therefore we have
considered only pavement structural sections with a permeable drainage layer in our
analyses for the project
Based on our field exploration we anticipate that the majority of the pavement
subgrade soils will consist generally of medium stiff to hard clayey silts and silty clays for
the new roadway lanes Based on the traffic data provided the relatively low R Value of
the subgrade soils and the economic analyses performed we recommend that a flexible
pavement structural section be used for the new pavements The recommended flexible
pavement section of 4 inches of AC on 9 5 inches of ACB over 10 0 inches of UTPB over
9 0 inches of ASB should be used for the design of pavements for the new Fort Weaver
Road Route 76 Widening from Aawa Drive to Kolowaka Drive A pavement structural
section consisting of 4 inches of AC on 9 5 inches of ACB over 10 0 inches of UTPB over
6 0 inches of ASB should be used for the design of pavements from Kolowaka Drive to
Geiger Road
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils It should be noted that the clayey
subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and or loss of strength upon saturation
Therefore special attention should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements As
a minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures To
collect infiltrated water into the pavement structural section we recommend that a
pavement edge drain system hydraulically connected to the untreated permeable base
be provided along the edges on the low side of the new pavements
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific recommendations for design of pavements

1 1 Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our engineering
analyses for the design of new pavements for the Fort Weaver Road Route 76
Widening Aawa Drive to Geiger Road project located in the District of Ewa on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavements
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

The proposed project is located along Fort Weaver Road Route 76 in the
District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project starts from approximately

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased
lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a
broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to
the south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
Development within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 13 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 13 The borings drilled were
advanced to depths of about 10 8 to 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 3 1 through 3 7
The majority of the borings drilled generally encountered surface fills consisting
of dense sandy gravel underlain by alluvium In general the surface fills were less than
about 2 feet in thickness The alluvium encountered generally consisted of medium stiff
to hard silty clays and clayey silts Coralline detritus consisting of dense to very dense
sands and gravel were encountered below the alluvium in Boring Nos 8 and 13 at
depths of about 24 feet and 8 feet below the ground surface respectively
Boring Nos 1 through 4 generally were drilled near the Honouliuli Stream Bridge
crossing These borings generally encountered fills consisting of medium dense sands
and stiff to hard silty clays extending from the ground surface down to about 10 to
18 feet below the ground surface The fill materials generally were underlain by soft
and or loose alluvial and lagoonal deposits The alluvial and lagoonal deposits
generally consisted of soft sandy and clayey silts and very loose silty sands The soft
and or loose soil deposits generally extended down to about 28 feet below the ground
surface in Boring Nos 1 and 4
The alluvial and lagoonal deposits were underlain by medium dense silty sands
and very stiff to hard clayey silts extending down to the maximum depths explored of
about 41 5 feet below the ground surface in these borings It should be noted that the

3 1 Methodology of Pavement Design
Two types of pavement structural sections flexible and rigid pavements were
considered in the pavement analyses for this project The flexible pavement sections
presented in this report generally were determined based on the methodology described
in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 The
Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and Research Branch The
pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed
and used by the California Department of Transportation Caltrans
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections generally are based on
the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association Thickness Design
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements

3 3 Design Subgrade Conditions
We envision that the majority of the widened roadway generally will be
constructed near the existing grades or in a cut condition Based on our field
exploration we anticipate that the majority of the pavement subgrade soils generally will
consist of very stiff clayey silts and silty clays for the new travel lanes
We have performed our pavement designs assuming that the pavement
subgrade soil generally will consist of the near surface clayey silt and silty clay soils
encountered in our borings with a laboratory Resistance R Value of about 7
Therefore an R Value of 7 has been adopted for the subgrade soils in our pavement
design analyses for this project The results of our laboratory R Value and California
Bearing Ratio CBR tests are presented on Plates B 5 through B 12 of Appendix B If
site grading exposes soils other than those assumed additional tests should be
performed to confirm and or revise the recommended pavement section for actual field
conditions

3 4 Design Pavement Section
Because of the clayey nature of the pavement subgrade soils we recommend
that a permeable drainage layer be incorporated into the pavement design Therefore
we have considered only pavement structural sections with a permeable drainage layer
in our analyses for the project Details of the drainage analysis used for this project are
presented on Plate C 2 of this report
Based on the relatively high traffic volume anticipated for Fort Weaver Road we
believe that asphalt concrete base ACB should be used as the base material
Therefore pavement sections utilizing untreated aggregate base courses were not
considered in our pavement design analyses
As mentioned previously the on site silty clay and clayey silt soils generally have
relatively low CBR values and may exhibit moderate to high expansion potential when
subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we recommend that a minimum of
24 inches of subbase materials be provided below the concrete pavements for the rigid
pavement design options

3 6 Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of
general fill materials Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive
select granular material such as crushed coralline or basaltic materials The materials
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site
for the intended use
3 7 Compaction Requirements
In general fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Aggregate
subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to the requirements
stipulated in Subsection 703 16 of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 HSS The aggregate subbase
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to not less than 95 percent of the maximum
density according to Section 106 09 Special Test Methods of the HSS
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basaltic
aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to at least 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined
in accordance with AASHTO T 209 ASTM D 2041 test procedures
Asphaltic concrete AC materials should be constructed in general accordance
with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete Pavement of the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 and subsequent
amendments

3 8 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils It should be noted that the
clayey subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation
Therefore special attention should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements
As a minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should
be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage
structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction To collect infiltrated water into the pavement structural section we
recommend that a pavement edge drain system be provided along the sides of the new
travelway
We envision that an edge drain will be needed to collect and discharge infiltrated
water into the pavement structural section from the untreated permeable base layer
The edge drain should consist of a 6 inch PVC perforated pipe with perforations facing
down surrounded by untreated permeable base A permeable separator non woven
filter fabric conforming to Section 648 of the HSS should be placed around the
untreated permeable base to reduce the potential for migration of fines into the drainage
material as shown on the Typical Edge Drain Detail Plate 4 In addition a permeable

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored preliminarily by
drilling and sampling 13 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 13 The borings
were advanced to depths of approximately 10 8 to 41 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight auger tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the
project are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 7
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 13
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests performed on selected soil samples are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One Sieve Analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected
sample of the granular soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to
aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided
on Plate B 2
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The results of the direct shear
test are presented on Plate B 3
One Consolidation test with time rates of consolidation ASTM D 2435 was
performed on a sample of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the
compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
Four laboratory Resistance R Value tests AASHTO T 190 were performed on
selected bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 through B 8
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 also were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The samples tested were remolded to near the optimum
moisture content Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 9 through B 12
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 698B were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 13 through B 16
