INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our environmental site exploration of the vicinity
of the existing underground storage tank at the Waipahu Wastewater Pump Station facility
in Waipahu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general project area is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is an existing City and County of Honolulu wastewater pump
station facility To provide emergency power for the pumping station there is a standby
diesel generator at the site Fuel for this generator has been stored in a 2 000 gallon
capacity underground steel tank
Currently it is proposed to expand the facility with the addition of new pumping
capacity As part of the expansion project it is proposed to replace the existing storage
tank
The limited scope of our exploration and this report is intended to determine the
potential presence of contamination of the soils at the locations drilled and sampled for
this exploration by petroleum products which may have been released from the storage
tank
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions in the vicinity of the existing underground storage tank with respect to potential
contamination by hydrocarbon products This was accomplished by the collection of soil
samples from drilled borings in accordance with generally accepted protocols Selected

samples obtained from the borings were tested in general accordance with U S
Environmental Protection Agency methods
The information obtained was intended to provide a preliminary soil data set to aid
in determining the potential presence of contaminants in the soils in the immediate vicinity
of the underground storage tank

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated adjacent to Waipahu Depot Road in the southerly
portion of the town of Waipahu in the Ewa District on the island of Oahu Hawaii The site
is used as a wastewater pump station facility and is occupied by several structures related
to that use
The site is bounded to the west by Waipahu Depot Road to the north by the
Waipahu Refuse Convenience Center and by undeveloped land to the south and east
The topography of the site is relatively level with ground surface elevation ranging
between about 5 0 and 8 5 feet above Mean Sea Level

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two 2
extinct shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the
bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located along the southern flank of Koolau and _ its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and the
Ewa Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions along the majority of the routes are
dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid
during high stands of the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The
base of the stratigraphic section in this area is believed to be Koolau Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2979 20 Page 6
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted in
deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary
unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments

About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the deep valleys in the area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so sea level
has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to
accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits
of soft harbor sediments along the coast in the areas which were formerly valleys and
drainageways Along the northerly fringe of Pearl Harbor brackish water marshes
developed depositing thick layers of organic sediments in a lacustrine environment
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the area to its present form These projects involved the placement of man made fill over
the thick marsh deposits
Groundwater Conditions
The project site is located within the Pearl Harbor aquifer basin The primary
water bearing formation for this aquifer is Koolau Basalt In this basin where the
sedimentary caprock acts as a confining member artesian groundwater occurs within
Koolau Basalt The potentiometric head of this aquifer varies spatially temporally and in
response to pumpage
The Pearl Harbor aquifer is generally recharged in the Koolau Range and to an
extent in central Oahu The discharge area for this basin to the south The primary
discharge mechanism is through springs generally along the northerly portion of Pear
Harbor Secondary discharge is through pumpage for groundwater withdrawals and
minor leakage through the sedimentary caprock
In addition groundwater is also encountered in the other geologic units described
within the project area Generally this groundwater occurs as a basal water table at or
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2979 20 Page 8
near sea level This groundwater is usually brackish or saline In some isolated areas
perched groundwater and or sub artesian groundwater may be encountered
The groundwater encountered during our field exploration appears to be basal
groundwater occurring within the sedimentary caprock materials
Drinking water sources within the Pearl Harbor basin generally tap the artesian
Koolau Basalt aquifer It is believed that the shallow groundwater encountered below the
site in our borings is hydraulically isolated from the underlying basalt aquifer by confining
beds and by hydraulic gradient
FIELD EXPLORATION
Sample Collection Activities
The subsurface conditions in the vicinity of the existing storage tank were explored
by the drilling and sampling of three 3 borings to depths ranging between 19 5 and 20 5
feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
rotary hydraulic drill rig advancing hollow stem augers Samples were obtained from
borings in general accordance with ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last
12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample depths
Samples recovered from the split barrel sampler were examined and classified in
general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and
Identification of Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs
of the borings are shown on Plates A 1 through A 3
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2979 20 Page 9
During the field exploration headspace sampling of selected samples was
conducted by using an H Nu PW 101 photo ionization detector The PID was calibrated
on a daily basis using an isobutylene standard The readings obtained are shown on the
boring logs at the appropriate sample depth
All sampling equipment was cleaned and decontaminated prior to sampling using
steam cleaning washing with a Liquinox detergent solution and rinsing with deionized
water Drilling tools such as the hollow stem augers were decontaminated prior to the
drilling of each boring and prior to leaving the site by steam cleaning
Soil samples were contained in six inch long stainless steel sampler liners The
samples were sealed and packed in ice for transport to and for analyses by an U S
Environmental Protecting Agency certified laboratory The analytical results are presented
in Appendix C of this report
DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
General
Our field exploration indicates that the site is underlain by a crust of man made fill
consisting of clayey and silty soils Under this fill our exploration encountered soft
sediments to the maximum depths explored for this site assessment
The sediments appear to have high natural organic contents and have strong
organic odors
Analytical Results
The laboratory analyses conducted in conjunction with this exploration indicate that
the levels of benzene toluene and xylene which are typical indicators for petroleum
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2979 20 Page 10
contamination were below the detection limit In addition the levels of total petroleum
hydrocarbons as diesel were also below detection limit
The analyses showed only very low levels of polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons
such as benzo a pyene in samples from Borings 1 and 2 Since indicator parameters
for petroleum hydrocarbons were below detection limits it is believed that the low levels
of aromatics may due to the organic nature of the soils

Additional Testing
Our findings are based on the drilling and sampling of three 3 shallow borings in
the vicinity of the existing tank Although there is no direct evidence that there was any
release of diesel fuel from the tank or related piping system it should be realized that the
permeabilities of the fill and the organic soils encountered at the site are relatively low
This could retard the transport of any petroleum product in the subsurface
It is recommended that the tank removal work be performed in accordance with
Hawaii Department of Health guidelines including additional sampling of the soils
encountered in the excavation
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as drilled probes Variations of
conditions may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until excavation at the site or removal of the existing storage tank If variations
then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the findings presented in this
report
