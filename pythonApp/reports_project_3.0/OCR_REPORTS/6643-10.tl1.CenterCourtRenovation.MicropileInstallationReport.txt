As requested, Geolabs, Inc. provided geotechnical engineering observation and 
consultation services during the micropile foundation installation for the above project. 
The following letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering observations 
during installation of the micropile foundations for the Center Court Renovation at 
Ala Moana Center project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Geolabs submitted a geotechnical engineering consultation for the design of the 
micropile foundation, and our recommendations are presented in the letter report 
entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Consultation, Micropile Foundations, Center Court 
Renovation, Ala Moana Center, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii”, dated June 29, 2012. The 
purpose of our geotechnical engineering observation during construction was to provide 
engineering consultation during the construction of the micropile foundation and to 
provide “Special Inspection” services by observing the micropile foundation installation 
at the project for general conformance with the project plans and specifications. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The existing Center Court area is at the street level of the Ala Moana Center in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that the Center Court and the 
stores around it will be reconfigured to create a central walkway/entrance to the escalator. 
To accommodate the new design layout, the existing columns supporting the canopy 
structure and escalator will need to be removed and replaced with new wide flange 
columns at different locations.

Based on our experience in the Ala Moana Center area, we anticipate the project 
site is generally underlain by loose compressible lagoonal deposits overlying coral 
formation and coralline detritus deposits at greater depths. Based on the anticipated 
subsurface conditions and structural loads, a deep foundation system consisting of 
micropiles was required to support canopy/escalator loads to reduce the potential for 
appreciable total and differential settlements. As part of the construction requirements, 
one proof test was conducted to evaluate and confirm the micropile foundation design. 
The scope of our work discussed in this letter report includes observation of the 
micropile foundation construction for the Center Court Renovation project only.  The 
micropile contractor for the project is Structural Systems, Inc. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site consist of about 10 feet of surface 
fill and about 20 feet of loose/soft lagoonal deposit overlying medium dense coralline 
detritus extending to depths of approximately 40 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Below the coralline detritus, a coral ledge (coral formation) was encountered extending 
to the maximum micropile installation depth of 65 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
We encountered groundwater at a depth of about 8 feet below the ground surface 
at the time of the micropile installation. The groundwater levels generally correspond to 
about Elevations +0.0 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). Due to the proximity of the project site 
to the Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations. It should be 
noted that water levels also may vary with seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, 
groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the project vicinity, the 
micropiles were specified to extend through the loose lagoonal deposits and derive load 
bearing support from a 23-foot bond zone developed within the coralline materials. To 
reduce risk of micropile “necking” within the loose/soft lagoonal material, permanent 
steel casing was specified to be left in-place at the top of each micropile. The design 
micropile allowable compressive load capacity was specified to be 80 kips.

In general, the micropile was drilled using a Beretta T24H rig equipped with 
down-the-hole hammer and advanced with 5.5-inch Outside Diameter (O.D.) temporary 
steel casing to the specified depth. Advancing steel casing to the bottom of the specified 
depth was required in order to facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of 
the grouting. Air pressure up to 100 pounds per square inch (psi) was utilized during the 
drilling operation to create a bulb around the drilling bit (4-inch diameter) for advancing 
temporary steel casing (5.5-inch O.D.). 
After the drilled holes were extended to the specified depth and cleaned out, a 
reinforcing bar consisting of Grade 75 all threaded #11 rebar was placed in the cased 
hole with centralizers. Grout was batched on site utilizing a colloidal high-shear and 
high-speed ChemGrout 500H mixer equipped with a grout pump. The grout mix 
consisted of one sack of 94-pound Portland Cement Type I and/or II, 87.4 pounds of 
masonry sand and 5 gallons of water with a design 28-day compressive strength of 
4,000 psi. 
The grout was placed in the cased hole through a 1-inch PVC tremie pipe from 
the bottom of the cased hole. After sound grout appeared at the top of the cased hole, 
the temporary steel casing was removed one section (5-foot long) at a time until the 
bottom of the steel casing approached the specified top of grout bulb zone. 
Production Micropiles 
Structural Systems, Inc. installed a total of six micropiles from March 21 through 
29, 2013 at the approximate locations shown on the Micropile Foundation Plan, Plate 2.  
The micropiles generally extended to a depth of about 64 feet below the existing ground 
surface, or about 23 feet into the hard coral formation. Details of the micropile 
installation are presented in the Micropile Installation Records in the Appendix. 
To facilitate drilling equipment in the low-headroom space, a working pit was 
excavated at each column location to approximately 4.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The pit was also used as a detention basin to contain drilling water and cuttings.  
The permanent steel casing was left in the ground up to about 12 inches above the 
existing ground surface (Elevation +9 feet MSL). Because the design pile cap will be 
constructed at about 4 feet below the existing ground surface (Elevation +4.0 feet MSL), 
about 5 feet of the permanent steel casing will be removed during the footing construction. 
Positive grout in-take was maintained during the grouting operation. No significant 
micropile “necking” was indicated based on review of the micropile grouting records. The 
actual grout volume placed in each micropile generally ranged from about 130 to 
180 percent of the theoretical grout volume.

Micropile Testing 
In order to evaluate the load carrying capacities of the installed micropiles, a pullout 
test (proof test) was conducted on a selected micropile (No. P-5) on March 29, 2013. In 
general, the micropile was loaded to a maximum test load of 120 kips (150 percent of the 
design load of 80 kips) in approximately 20-kip load increments. The test micropile was 
held at each load increment for 5 minutes and were held at the maximum test load of 
120 kips for 10 minutes to evaluate creep effects. At the maximum test load of 120 kips, 
the creep movement between 1 and 10 minutes holding period was about 0.003 inches, 
which is less than 0.04 inches typically allowed for the ground anchor system. 
Based on our field observations and the proof test conducted, it is our 
professional opinion that the specified micropiles for the project have been installed to 
the required diameter and sufficient depth providing the design load capacity of 80 kips 
per micropile with a factor of safety of 2.0.
