REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Regional
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3098 00 Page 5
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is situated on the northern bank of the Nuuanu Stream on the
Honolulu Coastal Plain and on the southern flank of Koolau Its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during
the Pleistocene epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated by interbedded marine
and terrigenous sediments and volcanic deposits The base of the stratigraphic section
encountered in our exploration consists of basaltic lava flow from the Honolulu Volcanic
Series This basalt was deeply scoured by stream erosion at a time when sea level was
as much as 300 feet below its present elevation As the great continental glaciers of the
Ice Age melted and the sea level rose erosion of the basalt ceased Terrigenous and
marine sediments accumulated in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose alluvium Generally the alluvial deposits and volcanic ash
were transported from Nuuanu Valley upstream of the site
Subsequently the alluvium was covered by sandy beach deposits and coralline
reef formation which developed when the sea level rose to an elevation higher than its
present level As continental glaciers formed again sea level dropped the reef and
alluvial deposits were eroded When sea level rose again to its present level the stream
channel was filled with fine grained organic soils deposited in a quiet lagoonal
environment
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Honolulu Coastal Plain to its present form Many of those projects were originally
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3098 00 Page 6
constructed done for agricultural residential and or military development Many of the
resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural loads

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration thick compressible deposits consisting of soft
clayey silt materials were encountered below a surficial fill crust to depths of about 45 to
70 feet below the surface Because of the large structural loads anticipated for this
project a shallow foundation system used to support the proposed office building would
be subjected to appreciable total and differential settlements As a result we recommend
that a deep pile foundation system deriving support principally from end bearing on the
basalt rock stratum be used to support the proposed office building
Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to pile foundation
design site grading and pavement design are further discussed in the following sections
of this report
Pile Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we estimate that
a shallow foundation system bearing on the near surface fill crust would be subjected to
settlements on the order of four inches 4 under the anticipated structural loads
Therefore we recommend that pile foundations deriving support principally from end
bearing on the underlying basalt formation be used to support the proposed structure
Based on the data obtained from our field explorations and laboratory testing piles driven
through the upper fill crust and compressible deposits to a depth of approximately 45

the proposed structure

Downdrag Loads
Where fill is placed over a compressible soil the latter will consolidate and move
downward relative to the pile This downward movement may result in negative
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3098 00 Page 9
skin friction forces generally known as DOWNDRAG forces on the pile shaft The
magnitude of the downdrag force will depend on the thickness and property of the
compressible layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the pile perimeter
area Due to the on going consolidation of the soft compressible soils the
estimated settlements will result in downdrag loading of deep foundations
extending through the thick layer of recent alluvial deposit Therefore the
allowable compressive load capacities of the piles will need to be reduced by the
expected downdrag loads The downdrag load on the pile shaft is estimated to
be about 50 kips for the 16 inch octagonal pile Therefore higher pile capacities
are recommended for the combined downdrag and building load Battered piles
are not recommended because of the additional bending forces imposed on the
piles due to the ground settlements
To reduce the downdrag forces coating of the portion of the pile shaft within the
compressible layer with bitumen coating may be considered Generally the
bitumen coating should only be applied to the portion of the pile which will be
embedded in the negative skin friction zone or compressible soil layer However
due to the anticipated hot temperature and high humidity depending on the time
of the year placement of the bitumen coating may be difficult during pile
installation and therefore is not recommended
Foundation Settlements
Settlements of pile foundations will result from the elastic compression of the pile
member and the response of the pressure bearing basalt rock stratum We
estimate that the total settlement of the pile supported foundations will be less than
3 4 inch with differential settlements between piles not exceeding about one half
that amount We believe these settlements will be essentially elastic and should
occur immediately as the loads are applied

Lateral load imposed on the pile foundations may be resisted by a combination of
1 resistance of the pile soil system to deflect and 2 passive resistance against
the vertical faces of foundation elements such as pile caps The frictional sliding
resistance between the pile caps and the supporting subgrade should be
neglected due to uncertainties regarding long term soil contact
Lateral load resistance for driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection at the top of the pile
and induced moment in the pile The allowable lateral load capacities and

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of 45 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
level control so that driving within the loose compressible soil may be controlled
at a reduced energy to reduce the tensile stress development within the piles
which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data form to Geolabs
Hawaii prior to bidding for evaluation Geolabs Hawaii will review the submittal and
assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer Due to
possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained during
bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four 4 hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop in the pile

Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This can be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the
more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to verify the pile bearing capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the whole pile

Site Preparation

thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation rubbish old pavements

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of two
2 borings to depths of 48 5 and 68 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown
on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
