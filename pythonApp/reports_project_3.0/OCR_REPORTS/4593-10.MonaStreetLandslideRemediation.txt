surface fill and soft to medium stiff younger colluvium deposits over very stiff to hard older
colluvium deposits and weathered basalt rock at greater depth We encountered

instrumentations installed previously we believe that the groundwater encountered in the

Several available remedial methods were considered in the feasibility study for
mitigation of the active ground movement These remedial methods include MSE retaining
structure tieback retaining system deep mix slurry wall jet grouting deep soil mixing and
drilled shaft wall The feasibility study is based on existing ground surface and topographic
conditions existing underground utilities City s right of way property lines construction
experience in Hawaii risk assessment and comparison of approximate construction cost
Based on the feasibility and efficiency studies we recommend using drilled shaft wall to
mitigate the ground movement and to protect the existing underground utilities and private
properties on Mona Street
Based on the results of the on going ground movement monitoring and the slope
stability analysis it is our opinion that the potential slip failure plane may be at depths of
approximately 10 to 15 feet below the ground surface The slope stability analysis
indicated that the factor of safety for slope stability at the site may be greater than 2 0 in
short term while soil cohesion is mobilized However the factor of safety may reduce to
less than 1 5 in long term when soil cohesion is lost due to saturation and soil creep
We recommend installing 42 inch diameter drilled shafts extending to about 50 feet
below the ground surface and spaced at 5 foot center to center We believe that a
minimum of 80 percent of the lateral earth pressure within the gap between the drilled
shafts would be transferred to the drilled shafts by soil arching However considering
long term creep of the cohesive soils we recommend designing the drilled shaft wall to
resist 100 percent of the lateral earth pressure exerted within the gap between the drilled
shafts
We recommend constructing a concrete sidewalk on top of the drilled shafts acting
as a rigid tie beam in order to reduce lateral deflection by utilizing stiffness interaction We
also recommend installing a deep trench drain to minimize the excessive water pressure

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series The project site is at the southwestern flank of the Koolau
Mountain Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys the erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and
are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium
Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes
become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
The project site is near the head of Wailupe Valley which trends roughly north to
south from the Koolau Mountain Range toward the Pacific Ocean as shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 Wailupe Valley is essentially a deep erosional valley

2 4 History of Mona Street Landslide and Remedial Efforts
The subdivision at Mona Street was initially designed and constructed between
1956 and 1958 Signs of ground movement were not noticed until early 1966 as
documented in the report entitled The Hind luka Landslide and Similar Movements
Honolulu Hawaii by Ralph B Peck and Stanley D Wilson dated 1968 Several water
line leakage reports were received by Board of Water Supply BWS A ground
movement monitoring program using survey was then initiated by the City and County
of Honolulu in late 1967 The ground movement accelerated in about March 1968
coinciding with the peaks of accumulated weekly rainfall The rate of the movement was
estimated up to about 2 feet per month
Remedial efforts to prevent water entering the slide areas were recommended
and implemented These efforts included removing sources of infiltration of water or
sewage into the disturbed ground mass installing storm drainage systems and
discharge line into Wailupe Stream repairing water and sewer lines rapidly controlling
house roof run off water and so on However the above remedial efforts did not
eliminate the ground movement The ground movement monitoring program suggested
that creep rate of the ground surface was probably on the order of 0 25 to 0 50 inches
per year
Walter Lum Associates Inc conducted an extensive subsurface investigation
including borings and inclinometers The results of the inclinometer monitoring were
presented in the report entitled Slope Indicator Data Hind luka Slide Wailupe Valley
Honolulu Hawaii dated The monitoring program confirmed the
creep rate of the ground surface estimated by Peck 1968 which was about 0 25 to
0 50 inches per year However the outline of the slide estimated in April 1968 as
shown on Plate 2 was enlarged to extend further north into the valley

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of the field exploration slope stability analysis and on going
instrumentation monitoring program it is our opinion that the slide zone may be at
shallow depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the ground surface Based on the
significant variation of groundwater encountered in the borings and measured by the
instrumentation we believe that excessive pore water pressure from heavy rain may
exert destabilizing forces on the sliding wedge
Several permanent remediation methods were considered and assessed including
mechanically stabilized earth MSE retaining walls tie back retaining system deep mix
slurry wall jet grouting deep soil mixing and drilled shaft wall Based on the site
conditions sliding mechanism and sliding pattern we believe that the drilled shaft wall
may be a feasible and cost effective remedial solution In addition a trench drain would be
installed on Mona Street behind the drilled shaft wall and extended down to a depth of
about 15 feet below the ground surface to intercept and drain subsurface seepage water
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of the drilled shaft wall and
trench drain are presented in the following sections
3 1 Remediation Consideration and Assessment
Several permanent remediation methods were considered and assessed from
feasibility effectiveness and economy aspects
Mechanically Stabilized Earth MSE Retaining Wall
A MSE retaining wall may be installed to support the sliding wedge To provide
sufficient lateral resistance the MSE retaining wall would be approximately 15 to
20 feet deep and a minimum 15 feet wide and backfilled with reinforced granular
fill
However the MSE wall may only provide resistance against a shallow slip failure
no more than 10 to 15 feet deep Therefore it is our opinion that a MSE retaining
wall would be susceptible to deeper slip failure if it occurs In addition the size of

Tieback Retaining System
A tieback retaining system may consist of multiple rows of tieback anchors

The tieback anchor wall would likely extend beyond the City s right of way in order

susceptible to deep slip failure if it occurs
Therefore the tieback retaining system may not be a suitable remediation solution

A deep mix slurry wall may consist of a continuous diaphragm structure wall

encountered in the borings drilled would likely pose difficult excavation

Deep Soil Mixing
Deep soil mixing is a soil improvement technology used to construct cutoff or
retaining walls and to treat soils in place A series of overlapping stabilized soil
columns are formed by advancing a series of mixing shafts into the soil and
pumping grout or slurry through the hollow stem of the shaft at the tip at the
same time The auger flights and mixing blades on the shafts blend the soil with
the grout or slurry
In general the deep soil mixing method has been successfully implemented
under uniform sandy and cohesive ground conditions The subsurface conditions
of the project site consist of abundant cobbles and boulders It would be difficult
to auger through as well as to properly mix grout or slurry with the dense
cobbles and boulders layers Therefore the deep soil mixing method may not be
feasible to provide the desired permanent landslide remediation
Jet Grouting
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with radial
horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil cement columns The jet grouting
technique is a process that produces soil cement columns by pumping neat cement
grout slurry through horizontal jets injected at high pressures The horizontal jets of
cement grout slurry cuts and mixes the surrounding in situ materials with the neat
cement slurry grout as the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a
soil cement column
Given the subsurface conditions encountered we envision the drilling for the jet
grouting column would be difficult because of abundant cobbles and boulders in the
colluvium deposits In addition the silty and clayey soil conditions may cause
significant grout spoil return during pressure jetting operation Containing grout spoil

beyond the depth of a potential sliding surface and into a firm non yielding soil
strata underneath
There are two major advantages of this system First the depths of the drilled
shafts may be deepened to allow sufficient resistance in the event that a somewhat
deep slip plane develops in the future Second the installation of the drilled shafts
will have less impact to the residents as the working area required is less than other
methods
Considering the limits of the City s right of way the existing underground utilities on
Mona Street the relatively well defined sliding limits and geometry and the
successful construction experience of the drilled shaft walls in the State of Hawaii it
is our opinion that drilled shaft walls may provide a reliable permanent remedial
solution for the Mona Street landslide

Lateral Load Resistance
To reduce the excessive movement the drilled shafts should be installed beyond
the depth of the potential sliding surface and into a firm soil layer Similar to a
cantilever beam the earth pressure developed due to a potential sliding soil mass
is transferred to the soil beneath the potential sliding surface
Because the drilled shafts are installed at approximately 5 feet center to center the
lateral earth pressure and excessive groundwater pressure on the soil mass
between the drilled shafts gaps would be re distributed by soil arching as shown
on Plate 10 The soil arching effect results in transferring a portion of the lateral
pressure onto the drilled shafts and reducing the driving force exerted on the soil
mass between the gaps Therefore the effectiveness of using drilled shafts to
stabilize potential landslide is highly dependent on the drilled shafts size spacing
and the soil conditions
As previously mentioned the potential slide surface may be at depths of about
10 to 15 feet below the ground surface Based on the drilled shaft spacing and
diameter ratio of about 1 4 and relatively soft soil conditions within the potential
sliding zone we estimate that approximately 80 percent of the lateral pressure on
the soil mass between the gaps may be transferred onto the drilled shafts due to
the soil arching
In general lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The following
conditions would significantly affect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
The load capacities of drilled shafts depend to a significant extent on the friction
between the shaft and surrounding soils to engage sufficient lateral resistance
Therefore proper construction techniques especially during the drilling operations
and concrete placement are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the drilled holes
Abundant cobbles and boulders in the clayey and silty soil matrix were encountered
in the borings drilled for the project The boulder size varies from 1 foot to greater
than 5 feet in diameter In general the thickness and hardness of the cobbles and
boulders appear to vary significantly across the project site Therefore some
difficult drilling conditions likely will be encountered and should be expected The
drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill
through these types of natural deposits where encountered
The drilled shafts are designed to be embedded into the very stiff to very hard older
colluvium materials encountered in the borings The drilled shaft contractor will
need to demonstrate that the proposed drilling equipment and coring tools where
appropriate will be capable of installing the drilled shafts to the recommended
depths and dimensions by the performance of a trial shaft program
Based on the field explorations soft and or loose younger colluvium deposits may
be present at the project site Due to the soft and or loose consistency of these
materials some caving in and or sloughing of these materials may occur during the

Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing where required

Deep Trench Drain
One of the driving forces causing ground movement is excessive groundwater
pressure Based on the borings drilled for the project groundwater varied
significantly from 12 to 53 feet below the existing ground surface during the drilling
operations
The piezometers installed on Mona Street indicated that the groundwater level
changes significantly at shallow depth during and after rainfall events The
groundwater level could build up to 6 feet below the existing ground surface
measured by the piezometers installed at about 15 foot depth However the
groundwater level is relatively stable at the 20 foot depth measured by the
piezometers installed at about 25 feet depth indicating relatively small influence of
deep water infiltration Therefore intercepting water infiltration in the upper 5 to
15 feet may be effective in reducing ground movement
To reduce water infiltration into the soil mass we recommend installing a trench
drain on Mona Street behind the drilled shaft wall extending to a depth of about
15 feet below the ground surface with a minimum width of about 24 inches The
deep trench drain would serve to intercept and drain the upper subsurface water
from run off infiltration A typical section of deep trench drain is shown on Plate 10
We recommend installing 6 inch diameter perforated subdrain pipes near the
bottom of the trench and backfilling with free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The 6 inch perforated pipe
should be installed properly and connected to adjacent storm drain inlet structures
The free draining granular materials should be wrapped around by the geotextile
filter fabric

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the landslide remediation project site
by drilling and sampling nine borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 each
extending to a depth of about 100 feet below the existing ground surface The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and
coring tools The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our engineer geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by
visual and textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1
through A 9 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Ninteen Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B 1 and B 2
Fourteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plates B 3 through B 5
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression TXUU _ test
ASTM D 2850 was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
relatively undisturbed soil sample The test results and the stress strain curves are
