GEOLOGY
General
Kamanaiki Valley was formed by erosion of the Koolau Volcanic
Range basalts The weathered debris alluvium and colluvium
washed and fell down the slopes to partly fill the valley below
The valley fill consists of older alluvium highly weathered
basalt boulders in dark brown to red brown clayey SILT and younger
alluvial debris fresh to slightly weathered basalt boulders in
black to gray Adobe CLAYS

Specific
The site can be divided for discussion into three main topo
graphic and roughly corresponding geologic types 1 Alluvium
and colluvium partly reworked by stream action slopes approximately
to boulders and silts and clays slopes
approximately 25 to 40 3 Basalt bedrock and talus covered
basalt bedrock slopes approximately 35 to vertical to depths
of a few tens of feet
1 The alluvial and colluvial Stream channel and valley
bottom forming materials consisted at the surface
of basalt boulders and clay Boulders as large as
approximately 500 cubic feet were observed The
average boulder size appears to be about 10 cubic
feet Point to point contact of the boulders is
common in this material
2 The colluvial material appeared to consist at the
surface of boulders and Adobe CLAY Much of the
ground appeared to be hummocky The surface of this
material is often grass covered with swales in the
surface covered by trees The subsurface geology of
these areas is not known but it may consist of recent
alluvium overlying older alluvium to unknown depths

CONCLUSIONS BASED ON PRELIMINARY STUDY
a
Site development may be costly The presence of basalt
bedrock outcrops at the end of the bridge crossing the
stream may not be rippable and may require blasting to
make room for the proposed road
The abundance of large boulders will probably necessitate
the use of heavy construction equipment to level the site
These boulders in the subsurface will probably make exca
vation for utilities difficult and expensive
The flatter portions of the property alluvial and colluvial
materials in the valley bottom can probably be developed
The area containing a high proportion of talus and colluvial
material property mauka of proposed Haaliki Bridge will
require a more detailed investigation to determine its
suitability for development

RECOMMENDATIONS
A second phase of exploration is needed to provide engineering
data for construction This exploration should consist of
1 Several 15 to 20 foot deep borings in the flatter
portions of the property makai of the proposed Haaliki
Bridge to determine subsurface conditions and the
Suitability for development
2 Three or four deep borings to 35 feet deep dependent
upon preliminary boring results be made on the sloping
ground mauka of the proposed bridge to determine soil
types and possible stability problems
3 Further geologic mapping should be performed after the
Site has been cleared of brush and trees
