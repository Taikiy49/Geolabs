Low Strain Impact Testing Report  
Existing Concrete Pile 
Maunalua Bay Boat Ramp Loading Dock Repair 
Hawaii Kai, Oahu, Hawaii 
Dear Mr. Ball: 
As requested, we performed Low Strain Impact (LSI) Tests on an existing concrete 
pile at the Maunalua Bay Boat Ramp. Based on our field observations and the LSI testing 
results, we provide the following findings and conclusions for your review and 
consideration. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves repair of the existing boat ramp dock at Maunalua Bay in the 
Hawaii Kai area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. The existing boat ramp dock was supported on a single 12 x 12-inch precast 
concrete pile. The dock collapsed previously due to broken struts. It is desired to evaluate 
the existing concrete pile integrity and bearing capacity in support of the new concrete 
dock foundation design. 
The Low Strain Impact (LSI) testing was performed on the existing concrete pile 
on July 15, 2019. The available as-built drawings of the concrete pile and photos of the 
LSI testing performed are presented on Plate 2. 
LOW STRAIN IMPACT (LSI) TESTING 
In general, the LSI testing was conducted in accordance with ASTM D5882 
utilizing a Pulse Echo Tester (PET) manufactured by PileTest. The PET system provides 
an analog to digital resolution of at least 24 bits with a sampling frequency exceeding 
50,000 Hz and data storage capacity. The PET system also provides a display to view 
the wave transmitting in the field for assessment of data quality and interpretation.

The top of the concrete pile was prepared by grinding and smoothening the testing 
areas for proper instrumentation, as shown in Photograph - 1 of Plate 2. A special 
handheld hammer was used to generate energy impact at the top of the concrete pile. An 
accelerometer was attached to the top of the concrete pile with coupling putty for 
recording the compression strain wave acceleration generated by the hammer. The 
testing setup is generally illustrated in Photograph - 2 of Plate 2. Each LSI test consisted 
of striking the top of concrete pile 20 to 30 times until multiple consistent hammer impact 
was recorded. Responses of these hammer impact were then averaged and stored by 
the PET system. 
The LSI velocity records obtained for the concrete pile are presented on Plates 3.1 
through 3.4. The LSI velocity records were integrated, filtered and magnified to enable 
interpretation of the data along the concrete pile depth. The data were reviewed for 
responses that may be a result of the reflection from the tip of the concrete pile or 
reflections that may have occurred at an anomaly or broken part above the tip of the 
concrete pile. Where the pile tip is typically in a material that is less dense than concrete, 
a downward reflection is generally presented at the pile tip in the same direction and the 
impact indicates a decrease in impedance. An upward tip reflection in the opposite 
direction as the impact indicates an increase in impedance, typically indicates the 
concrete pile tip is on the materials that are denser than concrete. An early downward 
reflection in the same direction as the impact pulse suggests a reduction in concrete pile 
impedance (area and/or modulus) or soil friction. An early upward reflection opposite of 
the impact pulse suggests an increase in concrete pile impedance (area and/or modulus) 
or soil friction. In general, the LSI testing method is effective to the maximum ratio of pile 
length to diameter (L/D) up to about 30 to 40. Reliable test data at these larger ratios may 
be obtained depending on the soil layering and foundation geometry. 
FINDINGS AND CONCLUSION 
Early reflection of the curve indicates the pile entering the soil layer below the 
water, coinciding with the upward reflection observed in the velocity curve at a depth of 
about 6 feet which indicates the waves entered an area with more friction or an increase 
in cross-sectional area. We believe that this reflection is induced due to the change in the 
surrounding material of the pile from water to the soil. Consistent downward reflections at 
a depth of about 35 feet were observed during the testing. 
Based on the results of the LSI testing conducted, we believe that the existing 
concrete pile is located at the depth of about 35 feet, equivalent to an elevation of about 
-27 feet below sea level. Based on the consistent reflection responses, we believe that 
the existing concrete pile is in relatively good condition, providing sufficient bearing 
capacity to support the planned boat ramp loading dock similar to the original concrete 
dock.
