SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings at the project site encountered various subsurface conditions generally 
consisting of residual soils, beach deposits, and alluvial deposits.  Boring Nos. 1 and 4 
encountered residual soils consisting of stiff to hard silty/sandy clay and very dense cobbles 
and boulders extending to about 21.5 feet in depth.  A surface fill layer consisting of very 
stiff silty clay with some gravel was encountered in Boring No. 1, extending to about 4 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
Boring Nos. 2 and 3 encountered a surface fill layer about 1.5 to 4 feet thick consisting 
of medium dense silty sand underlain by very loose to dense beach deposit and coralline 
detritus consisting of sand and silty sand/gravel extending to about 21.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. An alluvial deposit was encountered below the beach deposit in 
Boring No. 3. 
Boring No. 5 encountered very soft to medium stiff and very loose to loose alluvial 
deposits extending to about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The alluvium 
consisted of silty clay, sand, and silty sand.  
We encountered groundwater in three of the drilled borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 2, 3, and 5, at depths of about 6.5 and 7.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The groundwater levels encountered correspond to elevations from about 0.5 to 
10.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The groundwater levels in the beach and alluvial deposits 
were 0.5 and 1.5, and 10.5 feet MSL, respectively. The high groundwater level encountered 
in Boring No. 5 appears to be associated with the artesian conditions previously 
encountered by others at the Kekupua Fishpond. It should be noted that groundwater levels 
are subject to change due to tides, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water 
runoff, and other factors. 
Based on the geologic map, the majority of the site is underlain by recent alluvial 
deposits.  These very soft/very loose deposits are highly compressible when load is applied 
onto the deposits.  Should fill be placed in the area underlain by very soft/very loose alluvial 
deposits, we recommend that a settlement monitoring program be implemented. 
It should be noted that the on-site clayey fill and alluvial soils exhibit a slight to 
moderate shrink/swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations.  Therefore, special 
attention should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade soil preparation 
below the slabs and pavements in order to reduce the potential shrink/swell effects of the 
surface clayey soils.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is a dissected basaltic dome that was initially formed by a 
single large shield volcano, which has subsequently been deeply eroded and partially 
covered with later (younger) volcanic materials. The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the 
main Hawaiian Islands and today encompasses approximately 562 square miles in area 
with a maximum elevation of about 5,148 feet above mean sea level at Mt. Wai’ale’ale 
near the center of the island. It is believed that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 
6 million years ago in the Pliocene Epoch), basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea 
Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly circular island volcano from the ocean floor that 
was estimated to have been about 20,000 feet deep at the time. Near the end of the 
Pliocene Epoch, this main shield building volcanic phase came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was 
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the 
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This 
post-erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic 
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Because of 
the long period of erosion between the eruption of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series 
and the Koloa Volcanic Series, deposits of the Koloa Volcanic Series typically filled the 
erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that time. Consequently, the basaltic 
rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are found commonly overlying thick residual soil and 
alluvial deposits. 
The dominant geologic processes of weathering, landform erosion, and sediment 
re-deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs. Combined with 
the erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea-level fluctuations, which 
occurred in response to the worldwide advance and retreat of the continental ice sheets, 
the Island of Kauai has evolved to its present form. 
The project site is situated on a low-lying coastal delta, which exhibits a 
composition predominantly comprised of man-made fills, recent alluvial materials

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily 
to volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs 
before or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive 
instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and 
a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes 
on the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high 
seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance 
from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous 
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not 
confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the 
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, several 
small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake 
of 1938 (M6.8). In addition, some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly 
damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). However, the effects 
normally are minor when the seismic waves travel to the Island of Kauai. Seismic 
hazards on the Island of Kauai generally are considered to be low.

2.4.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the IBC 2018, the project site may be subjected to seismic activity, and the 
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be evaluated. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build‐up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume, causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil 
shear strength to near zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and vertical 
movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

the project constraints. Construction should be delayed until the settlement monitoring 
program has been completed.

we believe that cut slopes will expose stiff clayey soils and medium dense sands.  In 
general, the cut slopes exposing clayey soils and sands may be designed with a slope 
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter, and 4H:1V or flatter, 
respectively.  
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the approved on-site materials (or 
imported select granular fill materials) may also be designed with a slope inclination of 
2H:1V or flatter. Should the on-site sands be used for the permanent fill slopes, the fill 
slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 4H:1V or flatter.  Fills placed on 
slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest point 
and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill 
placement recommendations.  Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting 
back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face.  In addition, slope 
planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the 
finished slopes. 
3.2 
Foundations  
In general, we believe that lightly-loaded one to two-story structures may be 
supported on a shallow foundation support system bearing on the recompacted on-site 
soils or new compacted fills placed to achieve the finished grades at the site.  Based on 
the stiff and medium dense soils encountered at the residual soil and beach deposit areas, 
we believe that shallow foundations consisting of reinforced concrete slabs-on-grade with 
thickened-edge slab footings may be used to support the lightly loaded one to two-story 
structures.  For the structures located above the soft recent alluvium area, it should be 
emphasized that building construction should not commence until the settlement waiting 
period has been completed or the rate of settlement has stabilized based on the results 
of the settlement monitoring program. 
A preliminary allowable bearing pressure on the order of about 3,000 and 
1,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the conceptual design of shallow 
foundations bearing on the recompacted on-site residual and beach deposits, and soft

3.3 
Slabs-on-Grade 
As indicated previously, the site generally is underlain by a surface layer of slightly 
to moderately expansive clayey soils. To reduce the potential for structural distress 
resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend that the subgrade soils be 
properly prepared prior to fill placement. In addition, a minimum of 12 to 24 inches of 
non-expansive, select granular fill material should be provided below the building slab 
areas.  The non-expansive, select granular fill also should extend beyond the perimeter 
of the building a minimum of 24 inches (or extend to the edge of the concrete sidewalk 
surrounding the building, if applicable). 
For both expansive soil considerations, we recommend that the concrete 
slabs-on-grade be at least 5 inches thick (in lieu of the traditional 4 inches thick) and 
reinforced with No. 4 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center each way (in-lieu of 
the standard welded wire fabric).  This recommendation is provided to reduce the 
potential for significant structural distress resulting from differential movements of the 
slabs due to the nature of the on-site soils.  We also believe that the potential for

3.4 
Pavements 
We anticipate that flexible pavements will be used to serve the new development.  
In general, we have considered only light vehicle loading for the roadways of this 
development consisting primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick-up trucks with less 
than 1 million equivalent single axle loads over the 40-year design life of the pavement.  
Based on the above assumptions, we recommend the following flexible pavement section 
be used for planning purposes: 
Flexible Pavements (Low-Volume Roads)

The subgrades below the aggregate subbase materials should be scarified to a 
depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base course 
and aggregate subbase materials (where required) should consist of crushed basaltic 
aggregates compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction. California Bearing 
Ratio tests should be performed during detailed design to confirm the adequacy of the 
above pavement structural section. 
3.5 
Surface Drainage 
In general, paved areas should be adequately sloped, and drainage gradients 
should be maintained to carry the surface water off the site.  Surface water ponding should 
not be allowed on the site during or after construction.  Where concrete curbs are used 
to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend that the curbs 
extend a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base course and 
aggregate subbase layers to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape 
water into the pavement section.

3.6 
Underground Utility Lines 
New utility lines and utility connections, such as drain lines, sewer lines, and water 
lines, will be required for the development of the site.  Because of the presence of 
compressible alluvial deposits at the site, the costs for the installation of gravity utility lines 
will be more costly than typical conditions.  Drainage structures, such as box culverts and 
major drain lines, to be installed in the areas underlain by soft soils likely will need to be 
supported on a deep foundation system.  Due to the stringent grade requirements for gravity 
sewer lines, gravity sewer lines likely will need to be supported on a deep foundation system. 
The deep foundation system may consist of driven piles or micropiles installed 
through the soft alluvial deposit and embedded within the stiff underlying soils.  The pile 
foundations would derive support principally from side resistance between the sides of the 
pile and the subsoil deposits encountered in the borings drilled.  Alternatively, the use of a 
low-pressure sewer system may be considered for the development, if acceptable to the 
agencies. Installation of the low-pressure sewer system would not require a deep foundation 
system for support of the sewer lines. 
For the other utility lines not supported on a deep foundation system, we recommend 
that flexible pipes, such as polyvinyl chloride and high density polyethyline pipes be utilized.  
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) may be used under the pipes in firm and/or dense ground conditions.  If 
soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the inverts of the pipes (not supported on 
a deep foundations system), we recommend that an additional 18 to 24 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation or No. 67 gradation) wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) be provided below the bedding layer for 
uniform support. 
Because of the relatively low existing ground elevations and the high groundwater 
levels encountered in some of the borings drilled, the utility line trenches may extend

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths between about 
18 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock Cores are described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-4 through B-6. 
Four Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on a selected 
in-situ sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site soil. The 
test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
stress-strain curve of the unconfined compression tests is presented on Plates B-7 
through B-10. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-11 through B-13.
