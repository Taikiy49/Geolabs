PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The bridge repair project is at the Wailua River Bridge located along Kuhio Highway on 
the Island of Kauai, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. We understand that 
an assessment of the bridge foundations was conducted after the heavy rain events that occurred 
on March 17 and 29, 2020. Based on the assessment, it was determined that the streambed 
elevation was below the critical scour level; therefore, an emergency bridge closure was 
implemented on April 13, 2020. We understand that the interim repairs were conducted by 
placing the grout bags around the bridge pier footings, minimizing further streambed scouring, 
while the permanent bridge design was in the process of finalizing. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the bridge repair will consist of 
keeping the existing bridge deck and abutments and replacing the seven existing piers and the 
pile foundations with new piers/foundations. The new piers/foundations will consist of 6-foot 
diameter cast-in-place drilled shafts. We understand that the new bridge foundations will need 
to consider scour in the foundation design. Based on the information provided, the estimated 
scour elevation is about -26 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The bridge foundations will be designed 
based on the Load Resistance Factor Design (LRFD) method. 
Since the existing bridge deck will remain in place, temporary support/shoring of the 
bridge will be required when the piers are demolished during construction. In addition,

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the extrusion of 
lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch (more than 21/4 
million years before present). Following the cessation of this main shield building phase, renewed 
volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic 
Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic 
Series. These lavas consist of four distinct formations:  Napali, Olokele, Haupu, and Makaweli. 
These formations are comprised of thin-bedded a`a and pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows 
that ponded in calderas and graben. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai. 
These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded from groups of 
vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the vents are pyroclastic 
materials, which usually form low cinder cones at the vent.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental glaciers 
accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to fill the oceanic basins. 
Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose because more water was 
available. The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes and the

sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous 
sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher 
elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. 
The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series is generally 
characterized by flows of joint dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers. The 
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth. In 
general, saprolite is mainly composed of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of 
volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to a basaltic rock formation with depth. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions across the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 91.5 to 181.5 
feet below the mudline. 
In general, our boring exploration generally encountered a beach deposit layer of about 
4.5 to 23 feet thick, consisting of loose to medium dense sands. The beach deposit was underlain 
by an estuarine deposit with thickness varying between about 24 and 101.5 feet, consisting of 
very soft to soft sandy silt and/or clayey silt soils. The soft estuarine deposit was overlying 
medium stiff recent alluvium and/or weathered basalt rock, extending to the maximum depth 
drilled of about 181.5 feet below the mudline. Detailed thickness of each soil deposit and/or rock 
formation is summarized in the table below for ease of reference. 
It should be noted that a concrete lining about 1.5 and 3.5 feet thick was encountered in 
Boring Nos. 1 and 5 at depths of about 2.5 to 3 feet below the mudline. Based on the information 
provided, we understand this concrete lining was installed previously around the existing Pier 2 
footing using cement bags as part of the scour mitigation efforts. 
Basalt rock formation was encountered in Boring Nos. 1 and 5 at depths of about 28.5 to 
42.5 feet below the mudline, equivalent to elevations of about -35 to -49.5 feet MSL, extending 
to the bottom of the borehole drilled. The rock formation encountered in Boring Nos. 1 and 5 
consisted of closely to severely fractured, moderately weathered, and very hard basalt 
interbedded with highly weathered soft volcanic breccia. The top of the rock ledge inclines in a 
slope inclination of approximately 1.8H:1H between Boring Nos. 1 to 5. 
The rock formation was also encountered in Boring No. 2 at a depth of about 124.5 feet 
below the mudline, equivalent to an elevation of about -127 feet MSL, consisting of highly to 
completely weathered volcanic breccia rock. This indicated the rock ledge inclining rapidly from 
-49.5 to -127 feet within the horizontal distance of about 60 feet, in a slope inclination of 
approximately 0.77H:1V (steeper than 1H:1V). The rock formation was not encountered in Boring

SOIL BORINGS, ROCK CORING, AND ASSOCIATED FIELD TESTING 
The field exploration is described in more detail in the introduction to Appendix A. The 
following discussion provides an overview of the methods and procedures. Typically, the soil 
borings were advanced to the PQ-size casing through the existing 8-inch thick concrete deck into 
the subsurface materials below the mudline. In general, the wash boring method was utilized in 
areas where soil deposits exist, and the wireline casing method was switched over when hard 
clays, boulders, and/or rock formation were encountered. Sampling of the soils was performed 
using AWJ drill rods (1.75 OD/1.25 ID) at about 5-foot intervals. Drive samples were obtained 
using the modified California sampler (ASTM D3550) and the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) 
split-spoon sampler (ASTM D1586). 
The size of the borehole is on the order of about 6 inches in diameter, including an outer 
casing. The boreholes extended to depths of about 91.5 to 181.5 feet below the existing mudline, 
which is at about 114.5 to 203 feet below the bridge deck. Typically, soil borings were advanced with 
PQ-size wireline coring methods to advance the PQ-size casing. Sampling of the soils was performed 
using the modified California split-barrel sampler (ASTM D3550) and the split-spoon sampler 
(ASTM D1586). In the softer soils, “relatively undisturbed” samples were obtained for testing by 
driving a ring-lined modified California split-barrel sampler (ASTM D3550) into the soil formation. 
Where the borehole was advanced utilizing the PQ core barrel in the stiffer soil formations, 
samples of the soils recovered from the PQ core barrel were retained in core boxes for visual 
observation. For intervals where the borehole was advanced utilizing the PQ casing as a rotary wash 
drilling method, there were no soil samples collected. The borings were advanced into the rock

In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the SPT, which was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. Standard Penetration Test (SPT) sampling provides 
a measure of the resistance or consistency of the soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-value, 
which is defined as the number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon sampler 
12 inches with a 140-lb. hammer, after 6 inches of initial seating. The penetration resistance of 
the Modified California Sampler was recorded in a similar manner, and the Modified California 
Sampler blows per 12 inches can be approximately related to the SPT N-value through 
correlation.  
Rock core recovery (REC) and rock quality designation (RQD) were measured as described 
in Appendix A. ASTM D6032 – Standard Test Method for Determining Rock Quality 
Designation (RQD) of Rock Core states that “Core sizes from BQ to PQ with core diameters of 
36.5 mm (1.44 in.) to 85 mm (3.35 in.), respectively, are normally acceptable for measuring RQD 
as long as proper drilling techniques used do not cause excessive core breakage or poor recovery, 
or both. The NX-size (54.7 mm [2.16 in.]) and NQ-size (47.5 mm [1.87 in.]) are the optimal core 
sizes for measuring RQD. The RQD is also useful for large core diameters provided the core 
diameter is clearly stated.” The rock core photographs are included in Appendix C for information 
and reference. 
GEOTECHNICAL LABORATORY TESTING 
Geotechnical laboratory testing was performed on soil and rock core samples to 
determine the physical and engineering properties. Laboratory tests performed included 
moisture content, unit weight, Atterberg Limits, grain size distribution, uniaxial compressive 
strength of rock, Unconsolidated-Undrained (UU) Triaxial Compression, Consolidation, Soil 
Resistivity, and Chemical Analyses (pH, chloride content, and sulfate content) tests. The 
laboratory test results are provided in Appendix B.

Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at selected locations across the project site by 
drilling and sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of 
about 91.5 to 181.5 feet below the mudline. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The soil borings were advanced to the PQ-size casing through the existing 8-inch thick 
concrete deck into the subsurface materials below the mudline. In general, the wash boring 
method was utilized in areas where soil deposits exist, and the wireline casing method was 
switched over when hard clays, boulders, and/or rock formation were encountered. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the 
laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for 
Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on 
the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered and other observations, such as drilling rate, etc., are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-5.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Standard Practice for Thick Wall, Ring-Lined, Spilt Barrel, Drive 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer 
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer 
and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved 
in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by using 
diamond core drilling techniques, consisting of a PQ wireline coring system with double tube core 
barrel (core size of 3.35 inches), in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling 
for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the

Recovery (REC) is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring attempt 
versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings 
as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is a guide to the relative quality of rock masses. RQD 
is defined as the percentage of the total core run in rock that is sound material in excess of 
4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, mechanical, and handling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in 
rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%.

PRIMARY constituents are based on plasticity. Primary

SECONDARY constituents are composed of a
percentage less than the primary constituent. If the soil
mass consists of 12 percent or more fines content, a
cohesive constituent is used (SILTY or CLAYEY);
otherwise, a granular constituent is used (GRAVELLY
or SANDY) provided that the secondary constituent
consists of 20 percent or more of the soil mass.
Secondary constituents are capitalized and bold (i.e.,
SANDY GRAVEL, CLAYEY SAND) and precede the
primary constituent.

constituent.

Geotechnical Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for the project were 
visually examined and classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected samples were 
tested to evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils 
encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included determination of natural moisture content, dry 
density, Atterberg Limits, sieve analysis, uniaxial compressive strength for rock cores, triaxial 
compression, consolidation, and corrosivity tests. Detailed descriptions of the various tests 
performed for this exploration are presented below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid 
in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between test borings. The moisture content and unit weight tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D2216 and ASTM D7263, respectively. The test results are presented on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
Fourteen Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an aid in classifying the soils. The 
Atterberg Limits tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D4318. The test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations 
of the test results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, nine 
Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the clayey/silty soils, nineteen 
Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected 
relatively undisturbed soil samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-5 through B-23.

Consolidation 
Five Consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
compressibility characteristics of the on-site soils. The tests were performed in accordance with 
ASTM D2435, and the test results are presented on Plates B-24 through B-28. 
Uniaxial Compressive Strength – Rock Cores 
Twelve Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests were performed on selected rock cores to 
evaluate their uniaxial compressive strength of the rock core in general accordance with 
ASTM D7012, Method C. Uniaxial compressive strength test results for the selected rock cores 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and also presented on 
Plate B-29. 
Soil Resistivity and Chemical Analyses 
Two sets of soil corrosivity tests, including minimum resistivity (ASTM G57), pH 
(ASTM G51), chloride (EPA 300), and sulfate (EPA 300) content tests were performed by our office 
or Eurofins TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test 
results are presented on Plate B-30.
