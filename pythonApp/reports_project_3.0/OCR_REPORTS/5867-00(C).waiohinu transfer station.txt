1.1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed County of Hawaii Transfer Station Replacements, Waiohinu
Transfer Station project in Waiohinu on the island of Hawaii. The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations,
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, wastewater systems, drainage and
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report.

the County’s increased recycling and waster diversion goals and improve the overall
efficiency of the solid waste management system. The first phase of this replacement or
renovation program consists of four stations:

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 10,360 square kilometers (4,000 square miles). The island was
formed by the activity of the following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct),
Mauna Kea (activity during recent geologic times), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 -
1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active).
The site is situated on the southern flank of Mauna Loa on rocks of the
Prehistoric Member of the Kau Volcanic Series. In some areas, these rocks are typified
by relatively thin-bedded pahoehoe basalt lava flows with a surface mantle of soft, wet
silty soil (Pahala Ash). The ash is characterized as having very high in-situ moisture
contents and is usually highly compressible. The ash also frequently exhibits thixotropic
properties, i.e., the material loses strength when disturbed by remolding or when
subjected to earthquake vibrations. However, this ash material was not encountered in
our exploration at the site.
The U.S. Department of Agriculture classified the surface soils in the area as
Punaluu extremely rocky peat (rPYD) on 6 to 20 percent slopes. This soil is found at
lower elevations on the leeward side of Mauna Loa; and, consists of thin organic soils
overlying pahoehoe basalt. Typically, 40 to 50 percent of the ground surface is rock
outcrops.
Typically, the surface soil layer is black organic material and averages only a few
inches in thickness. The soil is extremely permeable and the underlying rock formation
has much lower permeability.

3.1. Building Foundations
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration, we believe that shallow
spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support new building structures at
the site. An allowable bearing pressure of 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be
used for the design of foundations bearing on compacted subgrade prepared in
accordance with recommendations presented herein.
The bottom of footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction (omitted for rock formation subgrade) prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at
the bottom of the footing excavations should be over-excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials. The over-excavated area may be backfilled with new fill
material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the bottom of
footing may be extended down to bear on competent materials at a greater depth.
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades. Retaining wall foundations should bear on in-situ basalt rock
formation. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes, or on sloping ground, should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 8 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope. Footings
adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to
avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the planned retaining walls should
be designed to resist the additional structural loads.
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent below-grade utility lines or structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement.
Lateral loads acting on building structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth

3.2. Foundation Probing
Small voids and/or cavities were encountered only in Boring No. 1 during our
exploration. However, based on our experience in the area, there is a potential for
presence of lava tubes, blisters and/or cavities in the basalt formations. In order to
reduce the potential for structural damage due to collapse of voids/cavities, a probing
and grouting program is often conducted below structures. However, due to the
relatively light structures anticipated for this project, we believe that the potential for loss
of foundation support resulting form collapse of the cavities below the foundation is
relatively small and the probing and grouting program may be omitted if this small risk is
tolerable. However, if it is desired to further reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support, then a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations
should be conducted during construction.

3.3.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Soft and/or
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to
receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or
organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas,
where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in
the over-excavation quantities.
After clearing and grubbing, subgrades, finished subgrades, and areas designated
to receive fills should then be scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.

3.3.4 Excavation
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by shallow
surface fills with basalt formation encountered at depths ranging from about 1.5 feet
to about 11 feet below the existing ground surface. We anticipate that the
near-surface materials may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation
equipment, such as backhoes, and ripped with large bulldozers. However,

hoerams, chipping, or blasting, if permitted.
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based

the borings drilled and sampled at the site. Contractors should be encouraged to
examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation.
3.3.5 Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe
that cut slopes for this project will expose competent granular materials or rock
formation. In general, the cut slopes planned may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill
materials) may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well-compacted slope face. In addition, slope planting should be provided as soon
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic, such as trucks and/or
forklifts, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs
in lieu of the 4-inch cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
For slabs that will be subjected to heavy equipment traffic, the structural engineer
should design the concrete slab for the intended loads. For design of structural slabs, a
modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per cubic inch (pci) may be used for the
compacted base course. Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the
slab joints that are subjected to vehicular traffic.

3.5.3 Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Because the project site is on the seismically active Island of Hawaii
(Seismic Zone 4 in accordance with the 1997 Uniform Building Code), forces due to
dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining
structures. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures (Amax=0.40g) may be
estimated using 16H? pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions,
where H is the height of the wall in feet. For a maximum 3H:1V sloping backfill
condition, dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated
using 28H“ pounds per lineal foot of wall.
It should be noted that the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures provided
assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally in the event of an earthquake
by up to about 2 to 4 inches. If this lateral movement amount is not acceptable, the
retaining wall should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a
semi-restrained wall condition. For a semi-restrained wall condition, the forces due
to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be estimated using 28H? pounds per lineal
foot of wall for level backfill conditions.
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the
mid-height of the wall. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures. An appropriately reduced
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted
for in the design of the retaining structure.

3.5.4 Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up of
hydrostatic pressures.
For site retaining walls, the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic foot of
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),
wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of the weephole locations. The
weepholes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. As an alternative, a
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used
instead of the permeable drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining wall.
Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material (well-graded materials) to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure.
3.5.5 Backfill Materials
The backfill material for the retaining walls should consist of on-site materials or
imported select granular materials. The material should be well graded from coarse
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material
should also contain less than 15 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The
material should have a CBR value of 25 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.

should be confirmed during construction.
3.8 Drainage
As previously indicated, proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations and slabs. Therefore, the finished grades outside the structure should be
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding.
It is also advised to install gutter systems around the building and to divert the discharge
away from the foundation and pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided.
The foundation excavations should be backfilled properly against the walls or
slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water
infiltration. In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from foundations and slabs.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling seven borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of approximately 11.0 to
31.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with rotary
coring tools.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7.
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method, which uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. If 3.7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5.0-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals."

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The tests results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a
bulk sample of in-situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils.
The test result is presented on Plate B-1.
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils, one
Unconfined Compression test was performed on a selected in-situ sample in
accordance with ASTM D 2166. The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depth and the stress-strain curve of the unconfined compression
test is presented on Plate B-2.
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D 2938) was performed on a selected
rock core to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation
encountered. Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plate B-3.
One set pH (EPA SW9045B) test and one Resistivity (EPA 120.1) test were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results
are summarized on Plate B-4.

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the on-site soils was evaluated by performing two falling
head permeability tests at depths of about 4.5 and 5 feet below the existing ground
surface. The field permeability test locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
During the falling head tests, the boreholes were carefully filled with water. The
time intervals and water drops in the boreholes were then recorded to provide data
upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface soils. The
test results are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
