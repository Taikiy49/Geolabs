This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Architectural Barrier Removal project at Ernest B De Silva Elementary
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Ernest B De Silva Elementary School
in the Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct various improvements at
the project site as part of the architectural barrier removal project The improvements include
new accessible walkways storage sheds a covered bus shelter a bus turn around extension
to the existing asphalt concrete parking area and other minor structures

locations within the school site A golf cart storage shed will be located to the west of the

around area The proposed improvements are shown approximately on the Site Plan
Plate 2 In general we anticipate that the site grading work will consist of minor cuts and fills
on the order of about 3 feet or less to achieve the design finished grades

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These flows are of the Pliocene to
Pleistocene Epochs and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series The
lava flows were subsequently covered by volcanic ash deposits known locally as Pahala
Ash
The lava formation encountered at the project site appears to be of pahoehoe flow
which is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of approximately 10 to 10 9 feet

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface layer of volcanic ash material
with variable thickness consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey silts with some basalt gravel
overlying soft to medium hard basalt rock formation The volcanic ash materials exist in a
relatively high moisture condition with moisture contents on the order of about 80 to
100 percent The volcanic ash deposits were underlain by highly to moderately weathered
basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depths explored In some areas of the site
the volcanic ash layer was absent in the borings Groundwater was not encountered in the
borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on our field exploration we anticipate that a majority of the subgrades for the
covered walkways covered bus shelter and golf cart paths will likely expose volcanic ash
materials Therefore we recommend that a minimum 12 inch thick layer of select borrow
subbase be provided below the footings and slabs on grade for the proposed covered
walkways bus shelter and golf cart paths to provide more uniform support and to act as a
protective cover or a working platform Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in
the vicinity of the covered walkways and bus shelter we believe that a shallow foundation
system consisting of slabs on grade with thickened edges bearing on the 12 inch thick layer
of select borrow subbase may be used to support the walkways and bus shelter
In contrast we anticipate that the subgrades for the golf cart storage and storage
sheds will likely encounter basalt rock subgrades Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered a shallow foundation system bearing on the basalt rock may be used to support
the storage and golf cart storage sheds We envision that concrete slabs on grade with
thickened edge footings will be used for the proposed sheds We understand that a layer
of basaltic termite barrier BTB will be required below the slabs for the proposed sheds
Therefore we recommend that a minimum 6 inch thick select borrow subbase layer be
provided below the BTB layer to reduce the potential for migration of the finer grained BTB
material into the fractures that may be present within the basalt rock at the slab subgrade
level

Covered Walkways Covered Bus Shelter and Golf Cart Paths
Our borings drilled in the vicinity of the covered walkways bus shelter and golf cart
paths encountered a surface layer of volcanic ash material of about 3 5 feet thick underlain
by basalt rock formation Due to the open nature of the covered walkways and bus shelter
these structures will be subjected to high uplift forces and overturning moments caused by
wind Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the vicinity of the covered
walkways and bus shelter the covered walkways and bus shelter may resist the uplift forces
by the dead weight of the structure and the foundation elements The foundation elements
of the covered walkways and bus shelter should act as an integral unit to resist the uplift
forces Therefore we recommend that the covered walkways and bus shelter be supported
by concrete slabs on grade with thickened edge footings The golf cart paths should also
consist of concrete slabs on grade with thickened edge footings
To provide more uniform support above the volcanic ash materials we recommend
that a minimum 12 inch thick layer of select borrow subbase be provided below the concrete
slabs on grade with thickened edge footings The select borrow subbase would act as a
protective cover or serve as a dry working platform over the high moisture volcanic ash soils
In general the select borrow subbase should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction If the area is subjected to vehicular traffic such as the golf cart paths
the compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography we anticipate that site grading work will consist only
of minor cuts and fills on the order of about 3 feet or less to achieve the design finished
grades A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe grading and earthwork
operations It is important that a representative from our office be present to observe the site
preparation to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation
process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in

At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered
during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with
compacted select granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed

Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed with a slope
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to
provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill
placement recommendations presented above Surface water should be diverted away from
the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the cut and fill slopes
are completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site consists of a thin layer of volcanic
ash material underlain by basalt rock formation It is anticipated that the volcanic rock may
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers
where the rock is near the existing ground surface However excavations into the deeper
formations may require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on
our visual observation of the existing rock formations and field data from the borings drilled
at the site We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this project be encouraged
to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 10 to
10 9 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the basalt rock formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as REC 80
Appendix A continued
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a high
RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate B 3

on the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soil The
results of the test are presented on Plate B 4

near surface soils to determine the dry density and moisture content relationship Results
of the test are presented on Plate B 5
