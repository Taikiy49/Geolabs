INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soil investigation for
the proposed retaining wall at Highlands Intermediate School
in Pearl City Oahu Hawaii
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed retaining wall will be of cement rubble masonry
or reinforced concrete construction The height of the wall
will vary from about 5 feet on the makai side stepping up to
about 10 feet at the intersection of Hoolaulea and Hoomaemae
Streets The wall will be of L shaped cantilever design
with vertical or battered walls

SITE DESCRIPTION
General
The site is located on the Diamond Head side of the existing
school An existing slope varying from 3 to 10 feet in height
is located adjacent to the sidewalk and will be contained by
the proposed wall
To explore the subsurface conditions at the site three borings
were drilled at the locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
Samples of the soil were obtained for examination classifica
tion and laboratory testing A more detailed description of
the field exploration and laboratory testing is contained in
the Appendix which also includes the log of each boring and
the results of our laboratory tests
Geology
The site is underlain by decomposed basalts of the Koolau Range
lavas that erupted during Pleiocene times Because of the
uniformly high tropic temperatures chemical weathering gradu
ally reduced the basalts to deep deposits of lateritic soils
by leaching of the more unstable minerals until the more stable
minerals sesquoxides remained giving the soil its reddish
color The weathering took place along the joints and fissures

of the rock until only spheroids of the more resistant rock
remained
Soils
The exposed slope and borings revealed that the site is under
lain by decomposed basalts in varying degrees of weathering
The soil matrix is a reddish brown clayey silt that still
retains its rock like structure saprolite Numerous
spheroidal boulders were found in the exposed slope and in our
borings The varying degrees of weathering are evident around
exposed rock that are from cobble to large boulder size
The existing slope is stable Some erosion has taken place
and eroded material is found along the toe
GROUNDWATER
Groundwater was not encountered in any of the borings and is
believed to be a considerable depth below the explored ele
vations
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It is our opinion that the site is suitable for the planned
retaining wall It is our recommendation that the wall be
supported on shallow footings bearing on the site soil and rock

FOUNDATIONS
The retaining wall footings should be bottomed a minimum of
18 inches below the outer adjacent grade Soil bearing value
of 3000 psf may be used for maximum soil pressures
Maximum toe settlements will be less than one quarter of an
inch Because of the variable amounts of soil and rock at
this location the footing excavations should be checked by
a qualified soils engineer
Due to the presence of a substantial amount of boulders in
the site soils some difficulty may be encountered in the
footing excavation Jack hammering may be required to break
up and remove large boulders in the footing trench Any voids
due to the removal of rock should be filled with footing
concrete or granular material compacted to a minimum of 90
of maximum density as determined by ASTM D 1557 70
LATERAL PRESSURES
A Active
The lateral pressures developed against the structure by
the weight of the soil behind the wall will depend upon
the unit weight of the soil the inherent strength of the
Page 5
W O 489 00
soil and the angle of friction between the wall material
and the soil For simplification in design the lateral
active pressure developed by the soil behind the wall can
be distributed in the same manner as a fluid pressure
Therefore the wall should be designed to resist the pressures
developed by a fluid with a density of 35 pounds per cubic
foot The backfill immediately against the wall should be
an open graded material properly compacted to at least
90 percent of maximum density Weepholes should be provided
to prevent additional fluid pressures building up behind
the wall
Passive and Friction
Resistance to horizontal sliding forces can be resisted by
friction along the base and by passive pressures A
coefficient of friction of 0 4 can be assumed between
concrete and the site soils
Passive pressures can be considered equal to the pressure
developed by a fluid with a density of 300 pounds per cubic
foot The upper foot and any loosely placed backfill
should not be considered in determining passive pressures
If the passive pressure and friction developed along the
footing base is not sufficient to resist the horizontal

The resistance to overturning of the wall can be obtained partly

APPENDIX
FIELD INVESTIGATION
Subsurface soil conditions at the site were investigated by
drilling three 3 test borings utilizing a skid mounted
rotary auger with rock coring attachments to drill through
large boulders The depths of the borings ranged from 11
to 13 feet below the existing ground surface Locations of
the borings are shown on Plate 1 in the text of this report
The logs of borings are presented in Plates A 1A through
A 1C The soils and rock were classified by visual and
textural examinations on the field by our engineer who con
tinuously supervised the drilling operations The classi
fications were verified by inspection and testing in the
laboratory All soils were classified in accordance with
the Unified Soil Classification System
The blow counts to drive the soil sampler were obtained from
the last twelve inches of penetration by using a 140 pound
hammer free falling thirty inches and are presented on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate depths
