REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea fronting the
shoreline on the western portion of the Island of Hawaii The project site is underlain by
fill materials and alluvial soil deposits The lava formation encountered at the project site
appear to be of pahoehoe and a a type lava flows which spread downslope from vents
further up on the mountain flank Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like
GEOLABS INC
W O 4376 00 Page 5
or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock A a lavas are
typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a denser and
layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core
Cavities such as lava tubes or other void spaces are quite commonly encountered
buried within the pahoehoe and a a lavas Many of these cavities were formed when the
still molten lava created the structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava The
cavities represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as
blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
The pahoehoe and a a basalt lava rocks and clinkers encountered at the site are
generally considered to be permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both the
horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix
along joints fractures inter flow contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers
The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water
transmission features

Lateral Load Resistance
As previously mentioned several shear walls are planned for the new residence
According to the project structural engineer the lateral loads imposed on the shear
walls range from approximately 3 to 12 kips The lateral loads imposed on the shear
walls may be resisted by a combination of the following 1 resistance of the drilled
shaft soil system to deflect 2 passive resistance on the vertical faces of the grade
beam footing where applicable and 3 side shear between the footings and soils
Passive resistance on the grade beams footings may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf for
footings embedded in the near surface in situ medium stiff to stiff soils For side
shear between the sides of the grade beams footings and adjacent soils a shear
value of 150 pounds per square foot psf may be used As previously mentioned the
drilled shafts supporting the shear walls should be embedded a minimum of 3 feet
into the medium hard to hard basalt formation to provide the necessary lateral load
resistance
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the drilled shaft allowable deflection at the top of the
drilled shaft and the induced moment in the drilled shaft The lateral load analyses
were performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded deep foundation
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral loads
as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use of non linear p y
curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then computed using the

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractors method of installation and construction procedures The following
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on end bearing
Therefore proper construction techniques and clean shaft bottoms are important The
contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into
GEOLABS INC
W O 4376 00 Page 12
the drilled holes The drilled shafts are designed to be embedded 1 or 3 feet into the
medium hard to hard basalt formation encountered in the borings Therefore special
tools may be required to drill through the medium hard to hard basalt formation
Our field exploration generally encountered medium dense gravelly soils and soft to
medium stiff clayey soils near the ground surface Based on our observations during
our field exploration it was noted that the sidewalls of the uncased borings 4 inches
in diameter held up well without caving in until the end of the day Therefore the
need for temporary casing of the drilled holes should be determined in the field on a
shaft by shaft basis In addition we recommend that the concrete for the drilled
shafts be placed promptly on the same day after the shaft holes are drilled
Based on the depth to the basalt formation derived from our borings groundwater is
expected in the majority of the drilled holes during the shaft installation Since
groundwater will likely be encountered in the drilled holes placement of concrete by
tremie method is recommended During placement of concrete a minimum of 5 feet
of concrete head should be maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe A low
shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range concrete mix should be used to provide
close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete
should be placed promptly after drilling to reduce the potential for softening and
caving in of the sides of the drilled holes
It is imperative that a representative of Geolabs be present at the site to observe the
drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction Drilling by methods
utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended because use of drilling fluids may reduce
the frictional resistance between the drilled shaft and surrounding soils As previously
mentioned the load bearing capacity of the drilled shaft is primarily from end bearing
therefore it is essential that the bottom of the drilled hole be relatively free of loose
materials prior to the placement of concrete

Garage and Driveway Slabs
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the garage and
driveway areas Based on our laboratory tests performed on the near surface clayey
materials at the garage area the clayey soils exhibit a low potential for expansion when
subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for distress to the future slabs
we recommend that the soils at the slab subgrade be scarified to a depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In general we recommend a minimum 6 inch layer of granular fill or aggregate
subbase be placed below the slab for more uniform support The granular fill or aggregate
subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Concrete slabs should be designed for the intended loads For design
of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch

made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will subjected to vehicular traffic
Exterior concrete walkways that are planned for the proposed project may be
supported on a minimum 4 inch thick layer of aggregate subbase compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Crack control joints should be provided at intervals
equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at

dimension Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with the
above recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment In general materials to be used as fills or
backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The granular fill or aggregate subbase below the garage and
driveway slabs should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
fill placement recommendations presented above Surface water should be diverted away
from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the cut and fill
slopes are completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Utility Trench
We envision that some new utility lines and utility line connections will be required for
construction of the new residence It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will
be excavated in the near surface soils encountered in the borings In general granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended below the pipes for uniform support If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at or near the invert of the pipes an additional 18 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine
gravel wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided
below the bedding layer for more uniform support
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
or above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical
to use the free draining materials to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular materials
generally less than 6 inches in maximum size as recommended in the Fills and Backfills
subsection compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches are
located in areas subjected to vehicular traffic the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the residence should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation excavations should
be properly backfilled against the walls immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible
and maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX _A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the residential building site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about
13 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
