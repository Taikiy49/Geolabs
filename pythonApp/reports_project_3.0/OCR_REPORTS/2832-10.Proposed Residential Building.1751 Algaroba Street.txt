INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed apartment building at 1751 Algaroba Street in the Moiliili
District of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the field exploration These recommendations are intended for design input and
may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates or for
construction bidding purposes

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
it is our Opinion that the proposed seven story high apartment structure with a
planned below grade basement is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view

Shallow Spread Footing Foundation
Our field explorations generally indicated that the site is underlain by soft loose
compressible layers of varying thickness within the upper 40 feet below the existing
ground surface We believe that these loose and compressible deposits cannot provide
adequate support for the proposed building loads for shallow spread footing foundations
without causing excessive footing settlements under the heavy building loads For the
anticipated maximum column load of up to 700 kips estimated settlements for
conventional spread footings are estimated to be on the order of about inches total
and inch differential
Surcharging of the site to accelerate the anticipated ground settlement is
considered to be not feasible due to the proximity of neighboring structures The
surcharge would produce ground settlement whose influence would extend to these
adjacent structures with a potential for structural distress
In consideration of the high footing settlements anticipated under the relatively high
column loads supported by spread footings pile and mat foundations have been
evaluated as alternate foundation support systems

Mat Foundation
To reduce the amount of potential settlement below the foundation of the proposed
structure a mat foundation bearing on the very stiff to hard clayey silt and silty clay layer
may be considered for the project The mat foundation should extend below the upper
soft clayey silt and loose sand deposits to depths of about eight 8 to nine 9 feet below
the existing ground surface
The soil below the bottom of the mat foundation should be over excavated for a
minimum depth of 18 inches The bottom of the over excavation should be performed
carefully to avoid excessive disturbance of the bearing surface The over excavation
should then be backfilled with No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel which wil
serve to provide uniform bearing and also as a working platform for dewatering
operations and construction of the mat foundation
For the design of a mat foundation bearing directly on the compacted gravel
cushion an allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f and a modulus of subgrade reaction
of 10 k c f may be used Based on our exploration and analysis we estimate that the
total settlement under the anticipated total building load of 5 600 kips could be on the
order of one half 1 2 of an inch to one 1 inch
However the high groundwater table and porous nature of the sandy subsoil
conditions encountered may pose some difficulties for the dewatering and shoring
requirements which are required for the construction of a mat foundation Special
consideration should be given to the excavation support and shoring which will be
required to assure the stability of the deep excavation and to protect the neighboring
structures during construction

W O 2832 10 Page 9
Finally based on the variable nature of the soft loose subsoil conditions
encountered in the borings for the upper 40 feet it is possible that these conditions could
vary significantly across the project site Therefore we believe that a mat foundation
system will present a higher degree of risk and uncertainty when compared with the use
of an alternate pile foundation system
Pile Foundation
In order to reduce the foundation settlements and the uncertainty associated with
the variable soft loose upper subsoil conditions we recommend that a pile foundation
system to be considered The piles should be driven through the upper soft subsoils and
stiff clay layer to bear in the underlying coral detritus formation
Pile Capacities
Twelve inch square precast prestressed concrete piles with an allowable vertical
pile capacity of 70 tons per pile may be used to support the proposed structure
The piles should penetrate through the upper soils to develop shaft friction and or
adhesion and then embedded into the underlying coral detritus formation to
develop the required additional end bearing capacity
A minimum pile spacing of five 5 feet on centers is recommended Pile
settlements on the order of less than one half 1 2 of an inch are anticipated
Pile Lengths

below the existing ground surface may be used The above estimated pile length

Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of about 32 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control so that driving within the loose deposits may be performed at
a reduced energy level to minimize the development of tensile stresses within the
piles which could cause damage cracking of the piles
Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the upper soft subsoils
the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with reduced energy levels
so that excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the piles
3 The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts steadily increase
with depth and reach six 6 blows per inch for the last three inches or
when aggregated blow counts reach 70 blows for the last foot

Test Pile Program
The pile capacity and driving criteria recommended herein were developed from
our analyses of the data obtained from our explorations consisting of five 5 borings A
test pile program is recommended to provide field data to verify some of the design
assumptions and to develop an economical pile foundation system for the proposed
building
The test pile program should be undertaken to satisfy the following objectives
1 To examine the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
2 To more accurately determine production pile order lengths and to further
verify delineate potential areas requiring different production pile lengths
3 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the driving criteria
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the test pile program should
consist of a minimum of ten 10 percent of the total number of piles as indicator piles for
the project Test piles that meet the specified driving criteria may be incorporated into the
actual foundation system on approval by the engineer
When the final foundation plan is available the project geotechnical engineer
should be consulted for selection of the test pile locations The initial test piles should be
cast ten 10 feet longer than the estimated production pile length or to about 90 feet
long Also the test pile program should provide flexibility by allowing for addition or
relocation of the test piles as may be deemed to be necessary by the project geotechnical
engineer to suit the conditions encountered during the test pile driving

A number of splice cans should be made available at the site during the test pile
driving program Splice cans may be fabricated with 1 4 inch thick high grade steel and
should be at least six 6 feet long
A minimum of one 1 pile load test should be performed as a part of the test pile
program The selected test pile should be load tested to two 2 times the design pile

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be shaped to shed water away
from foundations and to avoid ponding conditions Also it is advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the founda
tion and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to prevent water from entering the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause excessive subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled tight against the walls or slab footings immediately after
setting of the concrete to prevent ponding conditions
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of
three 3 borings to depths ranging between 74 0 and 111 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations snown on the Site Tne borings
were drilled by truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary
and core drilling tools as appropriate The entire field exploration involved a total of
approximately 264 5 lineal feet of drilling and sampling
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by field engineers and geologist from our firm The
reference
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound materials is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 and should be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
samples
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results ana at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or stability of a soil
Direct Shear Tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data are used
in various engineering calculations The results of the direct shear tests are presented
on Plates B 1 and B 2
