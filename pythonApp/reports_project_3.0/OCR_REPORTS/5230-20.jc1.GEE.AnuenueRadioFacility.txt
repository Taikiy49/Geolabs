This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Evaluation of Fill Compaction at Anuenue Radio Facility project at the
Koko Head area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
This letter report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents
our geotechnical recommendations based on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and
engineering analyses. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The existing Anuenue Radio Facility is located at the tip of Koko Head, overlooking
the ocean opening of Hanauma Bay. The facility was reconstructed in 2005. During the
construction, minor fills were placed to support the building and concrete driveway around
the southwestern side of the project site. Compaction testing was performed during the fill
placement in the building areas. However, the compaction testing was inadvertently
omitted for the fills placed undemeath the concrete driveway.
Based on the review of the grading plan provided, the fill thickness below the
concrete driveway may be up to 4 to 6 feet. To evaluate the fill compaction, three borings
were drilled within the concrete driveway area. The boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 1.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling three borings in the concrete
driveway, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of approximately
9 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 1.

Based on the subsurface materials encountered in the borings drilled, the
subsurface conditions consist of 12 to 18 inches thick of concrete pavement and 4 to 6 feet
of granular fill material overlying weathered volcanic tuff extending to greater depth. We
did not encounter groundwater at the time of our field exploration.
In general, the borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers. Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings
by visual and textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate 2. Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the test borings drilled for the project are presented on the Logs of Borings,
Plates 3 through 5.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 and C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are
provided on Plate 6.

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon
information obtained from the borings drilled. Variations of the subsurface conditions
between and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until a later date. If variations then appear evident, it
will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided in this letter report.
