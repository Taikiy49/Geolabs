REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene
Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two
GEOLABS INC
W O 3758 00 Page 6
volcanoes The project site is located near the northern end of the island or on the lower
northern slope of the East Maui Volcano Haleakala
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows The
lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch
consists of thinly bedded basaltic pahoehoe and a a lava These lavas tend to be very
permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series early
Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker alkalic a a flows which may contain
many inter stratified thin ash soil layers A long quiescent period followed during which
time canyons were carved in the volcano although a few eruptions may have occurred
during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes higher and lower
stands of the sea level At the end of the rest period copious flows erupted along the
southwest and east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit known as the Hana Volcanic
Series Pleistocene Epoch The lava range in composition from ultra basic olivine
porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts
The project site is located in an area associated with the Pauwela Haiku
association This association consists of well drained fine textured soils on low uplands on
the north facing slopes of East Maui These soils are gently sloping to moderately steep
and developed in material weathered from basic igneous rock

designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 at the proposed bridge abutment and intermediate
pier locations The borings were extended to depths ranging from about 40 to 113 feet
below the existing ground surface In addition four shallow borings designated as
Boring Nos 101 through 104 were drilled at selected locations along the new bridge
approaches extending to depths of about 11 5 and 15 4 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the borings at the abutment locations Boring Nos 1 and 4
encountered a surface layer of residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays
and clayey silts extending to depths between about 4 and 8 feet below the existing
ground surface Our field exploration at the intermediate pier locations Boring Nos 2
and 3 generally encountered a surficial fill layer consisting of stiff clayey silts and
dense silty gravel extending to depths between about 4 and 13 feet below the ground
surface

Based on our field exploration and the foundation loads provided we recommend
that deep foundations consisting of drilled shafts be used for foundation support of the
proposed bridge structure The drilled shaft foundations would derive support
principally from adhesion between the drilled shaft and the stiff saprolite and the
medium hard to hard basalt rock formation encountered in our borings Based on the
foundation loads we recommend that a shaft diameter of 5 feet and embedment depths
ranging between about 53 and 67 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevations be
used for the drilled shaft foundations

Stream Channel Material for Scour Analysis
As previously indicated one of the most common causes of bridge failure stems
from scouring of bridge foundations from flood or other water flow damage Therefore
the foundation design of the bridge abutments and intermediate piers will need to take
into consideration the potential for stream channel scour
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying
material away from the bed and banks of streams channels Total scour over a period
of time generally consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation
2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of
factors such as the shape and dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity
of approach flow size and gradation of stream channel bed material and bed
configuration
Based on the preliminary plans provided we understand that the planned
locations of the abutments and intermediate piers will be located outside the lateral
limits of the 500 year storm event flood plain In addition considering the relatively
straight alignment of the stream and the geologic conditions at the site weathered
basalt rock we do not believe that significant lateral migration of the stream would
occur at the location and within the anticipated life of the bridge Therefore in
accordance with the design guidelines of FHWA for highway bridges consideration for
the potential for stream channel scour at the abutment and intermediate pier locations
would not need to be included in the analyses for the replacement bridge foundations

the Kaupakulua Replacement Bridge generally encountered medium dense to dense

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the drilled shaft and the subgrade response We estimate that
the total settlement of the drilled shaft supported foundation to be less than
1 inch with differential settlements between drilled shafts not exceeding about
one half that amount We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic
and should occur as the loads are applied
Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these
potential variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be
present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our
opinion the following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost
of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on our field exploration some of the proposed abutment and intermediate
pier locations are underlain by silty sands and gravel extending to a depth of
approximately 45 feet below the existing ground surface Due to the granular
nature of these materials there is a potential for caving in of the materials during
the drilling operations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes
temporary casing of the drilled holes will likely be required during drilled shaft
installation Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to
reduce the potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum
5 foot head of concrete above the bottom of the casing or adequate concrete
head to counter hydrostatic pressures should be maintained during removal of
the casing The anticipated groundwater level at the intermediate pier locations
may also pose construction difficulties since proper observation of the sides and
bottoms of the drilled shaft may not be possible

dense basalt rock formation will be required The drilled shaft contractor will need to

of installing the drilled shafts to the recommended depths and dimensions by
performance of a trial shaft program
Due to the high structural capacities recommended for the drilled shafts the
sidewalls of the drilled shaft holes will need to be rifled with grooves to provide the
necessary contact and side shaft resistance assumed in our analyses The grooves
should be at least 2 inches deep by 3 inches wide and should run spirally along the
shaft circumference at a pitch of about 12 inches The rifling procedure may be
omitted in the hard rock formation and in the silty sand deposits The rifling
procedure will be required in the saprolite soils and weathered rock formation
Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes in rifling the sides of the drilled shaft holes and in placing
concrete into the holes
GEOLABS INC
W O 3758 00 Page 19
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended
Because of the groundwater table anticipated within the depths of the drilled
shaft excavations especially at the intermediate pier locations concrete
placement by tremie method may be required during construction of the drilled
shafts under such conditions The concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of the
drilled hole A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range
should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the
surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce
the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix A
representative from Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of
drilled shafts during construction especially since high capacities are
recommended for these drilled shafts
Trial Shaft Program
A trial shaft program is normally required and highly recommended for bridge
projects Considering the diameter and structural load capacities of the drilled
shafts we recommend that a trial shaft program including the performance of an
instrumented load test be undertaken to fulfill the following objectives
e To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the drilled shafts into the dense basalt rock formation
e To confirm or modify the estimated tip elevations of the drilled shafts
To assess the contractor s method of rifling the sidewalls of the drilled
shaft holes prior to placing concrete
To assess the contractor s method of placing and extracting the temporary
casing if required for the drilled shaft
To assess the contractor s method of tremie concrete placement
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the trial shaft program consist
of drilling a 5 foot diameter trial shaft extending to a depth of about 70 feet below
the ground surface at the location of Abutment 2 west abutment The location
GEOLABS INC
W O 3758 00 Page 20
of the trial shaft should be near but outside of the abutment foundations After
drilling of the trial shaft the trial shaft should be structurally reinforced and
instrumented with embedment strain gauges for load testing purposes The
embedment strain gauges should be placed starting from the bottom at an
elevation of about 5 feet above the tip of the trial shaft and subsequently at
10 foot intervals as shown on the Drilled Shaft Load Test Detail Plate 4
Due to the high capacities recommended for the drilled shafts a conventional
load test would not be practical and would be costly to conduct Therefore after
installation of the trial shaft a bi directional axial load test should be conducted
on the reinforced trial shaft using an expandable base load cell Osterberg Load
Cell The expandable base load cell will need to be attached to the bottom of
the trial shaft reinforcing cage prior to lowering the cage in place The drilled
shaft load test should be performed in general accordance with the Quick Load
Test Method of ASTM Test Designation D 1143 The trial shaft should be loaded
to failure to evaluate the ultimate side shear resistance of the trial shaft
Installation of the expandable base load cell and embedment strain gauges
performance of the bi directional axial load test and analyses of the load test
data should be performed by a qualified professional experienced in these types
of load testing procedures
Considering the specialized nature of the trial shaft program we recommend that

embankment construction The markers should be read optically once a week by a

18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 1
Ten sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 including six hydrometer tests ASTM
D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics
of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the test results are
provided on Plates B 2 and B 3
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 4
Three unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of
the saprolite soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures were used
as the applied confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil samples The test
results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 5 through B 7
Eleven unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on
selected rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strengths of the
underlying basalt rock formation The test results are summarized on Plate B 8
Two Standard Proctor tests ASTM D 698 were performed on bulk samples of the
near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The
results of the tests are presented on Plates B 9 and B 10
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture
content of the soils Compaction of the samples was performed by dropping a 10 pound
hammer from a height of 18 inches The samples were compacted in five equal layers with
each layer receiving 10 blows of energy The samples were soaked in water prior to the
penetration test The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 11 and B 12
