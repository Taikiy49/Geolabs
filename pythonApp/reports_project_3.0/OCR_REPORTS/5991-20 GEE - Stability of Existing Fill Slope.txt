Dear Mr. Arakaki:
We understand that distresses, such as separation, heaving, and/or settling, were
observed in some of the sidewalk slabs along Kapolei Parkway at the above project. We
have explored the subsurface conditions underneath the distressed sidewalks in an
attempt to evaluate the probable cause(s) of the observed distresses. Our findings and
discussions were presented in our letter report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering
Evaluation, Sidewalk Distresses along Kapolei Parkway, Kapolei City Urban Core 4
Roads, Kapolei, Oahu, Hawaii” dated March 7, 2011.
We understand that concerns were raised regarding the potential instability of the
existing fill slope behind the distressed sidewalks that could have contributed to the
sidewalk separation observed at the site. This letter report summarizes the
observations and findings and presents our discussions with respect to the existing fill
slope stability behind the distressed sidewalks based on our additional field exploration,
laboratory testing, and engineering evaluation.
Additional Field Exploration and Laboratory Testing
In order to evaluate whether the conditions of the existing fill slope behind the
distressed sidewalks could be a contributing factor to the observed distresses, our office
conducted an additional field exploration and laboratory testing program on the subsurface
conditions at the existing fill slope behind the distressed sidewalks. Our additional field
exploration program consisted of drilling a series of borings at three selected locations
along the existing fill slope on April 29, 2011. Two of the locations were conducted in
areas where sidewalk distresses were observed and one location was conducted in an
area where sidewalk distresses were not observed to serve as a control area for
comparison. Each location was investigated by drilling three borings, located at the top,

The test borings at the top of the existing fill slope indicated that the existing
sidewalk is generally underlain by 5 to 6 feet of coralline materials over the in-situ alluvial
clay soils. This corresponds to the fill thickness required to raise the original ground to the
finished grades as interpreted from the site grading plan and also confirmed the finding
from the earlier test pit excavated in February 2011. Detailed descriptions of the materials
encountered in the drilled borings are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates 1.1
through 3.3.
In accordance with the project plans and specifications, the non-expansive capping
fill and the underlying clayey subgrade soils should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
The relative compaction of the coralline capping fill and the underlying clayey soils
were evaluated by comparing the in-situ dry density of the material to its maximum dry
density (proctor value). Results of the relative compaction of the soil samples obtained at
selected depth increments are summarized in the following table.

Based on the compaction test results shown in the above table, it appears that the
coralline materials at the existing fill slope behind the distressed sidewalks are generally
compacted to between about 82 to 86 percent, which did not meet the minimum
compaction requirement of 90 percent. On the other hand, the existing fill slope at the
control location appears to be compacted to about 90 percent relative compaction. These
compaction test results appear to be consistent with the relatively low penetration
resistances (blow counts) of the soil sampler as shown on the Logs of Borings. The
penetration resistances of the soil sampler in the drilled borings at the control location are
significantly higher than those obtained in the other two distressed locations.
As shown in the above table, the clayey subgrade soils below the coral capping fill
appear to be adequately compacted. Based on the penetration resistances and pocket
penetrometer test results obtained during the field exploration, the in-situ clayey soils
generally exist in a very stiff to hard condition.

Discussions and Evaluation
As previously mentioned, some sidewalk slab movements have been observed
along the mauka side of Kapolei Parkway. This additional field exploration and
laboratory testing program focused on the conditions of the existing fill slope behind the
distressed sidewalks.
Based on our findings, it appears that the existing fill slope behind the distressed
sidewalk slabs was not adequately compacted to the minimum requirement of
90 percent relative compaction, as indicated by the relatively low penetration resistance
during soil sampling operation and the compaction test results obtained from the soil
samples collected. However, the underlying in-situ alluvial clays appear to exist in a
very stiff to hard consistency with no signs of softness. Based on the competent soil
conditions encountered at the deeper subsurface and our engineering analyses, it is our
opinion that there is no global instability (slope failure) issue at the distressed sidewalk
site. However, shallow movement of the inadequately compacted coral capping fill
could have occurred at the existing fill slope and might have contributed to the sidewalk
movement. We understand that the existing light poles and electrical boxes were
embedded to depths of up to about 5 feet below the sidewalk slabs. Therefore, it is our
opinion that the shallow movement of the coral capping fill, if occurred, could not cause
lateral movement of the deeply embedded light pole foundations and electrical box
