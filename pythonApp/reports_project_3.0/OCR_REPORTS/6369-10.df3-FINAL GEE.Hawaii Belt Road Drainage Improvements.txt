Introduction
This report presents the results of our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses performed for the design of the Hawaii Belt Road Drainage
Improvements project located at Papaaloa in the District of North Hilo on the Island of
Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our testing and analyses for the design of
the drainage improvements only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The project site is at Milepost 24 of the Hawaii State Route 19 Hawaii Belt Road
in the Papaaloa area on the Island of Hawaii The existing Hawaii Belt Road in the
project vicinity is a two lane undivided roadway About 300 feet northwest from the
intersection with Papaaloa Avenue there is an existing 36 inch pipe culvert that crosses
Route 19 and drains to the north into a rectangular concrete lined ditch
Based on the preliminary information provided we understand that there have
been flooding issues associated with this culvert crossing and that it is desired to install
a new storm drain system on the northeast side of the highway that diverts the surface
water and discharges to the adjacent Kaiwilahilahi Stream
1 3

and work efforts

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the eruptive
activity of five major shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both active
The project site is on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The most recent lava flows that comprise the ground surface and
shallow subsurface at the project site are believed to be late Pliocene to
Mid Pleistocene in age approximately 1 2 million years to 2 million years before present
time and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series These lava
flows were capped by Pahala ash deposits
The basalt formations of the project area are typically layered with thin flows of
dense basalts and scoria basalts Clinker layers are also found commonly interbedded
between the a a lava flows In situ weathering of the basalt formation has yielded layers
of soft highly weathered basalts interbedded with more competent hard basalts Our
borings drilled for this exploration generally encountered volcanic ash soils overlying
saprolite i e weathered basalt that retains its parent structure but mechanically
reduces to a soil material Drive sampling reduces the material to a silty sand with
much gravel and some cobbles representing relatively unweathered corestones in the
saprolite matrix
Cavities or voids are encountered commonly in pahoehoe lava Cavities form
when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and inter flow cavities blisters and pockets Lava tubes form when molten lava drains
from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizontal extent However we did not encounter cavities in our drilled borings

Site Description
The project site is along the portion of Hawaii Belt Road Route 19 at the
Honokaa side of its intersection with Papaaloa Avenue in the District of North Hilo on
the Island of Hawaii In this area Route 19 is a two lane undivided highway surfaced
with asphaltic concrete Overhead electric power lines parallel the Hilo bound lane of
the highway and an underground waterline exists along this side
The proposed drain line alignment is situated on the northeasterly side of the
highway extending from the existing 36 inch culvert crossing the highway to
Kaiwilahilahi Stream to the east
Based on the available plans the existing ground surface elevations along the
drain alignment range from about 220 feet Mean Sea Level MSL in the stream
channel to about 291 feet MSL in the upper portion of the alignment

designated as Boring Nos 101 through 103 extending to depths ranging from about

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration it appears that the drain line may be installed
using the cut and cover method with conventional earthmoving equipment However
it is cautioned that since the soils at the site are derived from the in situ weathering of
relatively young basaltic rock remnant boulders or ledges of hard material may be
present
In general the upper ash soils are not acceptable for re use as fill or backfill
material since their very high moisture contents and possible thixotropic properties will
present difficulty in handling and compacting the soils The deeper residual soils may be
re used as a source of general fill materials provided that they are free of vegetation
deleterious materials and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in
maximum dimension It should be noted that the project is located in a high rainfall
environment condition throughout the year therefore on site soils will constantly be in a
very moist to wet condition We believe it would be very difficult to achieve the normal
90 to 95 percent relative compaction requirement without significant aeration of the
on site clayey soils However it may not be feasible and practical to aerate the on site
clayey soils therefore we recommend compacting the on site clayey soils to a
minimum of 85 to 90 percent relative compaction
It should be noted that subgrade preparation and compaction of fill materials are
critical elements of earthwork Therefore Geolabs should perform observations and soil
density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree
of compaction and the proper moisture content

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be

Fill and Backfill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Because the project is located in a high rainfall environment the on site soils will be
in a very moist to wet condition Therefore drying or aerating the excavated
materials may be necessary prior to their use as general fill or backfill Aeration to
lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort along with thinner fill lifts to
achieve the specified compaction may be necessary and generally would reduce
the rate of fill placement for this project
Fill or backfill materials consisting of the on site clayey soils should be
moisture conditioned to within 2 percent of the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 85 to 90 percent relative compaction
Imported fill or backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Aggregate base
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Excavation
It is anticipated that the on site soils may be excavated with normal heavy
excavation equipment However there is a potential for encountering large
boulders and harder less weathered zones of volcanic rock at unpredictable
depths The contractor for the project should be cautioned that these hard volcanic
rock zones could be encountered in the excavations and may require chipping
and or the use of hoerams to excavate the materials
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are
based on field observations and our experience in the project vicinity Contractors
bidding on this project should be encouraged to review and understand the
geologic environment of the project site and to examine the site conditions to make
their own interpretation
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 5 feet or more below the
ground surface will be required for the installation of new drainline Where
excavations greater than 5 feet in depth are planned temporary shoring or
sloping and benching should be used Based on the existing site conditions we
believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations are not practical On
this basis trench walls would have to be cut vertically requiring the use of
shoring during construction
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable
safety requirements The contractor should be solely responsible for the
adequacy and safety of the shoring installation The contractor s representative
i e competent person as defined in OSHA regulations should be on site at all
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly
observe changing or unforeseen conditions such as high groundwater
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect the
shoring stability

Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the

It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction The properly braced
shoring is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of
the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in potential settlement
or distress to adjacent structures or other improvements
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to
accurately estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends
greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and
bracing supports are installed the subsurface conditions the size of the
excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical The adjacent ground should be continuously
monitored by the contractor s competent person for cracks dips and or other
indications of movements until the drainline is finally backfilled

Retaining Structures
Based on the information provided the inlet outlet structures will function as
retaining walls Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions the following general
guidelines may be used for design of the retaining structures planned for the project
Wall Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
using an allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds per square foot psf for
design of the retaining structures bearing on recompacted on site soils This
bearing value is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Footing subgrades should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction to provide a relatively firm and unyielding surface prior to construction
of the retaining walls Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of
footing excavations should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with fill materials compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing may be deepened to
bear on the underlying firm materials In general footings should be embedded a
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structures may be resisted by friction between
the base of the retaining wall foundation and the bearing materials and by passive
earth pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion
of foundations A coefficient of friction of 0 36 may be used for footings bearing
directly on the on site soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot
of depth pcf assuming that the soils around the footings are well compacted
Unless covered by slabs the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches
below the finished grade should be neglected

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Because the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii forces
due to dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of
retaining structures The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures associated
with seismic loading may be estimated using 16H2 pounds per foot of
wall length for level backfill conditions where H is the height of the wall in feet The
dynamic lateral earth force generally would act at the mid height of the wall
It should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the
wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in the event of an
earthquake If this amount of lateral movement is not acceptable the retaining wall
should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a semi restrained wall
condition For a semi restrained wall condition dynamic lateral forces due to
seismic loading may be estimated using 28H2 pounds per lineal foot of wall for level
backfill conditions
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the
mid height of the wall The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures An appropriately reduced
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted
for in the design of the retaining structure It should be noted that due to relatively
high dynamic lateral forces sloping backfill behind retaining structures should be
avoided
