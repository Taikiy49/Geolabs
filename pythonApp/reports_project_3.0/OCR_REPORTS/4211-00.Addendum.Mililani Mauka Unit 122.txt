REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred
to as the Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch
The dips of the lava beds are near horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal The
lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and
are characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as
Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are the result of this in situ weathering
This in situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that
grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and
is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks Spheroidally weathered boulders are
typical in the Schofield Plateau The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
All fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to a minimum of 2 percent above
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Due to the high natural
moisture content of the soils the excavated material may require aeration and
reworking prior to full placement
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheei pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with low to moderate swelling potential and moderate to high
shrinking potential Unless floor slabs are properly designed and constructed there is a
potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and
swelling of these soils due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for
structural distress resulting from the shrinking and swelling of the subgrade soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils be kept within about 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content prior to final placement of concrete Conventional slab on grade design
4 inch cushion vapor barrier and 2 inch sand may be utilized above the properly prepared
subgrade soils
In addition we also recommend that one of the following recommendations be
implemented to help stabilize the subgrade soil moisture below the house structure
e Deepen perimeter footings to 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade
e Construct a 3 foot wide sidewalk with a 12 inch deep
thickened edge adjacent and surrounding the entire house
structure
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding around the
foundation
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed below the
interior slabs The cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB should provide more uniform support of the
slabs and should serve as a capillary break To reduce the potential for future moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier
such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel or BTB cushion layer
Consideration should be given to placement of two inches of moist sand or BTB above the

plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab

designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate small amount of possible ground
movements
For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as single family

Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project The

subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with all

In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be
used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill
immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or select granular material The
backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical
compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project site Water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill material

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
ttre
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings and Logs of Test Pits at the
appropriate sample depths
Six 6 sets of sulfates and chlorides tests were performed
by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific on selected samples to evaluate the corrosion
potential of the soil The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 1
Six 6 sets of resistivity ASTM G 57 78 and and pH tests ASTM G 51
and EPA 9045 were performed on selected samples to evaluate the corrosion potential of
the soil The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 1
Thirty 30 one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge loads
These tests were run on air dried and non air dried samples Results of these swell tests are
presented on Plates B 2 through B 7
Twenty 20 one inch ring shrinkage tests were performed on relatively undisturbed
samples and remolded samples to evaluate the shrinking potential of the soils when dried
Results of these shrinkage tests are presented on Plates B 8 through B 11
Three 3 Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings and the Logs of Test Pits at the
appropriate sample depth Graphic representations of the test results are provided on
Plate B 12
Two 2 Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
near surface soils and soils at the anticipated finish cut elevation Results of these tests are
presented on Plates B 13 and B 14
Four 4 California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of
near surface soils and soils at the anticipated finish cut elevation to evaluate the strength
characteristics for pavement subgrade support Results of the CBR tests are presented on
Plates B 15 through B 18
