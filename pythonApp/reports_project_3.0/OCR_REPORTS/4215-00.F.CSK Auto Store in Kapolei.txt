INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed CSK Auto Store at Kapolei in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our finding and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for design of foundations slabs on grade
site preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
It is proposed to construct a new one story auto parts store with associated parking areas
around the building Based on the information provided the project site encompasses an
area of approximately 11 000 square feet which includes the new one story store and
parking areas as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The site is currently a vacant lot adjacent
to a parking lot with asphalt concrete pavement
The new store building will have a footprint of about 7 200 square feet Based on the
surface conditions at the site we anticipate that the finished floor of the new store will be
near the existing grades Therefore we envision that minor site grading consisting of cuts
and fills on the order of about 1 foot or less would be needed to achieve the design finished
grades Further details pertaining to the site grading were not available at the time this report
was prepared
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that the
subsoil conditions at the proposed project site may consist of some surficial fill underlain by
residual soil deposits Special attention was given to the foundation support characteristics
and expansion potential of the near surface materials

REGIONAL GEQLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from the Koolau
Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as
the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank of the
Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary
wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet
at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or
caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has
continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading
work have brought the Kapolei area to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the majority of the new building site is generally
underlain by a surficial fill layer of moderately expansive clayey soils of about 6 to 8 feet
thick Below the surficial fill clayey soils our field exploration generally encountered
medium stiff to stiff residual soils consisting of silty clays extending to the maximum depth
explored of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater was not
encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on relative stiff dense subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we
recommend that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings be
used for support of the proposed new building An allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the
re compacted on site soils
Based on our laboratory tests the surface clayey soils exhibit moderate shrink swell
characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents As a result
we recommend that a minimum of 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill material
be provided below the building slab cushion Detailed discussion of these items and our

Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed store building An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for
design of foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills This
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the
foundations should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts
of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
For footings bearing on the in situ soils or new fills the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior
to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedure Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing may be extended
down deeper to the underlying competent material

We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills to be less than 1 inch total
with differential settlements on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the building may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of
0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted
fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the competency of the
foundation bearing materials and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
As previously indicated our laboratory tests indicate that the clayey silts encountered in our
borings exhibit a moderate potential for shrink swell when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for structural distress of the slabs on grade resulting from swelling
of the clayey silt soils we recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be
properly prepared and capped with at least 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill
material below the slab cushion The non expansive select granular fill capping fill should
also extend beyond the building perimeter a minimum of 24 inches
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should
be thoroughly moistened and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils
GEOLABS INC
W O 4215 00 F Page 9
be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with weli compacted fill
For interior slabs we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer
consisting of free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation be provided below the slab The cushion layer would provide uniform support
for the slab and serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture
vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the cushion layer
Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide
protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the
2 inches of moist fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left to the discretion of the
project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should
be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should
be backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils on site soils and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around
foundations
Site Preparation
Based on the surface conditions at the site we envision that minimal site grading
work will be required for the proposed project construction In general we recommend that
the following guidelines be used for the site grading work anticipated At the on set of
earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared
Vegetation debris rubbish asphaltic concrete pavements concrete slabs and other
unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and disposed of properly
off site
GEOLABS INC
W O 4215 00 F Page 10
Soft loose and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated
to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft loose and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed The resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction
In areas to receive fills the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The subgrades for areas subjected to
vehicular traffic such as pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and
prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the loose soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no
additional cost to the owner
If imported fill materials are required for the project construction imported fill
materials should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral basalt

larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition the material should contain between

Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these

Corrosion Evaluation
One of the subsurface soil samples retrieved from our field exploration at the site
was subjected to chemical analyses for soluble chloride and sulfate concentrations The
chemical analyses were performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific The results
of these tests are presented on Plate B 4 of Appendix B Minimum resistivity and
pH analysis tests were also conducted to evaluate the potential for corrosion to metallic
substructures The results of these tests are also presented on Plate B 4 of Appendix B
Based on a minimum resistivity of 680 ohm cm the on site soils tested may be considered
extremely corrosive to buried metallic substructures
Based on the test results the soluble chloride and sulfate concentrations in the soil
sample tested were 179 and 267 Part Per Million PPM The pH of the soil was 8 4
greater than 5 5 Based on California Test 532 Method for Estimating the Time to
Corrosion of Reinforced Concrete Substructures the on site soils tested may be
considered to be generally non corrosive to reinforced concrete in contact with the soils
However normal precautions to reduce the potential effects of corrosion such as minimum
concrete cover should be observed for reinforced concrete substructures

Pavement Design
We understand that asphaltic concrete pavements will be required for the parking
areas around the proposed building In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the
proposed store will consist primarily of passenger vehicles pick up trucks and occasional
moderately loaded trucks Our laboratory tests performed on the near surface silty sands
encountered beneath the existing pavement appear to have a CBR value of 9 2 with a swell
value of less than 1 percent Considering the generally light to medium traffic anticipated for
the proposed project we recommend that the following pavement design be used for
preliminary budgeting purposes
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The subgrade soils for the new pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted not less than 95 percent relative
compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be
extended a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course
to reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system is recommended to collect the excess water from
landscape irrigation For long term performance we believe that a subdrain system should
be considered adjacent to the paved landscaped areas

Utility Trench
We envision that some new utility lines and utility connections may be required for
the proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for support
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
about 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils

These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation

to reduce the potential for water infiltration
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of this
test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on natural samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The results of the tests are presented on Plate B 3
One set of chemical analysis tests including Chlorides EPA Test Method 325 2 and
Sulfates EPA Test Method 375 2 was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate their corrosion potential The chemical analysis tests were performed by
Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific Results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 4
One set of corrosion evaluation tests including pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum
resistivity ASTM G 57 was performed on a selected sample of the soils The results of the
tests are summarized on Plate B 4
