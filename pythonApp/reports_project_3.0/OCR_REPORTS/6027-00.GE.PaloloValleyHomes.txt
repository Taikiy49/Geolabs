REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
filled pre existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and at the heads of valleys erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and
transportation down slope toward a valley axis primarily by gravity as colluvial earth
materials Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley stream
flow processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as
alluvial earth materials
The project site is along the lower flanks of the mountainside forming the western
slopes of Palolo Valley as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The western valley
slopes are composed of sequential layered basaltic lava rock erupted from the Koolau
Volcano The exposed rock material appears to represent both a a lava flow typified by a
dense lava flow core with surrounding weaker rock interbeds composed of clinker and
pahoehoe lava flow typified by thinly bedded vesicular lavas Therefore individual hard
lava beds range from relatively thin to moderately thick with some interbedded medium
hard and friable clinker material Differential erosion of the exposed basaltic rock layers
has developed a stepped slope character consisting of narrow flattened ledges and
sub vertical inclined rock face features The base of the basaltic rock slopes abut a gently
to moderately sloping talus apron which extends further down slope toward the rear of the
housing development These high rock slopes may produce potentially dangerous rockfall
conditions which could encroach on the housing development

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is at the existing Palolo Valley Homes housing development
adjacent to Palolo Avenue in Palolo Valley on the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 The project site is along the lower colluvial slope of the
western mountainside Ground surface elevations at the rear of the housing development
are on the order of about 250 to 280 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The adjacent
mountain slopes composed of basaltic rock formation rise to elevations of over 850 feet
MSL above the development The slopes contain surface exposures of layered rock
material that is susceptible to natural rock slope erosion and the mass transfer of detached
rock materials toward the lower elevations Photograph No 1 on Plate A 1 shows an
overview of the project site and adjacent mountain slopes from across the valley
Based on our hillside reconnaissance two large and highly visibly existing rock
outcroppings were identified above the housing development between about
Elevation 400 and 500 feet MSL The outcrops are identified as Hazardous
Outcrops A and B and have similar physical characteristics Outcrop A is above
Buildings 122 through 124 at an elevation of about 480 feet MSL Outcrop B is above
Buildings 120 and 121 at an elevation of about 430 feet MSL Both outcrops have the
potential to generate hazardous rockfall by block detachment from existing wide open
vertically oriented fractures spanning the height of the outcrops Both massive rock
outcrops are generally about 20 to 25 feet high by about 15 to 25 feet across and about
10 to 12 feet deep We observed wide open fractures spanning the height of the outcrops
with a measured aperture width between 1 and 3 inches Based on our observations it
appears that Outcrops A and B could experience a detachment of large portions of the
outcrop to produce falling boulders and or slabs of rock Based on our observation of the
visible portion of the fractures around the exterior of the outcrops some large individual
boulders on the order of about 3 to 6 feet in dimension could be produced as a result of
rock outcrop failure along the fractures Based on our observations the slope immediately
below Outcrop A contains a steep slope escarpment therefore boulder rolling
momentum could be easily developed Directly below Outcrop B the slope topography
consists of a shallow basin and ledge area that should reduce the potential for initial rolling
momentum to develop Both Outcrops A and B pose a danger for potential rockfall
conditions that could encroach on the housing development Outcrop A is believed to be
the more hazardous outcrop of the two due to the steeper slope condition directly below
the fractured outcrop face The approximate location of Outcrops A and B is shown on
the Generalized Slope Profiles Plate 3 Various photographs of the outcrops and
surrounding conditions are presented on Plates A 1 through A 6

Computer Rockfall Simulation Analysis
As part of our evaluation of the potential rockfall conditions at the site we
performed a computer simulation of potential falling rock activity using input data collected
during our site reconnaissance We used the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program
CRSP Version 4 0 for our analysis Topographic information was obtained from the
available USGS Quadrangle map of the hillside The purpose for the CRSP analysis was
to model the existing slope and potential rockfall conditions to obtain a statistical analysis
of the simulation results to assist in the evaluation of potential rockfall encroachment and
design parameters for an appropriate rockfall containment structure
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall based on input parameters
that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project were
assigned based on our observations and measurements collected in the field The
program provides a statistical analysis of potential rockfall activity based on the hill slope
topographic profile and other specific input information such as rock size shape and
numerical parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions Information
obtained from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity bouncing
height kinetic energy and encroachment distance The output information is useful to
assist in the site specific design and physical location of an appropriate rockfall
containment system
Two selected hill slope model profiles identified as Slope Profiles A and B were
developed to support our CRSP analysis The slope profile location and graphic
representations of the slope profiles used in our potential rockfall analyses is shown on
Plate 3
Our CRSP analysis was performed using spherical and cylindrical shaped boulders
conservative scenario ranging from 3 to 6 feet in dimension initiating from the upper rock
slope area identified during the field reconnaissance Zone 1 For each rockfall simulation
run 1 000 source rocks were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results
Numerical input coefficients were used which approximated the typical ground surface
relief and roughness conditions
Our minimum rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable interception
and catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the computer
rockfall simulation analysis CRSP The 90 percent catchment criteria is a target that is

Proposed Rockfall Mitigation Scheme
Based on our study of the potential rockfall hazard conditions the existing housing
development is exposed to potential rockfall hazards from the slopes within Zones 1 and 2
The Zone 1 slopes have a greater potential for producing dangerous rockfall
encroachment at the housing development The Zone 2 slopes have a lower level of risk
for producing dangerous rockfall encroachment mainly at the rear of the housing units We
believe that an appropriate rockfall mitigation plan should consist of the construction of a
rockfall impact barrier fence along the existing swale trail feature in Zone 1 In addition
some selected potentially hazardous boulders in Zone 2 should be demolished and the
broken rock fragments scattered in stable settings on the hillside
Zone 1 Rockfall Impact Barrier
Rockfall impact barriers are usually constructed on hillsides where the protection of
down slope areas from varied and potentially widespread sources of falling rock is
necessary Rockfall impact barriers are considered to be a viable rockfall protection
measure where a specific and limited source of potential rockfall cannot be readily
identified or where the source area may encompass disturbance sensitive land that
makes other mitigation measures less practical to implement Rockfall impact
barriers are specially designed fences consisting of steel support beams linked by
wire rope cable or steel wire ring nets which are designed to flex and absorb
rockfall impact energy The nets and beams are supported by drilled and grouted
ground anchors The barrier height is typically constructed 8 to 14 feet above
ground level and the galvanized steel components are typically colored black for
reduced visibility Rockfall barrier fences require periodic inspection and future
maintenance to remove accumulated boulder debris and replace worn or corroded
steel components The components of the barrier are generally considered heavy
duty however if a very large impact causes severe deformation portions of the
barrier may require substantial repair or replacement Ultimately the rockfall barrier

Zone 2 Boulder Demolition
The boulder demolition alternative would involve the use of destructive force to
mechanically break up selected potential boulder hazards for removal or
displacement to other stable ground areas Boulder destruction may be achieved
by several methods including the use of chemical agents low explosive charges
and mechanical tools to demolish the rock into smaller pieces that can be removed
or relocated at the site
With the use of chemical agents the chemical mixture is injected into drilled holes
in the rock and allowed to slowly expand and crack the rock The time needed to
crack the rock ranges from 1 to 20 hours depending on the chemical mixture and
quality of the rock thus monitoring of the cracking procedure may be warranted
where precarious boulder positions are encountered The use of chemical agents is
appropriate in sensitive environmental conditions where noise vibration dust and
fly rock must be eliminated from concern

The use of explosives for rock demolition is not recommended at Palolo Valley
Homes due to the developed urban environment and the potential for the
production of dangerous fly rock debris
Rocks may also be broken with the use of mechanical force such as special rock
splitting tools inserted into drilled holes and pneumatic percussion hammers The
mechanical methods are labor intensive and typically require the use of
compressed air air transmission hoses and other hand tools Dust and noise are
anticipated by products of the mechanical destruction procedure
It should be emphasized that a temporary restraint system must be employed to
control the potential movement of the rock during the boulder demolition activity
The purpose for the temporary restraint system is to control the rock fragment
production and movement to reduce the possibility of undesired rockfall initiation
Various methods of temporary restraint may be utilized based on the size of the
target rock anticipated size of rock fragments that would be produced and the
surrounding conditions
We envision that potentially unstable boulders would be selectively targeted for
mechanical destruction of the rock The rock pieces would then be removed or
spread and redistributed at flatter portions of the slope Based on the estimated
number of boulders that may be demolished some disturbance to the existing
ground surface and vegetation is anticipated to occur as a result of access trail
clearing equipment mobilization and other construction related impacts The
ground disturbance may result in the engineer s identification of additional boulders
for demolition during the rock stabilization work
The construction cost for the demolition of about 40 boulders ranging in size
between about 2 and 6 feet could vary substantially depending on the method of
destruction selected by the contractor For budgeting purposes we estimate the
construction cost for boulder demolition to range between about 50K and 100K

LIMITATIONS
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from visual site observations and computer simulation and statistical
analysis of potential rockfall activity only Variation in the surface and subsurface
conditions between our observations and analysis points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field exploration or

re evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials rock slopes deteriorate
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes wet dry and hot cold
cycles and erosion conditions Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting
from natural processes weathering wet dry and hot cold cycles and erosion conditions
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time Therefore
the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only within a reasonable
time from the date of issuance of this letter report Land use site conditions and or other
factors may change with the passage of time Therefore additional work to evaluate the
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report
due to changes with the passage of time will be required
