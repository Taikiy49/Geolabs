SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kauhola Point Light project at Kapa au in the District of
North Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended to assess the stability of the existing
light tower and the design of a new monopole light structure including the design of
foundations slabs on grade and site grading The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The existing Kauhola Point Light is a 77 foot reinforced concrete structure that
was built in 1933 on the Kauhola Point peninsula The peninsula is bounded by steep
exposed sea cliffs and the ocean Over the years undocumented sea cliff erosion has
reduced the distance between the top of the cliff and the light tower foundation
Documentation of the on going cliff erosion and top of cliff retreat began in about 2003
Following the October 2006 earthquakes on the Island of Hawaii the sea cliff
adjacent to the existing light structure experienced an accelerated collapse event
involving several hundred cubic yards of earth material As a result of the continued cliff
erosion the top of the cliff has retreated toward the lighthouse and currently resides
within about 20 feet of the edge of the existing tower foundation
In consideration of the active sea cliff erosion and potential for catastrophic
collapse the project involves the evaluation of the stability and possible remaining
lifespan of the existing light structure In addition we conducted a geotechnical
engineering exploration for the design of a new monopole light tower located further
inland from the existing light structure

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is at Kauhola Point near Kapa au in the District of North Kohala
on the Island of Hawaii The site is on a relatively flat soil surfaced peninsula bounded
by coastal sea cliffs facing towards the northeast north and westerly directions The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The peninsula harbors the existing Kauhola Point light tower which is a concrete
reinforced structure of about 77 feet high Based on the available construction plan of a
similar tower the concrete foundation of the tower is approximately 24 feet wide and is
believed to be embedded about 3 to 4 feet below the ground surface Some abandoned
tower piers and concrete tank enclosures are in the vicinity of the light tower The
existing ground surface elevation at the peninsula is about 30 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL
A sub vertical inclined 70 to 80 degrees sea cliff bounds the existing light tower
towards the north through northeast The cliff face is devoid of vegetation and provides
visible exposure of the irregular slope face consisting of highly to extremely weathered
saprolitic basaltic rock material comprising the slope A near shore submerged rock
shelf appears to cause waves to break offshore with wave wash and associated wave
run up reaching the narrow cobble boulder shoreline at the base of the cliff The
predominant wind and wave direction is from the north through east thus the cliff
appears to be exposed to near continuous environmentally induced weathering
The existing cliff line inclined at approximately 70 to 80 degrees fronting the
light tower shows evidence of long term continual erosion and shallow thin skin failures
of the highly weathered rock and soils comprising the slope face Adjacent to the tower
the cliff juts inland and reaches within about 20 feet of the edge of the concrete tower
foundation Stress cracks oriented parallel with the portion of the cliff encroaching on
the tower were observed in the near surface soils The stress cracks were observed to
extend inland an additional 2 to 4 feet towards the tower The existing stress cracks

2 2 Slope Instability Background
Based on our review of the available light tower construction plan and some
historic photographs the top of the cliff was a horizontal distance of approximately
85 feet from the tower edge at the time of construction in 1933 In September 2007 due
to the erosion and retreat of the cliff that distance was measured as approximately
20 feet Therefore it appears that over a period of about 74 years the cliff has
experienced about 55 feet of landward retreat adjacent to the light tower
We understand that Coast Guard monitoring and documentation of the cliff
erosion began in about 2003 Based on the available time sequence photographs it
appears that approximately 15 feet of cliff retreat was experienced adjacent to the tower
between 2003 and 2007 alone Of the 15 feet of retreat between 2003 and 2007 about
5 to 6 feet of retreat appears to have occurred during and following the
Island of Hawaii earthquakes The large magnitude earthquakes
substantially accelerated the erosion as would be anticipated for the steep slope
condition However the overall rate of erosion appears to be increasing with the
passage of time
Based on our experience with other projects along the Hamakua Coast similar
landslide erosion and retreat of sea cliffs has been observed We believe the cause is
related to the natural erosion and mass wasting of the deeply weathered earth materials
saprolitic basaltic rock comprising the steep exposed sea cliffs In our review of the
available information the Kauhola cliff erosion rate is highly variable We anticipate that
the erosion accelerates appreciably during earthquake activity and severe storms
which may be accompanied by heavy rainfall and large storm surf Generally the
occurrence of these environmental effects is not predictable therefore the rate of cliff
erosion and lifespan of the existing tower is difficult to estimate by the erosion rate
alone

3 1 Existing Light Tower
The existing Kauhola Point Light is in danger of being lost due to progressive
mass wasting and erosion of the adjacent deeply weathered sea cliff The cliff is
approximately 30 feet high and inclined at about 75 degrees The edge of the cliff
erosion is presently within about 20 feet of the tower foundation Additional open ground
cracks present along the top of the cliff suggested latent slope instability and the
likelihood for additional collapse in the near term
Based on our review of the available information documenting the cliff erosion
our opinion is that the existing light tower may experience structural damage or collapse
within about 2 to 5 years time as the proximal edge of the cliff continues to retreat
sporadically towards the existing tower foundation
Based on our slope stability analysis for the existing light tower under the existing
site conditions a factor of safety against slope instability of 1 3 was calculated Our
analysis shows that as the cliff edge encroaches closer to the foundation the factor of
safety decreases and the potential for a slope failure surface to intersect the existing
tower foundation increases The slope stability analysis suggests that additional shallow
failures and landward cliff retreat should be expected

Monopole Shaft Foundation
As an alternative to a shallow concrete mat foundation the proposed new
monopole could utilize a drilled shaft foundation We anticipate that the proposed
monopole will be subjected to ground line overturning moment and lateral forces
at the foundation level Structural loads and loading conditions for the new
monopole are not available for our analyses
Based on the anticipated loading conditions and the subsurface conditions
encountered we believe that the monopole may be supported by drilled shaft
foundation The major considerations in our foundation analyses included the
lateral loads and ground line overturning moments induced in the shaft
foundations
In general lateral load resistance for shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the
shaft and induced moment in the shaft
The performance of the shaft will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result a Geolabs
representative should be present to observe the installation of the drilled shaft
during construction In our opinion the following may have a significant impact on
the effectiveness and cost of the shaft foundations
The load carrying capacity of shaft depends to a large extent on the contact
between the shaft and the surrounding rock as well as the hardness of the rock
Therefore proper construction techniques are important The contractor should
exercise care in drilling or excavating the shaft hole and in placing concrete in the
hole The bottom of shaft should be cleaned of loose or disturbed material prior
to constructing the foundation The bottom should also be fairly level The
concrete should be poured neat against the excavation sidewalls Backfill against
the shaft foundation should not be allowed

3 4 Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade may be used for a sidewalk We

3 6 Utilities
We anticipate that the underground utilities may primarily consist of electrical
conduit lines In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the pipes to support the
utility lines The initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should
consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
pipe damage from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular
material as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use
of clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical
compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials Compaction
efforts by water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed

3 7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the foundation should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundation and to reduce the potential for ponding
This drainage requirement is essential for the proper performance of the
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be backfilled properly against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 40 0 and 46 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 We drilled the borings using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous
flight augers and HQ rock coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory
Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System
as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are provided
on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition we obtained
some samples from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
