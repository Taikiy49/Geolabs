2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch Ice
Age by basaltic and andesitic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai
Mountain and West Molokai Mountain The project site is located on the western side of
the West Molokai Mountain Prior to weathering and erosion the West Molokai Mountain
was a typical elongated basaltic shield shaped dome It was built over the northwest and
east trending rifts with a steep slope on the north side where the lava flows plunged into
deep water and a gentle slope on the east side where the lava flows banked against the
East Molokai dome and formed the Hoolehua Plain
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea level rose
because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level on Molokai Chemical and physical weathering have produced a
residual lateritic soil cover on the saprolite soils and basalt formation underlying the site

Our field exploration generally encountered fill materials consisting of stiff to hard

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that a
shallow foundation system consisting of continuous footings be used to support the new
perimeter wall supporting the new roof structure planned at the Maunaloa Reservoir Due
to the potential for variation in the consistency of the fill materials encountered at the
reservoir berm we recommend that the bottom of footing elevations be over excavated a
minimum of 24 inches The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with select
borrow compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The select borrow
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of footings bearing on the compacted select borrow material over the
stiff to hard silty clay and clayey silt This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below
the lowest adjacent finished grade For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on
sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum
horizontal set back distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to
the slope face
Foundations located next to other foundations utility trenches or easements

bottom edge of the utility trench As an alternative the footings may be extended to a
depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for appreciable foundation settlement

3 4 Horizontal Directional Drilling
We understand that an intake line from the MG Reservoir to about
Station 2 60 may be installed by horizontal directional drilling WDD methods We believe
that horizontal directional drilling may be used for the installation of the intake line from the
reservoir to about Station 2 60
Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using a
multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface The pilot
hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical cutting or a
combination of the two methods The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to accommodate
the pipe to be installed The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling
machine Most HDD systems use a slurry to stabilize the walls of the borehole and to
reduce the frictional drag on the pipeline being installed Steering corrections are made in
the soils by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired orientation and then advancing
the shoe and string without rotation
In general the HDD pilot hole would be drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit
angles limited to a maximum of about 14 degrees measured from horizontal The drill unit
should be positioned at an appropriate setback distance such that the required depth
starting from the entry point can be achieved In general the product pipe or utility line
should be installed in one continuous operation preferably without pause and should

Geotechnical Condition
The technical feasibility of the HDD method depends on the characteristics of the
materials at the depth of the pipeline profile as well as the length of the drill path
and diameter of the pipe to be installed American Gas Association 1995 In
general gravel size material cannot be readily fluidized by the drilling fluid and may
not be stable enough to be cut and removed by the drilling fluid without caving in
Therefore subsoils containing a high percentage of gravel may present difficulties
with the utilization of the HDD method In addition it should be noted that
obstructions such as cobbles and boulders within the drill path might halt the
advance of the drill bit reamer or pipeline during pullback
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions along the intake pipe
consisted of fill material and residual soils extending to the maximum depth

For the HDD method the most important design consideration for the pipe is the
tensile stresses imposed on the pipe when it is pulled into place through the drill
hole The magnitude of these tensile stresses is a function of the length of the
installation as well as the subsurface conditions Other important pipe design
considerations are the internal and external pressures acting on the pipe during and
after installation In addition the selected pipe material must have adequate
corrosion resistance or be protected to resist internal and external corrosion
HDPE pipes generally have low tensile strength and may be susceptible to failure
during installation This limits the length of installation that can be completed with
an HDPE pipe Therefore it is critical that the pipe be selected with an adequate
allowable pulling tensile capacity Pipe stress analyses should be performed to
evaluate if an HDPE pipe can withstand the pulling stresses during pipe installation
and the external pressures hydrostatic and soil on the installed pipeline

Construction Considerations
Buried underground hazards including electrical cables water lines and
telecommunication cables may be present within the limits of the drill path during
the HDD operation Therefore the directional control of the pilot drill bit is critical to
the success of the proposed pipeline installation by HDD method These
underground utilities and structures should be identified located and avoided
during the project construction It is important that the HDD contractor utilize an
in hole steering tool in conjunction with a surface tracking system to provide
reliable horizontal and vertical directional control during the pipeline installation
The primary selection criteria for the HDD machine are thrust pullback and torque
capabilities However project related requirements such as length of installation
and diameter of the product pipe play an important role in the selection process
Depending on the length and size of the drill hole required for the HDD installation
the size of the drilling equipment and its capacity in rotational power as well as
pulling power should be checked by the contractor for the specific project

well compacted engineered fill Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
the soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project sites were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about
16 5 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate locations
of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
