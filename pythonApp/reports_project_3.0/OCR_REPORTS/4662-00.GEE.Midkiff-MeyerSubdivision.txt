2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the wesiern third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into
the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder
and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport of
sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base level change these sediment loads are left as deposits
The project site is located at the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano The project
site consists of both alluvial deposits and basaltic rock In situ chemical weathering has
occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a mantle of
residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth

3 1 Structure Foundations
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we recommend
that the new structures be supported on a slab on grade foundation with thickened edge
footings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new
compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
in general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge
of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Our laboratory tests indicate that the in situ soils have high moisture contents
Therefore it would be difficult to achieve the normal 90 percent relative compaction
requirement As a result the compaction requirement should be reduced to a minimum of
85 percent relative compaction Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of
the slab on grade foundation excavations should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials The over excavation may be backfilled with fill material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that footing settlements bearing on the recompacted
in situ soils or new compacted fills to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements
on the order of one half 72 inch

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth

provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on the
backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material
and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site excavated soils The
on site backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence If imported trench backfill is used the compaction requirement
should be increased to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

3 10 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled
against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the
potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible
and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away from the slab and footing
foundations

testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified

through A 3
