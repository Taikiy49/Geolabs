the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1 We understand that a highly visible rock outcropping
boulder located on the hillside above the Kalaheo Hillside residential subdivision was

of the rock outcropping and provide alternatives for the stabilization of the potential rockfall
hazard The scope of our services on this project is limited specifically to the stability of
the subject rock outcropping boulder only

Regional Geology
Based on our review of available geologic information the Oneawa Hills are
composed of basaltic rock and some breccia rock that are intruded by a series of volcanic
dike rock formations The basaltic rocks are believed to represent the caldera filling
materials of the old Koolau Volcano Therefore the rock materials are generally highly
weathered and deeply altered by volcanic activity in geologic history Most of the hillside
surface is composed of low relief friable soil like products with widely scattered hard rock
outcroppings
The deep weathering and volcanic alteration of the rocks have created generally
isolated outcroppings of massive hard rock that rise above the typical low relief hillside
terrain The potentially unstable rock outcropping at the site is typical of the hillside
condition and consists of a boulder size rock perched atop several smaller boulders that
are embedded in the surrounding earth The subject boulder is highly visible from the
street and is painted blue with the letter S Photographs of the potentially unstable
boulder and the surrounding ground conditions are provided on Plates 2 and 3
Site Description
Based on our observations the subject boulder is positioned precariously on top of
some smaller sized partially embedded rocks The subject boulder appears to be
undermined with some loss of support along the downslope perimeter of the boulder A
prominent wide open fracture is apparent at the base of the large boulder separating it
from the smaller rocks beneath The fracture is exposed to the predominant wind direction
and is therefore susceptible to wind and rain erosion that may further weaken the limited
contact with the surrounding ground surface and supporting rock pedestal The boulder
is approximately 6 feet long by 4 feet wide by about 3 feet thick and is somewhat flattened
and concave shaped on the bottom half The rock is massive and generally un fractured
and exhibits a very strong ping with minimal deterioration when hit with a rock hammer
The weight of the boulder is estimated at about 5 to 6 tons
The boulder does not appear to be embedded nor strongly attached to the
surrounding ground surface The generally rounded shape and massive character of the
boulder situated above a steep segment of the upper slope appears to have the potential
to fall and roll toward the base of the hillside In addition there are some other massive
rock outcrops located immediately below the subject boulder that protrude as ledges
from the slope These lower elevation outcrops although appear to be stable and firmly
embedded in appearance at this time form scarps of about 5 to 15 feet in height and have
the potential to initiate significant bouncing motion in rocks that may fall from above
Therefore it is our professional opinion that the subject boulder may pose a significant
potential rockfall hazard

RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our site reconnaissance the subject boulder appears to present a
potential rockfall hazard To eliminate the potential rockfall hazard the boulder should be
completely removed from the site An alternative to elimination of the potential rockfall
hazard which would reduce the potential rockfall hazard is to stabilize the boulder
in place It is our professional opinion that the subject boulder should be removed to
provide a permanent cost effective solution to the potential rockfall hazard
In place stabilization of the boulder may be considered however the cost is
anticipated to be similar to the cost of removal and the on site boulder stabilization would
have an environmental impact on the surrounding terrain and leave open the potential for
possible future degradation of the stabilization system In addition some periodic
inspection and or maintenance of the boulder stabilization may be needed to monitor the
integrity of the stabilization system Therefore we believe that the better mitigation
alternative for the potential rockfall situation is complete removal of the boulder from the
site
Boulder Removal
Removal of the intact 5 to 6 ton boulder by helicopter airlift is not practical due to
the limited lift capacity of commercial heavy lift helicopters which is only about 2 tons
Removal of the boulder by rolling it downhill is not advisable due to the difficulty in
controlling the path of the falling boulder and debris generated located adjacent to
developed areas Therefore the boulder should be broken into manageable pieces for
controlled removal from the site
Destruction of the boulder breaking the boulder into smaller and more manageable
pieces would likely require the use of a pneumatic hammer and or the use of a special
rock joint expansion compound to fracture the rock and facilitate the mechanical
destruction of the rock The rock would initially need to be restrained in place prior to the
work to reduce the potential for inadvertently causing the rock to fall The anchorage
system employed to temporarily restrain the rock during removal preparation may consist
of reinforced wire or cable mesh with grouted anchors or rock bolts installed into the
surrounding ground surface
It is envisioned that a small air compressor tools and materials could be airlifted
using a heavy lift commercial helicopter to the site as overhead obstructions generally
are absent and the terrain appears to be suitable for airlift support A suitable disposal site
for the boulder fragments located on nearby flat low elevation ground should be selected
prior to the removal process

On Site Stabilization
If desired the on site stabilization of the boulder could be achieved by two
alternative approaches The first alternative involves the construction of a permanent
anchor system to retain the boulder in its present position The anchor system may
consist of a special cable or ring net which is anchored to the surrounding terrain by the
use of drilled and grouted anchors embedded about 6 to 10 feet in the ground The
anchor system may be visible from a distance and the life expectancy is believed to be on
the order of about 10 to 20 years depending on corrosion which is difficult to predict The
anchor system may also impede hiking access around the knoll or present a trip hazard It
should be noted that permits may be required for the construction of the anchor system
Furthermore construction of the anchor system should account for the possible future
erosion of the existing pedestal support that the boulder is currently sitting on
The second alternative involves carefully moving the boulder from its precarious
pedestal position to a stable cradle position located adjacent to its current location
This alternative would also require the construction of a temporary restraint system to
avoid loss of control of the boulder during the boulder movement Based on our site
observation some generally flat terrain located adjacent to the boulder at the top of the
knoll may be prepared to accommodate the boulder in a relatively stable cradle position
that has less susceptibility to erosion and initiation of rolling movement
The site preparation for the cradle may consist of excavating a shallow pit in the
ground surface that the boulder could be positioned in with the concave and elongated
side of the boulder facing downward This alternative may cause the least surface
disturbance to the site and does not involve intentionally breaking the boulder or removing
it from the site The relocation stabilization is considered to be a long term remedial
alternative however subsequent erosion around the man made boulder cradle is difficult
to assess Therefore there is the remote possibility that erosion or changes in the
topography may work to de stabilize the boulder with the passage of time The boulder
should be seated at a minimum depth of about 2 feet below the lowest adjacent ground
and a minimum distance of about 5 feet from the top of the slopes
For safety reasons we wish to emphasize that an adequate temporary restraint
system must be utilized prior to initiating work that may involve movement vibration or
excavation around the boulder Provided that adequate safety restraint measures are
effectively implemented it is our opinion that the removal or stabilization of the subject
rock outcropping boulder may be viable alternatives
In general the site is accessible by a relatively short foot trail that traverses the
ridgeline which leads to the subject boulder location However it should be noted that
heavier equipment such as an air compressor and restraint anchor materials may need
to be airlifted using heavy lift commercial helicopters to the site

LIMITATIONS
The findings and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from site observations only Variation in the surface and subsurface
conditions between our observations may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until construction is underway If variations then
appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the findings and recommendations
provided in this letter report
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials rock slopes deteriorate
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes wet dry and hot cold
cycles and erosion conditions Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting
from natural processes weathering wet dry and hot cold cycles and erosion conditions
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time Therefore
the findings and recommendations contained in this letter report may be used only within a
reasonable time from the date of issuance of this letter report Land use site conditions
and or other factors may change with the passage of time Therefore additional work to
evaluate the applicability of the findings and recommendations contained in this letter
report due to changes with the passage of time will be required
