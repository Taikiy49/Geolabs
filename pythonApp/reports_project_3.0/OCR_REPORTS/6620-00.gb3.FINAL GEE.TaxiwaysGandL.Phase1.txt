SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Widen Taxilanes G and L – Phase 1 project at the Honolulu 
International Airport (HNL) in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of taxilane pavements, 
retaining structures, box culvert, and site grading only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
Taxilanes G and L at the Honolulu International Airport were constructed in the 
1970’s for Group III aircrafts.  As part of the airport modernization plan, the taxilanes will 
be widened and separation will be increased to accommodate larger Group IV and 
Group V aircrafts in association with the construction of the new Mauka Concourse.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in 
age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became 
extinct while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lava. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the 
Honolulu Coastal Plain. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the continental glaciers advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present 
stand. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
Glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and 
deposition. The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal 
deposits, and induration of the calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial 
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils 
under relatively high-energy conditions.

During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial delta and fans seaward by deposition of alluvium over the 
previously deposited lagoonal sediments. 
The geology of the project area has been further complicated by the deposition of 
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of vents of the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series, which included the nearby Aliamanu-Salt Lake-Makalapa vent. These 
eruptions were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations. 
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy 
estuarine or lagoonal environments, resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments 
along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways. Urban 
development and reclamation projects have since altered the natural formation of the 
area. 
2.2 
Site Description  
The project site is at Taxilanes G and L near the Inter-Island Terminal (IIT) within 
the Honolulu International Airport (HNL) on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The site begins 
at the intersection with Taxiway A and extends north to the west side of the IIT. 
Taxilanes G and L consist of asphaltic concrete pavements that have generally 
low to moderate distresses.  Taxilanes G and L are parallel taxilanes that generally 
have a straight alignment near the Inter-Island Terminal then doglegs near the south of 
the IIT.  The taxilanes straighten near the intersection with Taxiway A.  Taxilanes G and 
L are about 140 feet apart at the north side of the site.  The separation between 
Taxilanes G and L is about 200 feet between the dogleg portion of Taxilanes G and L.  
The taxilane separation increases to about 300 feet near Taxiway A.  Taxilanes G and L 
are separated by a grass median from the north side of the dogleg to the intersection 
with Taxiway A. 
Based on the topographic survey map provided, the project site is relatively flat 
with existing ground surface elevations ranging between about +16 and +20 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL).

2.4 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
16 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 110, 201 through 204, 301, and 
302, extending to depths varying from about 3.8 to 33 feet below the existing ground 
surface. In addition, 4 pavement corings were drilled along the dogleg portion of 
Taxilanes G and L.  The approximate boring and coring locations are shown on the 
enlarged Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. 
Based on the borings drilled between Taxiway A and the Study Area, fill was 
encountered below the pavement structural sections.  The fill consisted of very dense 
sandy gravel (basaltic and coralline), dense to very dense coralline silty sand, and very 
stiff to hard clayey silt extending to the maximum depths explored of about 3 to 5.3 feet 
below the existing pavement surface.  Medium stiff, highly plastic silty clay (CH) was 
encountered in Boring No. 3H drilled south of the Inter-Island Terminal.  This boring was 
drilled during a previous study. 
The borings drilled along the west side of the Study Area generally encountered 
fill consisting of very stiff to hard clayey silt with some gravel below the pavement 
structural sections.  The borings drilled along the east side of the Study Area generally 
encountered fill consisting of hard clayey silt and very dense silty sand below the 
pavement structural sections. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at Manuwai Canal consisted of fill 
extending to depths of about 3.5 to 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The fill 
consisted of medium dense to dense gravel and sand and stiff silty clay.  Below the fill, 
lagoonal deposits consisting of soft clay and silt and loose silty sand and gravel were 
encountered extending to depths of about 9 to 10 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  The lagoonal deposits were underlain by soft to medium hard coral formation 
extending to the maximum depths explored of about 31 to 33 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  The coral formation was interbedded with layers of medium dense to 
dense silty sand. 
We encountered groundwater in the borings drilled at depths varying from 
approximately 7 to 9.3 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field

2.5 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Testing 
In addition to borehole drilling and sampling, our field exploration program also 
included performance of Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing at nine boring 
locations to provide additional data and information for our evaluation and analyses. 
The Dynamic Cone Penetration test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ 
resistance to penetration. The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground 
by repeatedly striking it with a 17.6-lb (8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 
22.6 inches (575 mm). The penetration of the cone is measured after each blow and is 
recorded to provide a continuous measure of shear resistance up to about 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Test results can be correlated to California Bearing Ratio, 
in-situ density, resilient modulus, and bearing capacity. 
The DCP tests were generally conducted at 1-inch (25.4-mm) discrete depth 
intervals between depths of about 16 to 60 inches below the existing pavement surface 
at the test locations.  Field logs of the DCP tests are included in Appendix B of this 
report for reference.

Circular No. 150/5320-6E “Airport Pavement Design and Evaluation” prepared by the

In accordance with the design guidelines provided in the Advisory Circular 
mentioned earlier, stabilized base and subbase courses are necessary for new 
pavements designed to accommodate jet aircraft weighing 100,000 pounds or more. 
Therefore, we recommend using stabilized pavement sections for the project.

analysis using the FAA elastic layered design approach.  The FAARFIELD pavement 
design program automatically assigned modulus to the AC (P-401), PCC (P-501), 
AC stabilized base (P-401/P-403) and base rock (P-209) materials, and these values by 
default cannot be changed.  The results of the FAARFIELD – Airport Pavement Design 
are presented on Plates D-1 through D-8 of Appendix D.

3.5 
Quality Control 
Quality control on Hot Mix Asphalt (HMA) (AC) construction is critical to the 
long-term performance of the asphalt pavements, regardless of overlay construction or 
new AC pavement construction.  Asphalt concrete pavement should be constructed in 
strict accordance with FAA P-401, especially under Section 401-5.2 related to mat 
density and joint density. 
The paving contractor should submit a job mix formula (JMF) prepared by an 
approved independent material testing laboratory having at least 10 years of experience 
in designing asphalt concrete mix.  The JMF should not be more than 90 days old at the 
time of the submittal, and should have been produced with samples of materials 
submitted by the contractor to be used on the project, not from stock materials the 
laboratory has on hand. 
Asphalt concrete should be placed in lifts of no more than 3 to 4 inches thick 
each, but should be no thinner than three times the nominal maximum size aggregate. 
Thicker asphalt concrete layers should be placed in two or more lifts of approximately 
equal thickness.  In addition, asphalt concrete should be compacted to meet the 
requirements of Section 401-8.1. 
3.6 
Pavement Subgrade Preparation 
The pavement subgrades, consisting of granular materials, should be scarified to 
a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  If 
unsuitable soils (such as clay and soft/loose soils) are encountered at the pavement 
subgrade, the unsuitable soils should be over-excavated a minimum depth of about 
3.5 feet and replaced with structural fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as 
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance

We understand that the existing box culvert crossing Taxiway A will be extended

dewatering should be expected during construction of the box culvert extension

during the dewatering operations.

3.7.3 Foundation Settlements 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations would result primarily from elastic 
compression of the drilled shaft and subgrade response. We estimate that the total 
settlement of the drilled shaft supported foundation to be less than 0.5 inches with 
differential settlements between drilled shafts not exceeding about one-half that 
amount. We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur 
as the loads are applied. 
3.7.4 Construction Considerations 
The performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the contractor's 
method of construction and construction procedures. As a result of these potential 
variations, we recommend a Geolabs representative be present to observe the 
drilled shaft installation during construction. In our opinion, the following may have a 
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations. 
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper

construction techniques are important. The contractor should exercise care in 
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
Based on our field exploration, medium hard coral formation was encountered in 
our borings extending to the maximum depths explored.  Therefore, special drilling 
tools for coring into coral formation will be required. 
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at -0.2 to -2.3 feet MSL at the time of 
our exploration. Therefore, groundwater is anticipated in the drilled shaft hole.  A 
low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should be used 
to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. The 
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for 
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix. In addition, the concrete 
should be placed promptly after drilling (within 24 hours after drilling of the holes) to 
reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled holes. Furthermore, 
drilling adjacent to a recently constructed shaft (within three shaft diameters of the 
recently constructed drilled shaft) should not commence until the concrete for the 
recently constructed drilled shaft has cured for a minimum of 24 hours. 
Based on our experience with drilled shaft foundations, the actual volume of 
concrete required to fill the shafts may be appreciably more than the theoretical 
concrete volume. Based on our experience with drilled shafts in similar subsurface 
conditions, we envision that an average grout factor on the order of about 20 to 
40 percent over the theoretical grout volume may need to be accounted for the 
drilled shafts. 
It is imperative that a Geolabs representative be present at the project site to 
observe the drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction. Although 
the drilled shafts are designed based primarily on skin friction, the bottom of the 
drilled hole should be relatively free of loose materials prior to placement of 
concrete. Therefore, it is necessary for Geolabs to observe the drilled shaft 
installation operations to confirm the assumed subsurface conditions and should be 
designated a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of the 
International Building Code (2006 Edition).

3.7.5 Trial Shaft Program 
A trial shaft program normally is required and highly recommended for the drilled 
shafts. Therefore, we recommend undertaking the trial shaft program to fulfill the 
following objectives. 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the 
contractor to install the drilled shafts into the coral formation and coralline 
materials. 
To assess the contractor’s method of placing and extracting the temporary 
steel casing for the drilled shaft, if appropriate. 
To assess the contractor’s method of tremie concrete placement. 
To achieve these objectives, we recommend that the trial shaft program consist of 
drilling one sacrificial trial shaft at a selected location. The trial shaft location should 
be near, but outside of, box culvert foundations.  
We also anticipate that temporary steel casing will likely be required during the trial 
shaft installation to reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes. After 
drilling the trial shaft, the trial shaft should be backfilled with unreinforced concrete 
in the same manner that the production shafts are to be constructed. 
In addition, coring of the trial shaft should be conducted to examine the integrity of 
the concrete placed utilizing the tremie method. Compressive strength testing of the 
concrete should be conducted on selected core samples to confirm that the 
concrete placed in the drilled hole meets the required concrete strength at 28 days. 
A Geolabs representative should be present during the trial shaft program to 
evaluate the contractor’s method of drilled shaft installation and to evaluate the 
subsurface materials encountered.

As part of the pre-construction activities, we recommend conducting one static load 
test for the project. The results of the load test will be used to confirm or modify the 
estimated tip elevations of the production drilled shafts.  The load test shaft should 
be structurally reinforced.

one percent in accordance with ASTM D1883.

accordance with ASTM D 1883.  The excavated on-site granular materials may 
be used as a source of structural fill provided that it meets the requirements 
mentioned above.

3.9.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at 
least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is 
the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory 
rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
3.10 Underground Utilities 
We envision new on-site utility lines (i.e. water, sewer, and drain lines) and utility 
line connections may be required for the project. We anticipate most of the trenches for 
utilities will be excavated in the fills.  
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
free-draining granular materials (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the pipes for 
uniform support. Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to 
about 12 inches above the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce 
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of the pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes 
and improper placement of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site 
materials with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or structural fill materials. The backfill 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent

3.11 Drainage 
The finished grades along the taxilane should be sloped to shed water away from 
the pavement section and to reduce the potential for ponding. Excessive landscape 
watering near the pavement should also be avoided. These drainage requirements are 
essential for the proper performance of the above pavement recommendations since 
ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of 
strength. In addition, areas around the pavement should be properly backfilled to reduce 
the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface. 
In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should 
be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the pavement.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
twenty borings/corings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 110, 201 through 204, 
301 and 302 and Coring Nos. 1 through 4. The borings extended to depths of about 
3.8 to 33 feet below the existing ground surface and the corings extended to depths of 
about 2.5 to 3 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  Enlarged Site Plans are provided on 
Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-20.  Logs of borings from our previous explorations are presented 
on Plates A-21 through A-31. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing was performed in 13 boring locations 
(Boring Nos. 101 through 109 and 201 through 204) to obtain pavement support 
characteristics of the in-situ soils. The tests were performed using a Salem Dual Mass 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer to a depth of about 1.3 to 5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the 
lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The 
upper rod holds a 17.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning 
of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone 
is just below the testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. 
The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the 
strength of the soil at the test location.  The number of blows for a minimum of 1-inch 
penetration is recorded.  The process is continued until the desired depth of testing is 
reached or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply 
the penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as 
millimeters per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated 
with common pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the DCP Logs, 
Plates C-1.1 through C-13.
