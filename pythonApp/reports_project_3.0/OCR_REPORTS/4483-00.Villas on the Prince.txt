INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Villas on the Prince project within the Queen Emma s Bluffs Subdivision in
the Princeville area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining structures and site
preparation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located within the Queen Emma s Bluffs Subdivision
in the Princeville area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii Based on the information provided
we understand that it is proposed to develop the project site including Lot Nos 1 through
16 and 90 into a multi family housing facility with a total of 41 units The proposed
development will consist of 16 two story side by side townhouse buildings in duplex triplex
and quadplex configurations The new buildings will be of conventional wood framing with
faux stucco exteriors and tile roofs In addition infrastructure such as access roadways
and some utilities were already in place at the time of our field exploration
Based on the information provided we understand that fill materials have been
previously placed at the project site to raise the original ground to the existing grades
during the mass grading operations of the entire subdivision development Therefore
special attention was given to the quality of the previously placed fill materials and its bearing
soil conditions as it would affect the foundation design and support of the proposed new
structures Based on the graded topography and existing site conditions we envision that
minimal site grading work will be required for the project construction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial sediments of
the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded from
groups of vents aligned in north south trends at various locations Associated with the vents
are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
growth and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water
was available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the current sea Conversely
as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because more water was available
The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes and the
fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of
the sea which were both higher and lower relative to present sea level of Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly
higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing
reefs
The project site is situated on a broad gently sloping plateau along the north shore of
the Island of Kauai The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
the lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers The weathering process
has formed a mantle of residual soils near the surface In general residual soils are
GEOLABS INC

composed mainly of clayey and silty materials which are typical of the tropical weathering
of volcanic rocks The residual soils generally grade to saprolite and basaltic rock formation
with depth The residual soils near the surface were subsequently covered with surface fills
of similar materials by human activities

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff clayey
soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted clayey fill soils may be used to support the proposed new townhouse buildings
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit slight to low
shrink swell potential when subjected to normal fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To
reduce the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from the
swelling of the near surface soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Use
of the vibratory action of a compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid inducing a
pumping subgrade condition The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage
cracks prior to placement of cushion fill and or slab concrete Our geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations retaining structures and site
preparation are further discussed in the following sections of this report

Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new
construction An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted stiff clayey fill
soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the stiff clayey fill soils the bottom of footing excavations
should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement
of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom
of footing excavations the material should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with select granular materials
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In lieu of backfilling the over
excavation with compacted granular fill the footing bottom may be extended down to the
underlying competent material It should be emphasized that if the footing subgrades are
saturated or softened after the visual observations probing or compaction testing due to
inclement weather the footing subgrades may need to be over excavated to expose firm
ground and backfilled with compacted fill As an alternative to backfilling with compacted fill
the bottom of footing may be extended deeper to bear on the firm over excavated subgrade
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
conditions the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the
slope Footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be embedded deep
enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should be
designed to resist the additional structural loads
GEOLABS INC
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted stiff fill soils Resistance
due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located
on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff fill soils as recommended herein to be on the order
of about one 1 inch with differential settlement on the order of about one half 2 inch
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with slight to low expansion potential when subjected to normal moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture
content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared in accordance with
the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The
GEOLABS INC
W O 4483 00 Page 9
prepared subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below the slabs to serve as a
capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier
such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the No 3B Fine gravel or BTB layer
Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide
protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provisions of the
2 inches of moist sand above the vapor membrane will be left up to the discretion of the
project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage slabs we
recommend that a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase be provided in lieu of the 4 inch thick
cushion layer The 2 inch moist fine sand and vapor barrier may also be omitted for these
slabs The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus
of subgrade reaction of about 200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may
be used for the compacted aggregate subbase Provisions should be made for proper load
transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic
Exterior concrete walkways will be required for the proposed project We recommend
that concrete walkways be supported on a minimum of 4 inches of select granular fill
material The select granular fill material should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative
GEOLABS INC

compaction Crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The thickened edges of slab adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should
be backfilled tightly against the slab with on site soil and graded to divert water away from
the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately
around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated
drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site clayey silt or
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches
of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the wails
Site Preparation
Based on the existing site conditions we anticipate minimal site grading work will
be required for the proposed development At the on set of earthwork the area within the
contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared Debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site Soft and yielding areas encountered
during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and deductive
unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in
the over excavation quantities
After clearing the subgrade surface within the future slab on grade limits should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
GEOLABS INC
W O 4483 00 Page 13
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Due to the high moisture contents
of the in situ soils and the generally wet environment in the project area use of the vibratory
action of a compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid inducing a pumping subgrade
condition
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping subgrade
may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping subgrade
conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately 25 square feet
of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a woven geotextile
fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the subgrade prior to
placement of subsequent lifts of fill
Slab subgrades should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks until covered by
slab cushion fill or concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soil be re scarified and prepared as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and
poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well
compacted fills
The on site clayey soils generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of
general fill material if needed Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results it
appears that the in situ soils have high moisture contents Therefore drying or aeration of
the excavated materials will be necessary prior to their use as engineered fills Aeration to
lower the soil moisture content and additional compaction effort with thinner fill lifts may be
necessary to achieve the specified compaction and wouid generally reduce the rate of fill
placement for this project
Imported fill material if needed should consist of non expansive select granular
material such as crushed basalt or coral The material should be well graded from coarse

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation

also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from
the slabs and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings to depths of about 11 5 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
