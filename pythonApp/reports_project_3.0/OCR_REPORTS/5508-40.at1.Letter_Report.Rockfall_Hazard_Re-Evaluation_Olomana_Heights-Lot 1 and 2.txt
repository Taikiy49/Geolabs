REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two separate
submarine shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and Koolau
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 5 million years
ago After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed
by cinder and tuff cone formations These more recent series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series and include some prominent landform features such as the
Diamond Head and Koko Head Craters

During the latter stages of the Koolau Volcano eruption the central Koolau Caldera
was filled with thick ponded lavas and intruded by a myriad of narrow lava feeding volcanic
dike structures which have been collectively identified as the Kailua Volcanic Series The
Koolau Caldera structure is believed to have encompassed the entire Kailua area
extending roughly from the existing Koolau Pali northeastward toward the existing
coastline and beyond Following the deposition of the caldera filling lavas the caldera
structure subsided and the basaltic rocks of the Kailua Volcanic Series were deeply
altered by percolating underground fluids and gases In addition surface streams were
incised and dissection and erosion of the exposed rock materials commenced Therefore
only scattered erosional remnants of the original caldera structure and lava filling deposits
remain exposed as relatively low hills surrounding the Kailua area which include
Mt Olomana located in the project vicinity
The project site is situated on the lower northeasterly facing foothills of
Mt Olomana as indicated on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on our review of
available geologic map information the majority of the project site is underlain by
weathered basaltic rock and derived soils generally consisting of reddish tan to brown
colored silty and clayey residual saprolitic soils The near surface soils represent the
extremely to completely weathered basaltic rock products belonging to the Kailua
Volcanic Series
The basalt rock underlying the project site is unlike the typical layered basalt rock
belonging to the Koolau Volcanic Series Sequential layers of interbedded basalt clinker
and hard basalt rock layers typify the Koolau Volcanic Series The basalt rock of the
Kailua Volcanic Series generally consists of thick lavas that are locally brecciated and
intruded by volcanic lava feeder dike structures The near surface basalt rock
exposures encountered at the project site appear to be generally devoid of weaker
interbedded clinker layers As a result of the deep weathering and erosion of the Kailua
Volcanic Series parent rock materials the lower foothills of Mt Olomana typically are
underlain by residual and saprolitic soils containing some remnant surface rock
outcroppings and isolated boulders
The majority of the project site appears to be underlain by very stiff to hard
residual and saprolitic silty and clayey soils derived from the deep weathering and
hydrothermal alteration of the parent basaltic rock The central portion of the project site
consists of a broad lower elevation basin surrounded by hilly terrain The near surface
soils within the basin region appear to consist of stiff silty and clayey alluvial deposits
with some buried boulders Hard rock formations and individual surface boulders
outcroppings protruding from the surrounding terrain generally reside at scattered
locations within the upper elevation portions of Lot Nos 1 and 2 Some of these surface
boulders and rock outcrop formations have the potential to become destabilized with the
passage of time and could potentially become falling rock hazards

GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
The Olomana Heights Subdivision is on the windward side of the Island of Oahu in
the Kailua Waimanalo area of the District of Koolaupoko The existing Old Kalanianaole
Road and Kalanianaole Highway bound the subdivision toward the northeast Waimanalo
Ridge toward the south and other undeveloped lands toward the northwest through the
south of the project site as indicated on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project site
encompasses Lot Nos 1 and 2 within the subdivision and is bounded by Lot Nos 3 and
5 to the north and east respectively Waimanalo Ridge to the south and the slopes of
Mt Olomana to the west as shown on Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site slopes down from southwest to northeast with ground
surface elevations ranging from about 750 feet MSL to about 300 feet MSL
respectively Previous mass grading and erosion control operations have been conducted
at the project site sometime between the Year 2013 and the time of our site
reconnaissance An updated grading plan was not available at the time this report was
prepared
Geolabs previously conducted a rockfall hazard evaluation for the overall
subdivision area and recommended installing rockfall impact barriers in our report titled
Geotechnical Engineering Consultation Services Slope Hazard Evaluation and Potential
Hazard Mitigation Olomana Agricultural Subdivision Kailua Oahu Hawaii dated
The two rockfall impact barriers are denoted as Segments A and B
as indicated on the Rockfall Barrier As Built Plan Plate 3
The project site can generally be divided into two portions in terms of type and
extent of vegetation growth The southwestern corner of the site is mostly untouched by
grading operations and mainly consists of medium to large size trees with limited low lying
vegetative undergrowth Northern and eastern portions of the site with signs of previous
grading operations generally consist of heavy California grass growth with occasional
small to medium size trees

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our reconnaissance and re evaluation of the project site we believe
the existing slopes within the project limits may contain multiple rockfall hazards These
rockfall hazards are more evident in the southwest section of Lot No 1 within Rock
Deposit Areas 1 and 3 Nearly all of the rockfall hazards that were identified in our
previous evaluation in these areas were still observed In addition we encountered
previously unidentified boulders within Rock Deposit Area 1 that may present additional
hazards
Due to the extensive California grass growth within Rock Deposit Areas
and 6 we were unable to access or observe some of the previously identified rock
deposit areas Therefore we believe that potential rock fall hazards may still exist within
these rock deposit areas More extensive vegetation clearing may be required in order
to observe and locate all the potential rockfall hazards within project limits
Additionally we observed and evaluated the two existing rockfall barrier
segments that currently exist on the project site In general there are numerous
locations along both barrier segments that are missing shackle connections between
fence mesh splices and connections between the fence mesh with the top and bottom
wire ropes Also extensive punctures along the mesh fence and damages to the barrier
posts were observed In addition the 4 to 8 feet grading cuts close to the footprint of
some of the posts foundations may diminish the foundation s passive resistance We
believe that deficiencies along the barrier segments are extensive and they decrease
the effectiveness of the barrier systems during a rockfall event
As previously mentioned during our site reconnaissance we observed that
nearly all of the rockfall hazards identified in our previous study beyond the rockfall
impact barriers still exist Additionally new potential hazards were identified during our
re evaluation Therefore we recommend that the existing rockfall impact barriers
remain in place to mitigate future potential rockfall hazards In addition based on the
deficiencies observed along both barrier segments we recommend that the barrier be
inspected and repaired by a qualified contractor as soon as possible to restore the
barriers to their original function These repairs include but are not limited to the grading
of the cut slope in front of post foundations to restore the passive resistance of the soil
repair of punctures and opening along the fence mesh repair of damaged posts and
anchors and replacing missing shackle connections Geolabs should be present to
observe the implementation of the repairs and to re evaluate the effectiveness of the
Rockfall Impact Barrier system including the posts and foundations
