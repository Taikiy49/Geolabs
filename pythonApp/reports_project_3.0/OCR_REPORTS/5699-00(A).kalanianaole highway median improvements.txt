SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Kalanianaole Highway Median
Improvements Vicinity of Olomana Golf Course project Based on our field exploration we
anticipate that the majority of the pavement subgrade soils for the highway widening will
generally consist of silty clays
We understand that using portable concrete barriers during construction will require
performing the widening in longitudinal increments across the widened section The
concrete barriers will be used for safety reasons to shield motorists as well as to protect
the work zone Because of the difficulties in constructing the road in longitudinal
increments using untreated permeable base course the use of untreated permeable base
course within the structural pavement section was not considered Also the existing
pavement section does not have an untreated permeable base layer Based on the traffic
data provided the R value of the subgrade soils and the economic analyses performed
the costs of flexible and rigid pavement sections were relatively close Since the existing
pavement along Kalanianaole Highway consists of a flexible pavement section and the
costs for flexible and rigid pavement sections were relatively close we recommend using a
flexible pavement structural section for the new pavements for the roadway widening The
recommended pavement structural section consisting of 4 0 inches of AC on 7 5 inches of
ACB over 15 0 inches of ASB should be used specifically for the design of pavements for
the Kalanianaole Highway Median Improvements Vicinity of Olomana Golf Course
project
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific pavement design recommendations

1 1 Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed
for the design of new pavements for the Kalanianaole Highway Median Improvements
Vicinity of Olomana Golf Course project in the District of Waimanalo on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on _ the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our

only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project is along Kalanianaole Highway between the Old Kalanianaole
Highway and Flamingo Road in the Waimanalo area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project includes widening the eastbound lanes of the highway for a distance of about

retaining wall may be needed near the eastern end of the project

an existing slope about 300 feet east from the Olomana Golf Course entrance We
understand that a Concrete Rubble Masonry CRM retaining wall near the property line
may be utilized in this area to accommodate the roadway widening Based on the

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago After a long period of
volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield
volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone
formations These more recent series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series and
include some prominent landform features as the Diamond Head and Koko Head Craters
During the latter stages of the Koolau Volcano eruption the central
Koolau Caldera was filled with thick ponded lavas and intruded by a myriad of narrow
lava feeding volcanic dike structures which have been collectively identified as the
Kailua Volcanic Series The Koolau Caldera structure is believed to have encompassed
the entire Kailua area extending roughly from the existing Koolau Pali northeastward
toward the existing coastline and beyond Following the deposition of the caldera filling
lavas the caldera structure subsided and the basaltic rocks of the Kailua Volcanic
Series were deeply altered by percolating underground fluids and gases In addition
streams were incised and dissection and erosion of the exposed rock materials
commenced Thus only scattered erosional remnants of the original caldera structure
and lava filling deposits remain exposed as relatively low hills surrounding the
Kailua area which include Mt Olomana near the project vicinity
The project site is situated near the base of the southeastern slope of
Mt Olomana as indicated on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on our review of
available geological map information the majority of the project site is underlain by
weathered basaltic rock and derived soils generally consisting of residual saprolitic

2 2 Site Description
The highway widening project site is near Olomana Golf Course on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii As part of the project the eastbound lanes of Kalanianaole Highway will
be widened The roadway widening will generally start from about 600 feet south of
Old Kalanianaole Highway and end at about 500 feet south from the entrance of
Olomana Golf Course as shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
The existing Kalanianaole Highway within the project site generally consists of
one traffic lane in each direction with asphaltic concrete paved shoulders Concrete
traffic barriers are within the median area north of the Olomana Golf Course entrance
The existing asphaltic concrete roadway generally slopes down towards the
south at an inclination of about one hundred horizontal to six vertical 100H 6V Based
on the topographic map provided the elevations of the existing roadway grade range
from about 41 to 92 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The existing pavement generally
appeared to be in relatively good condition at the time of our field exploration

the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association Thickness Design
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements

3 4 Design Pavement Section
We understand that using portable concrete barriers during construction will
require performing the widening in longitudinal increments across the widened section

Preparation Below Pavement Section
We anticipate constructing the majority of the new roadway pavements at or near
the existing grades or in a cut condition In general the area within the contract grading
limits should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation
debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
properly disposed off site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for
contaminating the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose
stiff and or dense materials The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
aggregate subbase materials The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed off site
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated
where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate
subbase course layer The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T 180
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend scarifying the soils and preparing again as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with

3 6 Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches
may be re used as a source of general fill material Imported fill materials if required
should consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coralline or
basaltic materials The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The materials should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
3 7 Compaction Requirements
In general fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Aggregate
subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to the requirements
stipulated in Subsection 703 17 of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
and Bridge Construction 2005 HSS The aggregate subbase should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level
loose lifts and compacted to no less than 95 percent of the maximum density according
to Section 203 03 C 2 Relative Compaction Test of the HSS
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basaltic
aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined
in accordance with AASHTO T 209 ASTM D 2041

3 8 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils As a minimum the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding
should not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where landscaping is
planned adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend constructing a subdrain
system to collect excess water from landscaping irrigation and to reduce the potential
for migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section
Subdrains should be provided in areas where seepage is observed during and
after grading or as recommended by the Engineer during construction Where needed
subdrains should conform to the requirements of Section 605 Underdrains of the State
of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005 HSS As a
minimum the subdrain pipes should consist of 6 inch diameter perforated pipes
conforming to Subsection 706 12 of the HSS with the perforations facing down The
subdrains should be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for proper
discharge

The subsurface conditions along the roadway widening were explored by drilling
and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about
11 5 and 20 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
