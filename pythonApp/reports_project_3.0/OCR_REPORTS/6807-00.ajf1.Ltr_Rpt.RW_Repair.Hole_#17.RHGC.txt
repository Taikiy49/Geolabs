REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the 
sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such 
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea. The processes of erosion and 
deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations. When the sea 
level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly lower, and valleys were 
carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level was high, the erosional 
base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope 
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach 
the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. In general, stream flows in Hawaii 
are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water for 
very short durations. Because of this, transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk 
of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, 
gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional base levels change, these sediment loads 
are left as deposits. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The existing washed-out retaining wall generally is near the southeastern side of 
the Hole #17 Blue Tee Box adjacent to Makawao Stream. The location of the site is shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Roughly 40 linear feet of the existing CRM retaining wall has 
been damaged and washed-out along with a portion of the existing backfill material, which 
has been eroded roughly 7 feet laterally toward the tee box.  
Existing ground surface elevations behind and below the existing retaining wall are 
roughly +530 and +518 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), respectively. At the time of our field 
exploration, the site was covered with mown lawn grass near the tee box area and was 
heavily vegetated in the Makawao Stream below. In addition, numerous rounded boulders 
up to about 2 feet in diameter were observed in the adjacent stream.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our boring drilled at the existing washed-out retaining wall site generally 
encountered fill materials consisting of medium dense poorly graded sands and medium 
stiff to stiff clayey silts underlain by alluvial soil deposits consisting of stiff silty clays and 
dense silty gravel. A relatively hard boulder was encountered at a depth of about 16 feet 
below the existing ground surface that could not be penetrated with the auger. 
We encountered groundwater in the boring at a depth of about 14 feet below the 
existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. Considering that the project is 
located adjacent to a stream, the groundwater levels likely will vary in response to the 
water level in the stream. In addition, it should be noted that groundwater levels are 
subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water 
runoff, and other factors.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration encountered relatively stiff ground conditions at the existing 
washed-out retaining wall site. Based on our evaluation of the materials encountered, the 
excavated on-site clayey soils may be re-used as a source of fill provided they are free of 
vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum 
dimension.  
It should be noted that the project site is located in a high rainfall environment 
throughout the year; therefore, the on-site soils will be constantly in a very moist to wet 
condition. We believe it may be difficult to achieve adequate compaction without 
significant aeration of the on-site soils. However, it may not be feasible and practical to 
aerate the on-site soils, and it may be advantageous to replace the on-site soils with 
select granular fill materials when used for structural purposes. 
We understand it is desired to replace the retaining wall with a segmental retaining 
wall system, such as a Keystone retaining wall. The Keystone retaining wall system is a 
composite wall system, which utilizes high density polyethylene, or other reinforcing 
elements, to reinforce the backfill zone and improve the shear strength of the reinforced 
soil zone. This composite system essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to 
tolerate significant total and differential settlements. In addition, Keystone retaining walls 
also are desirable due to the flexibility of the wall, ease of construction, high load carrying 
capacity, and economy. 
Design of the retaining wall system will need to take into consideration both the 
external and internal stability of the structure. In evaluating external stability, the retaining 
wall must satisfy four stability conditions: (1) bearing failure, (2) translational sliding, 
(3) overturning stability, and (4) overall slope stability. Geotechnical design parameters to 
evaluate these stability conditions are presented below. Some of the geotechnical 
parameters necessary in evaluating the internal stability of the retaining wall are presented 
in the "Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials" section. 
Keystone Retaining Wall Foundations 
Based on discussions with the project ownership, the Keystone retaining wall 
foundation design should consider the potential for scour at the wall repair location. Based 
on this direction, the Keystone retaining wall foundation should be embedded to a depth of 
at least 24 inches below the estimated scour depth at the wall repair location. It should be

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend 
utilizing an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for 
design of the Keystone retaining wall bearing on the underlying stiff clayey soils. This 
bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.  
In general, the Keystone wall foundation should be embedded a minimum of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade or the estimated scour depth. The wall 
subgrades should be proof-rolled and recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface. Soft 
and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material. The over-excavation should be backfilled with 
lean concrete, or the bottom of footing may be extended deeper to bear on the more 
competent subgrade materials. To provide uniform support of the foundation, we 
recommend placing a minimum 6-inch thick concrete leveling pad below the first row of 
keystone blocks to serve as a leveling course. Due to the wet environment in this area, we 
do not recommend using gravel or similar soil materials to serve as a leveling course. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between 
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing materials. A coefficient of friction of 0.37 may 
be used for footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils. Passive pressure resistance of the 
on-site materials acting on the sides of the structure should be neglected down to the 
estimated scour depth.

Overall Slope Stability 
We evaluated the overall slope stability of the Keystone wall structure planned for 
the project. Based on our analyses, the factor of safety for short-term and long-term 
stability of the segmental retaining wall is at least 1.5, which is the minimum factor of 
safety normally recommended. Based on our slope stability analyses, a minimum base 
width (geogrid length) to wall height ratio of at least 0.80 is recommended for the Keystone 
retaining wall structure using on-site clayey soils for backfill material.

Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials 
We understand the use of on-site soil is desired for the reinforced fill and retained 
soil portions of the Keystone retaining wall. Based on our field exploration, the excavated 
on-site clayey soils may be re-used as a source of fill provided they are free of vegetation, 
deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension. 
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results, the on-site soils have high in-situ 
moisture contents. Therefore, drying or aerating the excavated materials may be 
necessary prior to their use as fill. It should be noted that aeration to lower the soil 
moisture and more compaction effort along with thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified 
compaction may be necessary and generally would reduce the rate of fill placement for 
this project. 
It should be noted that the project site is located in a high rainfall environment 
throughout the year; therefore, the on-site soils will be constantly in a very moist to wet 
condition. We believe it may be difficult to achieve adequate compaction without significant 
aeration of the on-site soils. However, it may not be feasible and practical to aerate the 
on-site soils, and it may be advantageous to replace the on-site soils with select granular 
fill materials. 
Select granular fill materials should consist of crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials and should be well-graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension. In addition, this material should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve, and have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or 
more with a corresponding maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D 1883. Geolabs should test the imported fill materials for conformance with 
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Reinforced fill and backfill materials should be placed in level loose lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent of 
the maximum dry density established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 at moisture 
contents above the optimum moisture content.

Drainage 
The Keystone retaining wall should be well drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch 
wide zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation), placed directly behind the keystone blocks. In addition, the upper 12 inches of 
the retaining wall backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the 
potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining wall unless covered by 
concrete slabs at the surface. 
SITE GRADING 
We anticipate the site grading work for the project generally will consist of 
excavations for foundation construction and the placement of backfill material behind the 
new retaining wall. At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the excavation limits should 
be cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or in a designated 
area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with well-compacted fill. Over-excavations beneath the retaining wall foundation should 
be backfilled with lean concrete, or the bottom of footing may be extended deeper to bear 
on the more competent subgrade materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils 
should be properly disposed of off-site or used in landscaping areas, if appropriate.  
After clearing and grubbing, areas receiving fill should be proof-rolled to obtain a 
firm and unyielding surface. If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade 
preparation, the pumping subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the 
potential for pumping subgrade conditions. As a guide, one sack of cement may be used 
for approximately 25 square feet of subgrade area. Alternatively, the subgrade may be 
proof-rolled and a woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent, should be 
placed on the subgrade prior to placement of subsequent lifts of fill. 
The excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of backfill material 
provided they are free of deleterious materials and contain rock fragments no larger than 
6 inches in maximum dimension. Based on our field exploration and laboratory test 
results, the on-site soils have relatively high in-situ moisture contents. Therefore, aerating 
the excavated materials likely will be necessary prior to their use as compacted backfill. It 
should be noted that the backfill requirements presented herein are intended as 
guidelines only and may be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and 
field observations on the available fill materials. 
Imported backfill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select 
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. In addition,

hoerams or trenching machines.
