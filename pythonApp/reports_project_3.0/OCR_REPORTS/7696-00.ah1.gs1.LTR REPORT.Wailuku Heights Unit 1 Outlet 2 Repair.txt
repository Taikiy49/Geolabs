PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located south of Kuikahi Drive in Wailuku Heights on the Island 
of Maui, Hawaii.  Outlet 2 is located at the northwest side of an existing reservoir.  Near 
Outlet 2, significant erosion had occurred which eroded the soils around a manhole 
structure and the adjacent soil banks.  We understand that it is desired to repair these 
eroded slope areas.  
Based on available drawings, Outlet 2 consists of a 66-inch Corrugated 
Galvanized Metal Pipe (CGMP).  A manhole structure is located near the end of the 
drain pipe.  An existing ditch structure with a concrete spillway was observed running 
parallel to the drain line and discharging the water from a higher elevation to just below 
the eroded area. We understand that the existing ditch structure transports water from 
Iao Stream to the reservoir. The eroded slope area is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago. It was formed by the 
activity of two volcanoes, East Maui or Haleakala, and West Maui. The broad, gently 
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes, known as the Maui Isthmus, was formed when 
lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui Volcano. 
The East Maui Volcano, better known as the Haleakala Mountain, makes up the 
eastern portion of the Island of Maui. Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant with 
the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790. 
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the

The volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into three volcanic 
series. The oldest, Wailuku Volcanic Series, is comprised of basaltic lava flows and 
associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks, which built the major shield volcano. The 
Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin, discontinuous “frosting” of andesitic and 
trachytic flows, domes, and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic Series. 
After a long period of inactivity, where erosion incised deep valleys into the volcanic flows, 
volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and cones of the Lahaina 
Volcanic Series. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, erosion processes are dominated by the 
detachment of soil and rock materials from steep valley walls. The detached materials fall 
or are otherwise transported down slope toward the valley axis, primarily by gravity as 
colluvium. Once these materials reach the stream in the valley, stream flow processes 
become dominant, and the materials are further transported and may be deposited as 
alluvium. The project site is located on the lower eastern slope of the West Maui Volcano. 
SITE DESCRIPTION 
The project is located in the Wailuku Heights area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  
The proposed outlet repair project is located near the northwest side of an existing 
reservoir.  
The existing reservoir slope was covered with grass and light brush at the time of 
our field exploration.  The existing slope adjacent to the outlet pipe area was eroded with 
bare soil exposed at the slope face.  The existing reservoir slope range from about one 
and one-half horizontal to one vertical (1.5H:1V) to 1H:1V inclination in the eroded slope 
area.  In addition, erosion of the surrounding slope materials below the top of the manhole 
structure and exposing the structure was observed. 
An existing ditch structure with a concrete spillway discharges water into the 
reservoir from about 9 feet above the mudline.  The water in the ditch was flowing 
continuously during our site visits. 
Based on our field observations and the topography survey map provided to us, the 
project site generally slopes downward to the reservoir with existing ground surface 
elevations between about +724 and +744 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our boring generally encountered alluvial deposits consisting of stiff to very stiff 
clayey silt, silty clay and sandy silt to a maximum depth explored of about 31.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled boring. 
The probable cause of the erosion observed and recommended remedial 
measures are provided. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the design of the 
project are presented in the following sections. 
Probable Cause of Erosion 
Surface erosion channels or ditches were not observed above the top of the slope 
of the eroded slope areas.  Therefore, it does not appear that surface runoff from above 
the slope caused the erosion of the slope adjacent to the outlet pipe.  
As indicated previously, an existing ditch structure with a concrete spillway 
discharges water from the ditch into an area at the bottom of the eroded slope area. In 
addition, the ditch is elevated and water flowing from the ditch drops about 7 feet to the 
water below.  
During heavy rainfall, we anticipate that a large amount of ditch water drops 
adjacent to the bottom of the slope and may have caused erosion of the slope toe. In 
addition, the slope face may have been saturated and the shear resistance of the slope 
face material reduced. We believe that the erosive force of the ditch water at the slope toe 
and the saturated slope face conditions may be the potential causes of the slope erosion 
observed adjacent to the existing manhole structure.

Slope Stability 
Based on the topographic plan, the eroded slopes adjacent to the existing manhole 
structure ranged from about 1.5H:1V to 1H:1V inclination as shown on the Generalized 
Geologic Cross-Sections, Plates 3 and 4. Based on the results of our field exploration, 
design soil parameters were developed and slope stability analyses were performed on 
the existing eroded slopes. Based on our slope stability analyses, finished slope inclination 
of about 1.5H:1V or flatter is needed to provide long-term stability of the slopes.   
Remedial Measures 
To reduce the erosion and scour of the slope toe, the installation of 2 to 3-foot 
boulders is recommended. The boulders should be underlain with a woven geotextile 
(Mirafi FW700 or equal) to provide separation and reduce the migration of fines from the 
subgrade soils. The boulders should be carefully placed to prevent damaging the 
geotextile. Prior to placing the geotextile, the mudline should be graded fairly level. 
Should the property owner of the reservoir not allow the placement of the boulders, 
the installation of a concrete cut-off wall at the toe of the slope is recommended. The 
concrete cut-off wall should be a minimum of 2 feet wide and extend a minimum of 3 feet 
below the bottom of the existing mudline. 
The eroded slopes should be flattened to about 1.5H:1V inclination. The flattened 
slope on the south side of the manhole structure will extend into the existing gravel road.  
Therefore, a portion of the gravel road needs to be re-aligned in this area and the eroded 
slope on the north side of the manhole structure about 1.5H:1V inclination. 
To reduce the potential for future erosion of the slope face, we recommend 
installing a turf reinforcing matting (North American Green Vmax C350 or equal) on the 
slope face. Because of the sloping conditions, the matting should be properly anchored 
per manufacturer’s recommendations. Alternatively, the installation of Grouted Rubble 
Paving (GRP) on the slope face may be considered. The GRP should be anchored at the 
top and bottom of the slope with keyways. The upper keyway should be a minimum of 2 
feet wide and extend to a minimum of 3 feet below the existing grade. The lower keyway 
should be a minimum 2 feet wide and 3 feet below the mudline. If a concrete cut-off wall is 
being installed, the lower keyway may be omitted and the GRP should butt against the 
concrete cut-off wall. 
The eroded area below the top of the manhole structure should be backfilled with 
concrete.  Prior to concrete backfill placement, loose/soft materials encountered at the 
ground surface should be removed.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition at the project site was explored by drilling and sampling 
one exploratory boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 
31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The boring was drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
4-inch OD solid-stem augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Log of Boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
It should be noted that the efficiency of the safety hammer used for this project was 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratio is shown on the Log of 
Boring. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring log have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.
