This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the new monopole antenna at the Kawaihae Harbor in the Kawaihae area
on the Island of Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations and site
preparation only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
Please note that the soil samples recovered during our field exploration (remaining
after testing) will be stored for a period of two months from the date of this report. The
samples will be discarded after that date unless arrangements are made for a longer
sample storage period. Please contact our office for alternative sample storage
requirements, if appropriate.
Project Considerations
The project involves the construction of a monopole for the PACMERS —
Kawaihae Harbor Monopole project in the Kawaihae area on the Island of Hawaii. In
addition, a prefabricated shelter will also be constructed to support the facility. The
focus of our geotechnical engineering exploration is the foundation of the proposed
monopole.
Based on the available information, the proposed monopole will be 120-foot high,
18-sided polygon pole. The monopole is also expected to resist wind at 50 miles per
hour (MPH) for daily operability and wind at 150 MPH for structural survivability.

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration for the project was to
obtain an overview of the subsurface conditions at the site to develop a subsurface data
set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the
design of the monopole foundations. Our work was performed in general accordance with
our fee proposal dated January 18, 2005. Our scope of work included the following tasks
and work efforts:
1. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig, presently on the
Island of Hawaii, and two operators from Honolulu to the project site and
back.
2. Drilling and sampling of one borehole to a depth of approximately 66.5 feet
below the existing ground surface.
3. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the boring by a
field engineer from our firm.
4. Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties.
5. Analyses of field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the proposed monopole.
6. Preparation of six copies of this letter report summarizing our work on the
project and presenting our findings and recommendations.
7. Coordination of our overall work on the project by a project engineer from
our firm.
8. Quality assurance of our work, and client/design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm.
9. Miscellaneous work efforts, such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.
Site Description
The project site is located at the southern end of Kawaihae Harbor. The site is
relatively flat with chain link fence enclosure and a wooden one-story building beyond
the fence. The site appears to have been filled to the existing ground surface recently,
an evidence of which is the concrete top of a small boat loading ramp just to the north of
the project site.

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of approximately 66.5 feet
below the existing ground surface. The approximate location of the drilled boring is shown
on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
In general, our field exploration encountered loose to medium dense coralline sand
and gravel material at the project site. Groundwater was encountered in the drilled boring
at approximately 8 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field
exploration.
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the boring are presented on
the attached Log of Boring, Plate 4.
Laboratory Testing
Selected soil samples from the field exploration were tested in the laboratory with
an emphasis on evaluating the moisture and density characteristics. The following
laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from the field
exploration.
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate 5.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plate 6.
Discussion and Recommendations
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by loose to
medium dense coralline sand and gravel. Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at
the project site, we believe that a deep foundation may be used to support the proposed
monopole.
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundations, site
grading, and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following
sections of this report.

Monopole Drilled Shaft Foundations
We understand that the proposed monopole will be subjected to ground line
overturning moment and lateral forces at the foundation level. Based on the
loading conditions provided and the subsurface conditions encountered, we
recommend that the monopole be supported by drilled shaft foundations.
Based on the preliminary design of the monopole foundation, 5-foot diameter drilled
shafts were considered in our analyses. The major considerations in our
foundation analyses included the lateral loads and ground line overturning
moments induced in the shaft foundations.

In general, lateral load resistance for shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable deflection at the top of the
shaft, and induced moment in the shaft. We evaluated the required shaft lengths
using the computer software program, LPILE-Plus for Windows. This program is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded pile program
originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program solves for
deflection and bending moment along a shaft under lateral loads as a function of
depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of internally generated non-linear
“p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed
using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths. The analyses were performed
for laterally loaded 5-foot diameter, cast-in-place concrete shafts. The results of our
engineering analyses are summarized in the following table. It should be noted that
the maximum lateral deflection is located at the finish grade.

Monopole Construction Considerations
The performance of the shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor's
method of construction and construction procedures. As a result, we recommend
that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe the installation of the
drilled shafts during construction. In our opinion, the following may have a
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the shaft foundations.
The load carrying capacity of shafts depends, to a large extent, on the contact
between the shafts and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper construction

techniques are important. The contractor should exercise care in drilling or
excavating the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes. The bottom of
shafts should be cleaned of loose or disturbed material prior to constructing the
foundation. The bottom should also be fairly level. The concrete should be poured
neat against the excavation sidewalls. Backfill against the shaft foundations should
not be allowed.
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should be used
to provide close contact between the shafts and the surrounding soils. The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from the hole.
A representative from Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of the
drilled shaft during construction to confirm the subsoil conditions and embedment
depths.
As previously indicated, the recommendations pertaining to the monopole
foundations were based on loading conditions taken from a similar project. Therefore, the
actual loading condition for the particular monopole proposed for this project should be
verified. It should be noted that re-design of foundations may be necessary if actual
structural loads differ significantly from assumed loads in this letter report.
Site Grading
Based on ihe existing topography, we envision that the site grading work for the
proposed project will generally consist of minor cuts and fills.
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared. Debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed of off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-
excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off-site. Contract documents should include additive and deductive
unit prices for over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in
the over-excavation quantities.
The materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of general
fill material, provided that they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock
fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension. If required, import fill should
consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed basalt or coral. The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent. Imported fill material should be
tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the

Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness,
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures.
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the
maximum dry density.
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding
should not be allowed to compact the on-site soils.
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs. It is
important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered and whether the exposed
soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration.
Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations should also be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration
into the subsoils. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain surface run-off away from the foundations.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface soils. The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.

Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from the field boring. Variations of subsoil conditions may occur, and
the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is
underway. If variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate
the recommendations presented in this report.
The location of the field boring is approximate, having been estimated by taping
from the features shown on the site plan transmitted by Ohana Telcom/Construction Inc.
on April 27, 2005. The location of the field boring should be considered accurate only to
the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the boring depict
the approximate boundaries between the soil and rock types, and as such, may denote a
gradual transition. Water level data from the boring was measured at the times shown on
the graphic representations and/or in the text of this report. These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, tides, temperatures, and other
factors.
lt should be noted that the engineering recommendations pertaining to the
monopole foundations were based on loading conditions taken from a similar project.
Therefore, it may not be the actual loading condition for the particular monopole proposed
for this project. We emphasize that it must be brought to our attention should actual
structural loads differ from assumed loads in this letter report, or Geolabs cannot be
responsible for misinterpretation of our recommendations.
