1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
engineering analyses performed in support of the preliminary design of the proposed
Waipahu Flood Control Phase project located in Waipahu on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our preliminary geotechnical
engineering recommendations based on our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses performed for the proposed project The recommendations
presented in this report are intended for the preliminary design of foundations retaining
structures site grading and pavements for the project only A detailed design of the
various flood control system will be conducted in the Phase II portion of the project The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the additional services
and limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located in an area dominated by recent stream alluvial
deposits overlying formations of volcanic origin During the Pleistocene Epoch
Ice Age sea levels fluctuated and reached as much as 300 feet below the present sea
level Stream transported sediments consisting mainly of boulders cobbles and gravel
were deposited at a lower sea level As the sea level began to rise the capacity of the
streams was reduced and finer sands and silts were subsequently deposited At some
point the accumulation of sediments could not keep up with the rising sea level and the
area became flooded As a result very fine grained sediments were then deposited in
marine or estuarine environments
Eventually the sea level stabilized while the sedimentation process of
fine grained sediments and shell beds continued Sediments with shell beds are
characteristic of near shore deposits Subsequently organic sediments which are
peaty and contain plant fibers and fragments accumulated and are indicative of marshy
conditions Land development within the last 100 years has brought the site to its
present form Many of the resulting fills over the alluvial and marsh deposits are of poor
quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads

2 4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC current code adopted by the
City and County of Honolulu the Island of Oahu is located within Seismic Zone 2A with
an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 15g Therefore the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be
addressed for the project The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity
of the Island of Oahu the soil profile for seismic design and the potential for
liquefaction at the site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they could only be detected by
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage

In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of high
seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance
from the Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not
confined only to the Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example
several small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui
Earthquake of In addition some houses on the Island of Oahu were
reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
Over the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of reported
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 occurring on the Island of Oahu We
understand that an earthquake of Magnitude 4 8 to 5 0 occurred along the
Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island of Oahu The moderate tremor resulted
in broken store windows ruptured building walls and broken underground water
mains

3 2 Flood Wall
We understand that a flood wall of up to about 6 feet in height may be required
along Station No 0 00 through 4 00 We also understand that the flood wall will have
a free board height of 3 feet Based on the anticipated flood elevations ranging from
about 27 to 80 feet MSL along this area the top of wall elevations will range from
about 30 to 33 feet MSL
The new flood wall will generally be located along the top of existing slope In
general we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of continuous strip
footings may be used to support the proposed flood wall An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of
footings bearing on the compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind
or seismic forces
In general footings for walls should have a minimum width of 18 inches In
addition wall foundations located on relatively flat areas should be embedded a
minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade In addition the
footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings
oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structure may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system
A coefficient of friction of 0 85 may be used for footings bearing on fill materials
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf assuming that the soils
around the footings are well compacted The passive resistance in the upper 12 inches
of the soil should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs

proposed earth berm Therefore percolation tests were performed in selected borings

borings at selected depths to provide permeability data for the subsurface materials at
the project site The percolation tests were performed at depths ranging from about
5 5 to 16 4 feet below the ground surface

coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 1 7 x 10 to 9 9 x 10 feet per
minute which correspond to about 8 7 x 10 to 5 0 x 10 centimeters per second It
should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on site materials may be
highly variable due to the presence or absence of sand lenses and other permeable

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 at the project site The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1
and 2 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by a geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches Some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an
indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample The torvane shear
test provided a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample
Results of the pocket penetrometer tests and the torvane shear tests are presented on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
As part of our field exploration program we conducted six percolation tests in the
drilled borings Two percolation tests were performed using the constant head method
The other four tests were performed using the falling head method In general the drilled
borings had a diameter of about 4 inches and extended to depths of about 5 5 to 16 4 feet
below the ground level at the time of the tests A water truck was used to pump clear
water into the boreholes for the constant head test method The flow rate was recorded by
timing and calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved
For the falling head method water was filled in the boreholes and was allowed to
percolate into the ground The water levels and corresponding times were recorded for
the calculations of subsurface permeability The results of the percolation tests
conducted are presented on Plates C 1 through C 6
