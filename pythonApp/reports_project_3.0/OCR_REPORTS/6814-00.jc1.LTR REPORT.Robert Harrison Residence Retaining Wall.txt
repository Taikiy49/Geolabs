Project Considerations 
The project site is located at 1502 Ualakaa Place, TMK: 2-5-3:30, in Honolulu, on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. An existing 2-story house is located near the lower portion 
of Makiki Heights on the eastern flank of Punchbowl crater. Existing CRM and CMU 
retaining walls along the southern and western sides of the property are up to about 
5 and 12 feet high, respectively, for grade separation.  
We understand that cracks were observed at several locations of the existing 
CRM and CMU walls due to ground settlement. It is proposed to construct a new 
retaining wall, to replace the existing CRM and CMU terrace walls.  
We understand that the southern side of the existing retaining wall is adjacent to a 
private driveway. Therefore, one of the design considerations was to keep the new 
retaining wall footing within the property line.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present sea level. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the 
sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site is on the eastern flank of Punchbowl Hill (Puowaina), which is a tuff 
cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of the Koolau Range. The 
tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with scattered fragments of coral 
limestone and Koolau basalt.

Discussion and Recommendations 
We understand that new retaining walls up to 17 feet high may be constructed to 
replace the existing deteriorated CMU and/or CRM walls, to support the existing house 
and new lanai platform extension. Based on the information provided, we understand 
that the new retaining wall alignment is located adjacent to the private driveway and 
approximately 17 feet in horizontal distance from the house footprint. Therefore, 
conventional retaining walls may not be feasible without an extensive shoring plan in 
order to construct the retaining wall footing. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings drilled, we 
recommend installing soil nails to support the new retaining walls. The soil nails will 
provide lateral resistance to the new retaining wall in order to eliminate the wall footing. 
The soil nail retaining wall could be constructed using the top-down technique, serving 
both as temporary shoring of the excavation and the permanent retaining wall. 
Soil Nail Retaining Walls 
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope. A shotcrete facing is 
generally applied to the face of the excavation providing as either temporary support or 
the permanent retaining wall facing. The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile 
and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the excavation. The 
reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a 
footing. In general, the face of the soil nail retaining wall system is slightly battered at a 
slope inclination of about one horizontal to twelve vertical (1H:12V). 
Soil nail retaining walls are normally installed in a cut condition only. We 
understand that the proposed retaining walls may require partial fills. We recommend 
that the fills be placed first using shotcrete or flowable fill, to allow construction of the 
soil nail retaining wall entirely in a cut condition.

The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/soil 
interface contact, is variable depending on the type of soil and grout, the 
overburden stress, and the construction procedures used in installing these 
grouted reinforcing bars. For a rough estimate, an average bond stress of 
1,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the on-site sandy soils. The 
bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed in the field during 
construction. 
Based on our preliminary slope stability analyses, the length of the soil nails may 
be about 100 percent of the height of the wall depending on the geometry of the 
slope. The soil nail retaining wall system of up to about 17 feet high should have 
a factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external stability 
for the service loading condition. For the seismic loading condition, a factor of 
safety of 1.0 or greater for the combined internal and external stability should be 
achieved.

The design of the soil nail retaining wall system does not include hydrostatic 
pressures that might be caused by groundwater or water trapped behind the 
walls. Therefore, the soil nail retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 
2-foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or 
EnkaDrain, placed on the face of the excavation between the soil nails. The 
prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top to the base of the wall 
and should be hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall, as 
shown on Plate 3. The weepholes should discharge to appropriate outlets. 
Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nail retaining 
walls, we recommend performing verification tests on a minimum of three 
production soil nails. In addition, proof tests should be performed on at least 
10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond 
stresses used in the design. 
The verification tests should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design 
load for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 
200 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment 
should be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 
60 minutes. The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 
150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 
10 minutes (until stable). The test nails may be incorporated into the permanent 
soil nail retaining wall provided that they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and 
creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nail 
retaining wall system. Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout tests and 
creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs representative.

CMU Retaining Walls 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the CMU retaining walls up 
to 6 feet high are planned to be installed for planter and other structures. We envision that 
the proposed CMU retaining wall foundation subgrades will consist of medium dense 
sandy soils. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design 
of foundations. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade with a minimum width of 18 inches. 
For sloping ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 
6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the 
face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be 
stepped.

It is assumed that the backfill behind retaining structures will be compacted to between

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, traffic loads, line loads, and point 
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in 
the design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a 
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 30 percent of the vertical surcharge 
pressure acting on the entire height of the wall, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be

of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may be used for 
design. Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge 
effects of point loads and line loads.

In general, retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), placed 
directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the walls 
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated 
drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the drainage 
material. The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of free-draining 
compacted fills, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), where 
specified by the designer. Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of 
backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for 
excessive water infiltration behind the walls.
