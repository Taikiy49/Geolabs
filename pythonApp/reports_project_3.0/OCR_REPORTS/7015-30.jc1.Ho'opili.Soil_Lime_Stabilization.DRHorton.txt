PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed Ho`opili Development project is located south of Interstate Route H-1 
Highway and west of Fort Weaver Road in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
The proposed development will encompass an area of approximately 1,500 acres. The 
project site is gently sloping and is currently used for agricultural cultivation. 
Most of the development site is underlain by lower quality alluvial clayey soil. These 
soils generally have moderate to high swelling potential and relatively low bearing qualities 
for pavements and foundations. It is desired to evaluate the feasibility of utilizing soil-lime 
stabilization to improve the subgrade soils under the development roads in order to reduce 
the requirements for imported structural fill. 
Soil-lime stabilization is a well-known method to improve expansive soils and has 
been used for many decades. In general, the soil-lime mixture permeates high calcium 
hydrated lime Ca(OH)2 into the expansive soil, developing strong lime-modification and 
producing soil-lime pozzolanic reactions. During the chemical reaction process, the 
calcium cations from the hydrated lime replace the cations on the expansive clay mineral, 
promoted by the high pH environment. The pH of the soil-lime mixture quickly increases to

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southwestern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet 
waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys 
in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous 
reef materials.

To evaluate compressibility of the soil-lime modification to support the road 
pavement section, we also performed California Bearing Ratio (CBR) and proctor testing 
on the soil-lime mixtures with 4% to 8% of hydrated lime. To evaluate the sensitivity of 
soil-lime modification after initial pozzolanic reaction, the compacted samples were broken 
apart and remolded for a repeat CBR test. Results of CBR and proctor testing are 
summarized in Table 3 below. Details of the CBR and proctor testing are presented in 
Appendix A. 
Based on review of CBR and proctor testing results, we believe that soil-lime 
modifications also significantly increase CBR value and decrease percent swell of the 
on-site clayey soil, while slightly decreasing maximum dry density. Remolding soil-lime 
mixture after the initial pozzolanic reaction may reduce CBR value; however, it is still 
significantly higher than untreated on-site clayey soil 
Considering corrosivity of high pH soil-lime mixture, resistivity testing was 
conducted on the soil-lime mixture and in-situ untreated clayey soil for comparison. Based 
on the testing results, the in-situ clayey soil has resistivity values ranging from 
approximately 450 to 850 ohm-cm and may be classified as “Extremely Corrosive”.

Based on the results of the laboratory testing, we believe that 6% hydrated lime by 
dry weight of the dry soil is capable of achieving optimum soil-lime pozzolanic reaction. It is 
our opinion that the soil-lime stabilization is capable of mitigating in-situ clayey soil 
expansion when subjected to moisture fluctuations and thereby permanently increase the 
bearing capacity of the soils for road pavement support. Consequently, the stabilization will 
reduce pavement design thickness and could generate construction cost saving. 
Therefore, we recommend implementing 6% hydrated lime treatment within the 
roadway limits, subjected to site-specific verification testing in the field. Our preliminary 
cost estimate indicated that the lime treated asphalt concrete pavement construction may 
save up to about 30 percent of cost for the asphalt concrete pavement without soil-lime 
treatment.

CONSIDERATIONS OF SOIL-LIME STABILIZATION CONSTRUCTION 
It is critical to ensure a pozzolanic reaction of the soil-lime mixture. The key to 
pozzolanic reactivity and stabilization is a good mix design protocol and reliable 
construction practices. We recommend performing a test section to examine effectiveness 
of the construction method and verifying the in-situ soil-lime stabilization prior to the 
pavement construction. In addition, soil samples should be collected at the pavement 
subgrade level for the soil-lime reactive testing, to verify site-specific effectiveness of 
soil-lime stabilization and to modify the soil-lime mixture ratio as appropriated.

Soil-lime subgrade stabilization should be extended to a minimum of 12 inches 
below the pavement finished subgrade level and at least 5 feet beyond the pavement 
limits. The subgrade material should be thoroughly scarified and appropriately blended 
with hydrated lime by a rototiller. The lumps of the clayey soils should be broken down to 
no larger than 1.5-inch size in the largest dimension. 
Mixing and pulverization should be used to thoroughly blend the lime and soil. For 
heavy clays, it may be prudent that pre-mixing be implemented with 24 to 48 hours 
(or more) of moist curing prior to final mixing. The soil-lime mixture should be moisture 
conditioned to about 4 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
soil-lime mixture dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density 
of the same mixture established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
When the design requires stabilization to a depth greater than 12 inches, the 
subgrade soil should be treated in equal layers no thicker than 12 inches of each layer. 
The top layer(s) of subgrade soil should be removed and stockpiled. The lower layer of 
subgrade soil should be treated and allowed to cure in place and compacted after final 
mixing. The stockpiled soil should then be placed, treated, mixed and compacted in 
successive no thicker than 12 inches of loose lifts. 
To provide consistency of the soil-lime mixture, the process of spreading, mixing 
and pulverization should be conducted in a dry soil condition. Therefore, dust control 
measures are necessary in compliance with the health and safety regulations. The time 
interval between lime spreading and mixing should not exceed more than 5 hours, to 
reduce risk of lime carbonation and maximize a high residual pH as well as sustain 
long-term pozzolanic reactions. To verify uniformity and effectiveness of the mixing 
process, we recommend performing in-situ pH testing on the randomly selected soil-lime 
mixture.  
The subgrade soil may be aerated or moisture conditioned as necessary to provide 
and maintain the desirable moisture content. To achieve a complete chemical reaction, 
water should be used to maintain moisture content at optimum to 4% above optimum of 
the soil-lime mixture prior to beginning compaction.
