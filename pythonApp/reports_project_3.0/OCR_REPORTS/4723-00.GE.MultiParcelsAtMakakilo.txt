SITE DESCRIPTION
The project sites are located along Makakilo Road the northern side of the existing
Westhills Phase III development and the southern side of the existing Palehua East B
development in the city of Makakilo on the Island of Oahu Hawaii These Parcels vary in
shape and size
Based on our field reconnaissance the project sites presently consist of vacant land
with a sparse cover of dry vegetation and some deleterious materials and debris scattered
over the existing ground surface Although shrinkage cracks were not visible at the time of
our field reconnaissance they could exist on each site and should be considered due to the
type of soil encountered A significant number of erosion gullies some measuring over
5 feet wide and 5 feet deep at the Parcel C 3 and Palailai Mauka site were observed along
the edges and over the face of the existing slope at each of the project sites
Based on the topographic survey map provided the project sites exhibit slight to high
relief with elevations ranging from about 200 to 1200 feet Mean Sea Level MSL south
to north respectively
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
A field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling borings was not conducted for
this due diligence study Instead research and review of available soil and boring data in
the vicinity of the proposed project site was conducted in order to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the proposed project site
Based on our field reconnaissance and the available information it appears that the
majority of the project site is covered by a deposit of reddish brown to brownish red silty
clay to clayey silt soils near the existing ground surface Our field observations indicate
that the near surface silty clay to clayey silt soils generally exist in a very dry and loose
state Commonly encountered in the area In general we envision that these soils will
increase in stiffness and moistness with increasing depths In addition our experience in
the Makakilo area suggest that the near surface soils may exhibit moderate to high
shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
Groundwater is not anticipated at shallow depths below the existing ground surface
In general the site is at an elevation several tens of feet above the highest basal water
table in the area However groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on
seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factor

Slope Erosion
Based on our field reconnaissance a significant number of erosion gullies was
observed along the edges and over the face of the existing slopes generally along the
eastern and northern portions of the project sites typically due to existing deep ravines
and or gullies Some of these erosion gullies measured over 5 feet wide and 5 feet deep with
the surrounding soils existing in a very dry and loose state We believe that the erosion
gullies present at the site are a result of surface water runoff over the sparely vegetated
ground surface underlain by the highly erosive silty clay soils present across the project site
We believe that these erosion gullies if left unattended may increase further in size depth
and number and could eventually progress to a state causing failure of the existing slope
Because the proposed developments may encroach along the edge of the existing
slope we believe that the design and construction of the projects should address the
following items
e Removal of Erosion Gullies
e Cut and Fill Slope Design
e Erosion Protection and Long Term Slope Stability
Removal of Erosion Gullies
In general the exposed erosion gullies should be over excavated down to the bottom
of the erosion gullies Based on our field observations we believe that an
over excavation depth on the order of about 4 to 5 feet deep may be anticipated for
the gully removal work Due to the deteriorated condition of some of the existing
slope faces we believe that substantial excavation of the existing slope may be
required to remove the eroded portion of the existing slopes
Prior to the placement of new fill materials the over excavated subgrade resulting
from the gully removal work should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Cut and Fill Slope Design
As mentioned previously erosion gullies were also observed over the existing slope
faces at the project site Depending on the final grading concept significant portions
of the existing slope may need to be removed and reconstructed to provide a stable
embankment for the construction of improvements on top of the embankment In
general we believe that the planned cut and fill slopes may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter

New fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of
the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill
placement recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face
Excessive surface water runoff over the slope face may cause erosion of the exposed
soils thus potentially jeopardizing the long term stability and performance of the cut
and fill slopes Therefore the slope face should be protected by appropriate slope
planting or other means as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant
erosion of the finished slopes In addition V ditches may be considered at the top
and bottom of the planned slopes to provide proper drainage of surface water runoff
Erosion Protection and Long Term Slope Stability
Implementation of an adequate erosion control program would be crucial to the
satisfactory performance of the planned cut slopes and fill embankments In general
erosion of the existing slopes planned cut slopes or new fill embankments are
caused by surface water runoff General guidelines such as establishment of
vegetation and provision of interceptor drainage ditches and earth berms have been
discussed above and should be incorporated into the design and construction of any
new developments It should be noted that provision of permanent ground cover
vegetation will likely require that a permanent irrigation be installed to irrigate the
ground cover considering the dry climatic conditions of the area
Other erosion control methods or applications such as Grouted Rubble Paving GRP
and concrete lining may need to be considered as part of the erosion control program
of the existing slopes generally along the northern to eastern boundaries of the
project sites In addition to these conventional rigid lining systems the use of flexible
geosynthetic matting as a viable solution for slope erosion control has become
increasingly more acceptable and may be considered for this project
Boulders
The residual soil and weathered rock formations encountered in the area generally
contain significant quantities of weathered boulders which are a normal result of the
weathering of rock formations Therefore a significant quantity of boulders may be
generated from the earthwork and utility installation operations As a result hauling boulders
off site and hauling import fill material onto the site to replace the boulder quantity may be
necessary The amount of boulder disposal may be reduced by on site crushing or
placement of the boulders in deep fill areas if available

Expansive Soil Conditions
Eleven one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plates 3 1 and 3 2 It is our opinion that these results are generally representative of the
near surface soils in the area Our experience in the Makakilo area suggests that much of
the near surface silty clay to clayey silt soils generally exhibit low to moderate shrink swell
characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents However
significant portions of the site particularly in the higher areas contain zones of highly
expansive soil
In order to reduce movements of the structures due to shrink swell movements of the
expansive soils the expansive clayey soils may be capped with non expansive select
capping fill material The thickness of the capping layer required is dependent on the swell
potential of the expansive clay Typically the capping fill thickness is in the range of 1 to
3 feet
Alternatively structural slab systems or deep foundation support systems drilled
shafts may be used for house foundations on moderately expansive soils
Rock Excavation
Much of the surface soils at the parcels have been eroded Therefore deeper
excavations for site grading and utility installation may encounter rock formation The rock
formation is generally hard and may require hard ripping with bulldozers and possibly
pneumatic tools such as hoe rams The excavated rocks may generally be used in the fills
provided they are reduced to smaller than 6 inches in size The rock formation will proved
competent bearing for structures however grading costs are higher for these types of
formations
Additional Exploration
Additional field exploration and geotechnical engineering analysis should be
conducted for the final design of the site grading and structure foundations
