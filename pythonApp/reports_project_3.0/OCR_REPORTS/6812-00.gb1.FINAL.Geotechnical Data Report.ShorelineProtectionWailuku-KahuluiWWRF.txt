INTRODUCTION 
This report presents geotechnical data from our geotechnical engineering 
exploration performed for the Shoreline Protection Project at the Wailuku-Kahului 
Wastewater Reclamation Facility on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report provides subsurface information in the project vicinity derived from 
our field exploration and laboratory testing. The subsurface information presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is at the Wailuku-Kahului Wastewater Reclamation Facility in the 
Kahului area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  The project site is located along the north 
side of Amala Place.  The project site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
Maui, the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago, was formed by the 
activity of two volcanoes, East Maui or Haleakala, and West Maui. The broad, gently 
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes, known as the Maui Isthmus, was formed 
when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui 
Volcano. 
The East Maui Volcano, better known as the Haleakala Mountain, makes up the 
eastern portion of the Island of Maui. Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant 
with the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790. 
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the 
mountain from three rift zones, probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene 
Epochs. 
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into 
three volcanic series. The oldest Wailuku Volcanic Series is comprised of basaltic lava 
flows and associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks, which built the major shield 
volcano. The Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin, discontinuous “frosting” of 
andesitic and trachytic flows, domes, and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua 
Volcanic Series. After a long period of inactivity, where erosion incised deep valleys into 
the volcanic flows, volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and 
cones of the Lahaina Volcanic Series. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes. During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
the present sea level, exposing large areas of coralline sands on what was the ocean 
bottom. Wind action transported these coralline sands inland, forming extensive areas 
of sand dunes. The project site is located on the Maui Isthmus and is underlain by sand 
dune deposits. 
SOIL SAMPLING AND FIELD TESTING 
A total of five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extended to 
depths of about 16.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill equipment with continuous 
flight augers. Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual 
observation and laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general conformance with 
the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A of Appendix A.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered in the drilled borings are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5 of Appendix A. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550 and D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch O.D. Modified California sampler and a 2-inch O.D. standard penetration sampler 
with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24-inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
LABORATORY TESTING 
Laboratory testing was performed on selected soil samples obtained during the 
field exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their engineering 
properties. The tests performed are described below. 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Ten Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation test results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D 1883) was performed 
on the near-surface soils to evaluate the support characteristics of the soils. The test 
results are presented on Plate B-3.

between and beyond the exploration points may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway.

approximate, having been estimated using a hand held GPS system with coordinates

the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data presented in the text herein were based on measurements 
recorded in the borings at the times shown on the graphic representations of the 
borings. These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of 
this report. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tide, 
rainfall, temperature, and other factors.
