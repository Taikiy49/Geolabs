PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this geotechnical exploration was to explore the
general subsurface soil conditions along the sewer alignment at selected
locations to provide preliminary soil parameters for the design and
construction of the sewerline and pump stations
The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which we
used in the engineering analysis to formate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations

The scope of work performed in this exploration was in general
accordance with our proposals dated and
The issuance of this report was delayed to allow further study of
alternate sewer alignments Our scope included drilling and sampling nine
9 borings to depths varying from 15 to 36 feet below the existing ground
surface laboratory testing engineering evaluation of the field and
laboratory findings and report preparation
GENERAL PROJECT DESCRIPTION
The proposed Kapaa Sewage System Phase I consist of three 3 pump
stations and 4N and a sewerline that runs across the towns
of Kapaa and Waipouli on the island of Kauai
The proposed sewerline starts off at the intersection of Lehua Street
and Kuhio Highway next to the Moikeha Canal The sewerline will then run
in the westerly and southerly directions along Ulu
and Ohia Streets From Ohia Street the sewerline will extend southwards
along the general vicinity of the Kuhio Highway and terminate at the
existing LS No 3 Wet Well with an invert elevation of 6 9 feet below Mean
Sea Level
It is our understanding that the invert elevation of the gravity
sewerline varies from about 10 to 22 feet below the existing ground
surface Invert elevations of 14 10 and 14 feet below the Mean Sea Level
are planned for the sewage pump stations No 2N 3N and 4N respectively
GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located between the towns of Kapaa and Waipouli
on the island of Kauai The general project area is presently occupied
mostly by commercial and residential buildings

The project site is bordered by Moikeha Canal on the north The
proposed sewerline also runs across the Waikaea and Waipouli Drainage
Canals at approximately the middle and southern boundary of the project
site respectively
In general the new sewerline will be along relatively flat
asphaltic paved areas where the ground elevation varies from about six 6
to twelve 12 feet above Mean Sea Level

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The exact invert elevations of the proposed sewerline were not
available at the time of our exploration However it is anticipated that
they would be about 10 to 22 feet below the existing groundwater levels
Fair to poor soil conditions were encountered below the site
Relatively high water levels and loose sand deposits were encountered
throughout the area Therefore proper dewatering trench shoring and
bracing would be needed to reduce loss of ground and resulting damage to
existing facilities
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Toads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench
the size of the pipe the unit weight of backfill material and the
frictional resistance between the backfill material and the trench side
walls To compute the vertical loads on the pipes an average unit weight
of 120 p c f for backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 25
are recommended Earth forces acting upon the pipe increase rapidly with
the width of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should be
kept to a minimum
Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The underlying layers of beach and lagoonal marine deposits have poor
to good bearing capacity depending on where the invert elevations of the
sewerline will be located
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 5
The upper medium dense sand layer encountered approximately 0 to 9 5
feet below the existing ground surface has fair to good bearing capacity
The loose silty gravels to soft clayey silt lagoonal marine deposits
beneath the medium dense sand layer have poor to fair bearing capacity
The pipe bedding should provide the necessary stress distribution to
reduce the stress on the poor soil conditions
The new sewerline construction in general will not materially
increase the vertical loading upon these underlying soils if no additional
fills are placed upon the existing ground surface The net difference in
loading between the weights of the excavated material and backfill will be
essentially small Therefore significant settlements are not
anticipated if proper construction techniques particularly for shoring
and dewatering are utilized
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great
effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe
bedding is an important design consideration
For pipes bearing on the medium dense sand layer a minimm of six
6 inches of compacted granular bedding material should be placed below
the pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a
minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend
half way up the pipe sides The bedding material should consist of clean
granular material such as 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
For pipes on the loose sands and gravels and soft clayey silt an
additional 18 inch layer of clean granular material should be provided
below the utility pipe and bedding layer to provide more uniform support
for the pipe

Trench Excavation
The anticipated depth of excavation is 10 to 22 feet The method and
equipment used for excavation should be the choice of the contractor
within practical limits
Relatively easy excavation is anticipated for the soils encountered
Due to load considerations discussed in the preceding sections the
width of the sewer trench excavation should be kept to a minimm for
proper sewer installation and backfill
Trench Wall Support
It is our opinion that the trench walls in the loose sand deposits
and shallow groundwater level conditions would require continuous sheet
piling support It is important that adequate sheeting work be installed
prior to the excavation and maintained tight against the trench walls with
proper bracing to reduce lateral movements of the adjacent ground into the
trench excavation and resulting settlements of adjacent structures This
is particularly critical since most if not all of the existing
structures may be supported on spread footings bearing on the upper sandy
soils
The trench sheeting should extend down a sufficient depth to increase
the stability of the trench bottom and to reduce the possibility of bottom
heaving or quick conditions due to up lift seepage pressures
The recommended depths of penetration of the sheeting to reduce the
occurrence of upheaval and piping are presented on Plate 3 A minimm
factor of safety of 1 5 is recommended
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 7
The required depths of penetration are also affected by the method of
dewatering The curves presented are based on the assumption that there
is no drawdown on the outside of the sheeting If drawdown by means of a
deep well system outside the sheeting is used the depths of penetration
can be reduced However care must be taken to prevent upheaval or piping
when pumping is stopped
Sheeting and bracing must comply with all applicable safety
requirements The adequacy and safety of the sheeting and bracing
operations should be made the responsibility of the contractor since his
representative is continuously present at the site to observe any changing
conditions which might affect stability
For calculating earth loads in struts of braced excavation the
apparent pressure diagram presented on Plate 4 may be used as a guide
These pressures do not contain a factor of safety Additional loads due
to surcharge traffic or sloping ground condition close to the edge of the
excavation should be added to the recommended pressures
Even with good construction procedure some movement of the
excavation bracing system might occur because of changes in stress during
excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship for example how
quickly and tightly the supports are installed It also depends on the
type of soil the depth and width of excavation and the type of support
In view of the complexity of the problem it is difficult to accurately
predict movement of the bracing system and the adjacent ground
Therefore it is important to realize that the trench wall supports should
be installed as early as practicable and that measurements of vertical and
horizontal movements are useful especially in areas where damage to
existing structures may occur

The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should be
continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of
movements and possible sloughing of the trench walls Such monitoring is
particularly critical in areas where existing structures and utilities are
adjacent to the trench excavation
Dewatering
Dewatering is necessary because groundwater will normally be
encountered above the proposed invert level throughout the site The
deposits encountered in the borings consisted mostly of silty sands which
are relatively permeable and susceptible to piping and quick
conditions The project dewatering operations should be done without
pumping out the soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with the excavation shoring support so that the stability of
the excavation is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out
without creating a quick condition or softening the bottom of the
excavation
The method and equipment used for the dewatering should be the choice
of the contractor It should be realized that the dewatering system
chosen will have an effect on the depth of sheeting used
In the determination of the preferred method of dewatering three 3
basic selection criteria are suggested
a The method should impose the least disturbance or damage to
adjacent development
b The method should maintain adequate excavation stability and
provide a safe working condition
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 9
c The method should be flexible with easy modification to
accommodate various conditions
Dewatering by means of a well point system or deep wells along the
outside of the excavation could be used Although this method could
require a shallower sheetpile penetration the drawdown is likely to
affect the groundwater conditions below buildings in areas near the sewer
excavation Hence recharging injecting the pumped water back into the
soils to maintain the water level and minimize drawdown in areas near the
existing structures may be required to prevent drawdown and reduce the
risk of settlement to these buildings Additionally a well point system
may not be efficient enough to pump water out at depths more than ten 10
feet below the groundwater level
It appears that sump pumping from inside the trench may also be used
if sufficient penetration of the sheeting to prevent piping and upheaval
at the bottom of the trench is achieved
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be in accordance with the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986
The first zone of backfill extends from the bedding material to at
least 12 inches above the top of the pipe The first zone should consist
of selected free draining well graded granular material placed by hand or
by suitable equipment so that the backfill material is in contact with the
entire periphery of the pipe and in such a mamner as not to disturb the
pipe The granular material is recommended to insure high density
backfill with minimum compactive effort Compaction of this portion of
the backfill by ponding and jetting may be permitted provided the backfill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 10
material is self draining The material should be well graded less than
three 3 inches in size and with no more than 10 passing the No 200
sieve
Except for the organic clays and silts most of the excavated on site
sandy and gravelly soils can be re used as the upper zone of the trench
backfill from above one foot over the pipe to within two 2 feet of the
finished grade Backfill should be mechanically compacted to a minimum of
90 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 Below roadway
areas the backfill within two 2 feet of the finished grade should
consist of select borrow and be compacted to 95 of its maximm dry
density per ASTM Test Method D 1557
Before and during the backfilling of a trench precautions should be
taken against the floatation of pipelines due to the entry of large
quantities of water into the trench which may cause an uplift on the
empty or partly filled pipeline
If practicable the trenches should be kept free of water during
backfilling Should backfilling under the water be necessary granular
material such as base course rock should be utilized to reduce excessive
ground settlement of the trench backfill in the future

Joints and Connections
Due to the various soil conditions which will be crossed by the pipe
flexible pipe joints are recommended to allow for some amount of
differential settlement These flexible connections are particularly
important in the loose sand areas All sewerline line connections at the
pump stations should also have flexible connections to accommodate
possible ground settlements

Manhole Structures
Structures supported on the underlying medium dense sand may be
designed using an allowable bearing value up to 2000 p s f Minimal
settlements are anticipated for these structures
If it is necessary to place the manhole structures on the loose sandy
soils an 18 inch layer of gravel 3B Fine should be provided below the
structure to provide uniform support An allowable bearing value of 1500
p s f may be used on the 18 inch gravel layer The increase in load on
the subsoils due to the manhole structures is small Therefore large
settlements are not anticipated if proper shoring and dewatering
techniques are utilized
The lateral earth pressure acting on the underground structures will
depend on the type of backfill used the extent of the backfill material
and the compactive effort required on the material behind the structure
walls Granular material such as the select on site sand is recommended
for the backfill adjacent to the structures to reduce the compactive
effort required and to minimize the build up of additional lateral earth
pressures We recommend that the manholes be designed to resist the
following lateral earth pressures
1 Above water level 50 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
2 Below water level 25 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
plus water pressure
The above values assume that the backfill around the manholes is
properly placed and compacted Surcharge loading due to traffic or other
loads must be added to the above values

Pump Stations
Three 3 sewage pump stations and 4N are included in
the proposed Kapaa Sewage System Phase I It is our understanding that
the proposed pump stations will be approximately 12 by 12 feet in plan
An emergency generator building of 15 by 18 feet or 18 by 20 feet in plan
is planned for each of the pump stations
Invert elevations of 14 10 and 14 feet below Mean Sea Level are
planned for the pump stations 2N 3N and 4N respectively Therefore
construction of the proposed sewage pump stations will require substantial
excavation below groundwater level through deposits of loose gravelly
sand Adequate shoring and careful dewatering work will be required by
the contractor during construction
For the pump station facilities the recommended construction
sequence is that the site will first be graded then excavated building
structure constructed and backfilled the connecting inbound gravity line
and outbound sewer force main will then be trenched and tied to the
completed structure
Site Grading
Minimal site grading is recommended However it is our
understanding that 2 to 4 feet of fill is planned for the pump
stations No 3N and 4N sites Due to the loose sandy subsoil
conditions we recommend that the required fill should be placed at
the start of construction Settlement gauges should be installed in
the site filling operation to monitor the ground settlement due to
the fill Construction of the pump stations should only be started
after most of the ground settlement due to the fill has occurred as
revealed by the monitoring of settlement gauges

Foundation Excavation and Dewatering
The excavation for the pump stations and connecting sewer lines
should be shored The appropriate method of excavation and shoring
should be at the contractor s option Dewatering or lowering of the

drawdown should be carefully evaluated by the contractor to avoid
subsidence problems associated with lowering of the water level in
the surrounding areas A deep drawdown of water level outside the
excavation area may cause ground settlements in the surrounding area
which could cause damages to existing structures and buried lines in
the near vicinity
If excessive drawdowns are occurring in the surrounding area pumping
may need to be suspended immediately and or recharging of groundwater
outside the sheetpile may need to be implemented immediately

Foundation Support
It is anticipated that the foundation of the pump station will
consist of a thick mat slab The bottom of foundation excavation
should be dewatered so that concrete can be placed in a dry
condition
Due to the anticipated loose sandy subsoil and groundwater
conditions a working platform of 2 feet of 3B Fine gravel over a
geotextile such as Mirafi 140 should be placed below the bottom of
the mat foundation to provide additional support An allowable
bearing value up to 1500 p s f may be used on the 2 foot gravel
layer
The increase in load on the subsoils due to the pump station
structure is relatively small Therefore large settlements are not
anticipated if proper shoring and dewatering techniques are utilized

Underground Fuel Tank
A 500 gallon underground fuel tank approximately 4 foot in diameter
by 8 foot long is anticipated for the emergency generator building
We believe the fuel tank can be supported on the near surface soils
Due to the shallow groundwater level a stabilization pad consisting
of 12 inches of 3B Fine gravel may be required below the footing
bottom to provide uniform support if the bottom of excavation is
close to or below the groundwater level
Depending on the bottom elevation the proposed fuel tank may
experience buoyant force that can cause possible floatation of the
empty or partially filled fuel tank during construction
Precautionary measures such as temporary tie down of the tank to
prevent floatation should be provided by the contractor before and
during the backfilling operation if the anticipated tank bottom
elevation will be lower than the groundwater level

and gravel to evaluate its grain size distribution The results of the
tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 4
One 1 Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test result is
shown on Plate B 3
Four 4 direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate their shear strength parameters The test results are presented
on Plates B 5 through B 8
