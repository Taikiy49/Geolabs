REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is in an area dominated by recent stream and lagoonal deposits
overlying formations of volcanic origin During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels
fluctuated and reached as much as 300 feet below the present sea_ level
Stream transported sediments consisting mainly of boulders cobbles and gravel were
deposited at a lower sea level As the sea level began to rise the capacity of the streams
was reduced and finer sands and silts were subsequently deposited
At some point the accumulation of sediments could not keep up with the rising sea
level and the area became flooded As a result very fine grained sediments then were
deposited in marine or estuarine environments Eventually the sea level stabilized while
the sedimentation process of fine grained sediments and shell beds continued Sediments
with shell beds are characteristic of near shore deposits Subsequently organic

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 each extending to a depth of about
82 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site is underlain by a surface fill layer on the order of about
5 to 5 5 feet thick consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays and medium dense sands with
some cobbles and boulders Below the surface fill layer our borings generally
encountered recent alluvium consisting of soft to very soft clayey silts The soft recent
alluvium varied in thickness in the three borings Our field exploration encountered about
33 5 feet of recent alluvium in Boring No 1 about 61 5 feet of recent alluvium in
Boring No 2 and about 57 feet of recent alluvium in Boring No 3
The recent alluvium was underlain by old alluvium consisting of medium stiff to very
stiff silty clays and sandy clays extending to the maximum depths drilled of about 82 feet
below the existing ground surface
We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths ranging from about 8 7 to
9 1 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration It should be
noted that the water levels at the project site may be influenced by seasonal precipitation
surface water runoff storm surge conditions and other factors

EVALUATION
From our field exploration and laboratory testing it is apparent that the primary soil
layer contributing to the ground settlement and observed differential settlement is the
recent alluvium encountered in the borings The old alluvium below the recent alluvium
does not appear to contribute to the observed settlements and is the bearing stratum for
driven piles supporting the various structures
It is apparent that the on grade sidewalk slabs and pool deck concrete slabs have
settled from their original grades and differential settlements of the slabs have occurred
resulting in uneven slab surfaces at the site In addition some cracking of the slabs and
separation of the slabs from fixed structures such as the pile supported swimming pool
and buildings are evident Based on our evaluation the observed settlements total and
differential settlements may be attributed to the ongoing consolidation of the soft to very
soft clayey soils recent alluvium present across the project site induced by the weight of
the fills placed over the recent alluvium The differential settlements may be attributed to
the variable thickness of soft soils across the project site and also to the different supports
of the structures pile supported versus on grade support
As indicated previously a relatively thick layer of soft to very soft recent alluvium
was encountered in our borings and in the borings by others The recent alluvium ranged
from about 33 5 to 61 5 feet thick Based on the laboratory tests and our field
observations it is evident that the consolidation process of the soft recent alluvium
induced by placement of fill materials at the park site is still ongoing and additional ground
settlements will occur in the future

DISCUSSIONS
Based on our field observations and engineering analyses the following are some
of the options we believe may be considered for implementation at the project site to
accommodate the ongoing and future ground settlements due to consolidation of the soft
recent alluvium Please note that consolidation settlement of the soft recent alluvium is an
ongoing and continuing problem at the site Therefore there is no cost effective solution to
eliminate the settlement issue at the park site
1 We believe that the most effective measure to reduce the effects of the
ongoing and future ground settlement and differential settlement of the
existing improvements is to support all the structures such as the buildings
sidewalks and underground utility lines on a deep foundation system such
as driven piles or drilled micropiles The deep foundation system should
extend through the soft recent alluvium and bear in the stiff and or dense old
alluvium underlying the soft soils The deep foundations will need to be
designed to support the structural loads and the downdrag load caused by
the settling ground around the piles Extending the pile foundations through
the soft soils to bear in the underlying stiff and or dense old alluvium will
result in minimal settlement and differential settlement of the supported
structure However this mitigation measure will be costly to implement for
this project
2 To accommodate the ongoing and future ground settlements at the park site
we recommend removing the existing sidewalk slabs and relocating the
replacement sidewalk slabs as far away from the pile supported building

Because of concerns with vibrations associated with a driven pile foundation and
the lack of space for construction activities we recommend utilizing a drilled micropile
foundation system for support of the new structures Considering the limited space around
the existing swimming pool and building structures and the effects of noise and vibrations
generated from a driven pile foundation on the adjacent structures we recommend
utilizing micropile foundations to support the new structures and underground utilities Our
geotechnical recommendations for the design of the micropile foundations are further
discussed in the following sections
Micropile Foundations
In general the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually
less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile
foundation typically is constructed by drilling a hole with or without casing placing
reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the hole Micropiles are desirable because
they can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil
types and ground conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally causes
minimal disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we
recommend using a micropile system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5 5 inches
to support the new structures Each micropile may be designed based on an allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 20 tons 40 kips The allowable compressive load
capacity of the micropiles is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be increased
by up to one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces Higher
micropile capacities may be achieved with a deeper micropile or a larger diameter
micropile such as a 7 5 inch diameter micropile If needed Geolabs should be
contacted to provide supplemental micropile foundation recommendations
To provide an allowable compressive load capacity of 20 tons 40 kips we
believe that the micropiles should be embedded a minimum of 85 feet below the
existing ground surface or at least 25 feet into the bearing layer consisting of old
alluvium The micropiles would derive its vertical support primarily from skin friction
between the grout bulb bonded zone and the surrounding old alluvium
Based on the subsurface conditions at the project site we recommend using a
permanent casing at the upper portion of the micropile unbonded zone The
permanent casing should have a minimum outside diameter OD of about 5 5 inches
same as the grout bulb size The tip of the permanent casing should extend to the top
of the old alluvium layer located at about 60 feet below the ground level Based on the
depth of the permanent casing the micropile tip would extend to about 85 feet below
the ground surface In general the micropiles should have a minimum center to center
spacing of about 30 inches to avoid reduction in vertical load capacity due to group
action and to facilitate drilling of the micropiles

Downdrag Loads
Due to the ongoing settlement of the soft recent alluvium downdrag loading of
the deep foundations extending through the soft recent alluvium will occur Therefore
the allowable compressive load capacity of the micropiles has been reduced by the
expected downdrag loading in the foundation analyses Based on our foundation
analyses we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the vertical micropiles may
be on the order of about 25 kips per micropile for the 5 5 inch diameter micropile with
steel permanent casing
Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by shear along the grout
bulb bonded zone and the adjacent soils In general an uplift load capacity of up to
60 kips per pile may be achieved with a bonded zone extending a minimum of 25 feet
below the bottom of casing The uplift load capacity provided is intended for transient load
applications only Therefore the uplift load capacities should not be further increased for
transient load applications For sustained uplift loads we recommend reducing the uplift
load capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads The capacity of
the pile member in tension should be checked by the project structural engineer
Lateral Load Resistance
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the lateral load
resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected Lateral loads
imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily by passive earth
pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade beams and by
shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth pcf above the groundwater level and 100 pcf below the
groundwater level The groundwater level at the site may be assumed at about
3 feet MSL for design purposes Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided
by shear resistance between the sides of the pile caps and grade beams and the
adjacent soils A side shear resistance of up to 100 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only

Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the compressive load capacity of the micropiles is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor installing the micropile Therefore the compressive load capacity of the
micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation
systems In order to determine whether the contractor s methods of micropile installation
are adequate and to determine the ultimate compressive load capacity we recommend
performing one pre production compressive load tests in the general vicinity of the existing
swimming pool In general the purpose of the pre production load test on a micropile is to
fulfill the following objectives
e To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor installing the micropiles to the depths required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
e To evaluate the degree of long term settlements of the micropile
In general the pre production load tests should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1143 Standard Loading Procedure Based on experience we believe that the
load tests should be conducted at least seven days after completion of the micropile
installation to allow the grout adequate time to cure Two to four additional micropiles may
be used for reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre production load test
micropile The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load test
micropile to provide adequate reaction in uplift to be determined by the contractor
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 300 percent of the
allowable design load in compression In addition we recommend holding the micropile
for a minimum of 4 hours and 8 hours at the 150 and 250 percent of the allowable
design load respectively depending on the recorded movements of the load test
micropile Please note that the pre production load test is an integral part of the design
of the micropile foundation system
In addition to the pre production load test we also recommend performing pullout
tests proof tests on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the load carrying
capacity of the installed micropiles We recommend testing a minimum of 10 percent of
the total number of micropiles or minimum of 4 micropiles for pullout The pullout tests
should consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 200 percent of the design load and
the maximum test load should be held for at least 10 minutes Pullout tests on the
micropiles also are integral parts of the design of the micropile foundation system

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 each extending to a depth of about
82 feet below the existing ground surface We used a truck mounted drill rig utilizing
solid stem augers and rotary wash drilling methods to advance the boring The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and laboratory testing Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of
the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
In addition relatively undisturbed samples of the soft recent alluvium were
retrieved by hydraulically pushing a 3 inch OD thin wall Shelby tube into the soil
formation The hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler are recorded as the
Penetration Resistance in pounds per square inch psi per foot of penetration on the
Logs of Borings
Pocket Penetrometer and Torvane Shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field The Pocket Penetrometer test provides an
indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample The Torvane Shear
test provides a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample Results
of the Pocket Penetrometer tests and the Torvane Shear tests performed on selected
samples are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples to aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Eleven Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to assist in soil classification and to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 1 and B 2
Three Consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 with time rates of consolidation were
performed on selected soft soil samples to evaluate the compressibility characteristics
of the recent alluvium encountered The consolidation test results are presented on
Plates B 3 through B 5
