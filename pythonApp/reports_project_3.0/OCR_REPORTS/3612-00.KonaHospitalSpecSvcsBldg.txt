INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kona Hospital Expansion project in the Kealakekua area of
Kailua Kona on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Piate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PR T IDERATION
The project site is located within the existing Kona Hospital facility in the
Kealakekua area of Kailua Kona on the island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a
2 story Special Services Building as part of the Kona Hospital Renovation and Expansion
Phase II project
The new building will measure approximately 60 by 80 feet in plan dimensions and
will be located on sloping ground west of the existing hospital building Based on the
existing topography and current design concept cuts and fills on the order of about 8 feet
will be required to attain the design finish grades A retaining wall on the order of
approximately 10 feet high will be required along the southwestern side of the new
building for grade separation

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is an open area between existing buildings within the property of
Kona Hospital in the North Kona District on the island of Hawaii The site is located on
the north side of the main driveway and at the north west corner of the property and is
currently occupied by two wood framed buildings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3612 00 Page 5
Based on the topographic survey map and a field reconnaissance the site vicinity
consists of a gentle rocky slope comprising the southwestern portion of Mauna Loa
Volcano The proposed building location is situated on a gentle slope with a series of
benches separated by retaining walls The northwestern portion of the proposed building
footprint is located at an existing retaining wall of approximately 10 feet in height
The surface condition of the site mainly consists of well maintained grass cover
with many tall trees within the site Two retaining walls consisting of grayish black basalt
cobbles divide the two existing building sites Based on the topographic survey map the
existing ground surface elevation ranges from approximately 1610 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL to 1630 feet MSL
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southern flank of Mauna Loa which has formed
the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site appears
to be pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3612 00 Page 6
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a
hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the
flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the proposed Special Services Building site encountered a
surficial layer of ash soils overlying hard basalt rock formation which extended to the
maximum depth explored of about 22 5 feet below the existing ground surface

On the basis of the competent subsoil conditions encountered at the project site it
is our opinion that shallow foundation consisting of spread and or continuous footings
may be used to support the proposed new building An allowable bearing pressure of up
to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on
the basalt rock formation encountered at shallow depths or on new engineered fills
We recommend that a program of probing and grouting the foundation locations
be implemented during construction to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
due to collapse of cavities and or lava tubes in the basalt formation
Due to the thixotropic nature of the surficial ash soils we recommend that the
surficial ash soils encountered within the building footprint be removed prior to any filling
operations Based on the existing topography and proposed finish grades for the project

12 inches of granular fill material to reduce the potential for slab cracking due to
differential settlements resulting from differing subgrade materials

Foundations
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new building
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3612 00 Page 8
for the design of footing foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation encountered at
shallow depths or on new engineered fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades For footings constructed near top of slopes or on sloping
ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope
face Footings located adjacent to the planned retaining walls should be embedded deep
enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should
be designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3612 00 Page 9
Resistance to the lateral loads due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f These
values assume that the concrete for footings is cast neat against the basalt rock or new
engineered fills A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the
underlying basalt rock formation or new engineered fills Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be
neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be
present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site To reduce the potential
for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing The recommended probing operations may be omitted for footings
located in fill areas where the underlying basalt rock formation is more than 10 feet below
the planned bottom of footings
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3612 00 Page 10
If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation
additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the vertical and
lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and any cavities discovered
should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures A
typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part cement and 0 7 part water
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the proposed building will straddle cut and fill transitions
To reduce the potential for differential settlements at the cut and fill transition due to
differing subgrade materials we recommend that concrete slabs be supported on at least
12 inches of granular fill material The granular fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet
beyond the perimeter of the proposed structure The intent of the 12 inch granular fill
layer is to reduce the potential for slab cracking due to differential settlements at the cut
and fill transition
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3612 00 Page 11
In addition to the 12 inches of granular fill we recommend that a minimum 4 inch
thick layer of cushion fill consisting of Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below the
slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To
reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top
of the BTB cushion layer Two inches of BTB should be placed above the plastic
membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered
since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended that interior walls
be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate small amount of possible
ground movements The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should
be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum of 12 inches of select fill be provided below the concrete
walkway slab Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The
surficial ash soils should be removed and may be stockpiled for use in landscaped
areas if appropriate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3612 00 Page 15
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should
be backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be
abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly
backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose level lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping
lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed building limits and the
area extending a minimum of 2 feet laterally from the edge of the building should
be over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch _ thick
capping layer of granular fill material The over excavated materials may be used
as fill materials provided that the materials meet the gradation requirements
presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection of this report Any loose or soft
spots encountered at the over excavated subgrade levels should be removed and
replaced with compacted select fill
The over excavated subgrade and areas designated to receive fills should be
proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of 6 passes
prior to fill placement to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities
Any loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations
should be excavated to expose firm materials and the over excavation backfilled
with compacted select fill Therefore contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities The proof

Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials to be used as general and structural fill material up to
slab subgrade level should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension The on site cut materials may be used as fill or
backfill materials provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively
well graded select granular material with a maximum size of about 6 inches Any
imported material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory
California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not
more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1883 The fill material should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the
No 200 sieve Any imported material should be observed and or tested by
Geolabs Hawaii for its suitability prior to being transported to the site
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3612 00 Page 17
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for
adequate compaction
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed expansion site is underlain at
shallow depths by volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard It is
anticipated that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the
existing ground surface However excavations into the deeper formations may
require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing rock formations and field data from the
borings performed at the site We recommend that all contractors proposing to bid
on this project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to
make their own interpretation
Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines i e water and sewer lines will be required for
development of this project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be
excavated in hard basalt rock A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3612 00 Page 18
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be
used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material
generally less than 6 inches in maximum size as recommended in the Fills and Backfills

should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Where the utility
line crosses beneath the footings the utility pipes should be concrete jacketed
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it is our opinion that trench work for utility installation may involve excavation
in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard ripping or hoerams Care must be
exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the
rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths of approximately 20 to 22 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
