Due to possible difficulties with the excavation and installation of deadman anchors 
at depths of 9 feet or greater, we recommended using 6.0-inch diameter micropiles to 
provide the shear and pullout resistance for the remaining anchors. 
Based on the pole type and ground line loading information provided by HECO, we 
recommended that the wooden poles be embedded in 24- to 36-inch diameter holes with 
minimum embedment depths of 6.5 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface. 
MICROPILE INSTALLATION AND TESTING 
Geotechnical recommendations for the micropile anchors were presented in our 
referenced memorandum dated June 23, 2022.  The purpose of our geotechnical 
engineering observation and consultation services was to observe the micropile anchors 
installation and testing for general conformance with our recommendations. 
Based on the structural loading and anticipated subsurface conditions at the site, 
the micropiles were specified to have an inclination angle of 15 degrees and use 
permanent casing extending to a depth of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface.  
To keep the micropile anchors for P.326 TEMP and P.326X within the State Right-Of-Way, 
we recommended the battered angle for the two micropiles be reduced to 5 degrees.  In 
addition, we recommended that the micropile length and unbonded diameter for Pole 
P.326X be increased to about 30 feet and 7.625 inches, respectively.  
Prometheus Construction installed a total of eight micropiles from July 6, 2022 
through July 18, 2022 at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 
through 3.3. The micropile locations were provided by HECO.

In general, the micropiles were installed using a TEI Rock Drill equipped with a 
down-the-hole hammer and advanced with 6.0-inch OD (Outside Diameter) steel casing to 
the specified depth of the micropile to create a drilled hole of at least 6.0 inches in 
diameter.  
After the drilled holes were extended to the specified depth and cleaned out, a 
Grade 75, #11 threaded reinforcing bar with centralizers was placed in the cased hole. 
Then, the micropiles were initially grouted. Grout was batched on-site utilizing a colloidal 
high-shear and high-speed ChemGrout CG-500 mixer equipped with a grout pump. The 
grout mix consisted of one sack of 94-pound Portland Cement Type I and/or II, and 
5 gallons of water.  
The grout was pumped into the cased hole through a Polyvinyl-Chloride (PVC) 
tremie pipe starting from the bottom of the cased hole. After clean grout appeared at the 
top of the cased hole, the temporary steel casing was removed one section at a time and 
re-grouted as necessary to maintain the grout level near the top of the casing. This 
process continued until all the temporary casing was extracted. The permanent casing 
was then placed to a final depth of approximately 10 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  
During extraction of the temporary casing on Pole P.326X, one section of the 
temporary casing got disconnected and was lost in the hole. Therefore, the P.326X 
micropile anchor was selected as one of the micropiles to be proof load tested. A summary 
of the micropile installation is presented on Plate A-1. Details of the micropile installation 
are presented in the micropile installation records, Plates A-2.1 through A-9.2. 
Micropile Proof Load Testing 
To evaluate the load carrying capacities of the installed micropiles, two proof tests 
were conducted on two micropiles. The proof tests were conducted on micropile anchors 
P.326X and P.327T-3. The micropile anchors P.326X and P.327T-3 were loaded to a 
maximum test load of 20 and 60 kips, respectively (100 percent of the test load). The 
maximum test load was held for 10 minutes to evaluate creep effects. Subsequently, both 
micropiles were unloaded to zero load in about five equal load decrements.  
In general, the proof-tested production micropiles met the acceptance criteria 
specified in the project requirements. A summary of test results at the maximum test load 
(MTL) is provided in the following table. The results of the proof tests are presented in 
Appendix B.

WOODEN HECO POLE INSTALLATION 
Geotechnical recommendations for HECO poles installation were presented in our 
referenced memorandum dated June 23, 2022. The purpose of our geotechnical 
engineering observation and consultation services was to observe the HECO poles 
installation for general conformance with our recommendations. 
Based on the height and type of the wooden HECO poles, we recommended each 
pole be embedded in a drilled hole with diameter of 24 and 36 inches, and to a minimum 
depth of 6.5 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface. Locations of HECO poles are 
presented on Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
LT Construction and HECO drilled and installed the wooden poles from 
July 19, 2022, through August 9, 2022. Generally, pole locations were drilled to the 
required depth using shovels and pneumatic tools. At locations where hard drilling 
conditions were encountered, an auger was used to advance to the required depth. It 
should be noted that at the Pole P.329X location, LT Construction was unable to excavate 
down to the required depth of 10 feet and thus the pole was installed with 9 feet of 
embedment.  The pole analysis was rerun with the presence of the hard layer.  Based on 
the analysis, the 9-foot embedment was satisfactory. 
Following completion of drilling operations, HECO installed the wooden poles and 
backfilled with No. 3B Fine gravel. It should be noted that Pole P.326 TEMP was 
embedded to a depth of 17 feet below the existing ground surface and backfilled with 
concrete. A summary of the HECO poles installation is presented on Plate C-1 in 
Appendix C. 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field observations of the HECO poles and micropile installation, and 
micropile proof load tests, it is our professional opinion that the HECO poles and the 
micropile anchors observed and tested have been installed to provide the required design 
load capacity with tolerable movements. 
LIMITATIONS 
The results submitted herein are based, in part, upon our field observations and the 
proof load tests performed during the HECO pole installation work. It should be noted that 
it is not possible to continuously observe all activities occurring simultaneously on a
