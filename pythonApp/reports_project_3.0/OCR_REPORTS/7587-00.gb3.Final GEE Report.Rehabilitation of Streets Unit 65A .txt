SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the existing pavement structures for the various 
roadways under this project generally consisted of 4 to 8 inches of PCC. In some borings, 
we encountered about 1 to 12 inches of base material over subgrade soils consisting of silty 
and clayey soil that were generally stiff and moist.  It should be noted that some of the 
borings did not encounter any base material below the PCC pavement. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
However, we encountered seepage water in the boring drilled at Yvonne Place at a depth 
of about 2.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.   
Based on the assumed design traffic and the subgrade conditions encountered, the 
new rigid pavement sections for the roadways under this project would consist of 6 to 
7 inches of PCC over 6 inches of aggregate base course. The aggregate base course was 
increased to 12 inches thick at Iholena Street due to the expansive nature of the subgrade 
soil. 
The in-situ moistures of the soils encountered at most of the roadways under this 
project were relatively high (40 to 50 percent).  Based on our experience, it would be difficult 
to achieve a relative compaction of 95 percent or higher (typically recommended for 
pavement subgrade soils) without significant aeration of the high moisture subgrade soils. It 
is not feasible or practical to aerate the subgrade soils since this would disrupt the local 
vehicular traffic for an extended period. Therefore, we recommend reducing the compaction 
requirement to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction for the pavement subgrade 
soils.  Where it is still difficult to achieve a relative compaction of at least 90 percent, we 
recommend providing a stabilization layer consisting of 8 inches of aggregate base course 
over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx TX7 or equivalent) underlain by a woven 
geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent). 
Our field observations indicated that the existing roadways under this project are 
located on inclines. Based on our experience, we have observed soft soils near the 
downslope end of roadways on inclines. We believe that the subgrade soils become 
saturated as a result of seepage water flowing down the slope and collecting at the point 
where the roadway gradient flattens out.  To reduce the potential for saturation of the 
subgrade soils at the downslope end of the roadways, we recommend placing a transverse 
subdrain (across the entire roadway width) near the bottom of the sloping roadway. In 
general, the subdrain should be a minimum of 18 inches wide and 12 inches deep below 
the pavement subgrade.  The subdrain should consist of a 6-inch PVC perforated pipe (with 
perforations facing down) near the bottom of the trench.  The perforated pipe should be 
surrounded by No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. The project site is at the southern flank of the Koolau 
Mountain Range. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium. Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial 
processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as 
alluvium.

2.2 
Site Description 
The project is located between Likelike Highway and Kaimuki in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The roadways under this project are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 2.1 through 2.16. 
In general, the roadways are located on relatively steep grades. The traffic volume 
for the roadways appears to be relatively low consisting mainly of passenger vehicles and 
occasional heavy trucks, such as garbage trucks.  
Based on our reconnaissance, the existing PCC pavements appear to be in 
relatively poor to fair conditions. The pavements have exhibited longitudinal and 
transverse cracking that are moderate to high in severity. Some corner breaks were also 
observed. We observed both asphalt concrete (AC) and PCC patches that appear to be 
relatively high in severity. 
Evidence of pumping was observed at Nihi Street, Kualono Street, Klebahn Place, 
Oswald Street, Yvonne Place, Puunoa Place, Driveway at 2521 Waiomao Road, and 
Halekipa Place.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

roadways under this project generally consisted of 5 to 8 inches of PCC. In some borings,

Pavement Design

a minimum 6 inches of aggregate base course below the PCC pavement. Due to the 
relatively high swell potential of the pavement subgrade soils encountered at Iholena 
Street, we recommend increasing the aggregate base course thickness to a minimum of 
12 inches. The aggregate base course layer above the pavement subgrade should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

Typically, pavement subgrade soils are compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction (ASTM D1557). However, the in-situ moisture of the silty and clayey 
soils encountered at most of the roadways under this project was relatively high (40 to 
50 percent). In addition, the areas are located in moderate rainfall environments. We 
believe that it would be difficult to achieve a relative compaction of 95 percent or higher 
without significant aeration of the subgrade soils. It is not feasible or practical to aerate 
the subgrade soils since this would disrupt the local vehicular traffic for an extended 
period. Due to the high in-situ moisture of the subgrade soils, we recommend not 
scarifying the subgrade soils and reducing the compaction requirement to a minimum of

90 percent relative compaction.  In addition, we recommend not using the vibratory action 
of the compactor during the compaction operations because the vibratory action will 
induce a pumping condition of the high moisture subgrade soils. 
If it is still difficult to achieve a relative compaction of at least 90 percent, we 
recommend over-excavating to a depth of about 8 inches below the pavement subgrade 
and providing a stabilization layer. The stabilization layer should consist of 8 inches of 
aggregate base course over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx TX7 or equivalent) 
underlain by a woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) as shown on 
Plate 3. The aggregate base course for the stabilization layer should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that the pavement subgrade at 
Anuu Place would consist of basalt rock. We anticipate that the pavement subgrade at 
Miller Street would consist of tuff, having varying degrees of weathering, and the 
pavement subgrade at Frear Street would consist of cinder sand. Where basalt and tuff 
are encountered at the pavement subgrade, we recommend proof-rolling the pavement 
subgrade with a 10-ton roller or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of eight passes 
to detect the presence and possible collapsing near-surface cavities and/or voids. 
Yielding areas, soft/loose areas, or cavities/voids disclosed during the proof-rolling 
operations should be exposed to firm ground and backfilled with aggregate base course 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
The cinder deposits anticipated at Frear Street should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The moisture should be maintained in the cinder sand and not allowed to 
dry. If allowed to dry, the cinder sand may loosen near the surface. The aggregate base 
course should be placed and compacted as soon as practical over the moistened and 
compacted cinder sand subgrade.

3.5 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subgrade soils. It should be noted that the silty/clayey subgrade soils 
are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. Therefore, special 
attention should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements. As a 
minimum, the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage, along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures, is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or 
premature failure of the pavements.  
The existing roadways are located on inclines. Based on our experience, we have 
observed soft soils near the downslope end of roadways (transition between AC and PCC 
pavements) located on inclines. We believe that the soils become saturated as a result 
of seepage water flowing down the slope and collecting at the point where the roadway 
gradient flattens out. To reduce the potential for saturation of the subgrade soils at the 
downslope end of the roadways, we recommend placing a transverse subdrain (across 
the entire roadway width) near the bottom of the sloping roadway. In general, the subdrain 
should be a minimum of 18 inches wide and 12 inches deep below the pavement 
subgrade. The subdrain should consist of a 6-inch polyvinyl chloride (PVC) perforated 
pipe (with perforations facing down) near the bottom of the trench surrounded by 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation). A non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N 
or equivalent) should be placed around the No. 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for 
migration of fines into the drainage material. A typical subdrain detail is provided on 
Plate 4 for reference.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the roadways under this project by drilling 
and sampling a total of 26 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 26, extending to 
depths of about 5 to 6 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.16. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring 
tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-26. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Twelve Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plate B-3. 
Eight One-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of 
the subsurface soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4. 
Twenty-six laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk samples of the subgrade soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-30.
