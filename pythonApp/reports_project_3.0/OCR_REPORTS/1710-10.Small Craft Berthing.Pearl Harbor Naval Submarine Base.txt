INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the new location of the proposed small craft
berthing pier in the Naval submarine base in Pearl Harbor on the island
of Oahu Hawaii
The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based
on the data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and are not intended for construction estimation
or bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project includes demolition of three existing Finger
Piers Nos 1194 through 1196 and the construction of a larger new
concrete pier with supporting facilities for the accommodation of larger
Torpedo Weapons Retriever crafts
The initial design of the new pier measured about 16 feet wide by 150
feet long and was located between the existing Finger Pier Nos 1193 and
1194 A previous field exploration had been performed at this pier
location and a soil report our W O 1710 00 dated had
been prepared and issued by Geolabs Hawaii

Currently the proposed pier will be relocated about 30 feet north of
the originally proposed location and reduced in size to about 12 feet wide
by 126 feet long due to operational and other considerations
Vertical loads on the pile foundations of the proposed pier which
will mainly be used for pedestrian traffic are anticipated to be low
Lateral loads on the piles supporting the proposed pier are not available
at this time However batter piles are anticipated for at least a
portion of the proposed pier based on previous analyses
The general layout of the proposed construction is shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2

the foundation design

proposed pile supported pier

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the results of a
complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by
glacioeustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities
during the Pleistocene Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau
Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine
environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps about 300
feet below and rising about 95 feet above the present sea level
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene era
eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash which
consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of tuff
During the Recent epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand
the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits These
deposits were subsequently covered by man made fills placed during the
middle portion of this century for land reclamation projects during the
construction of the existing Naval Submarine Base

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our borings along the new location of the proposed pier we
believe that the near shore portion of the new pier may be supported by
piles bearing on the hard weathered tuff formation encountered at a depth
of about 61 feet below water surface MLLW in Boring No 101
The hard weathered tuff formation was not encountered in Boring Nos
102 and 103 which were drilled at the center and the seaward end of the
proposed pier We believe that this portion of the proposed pier may be
supported by friction piles which should at least penetrate to the medium
dense gravel layer encountered at depths of about 72 to 97 feet in the
borings
To resist the design lateral loads batter piles may be required for
the full length or at least the seaward half of the pier where the
lagoonal deposits are thicker and weaker

A better estimate of the production pile lengths and an approximate
delineation of the boundary between the two different types of piles may
be obtained by implementing a test pile program
Pile Capacities
The proposed concrete pier may be supported by both vertical and
batter piles which should either bear on the weathered tuff formation or
penetrate to a sufficient depth below mudline to achieve sufficient pile
capacities
recommended
Vertical Pile Capacity
The end bearing piles would develop their vertical load capacity
primarily from bearing on the hard weathered tuff formation It is
anticipated that the hard continuous bearing stratum encountered in
Boring No 101 will vary in depth along the pier in the vicinity of
the boring Therefore it is estimated that the total vertical pile
length required may vary from about 65 feet below the proposed pier
deck level at elevation 104 feet near the shore to about 75 feet
below the proposed pier deck level in the area between Boring Nos
101 and 102
Where the hard tuff formation is not encountered or the pile is able
to penetrate the layer then the pile should be considered a friction
pile The friction piles would develop their vertical load capacity
primarily from adhesion between the piles and the medium dense tuff
gravel layer and or the very stiff Older Alluvium at depths of about
70 to 100 feet below the water surface MLLW and partially from
frictional resistance or adhesion between the piles and the upper
loose soft lagoonal soils and the loose tuff gravel layer

Therefore it is estimated that the friction piles should be driven
to a minimum total depth of about 110 feet below the proposed pier
deck level in order to develop sufficient frictional resistance or
adhesion between the piles and the subsoils
Based on the above estimated total pile lengths an allowable
vertical pile capacity of up to 100 tons per pile is recommended for
both types of pile Pile settlements on the order of less than
one half of an inch are anticipated for these pile foundations
If a lower vertical pile capacity may be used to support the proposed
pier it is our opinion that the friction pile lengths may be
shortened somewhat However the reduction in friction pile lengths
generally would be small since as a minimum all of the piles should
extend through the soft lagoonal deposits
For shorter friction piles with a minimum 100 foot length we
recommend a lower allowable vertical pile capacity of 50 tons per
pile Pile settlements of the same order are anticipated for these
shorter pile foundations
Due to the different subsoil conditions below lagoonal deposits along
the new pier location we believe that a better estimate of the total
and production pile lengths should be obtained by using a test pile
program
Lateral Pile Capacity
lateral resistance of the driven piles were analyzed by using the
appropriate lateral soil modulus values of the subsoils at various
depths below the mudline in Boring Nos 101 to 103 The analyses
were carried out by using a finite difference program BMCOLPY G
which is an extended version of the original program developed for
analyzing elastic beam columns on elastic foundations
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1710 10 Page 9
Dk No 55
The modified program is equipped with the capability to generate
internally P Y curves with given soil data which allows the analysis
of lateral piles in inelastic soil mediums idealized as beam columns
on inelastic foundations represented by P Y curves
Since the soil modulus varies with the deflection experienced the
computerized analysis first assumes a lateral modulus value then
calculates the lateral pile deflection based on the assumed modulus
The calculated deflection is then used to determine the lateral
modulus from the appropriate P Y curve which is compared with the
assumed modulus The procedure is repeated until the assumed and the
calculated moduli are essentially the same
The above analysis assumes a free pile head condition It is our
opinion that the free head condition may be appropriate for the piles
supporting the seaward end of the pier However the structural
rigidity of the near shore portion of the pier may provide some
fixity to the pile head and thereby may reduce the lateral
deflections of the supporting piles in this area
The results of the analyses showing the relationships between
calculated lateral deflections at the tops of the piles and the
applied lateral forces under a free head condition are summarized on
Plate C 2 Appendix C A summary of the calculated maximum bending
moments and the depths of the calculated points of fixity along the
piles under different lateral forces is presented on Plate C 1
It should be noted that the calculated maximum bending moments are
theoretical values obtained from numerical computation Physically
the induced bending moments along the piles should not exceed the

Uplift Capacity
Based on our analysis we estimate that an allowable uplift capacity
of 15 tons per pile may be used for the 65 foot long end bearing
piles It is also estimated that an allowable uplift capacity of 35
tons per pile may be used for the 110 foot long friction piles while
a lower allowable uplift capacity of 30 ton per pile may be used for
the shorter 100 foot friction piles
The above recommended uplift capacities consider continuous pile
lengths without splicing Therefore if pile splicing is
anticipated the recommended uplift capacities should not be included
in the design of the pile foundations
Pile Driving
The end bearing piles should be driven with a hammer delivering a
minimum of about 32 000 foot pounds of energy and to a resistance of 100
blows per foot for the last foot or 20 blows for the last inch for the
100 ton piles
The friction piles should also be driven with the same hammer
delivering a minimum of about 32 000 foot pounds of energy and to a
resistance of about 60 blows per foot for the last foot or 10 blows each
for the last two inches for the 100 ton piles The pile driving
requirements for the 50 ton friction piles using the same hammer may be
lowered to a resistance of about 30 blows per foot for the last foot or 5
blows each for the last two inches
The driving process should be continuous without interruption for
each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when
the driving process is interrupted for more than 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft soil
the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled so that excessive tensile
stresses are not transmitted to the pile

The pile driving should be monitored by a qualified geotechnical
engineer to see that the piles are driven either to a competent bearing
formation or to the required depth to achieve the design capacities
Test Pile Program
Due to the complex nature of the subsoils along the new pier
location a test pile program is strongly recommended in order to attempt
to achieve a safe and economical pile foundation design
The test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following
objectives
1 To approximately delineate the boundary and if necessary a
transition zone between the end bearing and friction piles
2 To more accurately determine the production pile lengths
3 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the pile driving
criteria
We recommend that about 10 percent of the total piles be test driven
at the site A substantial portion of the test piles should be driven
between Boring Nos 101 and 102 in order to achieve the Objective No 1
These test piles can later be incorporated into the actual foundation of
the proposed pier
Splice cans should be provided at the site during the implementation
of the test pile driving program to expedite splicing if required
Splice cans may be fabricated with one fourth of an inch thick high grade
steel and should be at least 6 feet long

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling three
3 offshore borings to depths of 81 4 to 136 5 feet below the Mean Low
Low Water MLLW level at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 A raft mounted drill rig was used to drill the offshore
borings In addition two 2 field vane shear tests were performed
during the drilling and sampling operations of a selected boring
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist who continuously
observed the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual observation and testing in the laboratory The soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 2 inch
standard split spoon sampler with 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches
or by hydraulically pushing a 3 inch diameter thin wall tube The blow
counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch penetration
are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Rock core samples and some soil samples were obtained by coring with
a NX core barrel The Core Recovery REC and Rock Quality Designation
RQD of the core samples are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
The field vane shear tests were performed by using a vane with
2 5 inch diameter and 5 inch length The vane was first pushed into the
undisturbed soil and then rotated The change of the measured torque with
angular rotation was monitored Maximum torques of both the undisturbed
and remolded soil condition were recorded The test results are
summarized and presented on Plate A 4 1

Lateral Pile Analyses

103 The analyses were carried out by using a finite difference program
BMCOLPY G

deflections at the top of the piles and the applied lateral forces under a

moments and depths of the points of fixity along the piles under different
