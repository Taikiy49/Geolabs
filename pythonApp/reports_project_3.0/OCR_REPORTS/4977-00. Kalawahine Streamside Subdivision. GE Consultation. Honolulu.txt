SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
An evaluation of the hillslopes located upslope of 35 residential jots at the
Kalawahine Streamside Subdivision was conducted in an effort to study the potential
rockfall hazards present at the project site The study area was separated into
two regions which encompass the developed hillside area located below the existing
drainage ditch Region No 1 and the undeveloped hillside area located above the
existing drainage ditch Region No 2 Our site reconnaissance and mapping effort
consisted of identifying potential boulder hazards and assessing a rating of either low
moderate or high potential hazard based on established evaluation criteria In addition
areas of surface rock outcroppings were mapped in an effort to identify possible source
regions for future rockfall boulder generation
Based on our site reconnaissance and mapping effort potential rockfall hazards
involving immanently hazardous conditions which would require immediate remedial
attention were not encountered Based on the mapping effort a total of 546 potential
boulder hazards were identified at the site Fifty six boulders were mapped as high
potential hazards 93 boulders were mapped as moderate potential hazards and
397 boulders were mapped as low potential hazards
Region No 1 contains very few potential boulder hazards however due to the
close proximity of the cut slopes to the rear of the homes some nuisance erosion
conditions involving surface runoff erosion and the deposition of eroded debris at the rear
patio area of some homes was identified Remedial efforts to reduce the potential erosion
and deposition of debris may include raking small stones and rock fragments from the
slope surface slope planting landscaping and construction of low barrier walls Potential
boulder hazards embedded in the rock cut slope may be restrained in place with
engineered solutions
Region No 2 contains a large number of potential boulder hazards that are widely
dispersed across the densely vegetated undeveloped hillside It appears that a suitable
remedial solution may involve the construction of a special rockfall barrier fence designed
to absorb the energy of rolling boulders The barrier fence system appears to be a
comprehensive solution for most boulder hazards located on the hillside Other remedial
alternatives involving individual boulder restraint systems and boulder destruction may

In accordance with your request and our fee proposal dated
we have conducted an evaluation for the potential rockfall hazards present in the vicinity of
the existing Kalawahine Subdivision located in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older shield volcano Waianae Volcano is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western
third of the island The younger shield volcano Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene Epoch to early Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the Koolau
Volcano remained active Therefore the older Waianae Volcano s eastern flank was
partially buried below the younger Koolau Volcano lavas that banked against the Waianae
Voicano s eastern flank
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by a
long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the dome
shaped Koolau Shield Volcano The erosional valleys grew larger by the processes of
stream erosion and mass wasting landslides and rockfall emanating from the steepened
valley sidewalls The deeper valley floors were filled with accumulations of alluvial
sediments and colluvial landslide materials as the island eroded and slowly depressed
beneath the weight of the new landmass
Approximately 850 000 to years ago some limited volcanic activity
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents scattered
across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu The eruptions occurred along the
established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano Some of the most notable
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head Craters
were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series In addition some Honolulu
Volcanic Series eruptions occurred in the Tantalus area that blanketed the Koolau
mountainside and Makiki vicinity with cinders Roundtop and Sugarloaf pyroclastic
deposits The eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series continued over a period of several
hundred thousand years which might have ended as recently as approximately
30 000 years ago

2 2 Site Geology
As mentioned previously the project site is situated on the hillside below Makiki
Heights The project site is located near the mouth of a relatively shallow depth valley
incised by Kanaha Stream which extends inland toward the Tantalus area The relief
from the valley floor to the top of the eastern ridge at Kalawahine Place is on the order of
less than about 200 feet in vertical elevation The Kanaha Stream valley axis drains
toward the southwesterly direction and the subject hillslopes within the project site slope
down toward the valley axis from the easterly direction
Based on our review of available geologic information and our site reconnaissance
the subject hillside is generally composed of layered A a type basaltic rock representing
the Koolau Volcanic Series lava flows that are covered by a generally thin surface veneer
of clayey and silty soils The A a lava flows were deposited sequentially as individual lava
flow layers during the eruption of the Koolau Volcano
Surface boulders and rock fragments generated by the erosion and breakdown of
the parent rock formation are encountered as widely scattered deposits throughout the
project site The boulders and rock fragments typically break from the parent rock
formation which may become cyclically unstable with the passage of time and effects of
erosion The fallen boulders and rock fragments then move downslope to form colluvial
talus deposits consisting of loose rock materials or individual boulder deposits The
boulders and rock fragments usually come to rest as relatively stable deposits on the
hillside Less frequently the boulders may roll or bounce downslope for considerable
distances if the hillslope conditions sustain the movement of the rock fragments In
addition some colluvial boulders and rock fragments may also temporarily rest in unstable
positions on steeper slopes Subsequent erosion or ground disturbance can re activate
the downslope movemeni of the rocks
Based on our site reconnaissance there does not appear to be an extensive
surface deposit of permeable sandy cinder material on the hillside which may have been
derived from the more recent eruptions that occurred at Tantalus However the hillslopes
appear to be generally uniform in contour without the presence of significant incised

The A a lava flows that compose the hillside form generally low relief outcrops at
the ground surface The rock outcrops observed at the site have generally about 3 to
6 feet of vertical relief with some prominent ledge outcroppings extending up to about
10 feet in vertical height The rock formation outcrops tend to create a stepped hillside
character with sporadic bare rock ledges or scarps interspersed with flatter terrace
features covered with surface soils The A a lava flows are typified by the presence of
hard rock layers interbedded with clinker seams that are composed of weaker more
erosive fragmented rock materials The bounding clinker seams are generally thinner than
the more massive hard rock layers that represent the core of the individual lava flows An
illustration showing the typical slope conditions of the hillside is provided on Plate 3
The hard rock layers usually form more erosion resistant ledge outcroppings with
near vertical scarp faces The underlying clinker seam is usually more susceptible to
erosion and weathering Therefore the weaker clinker seams erode faster than the
overlying hard cap rock creating undercut ledges and cave features As the erosion of the
clinker seam progresses the hard rock ledge becomes increasingly unsupported and is
susceptible to breakage If the undercut becomes pronounced the hard rock ledge may
fracture from the parent outcrop and fall to the ground thus initiating downslope movement
as a boulder Other possible causes of rockfall boulder generation include failure along
open fractures within the rock formation and the presence of voids and lava tubes which
may initiate instability in the overlying rock materials
It should be noted that rockfall and mass wasting of steep slopes are a fundamental
part of the geologic evolution of hillslopes within valleys It remains very difficult if not
impossible to predict when rockfall may occur as many variables have an effect on the
generation of rockfall events However experience has shown that rockfall hazards are
heightened most during and following periods of moderate to heavy rainfall where runoff is
produced and when disturbance to the slope surface is incurred

hazards present at the project site that are in immediate need of emergency mitigation
However we believe that some form of rockfall hazard mitigation should be undertaken
within a prudent time frame to address the potential hazards observed at the site Due to
the anticipated non emergency situation regarding the potential rockfall hazards the
rockfall hazard mitigation may be constructed in a phased approach as funds are made
available for the project within a reasonable time frame from the time of our
reconnaissance As mentioned previously it remains very difficult if not impossible for
engineers and or geologists to predict when rockfall may occur as many variables have an
effect on the generation of rockfall events

Potential Hazard Monitoring
The alternative may involve the development and implementation of a systematic
procedure to monitor for potential movement of boulder and rock outcrop hazards
with the passage of time The use of repeated visual observations combined with
physical measuring devices may be used to monitor the development of fractures
or changes in the stability of potentially hazardous rocks
Based on the large number of potential hazards present scattered across the
hillside we do not believe that this is a viable alternative for this project In addition
the primary mechanism of rockfall failure remobilization of existing boulders does
not lend itself well to the monitoring measurement of potential movements as the
rockfall initiation may occur in response to a triggering mechanism which may
occur rapidly and without much warning

The boulder destruction alternative involves the use of destructive force to

displacement to other stable ground areas Boulder destruction may be achieved

mechanical tools to demolish the rock into smaller pieces that can be removed from
the site or relocated at the site
With the use of chemical agents the chemical mixture is injected into drilled holes
in the rock and allowed to slowly expand and crack the rock The time needed to
crack the rock ranges from 1 to 20 hours depending on the chemical mixture and
quality of the rock thus monitoring of the cracking procedure may be warranted

Impact Fence
The impact fence alternative involves the construction of a special rockfall impact
fence designed to suppress and retain large boulder impacts In general the
impact fence consists of embedded fence posts or beams and lateral cable
supports constructed on concrete foundations Special ring nets and wire mesh
materials span between the posts The fence is designed to absorb the energy
from rock impacts and retain the fallen material The fence may be designed for
varying load impacts that are estimated based on the specific site conditions such
as boulder size slope inclination and computed rolling velocities and boulder
bounce heights
The fences are commonly used along highways and other areas where structures
located along lengthy slope segments must be protected from rockfall The fences
are suitable for sites where rockfall sources are widely scattered and where it may
be generally impractical to stabilize the individual hazardous blocks of rock because
of the quantity of hazards difficult slope access and other restrictions The fences
would require some periodic maintenance to check the performance of the system
and remove any accumulated rock material
