This letter report presents the findings from our geotechnical engineering 
consultation conducted in support of the evaluation of the structural integrity of the

area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Aliipoe Ditch is a rectangular concrete drainage ditch that is located between 
two rows of residences in the Halawa Heights area. The upper row of houses is situated 
along Lalawai Drive while the lower houses are situated along Aliipoe Drive. 
The 640-foot long Aliipoe ditch was recently reconstructed; however, cracks were 
observed along the ditch channel walls after reconstruction. The reconstructed ditch is 
approximately 8 feet wide and up to 8 feet high.  
The ditch channel design consultant has evaluated the conditions and has 
provided proposed remedial measures. We have been tasked by the City and County of 
Honolulu, Department of Design and Construction to review the soils report prepared by 
the design consultant’s geotechnical engineer and the remedial measures proposed by 
the ditch channel design consultant.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the majority of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the eastern two-thirds of the island. After a long 
period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the 
Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and 
tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano. The project area is 
generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic 
Series. These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt 
with interbedded thin clinker layers. In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows 
has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years. The weathering process has formed a mantle 
of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material 
while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical of the 
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual soils and saprolite grade to basaltic 
rock formation with increased depth.

In general, the surface behind the concrete channel wall along the north side 
slopes upward at an inclination ranging from one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) to 
2H:1V. The surface is covered with grouted rubble paving (GRP) with weepholes along 
the bottom of the GRP. 
The ground surface behind the south side channel wall slopes downward in the 
southern direction. The soils behind the south side channel wall consist of clayey soils 
that were generally stiff in consistency.   
We observed vertical and diagonal hairline cracks along the length of the channel 
walls. The cracks were spaced about 8 to 10 feet apart. We did not observe apparent 
rotational or transverse movements of the channel walls. We observed two construction 
joints, but no expansion joints. Based on our observations, we believe that the cracks 
that have developed along the ditch channel walls are due to the shrinkage of the 
concrete. Both channel walls do not have weepholes. 
We observed evidence of seepage water from the shrinkage cracks. This 
indicates that water builds up behind the channel walls (most likely during heavy rains) 
and slowly seeps through the cracks. Since the water seeps slowly through the cracks, 
it is likely that hydrostatic pressures act on the wall.  
REVIEW OF GEOTECHNICAL REPORT OTHERS 
As part our scope of work, we reviewed the report entitled “Geotechnical 
Engineering Evaluation, Aliipoe Ditch Channel Wall, Halawa Heights, Oahu, Hawaii” 
dated April 22, 2013 and prepared by Atlas Geotechnical and the proposed remedial 
measures by Lyon Associates, Inc. 
Based on our review, three borings were drilled behind the north side channel 
wall. The borings encountered an 11-inch thick layer of grouted rip rap (or GRP). Below 
the grouted rip rap, a layer of boulders and cobbles about 1.4 to 3.2 feet thick was 
encountered. Atlas Geotechnical refers to the layer of boulders and cobbles as 
ungrouted rip rap. The boulders and cobbles consist of hard, angular basalt ranging 
from 6 to 24 inches in maximum dimension. Atlas Geotechnical believes that the 
boulders were stacked in place. Below the layer of boulders and cobbles, residual soils 
consisting of stiff to very stiff silt and clay were encountered extending to the maximum 
depths explored of about 6.7 to 8.3 feet below the grouted rip rap surface. Groundwater 
was not encountered in the borings drilled by Atlas Geotechnical. 
Atlas Geotechnical concluded that the soils behind the channel walls do not exert 
lateral earth pressures on the walls. The only lateral pressures acting on the channel 
walls are hydrostatic pressure (4 feet high) and expansion (swelling) pressure from the 
residual soils consisting of silt and clay.

Based on the information provided by Atlas Geotechnical, Lyon Associates, Inc. 
recommended sealing the cracks with a crack repair product such as Xypex. In addition, 
they recommended placing a 10-inch high by 8-inch wide concrete curb along the base

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS 
Based on our review of the geotechnical report prepared by Atlas Geotechnical 
for the Aliipoe Ditch Channel Wall, we provide the following conclusions below. 
1. 
Due to the relatively thick layer of boulders/cobbles and the size of the 
boulders (24 inches) that were encountered, we believe that it would have 
been impractical to stack boulders (from a construction point of view) 
behind the channel walls. We believe that the boulders and cobbles were 
dumped behind the channel walls with construction equipment. Since the 
boulders/cobbles were dumped behind the channel walls, the layer of 
boulders and cobbles will exert lateral earth pressures on the channel 
walls. In addition, we believe that the boulders and cobbles were not 
placed level with the top of channel walls and is likely sloping upward; 
therefore, lateral earth pressures from the boulder/cobble layer should be 
analyzed with sloping ground conditions. 
2. 
To determine the lateral earth pressures imposed on the wall by the 
residual soils, we believe that the drained cohesion of the residual soils 
should be ignored; therefore, the residual soils should not be 
self-supporting in the long-term condition.  
3. 
The surcharge pressures from the 11-inch thick layer of grouted rip rap 
should be included in the analyses.  
4. 
As mentioned previously, we observed evidence of seepage water from 
the shrinkage cracks. We believe that hydrostatic pressures should be 
relieved by installing two levels of weepholes along the channel walls. The 
weepholes should be placed near the bottom and about 18 inches below 
the top of the channel walls. Weepholes may be installed by coring 
through the wall, placing a PVC pipe through the cored hole, and grouting 
the annular space. A filter fabric should be placed on the end of the PVC 
pipe that is located on the retaining side of the channel walls.  
5. 
Lyon Associates, Inc. recommends sealing cracks with a crack repair 
product such as Xypex. Based on our review of Xypex repair procedures, 
this sealant is applied at the concrete surface and will only extend about 
1.5 inches into the crack. The sealant will not seal the full depth of the 
crack. If this is the case, the acidic soil and groundwater will still come into
