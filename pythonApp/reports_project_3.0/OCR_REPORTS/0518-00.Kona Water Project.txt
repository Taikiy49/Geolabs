This report contains our recommendations based on a geological
reconnaissance and review of existing literature for the pro
posed water development shaft in North Kona Hawaii
The scope of our work was to provide preliminary recommendations
to evaluate construction methods for the water shaft tunnel
and pump room
GEOLOGY
The project area is on the western slopes of Hualalai Volcano
Hualalai Volcano is about 17 miles in diameter and is one of
the most symmetrical cones in Hawaii The internal structure
of Hualalai is not exposed but presumably is a typical shield
shaped dome of primitive lavas No faults are known on Hualalai

except for those bounding small pit craters or minor displace
ments along fissures in the rift zones none of which appears
to be found in the project area
The volcano is composed of rocks of the Hualalai Volcanic
Series which are chiefly olivine basalts with an incomplete
veneer of olivine rich basalts carrying augite phenocrysts
Several flows of basaltic andesite and one of andesite have
been found The entire mountain seems to have been built in
the Quaternary It is assumed that the whole mountain was
built by continuous activity but it is highly suspected that
erosional unconformities may exist Both Aa and pahoehoe
lava are abundant the flows averaging 15 to 20 feet in thick
ness The upper portion of Aa flows generally consist of
clinker basalt that usually is classified as soft rock in well
logs
Hualalai has been dormant since 1801 but several small earth
tremors indicate its magmatic hearth is stillsxactive A thick
black Aa lava flow enters the sea just north of Keauhou The
flow is about 12 miles long and as much as 2 miles wide It
probably issued about 250 years ago It appears that the
project site is located on this flow

INVESTIGATION
Our geologist was able to obtain the boring logs of 3 wells
two of which are adjacent to the project area The third
well is located about a mile southwest The locations of
these wells are shown on Plate 1 Project Area Map The
well logs are included in the Appendix A study of the
existing information was made with particular reference to
the geologic data and well logs Based on this information
we have prepared a generalized subsurface cross section
showing the wells in relation to the proposed shaft An
assumption was made to the effect that the ancient lava
flows occurred on slopes about equal to the existing surface
This cross section is illustrated on Plate 2 Our geologist
visited the site and made a surface inspection of the pro
ject area well sites and road cuts An engineer and
geologist from our firm inspected the Halawa Shaft in
Honolulu This shaft cut through Aa lava flows similar to
some degree with the lava flows along the proposed shaft
alignment
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The proposed shaft alignment will obliquely cut through lava
flows that are dipping about 10 percent in a westerly

direction These lava flows consist of lava tubes and scora
ceous zones that may contain trapped groundwater Available
data indicates that the project site area does not generally
contain groundwater impounded in lava flows by dikes or dense
lava flows A part of the water perched or impounded above
sea level discharges most of it at sea level as diffused flow
along the shore Some water seepage may be encountered during
rainy seasons where the shaft dissects across the water bear
ing zones The cross section on Plate 2 illustrates areas
where the well drilling lost water circulation and where it
may be expected in shaft excavations The mean annual rainfall
over the project area is about 40 inches the amount of water
seepage through tubes may be minimal during the dry season
A small amount of water will seep through the pourous rocks
during times of rain
Construction
Based on our reconnaissance and review of the well logs it
appears that the shaft alignment below elevation 200 MSL will
encounter the largest amount of soft to medium hard rock It
is anticipated that this zone is composed of thinly bedded Aa
lava flows with clinker rock Some smaller zones of soft
rock may be encountered near Elevations 320 and 500 MSL This
does not discount the probability of encountering thin beds
of soft or clinker rock at other elevations

A good indicator of rock quality is Deere s RQD Rock Quality
Designation which is based on a modified core recovery in
which pieces of core less than 4 inches in length in soft rock
are not counted Based on our experience in drilling into
lava rock we have found the RQD to generally vary between
40 and 100 In examining the unlined Halawaa water development
tunnel beds of clinker and soft rock from about one to four
feet in thickness were observed Some minor spalling had taken
place but it appeared the soft beds had become moderately
stable
In an article in Civil Engineer Cecil pre
sents the following table of empirical relationships for
Pre Cambrian rocks of the Scandinavian Shield A similar
relationship may be valid under a wide variety of loosening
ground conditions
RQD SUPPORT
90 Minimum rock bolts or none
60 90 Intermediate rock bolts and one
shotcrete application
60 Maximum rock bolts steel net and
multiple shotcrete applications
Based on our inspection of Halawa tunnel and interpretation of
the well logs it is our opinion that the tunnel excavation
above Elevation 200 may not require support other than shotcrete

in loose zones However below Elevation 200 the use of steel
ribs or steel nets or some other equal method of tunnel sup
port may have to be used Shotcrete may have to be used in
zones of clinker and soft rock but not for the more massive
lava rock
From our generalized cross section it appears the pump room
and water development tunnel may be in soft rock However
reinforced concrete walls will be used in the pump room and
this should overcome problems associated with loose conditions
However some shotcrete may be required in the water develop
