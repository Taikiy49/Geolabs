2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of
lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch The Koolau Volcanic
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials were
deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor The sea
level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 90 meters below and rising about
30 meters above the present sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the
late Pleistocene Epoch eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash which
consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers Subsequent erosion
dissected and thinned those mantles of tuff
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have
added man made fills bringing the site to its present configuration

3 1 Pump Station
We understand that the pump station will extend to depths of about 8 meters
below the existing ground surface Based on the subsurface conditions encountered
and the relatively light loads anticipated for the pump stations we recommend that a
mat foundation be used to support the pump stations An allowable bearing pressure of
up to may be used for design of mat foundations bearing on the
soft to medium hard coralline detritus This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 450 mm below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench As an alternative the footings should extend to a
depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement

The first zone of backfill for pipes extending from the bedding material to at least
300 mm above the top of the pipes or 300 mm above the groundwater level
should consist of free draining granular material such as No 3B Fine gravel It is
critical to use free draining granular materials to reduce the potential for formation
of voids below the haunches of the pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and placement could
result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The trench backfill from 300 mm above the top of the pipes or 300 mm above the
groundwater level or above the concrete jacket to the finished subgrade may
consist of the excavated on site soils having particles less than 150 mm in
maximum dimension or select granular material The backfill should be placed in
thin lifts normally 200 mm in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation

3 8 Horizontal Directional Drilling Methods
Two portions of the utility alignment was considered for installation by
horizontal directional drilling HDD The alignment crossing O Malley Boulevard and the
alignment crossing Vandenburg Boulevard near the intersection with Freedom Avenue
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at these locations we believe that
horizontal directional drilling is feasible for the installation of the electrical and
communication line conduits
Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using a
multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface The
pilot hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical
cutting or a combination of the two methods The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to
accommodate the pipe to be installed The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by
the drilling machine Most HDD systems use a slurry to stabilize the walls of the
W O 5020 00 B GEOLABS INC Page 23
Hawaii California

borehole and to reduce the frictional drag on the pipeline being installed Steering
corrections are made in the soils by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired
orientation and then advancing the shoe and string without rotation
In general the HDD pilot hole would be drilled nearly horizontal with entry angles
ranging between 12 and 15 degrees measured from horizontal Exit angles must
accommodate the pipe and are typically below 10 degrees measured from horizontal
The drill unit should be positioned at an appropriate setback distance such that the
required depth starting from the entry point can be achieved In general the product
pipe or utility line should be installed in one continuous operation preferably without
pause and should protrude a sufficient distance beyond the entry and exit points to
allow proper termination or connection at a later time Shallow working pits may be
required at each end of the bore to allow placement of the drill pipe and to allow
transition to the conventional pipe trench installation
The vertical and horizontal alignments of the drill hole are monitored from the
surface by a locator which tracks signals from the transmitter contained inside the drill
head housing Other methods of tracking the cutting tool such as in hole steering are
generally recommended to provide more precise line and grade information The
contractor should carefully monitor the drilling operation when approaching property
boundaries and the existing underground utilities or structures to assure proper
clearance Soils containing gravel cobbles and boulders may present difficulties in
steering control due to the effect of possible deflection or skidding around the potential
hard obstructions

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 18 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 18 extending to depths of
about 3 to 6 2 meters below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 18
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram
hammer falling 760 millimeters In addition some samples were obtained from the
drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration
Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 150 mm of an 450 mm drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
core samples to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the coral and
volcanic tuff encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate presented sample depths and on
Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples of the upper clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Five Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are
provided on Plate B 3
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the soils
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate B 4

APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing Constant and
Falling Head tests in Boring and 8 The tests were performed at depths
ranging between 3 and 3 2 meters below the existing ground surface
Constant Head Permeability tests were performed by pumping water into the
borehole until the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient
period of time The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured
Falling Head Permeability tests were performed by pumping water into the test
borings and the drop in water level with respect to time was recorded The results of
the field permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 3
