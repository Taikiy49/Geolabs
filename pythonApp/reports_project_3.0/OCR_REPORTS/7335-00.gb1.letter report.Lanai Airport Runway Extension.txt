In accordance with our fee proposal dated January 7, 2016, this letter report 
presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed for the design 
of the Lanai Airport Runway Extension project on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our site reconnaissance and engineering analyses for the 
proposed project.  These recommendations are intended for the design of the runway 
extension project.  The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
 The project site is located at the Lanai Airport on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii.  We 
understand that the existing runway will be extended an additional 500 feet into what is 
currently the northern safety zone.  We understand that the new runway extension will 
consist of rigid Portland cement concrete pavement. A new runway blast pad is also 
planned.  The runway blast pad will be about 200 feet wide by 200 feet long. 
The area beyond the current airport operations area (AOA) will be graded to create 
a new runway safety area.  The runway safety area will extend about 900 feet from the 
edge of the new runway blast pad. 
We previously conducted geotechnical engineering explorations at the Lanai Airport 
for the General Aviation Apron and for the Runway Safety Area Improvements.  We will 
use the information from these past explorations to determine the anticipated generalized 
subsurface conditions at runway extension site.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge, and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is a 
result of volcano building on the southwest rift zone. The summit of the shield collapsed to 
form a caldera from which a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, 
extends south-southeastward toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea 
cliff indicate that this Manele graben lies along another rift zone.  
The caldera was largely, but not completely filled by lava flows, and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between them is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the south side, 
the floor of Palawai Basin merges with that of the Manele graben, through which the last 
lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. On the 
northeastern side, Palawai Basin is bordered by a steep slope about 492 feet high, beyond 
which lies a nearly level bench of about 0.9 miles wide. The thick, massive character of the 
lava flows in this bench indicates that it is a part of the floor of the filled caldera. Beyond 
the bench, another steep slope rises to Lanaihale Summit (3,316 feet altitude). Southwest 
of the Lanaihale Summit, at an elevation of 1,000 to 2,000 feet, is the Central Plateau of 
Lanai. The soils consist of well-drained, fine-textured and moderately fine-textured soils 
developed in volcanic ash and in material weathered from basic igneous rocks.   
At the northern side of Lanaihale Summit is Maunalei Gulch, one of several pit 
craters filled by talus breccias and later lava flows. So far as is known, all of the lavas of 
Lanai are theolitic basalts. The lava flows range from about 1 foot to 100 feet in thickness 
but average about 20 feet and seldom exceed 50 feet, except where they have been 
ponded in depressions.    
The project site is on the west side of Miki Basin. Most of the soils in the project 
area are residual and saprolite soils, derived from the in-situ weathering of igneous rocks. 
In general, residual and saprolite soils grade to weathered basalt formations with depth.

The new runway extension and blast pad are within the existing AOA.  In general, 
we observed an existing asphaltic concrete pavement along the eastern half of the runway 
extension alignment.  The existing ground surface along the western half of the runway 
extension alignment consisted of brown clayey soils with some grass.   
The area of the proposed new runway safety area is located outside of the AOA.

site is generally level along the proposed runway extension area. The existing ground 
surface elevations along the runway extension area range from +1,307 to +1,315 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The area along the proposed new runway safety area slightly 
slopes upward in the northern direction.  The existing ground surface elevations at the 
proposed new runway safety area ranges from about +1,318 to +1,336 feet MSL. 
ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Based on our site reconnaissance and research and review of available subsurface 
information in the project vicinity, we anticipate that the subsoil conditions at the site will 
consist of stiff to hard residual soils overlying saprolitic soils.  The residual and saprolitic 
soils would generally consist of clayey silt and silty clay. We do not anticipate encountering 
groundwater in the excavations planned for the project. It should be noted that 
groundwater levels may change due to rainfall, seepage, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, and other factors.

As stated in the Advisory Circular, the common engineering practice to determine 
the design CBR value is to use the value that is one standard deviation below the mean. 
Therefore, the design CBR value is 17, which is equivalent to a subgrade resilient modulus 
of about 25,500 pounds per square inch (psi).  For the rigid pavement analyses, the 
design subgrade foundation modulus (k-value) that is equivalent to the design CBR value 
of the on-site silty clay and clayey silt is 214 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection 
(psi/in).   
Aircraft Pavement Design 
The design procedures used in determining the new rigid pavement section 
discussed herein are based on the design guidelines presented in the Advisory Circular 
No. 150/5320-6E “Airport Pavement Design and Evaluation” prepared by the FAA. 
In accordance with the design guidelines provided in the Advisory Circular 
mentioned above, stabilized base and subbase courses are necessary for new pavements 
designed to accommodate jet aircraft weighing 100,000 pounds or more. Since the 
aircrafts used in the pavement analyses weigh less than 100,000 pounds, stabilized 
pavement sections were not considered for the runway extension project.  
The FAA computer program FAARFIELD was used to perform pavement design 
analysis using the FAA elastic layered design approach.  The FAARFIELD pavement 
design program automatically assigned modulus to the PCC (P-501), and this value by 
default cannot be changed.   
Runway Extension 
Due to the potential for encountering subgrade areas consisting of moderately 
expansive soils, we believe that it would be beneficial to use a crushed aggregate 
base course layer (P-209) having a minimum thickness of about 12 inches to 
reduce the potential for structural distress to the concrete pavements from shrinking 
and swelling of the clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations.

Base Materials 
Portland cement concrete (PCC) should meet the requirements of P-501 
(Portland Cement Concrete Pavement), and should have a minimum flexural 
strength of 650 psi. The PCC pavements may be opened to traffic at a minimum 
flexural strength of 550 psi.   
Crushed aggregate base course should meet the requirements of P-209 
(Crushed Aggregate Base Course).  The crushed aggregate base course should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in thin lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to no less than 100 percent 
relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site to reduce 
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. Soft and yielding areas 
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill 
should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils 
should be disposed properly off-site. Contract documents should include additive 
and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and engineered fill placement to 
account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
Pavements to be demolished should be completely removed. Over-excavations 
resulting from the demolition operations should be backfilled with approved on-site 
fill material. Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and 
the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with the excavated on-site 
materials. The on-site materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in 6 to 8-inch level loose 
lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction outside of the

Fill and Backfill Materials 
The fill and backfill materials may consist of the excavated on-site soils, provided 
that they are screened of vegetation, deleterious materials and particles greater 
than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported general fill materials should consist 
of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension. The 
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or 
higher and a swell potential of no more than 1 percent when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements  
Fills and backfills outside of the aircraft pavement area should 
be 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to no 
less than 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of the soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory 
rollers, multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable 
compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting or ponding should not be allowed 
when compacting the fill material.

clay soils may be cut at an inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or

Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be 
accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching 
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and 
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
erosion of the finished slopes.

Laboratory Test Data 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. Graphic presentations of the test results 
are provided on Plate A-1. 
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate 
the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures. The tests were run on 
remolded samples. The test results are presented on Plate A-2. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) was performed bulk soil 
samples to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry density of 
the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plates A-3 through A-6. 
Four laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) was 
performed on bulk soil samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates A-7 through A-10.
