SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Replace Paint Booth at USCG Integrated Support
Command project at United States Coast Guard Integrated Support Command facility
along Sand Island Parkway Road in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are only intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade and site grading The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project involves the construction of a new paint booth adjacent to the new
welding shop We understand that the new paint booth will be sized for the largest buoy
and will be serviced by ISC Honolulu The buoy is 9 feet in diameter and 35 feet in
length Based on the available information the paint booth will be a concrete structure
with concrete slab and foundation Based on the grading plan minimum site grading
with cut and fill on the order of less than 1 foot will be required
1 3 Purpose and Scope

conditions to develop an idealized soil data set to formulate geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the project design Our work was performed in general
accordance with our revised fee proposal dated March 19 2007 Our scope of work
included the following tasks and work efforts

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site supporting the
proposed building on a shallow foundation system consisting of continuous footings
may result in settlements of about 1 5 to 3 inches If these settlements are not tolerable
we believe that the new building may be supported on the shallow strip and or
continuous footings provided that the underlying very soft lagoonal deposits are
removed The resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
materials As an alternative we recommend supporting the load bearing footings with a
micropile foundation system bearing on the dense silty sand and gravel
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections herein
3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the field exploration we encountered a 3 to 3 5 foot thick layer of very
soft lagoonal deposits in our borings The top of the lagoonal deposit layer was about
4 5 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface corresponding to an elevation of
approximately 3 feet MSL Based on our laboratory test results and engineering
analyses supporting the new building on shallow spread and or continuous footings
may result in a settlement of about 1 5 to 3 inches Two foundation design options are
provided below for the building foundation design
Shallow Foundations with Over Excavation
The footing bearing layer dense coralline silty sand is underlain by a very soft
lagoonal deposit at shallow depths We envision that this deposit will compress
and cause settlement of the building footings Based on the laboratory test
results and engineering analyses the new building may settle about 1 5 to
3 inches when subject to loads of 2 5 and 6 5 kips per foot respectively
Based on the available plans the new building will be constructed with concrete
walls and slabs We believe that the anticipated footing settlements may cause
cracking of the concrete walls and slabs of the new building Therefore we

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on dense coralline

This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted Unless

soil should be neglected
A Geolabs representative should monitor footing excavations prior to placing

Micropile Foundations
An alternative to reduce the footing settlement is to support the wall footings by a
micropile foundation system In general the micropile foundation system consists
of a small diameter usually less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with
steel reinforcing The micropile foundation is typically constructed by drilling a
hole with or without casing placing reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting
the hole Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in
access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground
conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal
disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment
We believe that a micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of 6 inches may be
used to support the structural footing loads An allowable compressive load
capacity of 30 kips per micropile may be used for an embedment depth of
25 feet The allowable compressive load capacity for the piles is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads
such as wind or seismic forces
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It should be noted that the micropile system provides low lateral load resistance
due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the lateral load
resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected unless the
micropiles are battered at an angle to take advantage of the lateral load
component of the micropile Lateral loads imposed on the building structure may
be resisted primarily by passive pressure resistance acting against the vertical
faces of the footings and or grade beams and by shear acting along the sides of
the footings and grade beams if applicable Passive pressure resistance against
the vertical faces of the footings and or grade beams may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
above the groundwater level Additional resistance to lateral loads may be
provided by shear resistance between the sides of the footings and or grade
beams and the adjacent soils A side shear resistance of up to 150 psf may be
used for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the pile supported foundations to be less than 0 5 inches with
differential settlements between footings supported on micropiles not exceeding
about one half of the total settlement We believe that these settlements are
essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
A specialty subcontractor experienced in the construction of a micropile
foundation system should install the micropiles Due to the specialized nature of
the micropile foundation construction observation of the micropile foundation
installation system and testing of the micropiles should be designated a Special
Inspection item Therefore monitoring of the micropile installation operations by
Geolabs Special Inspector is necessary to confirm our design assumptions and
should be designated a Special Inspection item in accordance with Section
1701 of the Uniform Building Code 1997

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new building We
anticipate that the proposed building will be situated on dense coralline silty sand
A cushion fill consisting of a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3 Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be placed below the slabs The open graded
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a
capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the No 3 Fine gravel cushion fill layer Flexible floor coverings
such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor
slab cracking
We envision that the concrete floor slab for the structure will be subjected to
forklift and equipment operation Therefore we recommend providing a 6 inch thick
layer of aggregate subbase course below the slab The aggregate subbase course
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The 6 inch thick
layer of aggregate subbase course would serve to provide uniform support of the slab
subjected to forklift and equipment vibrations
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the slab with the on site soil and graded to divert
water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious materials asphaltic
concrete and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly
off site Soft and or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas

resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fills The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be disposed properly off site and or used in landscape areas
where appropriate Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices
for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
Where soils are exposed in areas to receive fill or finished subgrades in cut areas
these areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction we
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above Saturation and

3 5 Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised to install
gutter systems around the building to divert the discharge away from the foundation
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to
reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be backfilled properly against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from foundations and slabs

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 25 3 and 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 We drilled the borings using a truck mounted drill rig and a portable drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test result is provided on Plate B 1
One Sieve Analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 2
One consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on a sample
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered The consolidation test result is presented on Plate B 3
