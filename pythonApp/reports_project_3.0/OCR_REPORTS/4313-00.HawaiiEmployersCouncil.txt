INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Building Renovation project for the Hawaii Employers Council in the District
of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project The recommendations presented are intended for the design of
foundations site preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials The project site is situated in an area where the

subsurface soil deposits are dominated by calcareous reef deposits Land development and
reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project area to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously indicated our field exploration generally encountered 3 to 5 feet of fill
materials consisting of loose to medium dense clayey gravel with some sand overlying
coralline deposits The coralline deposits generally consisted of silty or clayey gravel coral
detritus formation and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 26 5 feet below
the existing ground surface Groundwater was encountered in the borings at about 6 5 to
10 feet below the ground surface at the time of our exploration
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for
support of the new addition planned at the site Due to the presence of loose surface fill soils
at the site we recommend that a 2 foot thick layer of compacted fill material be provided
below the bottom of footings for uniform support The 2 foot thick compacted fill may consist
of the over excavated fill material and should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction
It should be noted that the surface fill soils encountered in the borings were generally
loose to medium dense in nature Therefore we recommend that the finished subgrades
GEOLABS INC

below the pavement structural section be scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches
moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements presented in the
Site Preparation section of this report to reduce the potential for future distress to the
pavements Detailed discussions and specific design recommendations pertaining to these

Foundations
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for
support of the new addition planned at the site Due to the presence of loose to medium
dense fill soils at the site we recommend that the shallow foundations for the new addition
be supported on a 2 foot thick layer of compacted fill to provide uniform support for the
footings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of foundations bearing on the minimum 2 foot thick compacted fill This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade In order to provide the 2 foot thick compacted fill below the footings the
footing locations will need to be over excavated to the required depth and backfilled with
compacted fill The over excavated materials may be re used as a source of the compacted
fill material provided that the materials are less than 3 inches in maximum size and are free
of deleterious materials In addition the 2 foot thick compacted fill should extend at least
1 foot beyond the edge of the footing Care should be exercised when over excavating
below the footings for the new CMU wall footing located adjacent to the existing building to
avoid undermining the existing building foundation Therefore it may be necessary to
excavate and backfill the 2 foot thick compacted fill below the footings in increments of 10
foot long sections
GEOLABS INC
W O 4313 00 Page 8
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below an imaginary 45 degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary
to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
For footings bearing on the compacted fills the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement
of reinforcing steel and concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the over excavated
footing excavations the soft and or loose materials should be removed to expose the
underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation may be backfilled with the
excavated on site materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of footings to be less than 1 inch
Differential settlements between adjacent footings should be on the order of about
0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on 2 foot thick compacted fill Resistance
due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4313 00 Page 9
footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive pressure
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
Observation and testing of the over excavation below the footings should be
designated as a Special Inspection item Therefore we recommend that footing
excavations be observed by a representative of Geolabs Inc prior to the placement of
reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the competency of the foundation bearing materials
and the required embedment depths
Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated at the site At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish asphalt concrete
pavements and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and
disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or for future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities Existing
underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials the fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
After clearing and demolition areas beneath the future pavement areas should be
scarified to a depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
GEOLABS INC
W O 4313 00 Page 10
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In addition the
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until placement of the pavement structural
section or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommend above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent
or less Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Site preparation should be cbserved by a representative of Geolabs It is important
that a representative from our office observe the site preparation operations to evaluate
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification
process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our
field exploration
Pavement Design
We understand that the existing asphalt concrete paved parking lots will be
demolished and reconstructed In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for this

project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks We have made

recompacted on site materials Based on the above assumptions the following pavement
section may be used for preliminary budgeting purposes
Flexible Asphaltic Concrete Pavements
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 percent relative compaction

Based on our field observations and the test borings loose surface fill soils consisting
of clayey gravel were encountered up to approximately 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Therefore we recommend that the subgrade soils below the new pavement section
be scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate
base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregates compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above
section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent
to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches
into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential for migration
of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system is
recommended to collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we believe that a subdrain system should be considered adjacent to the
paved landscaped areas

Utility Trench
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for the proposed project
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such
as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial
trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around
the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where
trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new addition should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundations and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
pavement areas should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the
subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
GEOLABS INC

subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
foundations and pavement areas
