landslides adjacent to two roadways on the Island of Tutuila American

Regional Geology
The Territory of American Samoa is an island archipelago located
approximately 2 300 miles toward the south of Honolulu Hawaii at about
14 degrees latitude south of the equator The total land area of American
Samoa consists of about 76 square miles and includes five volcanic islands
Tutuila Aunu u Ofu Olosega and Ta u and two remote atolls Rose and
Swains The Samoan archipelago was formed by submarine volcanic
activity that built up to form the volcanic mountains which emerged from the
ocean about 1 5 million years ago during the late
The Island of Tutuila is the largest island of the archipelago with a land area
of about 53 square miles and a maximum elevation of about 3 000 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL The Island of Tutuila consists of an elongated
east west trending island shape with a prominent sharp crested mountain
ridge spine spanning the length of the island The primary mountain slopes
are very steep with limited flatter terrain encompassing the island with the
exception of the coastal regions and the vicinity of the lava terrace identified
as the Tafuna Leone Plain

The Island of Tutuila was formed by the coalescence of five volcanoes
situated on two separate submarine volcanic rift zones The bulk of the
Island of Tutuila is composed of andesitic and basaltic lava flows with
volcanic tuff and cinder deposits Other volcanic rocks make up a small
fraction of the geology and coral reef and coralline sand deposits contribute
to the coastal lagoons and shoreline areas
The American Samoa island archipelago formed as an elongated island
chain that grew as the Pacific Plate moved westward in relation to a nearly
stationary hot spot of thermal activity located deep in the earth s mantle
As the island archipelago moves westward on the Pacific Plate it interacts
with the adjacent Australian Plate at a junction located approximately
100 miles toward the south of American Samoa This proximal plate
interaction is believed to be responsible for some earthquakes and renewed
volcanic activity in the area
Climate
The climate of American Samoa is generally classified as tropical and is
affected by the close proximity of the inter tropical convergence zone ITCZ
which is characterized by weaker wind fields higher air temperatures and
increased humidity Rainfall averages about 125 inches per year at the
lower elevations increasing to as much as about 300 inches per year at the
inland mountain summit regions Thunderstorm activity is commonly
accompanied by briefly heavy rainfall rates with sudden and intense runoff
generated from the steep mountain slopes The island may be affected by
hurricanes and other tropical weather systems which typically approach
from the north
The average annual air temperature is near 80 degrees Fahrenheit with
maximum temperatures recorded in the lower 90s and minimum
temperatures near 60 degrees Fahrenheit The island is densely vegetated
with tropical flora with many perennial streams flowing from the typically
small and steep drainage basins located within the interior sections of the
island
Storm Event
An extreme rainfall event which occurred on May 18 and 19 reportedly
produced the heaviest rainfall in nearly 20 years Rain gauges in Pago
Pago recorded about 10 7 inches of rain on May 19 that caused landslides
that killed four people and prompted a state of emergency declaration from
Governor Togiola Tulafono Associated Press

Description of Landslides
Numerous landslides occurred throughout the island and caused
widespread damage due to the heavy and intense rainfall The two
landslides that have affected roadways on the Island of Tutuila American
Samoa are located in the Fagatogo area herein referred to as the Fagatogo
Landslide and along Fagasa Road Route 005 herein referred to as the
Fagasa Landslide
In general the earth materials composing the slopes encompassed by the
landslides consist of weathered andesitic and basaltic lava rock and welded
pyroclastic deposits These materials are collectively referred to as volcanic
rock in the following discussion In addition colluvial deposits consisting of
rock fragments ranging from gravel to boulder size mixed with sandy and
clayey soils were observed as near surface deposits on the slopes
In general the Fagatogo Landslide is located adjacent to the Route 001 and
is located in the center of commercial part of the town The approximate
location of the landslide is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The
Fagotogo Landslide occurred and impacted a restaurant and a business
located at the foot of the hillside resulting in the death of a person
In general the Fagasa Landslide is located on the uphill side of Fagasa
Road Route 005 and is located in the upper reaches of the road near Mafa
Pass refer to the Project Location Map Plate 1 The landslide may be
technically classified as a debris flow landslide The debris generated from
the landslide traveled approximately 1 600 lineal feet down along a relatively
steep drainage gully and emptied into an open reservoir

Debris Flows
Debris flows involve the rapid and destructive transport of fluidized earth
materials downslope from a distinct source region such as the site of a slope
failure or landslide Debris flows are commonly produced by the failure of a
portion of a steep hillslope under intense rainfall that has produced saturated
ground conditions The slope failure may either be shallow and surficial or
deep and involving a large mass of unstable earth material
Debris flows typically produce a distinct scar or track extending from the
landslide initiation zone down the slope toward the deposition zone which is
usually located at the base of the slope or along flatter slope gradients
where the debris flow energy may dissipate
Various types of slope failures may transform into debris flows when the
initial fallen earth material scours the ground surface in its path thereby
entraining additional source debris The debris flow may grow considerably
larger if additional unconsolidated deposits such as colluvium topsoil and
vegetation are entrained Debris flows typically follow the path of
topographic gullies and other drainage features where additional surface
water runoff which adds to the mass of fluidized material may be
encountered
Debris flows may occur within unconsolidated materials such as colluvium
and alluvium deposits defined as having been mobilized and re deposited
by gravitational and surface water flow processes located within drainage
basins and mantling hillside areas Debris flows may also stem from the
failure of steep slopes involving in situ weakened rock and residual soil
materials Debris flows are more commonly formed when slope failures are

Basically three general stabilization methods may be applicable to mitigate
observed debris flows The first method involves the stabilization of
potentially unstable hillslopes and the associated deposits that may be
susceptible to debris flow initiation The stabilization method involves the
construction of engineered reinforcement or surface drainage alteration of
the hillside encompassing the potential debris flow deposits There are
many possible alternatives for slope stabilization which depend on the type
of material soils or rock involved and other factors such as topography site
construction access and area coverage
The second stabilization method involves the removal of the potentially
hazardous deposits from the slope The potentially unstable materials may
be completely excavated and removed to clear the potential source
materials or the slope may be cut and re sloped to achieve a flatter slope
inclination that is less susceptible to debris flow initiation The alternative
may involve large quantities of earthwork which may not be feasible for
some large sites and physical settings All vegetation and trees growing on
the affected slopes would need to be removed prior to the earthwork
Therefore re vegetation of the slopes may be necessary to reduce potential
surface soil erosion following the construction activity
The third stabilization method involves the construction of a debris flow
protection system designed to control the movement and deposition of
debris flow activity Protection systems are used to mitigate against the
effects of debris flows where the potentially unstable deposits and hillsides
cannot be stabilized due to cost or other reasons
An example of a debris flow protection system is a flexible barrier such as a
reinforced cable net which is typically constructed to span potential debris
flow tracks and run out chutes Another form of protection involves the
construction of a basin or other physical feature such as a berm to reduce
the velocity of the debris flow and collect the debris in a designated area

As for the Fagatogo Landslide we believe that a solution to mitigate the
landslide is to cut back the upper portion of the slope to a flatter and more
stable slope inclination along with some drainage improvements and erosion
control measures Because we understand that land acquisition could be
accomplished to accommodate the flatter slope inclination we generally
recommend the implementation of a flatter slope inclination as the
permanent mitigation measure for the Fagatogo Landslide
As part of the permanent mitigation measure we recommend that drainage
improvements be made to control and direct the surface and subsurface
drainage at the top and bottom of the landslide scar In order to promote
better surface and subsurface drainage at the bottom of the landslide scar
we recommend that a free draining retaining structure such as concrete crib
wall be considered at the bottom of the landslide scar or new slope Details
of the permanent mitigation measure should be included in the detailed
design phase of the project A sketch of our recommendations is provided
on the Conceptual Mitigation Measure Fagatogo Landslide Plate 6
As for the Fagasa Landslide the physical constraints at the site and the
steep slopes above the landslide scar make stabilization methods consisting
of retaining structures and or earthwork construction impractical Therefore
we believe that construction of a debris flow protection system such as a
flexible barrier along with small debris catchment area may be used at the
Fagasa Landslide to reduce the debris flow hazard at the site It should be
emphasized that this recommendation is not intended to eliminate any
debris flow movement but the mitigation measure should significantly reduce
the risk and impact by future debris flows occurring upslope of the existing
landslide scar It should be noted that occasional maintenance to remove
accumulated debris behind the flexible barrier likely would be required
In addition we also recommend that drainage improvements be made to the
drainage chute on the upslope side of the roadway along with debris
barriers Based on our field observations we believe that it may be
appropriate to enlarge the drain inlet structures and the two pipe culverts
crossing the roadway in this area to accommodate larger surface water
runoffs and the possibility of clogging of the drain inlet structures
As part of the permanent mitigation measures we also recommend that
improvements be made to the drainage gully to accommodate future debris
flows along this path to reduce the potential for impacting the private
dwellings located adjacent to the drainage gully

Additional Services
This technical memorandum is prepared for planning purposes only It is not
intended for use in final design and or preparation of construction
documents for this project Additional geotechnical engineering consultation
and reconnaissance of the project sites should be conducted for final design
We recognize the difficulty in performing geotechnical investigations at the
two landslide sites due to the inaccessibility of the sites and the lack of
equipment and firms capable of performing geotechnical investigations in
American Samoa Considering the relatively good exposed faces of the
landslide scar which afforded us the opportunity to evaluate the subsurface
conditions of the slopes we believe that geotechnical investigations of the
Fagatogo and Fagasa Landslides may be omitted However the
client owner should be aware that there would be some additional
geotechnical risks to the project designed and constructed based on field
reconnaissance only Therefore we recommend that a larger contingency
fund be established to account for the unforeseen and or unknown
subsurface conditions at the project sites
Considering the complex nature of the subsurface conditions and the lack of
detailed subsurface information we recommend that Geolabs be retained to
provide follow up geotechnical services during the design and also during
the construction of this project

REMARKS As discussed in our telephone conference on while we were
in American Samoa we are providing a list of immediate works that we
recommend be implemented as soon as possible ahead of the permanent
repair of the two landslides we have evaluated on the Island of Tutuila
American Samoa
Immediate Works for Fagatogo Landslide
Based on our evaluation of the existing conditions of the landslide scar we
believe that some amount of immediate works should be undertaken to
prolong the temporary stability of the landslide scar until the permanent fix
for the landslide may be implemented The immediate works that we
recommend generally consist of cutting back of the top of the slope which is
overhanging and consist of the weaker geologic materials
Remove or cut back the top 15 to 20 feet vertical height of the slope
to a 1H 1V slope inclination to increase slope stability The top of the
existing slope is comprised of weak soil materials and extremely
weathered rock and is slightly overhanging
Please note that cutting back of the top of slope will require providing
access to the top of the slope Providing access to the top of slope
will require demolition and removal of an existing dwelling The
existing dwelling has been red tagged or is unsafe
There is a block of soil and geologic material on the upper right hand
side of the landslide scar that needs to be removed This block of
soil weathered rock material has a large open fracture making this
block susceptible to additional movement and falling off Cutting back

Immediate Works for Fagasa Landslide
Based on our evaluation of the existing conditions of the landslide scar we
believe that some amount of immediate works should be undertaken to
prolong the temporary stability of the landslide scar and the roadway
embankment until the permanent fix for the landslide may be implemented
Put up concrete jersey barriers to replace the guard nails that have
been wiped out by the landslide on the makai side of roadway
Clean up debris tree trunks from the face of the embankment slope
on the downslope side of roadway Clean up loose materials from
the exposed face and also create some benches or other means of
erosion control at the base of the embankment slopes temporarily
Extend the culvert temporarily to discharge further down slope to
avoid erosion of embankment that has been compromised As an
alternative gabion baskets filled with rocks may be placed below the
existing pipe culvert outlet to protect the embankment that has been
compromised from further erosion
Create two benches for temporary catchment of debris on the uphill
side of roadway The two benches should be excavated into the
more stable geologic materials exposed at the landslide scar
