SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project site is in an area of relatively high rainfall and very moist soil conditions.
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by soft to medium stiff
silty and clayey alluvium overlying medium dense beach/lagoonal deposits generally
consisting of coralline sand with some coral gravel and cobbles. Groundwater was
encountered at depths of about 2 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface.
The project site encompasses an existing sand borrow pit that was backfilled with
imported stiff/very stiff clayey soils. Soft to very soft surface and near-surface alluvial clay
soils with organic matter were encountered at the far eastern portion of the coastal plain
region and fronting the mouths of the larger drainage valleys and bordering the existing
streams on the coastal plain.
Due to the existing steep slopes above the site, general building setback guidelines
for future residential structures should be established. Additional detailed geotechnical
consultation should be the responsibility of the individual purchasers to obtain
recommendations for the design of foundation systems and the site-specific assessment
of building setbacks and other potential geological and geotechnical constraints and
hazards.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is essentially a dissected basaltic dome that was initially
formed by a single large shield volcano, which has subsequently been deeply eroded
and partially covered with later (younger) volcanic materials. The Island of Kauai is the
oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands and today encompasses approximately 555 square
miles in area with a maximum elevation of about 5,170 feet above Mean Sea Level
(MSL) at Mount Waialeale, which is near the center of the island. It is believed that
during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene Epoch),
basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly circular
island volcano from the ocean floor, which is estimated to have been about 20,000 feet
deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this main shield building phase
came to a halt.
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This
post-erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Series. Because of the long
period of erosion that occurred between the eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series
and the Koloa Volcanic Series, the deposits of the Koloa Volcanic Series typically filled
the erosional valleys and depressions, which existed at that time. Consequently, the
basaltic rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found to overlie thick residual
soil and alluvial deposits.
The dominant geologic processes of weathering, landform erosion, and sediment
re-deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs. Combined with
the erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea-level fluctuations, which
occurred in response to the worldwide advance and retreat of the continental ice sheets,
the Island of Kauai has evolved to its present form.

SECTION 2 — SITE CHARACTERIZATION
The project site is situated on the coastal plain region below the eastern plateau
area of the Princeville development area. This coastal plain region consists of a very
low gradient coastal flat fronting the mouths of three primary valleys that drain from the
plateau above.
Based on a review of available geologic information, the coastal plain region is
generally underlain by soft alluvial sediment and calcareous beach deposits containing
shallow groundwater conditions. The calcareous sand deposits encountered in the
subsurface throughout most of the coastal plain region represent older beach and
shallow lagoon/embayment deposits, which were likely deposited during higher stands
of the sea. These deposits have been covered by a thin veneer of organic rich silty and
clayey terrestrial derived sediment, which likely washed onto the coastal plain from the
adjacent uplands.
2.2 Site Description
The project site is in the Kalihikai area of the District of Hanalei on the Island of
Kauai, Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The project site is to the
northwest of the existing Princeville Airport and is bound by the existing Anini Vista
residential subdivision toward the east, the north facing bluffs to the south, and Kalihikai
Beach toward the north, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The coastal plain region consists of relatively flat lowland terrain bounded by the
coastline toward the north and the steep slopes associated with the uplands toward the
south. A portion of the project site is partly developed with existing residential homes
along Anini Road. In addition, the Kauai Polo Foundation maintains a recreational
facility with associated open fields and fenced paddocks. Some existing Kuleana
properties are at other portions of the lowland project site. Vegetation consists of
various grasses, some shrubbery, and dense tangled groves of Hao tree growth. The
ground surfaces are typically flat ranging between about +7 and +10 feet MSL with only
minor ground surface elevation variations. Because the coastal plain spans the mouths
of several large drainage valleys with only a few established drainage ditches
channeling runoff toward the ocean, the ground surfaces were observed to be

2.3. Subsurface Conditions
The general subsurface conditions were previously explored by drilling and
sampling six test borings (designated as Boring Nos. 6 through 11) extending to depths
of about 21.5 to 35.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, seven test pits
(identified as Test Pit Nos. 101 through 107) were previously excavated and backfilled
along the proposed lowland roadway alignment. Test Pit Nos. 101 through 107 were
excavated to explore the subsurface conditions at existing difficult access and soft
ground locations that could not be reached by conventional truck-mounted drilling
equipment. The approximate locations of the six test borings and seven test pits are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Furthermore, the shallow subsurface conditions and surface limits of suspected
Borrow Pits “A”, “B”’, “C”, and “D” were previously explored by the excavation and
backfilling of exploratory test pits. Twenty-three test pits identified as Test Pit Nos. A1
through A3, B1 through BY, C1 through C8, and D1 through D3 were excavated as
shown on the Borrow Pit Area Site Plan, Plate 3.
Boring Nos. 6 through 11 were drilled at selected locations on the coastal plain
as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Boring Nos. 6 through 11 indicated that the coastal
plain region of the project site was generally underlain by a thin alluvial surface layer
that consisted of soft to medium stiff clayey silt with sand and extended to depths of
about 1 to 1.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Below the alluvial surface layer,
beach and lagoonal deposits that consisted of medium dense calcareous sand which
contained some silt, coral gravel, and coral cobbles were encountered and extended to
depths of about 18 to 25 feet below the ground surface. Occasional zones of dense
cemented sand and cemented coral detritus were encountered within the beach and
lagoonal deposits sampled at the project site. Depending on the location, the borings
then encountered silty and sandy alluvial/fluvial deposits with some embedded basalt
rock boulders and extended to the maximum depth explored on the coastal plain of
about 35.5 feet. Groundwater levels were encountered in Boring Nos. 6 through 11 at
depths ranging between about 2.9 to 5.5 feet below the ground surface, which generally
corresponded to elevations of about +3 to +5 feet MSL.

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration, most of the coastal plain region of the project site
is generally underlain by a thin veneer of soft/loose, organic silty and sandy soil
overlying medium dense coralline sand with some silt and coral cobble/gravels
extending to depths of about 18 to 25 feet. Below the coralline materials, generally stiff
clayey and sandy alluvium containing basalt boulders was encountered extending to the
maximum depth explored of about 35.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The
central and far eastern portions of the coastal plain region in the vicinity of proposed Lot
Nos. 3, 6, and 8 appear to be underlain by some thicker and more extensive deposits of
soft organic clays and silts with embedded loose sands and gravels as depicted on the
Site Plan, Plate 2. Other less extensive deposits of soft alluvial silt and clay are
anticipated at locations associated with the meandering stream channels that traverse
the low topographic gradient site. Shallow depth groundwater conditions were
encountered on the coastal plain at depths of about 2 to 4.5 feet below the existing
ground surface, which generally corresponds to elevations of about +3 to +5.5 feet
MSL.

SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We understand that the residential lots will be marketed on an “as-is” basis with
the individual purchaser being responsible for site improvements such as lot grading
and the design of foundations, and assessments for potential geologic hazards, such as
rockfall and slope stability issues. In view of this, we recommend that the individual
purchasers retain the service of a geotechnical engineer to provide individual

3.1. Coastal Plain Drainage
Based on the available conceptual development plan, we understand that a new
subdivision roadway is planned to traverse the coastal plain region. The new roadway
will perpendicularly cross several existing drainage features originating from the inland
valley region. We anticipate that the new roadway may either be constructed on a
raised embankment with associated new drainage culverts or the roadway may be
constructed on existing grades with depressions or ford crossings to allow sheetflow
runoff across the roadway surfaces. In order to evaluate the roadway design, additional
information pertaining to the anticipated flood level on the coastal plain is needed. In
addition, a drainage study should be performed to evaluate the impact of the new
roadway construction on the regional drainage.
3.2 Soft Soils and Potential Settlement
Our field exploration encountered some regions of soft alluvial clay deposits
ranging from about 2 to about 8 feet thick generally fronting the mouths of the drainage
valleys. The approximate location of the regions of soft soils is shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2. Other localized deposits of soft clay soils may be encountered in association
with existing and older abandoned drainage features at the site.
Based on the available conceptual plan, the new roadway planned for the coastal
plain region may traverse these regions of soft soil deposits. Settlement of the native
unconsolidated deposits may be experienced under additional loading induced by the
placement of new fills for the roadway construction. Therefore, we recommend
implementing a program of temporary soil surcharge to induce ground settlement prior

3.3 Existing Sand Borrow Pits
As part of our field exploration, it was desired to evaluate the limits of
four suspected borrow pit excavations on the coastal plain region identified as
Borrow Pits “A”, “B”, “C”, and “D.” The pits were excavated to extract natural sand
deposits that reside in the shallow subsurface. A field exploration consisting of test pits
was conducted to evaluate the limits of the borrow pits. Based on our field exploration,

appear to have been excavated and backfilled as a single continuous pit as shown on

clayey silt that contrasts significantly with the surrounding native sandy soils. The stiff to
very stiff clayey backfill soils appear to have been compacted and covered with a thin
veneer of native on-site soils.
Based on our field exploration, the proposed roadway alignment in the vicinity of
Borrow Pits “B” and “C” should traverse mainly native ground conditions (thin alluvial
clayey soil overlying coralline sand deposits) since the limits of the borrow pit
excavation and backfill reside just toward the north of the proposed road alignment.
Furthermore, we believe that the stiff to very stiff condition of the clayey silt backfill is
suitable for development without special consideration.

3.6 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we anticipate
that shallow spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support new structures.
As a general guide, an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square
foot (psf) may be used for the design of footings bearing on compacted fill or stiff on-site
clayey silt and/or silty clay materials. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent exterior grade. This increased embedment depth is recommended due
to the proximity of the site to the ocean and the potential for inundation and scour
resulting from storm surges or tsunami runup.
Foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, or easements should be
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge
of the utility trench, or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
appreciable foundation settlement.
In general, the subgrade soils at the bottom of footing excavations should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of

Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight)
corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations

top of the gravel. The gravel should be placed in the excavation and densified by
mechanical tamping. A layer of geotextile should then be placed over the gravel surface
and the fabric from the sides brought over to overlap.

3.7 Slabs-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the proposed project.
The near-surface soils generally consist of coralline sands and silty clays. The silty
clays exhibit a slight expansion potential when subject to moisture fluctuations.
Therefore, to reduce the potential for structural distress to the lightly loaded slabs, we
recommend providing a minimum of 6 inches of non-expansive select granular fill
material below the concrete slab.
The subgrades for concrete slabs-on-grades should be scarified to a depth of at
least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture,
and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum
dry density.
The non-expansive select granular fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral, basaltic gravel or cinder
sand. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger
than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less
when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. The material should also contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The select granular fill
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The slab subgrade should be
kept moist until covered by the select granular fill.
For the interior building slabs (which will not be subjected to vehicular traffic), we
recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill over the 6-inch select
granular fill materials for uniform support. The cushion fill should consist of open-graded

3.8 Retaining Structures
Some retaining structures, such as walls for roadway support and drainage
structure headwalls, may be required for construction. Therefore, the following general
guidelines are provided and may be used for the design of low retaining structures.
3.8.1 Retaining Structure Foundations
In general, we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the
"Foundations" section herein. However, the retaining structure footings should have
a minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, wall foundations on relatively flat areas
should be embedded a minimum depth of 30 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade for retaining structures of 5 feet in height or greater. A footing
embedment depth of 24 inches may be used for low retaining structures less than
5 feet high. The increased embedment depths are recommended due to the
proximity of the site to the ocean and the potential for inundation and scour

For sloping ground conditions, the footing should be extended deeper to obtain a
minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of
the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of
the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

APPENDIX A
Test Boring Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos. 6 through 11, advanced to depths
ranging from approximately 21.5 to 35.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight and
hollow-stem auger tools. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition,
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Test Pit Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at portions of the project site were explored by
excavating and backfilling 30 test pits designated as Test Pit Nos. TP-101 through
TP-107 and TP-A1 through TP-D3. The test pits were excavated to depths ranging from
approximately 3 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The test pits were
excavated using a Caterpillar 307 excavator. The approximate test pit locations are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2 and the Borrow Pit Area Site Plan, Plate 3.
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the excavation operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Descriptions of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits, Plates B-1 through B-15.
Selected “disturbed” soil samples were collected from the test pits and
transported to our laboratory for classification and testing of engineering properties.

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
and the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths.
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 and C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification.
The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 117 and C
136. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plate C-1.
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C-2.
A One-Inch Ring Swell test was performed on a selected sample to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near-surface soils. One Organic Matter test (ASTM D 2974)
was performed on a selected sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the organic
material content of the soil and to aid in the soil classification. The test results are
summarized on Plate C-3.
One Modified Proctor Compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on a
selected bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture
content relationship. The test result is presented on Plate C-4.
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils. The sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture content. The test
results are presented on Plate C-5.
