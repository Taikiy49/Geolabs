SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project involves converting the existing large capacity cesspools to seepage
pits for effluent disposal from new septic tank systems to be installed at various facilities in
Hawaii Volcanoes National Park This project is being undertaken to comply with new
wastewater regulations that prohibit the use of large capacity cesspools to dispose of
untreated sanitary wastes Our services provided information on the disposal capabilities
of the existing cesspools through conducting short term injection testing
The project involved a total of 24 cesspools at various facilities in the park It was
understood that the cesspools would be located and marked in the field for our testing and
made accessible by others for our inspection and testing
Injection testing was conducted by placing water into the cesspools using a water
truck As such the tests were short term injection tests However all conducted tests
exceeded the estimated daily usage of the cesspools tested i e the amount of water
placed into the cesspool during the test was more than the estimated average daily rate of
wastewater generation for the facility being served by the cesspool
Due to logistical and support problems at the project site the complete scope of
work could not be performed Of the 24 cesspools scheduled for testing twelve could not
be located one was located but was inaccessible for testing and two have failed
i e were full of sanitary waste A total of seven cesspools were tested The remaining
two cesspools were located and made ready for testing but could not be tested prior
project termination
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for design

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to perform injection
testing in the existing cesspools to confirm the effluent disposal capacity To
accomplish this we proposed a program consisting of the following tasks and work
efforts
1
Geologic logging of the visible portion of 24 existing cesspool sidewalls
after removal of the covers by an engineer geologist from our firm
2
Injection testing of approximately 24 cesspools in general accordance with
the Hawaii State Department of Health DOH requirements for injection
testing of drywells A water truck was used as a source of water for the
tests
3
Provide the necessary flow meter pipes and water level sounding device
to perform the injection testing
4
Analyses of the field data for the preparation of geotechnical findings
pertaining to the existing cesspools
5
Preparation of six copies of a report summarizing our work and presenting
our findings
6
Coordination of our work by an engineer geologist from our firm
7
Overall quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation
by a principal engineer from our firm
8
Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
Due to logistical and support problems at the project site the complete scope of
work could not be performed Of the 24 cesspools scheduled for testing twelve could
not be located one was located but was inaccessible for testing and two have failed
i e were full of sanitary waste A total of seven cesspools were tested The
remaining two cesspools were located and made ready for testing but could not be
tested prior to project termination

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five
shield volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated near the summit of Kilauea which has formed the
southeast portion of the island The project site appears to be underlain by basaltic lava
flows of the Puna Volcanic Series of Kilauea which were deposited during the
Pleistocene and Holocene Recent Epochs Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The lava formation at the site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe basalt
type flows A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow
surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the
lava flow core Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lavas are still in a
molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are generally
smaller in horizontal extent

Subsurface Conditions
Observations of the interiors of the unlined cesspools generally indicated that the
subsurface formation consists of interbedded layers of pahoehoe and a a basalt
Some voids were observed in the exposed rock formation These voids
appeared to be small lava tubes Lava tubes and cavities are commonly encountered in
basalt formations
Groundwater in the region of the site is anticipated to occur near sea level
However higher level dike impounded groundwater may be present in portions of the
site underlain by Kilauea s rift zones

Inspection and Injection Testing
The cesspools that were located and accessible for testing were first visually
inspected in an attempt to obtain a geological log of the formations penetrated by the
individual cesspool These cesspools were then sounded to determine the depth to the
bottom and the depth to any fluid in the cesspool Following this inspection testing was
conducted by placing approximately 3 000 gallons of clean fresh water into the
cesspools from a water truck and recording the time required for the water to dissipate
i e a falling head infiltration test Since the actual geometry of the wells were not
available and the actual characteristics of the receiving aquifer were not known the
performance test data was not subjected to rigorous analyses to estimate hydraulic
conductivities or maximum capacities The test data was reviewed on a subjective
basis to estimate the performance of the cesspools The following sections provide
discussions on the individual inspections and tests conducted
Cesspool No 2 Visitors Center
At the time of our field inspection this cesspool was filled to the top with septic
waste and could not be tested Based on discussions with National Park Service
NPS personnel this cesspool must be pumped out on a regular basis e g every
one to two months to prevent overflows It is our opinion that this cesspool is in
failure mode and should be replaced with a new seepage pit or leachfield
