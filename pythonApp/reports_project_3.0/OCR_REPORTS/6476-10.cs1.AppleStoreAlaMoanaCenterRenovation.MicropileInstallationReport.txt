Dear Mr. Oster: 
As requested, Geolabs, Inc. provided geotechnical engineering observation and 
consultation services during the micropile foundation installation for the above project. 
The following letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering observations 
during installation of the micropile foundations for the Apple Store Ala Moana Center 
Renovation project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Geolabs conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration for the renovation 
project, and our recommendations are presented in the report entitled “Geotechnical 
Engineering Report, Apple Store Ala Moana Center Renovation, Honolulu, Oahu, 
Hawaii”, dated June 16, 2011. The purpose of our geotechnical engineering observation 
during construction was to provide engineering consultation during the construction of 
the micropile foundation and to provide “Special Inspection” services by observing the 
micropile foundation installation at the renovation project for general conformance with 
the project plans and specifications. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Apple Store Ala Moana Center Renovation requires removal of three interior 
columns in the store, which will result in increased loading on the six surrounding columns. 
The existing columns extend from the mall level to ground level parking and are 
supported on 16.5-inch octagonal or 12-inch square precast and prestressed concrete 
piles in three or four pile group configurations. 
Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer, we 
understand that the original design column load is about 600 kips. Additional structural 
loads from the removed columns will be redistributed onto adjacent columns. Based on 
the structural evaluation, the unfactored column loads including dead, live and seismic 
loading on each column will increase by about 100 kips.

Due to highly variable subsurface conditions and lack of existing pile foundation 
installation records, two micropiles were designed and installed at each adjacent 
column to support the additional column loads generated by the interior column 
removal.  Therefore, the existing concrete piles under the existing columns would not be 
over-stressed due to increased column loads. 
The scope of our work discussed in this letter report includes observation of the 
micropile foundation construction for the Apple Store Ala Moana Center Renovation 
project only.  The micropile contractor for the project is Kelikai, Inc. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site consist of about 2 to 8 feet of 
surface fill and about 12 to 20 feet of loose/soft lagoonal deposit overlying a coral ledge 
(coral/limestone formation) and coralline deposit extending to a depth of approximately 
70 feet below the existing ground surface. 
It should be noted that the coral ledge encountered at the site is highly variable in 
thickness and in hardness.  The coral ledge was encountered within depths ranging from 
about 22 to 33 feet below the existing ground surface with thickness varying from 3 to 
32 feet.  Based on our past experience in the vicinity, the coral ledges are very erratic and 
usually not continuous. 
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 2 feet below the ground 
surface at the time of our field exploration. The groundwater levels generally correspond 
to about Elevations +0.0 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

Geotechnical recommendations for the micropile foundations were presented in

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the project vicinity, the 
micropiles were specified to extend to a minimum depth of about 55 feet below bottom 
of the pile cap with a 35-foot load bearing grout bulb developed within the 
coral/limestone and coralline materials and a 20-foot grout zone (neglected) within the 
lagoonal deposits. To reduce risk of micropile “necking” within the loose/soft lagoonal 
material, 20 feet of permanent steel casing was specified to be left in-place at the top of 
each micropile. The design micropile allowable compressive load capacity was specified 
to be 50 kips. 
In general, the micropile was drilled using TEI TD75 rig equipped with down-the-
hole hammer and advanced with 5.5-inch Outside Diameter (O.D.) temporary steel

casing to the specified depth. Advancing steel casing to the bottom of the specified 
depth was required in order to facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of 
the grouting. Air pressure up to 100 pounds per square inch (psi) was utilized during 
drilling operation to create a bulb around the drilling bit (4-inch diameter) for advancing 
temporary steel casing (5.5-inch O.D.). 
After the drilled holes were extended to the specified depth and cleaned with the 
air pressure, a reinforcing bar consisting of Grade 75 all threaded #10 rebar was placed 
in the cased hole with centralizers. Grout was batched on site utilizing a colloidal high-
shear and high-speed ChemGrout CG-620 mixer equipped with a grout pump. The 
grout mix consisted of one sack of 94-pound Portland Cement Type I and/or II, 
87.4 pounds of masonry sand and 5 gallons of water with design 28-day compressive 
strength of 4,000 psi. 
The grout was placed in the cased hole through a 1-inch PVC tremie pipe from 
the bottom of the cased hole. After sound grout appeared at the top of the cased hole, 
the temporary steel casing was removed one section (4-foot long) at a time until the 
bottom of the steel casing approached the specified top of the grout bulb zone. 
As part of the construction requirements and subsequent field observations, one 
static load test was conducted on selected micropile to verify the design perimeters and 
evaluate the adequacy of the foundation support system. 
Production Micropiles 
Kelikai, Inc. installed a total of 12 micropiles from September 15 through 29, 
2011. The approximate locations of the 12 micropiles installed are shown on the 
Micropile Foundation Plan, Plate 2.  The micropiles generally extend to a depth of about 
57 feet below the existing ground surface, equivalent to an Elevation of about -55 feet 
MSL. Details of the micropile installation are presented in Micropile Installation Records 
in Appendix. 
To facilitate drilling equipment in the low-headroom space, a working pit was 
excavated at each column location to approximately 2 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The pit was also used as a detention basin to contain drilling water and cutting. 
The micropiles were installed with about 36 feet of grout bulb and about 15 feet of neglect 
grout zone (below the design pile cap) within the permanent steel casing. 
It should be noted that the permanent steel casing, about 20-foot long, was left in 
the ground with approximately 1-foot above the bottom of the pit (Elevation about +1 feet 
MSL). Because the design pile cap will be constructed at about 6 feet below the existing 
ground surface (Elevation about -4 feet MSL), about 5 feet of the permanent steel casing 
will be removed during footing construction. Therefore, as-built permanent steel casing 
below the pile cap will be about 15 feet long, about 5 feet less than the contract 
requirements.

Positive grout in-take was maintained during the grouting operation. No significant 
micropile “necking” was indicated based on review of micropile grouting records. Total of 
actual grout volume placed in each micropile ranged from 170 to 290 percent of the 
theoretic grout volume. 
Micropile Testing 
In order to evaluate the load carrying capacities of the installed micropiles, a 
pullout test (proof test) was conducted on a selected micropile (No. P-3) on 
September 30, 2011. In general, the micropile was loaded to a maximum test load of 
100 kips (200 percent of the design load of 50 kips) in approximately 5-kip load 
increments. The test micropile was held at the design load of 50 kips for 10 minutes and 
the maximum test load of 100 kips for 60 minutes to evaluate the creep effect. The 
results of testing are presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.4 - Micropile Load Test Data 
Sheets. The testing load versus micropile displacement is presented on Plate 4, while 
the creep movement is presented on Plate 5. 
The micropile P-3 was loaded gradually to the design load of 50 kips and the 
maximum test load of 100 kips with about 0.132 and 0.32 inches of vertical 
displacement, respectively. Subsequently, the micropile P-3 was unloaded to zero load 
in about 25 kips decrement and reloaded/unload in four increments/decrements, to 
further evaluate micropile capacity. 
Based on our review and engineering evaluation, the micropile P-3 did not fail at 
the maximum test load of 100 kips. At the maximum test load of 100 kips, the creep 
movement between 6 and 60 minutes holding period was about 0.006 inches, less than 
0.08 inches typically allowed for the ground anchor system. 
CONCLUSIONS 
Based on our field observations and the proof test conducted, it is our 
professional opinion that the specified micropiles for the project have been installed to 
the required diameter and sufficient depth providing the design load capacity of 50 kips 
per micropile with a factor of safety of 2.0.
