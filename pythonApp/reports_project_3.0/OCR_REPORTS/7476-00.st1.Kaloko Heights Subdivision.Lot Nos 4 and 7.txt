SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered interbedded layers of dense basalt formation and 
a’a clinker. In general, the basalt formation is medium hard to hard and slightly to moderately 
weathered. A’a clinker generally consists of loose to medium dense gravel and cobbles. It 
should be noted that many voids and blisters were observed at the ground surface.  In 
addition, many voids of up to about 12 inches in height were encountered in our borings.  
Cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. 
Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should be expected during construction.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Due to the presence of numerous cavities and/or voids in the project vicinity, we 
recommend giving special attention to the site preparation recommendations prior to the 
commencement of fill placement and/or construction of improvements at the project site. 
In general, we recommend ripping subgrades of fill areas to a depth of about 3 feet 
below the existing ground surface to detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids. 
As a minimum, ripping of the subgrades should extend at least 3 feet laterally beyond the 
limits of fill areas. After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of about 3 feet below 
the ground surface, the ground should be proof-rolled with a Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer or 
similar size bulldozer a minimum of four passes to provide a relatively level surface. 
After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer, we recommend proof-rolling 
the fill subgrades with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum 20 tons static weight) for a 
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids. We 
also recommend proof-rolling the cut subgrades with a minimum 20-ton (static weight)

cavities and/or voids. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during clearing and 
proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill 
materials. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new residential structures 
planned.  Based on the relatively light structural loads anticipated for the one- to two-story 
residential structures, we believe the risk of potential collapse of cavities below the 
foundations would be relatively low. Therefore, we believe probing and grouting may

This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for Lot Nos. 4 and 7 of Kaloko Heights Subdivision project in Kailua-Kona in 
the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from

The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of mass 
grading, foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, underground utility lines, and 
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report.

Project Considerations 
The project consists of a residential development in the Kaloko area in the District 
of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The Kaloko Heights development encompasses 
an area of about 177 acres.  Phases A1 and B1 portions of the project consists of 
developing about 49 residential lots at each of Lot Nos. 4 (TMK 7-3-009: 059) and 
7 (TMK 7-3-009: 062) for a total of about 98 residential lots.  The residential lots will be

individual wastewater systems (IWS) with leach fields will be required for the new 
residences.  Therefore, percolation tests utilizing Department of Health (DOH) methods 
were performed in selected borings. 
Based on the preliminary plans provided, mass grading consisting of cuts and fills 
up to about 15 to 20 feet thick may be required to achieve the design grades.  In addition, 
site retaining walls of up to about 10 to 15 feet in height may also be constructed for grade 
separation.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain, which makes 
up the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the site 
appears to be of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they 
approached the ocean. A’a lava typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and 
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including 
cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy 
surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets generally 
are smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted 
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, 
and along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as the primary water transmission 
features because of their high permeability.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a minimum 
2-foot thick layer of structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist 
of well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum 
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted 
mass. In addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. In general, the excavated on-site 
materials may be reused as a source of structural fill materials, provided that they 
are processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements 
presented above. 
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2-foot thick structural fill layer may 
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches with 
an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 12 and 
18 inches in particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total 
volume of general fill materials. The excavated on-site materials, if less than 
18 inches in maximum dimension, may be used as a source of general fill or backfill 
materials (except for trench backfill), provided that they are processed to meet the 
above gradation requirements for general fill. If the excavated materials do not 
contain sufficient fines to produce a well-graded material, off-site borrow or on-site 
rock crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide 
the required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well-graded material. 
Where over-cutting of the finished cut subgrades occur during the grading 
operations, backfilling of the over-cut area should be treated as fill areas. 
Therefore, the above fill material recommendations should apply to the over-cut

3.1.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. General fills and backfills 
should be placed in level lifts of about 18 inches in loose thickness, moisture-
conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
"Rock" fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the 
majority of the rocks or 3 feet, whichever is less. Placement should be by 
haul-trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the 
currently placed lift. Spreading of the "rock" fill should be by a large bulldozer to 
facilitate "seating" of the rocks. The rock fill should be watered heavily during 
placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and 
spraying water continuously during placement. Compaction equipment (such as a 
Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer or similar heavy compaction equipment) providing 
suitable energy to achieve the required compaction should be utilized. The number 
of passes (usually six to eight passes) to be made to achieve proper compaction 
should be determined at the on-set of site grading by the performance of a trial 
compaction test or by plate bearing tests. Subsequent layers of rock fill above the 
previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of smaller-sized boulder 
materials to fill in the voids between the boulders of the preceding rock fill layer.

3.3 
Probing and Grouting 
As mentioned above, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows at the project site. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support 
resulting from the collapse of cavities below foundations, consideration may be given to 
implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the building foundations during 
construction. 
Based on the relatively light structural loads anticipated for the one- to two-story 
residential structures, we believe the risk of potential collapse of cavities below the 
foundations would be relatively low. Therefore, we believe probing and grouting may 
generally be omitted for the new residential structures planned at the site. However, if a 
higher degree of assurance against the potential collapse of cavities below foundations 
is desired, a probing and grouting program below the foundations may be implemented. 
Where the probing and grouting program is implemented, we recommend drilling 
probe holes at 10-foot on centers for the continuous strip footings. In addition, probe holes 
should be drilled at each isolated spread footing (or column) location (one probe hole per 
50 square feet of footing area). The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter 
and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of the 
foundation.  
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical 
and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities discovered 
should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a slump of about

3.4 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the 
new structures. For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. 
The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to

To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above 
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum

In addition, exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork will be required. 
Generally, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No67 gradation) or select borrow subbase below

crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways (or 
slabs) with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

We envision new underground utility lines will be required for the project

12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials (aggregate base materials). Where the 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials are used, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction.

the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular materials 
less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned

compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are located in

3.8 
Permeability Testing 
Six constant head permeability tests were conducted in selected drilled borings to 
provide data for the design of the storm water disposal system planned at the project site. 
The permeability tests were performed within the basaltic rock formation at a depth of 
about 20 feet below the ground surface.  The permeability test results are presented in 
Appendix C. 
Based on our field permeability tests, the subsurface materials generally have 
coefficient of permeability (k) of 6.2 x 10-2 to 1.4 x 10-1 feet per minute at the locations 
tested. Based on the field permeability test results, we believe an average hydraulic 
conductivity of about 0.1 feet per minute may be assumed in the subsurface materials 
encountered at a depth of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface. The hydraulic 
conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 1,080 gallons per 
day per square foot of surface area (0.0017 cubic feet per second per square foot) at 
those locations. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site basaltic rock 
formation is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids/fractures 
in the lava or the presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal 
capacity of the retention basin. In general, the greater the number of clinker seams and/or 
voids/fractures encountered at the retention basin, the higher the overall permeability and 
disposal capacity.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
twelve borings, designated as Boring Nos. 201 through 212, extending to a depth of about 
20 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with coring tools. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-12. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.2.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted six permeability tests in 
selected borings using the constant head test method. In general, the borings had a 
diameter of about 4 inches and extended to a depth of about 20 feet below the ground level 
at the time of the tests. 
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The 
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to 
base calculation of the subsoil permeability. Results of the permeability tests conducted in 
selected borings are presented on Plates C-1 through C-6.
