Project Considerations 
The Moanalua Hillside Apartments complex is located east of Moanalualani Place 
near the top of Red Hill on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Construction of mid-rise buildings 
with parking garages and other maintenance facilities is planned at the site. 
We conducted a geotechnical exploration consisting of eleven borings drilled to 
depths of 20 to 100 feet below the existing ground surface for the development.  
Geotechnical engineering recommendations were presented in the report entitled 
“Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, Moanalua Hillside Apartments, Ala Kapuna Street, 
Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii” dated January 22, 2014. 
We understand that the western portion of the development will be constructed over 
an existing BWS tunnel.  Based on the as-built information provided, the existing tunnel is 
about 10 feet high with a roof radius of 5 feet.  The top of the tunnel is at an approximate

Elevations +268 to +286 feet.  Therefore, the roof of the existing tunnel is about 108 to

Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu. 
The project site is along the distal southern flank of the Koolau Volcano; 
therefore, the project site traverses terrain that is mostly underlain by deeply weathered 
volcanic deposits erupted from the Koolau Volcano.  The volcanic deposits have been 
mapped and are referred to as the Tertiary Period of the Koolau Volcanic Series.  The

Subsurface Condition 
We explored the subsurface conditions at project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, extending to a depth of about 76 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  Previous borings drilled in the vicinity of the tunnel alignment are also 
shown on the Site Plan. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consist of surface fills and 
weathered volcanic tuff overlying alluvium and saprolitic soils and weathered basalt rock 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 76 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
The weathered volcanic tuff encountered in the borings was severely to closely 
fractured and extended to depths of 14 to 21 feet below the ground surface.  The 
weathered volcanic tuff was underlain by very stiff alluvium about 7 to 10 feet thick.  Below 
this, saprolitic soils consisting of stiff to hard clayey and silty soils was encountered over 
the weathered basalt rock formation. 
The basalt rock formation encountered in the borings ranged from severely to 
moderately fractured, highly to slightly weathered, and medium hard to very hard.  Clinker 
and/or breccias were interbedded in the basalt rock formation. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels can significantly vary depending on 
rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology and graphic

Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on the information provided, we understand that two mid-rise buildings (N2 
and N3) as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2, are located directly above the existing BWS 
tunnel with a design structure loading of about 1,620 psf (dead-plus-live loads).  
Building N2 will require approximately 15 feet of excavation to achieve the design lower 
level floor elevation of +260 feet.  Building N3 will require approximately 6 feet of 
excavation to achieve design lower level floor elevation of +280 feet.  Therefore, net

pressure at the bottom of the lower level foundation for Buildings N2 and N3 will be about 
100 and 1,000 pounds per square foot (psf), respectively. 
A section of the existing tunnel directly under Building N3 was selected for 
evaluation of stress distribution on the tunnel crown elevation due to the new development.  
The structural loads included net pressure influence from Building N3 (160 feet long and 
70 feet wide), Garage 2 (150 feet long and 70 feet wide) and Building N4 (160 feet long 
and 70 feet wide). 
Finite element analyses were performed at the selected tunnel section with the 
anticipated net pressure acting at the bottom of the building foundation using the computer 
program Phase 2, Version 6.0 from RocScience, Inc.  Finite Element Method (FEM) is a 
numerical technique for finding approximate solutions to boundary value problems for 
partial differential equations.  It uses subdivision of a whole problem domain into simpler 
parts, called finite elements, and variational methods from the calculus of variations to 
solve the problem by minimizing an associated error function. 
The FEM model consisted of approximately 30,000 mesh units, each with 6 nodes 
forming a domain of 1,600 feet long and 1,000 feet deep to encapsulate the areas that 
may potentially influence the stress distribution contour.  The FEM model was established 
in two-dimensional plane strain condition, to more accurately compute the stress level 
distributed on the tunnel that is induced by the net pressure from the new development. 
The subsurface ground conditions were modeled based on our field exploration, 
laboratory testing results, and interpretation of the geophysical surveys.  Seismic shear 
wave velocity profile was measured using the downhole seismic survey technique, for 
interpretation of the subsurface soil/rock modulus.  The subsurface soil resistivity was also 
measured using the electrical resistivity tomography technique for an idealized subsurface 
profile.  These geophysical surveys were used to refine the subsurface profile between 
borings and provide soil properties for the FEM analysis. 
Results of the FEM analyses are presented on the Finite Element Analyses Stress 
Distribution Contour, Plate 4.  The stress distribution within 30 feet above the tunnel is 
presented on the Tunnel Arching Effect Stress Distribution, Plate 5.  The displacement is 
presented on the Tunnel Displacement Distribution, Plate 6.  Based on the FEM analyses, 
we believe that the stresses at the crown of the tunnel section may vary between 
approximately 50 to 80 pounds per square foot (psf) with a displacement of less than 
0.05 inch. 
Based on the unconfined compression testing conducted on the rock core samples 
obtained from the borings, the compressive strength of the basalt rock may range from 
200 to 8,000 pounds per square inch (equivalent to approximately 28,000 to 
1,000,000 psf).  The results of the FEM analyses indicate that the stress distribution on the 
tunnel is minimal, compared to the strength of the rock formation. The stress reduction is 
primarily induced due to the “Arching Effect” that transfers most of the net structural load to 
the adjacent soil mass.  As illustrated on the Plate 4, the “Arching Effect” occurs about 1.5

to 2.5 times the tunnel diameter, approximately 15 to 25 feet above the tunnel crown.  It 
should be noted that stress concentration was estimated up to 1,200 psf at the corner of 
the tunnel crown.  We believe that the stress concentration was over-estimated due to the 
limitation of the unlined tunnel modeling.  Based on the typical ratio of 1.6 for normalized 
vertical stress of the horse shoe tunnel (Raji M. & T.G. Sitharam, 2011), the stress 
concentration may be up to 80 psf at the corners of the tunnel. 
To validate the FEM analysis, empirical computation using the elastic solution 
developed by Boussinesq (1885) and modified by Newmark (1935) was also used to 
calculate the stress distribution along the vertical subsurface profile above the tunnel 
without considering the “Arching Effect”.  Stress at the tunnel level was calculated under 
the rectangular loads from Building N3, Garage 2 and Building N4.  Based on the elastic 
solution computation, the vertical stress transferred on the top of the tunnel crown is about 
300 psf without reduction from the “Arching Effect”. 
Results of the above evaluation indicate that the vertical pressure acting on the 
tunnel crown would be reduced by approximately 75 to 80 percent due to the “Arching 
Effect”, which is consistent to what was presented by Ladanyi and Hoyaux, 1969.  Based 
on our observation in the tunnel directly under the proposed development site, the 
weathered rock formation is present at the tunnel crown. Based on the computed 
displacement of less than 0.05 inch and stress of about 50 to 80 psf on the tunnel crown, it 
is our opinion that the proposed development would have negligible effect for potential 
impact on the tunnel. 
Limitations 
The boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been taped 
from visible features at the project site.  Elevations noted on the borings were based on 
interpolation between the contour lines shown on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted 
by R.M. Towill Corporation.  The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition.  Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report.  These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report.  It should be 
noted that groundwater fluctuations may occur due to variation in tides, rainfall, 
temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, each extending to a depth of 
approximately 76 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  We drilled the borings using truck-mounted 
drilling equipment equipped with rotary coring tools. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1.1 through A-2.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, we obtained 
some samples from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the coral/rock formations encountered at the site were obtained 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral/rock formation.  The material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2.  The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of coral/rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Dry Density (ASTM D2937) tests were 
performed on selected soil samples to aid in defining the interface between the various 
soil layers encountered and correlating the layers between test borings. The test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Four (4) Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an 
aid in classifying the soils. The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on 
Plate B-1. 
Three (3) Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ 
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the 
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-2 and B-4. 
Seven (7) Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were 
performed on selected rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of 
the rock formation encountered.  Unconfined compression test results are presented on 
Plate B-5.

REPORT FOR GEOPHYSICAL SURVEYS FOR THE MAOANA LUAU 
HILLSIDE BWS TUNNEL STUDY, HONOLULU, HAWAII 
This letter report presents the results of the geophysical survey performed by Global 
Geophysics from March 10th to March 15th, 2015 for the Maoana Luau Hillside BWS 
Tunnel Project, Hawaii. The objective of the study is to study the subsurface conditions 
above the tunnel alignment. 
METHODOLOGY, INSTRUMENTATION AND FIELD PROCEDURES 
Downhole seismic and electrical resistivity tomography surveys were completed for this 
project. The following paragraphs describe the methods and field procedures. 
Downhole Seismic Survey 
The downhole seismic survey was carried out using a Geometrics Geode, 24-channel 
seismograph.  The downhole receiver was a GeoSpace downhole geophone with one 
vertical geophone for recording compressional waves and two horizontal geophones for 
recording shear waves. The geophone package was held against the borehole wall by two 
steel springs. The shear wave energy source consisted of a wooden beam, secured under 
the wheels of the survey vehicle that was impacted with a 20 lb sledgehammer. The 
wooden beam was placed approximately 16 feet away from the borehole. 
The field procedure consisted of lowering the geophone package to the measuring point 
in the hole and then mechanically coupling the geophones to the borehole wall.  Data 
were collected at three-foot intervals over the entire 73-foot depth of the borehole. The 
beam was struck horizontally from both ends in order to produce shear waves of opposite 
polarity. This procedure optimized the identification of horizontally polarized shear 
waves.  After identification of the seismic arrivals the data were stored digitally and the 
geophone package lowered to the next depth point where the procedure was repeated.

Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT) 
The electrical resistivity tomography technique maps differences in the electrical 
properties of geologic materials.  These differences can result from variations in 
lithology, water content, and pore-water chemistry.  The method involves transmitting an 
electric current into the ground between two electrodes and measuring the voltage 
between two other electrodes.  The direct measurement is an apparent resistivity of the 
area beneath the electrodes that includes deeper layers as the electrode spacing is 
increased.  Recent advances in technology permit rapid collection of multiple soundings, 
using up to 56 electrodes for each spread.  The data are modeled to create a 2-D geo-
electric cross-section that is useful for mapping both vertical and horizontal variations of 
the subsurface strata. 
The data were acquired with an AGI SuperSting R8 using up to 56 electrodes spaced at a 
10 feet interval.  Once the electrode array was installed in the ground, multiple soundings 
were automatically carried out by the control unit.  Downloading and routine modeling of 
the data was done on-site to provide preliminary analysis and QA/QC of the data. These 
results were displayed on a color monitor as cross-section that highlight changes in 
resistivity with depths along the transect.
