SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the Off Site Western Bypass Road project for the Kukui Ula Resort
Community in Koloa on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
In 1992 an exploration was conducted for others of this proposed road project
This reconnaissance serves to provide an update of that previous exploration and
includes a review of the information developed in the previous exploration along with a
visit to the project site to make observations of current conditions
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations derived
from our previous field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
project along with the current site reconnaissance These recommendations are
intended for preliminary design of the proposed project The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
The planned Kukui Ula Resort Community development encompasses about
1 045 acres in Koloa on the south shore of Kauai and includes hotel vacation
ownership commercial park golf course and residential uses The proposed project is
part of the planned infrastructure improvements for the Kukui Ula Resort Community
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
The proposed Western Bypass Road is intended to provide a bypass around the
town of Koloa to the Kukui Ula Resort Community and the community of Poipu The
planned road will be about 8 475 feet in length with a portion being within the Kukui Ula
Resort Community and a portion being off site on McBryde Sugar Company land

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation The project site is located on the southern flank of
the shield volcano
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern
half of the Island of Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers advanced and accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less
water available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or
melted global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of
Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were
eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that
were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on Kauai Most of the soils
in the project area are residual and saprolitic soils derived from the in situ weathering of
igneous rocks and alluvial soils deposited from streams

3 1 Site Grading
Based on the preliminary plans for this project we understand that cuts on the
order of 6 to over 20 feet deep and embankment fills on the order of about 5 to over
20 feet in thickness may be required for the proposed roadway Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered during the previous exploration it is our opinion that
the residual and alluvial soils encountered at the site can provide adequate support for
the proposed bypass road project In general conventional earthwork and construction
methods may be used for the project however isolated areas of hard excavation may
be encountered where hard basalt rock formation occurs at shallow depths
In general areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious material and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft
loose and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft loose and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
We recommend that the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas
should be scarified and recompacted to provide a firm and unyielding base Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we recommend that the
soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted or they should be
prepared as mentioned above

Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft and or loose areas
and replacement with well compacted fill
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately
25 square feet of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a
woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of subsequent lifts of fill
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill
or backfill materials provided that they are free of deleterious materials vegetation and
are screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension Based on our
previous field exploration and laboratory test results it appears that the in situ soils
have high moisture contents 30 to over 50 percent Therefore drying or aeration of
the excavated materials will be necessary prior to their use as compacted fills Aeration
to lower the soil moisture and additional compaction effort of thinner fill lifts to achieve
the specified compaction will be necessary and would generally reduce the rate of fill
placement for this project
Imported fill materials needed for the site should consist of materials with a low
expansion potential Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with
particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or higher and a swell potential of
not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1883 Imported fill material should be tested for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use Aggregate
base course required for the project should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and
should conform to Section 703 06 of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 HSS

Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration we
believe that planned cut slopes will expose the very stiff to hard alluvial soils
encountered at the project site In general cut slopes for the project planned at the site
may be designed with a maximum slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter
Permanent fill slopes planned at the project site may be designed with a
maximum slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than
5H 1V should be keyed and benched into firm and stable materials to provide stability of
the new embankment fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill
placement recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report Fill
slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to
obtain a well compacted slope face Excessive surface water should be diverted away
from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the cut and
fill slopes are completed to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished
slopes
Cut and fill slopes exceeding 20 feet in height should incorporate an 8 foot wide
bench at the middle of the slope It should be noted that the 20 foot height criterion is
applicable to the total slope height including to the invert of any drainage ditches or
swales along the toe of the slope

3 3 Utility Trenches
New underground utility lines e g waterlines and drainage lines are planned for
the project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of free draining granular
materials ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for uniform
support Free draining granular materials ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to
reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipe to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with
a maximum particle size of 3 inches The backfill should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Retaining Wall Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed immediately around the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
in lieu of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall Unless
covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of impervious
material such as the on site silty clay material to reduce water infiltration behind
the walls

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of
about 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation The project site is located on the southern flank of
the shield volcano
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern
half of the Island of Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers advanced and accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less
water available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or
melted global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of
Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were
eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that
were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on Kauai Most of the soils
in the project area are residual and saprolitic soils derived from the in situ weathering of
igneous rocks and alluvial soils deposited from streams

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of
about 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
