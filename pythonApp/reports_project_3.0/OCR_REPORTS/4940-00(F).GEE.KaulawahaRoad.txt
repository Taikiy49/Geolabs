EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located in the Makaha Valley area in the District of Waianae on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided an existing AC paved
road approximately 450 feet long exists from the intersection of Makaha Valley Road
and Kaulawaha Road towards the approach of the East Makaha Stream The proposed
roadway alignment beyond the stream is presently not accessible to vehicular traffic
and is relatively flat and sparsely vegetated with small trees and weeds Based on the
plan and profile drawing provided the topography along the roadway extension is
slightly sloping from about 34 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at Makaha Valley Road to
about 10 feet MSL at the intersection with Mahinaau Road
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three shallow
borings designated as Boring Nos 3 through 5 extending to a depth of about 6 5 feet
below the existing ground surface for the design of the new roadway pavement Two
deep borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 were drilled in proximity of the new
bridge abutment locations and extended to depths of about 70 and 81 feet below the
existing ground surface In addition two bulk samples of the near surface soils
designated as Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 were collected at selected locations along the new
roadway alignment to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near surface
soils The approximate locations of the borings drilled and bulk samples collected are
shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
In general our field exploration disclosed that the new roadway alignment may be
underlain by highly expansive adobe clays at shallow depths Based on a review of the
geology and our experience in the area we anticipate that the subsoils underlying the
proposed roadway alignment could range from Mokuleia clay consisting of recent alluvium
of high plasticity to coral deposits and consolidated terrestrial sediments in a very dense
state with cementation Laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests conducted on the
Mokuleia clays indicate the soils exhibit high swelling potential with relatively low CBR

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of
bridge foundations pavements and site preparation are presented in the following
sections of this report
Bridge Foundations
Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered
at the project site we recommend that the new bridge structure be supported by a
foundation system consisting of 36 inch diameter cast in place concrete drilled shafts
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the very stiff to hard alluvial deposits The
end bearing component of the drilled shafts has been discounted due to difficulties
associated with obtaining a clean bottom below the groundwater level during
construction

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the drilled shaft
and shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils In general an uplift load
capacity of up to 200 kips per shaft may be used for the drilled shafts The uplift
load capacity of the drilled shaft provided is intended for transient load applications
Therefore the uplift load capacity should not be further increased for transient load
applications For sustained uplift loads we recommend that the uplift load capacity
provided above be reduced by 50 percent to resist sustained loads The structural
capacity of the drilled shaft in tension should be checked by the project structural
engineer
Lateral Load Resistance
In general lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered at the site we have performed our lateral load analyses for both
free head and fixed head boundary conditions Results of our lateral load
analyses are presented on Plate 3 of this report

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the drilled shaft and the subgrade response We estimate that
the total settlement of the drilled shaft supported foundation to be less than
1 inch with differential settlements between drilled shafts not exceeding about
one half that amount We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic
and should occur as the loads are applied
Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these
potential variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be
present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our
opinion the following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost
of the drilled shaft foundations
Dense boulders river channel deposits were encountered in the borings drilled at
the proposed bridge foundation locations during our field exploration Therefore
the drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to
drill through these obstructions where encountered Appropriate measures will
also be needed to avoid dislodging boulders into the drilled shaft hole during the
drilling and shaft installation process
Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended
Because of the groundwater table anticipated within the lower depths of the
drilled shaft excavations concrete placement by tremie method may be required
during construction of the drilled shafts under such conditions The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward
fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole A low shrink concrete mix with high
slump 7 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide close contact between
the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a
suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from
the concrete mix A representative from Geolabs should be present to observe
the installation of drilled shafts during construction

Structure Approach Slabs
As previously indicated fills of up to about 8 feet thick will be required at the
abutment areas of the new concrete bridge To reduce the potential for abrupt differential
settlements between the drilled shaft supported bridge structure and the new compacted
fills we recommend that structure approach slabs be provided at the abutment locations
The structure approach slabs should be supported on a minimum of 8 inches of
aggregate subbase course placed on a prepared subgrade The subgrade should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The
aggregate subbase course should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete
curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we
recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below
the base and subbase course layers to reduce migration of landscape water into the
pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect
excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we
recommend that a subdrain system be considered along the sides of the roadway

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths ranging from
about 6 5 to 81 feet below the existing ground surface Two bulk samples designated
as Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 were obtained of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement
support characteristics The approximate locations of the test borings and bulk samples
are shown on the Site Plans Plate 2 1 and 2 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring
tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock coral formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation
