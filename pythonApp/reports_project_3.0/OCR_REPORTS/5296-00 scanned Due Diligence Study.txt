Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano
overlying the Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to
be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe
type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is

Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and
pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features
Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located on the western side of Volcano Highway in Keeau within
the Puna District on the Island of Hawaii The proposed development covers an area of
approximately 111 8 acres The property is located across Volcano Highway from Keeau
Marketplace and is bounded to the north and east by the W H Shipman Business Park
and Volcano Highway respectively The site is bounded by vacant undeveloped land to
the southeast
The area is heavily forested with dense vegetation ranging from grasses vines and
shrubs to trees over 100 feet in height In general most of the area can be described as a
tropical rainforest canopy with moderate to dense undergrowth
Ground surface elevations were not available at the time this letter report was
prepared In general the topography of the site is moderately undulating with higher
elevations in the southwest grading gently to the north and east Road cuts for the
Volcano Highway were observed along the eastern border of the property
In general the surface soils at the site consisted of dark brown clayey silts
volcanic ash with some organics and cobble sized basalt fragments Probing with picks
indicated that the soil depth was generally less than 1 foot thick with the average being
approximately 3 inches Cobbles and boulder sized basalt fragments a a clinker were
observed in the northern and northeastern portions of the site
Lot No 9009 is part of the existing W H Shipman Business Park and is relatively
flat and lightly vegetated with grass After mass grading operations the site is presently
vacant

Anticipated Subsurface Conditions
Based on our site observations and a review of available test boring information
from the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that the project site may be underlain by
hard basalt rock formation under a thin surface layer of volcanic ash The basalt rock
formation is believed to consist of both a a and pahoehoe lava rock with frequent cavities
and potential buried lava tube features Based on our experience in the vicinity of the
project site near surface lava tubes and cavities should be anticipated in the basalt rock
formation
Excavation of the basalt rock formation will likely require hard rock ripping using
heavy construction equipment Deep excavations may require controlled blasting
techniques if permitted
We anticipate that other buried cavities may be encountered during construction
An early program of subgrade cavity detection and collapse using heavy construction
equipment such as a D 9 Bulldozer and a 20 ton roller should be implemented to collapse
the near surface potential cavities A program of foundation cavity probing and grouting
may be necessary to stabilize potentially deeper cavities located below building footings
and other heavy loaded foundation systems
Preliminary Recommendations
Based on our preliminary findings it is our opinion that the site is suitable for
development from a geotechnical engineering standpoint We envision that the primary
geotechnical engineering considerations for this project may include
Hard rock excavation
e Processing and use of excavated materials such as rock fill
Subgrade preparation detection and collapse of potential near surface lava
tubes cavities
Cavity probing and grouting of foundation systems for deeper cavities
It is anticipated that the surface basalt rock materials may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavators and ripping with large bulldozers
However some of the deeper excavations into the dense lava may require the use of
hoerams chipping or blasting Generally blasting would be required to increase
production of the mass grading operations The excavation of the basalt rock formation at
the project site is anticipated to generate boulder size rock materials In order to produce
the desired gradation for rock fills we anticipate that on site crushing and processing of
the excavated materials may be necessary It is envisioned that some boulders up to
about 3 feet in dimension may be disposed of in deeper fills if available

In an effort to reduce the potential for collapse of near surface cavities and or voids
within the property we recommend that subgrade preparation procedures be employed
within the limits of grading at the commencement of the mass grading operations for the
project The intent of the subgrade preparation procedure is to assist in detecting and
collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the near surface rock
formations The subgrade preparation procedure recommended for this project is
consistent with the standard of practice for similar type projects and similar subsurface
rock conditions throughout the State of Hawaii
In general the subgrade preparation in cut areas consisted of proof rolling with a
Caterpillar 825 sheepsfoot roller or similar compactor minimum 20 tons for about
eight passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids Where
cavities and or voids were encountered during the proof rolling operations the cavities
and or voids generally were exposed and backfilled in near level lifts
To reduce the possibility for collapse of deeper buried cavities which may
adversely affect the building foundation performance it may be desired to perform a
program of cavity probing and grouting along the major foundation systems Probing and
grouting essentially consists of drilling an array of probe holes to depths of about 10 feet
below the finish grade in cut areas Cement grout is pumped at low pressure in the drilled
probe holes in an effort to stabilize the buried voids encountered by the probe holes
against potential collapse
It is recommended that exploration of the subsurface conditions at the project site
be conducted to evaluate the subsurface conditions for design of the proposed
development The exploration could be better defined when the footprint of the
proposed structures and site grading criteria are known
Limitations
The preliminary findings and discussion submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from site reconnaissance literature research and past project
experience in the vicinity of the project site Variations of subsurface conditions from those
described in this report may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until a geotechnical engineering exploration is conducted at the site Our
services were performed in accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical
engineering practice no warranty is expressed or implied
Based on the location of the project site it should be noted that the primary
potential geologic hazard at the project site is the potential for future volcanic activity from
the nearby Mauna Loa Volcano to affect the project site inundation by lava flows The
potential geologic hazard inundation by lava flows is a common hazard to the entire area
Based on information published by the U S Geological Survey we understand that the
Keeau area is rated as Zone 3 on a scale of Zone 1 highest hazard to Zone 9 lowest
hazard on the Island of Hawaii
