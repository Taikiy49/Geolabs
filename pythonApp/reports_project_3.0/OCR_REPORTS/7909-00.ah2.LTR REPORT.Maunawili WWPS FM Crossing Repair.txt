PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, we understand the project involves repairing 
the existing force main crossing near the Maunawili Wastewater Pump Station (WWPS) 
in the Maunawili area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
A portion of Maunawili Stream on the west side of Maunawili Road is concrete 
lined. The concrete lining terminates at the east side of the bridge structure. The bridge 
across Maunawili Stream is a two-span concrete structure. The existing force main 
crossing is located within the unlined stream channel. 
Based on the information provided, the existing force main crosses Maunawili 
Stream with a concrete jacket and has experienced settlement and/or undermining due 
to erosion from the stream flow. The depth of undermining varies from about 0 to 18 
inches deep.  In addition, the depression upstream of the force main crossing varies 
and is up to about 3 feet deep.  Therefore, it is desired to restore the eroded stream bed 
underneath and upstream of the concrete jacket, as well as restoration of the eroded 
stream banks (slope stabilization) on the two sides of the stream at the crossing.  The 
total area of restoration measures about 1,200 square feet in area.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano reached the end 
of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles 
of continental glaciation. As the continental glaciers advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stand. When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose above the present level.  
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans 
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits 
and induration of the calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of 
the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively 
high energy conditions. 
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii, chemical weathering has altered the 
basaltic flows/pyroclastic ejecta to a soil-like condition. Erosional processes, consisting of 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward 
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium, have also altered the natural condition 
of the volcanic flows. Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a 
valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and 
deposited as alluvium.  
When alluvial/colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical 
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering 
of the material. Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older 
alluvial/colluvial deposits. Depending on the rate of transport, the newer sediments are 
generally transient in terms of geologic time.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 36 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  
Our field exploration suggests that the project site at the roadway grade is generally 
underlain by surface fills, consisting of medium dense silty sands, to a depth of about 
2 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the surface fills, older alluvial deposits 
consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey silts were generally encountered to the maximum 
depth explored of about 36 feet below the existing ground surface.  It should be noted that 
a river channel deposit of dense cobbly boulders was encountered from about 4 to 8 feet 
deep. 
We encountered groundwater in the drilled boring at a depth of about 10.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. We believe this water 
level generally corresponds to the water level at the Maunawili Stream.  It should be noted 
that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
After completion of the drilling and sampling operations, the borehole was 
converted into a monitoring well for groundwater level monitoring from about February 10 
through March 9, 2020.   Results of the groundwater level monitoring are presented on 
Groundwater Monitoring Plot, Plate 3.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the channel bed and stream bank restoration work anticipated for the 
project, we believe the following three options may be considered for the project, including 
(1) Gabion Basket/Mattress; (2) Articulating Concrete Blocks; and (3) Fabric Form 
Concrete. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the three options are presented 
in the following sections. 
Gabion Basket/Mattress 
Gabions generally consist of rectangular cages made of hexagonal woven steel 
wire mesh laced together and filled with stone. Gabions are manufactured with    
double-twisted hegaxonal mesh woven from annealed mild steel wire that are zinc

coated.  The PVC coated mesh is made from wire with the above characteristic and 
coated by extrusion with a special formulated highly corrosion resistant PVC.  There are 
two types of gabions that may be used: a regular gabion basket at 1-foot thick; or a 
gabion mat at 6 or 9-inch thick. 
A gabion structure generally offers the following advantages: (1) flexibility; 
(2) permeability; (3) low environmental impact; and (4) ease of construction.  For this 
specific project application in restoring the channel bed and stream banks, gabions 
have an additional safety factor in that they are permeable and self-draining, thus 
allowing the stream water to run through the gabions when compared to the other types 
of restoration/stabilization structures. 
The disadvantages of this option generally lie in the availability of the stones and 
it is labor intensive in construction.  Our experience in the past for gabion construction 
projects is the difficulty in locating a source of the stones required that need to be of 
certain sizes, generally between 4 and 8 inches and have a minimum unit weight of 140 
pounds per cubic feet (pcf).  In addition, the stone should consist of hard basalt of such 
quality that the stone should not disintegrate on exposure to water or weathering during 
the life of the structure.  This type of stone may not be readily available and will need to 
be specially produced by the Contractor using screenings from the quarry.  Due to the 
relatively small area of restoration, the cost of producing the specific stones could be 
substantial. 
Articulating Concrete Blocks 
Articulating concrete block (ACB) mats generally consist of a flexible matrix of 
concrete blocks, either open-cell blocks or closed-cell blocks, with uniform size, shape, 
and weight used for hard armor erosion control. The blocks generally have specific 
hydraulic capacities and are laced longitudinally with galvanized steel, stainless steel or 
polyester revetment cables which provide ease of handling and installation. 
The ACB mats can be used in applications such as channel lining and stream 
bank protection. The ACB mats are installed on a prepared subgrade utilizing 
conventional construction equipment and site-specific filter fabric.  While both block 
types provide protection and stability, the open-cell specifically offers the void space 
necessary for revegetation. 
The disadvantage of this option for this specific project is that the dimension of 
the restoration area is relatively small, on the order of about 1,200 square feet, which is 
below the quantity of blocks needed to start up the production plant for dry cast blocks.  
Alternatively, factory-assembled ACB mats are available but the transportation cost 
could be substantial when compared to other options. In addition, the factory-assembled 
mats are heavy in weight and may require a large crane for handling, which may not be 
feasible for this project due to the existing site conditions (narrow Maunawili Road at the 
project location and an old bridge with a low weight limit).

Fabric Form Concrete 
Fabric form concrete is a concrete-filled system that provides performance in 
erosion control, scour protection and repair, environmental and marine construction 
applications.  They are used to protect channels and stream banks from the forces of 
flowing water. The fabric forms are constructed of woven, double-layer, synthetic fabric.  
The linings and mats are installed by positioning fabric forms over the areas to be 
protected and then pumping high-strength, fine aggregate concrete (structural grout) 
into the forms.  The fabric forms can be placed and filled either underwater or in-the-dry.  
The high-strength, fine aggregate concrete is used in place of conventional concrete 
because of its pumpability, high-strength, impermeability, and absorption resistance.  In 
addition to its significant performance advantages, fabric-formed concrete is economical 
to install.  It eliminates the need for heavy equipment and steel reinforcement.  It 
generally does not require specialized labor or dewatering of the project site prior to 
installation. 
EVALUATION AND RECOMMENDATION 
Each of the three options presented has its advantages and disadvantages.  From 
a technical perspective, we believe that Option 3 will provide the best solution to the 
existing stream erosion problem with considerations to the material source, site conditions, 
and accessibility. In addition, we envision that Option 3 will probably have the lowest 
construction cost among the three alternatives.  Based on the discussions presented 
above and our evaluation, we believe that the fabric form concrete option (Option 3) is a 
highly viable option meeting the objectives of this project when compared to the other 
alternatives considered. Therefore, we recommend selecting the fabric form concrete 
option (Option 3) for detailed review and implementation. 
SLOPE STABILITY EVALUATION OF STREAM BANKS 
As part of the project scope of work, we have performed engineering analyses to 
assess the slope stability of the existing stream banks using the computer software 
programs Slide 6.0 developed by Reoscience.  A generalized cross-section of the existing 
stream banks used in the global stability analysis is presented on Plate 4. 
Bishop’s simplified method of limit equilibrium was used to evaluate the balance of 
forces and moments acting within the slope section to determine the factor of safety for the 
slope based on the generalized subsurface profile and shear strength parameters. The 
Bishop’s simplified method of slope stability analysis assumes a circular slip surface, 
which is divided into slices with finite mass. The parameters used in our global slope 
stability analysis to evaluate the rotational slope failure mechanism include the following:

To evaluate the long-term stability of the stream banks, effective stress soil

Based on the results of the global slope stability analysis, the existing condition at 
the stream bank generally has a factor of safety of at least 3.4 under static loading 
condition as shown on Plate 4.  Therefore, it is our opinion that the existing stream banks 
would be stable under a static loading condition considered in our analyses.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition at the project site was explored by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 36 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The boring was drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD 
solid-stem augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representation of the materials 
encountered is presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Log of Boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on    
Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-3.
