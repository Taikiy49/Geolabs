PURPOSE AND SCOPE
We understand that the up country Kula water study in Maui is to
determine the feasibility of constructing two large rectangular shaped
lined reservoirs along with 1 5 to 2 miles or more of open concrete
lined ditch inlets for each reservoir The proposed upper Kula
reservoir will have a capacity of about 150 M G and the lower reservoir
about 300 M G The general location of the two project sites and their
vicinity is shown on the Vicinity Map Plate 1
The purpose of this soil engineering and geologic reconnaissance
was to explore the surface and subsurface soil rock and groundwater
conditions at both sites in order to provide preliminary data for use in
the feasibility study of the site grading design and construction for
the reservoirs and ditch systems
Our study consisted of a review of available soils and geologic
maps and literature concerning the area partial geologic field
reconnaissance a few auger probes laboratory testing and engineering
analysis
The results of our field reconnaissance and laboratory tests which
we used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendix of this report

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed water study plan is to construct a 300 M G reservoir
with plan dimensions of about 2500 by 800 feet for the lower Kula site
and a 150 M G reservoir with plan dimensions of about 2000 by 650 feet
for the upper Kula site Both reservoirs will be designed for a water
depth of about 30 feet In addition 1 5 to 2 miles or more of open
inlet ditches will be built with cross sectional dimensions of
approximately 13 feet wide and 7 feet deep along their lengths
It is anticipated that the site grading for the reservoirs will
involve cuts into the existing ground up to about 30 feet deep and
placing embankment fills of up to 50 to 60 feet or more on the downs lope
portion of the reservoir structure
The general locations of the proposed upper and lower reservoirs
and ditch alignments are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 2 A
typical cross section through the 300 M G reservoir is also presented on
Plate 2

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project sites are located on the north Slope of Haleakala
Mountain on the Island of Maui The lower reservoir site is located
about 4 miles east of Makawao and the upper reservoir located about 4 5
miles southeast of Makawao and approximately 1 5 miles south up slope
from the lower reservoir The approximate locations of the sites are
shown on the Vicinity Map Plate 1
The topography is usually characterized by rolling hills with
well defined gullies or drainageways
The sites are located in a wet high rainfall area that is densely
covered with brush ferns and trees Some Swamipy areas were noted
throughout the areas visited
The lower reservoir and ditch have higher rainfall and the
vegetation is denser but with fewer trees than at the upper site
Access through portions of the upper reservoir is by 2 and 4 wheel
drive vehicles over unimproved roads
Access into the lower area is inaccessible by 4 wheel drive vehicle
except for the easterly portion of the ditch line Access to the lower
reservoir is only by foot along an old trail or by traversing along an
existing 24 inch water line The route is difficult and tedious and in
some places nearly impassable
GEOLABS HAWAII
The northern side of the mountain Slopes down towards the ocean
with an overall relief of about 8 1 to 10 1 H V Numerous gullies and
drainageways cross through the sites generally trending from the south
towards the north
The ground elevations between the two sites range from about 2850
feet at the lower reservoir to about 4250 feet at the upper reservoir
An existing 24 inch pipe line runs in a east to west direction
about 200 to 500 feet down slope of the proposed lower reservoir and
ditch line
Two existing 50 M G tanks are located near the easterly end of the
proposed upper ditch line and a 12 inch pipe line generally runs along
the existing roadway from these tanks in a westerly direction towards
the upper reservoir site
In general the swampy ponded conditions noted at many of the areas
visited may be indicative of a low permeability of the soil cover and
underlying rock
Rock outcrops are mostly limited to exposures in the gullies and
drainageways and cuts made for access roads

Climatic Conditions
The average annual rainfall at the upper reservoir site varies from
about 90 to 110 inches and along the upper ditch line from about 110 to
210 inches
The average annual rainfall at the lower reservoir varies from
about 200 to 220 inches and along the lower ditch line from about 220 to
250 inches
PHYS LOGRAPHY
The study area is situated in the easterly part of the Island of
Maui The most prominent feature of the area is Haleakala Mountain
which is one of the two volcanoes that formed the island Water is
abundant on the northerly mountain Slope in the study area The geology
of the area is described in the following section
GEOLOGY
Maui the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers
an area of approximately 730 square miles The island was formed by the
activity of two volcanoes They are East Maui or Haleakala and West
Maui The isthmus in the central portion that separates the two
mountains is fairly flat and mostly used for sugarcane
Haleakala mountain which makes up the eastern portion of the
island is considered dormant with the last eruption taking place on the
southerly side of the mountain around 1790
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1398 00 Page 7
Haleakala consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that
built the mountain from three rift zones probably during Pliocene and
early Pleistocene times These lavas formed a dome about 8000 feet high
above sea level Later in early and middle Pleistocene time the Kula
volcanics covered the dome to about elevation 10 000 feet
The Kula Volcanics accumulated during a slowing down of the
mountain building phase Later during the middle to late
Pleistocene time and continuing to Recent time the Hana Volcanic phase
erupted that veneered large areas of the east and south sides of
Haleakala and filled valleys that were eroded after the Kula lavas were
laid down
Pertaining to the rock formations at the site the Kula Volcanics
consist of dense gray to steel blue commonly massive andesitic aa
type lavas with thick clinker beds that may range from 5 to 25 feet in
thickness The andesites are called Mugearite Trachyandesite by
Macdonald and Abbott 1970 because in Hawaii they are not the same as
the common andesites of continental areas which are richer in Silica and
sodium
Permeability of the Mugearites is generally poor except in the
clinker beds

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the two reservoir sites were
investigated by attempting to hand auger with small diameter augers to
help delineate the near surface soil conditions and to obtain small
samples for testing
At both sites the hand auger method proved to be inadequate for
penetration beyond depths of 2 to 3 feet The dense tropical vegetation
at the lower reservoir site made it impossible for the investigation
team to gain access to the actual site location without survey markers
and a trail cutting crew to cut paths through the dense forest
The approximate locations of the auger probes are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 2 Detailed descriptions of the materials
encountered are presented on the Auger Probe Logs attached in Appendix A
Soil Conditions
From visual observations of rock exposures in the many gullies and
drainageways crossed and the shallow depths augered it appears that
most of the areas are covered with a fairly thin less than 5 feet
thick surface soil layer of very wet clayey silt volcanic ash and
organic peaty material over weathered lava rock formations

LOND
The field observations and explorations disclose that the Sites are
generally underlain by lava rock formations at shallow depths
Based on the limited data it appears that the underlying volcanic
rock formation below the ash soil could provide adequate bearing for the
proposed high reservoir embankments
The excavated rock when broken down to various sizes could provide
a source as fill material for the high embankment The upper ashy soil
layer removed below the embankment fill should be stockpiled for disposal
or used as cushion layer above the exposed rocky reservoir surface
Depending on the structure of the underlying rock formation a choking
layer consisting of well graded rock materials may be required between
the underlying rugged rock surface and the upper soil cushion fill

Reservoirs Excavation
The field exploration indicates that the reservoir sites are
underlain at fairly shallow depths by dense andesite Aa basalt mant led
with a thin layer of ash and organic soils
It is anticipated that the clinker Layers may be excavated with
normal excavating methods
The more massive andesite will likely require special excavating
procedures such as blasting to make the proposed cuts and ditch line
excavations
It is anticipated that most of the ditch trenching will be done in
the massive andesite however some areas of clinker may be encountered
especially in the deeper trenches
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface
materials are based on our visual observations of the existing road cuts
and exposures in the vicinity
We anticipate that the excavated hard rock could have a swell value
of around 35 to 40 which may produce an excess material that may have to
be disposed of off site For preliminary costs estimate an overall
swell factor of 30 more or less may be considered for various rocky
materials

The rock excavation is expected to break within a size range of 6 to
30 inches Good gradation in sizes smaller than 6 inches may be less
than desired but this cannot be determined until exploratory borings in
the cut areas are made
Embankment Section
Suitable impervious embankment material for use in the high
reservoir embankment construction may not be readily available in the
general vicinity of the two reservoirs The shallow upper ashy
overburden soil will be difficult to compact in the high rainfall area
and will likely lose substantial amount of strength under saturated
conditions In addition soil erosion and piping could be a problem if
it is used in the reservoir facing
Reservoir leakage would most likely occur in the exposed in situ
clinker layers or if many fractures and joints are exposed in the
excavation The rock fill embankment should be considered as recommended
earlier

In general the excavated rock material less than 12 inches in
size may be used in the central core section of the fill
embankments The larger boulders used in the construction of fills
should be placed along the toe sections to be used for buttress
fills or revetments

If the excavated rock material does not contain sufficient fines to
provide a well graded material the surficial ash soil may be mixed
into the excavated rocky material to increase the percentage of
fines to provide a better graded material for compaction and
increase the stability of the fills
Because of the scarcity of soils in the area excess ash soils may
be stockpiled and saved for later use for filling in voids in the
broken rock The organic peaty soils should be wasted off site
It may be necessary to stockpile the clinker material while the deep
cuts are being made so that the boulder sized material may be
disposed of in the deeper portions of the fill
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in
excavated volcanic rock material due to the large particle size
Instead a testing program consisting of visual observations and
test pits to determine the number of passes a compactor is required
to achieve the desired compaction should be conducted at the start
of the project The number of passes can then be used as the
criteria for compaction

The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are not
intended to be used for final design purposes since they are based on
brief and limited preliminary geologic site reconnaissance of the two
sites and their surrounding area a literature review soil information
from a few random auger probings and limited amount of laboratory tests
performed on shallow surface soil samples obtained from this exploration
Unanticipated soil and rock conditions such as gully deposit or
pockets of ash fill cavities etc are commonly encountered and may
occur in localized areas Therefore additional field investigation as
outlined hereunder are needed before design and construction of the
structures The following explorations are recommended to further
determine the feasibility of this project
1 Much of the upper reservoir site is accessible and can be exp lored
by drilling with a 4 wheel drive truck mounted equipment and
obtaining cores of the lava rock formations The borings could be
supplemented by conducting several geophysical refraction seismic
traverses in conjunction with the drilling program to determine rock
depths and velocities for degree of hardness for rippability of the
subsurface materials Some dozer trails may be needed depending on
the actual boring locations

started
