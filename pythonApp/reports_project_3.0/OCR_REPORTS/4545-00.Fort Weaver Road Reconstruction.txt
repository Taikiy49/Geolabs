REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in Central Oahu
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has
subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbot 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of island subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated and advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was
less water available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher
elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by
fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action extended
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed distinct ledges of
reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located in the residential area of Ewa Beach at the southern
shoreline of the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The
project site extends easterly along Pohakupuna Road from Amio Street on the western end
toward Fort Weaver Road The project site continues along Fort Weaver Road in the
easterly direction toward the Fort Weaver Road intersection with Nalomeli Place as shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project consists of approximately 8 358 lineal feet of existing sewer lines ranging
from 24 inch to 36 inch diameter RCP Based on the available plans provided by
ParEn Inc the elevation of the existing ground surface ranges from about 4 to 8 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL The ground surface is relatively flat along the residential roadway
where the sewer alignment is located
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our review of the available subsurface information provided by ParEn Inc
the subsurface conditions at the project site generally consist of about 2 to 3 feet of clayey
and sandy fill soils overlying medium dense to dense silty coralline sand and coral reef
materials The coralline materials extend to the maximum depth explored of about 40 feet
below the ground surface
Based on the available boring log information groundwater was measured at depths
ranging between about 2 and 5 feet below the existing ground surface In general the
measured groundwater levels appear to correspond to about Elevation 0 5 feet MSL to
about Elevation 3 feet MSL

DISCUSSIONS AND PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the preliminary information provided we understand that most of the
existing RCP sewer lines will be rehabilitated utilizing trenchless technology such as CIPP
methods In addition some manholes will be rehabilitated to repair the observed corrosion
We understand that not all of the existing manholes have been inspected therefore there
is a possibility that later inspections may reveal other evidence that will require the
replacement of manholes for the project Furthermore based on the calculations for future
pipe flow it may be necessary to replace some sections of the pipeline to improve the
hydraulic capacity
Based on the available plans for the existing sewer lines the existing pipe inverts
range from about 7 feet MSL at the eastern end of the project to about 14 feet MSL at the
western end of the project This corresponds approximately to invert depths of about 11 feet
eastern end to about 19 feet western end below the existing ground surface
Based on our review of the existing boring information the subsurface conditions at
the existing pipe inverts generally consist of medium dense to dense silty coralline sands and
coral algal reef materials The subsurface materials encountered in the borings appear to
be relatively uniform in character along the length of the pipe alignment Based on the
previous boring information shallow groundwater conditions may exist at the project site As
mentioned previously groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at elevations
ranging between about 0 5 feet to about 3 feet MSL The project site contains generally
permeable subsurface conditions located in close proximity to the shoreline Therefore the
groundwater levels should be anticipated to fluctuate in response to changing tides and other
factors
As mentioned previously this sewer reconstruction project is anticipated to consist of
the rehabilitation of the existing pipeline and manhole structures only However there is a
possibility that some existing pipe segments and manholes may be replaced with new
structures Where the replacement of pipe segments and manholes may be required the
relatively deep excavations anticipated for the project will likely encounter the medium dense
to dense coralline materials and shallow groundwater conditions Therefore the principal
geotechnical engineering considerations for the project are related to excavation shoring
dewatering and pipe bedding and backfill conditions
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may be used for temporary
shoring of excavations in materials other than the dense coral formation especially where
dewatering of the excavations will be necessary Due to the potential for presence of hard
coral materials we believe that predrilling through these potential obstructions would be
necessary in order to facilitate the sheet pile installation operations For shallow excavations
and excavations in open areas we believe that a cantilever type of sheet piling may be
considered For deeper excavations and excavations located adjacent to buildings utilities
and pavements we recommend that interlocking steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing be

Excavation
Based on our review of the available subsurface information for this project the
existing gravity sewer lines are generally underlain by relatively uniform subsurface
conditions consisting of medium dense to dense silty coralline sands and coral reef materials
In addition shallow groundwater levels may be encountered at depths of about 2 to 6 feet
below the existing ground surface Therefore we envision that temporary shoring of the
sewer line and manhole excavations would be required for the project construction except
in the dense coral reef formation
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may be used for temporary
shoring purposes especially where dewatering of the excavations will be necessary For
shallow excavations and excavations in open areas we believe that cantilever type sheet
piling may be considered For deeper excavations and excavations located adjacent to
buildings utilities and pavements we recommend that interlocking steel sheet piling with
horizontal bracing be used for excavation support in order to reduce the potential for adjacent
ground movement
A monitoring program should be developed and implemented by the contractor to
detect ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the sewer line and manhole
excavations It should be noted that during and after installation of the sheet piles minor
settlements may occur Therefore it is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer
be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the sewer line trench and manhole excavations based on the subsurface conditions
encountered However it should be noted that dense coral reef formation may exist
at shallow depths that would require heavy equipment for excavation or removal
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal
to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in order to
reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In addition the excavated
soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground utilities to reduce
the potential for ground movement or subsidence which may damage the
underground utility lines

Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 20 feet may be required at the
western half of the gravity sewer line alignment Based on the subsurface conditions
anticipated we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavations will be necessary
in order to protect personnel working in the excavations The requirement for shoring
in dense coral reef material should be evaluated on a site specific basis
Temporary shoring of the excavations using sheet pile shoring especially where
dewatering of the excavations will be necessary may be considered However it
should be noted that the existing gravity sewer line alignment may likely traverse other
utility lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility
lines if sheet piles are to be used as shoring If sheet pile shoring is used it would
also serve as a cut off wall for the dewatering operations Therefore the sheet piles
should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential
for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the
excavations due to the high permeability of the subsurface conditions
It is important that adequate sheeting be installed prior to excavation and maintained
tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation The properly
braced sheeting is essential to reduce the potential for large lateral movements of the
adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in potential settlements or
distress to adjacent structures or other improvements such as roads or utilities
It should be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship
such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed on the
subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level is
above the bottom of the proposed excavation Based on the available boring logs
groundwater was encountered at depths ranging from about 2 to 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Based on the shallow groundwater levels anticipated and the nature of the
soils to be encountered during excavation dewatering will be required for the excavations
along the sewer line alignment Therefore dewatering provisions should be included in the
contract documents for the proposed construction Since the excavation will involve
discharge of groundwater a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES
permit may be necessary
Based on the anticipated depth of the sewer line pipes and manhole structures and
the relatively shallow groundwater table dewatering with cut off walls such as interlocking

Backfilling of Manhole Excavations
In general manhole excavations should be properly backfilled If backfilling below the
groundwater level is necessary we recommend that No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4
gradation be used from the bottom of the manholes up to an elevation of about the high
groundwater level The first 12 inches of backfill above the No 2 Rock should consist of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation After placement of the No 3B Fine
gravel a layer of filter fabric such as Mirafi 180 N or equivalent should be placed above the
No 3B Fine gravel prior to placement of compacted soil backfill to reduce the potential for
significant migration of fines into the underlying open graded gravel fill The excavated on
site soils excluding the soft and wet soils may be used as backfill above the filter fabric up
to the finished grades
The backfill should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with
each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
Sewer Line Pipes
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the sewer line pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used
for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or groundwater level to

reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or

pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
