Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Honoapiilani Highway Tsunami Damage Repairs in Ukumehame
project in the District of Lahaina on the Island of Maui Hawaii The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report presents the findings and our geotechnical recommendations derived
from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of retaining structures site grading and
pavement design only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
This project involves emergency road repair due to damages to the existing
shoreline structures along Honoapiilani Highway Route 30 between Ukumehame and
Papalua Beach Parks as a result of the tsunami wave generated by the March 11 2011
Japan earthquake The existing concrete barriers on the shoreline side of the roadway
has been displaced and undermining of the concrete barrier structures has been
observed In addition damage to the existing revetment has occurred
We understand that approximately 200 linear feet of new revetment will be
installed The revetment will consist of a 2 stone thick armor layer of 5 ton stone
3 foot nominal diameter stones underlain by 3 feet of 6 inch to 12 inch diameter stones
The bottom of the revetment will be at about Elevation 4 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
In addition approximately 1 260 linear feet of damaged concrete barriers will be
replaced with a concrete retaining wall with a revetment in front of the wall This revetment
will consist of a single layer of 2 ton stone nominal diameter 3 feet underlain by a 3 foot
thick layer of 6 inch to 12 inch diameter stones The bottom of the retaining wall will be at
about Elevation 1 feet MSL and the bottom of the revetment will be at about
Elevation 3 to 4 feet MSL

Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala
Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located
between these two volcanoes The project site is on the lower western slopes of the
older West Maui Volcano
The older West Maui Volcano is generally comprised of three series of lava
flows The lower unit was formed during the Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch and is
identified as the Wailuku Volcanic Series The lavas of the Wailuku Volcanic Series
generally consist of thinly bedded a a and pahoehoe lavas Overlying this unit are lavas
of the Honolua Volcanic Series late Pleistocene Epoch that consist of alkalic lavas and
stiff trachyte
Volcanic inactivity occurred during the middle Pleistocene Epoch resulting in
weathering of the lavas carving of canyons and deposition of materials During the late
Pleistocene Epoch renewal of volcanic activity occurred near the southern coast with
lava flows of the Lahaina Volcanic Series The lavas of the Lahaina Volcanic Series
generally consist of olivine rich basalt and nepheline basanite Near the shoreline
accumulation of alluvial deposits from drainageways lagoonal sediments and beach
sand deposited during the sea level changes are present at the project site

designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 at the proposed project site The borings
extended to a depth of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface In addition

Retaining Structure Foundations
The new retaining wall foundations will be designed based on the Load and
Resistance Factor Design LRFD procedures Based on the generally competent
subsurface conditions encountered we recommend utilizing a shallow foundation
system consisting of continuous strip footings bearing on the medium dense to very
dense beach sand deposit for support of the new wall structure Based on LRFD
procedures a bearing pressure of up to 8 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used to evaluate the extreme event limit state of the footings bearing on the
medium dense to very dense beach sand deposit To evaluate the strength limit
state of the wall foundations a bearing value of up to may be used
Based on a service limit state bearing pressure of we estimate the
foundation settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings bearing on
the dense to very dense beach sand deposit to be less than 1 inch A vertical
stiffness spring of 25 pounds per square inch of deflection may be used
In general we recommend providing a 12 inch gravel cushion layer between the
bottom of the footing and the underlying foundation soils to provide uniform bearing
support Since the bottom of the footing is near the groundwater level the gravel
cushion layer should consist of 6 inch minus surge rock material To reduce the
potential for loss of fines from the subgrade into the cushion material we
recommend using a filter fabric between the subgrade material and the cushion
material
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should
be over excavated to expose the underlying medium dense to dense beach sand
deposit The over excavation should be backfilled with 6 inch minus surge rock
wrapped with a filter fabric
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade

Site Grading
The grading required for extending the roadway generally will consist of fills of up
to about 3 feet In general site grading work should conform to Section 200 of the
State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005 and
the site specific recommendations contained herein
A Geolabs representative or a qualified engineer should monitor site grading
operations to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
grading work and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
In general areas within the designated grading limits should be cleared of
vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grub and or spoil materials should
be disposed properly off site Soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing
operations should be over excavated to expose firm or dense ground and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with aggregate subbase material moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general the excavated on site materials may be reused as a source of general
fill material provided that they are free of organic materials and contain no lumps or rock

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the emergency repair site were explored by drilling
and sampling five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to a depth
of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers In addition six test pits
were excavated to depths of about 4 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring and test pit locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1
through 2 3
The materials encountered in the borings and test pits were classified by visual
and textural examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the
drilling operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log
Legend Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings
and test pits are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5 and on the
Logs of Test Pits Plates A 6 through A 11
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Nine Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 1 and B 2
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 3
through B 6
Four laboratory Resistance R Value tests AASHTO T 190 were performed on
selected bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 7 through B 10
