PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the project site as a 43 lot residential
subdivision No site grading plans are currently available however we
anticipate that no major grading will be required for the proposed site
improvements
The project site is located in an unsewered area and there is no
known regional facilities plan scheduled to be implemented in the
foreseeable future Therefore it will be necessary to use on site
treatment and disposal for domestic wastewater generated within the
development area

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated on the northern coast of the island of
Kauai Hawaii The project site is located on the northwestern corner of
the town of Kilauea on the western side of Kilauea Lighthouse Road The
general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
Formerly the site was used for agricultural purposes Currently
the site is an open field with a thick surface cover of tall grasses
Based on our field reconnaissance the site is nearly level with a
very slight slope down towards the north Neither a topographic plan nor
the existing ground elevations were available at the time of our
exploration
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is situated on a gently sloping plateau along the
north shore of the island of Kauai This plateau was built during the
late Pliocene and Pleistocene by the extrusion of basaltic lavas and
pyroclastics from the Kilauea and related vents of the Koloa Volcanic
Series
Most of the soils in the project area are residual soils derived
from the in situ weathering of these basic igneous rocks The residual
soils grade with depth to saprolite a form of residual soil which retains
the relict structures vesicles and joints of the parent rock
At the project site the saprolite soils appear to be the weathering
products of pyroclastic cinders and still retain some of the open
structure of the original cinder deposit

Soil Conditions
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling
seven 7 borings to depths ranging between 30 25 and 46 5 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
In general the soil conditions encountered throughout the project
Site appear to be relatively uniform Most of our drilled borings
encountered a surface layer extending to about fifteen 15 feet below
the existing ground surface of medium stiff to stiff reddish brown to
brown clayey silt These are residual soils that derived from the in situ
weathering of Koloa Basalts and are generally structureless
Below this surface layer the borings generally encountered medium
stiff to stiff reddish brown to brown sandy silts to the maximum depths
explored in the drilled borings
These sandy silts are saprolite soils having predominantly reddish
brown to brown color mottled with black spots or streaks Occasional soft
pockets and or seams were encountered in the saprolite layers
During our field exploration groundwater was measured in the drilled
borings generally at depths of about 16 to 18 feet below the existing
ground surface This groundwater appears to be in a perched condition and
is possibly the result of low permeability ash beds or lenses within the
Koloa Volcanic Series Therefore groundwater may be encountered in deep
excavations within the project site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration encountered generally competent subsoils within
the project site However modified compaction standard and special
grading techniques are required for the earthwork portion of the proposed
site improvements due to the relatively high in situ moisture content of
the subsoils which will be the source of fill material from on site area
and the relatively high rainfall occurring in the area
We recommend that slab on grade and spread or continuous footing
foundations be used to support the proposed residential structures
Sewage Disposal
The project site is located in an unsewered area and there is no
known regional facilities plan scheduled to be implemented in the
foreseeable future The recent promulgation of Chapter 62 of the Hawaii
Department of Health Administrative Rules Wastewater Systems has
effectively prohibited the use of cesspools as a method of sewage
treatment and disposal The new rule requires some form of treatment
prior to disposal
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 7
For rural unsewered areas the usual treatment and disposal methods
are by using a septic tank or household aerobic unit for primary treatment
with leachfields or seepage pits as the disposal component of the system
In order for the disposal component to function it must be placed in soil
which is capable of absorbing or percolating the quantity of effluent
Which will be generated Proper design of the disposal system requires
data pertaining to the in situ permeability absorption capacity of the
soils at the site
To provide these data we have performed a series of falling head
percolation tests in five 5 of the drilled borings and three 3 short
supplemental borings drilled to test the in situ permeability of the upper
near surface soil stratum In addition we have conducted a field
permeability test in an existing seepage pit recently constructed in the
vicinity of the project site
The tests in the short supplemental borings indicated that the upper
clayey silt soils down to a depth of about fifteen 15 feet do not have
sufficient absorption capacity for the use of leachfields as the disposal
component of the individual wastewater systems which will be installed at
the project site The percolation tests performed in the five 5 deeper
drilled borings and one 1 existing seepage pit indicate that the
underlying sandy silt soils encountered at a depth of about 15 feet below
the existing ground surface have a relatively rapid absorption rate with
measured in situ permeability values ranging from 6x107 ft s to
1x1074 ft s Four 4 out of these six 6 percolation tests performed
give measured permeability values in the range of 3x10 to 1x104
ft s upper bound while the remaining two 2 give low measured
permeability values in order of 1x10 ft s lower bound The lower
permeability values measured in the small diameter boreholes could be due
to less permeable in situ soil conditions or subsequently disturbed
smeared soil conditions due to auger drilling method used or other test
procedures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 8
It is our recommendations that the proposed seepage pits must
penetrate sufficiently into the underlying sandy silt soils to be
effective However it should be noted that these permeable soils were
generally encountered below the groundwater level at an approximate depth
of 16 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface which will make the
pit excavation and installation difficult
Based on the methods given in Public Health Service Publication No
526 and the measured permeability values it appears that approximately
150 square feet of sidewall absorption area in the sandy silty horizon
will be required for the average 600 gallons per day of wastewater
generated by a typical 3 bedroom house unit
It should be noted that the sidewall absorption area is derived only
from the portion of the pit below the effective absorption zone of sandy
silt soils encountered at an approximate depth of 15 to 16 feet below the
existing ground surface
The sidewall area is a function of the diameter and depth of the
seepage pit Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion
that generally a seepage pit having a diameter of six 6 feet would have
to extend about twenty four 24 feet below the existing ground surface or
about eight feet below the water level to have sufficient sidewall area to
absorb 600 gallons per day Due to the encountered water level at and
below the permeable sandy silt zone it is anticipated that pit excavation
within this underlying zone will be difficult and to prevent ground
movement into the excavation would require the use of rings and gravel
backfill To minimize deep excavation below water level two 2 6 foot
diameter pits each to be excavated to depths of about twenty 20 feet
below the existing ground surface would provide equivalent sidewall
absorption area for 600 gallons of effluence disposal It should be noted
that the sidewall absorption area is derived only from the portion of the
pit below the effective absorption zone of sandy silt soils encountered at
an approximate depth of 15 feet below the existing ground surface

It is our understanding that the current practice is not to line
cesspools or seepage pits in the Kilauea area and that this practice has
been acceptable to the local offices of the Department of Health Our
previous experiences in the Kilauea and Princeville areas indicate that
the soils in this region tend to have lateritic properties i e the
soils tend to harden when exposed Based on the above it is our opinion
that continuation of the existing local practice of not lining the upper
15 foot of the cesspools or seepage pits maybe acceptable for the proposed
seepage pits at the project site if some localized sloughing of this
portion of the seepage pit walls is tolerable However protective rigs
and gravel backfill behind the installed precast concrete ring would be
required within the sandy soil below the water level
Site Grading
The proposed development is in the conceptual stage and grading plans
are not available However minimal cuts and fills are anticipated for
the project site grading The following grading recommendations are
presented as guidelines for the design of the project site grading
Fill Compaction
Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area most of
the subsoils within the top 15 feet had in situ moisture contents of
about 35 to 55 per cent at the time of our exploration These
moisture contents are considerably higher than the anticipated
optimm moisture contents which range from about 32 to 36 per cent
for similar soils in northern Kauai Therefore considerable drying
of the excavated materials would normally be required prior to their
use as fill materials
However in view of the relatively frequent rainfall that generally
occurs in the area throughout the year drying and placing of large
high quantities of high moisture borrow fill soils might not be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 10
readily achieved during mass grading operations We believe some
amount of drying or aerations of the moist soil would be required
prior to compaction of each fill lift Even if the required drying
of the fill soils is carried out within several hours of each grading
day we believe the normal 90 ASTM D 1557 78 compaction standard for
general site fills as used in drier areas may also have to be reduced
to 85 for this project Our previous experience in other similar
areas indicates that 85 compaction is generally adequate for the
normal site fills and light building loads It is recommended that
site filling should be initiated first and the fill settlements be
observed by settlement gauges to determine when building construction
can commence
Fills within six 6 inches below roadway or footing subgrade should
be compacted to a minimm of 90 of its ASTM D 1557 78 maximum dry
density
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Topsoil should be
stripped and stockpiled for later use in landscaping Any unsuitable
materials such as debris rubbish vegetation and other deleterious
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
The area to receive fill should be scarified to a minimm depth of 6
inches below the cleared grade level air dried if necessary and
recompacted to the above recommended minimum of 85 of its maximum
dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The ground surface below construction haul roads pavements or
footings should be scarified to a minimum depth of six 6 inches

Fill Placement
Based on the field exploration and laboratory test results it is our
opinion that the on site soils have low expansion potential and may
be used as a source of fill and backfill materials If additional
off site borrow is required the material should be tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site to
see that it is suitable for the proposed construction The material
should generally be non expansive and should not contain large
percentages of boulders organic or deleterious matters
On site borrow or off site fills should be placed in level horizontal
lifts not exceeding twelve inches 12 in loose thickness and
compacted to the level recommended above in Fill Compaction
Backfills should be placed in level horizontal lifts not exceeding
eight inches 8 in loose thickness and compacted as recommended
above
Fills to be placed on slopes steeper than five horizontal to one
vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched to properly bond the new
fill to the existing ground See Plate 3 for details
Cut and fill slopes should be designed with slope ratios of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Slope planting should
be provided as soon as practicable to minimize erosion

Foundations
Based on the soil conditions encountered in our exploration we
believe that spread and continuous footing foundations bearing on the
recompacted in situ subsoils may be used to support the proposed
residential structures
For preliminary foundation design an allowable bearing value of up
to 2 000 p s f with a minimum footing embedment of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade may be used The bearing value is for total dead
plus live load only and may be increased by one third for short duration
wind or seismic loading For the interior post footing foundation
located at the middle of the post and beam design residential structures
our previous experience has indicated that unless the footing is buried
very deep below the ground surface there is a high probability that the
near ground surface soil will experience shrinkage or ground settlement
due to the soil moisture loss under a protective shelter environment such
as those under the post and beam type of residential structure
Therefore we believe that the post footing extend down to a minimm
depth of about 2 feet or to minimize excessive footing settlements to
could occur in the future due to ground soil shrinkage It might be
necessary to establish or some provision to accommodate and to re level
for these footing movements particularly those in the middle portion of
the unit It should be pointed out in the future sale document to the
homeowner about the need to make the necessary adjustment under the
structural floor level Alternatively slab on grade with deep perimeter
thickened edge should be considered
For buildings to be constructed on or adjacent to slopes the
building should be set back or the depth of the footing embedment extended
so that there is a minimm of ten 10 feet between the toe of the footing
and the outside slope face
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 13
If a footing foundation is located next to a utility trench the
footing should be extended to the bottom of the utility trench or be
designed to span over the trench to reduce footing settlement due to
settlement of the trench backfill
Based on the boring data estimated total footing settlements under
the design loads are anticipated to be on the order of less than one half
1 2 of an inch differential Shrinkage of near surface soil layer would
provide an additional footing settlement if the footings are not properly
embedded
Resistance to lateral loads on the proposed structure may be provided
by passive soil pressure and frictional resistance A frictional
coefficient of 0 4 may be used to compute the frictional resistance
between the bottoms of the footings and the recompacted in situ soils In
addition an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth may
be used against the buried vertical faces of the footings to estimate the
passive soil pressure
Slabs On Grade
Slabs on grade construction with 4 inch concrete slabs placed over a
4 inch cushion layer of 3B Fine gravel may be used for the proposed
residential structures The thickened edges of the on grade slabs should
extend a minimum of eighteen 18 inches below the lowest outside adjacent
ground surface For slabs located adjacent to slopes the building should
be set back or the depth of embedment extended down so that there is a
minimum of ten 10 feet between the toe of the thickened edge and the
outside slope face
To protect against moisture damage of the floor covering material a
moisture barrier should be considered for use below the slabs
Our laboratory test results indicate that the moist on site soils in
general have low swelling potential at their natural moisture contents
Due to the high natural moisture content these soils exhibit a tendency
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 14
to shrink when allowed to dry Therefore the slab thickened edge should
be properly embedded below the ground surface along the building perimeter
and the subgrade soils below the slab should not be allowed to dry out
after concrete placement Landscaping and plantings should be away from
footing and slab perimeters to minimize the potential of moisture loss or
root growth in this building area
Utilities
Underground utilities are anticipated for this development It is
anticipated that most trenching will be in medium stiff residual or
saprolite soils
Our exploration encountered groundwater at depths of about 15 feet
below the existing ground surface Caution should be exercised in deep
excavations and particularly extending to depths of 15 feet or more where
the underlying soils are sandy and saturated Based on our percolation
testing data we anticipate that it will be relatively difficult to
dewater these excavations Interlocking sheetpiles extending to
sufficient depths below the future excavation level would be required to
provide the required shoring system
On site soils are generally suitable for trench backfill and should
be compacted as recommended in the FILL PLACEMENT section Mechanical
compaction is recommended as the on site fine grained soils are not
conducive to water tamping or jetting
Pavements

the project roads and driveways

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as
borings Variations of conditions between the field data points may
occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The locations of the field data points were determined by measurement
from reference points staked in the field by the client owner Elevations
of the data points were not available at the time of our exploration The
physical location and elevation of the field data points should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross section are based upon interpolation between field data
points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points were collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation in
water level may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature
and other factors
