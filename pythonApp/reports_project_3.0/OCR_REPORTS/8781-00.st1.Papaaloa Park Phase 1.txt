Our borings generally encountered volcanic ash and residual soils underlain by

ground surface. Residual soils generally consisted of stiff to very stiff clayey silts.  In some 
areas, residual soils extended down to the maximum depth explored of 31.5 feet below

basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should 
be expected during construction.  We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled 
at the time of our field exploration.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Papaaloa Park Phase 1 Development project in the District of North 
Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field 
exploration and laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations 
derived from our engineering analyses for the design of the proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of foundations, 
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, underground utility lines, and 
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project is part of a master planned development for the existing Papaaloa Park 
in the District of North Hilo on the Island of Hawaii to address the current and long-term 
recreational needs of the community. A new covered play court with restroom facilities, 
relocated open field, parking areas, and lighting are planned for Phase 1 of the 
development.  The new restroom facilities require an individual wastewater system (IWS) 
consisting of a septic tank and a leach field.  In addition, underground utility lines, 
stormwater disposal systems, and other associated improvements will be required for the 
project. 
Based on the plans provided, only minor site grading consisting of cuts and fills of 
less than about 1 to 2 feet may be required to achieve the design finished grades.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, 
which makes up the north-central portion of the island. Based on available geologic 
information and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain 
by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were 
deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in the Kohala and Mauna Kea 
volcanoes, activity from the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over 
much of the island. This ash has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified 
by low in-situ densities and high field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field 
moisture content is sufficiently high, the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the 
soil temporarily loses strength when remolded and regains its strength after internal pore 
pressures dissipate). Our exploration indicates that the site is underlain by a surface 
mantle of this volcanic ash soil, which should transition to residual soils, basalt formation 
and clinker at a deeper depth. 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe 
basalt type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava is 
typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged 
accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a denser and 
more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. Pahoehoe lava is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of 
vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas. Cavities 
form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes (intra-flow 
cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when molten lava

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is at the existing Papaaloa Park in the District of North Hilo on the

Subsurface Conditions

Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to depths of about 9.5 to 31.5 feet below the existing

2.4 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement 
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can 
only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong 
enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma 
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related 
to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes 
with increased distance from the active volcanoes located in the southern portion of the 
Island of Hawaii. 
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 6 (M6+), including the May 4, 2018 earthquake. Based on information obtained 
from the United States Geological Survey (USGS) Bulletin 2006, the following is a list of 
some destructive earthquakes that have occurred on the Island of Hawaii since 1868.

3.1 
Foundations 
A single-story covered play court is planned for the project. Based on the 
subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using shallow spread 
and/or continuous strip footings to support the new building planned. As an alternative, 
thickened edge slab footings may be considered in supporting the new structure planned 
for the project. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on recompacted on-site soils. This bearing value 
is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) when 
considering transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent exterior grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or 
on slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured 
from the outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is not less than 
6 feet.

Foundations located next to retaining walls, other foundations, utility trenches, or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the utility trench (or retaining wall). Alternatively, the footings 
should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines or the bottom of

at the footing subgrades, the footing subgrades should be over-excavated until firm 
materials are encountered and backfilled with compacted select borrow subbase 
materials (3-inch minus granular materials). The select borrow subbase material layer 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level 
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than 
1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials 
may be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing material and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. The coefficient 
of friction of 0.30 may be used for footings bearing on recompacted on-site soils. 
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid 
pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf), assuming the soils around 
the footings are well-compacted. The passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches 
of the soil should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions 
and the required embedment depths.

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the 
new building. For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. 
The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to 
serve as a capillary moisture break. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above 
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum 
of 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase materials (3-inch minus granular 
materials) should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. 
The select borrow subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The impervious moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs. 
In addition, exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork will be required for the 
project. Generally, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer of 
open-graded 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or select borrow subbase 
below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic. Select borrow 
subbase, where utilized, should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, 
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways (or 
slabs) with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

3.3.4 Drainage 
In general, retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 
12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) 
at the base of the retaining wall and along the length of the wall. The perforated pipe 
should discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.

the retaining wall. Unless covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete, the upper 
12 inches of the backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials, such as 
well-graded soils, to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the 
walls.

It should be noted that the project is located in a high rainfall environment throughout

discussed in the section below. 
3.4.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the volcanic ash soils present near the ground surface have very high 
moisture contents and have potentially thixotropic properties.  Therefore, 
near-surface volcanic ash soils should not be used as a source of fill or backfill 
material due to the potential difficulty in handling and compacting the soils. The 
residual soils present at deeper depths may be reused as a source of general fill 
or backfill materials, provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, 
and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension.

3.4.4 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by volcanic 
ash and residual soils over basalt formation. It is anticipated that the volcanic ash 
and residual soils may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation 
equipment, such as excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers. However, deeper 
excavations may encounter dense basalt formation and will likely require the use 
of hoerams, chipping or blasting. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our visual observation of the field data from the borings. Contractors 
should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to 
make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation.

3.7 
Permeability Testing 
Two head permeability tests were conducted in selected drilled borings to provide 
data for the design of the stormwater disposal system planned at the project site. The 
permeability tests were performed at a depth of about 10 feet below the ground surface. 
The permeability test results are presented in Appendix C. 
Based on our field permeability tests, the subsurface materials generally have 
coefficients of permeability (k) of 1.1 x 10-4 to 3.3 x 10-4 feet per minute at the locations 
tested. Based on the field permeability test results, we believe an average hydraulic 
conductivity of about 0.0001 feet per minute may be assumed for the subsurface 
materials encountered at a depth of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
hydraulic conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 1 gallon 
per day per square foot of surface area at those locations. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is 
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids/fractures in the lava or 
the presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal capacity of 
the seepage pits and drywells. In general, the greater the number of clinker seams and/or 
voids/fractures encountered at the seepage pits and drywells, the higher the overall 
permeability and disposal capacity.  We recommend that the stormwater disposal system 
be tested during construction to confirm design capacities.

3.8 
Percolation Testing 
Two percolation tests were performed in selected borings in general accordance 
with the procedures outlined by the Department of Health for the design of IWS. 
The percolation tests were performed in our borings at a depth of about 5 feet from 
the existing ground surface. Approximately 1 inch of gravel was placed at the bottom of 
the boring to protect the bottom from scouring and sediments. 
During testing, the test holes were filled with water and refilled as necessary. The 
time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the 
calculation of the percolation rates was based. 
Based on the percolation tests conducted, the percolation rate at locations tested 
are about 60 to 240 minutes per inch at the test elevations. In accordance with the 
"Manual of Septic-Tank Practice" prepared by the U.S. Department of Health, Education, 
and Welfare, the leach field location with the percolation rate of 60 minutes per inch 
obtained at the site would be suitable for siting of the absorption system. The percolation 
test results are presented in Appendix C.

Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from 
the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. In addition, it is advised 
to install roof gutter systems around the building and to divert the discharge away from 
the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and 
slabs also should be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have 
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into 
the subsurface, especially in subsurface conditions consisting of fill and volcanic ash 
materials. 
The foundation excavations should be backfilled properly against the walls or slab 
edges immediately after setting the concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water 
infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon 
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the 
foundations and slabs.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
nine borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 9, extending to depths of about 9.5 to 
31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-9. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Rock materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description 
System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is 
based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of 
Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 
1977).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Five Triaxial Unconsolidated Undrained Compression (TXUU) tests were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples in accordance with ASTM D2850 to evaluate 
the undrained shear strength of the soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed 
samples. The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 
through B-8. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Two Moisture-Density Relationship tests (ASTM D1557 A and B) were performed 
on bulk samples obtained to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and 
the dry density of the near-surface soils as fill materials. The test results are presented 
on Plates B-11 and B-12. 
Two sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by Geolabs and CERCO Analytical, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-13.
