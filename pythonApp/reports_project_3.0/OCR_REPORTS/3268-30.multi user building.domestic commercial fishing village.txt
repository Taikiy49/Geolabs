REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu The coastal plain
was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern two thirds
of the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium
derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits
GEOLABS INC
W O 3268 30 Page 5
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below
the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower therefore valleys were carved to depths below the present sea
level When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that
sediments accumulated at higher elevations
The project site is generally underlain by deposits of calcareous sediments alluvial
soil deposits and lagoonal deposits associated with the geologic development of Honolulu
Harbor A surficial layer of man made fill was placed over these soil deposits to reclaim
the land from Honolulu Harbor within the last century The entire Domestic Commercial
Fishing Village project is situated on man made fills placed to reclaim the land and extend
the shoreline further seaward

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 The borings were drilled to depths of
about 28 5 to 33 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of
the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field exploration our borings generally encountered a layer of surficial
fills consisting of a mixture of medium dense to dense gravelly soils and very stiff to hard
clayey soils The surface fills varied in thickness from about 8 feet near the Nimitz
Highway side of the project site to about 23 feet near the waterfront side of the project site
The surficial fills near the waterfront side of the project site contained varying amounts of
cobbles and boulders It should be noted that a black oily substance with hydrocarbon
odor was encountered in Boring Nos 1 and 2 between depths of about 1 and 3 feet below
the existing ground surface Strong hydrocarbon odors were noted in Boring No 3
between depths of about 3 and 6 feet below the existing ground surface
The surficial fill was underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft clayey
soils extending to depths of about 21 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface The
GEOLABS INC
W O 3268 30 Page 7
lagoonal deposits were underlain by coral detritus deposits and alluvial soil deposits
extending to the maximum depths explored of about 33 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The coral detritus deposits consisted of medium dense silty coral gravels and the
alluvial soil deposits consisted of stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts
Relatively undisturbed samples of the lagoonal deposits soft clays and silts were
obtained using a thin wall Shelby tube sampler A consolidation test was performed on
a relatively undisturbed sample of the lagoonal deposits and the results are presented
on Plate B 3 of Appendix B The consolidation test indicates that the soft lagoonal
deposits are normally consolidated to slightly over consolidated therefore the soft
lagoonal deposits may be considered highly compressible when subjected to loading
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 3 5 to 7 feet below the ground
surface at the time of our field exploration The groundwater levels measured correspond
to between about Elevations 0 5 and 0 9 feet MLLW Considering the proximity of the
site to Honolulu Harbor groundwater levels will likely fluctuate in response to the changes
in the tides It should also be noted that groundwater levels are expected to vary with
seasonal rainfall and time of the year

Based on the existing ground elevations and the design finished grades footings

borings and the new fills placed to raise the site to the grades shown on the infrastructure

reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrade elevations

over excavation to remove the soft and or loose materials should be extended until firm

materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In lieu of placing compacted select granular
fill in the over excavation the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on the
underlying competent material

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that the new floor slabs for the Multi User Building will be supported on
the existing fill or new fill materials to be placed at the site In general we envision that the
fill materials supporting the new slabs on grades will consist of granular materials After
proper preparation of the subgrade soils we recommend that the concrete slabs on grade
be supported on a minimum 4 inch layer of cushion fill for slabs on grade with
moisture sensitive surfacing The cushion fill should consist of open graded gravels such
as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for more uniform support of the slab
and to serve as a capillary break To reduce the potential for moisture infiltration through the
slab concrete a plastic vapor membrane should be provided below the slab Two inches of
sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide for protection of the
membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of fine sand
above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer
If the slabs on grade will be subjected to vehicular traffic the floor slabs should be
designed as a rigid pavement For slabs subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that
the slabs be supported on a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase to support the slabs
subjected to the dynamic loads due to the vehicles The aggregate subbase should consist
of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction For
design of structural floor slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square
inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted aggregate subbase We
recommend that the concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer for the intended
loads Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be
subjected to vehicular traffic

corrosion potential including pH and minimum resistivity The minimum resistivity value
of the soil sample is approximately 500 ohm cm which is indicative of a highly corrosive
environment to steel and metals in contact with the soils We recommend that metallic
underground utilities be properly designed to be protected against potential corrosion
considering the highly corrosive environment
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A

APPENDIXA
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of the six borings extended to about 28 5 to 33 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some relatively undisturbed samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft soil deposits were retrieved by
hydraulically pushing a 3 inch OD thin wall Shelby tube into the soil formation The hydraulic
pressures needed to advance the sampler are recorded as the Penetration Resistance in
pounds per square inch psi per foot of penetration on the Logs of Borings
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected cohesive
soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample The torvane shear test provides a quick
estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil Results of the pocket penetrometer and
torvane shear tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the hard boulders encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
