SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a relatively thin layer of surface fill 
material and loose lagoonal deposit over coralline reef formation extending to a depth 
explored of about 28 feet below the ground surface near the Lift Station 10. The fill material 
generally consisted of about 7.5 feet of dense gravelly sandy and gravelly soils. The 
lagoonal deposit consisted of very loose silty sand to a depth of about 23.5 feet below the 
ground surface. Groundwater was encountered at approximately 4.3 feet below ground 
surface. 
Our field exploration generally encountered a relatively thin layer of surface fill 
material and loose lagoonal deposit over coralline reef formation extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 33 feet below the ground surface near the Lift Station 12. The fill 
material generally consisted of about 6 feet of dense sandy and silty soils. Below the fill 
materials, the boring encountered very loose lagoonal material to a depth of about 26 feet 
below the ground surface. Groundwater was encountered at approximately 6 feet below 
ground surface. 
The loose lagoonal deposits are susceptible to liquefaction during a moderate 
earthquake event. Appreciable ground settlements on the order of about 5 to 8 inches may 
be anticipated during a moderate earthquake (Magnitude 6+) at the Lift Station 10 and 12 
locations, respectively. However, the liquefaction is not a design consideration for this 
project. 
We envision that the proposed standby generators and automatic transfer switch 
boxes will be supported on shallow slab-on-grade foundations with thickened-edges. Due 
to the presence of loose compressible lagoonal materials beneath the proposed standby 
generator pads, we anticipate ground settlement may occur under the slab-on-grade 
foundations. Based on our engineering analysis, ground settlement on the order of about 
2 to 3 inches may be anticipated under a uniform load pressure of about 1,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf). We anticipate most of the ground settlement may occur relatively quick 
and stabilize by the end of construction and equipment installation. 
In general, the near-surface sandy and gravelly soils encountered during our field 
exploration should be suitable for use as general fill materials provided that the maximum 
particle size is less than 3 inches in largest dimension. The on-site materials generated from 
the excavations may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials, provided they 
are screened of the over-sized materials and/or processed to meet the above gradation 
requirements (less than 3 inches in largest dimension).

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned 
with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch.  The Koolau Volcanic 
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows. 
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials 
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the Pearl Harbor area.  The 
sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 
100 feet above the present sea level.   
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits.  Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have 
added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Unified Facilities Criteria (UFC) 1-200-01, General Building Requirements, 
indicates that the current code in effect is the International Building Code, 2015 Edition 
(IBC 2015). Based on the IBC 2015, the project site may be subjected to seismic activity; 
therefore, seismic design considerations will need to be addressed.  
2.1.1 Earthquake and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity; 
therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of 
magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they 
can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes 
are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) 
are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground 
shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of 
Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); 
however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in the 
County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and 
Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small landslides occurred on 
the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (M6.8). Some houses 
on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake 
of 1871 (M7+).

2.1.2 Liquefaction 
Based on the 2015 International Building Code, the project site may be subject to 
seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be 
evaluated.  
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil 
shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, 
the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical 
movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands 
and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

3.1 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We envision that the proposed standby generators and automatic transfer switch 
boxes will be supported on shallow slabs-on-grade foundations with thickened-edges. We 
envision that the slabs-on-grade will be supported on recompacted fill materials near the 
existing ground surface. 
Due to the presence of loose compressible coralline materials, we anticipate 
ground settlement may occur under the slabs-on-grade foundations. Based on our 
engineering analysis, ground settlement on the order of about 2 to 3 inches may be 
anticipated under a uniform load pressure of about 1,000 pounds per square foot (psf). 
We anticipate the majority of the ground settlement may occur relatively quick and 
stabilize by the end of construction and equipment installation. Please note that these 
ground settlement values are in addition to liquefaction induced settlement caused by a 
moderate earthquake. 
It should be noted that our settlement analysis was performed based on the 
assumption of the uniform load pressure of 1,000 psf applied on a concrete slab 
dimension of 5 feet by 10 feet. The evaluation should be validated after detailed 
slab-on-grade design is available. 
We recommend increasing the slab thickness to a minimum of 5 inches and 
incorporating steel reinforcement into the concrete slab (No. 4 reinforcing bars spaced at 
12 inches each direction) to reduce the potential adverse effects on the slabs-on-grade 
resulting from ground settlement. 
The thickened edges of the slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded 
at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade.  It should be emphasized that the 
areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with 
low expansion potential, relatively impervious soils to reduce the potential for water 
collecting below the footings and slabs. These areas should also be graded to divert water

away from the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the footings

To provide a uniform support, we recommend properly preparing the 
slabs-on-grade subgrades by scarifying to a depth of about 12 inches, moisture

minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where shrinkage cracks are noted after

cracks or preparing the subgrades again. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the 
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require 
over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. 
New fills needed to raise the project site to the finished subgrades will induce some 
ground settlements due to compression of the underlying loose lagoonal materials. 
Therefore, we recommend placing the new fills as soon as practical to allow the 
anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to slabs-on-grade construction. 
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), 
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a 
capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through 
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is 
recommended on top of the gravel cushion fill layer. Flexible floor coverings, such as 
carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered because they can better mask minor slab 
cracking. 
Where the slabs-on-grade will be subjected to equipment vibration and/or vehicular 
traffic, we recommend placing the floor slab over 6 inches of aggregate subbase in lieu 
of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Where 
slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches 
may be used for preliminary design purposes. Provisions should be made for proper load 
transfer across the slab joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic.

and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive, 
select granular material should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks developing in the 
slabs, crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the 
walkways with expansion joints provided at right-angle intersections. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials.  The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in its largest dimension.  The material 
should also contain between 10 and 30 percent fines (particles passing the No. 200 
sieve).  Select granular fill should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

 Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, and other unsuitable materials 
should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials designated to be reused as fill and/or 
backfill. Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during site preparation operations 
should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with fill materials (well-graded 3-inch minus 
materials) compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrades due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
compacted fill material. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly 
disposed off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface should be scarified to a depth 
of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  For pavement subgrades, the 
compaction requirement should be a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two exploratory 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 28 to 33 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid stem augers and PQ coring. The borings were drilled at 
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil and samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One one-inch ring swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under different surcharge loads.  
The results of the test are summarized on Plate B-1. 
Four sieve analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136), including two hydrometer tests 
(ASTM D422) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.  Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-2. 
One direct shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. Results of the direct 
shear test are presented on Plate B-3. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. Results of the CBR test were also used to evaluate the quality of the materials 
tested for use as fill. The test results are presented on Plate B-6.
