SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered alluvial deposits over basalt rock and
clinker materials with varying degrees of weathering The alluvial deposits generally
consist of stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts with some boulders and cobbles The
underlying basalt rock was generally medium hard to hard and the clinker materials were
generally dense Residual and saprolitic soils were also encountered below the alluvial
deposits Soft soils extending to depths of about 2 to 5 feet below the existing ground
surface were encountered in several of the borings drilled We encountered groundwater
at depths of about 4 6 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface in three of the borings
drilled
Based on the generally competent subsurface soil condition encountered during
our field exploration the Wal Mart store building and the smaller retail buildings may be
supported on a shallow foundation system However based on the proposed finished floor
elevation and the available topographic map we understand that the Wal Mart store
building will be situated over both cut and fill areas Therefore special attention should be
given to the potential for differential settlement across the Wal Mart store building site To
reduce the potential for differential settlement at the Wal Mart store building we
recommend placing a minimum 24 inch thick layer of non expansive select granular fill
material below the building foundations situated in cut conditions The select granular fill
should also extend a minimum of 24 inches beyond the perimeter of the foundations
Based on the above recommendations an allowable bearing pressure of up to
may be used for design of building foundations bearing on the recompacted on site soils
or compacted select granular fill material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind
and or seismic forces
The near surface soils at the site generally consist of silty clay and clayey silt soils
with low to high potential for shrinking and swelling Therefore the near surface soils are
generally susceptible to shrinking and swelling when subjected to changes in the soil
moisture contents Unless the slabs on grade that are constructed above these types of
expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly
loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to
changes in the moisture content As a result we recommend providing a minimum of
24 inches of non expansive select granular fill material below the Wal Mart store building
and smaller retail buildings slabs The text of this report should be referred to for more
detailed discussions and specific design recommendations

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava
flows from Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau
now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern
flank of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness
of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1
Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern
Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the
present Within portions of the caprock wedge are volcanic deposits from late stage
post erosional vents of Waianae such as Puu Makakilo and Puu Kapolei In the region
of Puu Kapolei some of these deposits occur at very shallow depths
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to continental
glaciation cycles As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve

2 2 Climate
The Island of Oahu lies within in the tropics in the Pacific Ocean and is
characterized by mild temperatures throughout the year moderate humidity persistent
northeasterly trade winds significant differences in rainfall within short distances and
infrequent severe storms
The Island of Oahu is considered semitropical rather than tropical where the
temperature of the surrounding waters is about 10 degrees lower than that of some
other regions of equal latitude The temperatures on the Island of Oahu typically range
between 52 degrees and 90 degrees Fahrenheit The lowest temperature varies with
altitude Typically there is an average decrease of about 4 degrees in the mean
temperature with each increase of 1 000 feet in altitude
Trade winds northeasterly winds are persistent on the Island of Oahu
throughout most of the year Wind velocities at Honolulu average about 10 miles per
hour mph However Kona winds southerly winds occasionally may interrupt the
trade winds but for no more than a few days at a time The Kona winds tend to be
associated with high humidity The relative humidity may reach 95 percent on days
where Kona winds are present
The rainy season on Oahu begins around October and may extend up to April
The rainfall in the leeward areas on the Island of Oahu may range between 10 to
20 inches annually The leeward areas obtain their rainfall mainly from a few winter
storms The precipitation at the project site may be considered low

Earthquake and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that only sensitive instruments can detect them However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of

Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not
confined can result in significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine
grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to
liquefaction Generally liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is
deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface The greater overburden pressure
can be sufficient to prevent liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered the phenomenon of soil
liquefaction is not a design consideration for this project site The risk for potential
liquefaction is non existent at this project site based on the subsurface conditions
cohesive encountered at the site

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling a total of 75 borings The borings extended to depths of about 9 5 to 100 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate boring and bulk sample locations
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer and geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend
Plate A O1 Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 75
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Thirteen one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under a
surcharge pressure of 55 psf The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
Three sets of corrosion tests including Minimum Resistivity
ASTM G 57 Chloride Content and Sulfate Content
were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field
exploration The test results are summarized on Plate B 3
Four moisture ash and organic matter of soils tests ASTM D 2974 were
performed on selected soil samples The test results are summarized on Plate B 4
Ten Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 5
Three Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on
Plate B 6
Five Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Direct Shear test
results are presented on Plates B 7 through B 11
Two Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics The test results
along with the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 12 and B 13
Two Modified Proctor Compaction tests ASTM D 1557 A were performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 14 and B 15

During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top
of the hole The time intervals and water drops in the borehole were then recorded to
provide data upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface
soils Field permeability test are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4
