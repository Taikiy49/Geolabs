in general accordance with our proposal of June 13th, 1986.

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The GIE Mobilnet network of the island of Oahu includes several cell
sites which together will create a mobile communication network.
The facility at Wahiawa includes a new 12 by 20 feet by 10 feet high
prefabricated building placed on a permanent foundation, and a 190-foot
high self-supporting tower. It is our understanding that the tower will
be designed for the following base reactions:
Total Weight " 31.6 Kips
Moment - 4159.5 Kip/Ft.
Maximum Shear - 43.9 Kips Total
Maximum Compression - 228.9 Kips Per Leg
Maximum Uplift 207.8 Kips Per Leg
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the high ground area of the U.S.
Naval Station at Wahiawa on the island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general site
is an open grassed area, presently unoccupied. An existing unpaved dirt
road passes the northern part of the site.
Topographically, the site is high on a ridge with ground elevation
of about 1240 feet above Mean Sea Level. However, the tower and building
locations are in general flat. The distance from the proposed
construction to the nearest slope varies from about 20 to 70 feet.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Two borings to depths of 30 and 50 feet below the existing ground
surface were drilled at two of the three tower-leg locations indicated on
the Site Plan, Plate 2. In general, the site is underlain by stiff to
very stiff reddish brown clayey silt. However, at a depth of about 6 feet
from the ground surface, our borings encountered a 7-foot layer of medium
to soft soil.
Groundwater was not encountered during the course of our drilling
operation.
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATION
General
Despite the existence of a medium stiff to soft soil layer, we
believe that the soil conditions at the site are suitable for the support
of the proposed structures. Because of the anticipated very large base
reactions, we recommend that drilled piers be used to support the tower
structure. For the building, shallow spread footing foundation may be
used.
Tower Foundation
Due to the very high compression load (228.9 kips per leg), uplift
load (207.8 kips per leg) and moment (4159.5 kip/ft.) requirement for the
proposed tower structure, we would recommend that a large diameter drilled
pier be used as foundation support. The loading capacities of the drilled
pier would be derived mainly from the frictional resistance between the
shaft of the pier and the stiff in-situ soil.
With the use of one 42-inch diameter drilled pier for each leg, we
estimated that the pier should penetrate 45 feet into the existing ground
in order to generate sufficient uplift and bearing resistance to fulfill
the above mentioned requirement with a factor of safety of 2.5.

Drilled Pier Construction
The drilled pier construction should be closely monitored by the
soil engineer in order to see that adequate foundation support is
achieved. Since the majority of the loading capacities come from the
frictional resistance between the shaft and the in-situ soil, extra care
should be applied to the drilling of the pier hole, selection of concrete
mix design and the placement of concrete.
GEOLABS-HAWAII
W.O. 1703-30 Page 5

Drilling fluids should be avoided as much as practical, as they may
saturate and weaken the silty to clayey material. Therefore, the holes
should be advanced by auger drilling.
Prompt placement of concrete in the prepared hole, as soon as it is
completed, is recommended to reduce caving in of the hole. The concrete
mix should be designed to minimize shrinkage in order to maintain high
frictional contact value between the in-situ soil and pier concrete. The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to avoid segregation of the
aggregates.
Building Foundation
Shallow spread footing may be used for the one-story building
located to the west of the tower structure. An allowable bearing capacity
of 3000 p.s.f. can be used for the footing bearing on the stiff clayey
silt. The footing should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade. Settlement of the building is estimated to be on
the order of 1/4 inch total and 1/8 inch differential.
Resistance to lateral: loads for the building can be obtained by
passive pressure developed by the buried part of the footing and by
frictional resistance. A frictional coefficient of 0.4 may be used
between the bottom of the footing and stiff soil. In addition, an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p.s.f. per foot of depth may be used to
determine the passive resistance.
Design Review
Plans and specifications for foundation support.of the antenna
should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written comments
prior to construction to see that the intent of our recommendations is
included.

Construction Monitoring
We recommend that the excavations for the building foundation and
the drilling of the pier holes be inspected by the soils engineer prior to
placement of concrete or reinforcing steel to see that adequate bearing
has been obtained and that the subsurface conditions are consistent with
the findings of our subsurface investigation.
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report, then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made.
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon the
above inspection.

made in accordance with generally accepted soil and foundation engineering
principles and practices. This warranty is in lieu of all other

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling two
(2) borings to depths of 30 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. A
truck-mounted rotary drill rig was used to drill the borings.
The soil and rock encountered in the borings were classified by
visual and textural examination in the field by our geologist who
continuously monitored the drilling operations. The classifications were
further reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory. The
soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System. A graphic presentation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Boring Logs, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.1.
Soil samples were obtained from the boring by driving a 2-inch
standard split spoon sampler or a 2.4-inch I.D. split barrel sampler with
a 140-pound hammer free falling 30 inches. The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX Cc
Laterally Loaded Pier Analysis
Because of the anticipated large lateral force and moment acting on
the pier, a laterally loaded pier analysis was performed. The analysis
was carried out by using a finite difference program PILEDG, which was
derived from the program COM624.
The program adopts the P-Y-Curve approach and has the capability to
generate, internally, P-Y curves, given the soil data. Since the lateral
soil modulus varies with the deflection experienced, the computerized
analysis first assumes a modulus value, then calculates the pile
deflection based on the assumed modulus. The calculated deflection is
then used to determine the modulus from the P-Y curves. The calculated
modulus and the assumed modulus are then compared. The procedure is
repeated until the new assumed and calculated moduli are essentially the
same.
The results of the analysis, showing the relationship of lateral
deflection at the top of the pier and the applied lateral force under
different moment conditions, are summarized on Plate C-l. A summary of
maximum moment along the pier is under different boundary forces and
moment conditions is shown on Plate C-2.
A typical set of printout from the program PILEDG is shown on Plates
C=-3 through C-7.
