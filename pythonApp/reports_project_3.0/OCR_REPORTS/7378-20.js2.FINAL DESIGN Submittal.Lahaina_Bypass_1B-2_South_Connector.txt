REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Maui was built by two major shield volcanoes, the older as West 
Maui (Tertiary Epoch) and the younger as East Maui, or Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). 
The broad gently sloping low elevation plain, or Isthmus, in between the two volcanoes 
was formed by the younger Haleakala lavas banking against the older West Maui 
volcano. West Maui is classified as an extinct volcano while Haleakala is regarded as 
dormant, having erupted within the last several hundred years. The Island of Maui is 
actually part of a larger volcanic massif comprising about seven volcanoes and the 
Islands of Maui, Kahoolawe, Lanai and Molokai separated by relatively shallow sea 
depths. 
The West Maui Mountain volcanic deposits are divided into three parts from 
oldest to youngest and are identified as the Wailuku, Honolua and Lahaina Volcanic 
Series. Each volcanic series contains distinct lava types and distribution on West Maui. 
Similarly, Haleakala volcanic deposits are divided into three parts from oldest to 
youngest and are identified as the Honomanu, Kula and Hana Volcanic Series. 
The dominant erosional processes acting on the volcanic island mass is the deep 
incision of streams and subsequent detachment of soil and rock masses from the 
steepened valley bounding walls (mass wasting process). The detached soil and rock 
materials are transported down slope toward the valley axes primarily by gravity as 
colluvium or colluvial deposits. Once these eroded materials reach streams at the 
central portion of a valley, alluvial transport processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are further re-worked, transported and deposited as alluvium or alluvial 
deposits. 
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
streams transmit large volumes of water and entrained material for relatively short 
duration. As a result, the transport of rocks and sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of 
the stream's hydraulic load may consist of a poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, 
gravel, sands, and fine soils. The erosional base level of the island is affected by 
surrounding changes in sea level that occur as a result of the long-term waxing and 
waning of continental glaciation with the passage of geologic time. As continental 
glaciation advances, less water is available in the oceanic basins resulting in a global 
fall of sea level. Conversely when continental glaciation recedes, more water is 
available in the oceanic basins resulting in a global rise of sea level. Changes in sea 
level affect the erosional base level of the island mass. When sea level drops, the

erosional base level also drops and streams incise deeper into the island mass, 
generally resulting in increased erosion and removal of material. When sea level rises, 
the erosional base level also rises and stream deposition at higher elevation occurs 
resulting in greater deposition of materials.  Long-term fluctuation of sea level produces 
repeated periods of deposition and renewed erosion by stream incision which affects 
the type of alluvial/colluvial deposits and the seaward or landward shift in the location of 
material deposition. 
When earth material deposits remain buried or relatively undisturbed for long 
periods of geologic time (typically at lower elevations) chemical processes begin to alter 
the deposits causing internal decomposition or weathering of the materials. Chemical 
alteration processes may also produce induration (cementation) of the mixed rocks and 
sediment into a poorly-consolidated sedimentary rock known as conglomerate. 
Simultaneous to the induration and alteration processes of older earth deposits in the 
lowlands, continued stream incision and erosion in the upland headwaters produce 
additional unconsolidated source material as alluvium and colluvium. These generated 
loose rocks and soft sediments are transported down slope or downstream and may 
cover the older more indurated alluvial/colluvial deposits. Depending on the erosional 
base level condition and rate of transport, these newer sediments are generally 
considered transient in terms of geologic time and may be referred to as recent 
alluvium/colluvium.  Furthermore, their consistency and density are generally less than 
those of the older, semi-consolidated deposits.  
The project site is along the lower elevation foot of the west and southwest facing 
slopes and gulches of the West Maui Mountains in the vicinity of Olowalu. The larger 
drainage gulches in the region have produced extensive outwash fans of 
alluvial/colluvial materials that have coalesced to form the broad gently to moderately 
inclined slopes present today along the foot of the mountains. Thus, the majority of the 
project site appears to be underlain by extensive and variable alluvial and colluvial 
deposits shed from the adjacent uplands. The near-surface alluvial/colluvial deposits 
are believed to be underlain by basalt rock formation at depth.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program for the project consisted of drilling and sampling a 
total of two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 401 and 402, to depths of about 
25.0 and 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. One bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-16, was collected at Boring No. 402 for 
pavement design analysis.  The bulk sample was a composite sample of the 
near-surface soil cuttings generated from a supplemental hole drilled adjacent to Boring 
No. 402.  The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a surface soil layer consisting of sandy silt 
and silty sand ranging from about 1.5 to 3.0 feet thick, followed by an alluvial layer 
containing very dense boulders extending to a depth of about 6 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The boulder layer was generally underlain by loose to medium dense 
gravel, cobbles, and boulders in a silty sand soil matrix. The alluvium layer extended to 
the maximum depth explored of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered in Boring No. 402 at a depth of about 19.8 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. We did not 
encounter groundwater in Boring No. 401 at the time of our field exploration. It should 
be noted that groundwater levels throughout the project alignment may vary with 
seasonal rainfall, tides, time of year, and other factors.

Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials designated to be reused 
as fill and/or backfill. If soft or wet soils are encountered during clearing, 
over-excavation may be required to remove the soft or wet materials to expose 
firm and/or dense soils. The resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with 
compacted fill material. 
Prior to filling, the existing ground exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches (where possible), moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks or preparing the 
subgrade again. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade 
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the 
soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft soils 
may be used as fill, provided that they meet the requirements for fill materials. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557 (AASHTO T-180). 
Where scarification of the subgrade is not possible (especially subgrades of 
cobbles and boulders), we recommend proof-rolling the subgrades with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 15 tons static weight) for a minimum of eight 
passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids. The 
vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during the clearing and 
proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted 
fill materials. The depth of over-excavation should extend until dense underlying 
materials are exposed and should be evaluated by our representative in the field.

Fills and Backfills 
In general, we anticipate the excavations will likely encounter a surface soil layer 
consisting of sandy clay, silty sands, and cobbles and boulders in a sandy silt 
matrix, depending on the depth of the cut. The excavated materials may be used 
as a source of general fill and backfill materials provided that the material 
contains rock fragments less than 6 inches and contains between 10 percent and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. Excavated on-site materials 
consisting of boulders less than 3 feet in maximum dimension may be used as 
rock fill, provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 2 feet below 
the finished subgrade. In general, the placement of boulders in fills should be 
performed in accordance with the procedures recommended in the following 
"Boulder Disposal" subsection. 
Select granular fill materials should be used within the upper 2 feet below the 
finished subgrade to support the pavement structural section. Select granular fill 
materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 
6 inches in largest dimension, have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) 
value of 25 or more with a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less, and contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the 
upper 2 feet of materials below the pavement subgrade should have a pavement 
R-value of 50 or greater to receive the pavement structural section.  
Where R-Values of 50 or greater within the upper 2 feet from finish subgrades 
are not achievable, triaxial geogrids and aggregate subbase material may be 
used to enhance the subgrade R-Value to acceptable levels. We recommend 
placing 6 inches of aggregate subbase over a reinforcing geogrid such as Tensar 
Triax (TX7) Geogrid Grid (or equivalent) on the compacted subgrade. The 
geogrid would provide stabilization and reinforcement to subgrade soils, thus 
enhancing the R-Value of the subgrade soils below the pavement section. 
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to the Standard Specifications 
for the Construction of Roads and Bridges on Federal Highway Projects, FP-14, 
Section 703, dated 2014.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirement of the fills within 3 feet of the finished grades should be increased to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
"Rock" fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the 
majority of the rocks or 3 feet, whichever is less. Placement should be by 
haul-trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the 
currently placed lift. Spreading of the "rock" fill should be by a large bulldozer to 
facilitate "seating" of the rocks. The rock fill should be watered heavily during 
placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and

same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557 (AASHTO T-180) test 
procedures. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight)

Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during 
site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of 
compaction and the proper moisture content. Where compaction is less than 
required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of 
moisture content as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. 
Conventional compaction testing generally is not practical in fills composed of 
rock fragments and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the 
number of passes by a specific compactor needed to achieve the desired level of 
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project 
under the observation of a Geolabs representative. Based on this testing 
program, the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for 
adequate compaction.

Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, alluvium consisting of silty sands, and cobbles 
and boulders is prevalent throughout the proposed South Connector Road 
alignment. Therefore, the contractor will likely encounter some difficult excavation 
conditions during construction where the hard cobbles and boulders are 
encountered. 
We anticipate the cobbles and boulder alluvial layer may be excavated readily 
with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators, and ripped with 
large bulldozers. However, some of the deeper excavations into the alluvial 
formation and in isolated conditions may require the use of hoerams or chipping.

Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, 
the pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should 
be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage 
structures. Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after 
construction. Development of good shoulder drainage along with a program to 
prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly recommended to 
reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of the 
pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 401 and 402, extending to depths of about 
25.0 and 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths.
