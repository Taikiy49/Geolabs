2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai was built up by a single large shield volcano which has
subsequently been deeply eroded and partially covered with later volcanic materials The
Island of Kauai is the oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands and today encompasses
approximately 555 square miles in area with a maximum elevation of about 5 170 feet
above Mean Sea Level It is believed that during the Tertiary Period Pliocene Age
basaltic lavas from the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly circular island
volcano from the depths of the ocean which is estimated to have been about 20 000 feet
deep at the time Near the end of the Pliocene Age this main shield building volcanic
phase came to a halt
Following a long period of erosion and subsidence volcanism was renewed during
the early Pleistocene Age with the Koloa Volcanic Series This post erosional volcanic
activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded sediments over the older Waimea
Canyon Series
After the cessation of this period of renewed volcanism the geologic forces of
weathering erosion and deposition during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs have
shaped the island to its present form The rocks in the vicinity of the project site have
been mapped as Koloa Basalts In general Koloa Basalts are poorly to moderately
permeable The Koloa Basalt located in the vicinity of the project site is believed to have
erupted from nearby vents that appear today as circular shaped cinder and spatter cone
vents These vents are responsible for much of the Koloa Basalt that covers the
southeastern portion of Kauai including the project site

3 1 Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally competent subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that spread and or continuous footings may
be used for support of the proposed new buildings planned for the expansion project An
allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for design of footing
foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ dense clinkers or hard basalt rock formation the
bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted where possible to at least
95 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Soft and or loose materials
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be over excavated to expose
the underlying firm materials The over excavation may be backfilled with on site granular
soils compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom
may be extended down to the underlying competent materials For footings bearing on
new compacted fills the bottom of the footing excavations should also be recompacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades The footing embedment may be reduced to a minimum of
12 inches for interior footings We understand that some of the thickened edges of slabs

abutting existing and or new pavements will function as wall footings We recommend that
the bottom of the thickened edges for these slabs be embedded at least 16 inches below

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the construction of
the new buildings Based on the design finish floor elevations proposed for the new
buildings we envision that the majority of the new structures with the exception of
Building G will rest on the new fills placed to raise the existing ground surface to the
design finish grades However minor cuts into the existing ground may be required for
Building G to achieve the design finish floor elevation Based on the anticipated slab
subgrade conditions consisting of imported general fill material and or in situ clinker rock
formation we recommend that 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill material be
provided below the slab cushion for more uniform support
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be
placed below the slabs to provide the slabs with more uniform support The cushion fill
should consist of open graded gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation which would also serve as a capillary break To reduce the potential for future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture
barrier is recommended on top of the cushion layer Two inches of moist sand may be
considered above the moisture barrier to provide for protection of the moisture barrier and
to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist sand above the
moisture barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer

It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava
or the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of a
drywell In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or voids encountered by
the drywell excavation the higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity
In general drywells are typically 5 feet in diameter and 20 feet in depth The actual
size and depth of the subsurface disposal system will depend on the design quantity of the
future surface runoff Extension of the drywell from the planned 20 foot depth may be
required depending on the specific site conditions at the proposed drywell locations
Therefore we recommend that unit prices for additional drilling be obtained during bidding
Provisions should be incorporated into the construction documents to extend the drywell
depths from the planned 20 feet to about 40 feet below the ground surface using a
minimum 15 inch diameter extension hole or 5 foot diameter drilling equipment The unit
prices for drywell extension should be obtained in 20 foot increments Considering the
highly variable disposal percolation rates of the subsurface conditions at the project site
we strongly recommend testing of all the drywells be performed during construction
In general the type of drilling operation may alter the hydraulic characteristics of
formation materials at the edges of the borehole and may result in significant reduction of
the hydraulic conductivity of the drilled hole We envision that the drilling fluid may seal the
permeable surfaces of the drywell Therefore pressure washing or water jetting to remove
the seal should be included in the specifications if the drywells are to be installed by
drilling The contractor should forward the proposed excavation method and equipment to

3 7 Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed building sites are underlain at
shallow depths by volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard It is anticipated

However excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams
chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based

drilled at the site We recommend that all contractors proposing to bid on this project be

We anticipate that new utilities for the proposed expansion project may consist

Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site
However some of the utility trench excavations will likely encounter the dense to very
dense cobbles and boulders as described in the boring logs which may pose some
excavation difficulties during the trench excavation In addition the contractor should
anticipate significant over break of the sides of the trench excavations considering the
nature of the materials at the site
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for
the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material generally
less than 6 inches in maximum size compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Where trenches will be located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction Where the utility line crosses beneath the footings the utility pipes should be
concrete jacketed
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration it is our opinion that trench work for utility installation may involve excavation
in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard ripping or hoerams Care must be
exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the
rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity
3 10 Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water
infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about 12 to
20 feet below the existing ground surface Two additional borings designated as Boring
Nos P 1 and P 2 were drilled at selected locations for field permeability testing to
evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the in situ materials The approximate locations
of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6
inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

APPENDIX C

The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing one constant
head and one falling head tests at Boring Nos P 1 and P 2 respectively The tests were
performed at depths of about 10 5 and 15 feet below the existing ground surface
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until the
water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to
base the calculation of the subsoil permeability
During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water to a certain
elevation The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon
which to base the calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils The test results of
the field permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
