Improvements project at the Marine Corps Base Hawaii at Kaneohe Bay on the Island of

project. The geotechnical recommendations presented herein are intended for the design 
of foundations only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is at the existing Cove Outdoor Recreation Center and Marina at 
the Marine Corps Base Hawaii (MCBH) at Kaneohe Bay on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
We understand a new wave attenuator system will be designed and constructed near the 
entrance to the existing marina. As part of the marina improvements, we understand the 
project also entails adding two floating finger docks to increase the boat berthing capacity 
and widening two existing boat ramps at the cove. A preliminary project layout of the 
improvements and the new floating dock location are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
A feasibility study was conducted previously to determine if guide piles for the new 
floating docks are capable of providing an economical anchoring system for long-term 
performance. The study concluded that a foundation system consisting of 16.5-inch and 
24-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete piles are capable of resisting the 5 to

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its 
main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which erosion cut valleys deep into 
the Koolau Shield Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by 
cinder and tuff cone formation. These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. 
The MCBH site at Kaneohe Bay is located on the Mokapu Peninsula, which was 
built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. These vents are believed to 
be among the oldest of the series. The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from the vents 
formed salients permitting growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of the 
Island of Oahu. 
An extensive deposit of man-made fills composed of marine deposits overlying 
consolidated calcareous marine sediments cover the southwestern portion of 
Mokapu Peninsula along Kaneohe Bay adjacent to Nuupia Pond. Man-made fills 
consisting of marine sediments dredged from the ocean floor were used to build up the 
area around the southern and western shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula.

subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, 
and other factors.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We understand guide piles will be used to provide lateral support for the proposed 
floating wave attenuator and floating docks. The current plan is to use 16.5-inch octagonal 
or 20-inch square precast, prestressed (PCPS) concrete piles to provide the lateral 
support for the floating dock systems. For the floating wave attenuator with larger lateral 
load demands, we understand 20-inch or 24-inch square PCPS concrete piles will be used 
to support the lateral load demands of the structure.

bending moments induced in the pile. Based on information provided by Moffatt & Nichol,

analyses to determine the estimated pile length of the guide pile for the floating wave 
attenuator and floating dock systems.

The guide pile analyses were accomplished by using the computer program, 
LPILE, to estimate the lateral load, pile deflection and bending moment relationships. The 
program solves for deflection and bending moment along a pile under lateral loads as a 
function of depth. Analyses were carried out with the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to 
represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil 
moduli at various depths.

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the 
structural loading provided, we recommend installing the new guide piles to tip elevations 
also shown in the table above, to provide the necessary lateral load capacity and bearing 
resistance for the self-supporting guide piles.  
We recommend pre-drilling the pile locations to a minimum elevation of about 
+65 feet to facilitate the pile driving operation and to clear the pile driving path from 
obstructions. 
Based on our experience, we recommend using a hammer capable of delivering 
energies in the range of about 50,000 to 70,000 foot-pounds. The hammer should be 
equipped with an energy control level such that driving within the soft and/or loose soils 
could be controlled at a lower hammer energy setting to reduce the potential for tensile 
stress development within the piles, which could damage or cause cracking of the piles. 
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into 
consideration the "matching" of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven. 
This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the 
pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles. Geolabs should review the proposed 
pile driving equipment to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of installing the piles 
to the recommended pile capacity. 
Wave equation analysis should be performed after selection of the pile driving 
equipment to demonstrate that the proposed pile driving hammer and systems are 
capable of driving the piles to the design tip elevations without overstressing or damaging 
the piles.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until additional probing or construction is underway. If 
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations 
provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been taped from 
features shown on the site plan provided by Moffatt & Nichol transmitted to our office on 
February 21, 2013. Elevations of the borings shown on the Logs of Borings were 
determined from interpolating between the spot elevations and contour lines shown in the 
same plan. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four test borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 21.5 to 83.5 feet 
below the floating platform (for off-shore borings) or existing ground surface (for land 
borings). The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We drilled 
the off-shore borings using skid-mounted drilling equipment mounted on a floating 
platform. The land borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and rotary wash drilling/coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous 
(full-time) basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
outside diameter (OD) Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 
30 inches. In addition, we obtained some samples from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The 
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.
