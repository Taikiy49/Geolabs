SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the new aboveground fuel storage tank site generally 
encountered a surface fill layer about 2.5 to 4 feet thick underlain by medium dense to very 
dense dune sand and beach sand extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 17 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was encountered in 
the drilled borings at depths of about 14.3 to 14.9 feet below the existing ground surface at 
the time of our field exploration. These groundwater levels correspond to elevation of 
about -0.8 to +0.6 feet Mean Sea Level. 
Based on the structural loads provided and the subsurface conditions encountered 
in our field exploration, we believe a shallow foundation system consisting of concrete 
slabs-on-grade with continuous thickened-edge footings may be used to support the new 
aboveground fuel storage tank structure. An allowable bearing capacity up to 
2,500 pounds per square foot may be used for footings bearing on the stiff and medium 
dense fill material. To provide more uniform support for the fuel tank structures, we 
recommend supporting fuel tank concrete pad foundations on at least 12 inches of 
compacted structural fill. The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finish grades.  Footings constructed near slopes or on sloping ground 
should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set-back distance of 
5 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face. 
Evaluation of the future excavation and removal of the existing underground tanks 
was performed. Based on the relatively close proximity of the aboveground fuel tank 
embankment, conventional sloping excavation for the tank removal does not appear 
feasible. Therefore, a temporary shoring system such as continuous steel sheetpiling will 
be required for the future removal of the underground tanks.  
We believe that permanent fill slopes constructed of structural fill material may be 
constructed with a design slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  The embankment fill for the 
fuel tank pads should be constructed of structural fill material. Appropriate slope planting 
or other means should be used to protect slopes, as soon as possible, to reduce the 
potential for erosion of the finished slopes. The text of this report should be referred to for 
detailed discussion and special design recommendations.

Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch 
(more than 2-1/4 million years before present).  Following the cessation of this main shield 
building phase, there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of 
the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial 
sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon 
Volcanic Series.  These lavas consist of four distinct formations:  Napali, Olokele, Haupu, 
and Makaweli.  These formations are comprised of thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe flows to 
massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with the 
vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), there were many sea level changes as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Kauai remained 
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to present sea level of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic 
design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.3.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of

2.3.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of 
strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose 
saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the 
soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. 
Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the 
overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and 
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires 
mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not 
confined, will result in significant deformations.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the aboveground fuel 
storage tank site generally consisted of stiff and medium dense fill soils underlain by 
medium dense to very dense dune sand and beach sand deposits extending to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 17 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered about 14.3 to 14.9 feet depths. 
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, 
shallow foundations may be used to support the new fuel tank structures.  In addition, 
an evaluation of the future excavation and removal of the existing underground tanks 
was performed. Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the 
design of foundations, sidewalk slabs-on-grade, and site grading  are further discussed

3.1 
Aboveground Fuel Storage Tank Foundations 
Based on the structural loads provided and the subsurface conditions 
encountered, we believe shallow foundations consisting of concrete slabs-on-grade with 
thickened-edge continuous footings may be used to support the proposed new 
aboveground fuel storage tank structures. To provide more uniform support for the fuel 
tank structures, we recommend the subsurface materials below the bottom of the 
slabs-on-grade foundations for the tank pads be over-excavated a minimum of 1 foot 
and be replaced with compacted structural fill materials. The structural fill materials 
should consist of imported, non-expansive, select granular materials compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.  Prior to the structural fill placement, 
a geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or equal) should be placed on the subgrade to provide 
separation between the sand subgrade. 
For design of foundations bearing on the 1-foot thick structural fill placed over the 
in-situ fill and dune sand soils, we recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to 
2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) be used. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live

avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and 
to reduce the potential for foundation settlement. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, total settlement is estimated to be on the order of 
about one (1) inch with differential settlements less than about one-half (½) inch. 
Footing excavations should be observed by a Geolabs representative prior to 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions 
and the required embedment depths.

Based on the project drawings, we anticipate site grading work for the proposed 
new aboveground fuel storage tank project will consist of fills of up to about 2.5 feet in 
height.  In addition, we anticipate minimal cuts and fills at the pump structure facility. 
Site grading operations should be observed by a Geolabs representative. It is 
important our representative observe the site grading operation to evaluate whether 
undesirable materials are encountered during excavation and whether the exposed 
soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration. 
3.3.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Soft and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to 
receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site or used 
in landscaping areas, if appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas within the proposed aboveground fuel storage 
tank structures should be over-excavated to provide the minimum 1-foot thick 
compacted structural fill layer for more uniform support. Prior to placement of the 
structural fill material, the bottom of the over-excavation should be proof-rolled with 
a sheepsfoot roller a minimum of four passes to detect the presence of near-
surface soft and/or loose zones. The proof-rolling operations should be conducted 
under the observation of a Geolabs representative.  Subsequent to the proof-rolling 
and prior to the placement of the structural fill layer, a geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N 
or equal) should be placed on the subgrade to provided separation between the 
sand subgrade and structural fill material.

3.3.2 Fill and Backfill Materials 
The on-site silty sand soils may be re-used as a source of general fill material, 
provided they meet the requirements for general fills presented in the "Fills and 
Backfill Materials" subsection of this report.  Structural fill material should be used 
for the fuel tank pad embankments.  
General fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed 
finished subgrades (outside the fuel tank structure and pad embankment) may 
consist of on-site excavated silty sand soils, provided that they are free of 
vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension. Additional general fill needed for site filling may consist of 
imported materials with a low to moderate expansion potential. Imported general fill 
material should consist of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in 
maximum dimension. The material should have a laboratory California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) value of 8 or higher and a swell potential of less than 2 percent when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Structural fill required under the aboveground tank structure should consist of 
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The 
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a CBR value of 20 or 
higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. 
3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum

Structural fills required under the aboveground tank structures should be placed in

3.3.4 Excavation 
Our field exploration disclosed the proposed aboveground tank site is generally 
underlain by stiff and medium dense fill soils. It is our opinion the fill soils at the site 
may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as large 
backhoes or bulldozers. 
The above discussions regarding the excavation characteristics of the subsurface 
materials are based on the field data from the borings drilled at the site. We 
recommend that the contractor on this project be encouraged to examine the site 
conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation. 
3.3.5 Fill Slopes 
Permanent fill slopes constructed of structural fill material may be designed with a 
slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. If the on-site sands are used on the fill slopes 
(outside the fuel tank structure and pad embankment) and to avoid future raveling 
of the slope face, a slope inclination of 3H:1V or flatter should be used. Fills placed 
on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope 
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at 
the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed 
by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted

3.4 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the aboveground fuel tank structures should be 
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential 
for ponding. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent loss of strength.  The foundation excavations should be 
properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the 
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.  In addition, drainage swales 
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface 
water runoff away from the foundations and slabs. 
3.5 
Utility Trenches 
We anticipate new fuel lines will be installed to connect the new aboveground 
fuel tanks to the existing service system. For water lines located outside the water tank 
limits, a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 1-inch minus granular fills materials 
(or base course) is recommended below the pipes. The 1-inch minus granular fill 
materials should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded materials less 
than 3 inches in maximum dimension (structural fill material). The backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 10-inch level 
loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
Where trenches are located in paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below 
the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative 
compaction.

3.6 
Corrosion Potential 
One set of Corrosivity Tests, including pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), were performed by our office on selected soil samples obtained from our 
field exploration. Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity results, the near surface soils at the 
project site have a minimum resistivity of about 2,500 ohm-cm, corresponding to a 
corrosion rating of 2 in accordance with the Water System External Corrosion Control 
Standards, Volume 3 published by the Board of Water Supply (BWS), City and County 
of Honolulu, in 1991 and amended in 1995. A corrosion rating of 2 is considered “Very 
Corrosive” according to the BWS Standards. Design of metallic substructures, such as 
water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive 
environment on the substructure.  At a deeper depth, the subsurface soil tested had a 
minimum resistivity of about 7,500 ohm-cm corresponding to a “Moderately Corrosive” 
rating.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 5.5 to

Laboratory Testing 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in classifying and evaluating soil 
properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Results of the 
tests are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the characteristics of the near surface 
soils. Results of the tests are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Two pH tests (ASTM G51) were performed on selected soil samples obtained from 
our field exploration to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils. The results of the tests 
are presented on Plate B-7. 
Two minimum soil resistivity tests (ASTM G57) were performed on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils.  
The results of the tests are presented on Plate B-7.
