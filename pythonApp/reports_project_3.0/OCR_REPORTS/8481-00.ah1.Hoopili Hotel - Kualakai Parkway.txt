SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings within the planned 
hotel development as a supplement to the previous subsurface investigation program 
conducted by others. Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered fill 
materials overlying older alluvium consisting of stiff to hard silty/clayey soils extending down 
to the maximum depth explored of about 51.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate 
shrink-swell characteristics as well as potential collapsing when subjected to fluctuations in 
the soil moisture contents. Special foundation subgrade preparations should be considered 
to reduce the risk of structural distresses due to moderately expansive soil conditions. 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of non-
expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. General 
fills may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum particle size of 6 inches and should be 
free of deleterious materials. In general, the excavated on-site materials may be reused as 
a source of general fill material provided they are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious 
materials, and other unsuitable materials. 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive select granular fill 
materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished subgrades subjected to 
vehicular traffic). 
The on-site clayey soils should not be allowed to dry out and should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site 
clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks develop, the soils should be re-scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and recompacted again. 
Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot thick reworked 
soil layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The foundation subgrade 
should be kept moist to reduce the potential for additional shrinkage cracks from developing 
until the Wafflemat foundation construction is underway and completed.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments that were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic times through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the Ho`opili Hotel project site is generally underlain 
by fills overlying older alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of about 
51.5 feet below the ground surface. Groundwater was not encountered at the time of our 
field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to 
change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate 
shrink-swell characteristics as well as potential collapsing when subjected to fluctuations 
in the soil moisture contents. Based on the provided information, we anticipate the near-
surface soils underlying the planned development will consist of clayey soils with 
moderate to high shrink/swell potential. To reduce the risk of structural distresses due to 
the moderate to high shrink/swell potential of the clayey soils, we recommend 
implementing the following special subgrade preparation procedures during construction. 
Clearing and grubbing the existing ground surface; 
Over-excavating 12 inches of soils and moisture-conditioning the soils to 
about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content for use as general 
fill; 
Scarifying 12 inches of soils at the bottom of the over-excavation, 
moisture-conditioning to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacting to 85 and 90 percent relative compaction. 
The intent of the above site preparation recommendations is to create a 2-foot thick 
reworked clayey soil layer to close-up the existing shrinkage cracks throughout the site 
and to reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing the clayey soils with 
elevated moisture contents with reduced compaction. In addition, the reworked clayey 
soil with elevated moisture contents would decrease the affinity of the dry clayey soils for 
water and create a “barrier” to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the underlying 
dry clayey soils.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce 
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials.  
After clearing and grubbing, the subgrade soils should be over-excavated by at least 
12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Prior to 
replacing the moisture-conditioned soil, the bottom of the over-excavation should be 
scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to 85 to 90 percent relative 
compaction. It should be noted that the intent of reducing soil compaction with 
elevated moisture contents is to reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential. 
Details of the subgrade preparation recommendations are presented on Plate 3. 
The materials generated from the over-excavation and scarification process and/or 
from cut areas would likely contain large clayey clumps. To moisture-condition the 
soils effectively, the clayey clumps should be thoroughly broken down and mixed by 
the compaction equipment, such as large sheepsfoot compactors and/or large 
dozers, until a relatively uniform moisture condition is achieved. Contractors bidding 
on this project should anticipate additional processing of the on-site soils to break 
down the hard clayey clumps and achieve a relatively uniform moisture content. 
The 12 inches of over-excavated soils may be reused as a source of general fill 
materials, provided they are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious material and other 
unsuitable material and are moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the 
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. It should be noted that the 2-foot thick reworked soil layer should also

Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked soil 
layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a post-
tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. This proprietary system utilizes a series of 
recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side down on the

system, creating a very stiff foundation. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically

soil layer or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations presented 
herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.

3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
A set of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed (by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc.) on a bulk sample 
obtained from our field exploration. Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in 
Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soil at the 
project site has a minimum resistivity value of approximately 960 ohm-cm and a pH value 
of about 7.6, corresponding to a corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) based on the 
guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply.  
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a corrosion 
rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of 
metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider 
the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. 
In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Levels of the chloride 
and sulfate content of the on-site soils encountered near the existing ground surface are 
relatively low on the order of about 130 parts per million (ppm) and 250 ppm or less, 
respectively. Based on the laboratory-tested values of pH, chloride content, and sulfate 
content of the in-situ soils, we believe the near-surface soils at the project site may be 
considered “non-corrosive”; therefore, either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be

Prior to placing the 18-inch thick layer of non-expansive select granular fill, we 
recommend that the subgrade be properly scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to recompacting. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material 
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 31 and 30, 
respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, City & County 
of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance 
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and 
subbase course should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.

In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining 
backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from 
compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce 
the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site clayey soils as backfill 
directly around the utility pipes is not recommended and should not be allowed. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill material. 
The granular or on-site soil backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content or about 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
respectively, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable 
future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench backfill below the 
pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
For the deep manhole structures, we recommend the backfill compaction to be 
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence due to the stiffness difference between the 
concrete structure and soil backfill under continuous vehicular traffic dynamic movement. 
The limits of the increased compaction backfill around the concrete manhole structure 
may be extended to a horizontal distance beyond the edge of the manhole footprint with 
a ratio of the manhole depth.

from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations 
and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. Planters next to

3.11 Infiltration Tests 
We conducted a falling head infiltration test at Boring No. 102 to evaluate the 
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The test was 
performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of 
Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration test was performed by drilling to a depth of about 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid 
casing was set to the bottom of the drilled borehole to allow infiltration only through the 
soil exposed on the bottom of the borehole. The falling head infiltration test was performed 
to determine the average infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic 
readings over a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is then calculated based on the 
result of the fourth and last trial. 
Based on the infiltration testing performed at the project site, the subsurface soils 
at the selected test location have a calculated infiltration rate of about 0.05 inches per 
hour during the fourth and final trial, which is less than 0.5 inches per hour; therefore, 
infiltration/detention system and similar methods of storm water management is not

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, extending to depths of about 51.5 and 
5 feet below the existing ground surface, respectively. In addition, a bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-101, was obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136), including hydrometer testing 
(ASTM D422), was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification. A graphic presentation of the 
grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate 
the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Eight One-Dimensional Swell or Settlement Potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is remolded 
to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to simulate the 
anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle until it has 
stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil sample to evaluate 
its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-4 and B-5. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, an Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) test was performed on a selected in-situ soil 
sample in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-6. 
One Unconfined Compression (UC) test (ASTM D2166) was performed on a 
selected in-situ sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site 
cohesive soils. The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depth. Stress-strain curve of the unconfined compression test is presented on 
Plate B-7. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soil. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.

Field Infiltration Test 
A field infiltration test was conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The infiltration test was 
performed at a selected location, Boring No. 102, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of 
the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, 
Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s 
procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration test was performed by augering a borehole to a selected test 
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 
solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the 
soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The test consisted 
of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over 
a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is then calculated based on the result of the 
fourth and last trial for the test location. The test results are presented on Plate C-1.
