Our borings at the project site generally encountered a surface fill layer
approximately 3 to 6 feet in thickness The fill layer generally consisted of stiff to very stiff
clayey silt with gravel and cobbles The fill layer was underlain by a lagoonal swamp
deposit with thickness ranging from about 14 5 to 25 5 feet The lagoonal deposit was
comprised of very soft organic clayey silt and peat Below the lagoonal deposit an alluvial
deposit was encountered to depths of about 53 to 56 feet The alluvial deposit generally
consisted of stiff to very stiff sandy silt clayey silt and silty clay The alluvial deposit was
underlain by a residual soil deposit to depths of about 68 5 to 78 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The residual soil deposit generally consisted of stiff to hard clayey silt
Below the residual soil deposit a medium hard to very hard basalt rock formation was
encountered to 80 feet the maximum depth explored The alluvial and residual soil
deposits were not encountered in one of the borings Groundwater was encountered in
the borings at depths of about 9 9 to 10 4 feet below the existing ground surface The
groundwater levels correspond to elevations ranging from about 2 6 to 7 5 feet Mean
Sea Level
The new sewer line inverts are generally located within or in close proximity to the
very soft lagoonal swamp deposits encountered in the borings In order to provide
support for the new sewer line and manhole structures and to reduce the potential for
appreciable future settlements we recommend that jet grouted columns be installed under
the pipes along the sewer line alignment for foundation support In general the jet grouted
columns should consist of a minimum of 48 inches in diameter and should be installed
along the new sewer line alignment at about 5 feet center to center spacing to provide
support for each of the PVC pipe segments In addition four jet grouted columns should
be installed at each manhole structure for foundation support The jet grouted columns
should extend into the medium stiff to stiff alluvial deposits or basalt rock formation with
column lengths of about 10 to 28 feet
A granular bedding layer and a subgrade stabilization layer is recommended below
the new sewer lines In general the stress distribution against the bottom of the pipe has
a great effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe A granular bedding layer
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended under the sewer pipes In addition we recommend that a 24 inch thick
subgrade stabilization layer consisting of No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation

sewer lines for uniform support For the new manhole structures a granular bedding layer
consisting of 12 inches of No 3B Fine gravel is recommended

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The Waianae and Koolau Volcanic Shields
were built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western third of
the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island The main shield building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million
years ago It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located along the southern flank of Koolau The
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are directly related to the
glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the
genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions at
the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age alluvial soil deposits and related
deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous
sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
Evidence from wells drilled indicate that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as
1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott
1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill

the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded and melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within
the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of
the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During
that rise the deep valleys in the project area were submerged Within the last
10 000 years the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine

areas that were formerly valleys and drainage ways Along the northern fringe of

sediments in a lacustrine environment Land development and reclamation projects
within the last 100 years have brought the area to its present form These projects
involved the placement of man made fills over the thick lagoonal and marsh deposits

3 1 Jet Grouting
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with
radial horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil cement columns The jet grouting
technique is a process that produces soil cement columns by pumping neat cement
grout slurry through horizontal jets injected at high pressures The horizontal jets of
cement grout slurry cuts and mixes the surrounding in situ materials with the neat
cement slurry grout as the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil cement
column
The new sewer line inverts are generally located within or near the very soft
lagoonal deposits encountered in our borings In order to provide support for the new
sewer line and manhole structures and to reduce potential future settlements we
recommend that jet grouted columns be installed under the pipes along the new sewer
line alignment and under the new manhole structures The new sewer lines and
manhole structures will be supported on the jet grouted columns that bear on the
underlying medium stiff to stiff alluvial deposits or the basalt rock formation The
jet grouted columns would derive vertical load resistance primarily from end bearing and
skin friction within the medium stiff to stiff alluvial deposits and basalt rock formation

Construction Considerations
The use of micro piles to support the new sewer lines from potential future
settlements was also considered With the use of micro piles the construction of
concrete cradle structures is required to connect the micro piles and to provide support
for the new sewer pipes The pipe cradles are typically cast in place concrete
structures Therefore the construction period for the concrete pipe cradles supported
on micro piles alternative is typically longer than the granular rock layers supported by
jet grouted columns alternative In addition the cost for the micro pile alternative is
generally higher than the jet grouted columns Therefore the use of jet grouted
columns to support the new sewer pipes and manhole structures was recommended
Jet Grouted Column Foundation
The new sewer lines and manhole structures are underlain by very soft lagoonal
deposits To reduce future pipe and manhole settlements we recommend that the
new sewer line and sewer manhole structures be supported on jet grouted
columns Based on our past experience we believe that the jet grouted columns
should have a minimum diameter of 48 inches In addition the grout mix should
have a specific gravity of at least 1 5 and should be able to produce jet grouted
columns with a 7 day unconfined compressive strength of at least 150 pounds per
square inch psi and a 28 day unconfined compressive strength of at least 300 psi

required to avoid encountering the existing utility line In addition four jet grouted
columns should be installed at each manhole structure for foundation support The

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the

sewer pipes we recommend that an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic
foot pcf for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 be used Earth
forces upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench

It should be noted that due to the subsurface conditions at the site the trench
excavations would need to be shored using sheet pile shoring or other similar

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the sewer line pipes
The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width
of 24 inches A non woven filter fabric should wrap around the bedding material
and should extend up the walls of the trench excavation to a level of about
12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches above the groundwater level It should be
noted that the bedding layer recommended herein is in addition to the jet grouted
stabilization columns recommended below the sewer lines for this project
For the new sewer manhole structures a granular bedding consisting of 12 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel is recommended under the manhole base The granular
bedding should extend beyond the sides of the base a minimum width of 24 inches
A non woven filter fabric should wrap around the bedding material with a minimum
24 inches overlap at the top of the bedding layer

Construction Considerations
The settlement estimates presented in the Pipe Settlement subsection assume
that proper construction procedures and good workmanship will be consistently
practiced by the contractor during pipeline installation operations Based on our
knowledge and past experience with similar soft and or loose subsurface soil
conditions significant settlement of the pipes may be attributed to the construction
methodology and equipment used by the contractor Therefore special attention
should be given to the installation procedures for the proposed sewer lines
especially in soft and or loose subsurface soil conditions to reduce the potential
for settlement distress in the future
As recommended previously the sewer lines should generally be supported by
approximately 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel bedding layer over a subgrade
stabilization layer consisting of No 2 Rock wrapped in a non woven filter fabric
The non woven filter fabric is used to provide for separation between the coarse
aggregate No 2 Rock and the fine grained soils In general a minimum overlap
of 2 feet should be provided between the ends of each roll of the filter fabric placed
along the pipe alignment under dry conditions If the filter fabric is not properly
overlapped it is possible that the gravel bedding material placed may migrate
through the openings in the filter fabric and mix with the underlying soft and or loose
soils This may result in settlement of the pipes due to loss of cushion materials
into the soft and or loose subsoils
Considering the depth of the excavations some of the bedding layer installation
below the pipe invert may be performed in wet conditions Therefore we envision

Excavation Method
In general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
Based on our field exploration and the available information the surface fill soils

excavated with conventional earthmoving equipment
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the
excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In
addition the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from
existing underground utilities to reduce the potential for appreciable ground
movement or subsidence which may damage the existing underground utility line

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 The borings generally
extended to depths ranging from about 68 5 to 80 feet below the existing ground
surface and were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plates 2 1
through 2 3 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 4
Core samples of the consolidated coralline materials encountered at the site
were obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the consolidated
coralline formation The consolidated coralline materials left in the hollow core of the bit
are mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 1
Three Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification In addition hydrometer tests were performed on the samples
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B 2
Two One Dimensional Consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the consolidation characteristics of the soils Graphic
presentation of the tests results is provided on Plates B 3 and B 4
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on
selected in situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength
of the soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 5
through B 7
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of
the soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures were used as the
applied confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil samples The test
results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
Fifteen Torvane Shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the undrained shear strengths of the soils The test results are presented on
Plate B 10
Two Organic Matter tests ASTM D 2974 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the ash and organic matter content of the soils The test results
are provided on Plate B 11

One of the borings from the September 2003 report was drilled along Sewer Line B
to a depth of about 25 feet below the existing ground surface and about 6 inches into the
underlying basalt formation An artesian ground water elevation of 12 9 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL was encountered in the boring This is significantly higher than the artesian
ground water level encountered in our field exploration performed along Sewer Line B
Previously the installation of jet grouted columns was recommended under the
new sewer line for foundation support Because of the higher artesian water pressure
there is a potential for ground water movement within the jet grouted columns during
construction which will create difficulties during column construction and reduce the
integrity of the column Therefore we recommend that the new sewer line from SMH B 3
and SMH B 4 be supported by a pile foundation We understand that replacement of the
existing sewer line from SMH B 2 and SMH B 3 and the replacement of SMH B 3 are not
required for the project
Pile Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the low to
moderate structural loads imposed on the new pipe foundations we recommend that a
deep foundation system consisting of driven piles be used to support the new sewer line
between SMH B 3 and SMH B 4 Based on the structural load demands we recommend

The 12 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles may be designed with an

and would facilitate pile driving in groups
The new sewer line inverts range from about Elevation 7 to 10 5 feet MSL which
correspond to between about 7 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore
we anticipate that the average lengths of the piles to be approximately 10 feet below the
pile cap elevation in order to achieve the recommended allowable compressive load
capacity Because of the small number of piles required for the project it would not be
feasible to perform a test pile program to establish the actual production pile lengths
Instead it is recommended that an additional 5 feet of pile length be provided to allow for
variations in the depths to end bearing
A dense and stiff surface fill layer was encountered in our field exploration In
addition existing residential structures are situated near the sewer line alignment To
reduce the potential for significant noise and vibration during pile driving and to facilitate
pile installation through potential cobbles and boulders within the surface fill layer we
recommend that the upper 5 feet below the existing ground surface be predrilled prior to
pile installation The diameter of the predrilled hole should be at least 18 inches
Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of
the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the
pile supported foundations to be less than 0 75 inches with differential settlements
between the piles to be about 0 5 inches or less We believe that these settlements
are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

with energies greater than 50 000 foot pounds are not recommended for this
project due to the smaller 12 inch square piles The hammer should be equipped
with an energy level control such that driving within the very soft and or loose soils
