INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the Upgrade Hangar Complex project located within Hickam Air Force Base in Honolulu
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade retaining structures site preparation and shoring and dewatering only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western
one third of the island while the younger shield volcano Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in Central Oahu
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to giacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosional
volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during
the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows Following the cessation of
activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic
GEOLABS INC
W O 4468 00 Page 6
environment in the area of Pearl Harbor The sea level fluctuated several times receding
perhaps 90 meters below and rising about 30 meters above the present sea level Concurrent
with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Epoch eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa
member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of
volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of tuff
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have added
man made fills bringing the site to its present configuration

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by a very thin
surface fill layer on the order of about 0 5 meters thick Below the near surface fill layer loose
to very loose clayey sands with gravel were encountered to depths of about 4 to 5 meters
below the existing ground surface The highly compressible soil deposit was underlain by tuff
formation overlying medium dense to dense coralline materials extending to the maximum
depth explored of approximately 9 6 meters below the existing ground surface Groundwater
was encountered at depths of about 1 6 meters below the existing ground surface at the time
of our field exploration
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that concrete
slabs on grade with a perimeter ring footing may be used to support the new water storage
tank Due to the presence of loose sandy subsoils anticipated at the site a 900 mm thick
layer of compacted structural fill material should be provided below the slab subgrade of the
water tank for more uniform support The over excavation for the compacted structural fill
should extend beyond the outside edges of the ring footing a minimum of Prior
to placement of the compacted structural fill the bottom of the over excavation should be
proof rolled with a minimum 9 metric ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to
detect the presence of near surface cavities or loose zones Because soft and or loose soils
may be expected at the over excavated subgrade levels pumping of the subgrade soils may
occur during the proof rolling operations Therefore we recommend that the bottom of the
over excavation be lined with a layer of woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or
equivalent prior to placement of the structural fill material
Based on the relatively light structural loads anticipated for the proposed fire pump
house extension we believe that shallow continuous footings bearing on the re compacted
near surface soils may be used to support the proposed fire pump house extension The
footing subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 300 mm moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction We recommend that the extension be structurally connected with the existing

differential settlements
For the trap pit structure we believe that a concrete mat may be used to provide the
necessary bearing support and uplift resistance for the underground structure The concrete

dense to dense coralline materials Relatively deep excavations and subsequent backfilling

structure foundations If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the

the bottom edge of the utility trench or extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures

Underground Valve Box
Based on the information provided we anticipate that the bottom of the proposed
underground valve box may be embedded about 2 5 meters below the existing ground

underlying soils On this basis we recommend that a concrete mat also be used to provide
the necessary bearing support and uplift resistance for the valve box structure A preliminary

foundation This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased

Dewatering Method
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method

operations should be carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should be
written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table
and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed
structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may include but
are not limited to pumping and the use of cut off walls

a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage
to existing buildings roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of approximately 6 6 and
9 6 meters below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with hollow stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm
In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 300 mm of a 450 mm drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the tuff formations encountered at the site were obtained using core
drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow
bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically
recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if of material is
recovered from a core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
