The Ewa Villages project site is located in the Honouliuli area of the Ewa District
on the island of Oahu to the west of Fort Weaver Road The Ewa Villages site
encompasses approximately 150 acres in the vicinity of Ewa Town Renton and Verona
Village

Much of the site is currently used for the cultivation of sugar cane The existing

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shielded
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau
Weathering and erosion of this plateau generated sediments which were
transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south these alluvial
sediments accumulated along with and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero
feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the
Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal
aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier
geologic time through the present

Subsurface Conditions
Based on our current field exploration the soils encountered at the project site
may be generally described as follows
Coral Algal Deposits
This unit is the oldest of the soils and geologic units encountered at the project
site and consists of fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and their related detrital
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 8
debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant
portion of any coral rock assemblage
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral debris consisting
of gravel sand and silt sized clastic fragments Some of the coral fragments
were dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented and or
uncemented fine sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal deposits and the
degree of cementation frequently present this formation generally has high in
situ strength characteristics Structurally the Coral Algal Deposits are capable
of supporting heavy foundation loads
In the current exploration coral algal deposits were encountered only in the
deeper portions of Boring 303
and 424 Generally this formation
was encountered at depths which preclude this formation from being a
consideration for foundations
In Boring No 421 a relatively large cavity was encountered at a depth of
about 30 feet below the existing ground surface A supplemental probe drilled
next to this boring did not encounter the cavity
Percolation tests conducted in Boring 213 and 221 generally
indicate that the coral material has rapid percolation characteristics
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 9
Older Alluvium
The Older Alluvium is assigned the position of being the second oldest unit in
the stratigraphic column of the project area even though some of the
coral algal deposits may be intercalated with the alluvial deposits
This unit generally overlies the coral algal deposits and consists of terrigenous
sediments which have been transported by stream action from the upper
reaches of the streams The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard
brown silty clays and clayey silts Gravel lenses representing buried stream
channels are sometimes encountered Portions of the Older Alluvium unit
contain some weathered basalt boulders and cobbles Although no
groundwater was encountered during our field exploration these coarse
grained parts of the unit may also contain perched groundwater in isolated
areas
The Older Alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength values resulting in
relatively high bearing capacities Frequently these materials show a
moderate to high shrink swell potential Structurally the Older Alluvium
materials are considered to be sufficiently competent to support relatively
heavy structural loads with proper design
Older Alluvium deposits were encountered in most of the borings drilled and
sampled at the site The Older Alluvium consists primarily of stiff to hard dark
brown to brown clayey silts to silty clays with some sandy and gravelly
interbeds This unit is differentiated from the younger alluvial soils by its stiffer
or harder consistency often accompanied with weak cementation lower
moisture contents and a higher proportion of granular interbeds
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2819 00 Page 10
Alluvium
Recent to late Pleistocene alluvial deposits Alluvium were encountered in the
upper portions of some of the borings drilled and sampled at the subject site
These alluvial soils consist primarily of medium stiff to stiff dark brown to brown
silty clays and clayey silts These soils are distinguished from the Older Alluvium
by having a slightly softer consistency generally higher moisture contents and very
few interbeds of granular soils The unit also includes upper soils which have been
somewhat disturbed during deep tilling operations
In general most of the stiff to hard clay soils in both the Older Alluvium and
Alluvium units may be classified as having low to moderately high swelling expansion
potential when wetted it should be noted that clays with high shrink swell potential have
been encountered at nearby locations and are anticipated to exist at the site
Generally the borings drilled and sampled for this phase of exploration did not
encounter groundwater with the exception of Boring No 427 which encountered
groundwater at a depth of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater
was also encountered during our previous exploration of the project site in Boring Nos
107 and 108 at depths ranging from 10 5 to 18 3 feet below the existing ground surface
The borings in which groundwater was encountered were generally near irrigation
ditches or Kaloi Gulch The groundwater which was encountered appears to be in a
perched condition and the groundwater levels may vary Groundwater may occur at
other locations depending on seasonal conditions or as a result of other influences

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is Our opinion that the development project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view provided that our recommendations presented herein are
followed
In general our current field exploration indicates that the project site is underlain
by a surface layer of medium stiff to hard silty clays overlying medium dense to dense
coral gravel deposits The surface clay soils vary in thickness from approximately 2 0 feet
to the maximum thickness observed of 23 0 feet
Based on our laboratory test results the on site silty clays have swell potentials
that range from low to moderately high
It is our opinion that spread and or continuous footings may be used to support
proposed structures such as buildings related to the golf course residential structures
and public buildings if the foundations and subgrades are properly designed and
constructed
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of earthwork the site should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed of the existing pavement vegetation and other deleterious materials
Abandoned utilities should be removed and the trenches filled with engineered backfill as
described in the Utilities section of this report Deleterious materials should be removed
from the site and disposed of properly
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 12
Stripped asphaltic concrete paving material may be incorporated into general fill
materials provided that the material is reduced to less than three 8 inches in maximum
size The asphaltic material should be thoroughly blended into the fill and not nested in
pockets
Generally the upper one foot of the surface soils in areas used for the cultivation
of sugar cane are contaminated with organic matter In structural and future roadway
pavement areas this organically contaminated soil should be stripped and removed from
the area In cut areas this upper organic topsoil should as much as practicable be
segregated from soil that will be used as structural fill The stripped material may be
suitable for use in golf course grading and for landscaping purposes
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to firm ground and the resulting depression should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill

Stockpile Areas
It is our understanding that the future subdivision areas will be used as temporary
stockpile areas In the practice of stockpiling on areas designated for future
structural use there is a possibility that the lower portions of the stockpile which
is usually not placed or compacted in a controlled manner as fill may be left in
place during the final grading for the intended structural use
To reduce the potential of this occurrence we recommend that the existing ground
surface in the proposed stockpile areas should be topographically surveyed to a
degree of accuracy to within approximately one foot plus or minus When grading
operations commence the stockpiled material should be removed to the original
grades as determined by a second topographic survey and visual observation by
the project geotechnical engineer prior to the placement of any new structural or
embankment fill
Kaloi Gulch
Based on the information provided to us it is anticipated that portions of Kaloi
Gulch will be filled and the surface flows diverted In its present form the gulch is
a ditch or channel with raised levees on both sides
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 17
Hand auger probes made in selected locations along the alignment of the
gulch indicate that there is about one 1 to three 3 feet of soft sediment in
the bottom of the gulch
in order to minimize future differential fill ground settiements and to provide

Golf Course Water Features
Based on the plans provided to us several water features such as lakes or
ponds are proposed for the future golf course Based on our field exploration
it appears that the bottoms of several of these water features will encounter
coral algal formation
Our field percolation testing indicates that generally the coral materials
underlying the site have relatively rapid infiltration characteristics Therefore
it is anticipated that liners will be required in the bottoms of the water features
to retain the water We anticipate that the liners will be flexible synthetic
membranes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 18
To reduce the potential of punctures in the flexible membranes a cushion layer
of compacted moist silty soil or sand is recommended for the bottoms and
sides of the ponds and water features and the cushion layer should be
installed prior to the placement of the flexible membrane We believe the
cushion layer should be a minimum of six 6 inches thick
Although the flexible membranes are considered to be impervious there is a
possibility of seepage due to punctures in the liner or breaks in the seams
Leakage through the membrane could potentially cause piping losses or
weakening of the subgrade soils Piping losses could result in loss of
subgrade support leading to liner failure Weakening of the subgrade soils
could cause loss of stability in impoundment slopes potentially leading to slope
failure and or liner failure Therefore we recommend that a subdrain system
be installed below the cushion fill layer
Our experience with similar liner installations indicates that there is sometimes
a tendency for flexible liners to become detached at the top of the water
feature or impoundment It appears that this may be the result of inadequate
anchoring of the top of the liner and insufficient give in the liner It is our
recommendation that the liner manufacturer be consulted regarding proper
installation with particular attention to proper anchoring of the liner to reduce
the potential of liner failure

Utilities
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility line trenching at the site
The subsurface materials encountered at the site are generally stiff clayey soils
and coral which are generally competent However areas of loose materials may be

the sides of the pipes which could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to
the pipes The use of on site clayey soils as backfill in the bottom of the utility
trenches and around utility pipes is not acceptable
Subsequent trench backfills above the underlying granular backfill should be

Foundations
Our exploration indicates that most of the areas in Increments 2 3 and 4 are
underlain by moderately to highly expansive alluvial clay soils Unless foundations
supported by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress
to lightly loaded structures such as residential buidlings resulting from the swelling
of these clayey soils The following recommendations present alternatives for
reducing the potential of such structural distress for the proposed residential
structures
Slabs on Grade
Alternate No 14
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
on site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive off site select
material be used for a minimum depth of 24 inches below slab subgrade
This select material should be non expansive granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock or cinder The material should be well graded with
no particles greater than three 3 inches in size with a laboratory CBR swell
value not exceeding one 1 percent and CBR value of 20 or more It should
not contain large percentages of boulders organic or other deleterious
material It should contain sufficient fines so that is capable of forming a
competent stable mass when compacted
Prior to placement of the capping material the underlying soil should be
scarified to a depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to slightly above
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 21
optinum moisture content and recompacted to 85 of its maximum dry
density as established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The select capping material should be placed as soon as possible after the

moist soil above the potentially expansive soil and thereby reduce the loss of
moisture from the subgrade soils
Conventional slab on grade construction may then be utilized for proposed
structures constructed on the 24 inch thick capping fill However it is

For minor unoccupied structures such as bus stop shelters and electrical

The slab subgrade areas should be wetted and kept moist prior to placement

Subdrainage
It has been our experience with projects in the Ewa area and with projects
having similar soil conditions to find that when a granular select material is used as
subbase course or as a capping over clay soils there is a tendency for water to
accumulate at the interface between the select material and the underlying clay soils
It appears that the granular select material is relatively permeable and that the
Clay soils are effectively impermeable Precipitation and landscape irrigation water
infiltrate through the topsoil into the select material and form a perched water table
on the clay
Problems have occurred when this water reaches the subgrade of pavement
section prisms and when the select material daylights on top of the clay When the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 00 Page 29
water seeps into road subgrades it can soften the subgrade soils and weaken the
pavement section Where the seepage daylights continuously wet areas can
develop
To minimize the potential of accumulation of water along the select material
and clay soil interface we recommend that subdrains be installed to intercept water
and divert it to appropriate drainage locations such as drain inlets Generally the
worst problems have occurred where the water moves into road subgrades
Therefore we recommend that subdrains be installed along the sides of the road
prisms The subdrains could either be of the conventional type i e a perforated pipe
embedded in free draining gravel or one of the newer synthetic types e g MiraDrain
or ConTech 100

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our
field exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule
appropriate tests to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil
and or rock materials sampled
Ciassification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight
Determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining
certain properties such as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a
percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate
appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D are used to determine
the plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material
is used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results
of these tests are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to determine
the grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for
classification in the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other
engineering properties The results of these tests are summarized on Plate C 1 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical
parameters of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering
analyses pertaining to the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation
support and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate C 3 1
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the
angle of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data
are used in various engineering calculations The results of the direct shear tests are
presented on Plate C 4 1
