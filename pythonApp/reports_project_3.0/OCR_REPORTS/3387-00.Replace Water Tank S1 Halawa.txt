INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Replace Water Tank S1 Project located in the Halawa area on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1

This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input for foundation
site grading and trench excavation and backfill and may not be appropriate to form the
basis for the preparation of construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding
purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located off Bougainville Drive within the Aliamanu Military
Reservation in the vicinity of Halawa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed
project consists of the removal of the existing 6 million gallon steel rivet tank 1 and
the construction of a new 6 million gallon steel water tank as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
We understand that the proposed water tank will have an outside diameter of
about 165 feet and a height of about 40 feet The roof structure will span between the
perimeter wall and the center column The perimeter shell thickness of the steel tank
will vary from about inch at the bottom to 5 16 inch at the top The tank floor will
consist of a inch thick steel plate encasing a grid of cathodic protection wires The
steel floor plate will bear on a 6 inch layer of compacted sand
Based on the preliminary grading concept for the project the finish floor elevation
for the proposed water tank is planned at approximately 140 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL corresponding to about 240 feet U S Navy Datum Grading at the proposed
water tank site would generally require minor cuts and fills on the order of about 1 foot
to achieve a level pad for the new tank

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk
of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
After a long period of cessation of the shield building volcanic activity returned
with the formation of a series of lava flows cinder cones and tuff cones which are
known as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Many of the eruptions were rather explosive
in nature due to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3387 00 Page 5
explosions which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash
and cinder Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented together rapidly to
form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as mudrock
The project site is located on the western flank of the Aliamanu Crater of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series The subsoil conditions at the project site are generally
formed by the deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions
of the vents of members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt
Lake and Makalapa
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed project site is located within the Aliamanu Military Reservation in
the Halawa area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The water tank site is surrounded by
a chain link fence and is bounded by a dirt road to the north the existing Water Tank
S2 to the east Bougainville Drive to the south and a paved access road to the west
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The proposed circular water tank pad is located
on a relatively level ground During our field exploration the water tank pad was
surrounded by near vertical cut slopes exposing tuff formation on the west and south
sides
Our field expioration was performed following the completion of the existing tank
demolition work Based on the U S G S Pearl Harbor Quadrangle Map 1983 and
information provided to our office the elevation at the bottom of the demolished tank
is at about 140 feet MSL Therefore we generally believe that the ground surface
elevation of our borings would be close to 140 feet MSL

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed water tank site is underlain by tuff
formation at shallow depths Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3387 00 Page 7
respect to the proposed water tank construction the principal geotechnical engineering
considerations for the project include the following
1 foundation support for the proposed water tank
2 site preparation and
3 trench excavation and backfill of the utility lines
As with any storage tank total and differential settlements are important design
considerations Based on our boring data we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread footings and or continuous ring footing bearing on the tuff
formation encountered at shallow depths may be used to support the water tank
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundation site
grading trench excavation and backfill and other geotechnical aspects of the project
are presented in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the tuff formation encountered at shallow depths would provide suitable foundation
support for the proposed water tank structure Continuous ring and or spread footings
bearing on the tuff formation may be used to support the proposed water tank structure
An allowable net bearing pressure of up to 6 000 pounds per square feet p s f
may be used for design of the shallow foundations bearing directly on the tuff formation
The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 for transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces The
allowable bearing pressure provided above is a net value therefore the weight of the
foundation and the overburden pressure may be discounted
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3387 00 Page 8
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade Additional enbedment may be necessary for the footings to bear
directly on the tuff formation since some of our borings encountered the tuff formation
at about 2 feet below the existing ground surface Where footings will be located
adjacent to existing utilities the foundations should extend to a depth below an
imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the invert of the utility
pipe This requirement is necessary to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footings and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 60 may be used for the friction between the base of the
footings and the subgrade materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f These values assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly
against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finish grade should be neglected
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch The majority of these settlements should
occur during filling of the water tank as structural loads are placed
If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation
they should be over excavated to expose competent native material and replaced with
lean concrete or select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3387 00 Page 9
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
It is recommended that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii observe the
foundation excavations prior to placement of reinforcing steel to evaluate the
competency of the bearing materials and the embedment depths
Floor Plate
We understand that a inch steel floor plate will be used for the interior of the
proposed water tank Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results we
recommend that the steel plate be supported on 6 inches of sand compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction to provide a more uniform support for the steel plate
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for the friction between the base of the floor
plate and the compacted sandy material
The risk of corrosion relates directly to the potential soil induced chemical
reaction Results of resistivity and pH tests indicate that the on site near surface soils
have minimum soil resistivities ranging from about 4 200 to 4 800 ohm cm A summary
of the soil corrosivity test results is presented on Plate B 1
Site Grading
Based on information provided to us grading at the proposed water tank site will
consist of minor cuts and fills on the order of 1 foot or less
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3387 00 Page 10
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials
In areas to receive fills or the tank floor subgrade the subgrade should be proof
rolled to obtain a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative com
paction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91
Fills and Backfills
Generally the on site materials may be used as fill and backfill materials
Where used as fill and or backfill material the on site soils should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Any imported material should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior
to delivery to the job site Imported granular materials such as crushed coral
mudrock basalt or cinder sand may be used The material should be well
graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest
dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR swell value of less than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Seismic Coefficient
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered the subsoil generally consists of
tuff formation Based on this information the project site may be classified from a

seismic analysis standpoint as a site with a Type 1 soil profile corresponding to an S
soil coefficient of 1 0 Uniform Building Code Table No 23 J 1991 Edition
Drainage
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
surface run off away from the foundations The finish grades outside the proposed
water tank should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to reduce the
potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundations should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
Utility Backfill
For pipes bearing on the dense soils and tuff formation a poorly graded granular
bedding material with little fines such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67
gradation S4C or equivalent is recommended below the pipes as bedding material
Other alternate pipe bedding material should consist of granular material containing
minimal fines and no particles larger than 1 inch in maximum dimension
The first zone of backfill is the zone extending from the bedding material to about
12 inches above the top of the utility line Poorly graded granular materials are recom
mended for use as trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the top of the pipe to
provide adequate support around the pipe and to reduce compaction of the backfill
thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipe
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of the excavated on site
granular material containing no particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension

Trench backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in 8 inch maximum level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less
than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below the water tank structure the trench backfill
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig to depths ranging from
about 2 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface at the proposed water tank site
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch O D standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock
material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Boring as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Boring as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
