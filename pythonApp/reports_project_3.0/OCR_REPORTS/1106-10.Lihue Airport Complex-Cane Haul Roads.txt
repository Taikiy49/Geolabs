INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our subsurface investigation for
the design of the proposed rough grading realignment of cane haul
roads and irrigation system drainage channel and major drains at the
new Lihue Airport Complex State Project No
K 1072 The general site location is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This project is a part of the proposed new Lihue Airport Complex
which also includes construction of access roads a new terminal
building vehicle parking lots aircraft parking aprons and off site
sewer and water lines Soils investigations have been performed for
the other related projects and specific soils reports will be sub
mitted under separate covers The proposed general layout of the new
Lihue Airport Complex is shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 10 Page 2
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
This project includes the following
1 Rough grading for the new airport complex including the
access roads parking area terminal building aircraft
parking apron and taxiway extensions
2 Construction of new cane haul roads and irrigation ditches
Road A will be about 2600 feet long and extend along the
southwestern side of the site from Kapule Highway to the
existing sewage treatment plant Road B which will be
located about 470 feet southeast of Runway 3 21 will be
about 4430 feet long and extend parallel to the existing
runway Road B will intersect with Road A near the
sewage treatment plant Irrigation ditches will be located
on both sides of the cane haul roads and also along the
western side of Kapule Highway
Six culvert and siphon structures are planned below the haul
roads
For tractor crossing a concrete pavement section is planned
where Road A crosses Kapule Highway
3 Construction of drainlines and a drainage channel northeast
of the new airport complex and generally within the existing
airport area Drainline A will extend from the north
eastern end of the proposed terminal building through
existing cane fields and toward the rental car storage areas
then along Ahukini Road Drainline B will extend from near
the proposed extension of Taxiway H through the existing
public parking lot car rental area and air cargo frontal
road
Both drainlines will empty into a 7 by 7 feet box culvert near
the existing helicopter landing pad behind Ahukini Road The
box culvert will connect to an open drainage channel which
will run parallel to Taxiway A then head east across the
existing rubbish dump road through a box culvert structure
and finally empty into an existing gully
The locations of the proposed improvements are shown on the Site
Plans Plates 2 and 3

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the investigation is to explore the subsurface condi
tions at the site to provide soil parameters for the design of the
proposed improvements Specifically our services included
1 Drilling 62 borings to depths ranging from 16 5 to 31 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were also

Geology
The island of Kauai was built up by a single large shield volcano
During the Tertiary period basaltic lavas of the Waimea Canyon
Volcanic Series built a roughly circular island to a height over
17 000 feet above the surrounding sea floor Near the end of the
Tertiary Period this main shield building volcanic phase came to a
halt
Following a long period of erosion and subsidence volcanism renewed
during the Pleistocene epoch producing the Koloa Volcanic Series
These post erosional volcanics laid a veneer of fresh lavas over the
older Waimea Canyon Series across most of the eastern half of the
island
After the cessation of renewed volcanism the geologic forces of
weathering erosion and deposition during the late Pleistocene and
the Recent epochs have shaped the island to its present form
At the site the lava rocks of this late volcanism had been deeply
weathered to form a mantle of reddish brown residual clayey silt soil

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the new airport complex and related
off site utilities were explored by drilling 62 borings at the
locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 The soil samples
thus obtained were selectively tested in our laboratory to further
examine their geotechnical engineering characteristics Descriptions
of our field exploration the logs of borings the laboratory testing
and results are presented in the appendices
Subsurface Conditions
In general most of the area is presently under cultivation and the
subsurface conditions consist of a thin layer of topsoil over a
relatively thick mantle of stiff residual soil consisting of reddish
brown clayey silt
The topsoil at the surface consists of dark brown Slightly organic
clayey silt The topsoil has been filled and plowed over and inter
mixed with some decayed and burnt vegetation and other organic matter
from the cultivation activities The topsoil layer is generally
about 1 foot thick
The reddish brown clayey silt below the topsoil layer is very stiff
to hard at shallow depth however it grades somewhat less stiff at
depths of about 10 to 15 feet Generally it was noted that the
GEOLABS HAWAII

moisture contents tend to increase and densities tend to decrease at
10 to 15 foot depth The in place clayey silt soil generally exhi
bits low swelling potential However some of the laboratory test

the optimum moisture content the compacted soil may have moderate
swelling potential
Boulders were encountered in some of the borings Visual observa

amounts of boulders may be generated from the site cutting
Groundwater Level
Groundwater was generally encountered at about elevations rang ing
from 123 to 138 feet in three of the borings drilled for the cane
haul roads and four in the terminal and apron areas The remaining
borings did not encounter groundwater although many of them were
drilled to depths below elevation 138 feet It appears that the
observed groundwater level may reflect localized seepage conditions
possibly originating from irrigation ditches reservoir and other
sources The borings indicate that the seepage may be carried by a
weathered clinker or cinder layer underlying the area

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration generally encountered stiff residual soils
which are suitable for the proposed improvements provided our recom
mendations are followed
Rough Grading
In general the site grading consists of cutting the aircraft apron
and taxiway area down from 1 to 10 feet and filling the remainder of
the site from 2 to 4 feet It is anticipated that excess material
will be generated from the site grading operation and will require
hauling off site or placement in the designated disposal site
situated adjacent to the southern side of the complex Borings were
not drilled in this disposal site area however similar soil
conditions are anticipated and the recommendations presented under
ROUGH GRADING may also be applied for the disposal site
The laboratory tests indicate that most of the site soils are suit
able for use as fill material Some of the soils exhibit moderate
expansion potential if compacted too dry Therefore the fills
should be compacted wet of optimum moisture contents The in situ
soils presently have moisture contents about 5 percent above the
optimum moisture content
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 10 Page 9
Over most of the area the upper one foot of surface soils has been
highly disturbed by the sugar cane cultivation and contains signifi
cant amount of organic matter This layer should be stripped off and
wasted at the start of the grading operation or stockpiled for future
landscaping use
Ground Preparation
The site should be cleared of all vegetation debris headwalls
and other structures
The surface soils containing organic matter from previous sugar
cane cultivation should be removed We estimate that this layer
would be about 12 inches thick
Any local soft spots and soft material in the irrigation ditches
encountered within the construction area should be removed and
replaced with compacted fill
Following the clearing and grubbing all areas designated to
receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 6 inches and com
pacted to a minimum of 90 of the maximum dry density established
by ASTM D 1557 The moisture content of the soil should be above
the optimum moisture content

Drainlines
Drainlines A and B will consist of a total of 5700 feet of
reinforced concrete pipe ranging from 48 inches to 78 inches in
diameter Also included is a 7 by 7 feet concrete box culvert
about 240 feet long which connects the two drainlines to the open
channel section
Excavation
Generally the depth of excavation will vary from about 12 to 19
feet below the existing ground surface The borings indicated
that residual soil should be encountered ranging from very stiff
to hard and grading to stiff below about 10 to 15 foot depth
Relatively easy digging is anticipated for the trenches although
some amount of boulders should be anticipated in the residual
soil formation
Due to the depth of the excavation shoring of the trench walls
are recommended to minimize caving in of the trench walls The
excavation should be provided with proper bracing in accordance
with the applicable safety requirements The design and safety
of the trench supports should be the responsibility of the
contractor
GEOLABS HAWAII
Heavy equipment and stockpiles should be kept away from the tops
of the open trenches
The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should
be continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indica
tions of movement and possible sloughing of the trench walls
Such monitoring is particularly critical in the areas where
existing structures are adjacent to the trench excavation
Below 10 to 15 foot depth the soils are generally wet and could
be affected by irrigation and other sources of water Localized
water seepage may be encountered in the deeper trench excavations
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the
trench the size of the pipe the unit weight of the backfill
material and the frictional resistance between the backfill
material and the trench walls To compute the vertical loads on
the pipes a unit weigth of 120 p c f and a coefficient of
internal friction of 0 30 is recommended Earth forces upon the
pipe increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore
the trench width Bd should be kept to a minimum The enclosed
Plate 4 indicates three recommended trench sections

A minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material consisting of

Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be in accordance with the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction May 1975
Granular material such as 3 Fine gravel or sand should be used
for the backfill around the bottom half of the pipe to reduce the
compactive effort required and possible damage to the pipe and to
provide bearing support for the pipe
Except for the boulders most of the excavated on site soil can
be re used in the trench backfill to within 2 feet of finished
grade provide adequate compaction is achieved

Lateral Earth Pressure
The lateral earth pressure acting on the drain structures will
depend on the type of backfill used the extent of the backfill
material and the compactive effort required on the material
behind the structure walls Granular material is recommended for
the backfill adjacent to the structures to reduce the compact ive
effort required and minimize the build up of additional lateral
earth pressures
For the design of the box culvert and manhole structures an
at rest earth pressure of 50 p c f equivalent fluid pressure is
recommended

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit deter
minations were performed on selected samples to aid in the
classification and evaluation of soil properties The results
of these tests are presented on the log of borings at the
appropriate sample depths The Atterberg Limits are also
summarized on Plates B 1 through B 5 and plotted on Plate B 6
Two specific gravity tests were performed on typical
samples of the underlying soil The test results are presented
on Plates B 1 through B 5
Nine laboratory CBR tests were performed on bulk samples
obtained from the auger cuttings near the proposed finished
pavement elevation to evaluate the subgrade support Capacity of
the compacted soil The test results are summarized on Plates
B 1 through B S
Twenty one one inch ring swell tests were performed on
remolded and undisturbed samples to evaluate the swelling
potential of the soils in the natural and compacted condition
The test results are summarized on Plates B 7 and B 8
Eleven direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed
samples to evaluate the strength characteristics of the natural
soil The test results are presented on Plates B 9 through
B 19
Four consolidation tests were performed on undisturbed
samples to evaluate the settlement characteristics of the
natural soils The test results are presented on Plates B 20
through B 23
Nine pH and resistivity tests were performed on selected
samples to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils along
the utility alignments The test results are summarized on
Plate B 24
