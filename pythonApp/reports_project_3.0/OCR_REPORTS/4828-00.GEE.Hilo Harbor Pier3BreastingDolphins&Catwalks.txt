1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Pier 3 Breasting Dolphins and Catwalks project located at the
Hilo Harbor on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the Pier 3
Breasting Dolphins and Catwalks also referred to as the Pier 3 extension project The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of pile foundations only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 030 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has
had activity during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna
Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located within the Hilo Harbor area Hilo Harbor lies at the
intersecting slopes of the Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea Volcanoes The lava flows of the
Mauna Loa Volcano are relatively young in terms of geologic time and may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old Below the basaltic lava flows of the Mauna Loa
Volcano the underlying lava flows of the Mauna Kea Volcano are believed to be Pliocene
to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The land surrounding the Hilo Harbor area was reclaimed from the bay by the
placement of fill over the lagoonal deposits and or coralline detritus The project site is
generally underlain by lagoonal deposits and deposits of calcareous sediments also
known as coralline detritus Alluvial deposits associated with the Wailuku River underlie
the lagoonal deposits and calcareous sediments Basalt formation from the recent Mauna
Loa lava flows may be encountered at greater depths below the alluvial deposits
2 2 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes are
so small that they can only be detected by instruments However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage

3 1 Seismic Design Considerations
We understand that this project will be designed in general accordance with the
requirements of the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC It should be noted that the
Island of Hawaii is located within Seismic Zone 4 with an effective peak ground
acceleration of 0 40g Based on the subsurface materials anticipated at the site very soft
and or loose lagoonal deposits over coralline detritus and alluvium and the geologic
setting of the area the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint
as a Soft Soil Profile corresponding to a Soil Profile Type Se
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and a design peak ground
acceleration of 0 40g liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable settlements to the
mudline surface Therefore the effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into
account in the design of the new pier extension Soil liquefaction is a condition where
saturated cohesionless soils located near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of
strength due to the build up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress
applications induced by earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand
deposit is subjected to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify
and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure
If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water
pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained
vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure
essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous

and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained

The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake

Downdrag Loads
As previously mentioned the loose lagoonal and bay deposits may be susceptible
to liquefaction in case of a seismic event producing a peak horizontal ground
acceleration of about 0 10g to 0 15g at the site In the event of seismically induced
liquefaction settlement of the loose lagoonal and bay deposits will occur at the site
When the loose lagoonal and bay deposits move downward relative to the pile the
downward movement will result in negative skin friction forces generally known as
downdrag forces acting on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force
depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer relative density of the potentially
liquefiable loose lagoonal and bay deposits and the pile perimeter
Due to the potential for liquefaction of the loose lagoonal and bay deposits at the
site settlement of the loose lagoonal and bay deposits will result in downdrag
loading of the deep foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable
materials Therefore the compressive load capacities of the piles will need to be
reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses Based
on our foundation analyses we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the
vertical piles due to the potential for liquefaction of the lagoonal and bay deposits

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
up to about 105 000 foot pounds per blow The hammer should be equipped with
an energy control level such that driving within the loose soft soils may be
controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development within the
concrete piles which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing the potential for damage to the piles

Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 48 inch diameter steel pipe piles pile driving may be
terminated when the aggregated blow counts are at least 6 blows per inch for the
last 12 inches or 7 blows per inch for the last 4 inches The preliminary driving
criteria should be confirmed and or modified after selection of the pile driving
equipment and performance of a wave equation analysis In addition the pile
driving criteria may be modified during the test pile driving to accommodate
variations of soil conditions in the field Due to the uncertainties regarding the
selection of the pile driving equipment the driving criteria for the precast
prestressed concrete piles should be determined following the performance of a
wave equation analysis
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be
rejected or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is
interrupted for more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required
depth During soft driving such as when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the
hammer stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant
tensile stresses do not develop within the concrete pile
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils the supporting capacity of each pile should be confirmed in the field by a
representative from our office during the driving operation It should be noted that
the pile design was developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration
data A test pile program should be carried out to confirm our preliminary pile
design and establish the driving criteria for the 48 inch diameter steel pipe piles
The production pile driving criteria may be revised based on the test pile program
As previously mentioned considering the relatively small number of precast
concrete piles for this project we believe that a test pile program will not be
required for the 20 inch octagonal precast concrete piles

Test Pile Program
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a test pile
program be conducted for the 48 inch diameter steel pipe piles In general we
recommend that the test pile program consist of a minimum of four test piles
which includes at least one static load test The four test piles should be spread
out along the length of the new pier extension The preliminary locations of the
test pile program are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The test piles may be
incorporated into the actual foundation system upon the evaluation of the static
load test results In general the test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill
the following main objectives
1 To examine the ability of the selected hammer to drive the piles to
the recommended depths and driving resistance
2 To assess if the pre drilling depth needs to be modified or revised
3 To evaluate and modify if necessary the preliminary driving criteria
from the wave equation analysis
4 To more accurately determine the probable production pile tip
elevations
Test piles should be furnished at least 10 feet longer than the estimated pile length
to account for potential variation in the subsurface conditions In addition the test
pile program should provide flexibility to allow for addition or relocation of the test
piles as may be deemed necessary by our office to suit the conditions
encountered during the test pile driving Re striking of the test piles a minimum of
72 hours after the initial pile driving should be required as part of the test pile
program
One of the four test piles should be designated as the load test pile The load test
pile should be instrumented with steel tell tale rods for load testing purposes The
tell tale rods should be placed starting from the bottom at an elevation of about
2 feet above the tip of the steel pipe pile and subsequently at 10 foot intervals up
to slightly above the mudline The pile load test should be performed in general

Construction Considerations
We anticipate that the steel pipe piles may be filled with water during driving to
counter the buoyancy forces Due to the anticipated presence of water inside the
steel pipe piles concrete placement by tremie methods is recommended
The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an
upward fashion from the bottom of the steel pipe In general a low shrink concrete
mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of
the aggregates from the concrete mix

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 133 3 and
148 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a portable and
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with rotary coring tools The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock or consolidated materials encountered at the site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation or consolidated
materials The rock or consolidated material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
