2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from
Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as
the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south
these alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coraV algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from
zero feet in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach
This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over
the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has
continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

localizer antenna array site generally range from about 28 to 30 feet MSL

3 1 Glide Slope Antenna
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe
that a mat type foundation bearing into the hard coral formation may be used to support
the glide slope antenna planned for the project An allowable bearing pressure of up to
4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of mat foundations bearing
on the hard coral formation anticipated at shallow depths This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces For mat foundation design a modulus of
subgrade reaction of 150 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used
The subgrade modulus is the modulus of vertical subgrade reaction for a 1 foot square
test plate
Resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the dead weight of the structure and
the mat foundation Additional resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the weight of
the backfill material placed above the foundations if applicable A unit weight of
110 pounds per cubic foot pcf may be used for the on site coralline materials
The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 95 percent
relative compaction where possible to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing
surface prior to placement of the reinforcing steel and concrete If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation may be
backfilled with on site coralline materials or imported granular soils compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down
to the underlying competent materials Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil

established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the footings should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge
of the utility trench or the footing should be embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total foundation settlements under the anticipated light
structural loads to be less than 1 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 40 may be used between the mat foundation and the in situ hard coral
formation Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the backfills around the mat are well compacted and consist of granular
soils The passive resistance may be increased to 750 pcf if the mat foundation is cast
neat against the in situ hard coral formation Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

3 3 Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration small voids were present in one of the borings
drilled at the site In addition surface cavities that have been filled with gravel were also
observed during our field exploration Therefore there is a potential for presence of more
voids and cavities in the coral formations encountered at the site In order to reduce the
potential for structural damage due to collapse of the cavities below foundations a
program of cavity probing and grouting is often conducted at the foundation locations
during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that three probe holes in a triangular
grid pattern be drilled below the mat foundation at the glide slope antenna site For the
localizer antenna array we recommend that probe holes be drilled at 10 foot intervals
along the perimeter thickened edge footings The probe holes should be at least 3 inches

3 5 Site Preparation
Based on the relatively level topography at the project site we anticipate that
minimal site grading work will be required for the project construction At the on set of
earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to
expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or designated to receive fills should be
scarified to a minimum depth of about 8 inches where possible moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory
drum roller a minimum of eight passes to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing
near surface cavities Soft loose weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during site
preparation operations should be over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with structural fill material well graded 3 inch minus
granular materials compacted to a minimum 95 percent relative compaction The material
resulting from the over excavation should be removed and properly disposed of off site
The proof rolling operations should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs
Imported fill material required to raise the existing ground surface to the design
finish grades should consist of non expansive imported granular material such as crushed
coral or basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of
20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material
should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use

3 6 Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new facilities In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or S4C is
recommended under the electrical duct lines Free draining granular materials such as
clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 21 and
22 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate locations
of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of
an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
