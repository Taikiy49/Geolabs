PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located near the intersection of Kaumualii Highway and Kikialoa 
Harbor Road in Kekaha on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii.  We understand that a sinkhole was 
formed on Kaumualii Highway in the location of the three 60-inch diameter pipe culverts 
crossing the highway.  In addition, we understand that one of the pipes was filled with 
concrete to keep the highway open. 
Based on the information provided, the existing pipe culverts will be replaced with a 
concrete box culvert structure with a headwall and wingwalls on each side of the highway. 
The highway will be widened on both sides. In addition, we understand that a bypass road 
will be constructed for the construction of the new box culvert structure. 
We understand that this is an emergency project, and the culvert repair needs to be 
performed as soon as possible.  Therefore, we understand performing borings is not 
feasible.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site consisted of conducting six DCP tests, 
designated as DCP Nos. 1 through 6. The DCP tests extended to depths of about 11.3 to 
17.9 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, three bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 to Bulk-3, were obtained to evaluate the 
subgrade characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate DCP test and bulk 
sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer was used for probing by driving an 8-mm diameter 
60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. The probe 
penetrations were recorded per every five blows and converted to standard penetration 
resistance (SPT, ASTM D1586) using the correlation between Penetration Index (PI) and 
SPT, developed by Sowers and Hedges and later Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and SPT blow 
counts are shown on the Logs of DCP at the appropriate depths. 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the project site 
generally consisted of loose to dense soils extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 17.9 feet below the existing ground surface. The surface soils consisted of silty 
sand. Detailed results of the DCP testing are presented on the DCP logs, Plates A-1.0 
through A-6.3. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the DCP locations at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to tides, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of DCP 
are presented in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples are presented in Appendix B.

The active condition assumes that the walls will be allowed to move laterally by up 
to about 1 to 2 inches in the event of an earthquake. The at-rest condition should 
be used when walls are restrained (<0.5 inches of lateral movement). The resultant 
force of the dynamic lateral earth pressures should be assumed to act through the 
mid-height of the wall. It should be noted that the forces due to dynamic lateral 
earth pressures presented above are in addition to the static lateral earth 
pressures. An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic 
lateral earth forces are accounted for in the design of the retaining structures. 
Drainage 
Retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of permeable material, such 
as AASHTO M43, No. 67 gradation material, placed directly around a perforated 
pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate 
outlet or weepholes. 
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain, may be 
used instead of the permeable drainage material. The prefabricated drainage 
product also should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of 
the retaining wall. Unless covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete, the upper 
12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials, such as 
well-graded soils, to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the 
walls. The weepholes should be spaced no more than 8 feet apart.

Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, and other unsuitable materials 
should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials designated to be reused as fill and/or 
backfill. Soft or wet soils encountered during clearing below areas designated to 
receive fill and areas along the stream bank within the temporary roadway 
embankment alignment should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense soils. 
Prior to filling, the existing ground exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches (where possible), moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. For 
pavement subgrades, the compaction requirement should be a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry

Fills and Backfills 
In general, we anticipate the excavations will likely encounter fill, alluvial deposits, 
and beach sand deposits. The excavated on-site soils may be used as a source of 
fill material, provided that the material meets the following requirements.  
In general, the on-site surface soils encountered during our field exploration should 
be suitable for use as general fill materials, provided that the maximum particle size 
is less than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The excavated on-site materials may 
be used as general fill or backfill materials if they are screened of the over-sized 
materials and/or processed to meet the gradation requirements (less than 3 inches 
in the largest dimension). In addition, fill materials should be free of vegetation and 
deleterious materials. Excavated soft and wet soils may not be reused as a source 
of fill and backfill materials. 
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should consist of non-expansive 
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The select granular fill should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest 
dimension. The material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve. Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance 
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
All fills to be placed should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability for the new embankment fill against sliding.  The filling operations should 
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in 
accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented below. Fill slopes 
should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to 
obtain a well-compacted slope face. 
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where fill is 
located in paved areas along Kaumualii Highway at the culvert replacement, the

Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a

Excavation 
We anticipate that the project site is generally underlain by fills, alluvial deposits, and 
beach sand deposits. In addition, we anticipate that the surface fill, alluvial deposits, 
and beach sand deposits may be excavated readily with normal heavy excavation 
equipment, such as excavators. However, cobbles and boulders are frequently 
encountered in fill and alluvial deposits and should be expected. Excavations that 
encounter cobbles and boulders within the on-site soils may require the use of 
hoerams or chipping.
