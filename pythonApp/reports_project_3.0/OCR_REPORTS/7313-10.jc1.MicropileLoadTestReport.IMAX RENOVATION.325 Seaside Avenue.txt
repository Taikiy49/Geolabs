MICROPILE LOAD TESTING REPORT 
IMAX RENOVATION 
325 SEASIDE AVENUE 
HONOLULU, OAHU, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Wong: 
To evaluate the micropile ultimate load capacity, one static compressive load test 
and one tensile load test were conducted on two pre-production sacrificial micropiles for the 
IMAX Renovation project.  The project site is located at 325 Seaside Avenue in the Waikiki 
district of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  This letter report presents the results of 
our field observations, the test data collected during the testing conducted on July 31 and 
August 1, 2017, and our analyses and recommendations. 
The sacrificial compressive load test micropile (TP-1) is located at the rear of the 
building adjacent to an existing loading dock within the deep alluvial soil profile area. The 
tensile load test micropile (TP-2) is situated in the front of the building adjacent to Seaside 
Avenue within the coralline material soil profile area. Two load test micropiles were installed 
by Kelikai, Inc. on July 17 through 19, 2017, utilizing a Beretta T24 track-mounted drill rig. 
The compressive load test micropile (TP-1) extended to a depth of about 96 feet 
below the existing ground surface with 65 feet of 5.5-inch outside diameter (OD) steel casing 
left in-place as the unbonded zone (about 1 foot of the steel casing stick up above the ground 
surface).  The tensile load test micropile (TP-2) extended to a depth of about 75 feet below 
the ground surface with about 45 feet of 5.5-inch OD steel casing left in-place (about 1 foot 
of the steel casing stick up above the ground surface).  Records of the micropile installation 
are provided in this letter report for reference, as shown on Plates 1.1 through 2.2. 
Based on our field observations, two pre-production sacrificial testing micropiles were 
installed by advancing the steel casing to the design depths of about 96 and 75 feet below 
the ground surface. The testing micropiles generally encountered loose/soft lagoonal and/or 
alluvium deposits to medium dense overlying coralline materials occurring at depths of about 
62 and 40 feet below the ground surface.  Upon reaching the tip elevations, the cement 
grout was pumped via a tremie pipe from the bottom to the top of casing, displacing the

The steel casings were extracted from the bottom 32 feet of the testing micropiles, 
providing bond lengths within the coralline materials while the remaining of the steel casings 
about (65 and 45 feet) were left in-place at the top of the micropiles (unbonded zone) as 
permanent casings. The permanent steel casings were then further extracted and plunged 
3 feet into the coralline materials. It should be noted that the pre-production sacrificial 
micropiles were extended about 1 and 3 feet above the adjacent ground surface for the load 
testing access. 
The pre-production compressive load test was conducted in general accordance with 
ASTM D1143. The allowable compressive load capacity was specified as 80 kips for the 
micropiles. A hydraulic jack system reacting against a load frame supported by four reaction 
micropiles (one at each corner of the load frame) was used to apply the testing load to the 
compressive load test micropile. In general, the reaction micropiles extended to a depth of 
about 96 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The pre-production tensile load test was conducted in general accordance with 
ASTM D3689. The allowable tensile load capacity was also specified as 80 kips for the 
micropiles. A hydraulic jack system reaction against a reaction beam was used to apply the 
testing load to the tensile load test micropile. In general, the reaction beam was supported 
on the cribbing system placed at two ends of the beam. 
A load cell was used to measure the loads applied to the micropiles.  The micropile 
settlements were measured with two dial gages attached to a reference beam.  The dial 
gages were placed on the left and right sides of the load test micropiles. In addition, two 
1/100-inch division rulers were set on each end of the load frame and a remote reference 
was mounted on an independent beam that holds the dial gages in place, to verify potential 
ground settlement that could affect the dial gages readings. The surveyor’s level was used 
to observe the rulers and the remote reference for movement. 
In general, both compressive and tensile test micropiles were loaded to a maximum 
test load of 280 kips in a 20-kip load increment.  Each load increment was held for at least 
5 minutes.  The test load was held for 10 minutes at 120-kip load increment, about 1.5 times 
of the design allowable compressive and tensile load capacities, to evaluate the effects of 
creep.  The gross rates of settlement at the 120-kip load increment were less than 
0.01 inches per hour. The test load was then unloaded and reloaded, and further increased 
to the maximum test load of 280 kips in the 20-kip load increment. Subsequently, the 
micropiles were unloaded in approximately five equal decrements and held for 5 minutes.

Total micropile head displacements at 160-kip and 200-kip load increments were 
about 0.477 and 0.633 inches for compressive load testing, respectively, while 0.425 and 
0.549 inches for the tensile load testing, as illustrated on Plates 4 and 6. 
Based on the above load test results, we believe the 5.5-inch OD micropile installed 
at the project site with a bond length of about 32 feet in the coralline materials is capable of 
supporting allowable compressive load capacity of 120 kips (which are greater than the 
original design load capacities) with an adequate factor of safety and with reasonable 
settlement characteristics. 
Therefore, we recommend increasing the allowable compressive and tensile load 
capacities to 120 kips for 5.5-inch OD micropile with a bond length of at least 32 feet. 
Alternatively, the allowable compressive load capacity may be further increased to 150 kips 
for 7.0-inch OD micropile with a bond length of at least 32 feet with an allowable tensile load 
capacity of 140 kips. 
We also recommend using No. 11 Grade 150 reinforcing bar with a grout minimum 
compressive strength of 5,000 pounds per square inch (psi) and a water-cement ratio of 
0.40 or less. The structural engineer should be consulted to confirm the structural capacity 
of the micropile foundation.
