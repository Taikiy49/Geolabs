Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from 
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, 
lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a 
broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is located on the 
southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the vicinity of the project site 
and to the south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of 
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway 
to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.

During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet 
waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys 
in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous 
reef materials.  
Based on our experience in the Ko`Olina area, it appears that much of the area was 
a shallow lagoon situated behind a fringing reef along the vicinity of the current coastline.  
Fluvial processes transported sediments to the lagoon and filled it to create fast land.  This 
area was used for ranching and the cultivation of sugar cane from the late 1800’s through 
the 1970’s, at which time, agricultural use of the property was ended.  Placement of 
man-made fills and commercial development of the area within the last 30 to 40 years 
have brought the Ko`Olina area to its present form. 
Existing Conditions 
As mentioned previously, the project site is located within the Ko’Olina Resort area 
in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Plan, 
Plate. The project site (Lot Nos. 54B, 54C and 55) is bounded by Lot Nos. 53A and 53D to 
the north, Aliinui Drive and Mauloa Place to the east, Lot Nos. 24 and 34 to the south, and 
the Pacific Ocean to the west, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  Lot Nos. 53A, 53D and 
34 have already been developed for high-rise resort use. 
The majority of the project site is generally level with no local topographic highs or 
depressions.  Based on elevations from Google Earth™, the existing ground elevations at 
the site range from about +26 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) along Aliinui Drive to about 
+34 feet MSL along the western boundary over the beachfront promenade. The ground 
surface is generally covered with grass forming an open lawn area. 
Based on our research and review of available in-house information, fills of up to 
about 14 feet in height were placed at the makai portion of the project site during the initial 
development of Ko’Olina Resort. The fills placed at the project site appear to decrease in 
thickness when progressing toward the mauka portion of the project site. In addition, some 
areas in the mauka portion of the project site may have been mass graded in cut.

Field Reconnaissance 
On September 29, 2015, a field reconnaissance was made of the site by our project 
engineer/geologist.  The following were noted during that reconnaissance: 
The topography of the slope is nearly level with a nearly imperceptible slope 
from the western boundary down to the eastern side.  Based on Google 
Earth™ elevations, this slope lies at an inclination of about 0.8 percent, or 
8 feet in 960 feet. 
To the west of the western boundary, the ground slopes down to the 
beachfront promenade at a ratio of about two to two and one half horizontal 
to one vertical (2H:1V – 2.5H:1V).  This slope is heavily covered with low 
vegetation, such as wedelia; and, shows no signs of erosion or instability. 
The surface soil throughout most of the site is a medium brown silt with 
some clay.  The upper surface of the soil is generally dry and friable but the 
material is firmer below the upper inch or so.  This silty soil does not appear 
to be highly expansive. It is believed that the silty soil was imported as 
topsoil. 
In some areas of the site, tan sandy gravel is exposed at the surface.  This 
material is believed to be part of the mass grading fill material. 
At isolated locations on the site, primarily in the western portion of the 
property, cobbles were exposed at the surface along with a few boulders.  It 
is believed that these oversized materials were part of the mass grading fill. 
In general, the site appears to be suitable for development with no obvious 
soft areas or signs of instability. 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our archival review in the project vicinity indicates that the subsurface conditions at 
the site may consist of surface fills overlying lagoonal and coralline reef deposits. The 
surface fill materials may generally comprise medium dense to dense silty sands and 
medium stiff to very stiff clayey soils. Cobbles and boulders may be embedded in localized 
fill materials. 
The lagoonal deposits consist of loose and/or soft silty or sandy soils and are 
susceptible to soil liquefaction induced in a moderate seismic event (M6+). The 
liquefaction will induce appreciable ground settlements and should be taken into 
consideration in the design of the planned development.

The coralline reef deposits consist of medium hard to hard weathered coral and 
sandstone formation interbedded with medium dense coralline detritus. It is not uncommon 
to encounter large cavities embedded in the coral and sandstone formation. The large 
cavities may collapse under foundation loading, causing signification ground settlement 
and structure failure. 
There is evidence that an old alluvial channel may be traversing through the site. 
Along this channel, there will be a greater thickness of soft alluvial materials and the depth 
to the underlying coral formation would most likely be greater. 
We anticipate that groundwater levels will fluctuate from approximate 
Elevations +2 to -1 Mean Sea Level (MSL). Due to the close proximity of the project site to 
the Pacific Ocean, groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations, 
seasonal precipitation, storm surge, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and other 
factors. 
It should be noted shallow groundwater (i.e., the basal groundwater table), 
underlying the site is brackish to saline groundwater occurring in a shallow groundwater 
table aquifer in the upper coral and sediments (“caprock”) underlying the site. There is a 
deeper artesian aquifer occurring in the basalt formation that underlies the caprock 
formation. This basal artesian aquifer contains fresh to brackish groundwater confined 
under a positive piezometric head (i.e., the groundwater surface in this aquifer would rise 
above mean sea level). 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the project site 
may be subject to seismic activity.  Therefore, seismic design considerations will need to 
be addressed to evaluate potential for soil liquefaction. Based on the subsurface 
conditions anticipated and a design peak ground acceleration of 0.21g, liquefaction may 
occur resulting in appreciable settlements at the ground surface. Therefore, the effects of 
potential liquefaction should be taken into consideration in the design of the proposed 
development. 
Because of anticipated liquefiable soils, the project site would need to be classified 
as Site Class F based on IBC 2006 (Table No. 1613.5.2). Per Section 1613.5.5.1 of the 
IBC 2006, a site-specific ground motion study will need to be performed to determine the 
appropriate values for seismic design considerations. 
Based on our experience of site-specific seismic design response evaluation, we 
anticipate that Seismic Design Category “C” may be adopted in accordance with the 
International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006) and the City and County of Honolulu 
Amendments. We recommend performing seismic shear wave velocity testing to a 
minimum depth of 100 feet below the ground surface, to verify the seismic design Site

Deep Foundation System 
We envision that the proposed development will consist of new high-rise buildings. 
Therefore, we anticipate large column loads will be required for the design of the high-rise 
buildings. Therefore, we anticipate the new high-rise buildings to be supported on a deep 
foundation system, such as cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts or auger cast-in-place 
(ACIP) piles. 
Drilled Shafts 
The drilled shafts, consisting of 36-inch and/or 42-inch diameter, would derive 
vertical support primarily from friction between the concrete shaft and the surrounding 
subsurface materials (coral/sandstone formation and/or coralline detritus). The 
end-bearing component of the drilled shafts will be discounted due to difficulties 
associated with obtaining a clean bottom appropriate for end-bearing drilled shafts during 
construction. Each column would be supported on either a single and multiple drilled 
shafts designed to support the structural loads of the columns.  
The load bearing capacities of the drilled shafts will depend largely on the relative 
density of the soils and quality of the coral/sandstone formation within the bearing strata. 
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site, we envision that the allowable 
compressive load capacities of the drilled shafts may range from 500 to 2,000 kips with a 
factor of safety of 2.0. To achieve sufficient compressive load capacity, the drilled shafts 
would extend to tip elevations of -15 to -75 feet MSL. We assume cut-off elevation of 
drilled shafts at about +2 feet MSL. 
Localized zones of hard coral/sandstone formation should be anticipated during the 
drilled shaft installation. The thickness and hardness of the coral formation will vary across 
the project site.  In addition, cobbles and boulders are anticipated within the fill materials at 
the project site.  Therefore, some difficult drilling conditions likely will be encountered at the 
project site and should be expected. The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the 
appropriate equipment and tools to drill through these types of obstructions (hard 
coral/sandstone formation and cobbles/boulders). 
Based on our experience in the vicinity, we envision the drilled shaft construction 
method may consist of temporary casing or the use of drilling fluids (mineral and/or 
polymer slurry) with concrete placement by tremie methods. 
In addition, the actual volume of concrete required for the drilled shaft may be 
appreciably more than the theoretical concrete volume. Based on our experience in the 
project vicinity, the average concrete over-pour for the drilled shafts may vary from 50 to 
150 percent over the theoretical concrete volume. Therefore, sufficient contingent fund

Augered Cast-In-Place (ACIP) Piles 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we believe that ACIP piles may be 
considered to support the new high-rise building structures.  ACIP piles with diameter of 18 
or 24 inches may be extended to elevations varying from -15 to -70 feet MSL to achieve 
allowable compressive load capacities between 250 and 1,100 kips. We assume cut-off 
elevation of ACIP pile at about +2 feet MSL. 
The above allowable compressive load capacity for the ACIP piles is to support 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by up to one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such 
as wind or seismic forces. A factor of safety of 2.0 was used in arriving at the allowable 
compression load capacity. 
It should be noted that the load bearing capacity of the ACIP piles will depend 
largely on the relative density of the soils and quality of the coralline deposits within the 
bearing strata. Therefore, the foundation subcontractor’s personnel on the project site who 
will be responsible for the installation should have the relevant experience with the 
augering and pumping equipment, experience with placing ACIP piles under similar 
subsurface conditions, as well as experience with handling the special grout mixes, 
admixtures, etc. 
As indicated previously, cavities and voids are commonly encountered in the 
coralline deposits in the project vicinity. Therefore, the actual volume of grout required to 
fill the ACIP piles may be appreciably more than the theoretical grout volume. Based on 
our experience, we envision that average grout factor on the order of about 100 to 120 
percent over the theoretical grout volume (total of 200% to 220% of the theoretical grout 
volume) may be required for the ACIP piles.  
Shallow Foundations 
We envision that some one-story structures may be constructed at the project site.  
In general, we believe that minor structures with column loads of less than 75 kips may be 
supported on shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings.  In 
addition, structures supported on shallow foundations should not be structurally connected 
to the tower structures, which will be supported on a deep foundation system. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on the re-compacted on-site materials.  This 
bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.

Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent

sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope.  Foundations

footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We envision that ground floor slabs for the new building construction will consist of 
reinforced concrete slabs-on-grade. We assume that the slabs-on-grade for the proposed 
building structure will be supported either on new compacted fills placed to raise the 
existing ground surface to the finished subgrades or on the existing ground conditions. 
As mentioned above, the project site could be subject to appreciable ground settlements in 
the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event (M6+). Seismically induced 
ground settlements should be anticipated and the building floor slab may sustain some 
damage. Therefore, there exists the potential for damage and distress to the building 
slabs-on-grade (not structurally supported). To reduce the potential adverse effects on the 
building slabs-on-grade resulting from potential ground settlement, considerations may be 
given to increasing the slab thickness and incorporating steel reinforcement into the 
concrete slab. 
As an alternative to slabs-on-grade, consideration may be given to structurally 
supporting the ground floor slab on the ACIP pile foundations to reduce the potential for 
damage to the interior of the building in the event of seismically induced ground 
settlements.
