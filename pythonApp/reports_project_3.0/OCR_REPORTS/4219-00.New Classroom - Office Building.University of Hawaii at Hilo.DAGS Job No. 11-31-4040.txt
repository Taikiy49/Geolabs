INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed new classroon office building project located within the Hilo campus of the
University of Hawaii on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
classroom office building project These recommendations are intended for design input only
and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These lava flows are of the Pleistocene
Epoch and Recent Age and belong to the Ka u Volcanic Series The lava flows were
subsequently covered by volcanic ash deposits locally referred to as the Pahala Ash The
Pahala Ash is an air laid deposit of volcanic ash from the Hamakua Volcanic Series of
Mauna Loa Mountain during the Pleistocene Epoch The Pahala Ash generally has high
in situ moisture contents and low in situ densities The ash is characterized as having
thixotropic properties i e it is susceptible to significant loss of strength when remolded under
equipment vehicular or seismic loads
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular
porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas The cavities were
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4219 00 Page 5
formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow
cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally
smaller in horizontal extent

in an east west direction with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about
165 feet MSL at the west to about 142 feet MSL at the east

In general our field exploration at the northwestern portion of the project site Boring
Nos 1 and 2 encountered a surficial layer of volcanic ash soils consisting of soft to medium
stiff clayey silts extending to depths of about 5 to 9 feet below the existing ground surface
The ash material is characterized by a low in situ dry density 40 to 50 pounds per cubic foot
and a high in situ moisture content 60 to 70 percent The ash encountered at the project
site is considered to be sensitive to disturbance The surficial ash soil layer appears to be
thickest at the north Boring No 1 decreases in thickness towards the central portion of the
site Boring No 2 and completely disappears as the terrain transitions to the southern
portion of the project site Boring Nos 3 through 5
The surficial ash soil was generally underlain by hard basalt rock formation
interbedded with some thin clinker layers extending to the maximum depth drilled of
approximately 25 feet below the existing ground surface Cavities on the order of about 1 to
2 feet in vertical extent were encountered during our field exploration at depths of about
3 and 8 feet below the existing ground surface in Boring Nos 4 and 5 respectively
Water was encountered in Boring Nos 1 and 2 at depths of about 11 and 16 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration We believe that the
water levels observed in the drilled borings could be the result of our drilling operations and
do not represent the static groundwater table in the vicinity Groundwater was not
encountered in the other three borings drilled for this project However groundwater levels
can fluctuate depending on factors such as seasonal rainfall storm water run off
groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the northwestern portion of the project site generally
encountered a surficial layer of volcanic ash soils overlying clinkers and hard basalt rock
formation at greater depths Volcanic ash is generally a thixotropic soil i e the soil loses
significant strength temporarily when remolded and or disturbed especially during a seismic
event Due to the heavy structural loads anticipated for the proposed building we
recommend that shallow foundations extending through the surficial ash soils bearing on the
underlying dense clinkers or hard basalt rock formation be used for the foundation support
of the proposed 3 story building structure In addition it should be noted that the clinkers
encountered at the footing subgrade elevations might exist in a loose and uncompacted
state Therefore the loose clinkers if encountered at the footing subgrade elevations
should be over excavated to expose the underlying dense materials or hard basalt rock
formation
Due to the thixotropic nature of the volcanic ash soils encountered at the project site
we recommend that the surficial ash soils encountered within the building footprint be entirely
removed and replaced with granular fill Based on our field exploration and the proposed
finished floor elevations we anticipate that the new building slabs will likely straddle across
the compacted granular fill replacement for the volcanic ash the in situ clinkers and hard
basalt rock formation To reduce the potential for differential settlement and cracking of the
slab resulting from differing subgrade materials we recommend that the basalt rock
formation encountered at the slab subgrade level be over excavated a minimum depth of
12 inches and replaced with compacted granular fill material to provide more uniform support
for the slabs
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic
lava flows we recommend that the siab subgrades be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton

cavities in addition a program of probing and grouting the footing locations should be
implemented during the project construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the heavy structural loads
anticipated for the subject project we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread
and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed building structure Due
to the thixotropic nature of the volcanic ash soils encountered as discussed above we
recommend that the footings be extended through the surficial volcanic ash soils and bear
on the underlying dense clinkers or hard basalt rock formation An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of
foundations bearing on the dense clinkers or basalt rock formation anticipated at relatively
shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Depending on the depth of the clinkers or basalt rock formation
below the finished grades deeper footing embedment depths may be required at localized
areas to provide a competent bearing surface for the footings It should be noted that the
clinkers encountered at the footing subgrade elevations might exist in a loose and
uncompacted state Therefore we recommend that the loose clinkers if encountered at the
foundation subgrade elevation be over excavated to expose the underlying dense materials
or hard basalt rock formation The resulting over excavation may be backfilled with lean
concrete or the bottom of footing may be extended deeper to bear directly on the underlying
dense materials or hard basalt rock formation
For footings constructed near the tops of siopes or on sioping ground conditions the
footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face Footings
located adjacent to planned retaining walls or other below grade structures should be
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4219 00 Page 9
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or below grade
structure
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the footings are designed and constructed in accordance with the recommendations
presented herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance between
the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings A coefficient
of friction of 0 50 may be used for footings bearing on the underlying dense clinkers or hard
basalt rock formation Resistance to the lateral loads due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 400 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
pcf These values assume that the concrete for footings is cast neat against the basalt rock
or that the soils around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be
neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of

Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration and previous experience in the area cavities and or
voids may be present in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4219 00 Page 10
the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing
we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled at each spread
footing location one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area and at 10 feet on
center along wall foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and
should extend at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities
and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe
holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent
of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting
operations In lieu of the sand cement grout fluid lean concrete such as controlled low
strength material flowable fill may also be used for the grouting operation
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition we recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and
grouting operations are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between the
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the construction
schedule
The probing and grouting program should be designated as a Special Inspection
item and should be conducted under the observation of a representative from
Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities and or voids
and to allow for additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration and the design finished floor elevations for the project
we anticipate that the proposed building may straddle across the granular fill as
recommended above the medium dense to dense clinker materials and hard basalt rock
formation To reduce the potential for cracking of the slabs due to differential settlement
resulting from differing subgrade materials we recommend that the basalt rock formation
encountered at the slab subgrade level be over excavated a minimum depth of 12 inches
and replaced with compacted granular fill material for more uniform support
A basaltic termite barrier BTB sand layer should be incorporated into the
slab on grade design of the proposed building in accordance with the requirements of
D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61 dated Therefore for the interior
building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick
layer of cushion fill consisting of BTB be placed below the slabs to serve as a termite barrier
and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce the potential for future moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier
such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the BTB cushion layer Two inches
of BTB may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection for the
membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Where the slabs will be subjected to equipment operation such as the cooling tower
and chiller room a 6 inch layer of select borrow subbase compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction is recommended in lieu of the 4 inch thick BTB cushion layer
The 2 inch BTB and vapor barrier may also be omitted for these slabs The select borrow
subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of
about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
select borrow subbase
Based on the preliminary plans provided we understand that exterior pedestrian
walkways and concrete sidewalks are also planned for the subject project It is our opinion

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii dynamic
lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining structures
For seismic loading conditions a force of 13 5H pounds per lineal foot H in feet
due to dynamic lateral earth pressures based on a of 0 3g may be used for
design The dynamic lateral earth force presented above is in addition to the static
lateral earth pressures The resultant force should be assumed to act through the
mid height of the wall An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when
dynamic lateral earth forces are accounted for in the design of the retaining structure
Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide zone of permeable material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around a
perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to an
appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill material
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches to facilitate compaction of the backfill behind
the retaining wall Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill
should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for water
infiltration behind the walls

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths ranging between
about 10 and 25 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches
of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling
is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
