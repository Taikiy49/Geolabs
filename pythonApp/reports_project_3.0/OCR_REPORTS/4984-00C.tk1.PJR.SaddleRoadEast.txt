SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be required for the Saddle Road East Side roadway alignment
located between Milepost 6 and Milepost 28 of the existing Hawaii State Route 200
Saddle Road Because the project will involve more than 4 lane miles of new
pavements a detailed economic comparison involving a rigid pavement section and
flexible pavement section was performed for the new roadway project
Based on our field exploration program experience in the project area and the
current design concept we envision that the new pavements will be constructed either on
6 inch minus granular fill materials along fill areas or on the in situ basalt rock formation
in cut areas Where basalt rock is exposed at the pavement subgrade level the
subgrade should be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of
eight passes If volcanic ash soils are exposed at the pavement subgrade level we
recommend that the volcanic ash deposits be removed to provide a minimum of 2 feet of
compacted granular fill material below the pavement structure bottom of the ACB layer
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated the traffic data provided and our
preliminary cost comparisons we recommend that the pavement section for the Saddle
Road East Side alignment located between Milepost 6 and Milepost 28 of the existing
Hawaii State Route 200 Saddle Road consist of 4 inches of AC on 7 inches of ACB In
order to provide for proper grade control on the subgrade directly below the ACB
consideration should be given to providing a minimum of 4 inches of aggregate base
course 1 inch minus materials below the ACB layer for ease of construction and grade
control purposes
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be adequately sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the pavements to appropriate drainage structures The text of this report
should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific recommendations for pavement
design

Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our engineering
analyses performed for the design of new pavements for the Saddle Road East Side
Alignment of the new Saddle Road State Route 200 project located in the Districts of
North Hilo and South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii More specifically the project is
located approximately between Milepost 6 and Milepost 28 of the existing Hawaii State
Route 200 Saddle Road The general location and vicinity of the project limits are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the design of pavements resulting from our literature research
site reconnaissance field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
performed for the project The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest and youngest island in the Hawaiian
Archipelago covers an area of approximately 4 030 square miles The Island of Hawaii
was formed by the coalescence of five primary shield volcano mountains The five
primary shield volcanoes include the following
Kohala located at the northern tip of the island which is long extinct
Mauna Kea located at the northern central portion of the island which
has had some activity during recent geologic time
Hualalai located at the western portion of the island which last erupted
in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea located at the southern and eastern portions of
the island both of which are still active
Submarine volcanic activity on the Island of Hawaii is believed to have
commenced in the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch about 1 to 2 million years
ago and continues today with eruptive activity at Kilauea Volcano It is believed that
the Island of Hawaii breached the ocean surface approximately 500 000 years ago
Due to the coalescence of the flanks of the volcanoes composing the Island of Hawaii
the older mountain flanks have been overlapped and buried by recent volcanic
eruptions therefore the evolution of the broad dome shape of the Island of Hawaii is
visible today The summit elevation of Mauna Kea Volcano resides at about
13 796 feet Mean Sea Level MSL and the summit elevation of Mauna Loa Volcano
resides at about 13 679 feet MSL as shown on the General Alignment Plan Plate 2
The broad high elevation region of the Humuula Saddle is located between the
summits of the Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes The elevation of the Humuula
Saddle region is on the order of approximately 6 500 feet MSL
The Saddle Road East Side project site is located on the broad easterly facing
mountain slopes formed by the coalescence of lava flows erupted from both the Mauna
Kea Volcano and Mauna Loa Volcano as shown on the General Alignment Plan

The more viscous a a lavas typically consist of thick rubbly and irregular clinker
surfaces consisting of loosely indurated basalt rock fragments cobble to small boulder
size with layer thickness generally on the order of about 8 to 20 feet Below the clinker
rubble surface very dense and often massive basalt rock termed blue rock may be
encountered The dense rock contains the core of the a a lava flow which may range to
several tens of feet in thickness The flows advanced downslope at a slower pace due
to the thickened viscous character of the lava therefore there is typically greater
surface topographic relief associated with the deposition of a a lava flows The surface
relief takes the form of rolling terrain encompassed by steep sided elevated mounds of
piled rocks located adjacent to topographic depressions The surface of the a a lava
flows is generally very porous due to the rubbly clinker character which encompasses
considerable void space between the piled rock fragments

Pahoehoe lava flows typically consist of more fluid lavas that flowed more
rapidly as successive flow layers averaging about 3 to 10 feet in thickness with only
limited inter stratified clinker seams The lava flows may be thicker where underlying
depressions were filled as a result of ponding lava or very thin where the lava streams
flowed readily down steeper underlying terrain Pahoehoe lava flows typically comprise
lower relief terrain consisting of broad and low hummocky ground with frequent surface
cracks and crevices derived from the cooling of the rock layers The pahoehoe flow
surface is generally hard with a limited mantle of clinker material Pahoehoe lava flows
are generally less porous than the surface clinker of a a lavas however the multitude of
surface cracks permits water percolation into the subsurface
Pahoehoe lavas typically contain internal flow cavities and void spaces derived
from the transfer and cooling of molten rock within the flow core The cavities may
occur as localized pockets or as elongated lava tubes that may extend for significant
distances miles along the lava flow lobe Lava tubes represent the drained conduit
which supplied the advancing flow front with molten rock Lava tubes may range in size
from several feet to over 40 feet in diameter and may consist of tube alignments that
branch converge plunge and breach the ground surface The supporting roof over the
buried tube structure may be as thin as several feet and represents a potential hazard
consisting of tube collapse and overlying ground settlement

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions along the proposed Saddle Road East Side Alignment
were explored by advancing 38 borings to depths ranging from about 5 to 35 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings drilled along the roadway alignment are
identified as Boring Nos 1 through 38 and the approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Alignment Plans Plates 2 1 through 2 7
The borings were sited at selected accessible locations along the existing
Saddle Road alignment and along some of the off road vehicle accessible trails that
intersect the proposed alignment In general the boring locations were distributed
evenly along the roadway alignment in order to obtain a general overview of the
subsurface conditions throughout the length of the alignment
The borings were sited at closer intervals between Milepost 19 and Milepost 28
to obtain detailed shallow subsurface information for pavement design because this
segment of the proposed roadway was designated as a priority for design work
As mentioned previously 38 borings were drilled as part of the preliminary
geotechnical exploration for the project Twenty six borings were advanced to depths of
about 5 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface for the purpose of evaluating the
shallow subsurface conditions along the existing roadway with respect to pavement
design Twelve borings were advanced to depths of about 19 to 35 feet below the
existing ground surface to obtain a general overview of the deeper subsurface
conditions at the project site The borings were advanced using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with wireline coring tools and solid stem augers

Fill Materials
As indicated previously we have assumed that the recommended pavement
structural section will be underlain by structural fill materials 6 inch minus granular fills
in the fill areas In general structural fill materials should consist of well graded
granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient
fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass For this project we
anticipate that the majority of the fill materials required for the grading work will consist
of the basalt rock formation and existing fill materials from the existing roadway
generated from the cut areas Cut materials generated from excavations into the
underlying basalt formation may be used as structural fill materials provided that they
are screened of the over sized materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension
and processed to meet the above gradation requirements for structural fill
Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive granular
material such as crushed basalt or cinder sands The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The
material should have a laboratory R Value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell value of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use

Compaction Requirements
Granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement for fills placed within 3 feet from the finished pavement grade should be
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry

density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Aggregate Base AB material required for the new pavements should conform to
the requirements stipulated in the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and
Public Works Construction 1994 The aggregate base should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and
compacted to not less than 95 percent of the maximum density according to
Section 106 09 Special Test Methods of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
Bridge and Public Works Construction
Asphalt concrete base ACB materials should consist of asphalt treated basaltic
aggregates placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to more than 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with AASHTO T 209 or ASTM D 2041 test procedures
Asphalt concrete AC material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401
Asphalt Concrete Pavement of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge
and Public Works Construction and its amendments

Superpave System
Based on the information provided we understand that it is desired to implement
the Superpave SUperior PERforming Asphalt PAVEments system for the proposed
Saddle Road East Side Alignment project The Superpave system was developed by
the Strategic Highway Research Program SHRP established by Congress from 1987
to 1992 The system was developed to provide highway engineers and contractors the
tools they need to design asphaltic concrete pavements that will perform better under
extremes of temperature and heavy traffic loads
The Superpave system primarily addresses two pavement distresses pavement
deformation which results from inadequate shear strength in the asphalt mix and low
temperature cracking which is generated when an asphaltic concrete pavement shrinks

and the tensile stress exceeds the tensile strength The Superpave system consists of
three interrelated elements 1 asphalt binder specifications 2 volumetric mix design
and analysis system and mix analysis tests and 3 performance prediction system that
includes computer software weather database and environmental and performance
models The Superpave system incorporates the performance based asphalt binder
specifications mixture design and analysis to optimize resistance to rutting low
temperature cracking and fatigue cracking of the asphaltic concrete pavements

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed Saddle Road East Side Alignment
were explored by drilling and sampling a total of 38 borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 38 The borings extended to depths ranging from about 5 to
35 feet below the existing ground surface Twelve bulk samples designated as Bulk 1
through Bulk 12 were obtained of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement
support characteristics and the quality of the materials for use as embankment fills The
approximate locations of the test borings drilled and bulk samples collected are shown
on the Alignment Plans Plates 2 1 through 2 7 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and HQ
triple tube wireline coring tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 38
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the project site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Wight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 1
Sixteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates B 2 through B 5
Six laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 6 through B 11
Two Laboratory Resistance R Value tests AASHTO T 190 93 were performed
on selected bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 12 and B 13
