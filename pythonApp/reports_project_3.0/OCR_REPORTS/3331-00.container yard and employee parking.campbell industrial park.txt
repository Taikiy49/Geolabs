INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed container yard and employee parking project at the
Distribution Center of Fleming Companies Inc within the Campbell Industrial Park in
the Ewa District on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates
nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located at the Distribution Center of Fleming
Companies Inc within the Campbell Industrial Park in the Ewa District on the island
of Oahu Hawaii We understand that the site is currently used as a storage yard for
large containers It is proposed to convert the project site into a parking area for
containers and employee vehicles with asphalt concrete pavement Based on the
schematic site plan provided a total of 69 and 117 parking stalls are planned for the
containers and employee vehicies respectively Our discussion with the project
architect indicated that minimal cuts and fills are planned for the site grading
Our previous experience in the area suggest that the project site may be
underlain by coral deposits at shallow depths Particular attention was given to the
pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils

SITE DESCRIPTION

Companies Inc within the Campbell Industrial Park in the Ewa District on the island
of Oahu Hawaii It is bounded by Malakole Street to the north an open area to the
east which is bounded by Kalaeloa Boulevard a City and County of Honolulu property
to the south and the existing parking lot of the Distribution Center to the west
The project site is approximately L shaped covering an area of about 4 acres as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 It is currently used as a storage yard for large
containers An existing shallow swale with sparse vegetation was observed at the

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 10 borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to depths of approximately 6 to 21 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a thin surficial fill layer of
approximately 1 to 4 feet thick consisting mainly of dense silty and gravelly sands with
some clay pockets in localized areas This was underlain by a very dense coral sand
and gravel deposit extending to the maximum depths explored of about 6 to 21 feet
below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in the two deep borings drilled Boring Nos 2
and 3 during our field exploration at depths of approximately 13 and 14 feet below
the existing ground surface These depths generally correspond to elevations of
approximately 1 and 2 5 feet MSL Groundwater levels are expected to vary with
seasonal rainfall tidal fluctuations and time of year
For more detailed descriptions of the materials encountered please refer to the
Logs of Borings presented on Plates A 1 through A 10

DISCUSSION _ AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration encountered dense silty sand fill soils to depths
of about 1 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface at the project site The fill soils
were underlain by dense coral sands and gravels extending to the maximum depths
drilled of about 6 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on the information provided we understand that flexible pavements are
planned for the container yard and employee parking areas at the subject site Where
the pavement areas will be subjected to the semi truck traffic such as the container
yard area and the access roadways we recommend that new flexible pavements
consisting of 3 inches of asphalt concrete over 8 inches of base course be used for
preliminary design purposes For the design of the employee parking area a flexible
pavement section consisting of 2 inches of asphalt concrete over 6 inches of base
course may be used at the subject site
Laboratory tests performed on the surficial fill soils indicate that some of the fill
material exhibit moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade soil preparation for the
new pavements in order to reduce the potential shrink swell effects of these near
surface soils
The subject site is located in an area where cavities and or voids are frequently
encountered in the coral formation To reduce the potential for loss of subgrade
support due to a collapse of these potential near surface cavities and or voids in the
coral formation we recommend that a subsurface survey program using GPR be
implemented during the project construction to aid in locating and delineating any
potential cavities near the pavement subgrade elevations

The base course beneath the asphalt concrete should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density CBR and density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above design
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a
minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to
reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a
subdrain system is recommended to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation
For long term performance we believe that a subdrain system should be considered
adjacent to paved landscape areas
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the concrete slabs Construction joints
should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints
at right angle intersections

Site Preparation
Grading plans were not available to us at the time this report was prepared
However based on our discussions with the project architect we understand that
minimal cuts and fills are anticipated for grading of the subject project
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing all areas beneath the future pavement areas should
be over excavated to the proposed finish subgrades The over excavated surface
should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches moisiure conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with the
procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where scarification is not possible due to the presence of hard coral deposit at the
subgrade levels the subgrade should be proof rolled with a D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy equipment for a minimum of six passes to help detect and collapse near surface
cavities
Silty and clayey soils encountered during the scarification process should be
removed and disposed of off site Any loose soft spots and or cavities encountered
during the over excavation scarification and proof rolling process should also be
removed and replaced with select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Particular attention should be given to the areas along

Select granular fill material needed for backfilling over excavations should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction Select granular fill material should consist
of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a California Bearing Ratio
CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than 1 percent when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain less than
20 percent passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill material should be tested and
approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use

Cavity Detection
Subsurface cavities and other voids are commonly encountered in coral
formations To reduce the possibility of pavement distress resulting from the collapse
of such near surface voids we recommend that a subsurface survey program using
GPR be implemented during the project construction to help locate and delineate these
potential cavities Our own experience indicates that GPR can be an cost effective and
efficient method of detecting near surface cavities in a large area underlain by coral
formation at shallow depths
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3331 00 Page 11
This method uses impulse radar technology to obtain a near continuous profile
of the subsurface The impulse propagates through the subsurface materials until it
encounters a soil interface void or embedded object Depending upon the dielectric
constant of the interface void or object a portion of the signal is reflected back This
reflected signal can be intercepted recorded and interpreted
Strong reflections show up as anomalies within the recorded data field
Anomalies may be interpreted as interfaces between materials of different consistency
composition or structure Generally strong anomalies within consolidated coral
formations may be considered to be voids The site could be geophysically surveyed
to detect any strong anomalies by setting up a gridline pattern across the site

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
10 borings to depths of about 6 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentation
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through
A 10
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
