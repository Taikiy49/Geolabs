INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed relative to the earth movement in the vicinity of Ailuna and Leighton Streets in
Wailupe Valley Oahu Hawaii
Presented in this report are our findings from the field exploration and
instrumentation evaluation of the possible mechanisms of the earth movements and
analyses of the potential causes of the earth movements Also included in this report are
some recommendations for immediate and future remedial measures to reduce the
potential for expansion of the movement into adjacent areas
The general location of the site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface soil
conditions within the vicinity of the earth movements to aid in the evaluation of the
possible causes of the slide and potential remedial work which may be undertaken The
critical items of interest were
1 the soil profile including the classification and strength determination of the
in situ materials
2 groundwater levels and their fluctuation in response to rainfall
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 2
3 the depth of the existing slide and
4 the current rate of earth ground movements within the slide and in the
areas adjacent to it
Our work on the project has included the following efforts
1 Reconnaissance of the project site and vicinity to make visual observations
of the surface soil conditions and movement damage to structures
2 Review of available soils and geologic information pertinent to the project
site and its vicinity
3 Drilling and sampling ten 10 borings to depths of 38 8 to 100 0 feet below
the existing ground surface
4 Installation of seven 7 piezometers in selected borings to measure and
monitor groundwater levels and fluctuations
5 Installation of three 3 slope inclinometers in selected borings to measure
and monitor the rate and location of lateral ground movements
6 Installation of a rain gauge at 859 Ailuna Street to supplement rainfall data
obtained from the Division of Water and Land Development State of Hawaii
7 Mapping of visual indications of surface ground movement within the area
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 3
8 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
to determine the classification and engineering properties of the materials
encountered
9 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data to postulate probable
ground movement mechanisms and recommendations for possible remedial
measures
10 Preparation of this report presenting the findings and recommendations
resulting from the exploration and analyses
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and laboratory testing are presented
in the appendices of this report
Brief Site History
The subdivision development was completed during the early to mid 1950 s The
original grading plans for the subdivision were not available for use in our exploration and
analytical efforts However the relatively steep terrain and alignment of the streets
indicates that the grading probably consisted of cutting the areas uphill of the roadway
to generate fill for the areas below the roads
lt was reported by most of the residents that prior to about 1985 they noted only
minimal ground movements or damage
Some of the residents have reported increased water runoff and seepage within
the last 3 to 5 years 1985 1990 A few also noticed signs of increased movement within
this period
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 4
It is the general consensus that large ground movements occurred relatively
rapidly during the first half of 1989 following a period of heavy rains in January of that
year
Following the ground movements and based on reports of heavy runoff from the
hillside above the residences along the uphill side of Leighton Street visual observations
of the interceptor ditch behind these houses were made to evaluate the condition of the
ditch t was observed that the interceptor ditch was generally in poor condition and
damaged in places
Temporary repairs were made to seal the cracks in the ditch in June 1989
Permanent repairs consisting of grouting cracks and voids under the ditch and
replacement of some of the CRM walls were completed in February 1990
METHODOLOGY OF EXPLORATION
In general the study of earth movements is a complex task which should be
carried out systematically at various stages through the use of various methods and
techniques of data collection to obtain pertinent information for evaluation of possible
causes of the ground movement Only when possible causes of the movement are
identified may meaningful analyses be undertaken and potentially effective remedial
measures be developed Such a complicated study may generally be divided into three
3 basic stages
1 Data gathering
2 Engineering analyses and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 5
3 Design of remedial measures
Since earth movements are dynamic problems i e conditions within the system
change and are changed by external influences on a real time basis these stages often
overlap In fact newly gathered data may often necessitate modifications in previous
analyses and design of remedial measures
For the current study the exploration consisted of the following efforts and
methodology
Site Reconnaissance
The first data gathering task was to make visual observations at the site in order
to delineate the general limits of the earth movements and to define the relative magnitude
of distresses to structures This included discussions with the residents regarding the
amount and type of damage experienced and the time s at which the damage occurred
These preliminary data were used to hypothesize possible causes of movement for the
planning of the initial boring and instrumentation program
Borings
Borings were drilled within the public rights of way Ailuna and Leighton Streets
in order to obtain a general soil profile for slope stability analyses and to evaluate the
potential for movement in adjacent presently stable areas
It should be noted that most of the earth movements have occurred within private
property and therefore subsurface data in those private properties would be invaluable
However rights of entry could not be obtained to allow drilling in the private property
during our exploration Preliminary data from two 2 borings drilled by another
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 6
geotechnical engineering consultant who had been retained by the attorney for the
residents were made available to us
The logs of the borings drilled by Geolabs are presented in Appendix A
Laboratory Soil Testing
Selected samples from the borings were tested for classification and strength
parameters for use in the engineering slope stability analysis Moisture contents were
also used to attempt to delineate saturated zones indicative of seepage The laboratory
test results are presented in Appendix B
Instrumentation
Instrumentation was installed in drilled borings to allow the continued collection of
data on a real time basis Two 2 types of instrumentation were installed in the borings
Electrical vibrating wire piezometers were installed in Boring 5 to
measure the fluctuation of groundwater levels in response to weather conditions The
piezometers automatically record the water levels at set intervals of time This continuous
record allowed us to observe the fluctuation of water levels with respect to time and
weather conditions
A summary of the piezometer readings to date are presented in Appendix D
Slope inclinometers were installed in three 3 of the borings to measure the
amount and depth of lateral ground movements These inclinometers are read by our
personnel at two 2 to four 4 week intervals to measure the rate of ground movement
and to monitor the effects if any of heavy rain on the movements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 7
A summary of the inclinometer readings to date are presented in Appendix E
Rainfall Data
Rainfall data was used to determine if there was a correlation between the amount
of rainfall and the groundwater levels below the site
Rainfall data was obtained through the courtesy of the State of Hawaii Department
of Land and Natural Resources Division of Water and Land Development and by
observation of a rain gauge installed by Geolabs These data are presented in Appendix
C
Aerial Photogrammetric Survey
An aerial photographic survey was conducted by R M Towill Corporation This
photographic survey was analyzed by photogrammetric methods to develop a base
topographic map covering the affected area and its vicinity This base map was used as
an aid in the mapping and stability analysis and to attempt to locate general adverse
topographic trends such as drainageways which could affect the hillside stability
Damage Mapping
Visual field surveys were conducted in September 1989 and also February and
March 1990 to identify surface features which might be indicative of the movements
Features such as overturned or displaced walls cracks in concrete slabs settlement of
foundations etc were superimposed on the topographic survey
These data were used to approximately delineate the current extent of the earth
movements and serve as snapshots of the present conditions for comparison with visual
damage surveys to be conducted in the future The damage survey maps are presented
on Plates 3 through 6

GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located on the eastern Koko Head side of Wailupe Valley
approximately midway into the head of the valley
Leighton and Ailuna Streets are two of the uppermost streets in the area and
generally extend parallel to the ground contours
The ground surface in the area generally slopes down to the west at an average
gradient of about four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V with localized areas as steep as
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V There are many existing CRM retaining walls within
the area There is an elevation difference of about 50 to 60 feet between Ailuna and
Leighton Streets
The ground surface in the area above Leighton Street steepens to an overall
gradient of about one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V to one and one half horizontal to
one vertical The top of the ridge is about 500 to 600 feet above Leighton
Street
Most of the utilities and drain lines are located within the street right of way
Notable exceptions are a rubble and concrete interceptor ditch located about 75 to 250
feet above Leighton Street and a sewer line located parallel to and between Ailuna and
Leighton Streets
Presently most of the damaged area is situated between Ailuna and Leighton
Streets The head of the slide is visible as cracking and large settlements of the ground
along the edge of the pavement along 880 and 884 Leighton Street
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 9
The toe of the slide is visible as uplifted walls and sidewalks along the upslope
edge of the street from 821 to 853 Ailuna Street Between the top and toe of the slide
area the ground movements are visible as cracks and displacements in walls cracks in
concrete pavements and settlements or heaving of house foundations Maps indicating
the major signs of movement observed are included on Plates 3 through 6
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the eastern slope of Wailupe Valley at the base of a
ridge of the Koolau Range This ridge is an erosional remnant eroded from the southern
Leeward flank of the original Koolau shield volcano
The slopes above the study area consist of in situ basalt ranging from fractured
to massive with localized cliffs at resistant outcrops and boulders ranging from small
to very large Where the site is located at the base of the slopes weathering and
transportation of material from the upper slopes have resulted in the extensive deposition
of colluvium
The colluvium deposits are made up of gray clay with rocks that consist of poorly
sorted angular to subangular fragments of basalt ranging in size from less than one 1
inch to more than two 2 feet in diameter The colluvial material results from the
transport of erosional debris from the steeper slopes by gravity soil avalanche or by other
means of mass transfer

Soil Conditions
A total of ten 10 borings were drilled along Ailuna and Leighton Streets to
delineate the soil profile Based on the boring data the soil profile may be generalized
as follows
a Adobe clay Highly plastic adobe clay CH according to the Unified Soil
Classification System was encountered to depths of about 50 feet below
Leighton Street and 64 feet below Ailuna Street The clay soil was generally
stiff and moist with increasing amounts of boulders with depth
b Colluvium Stiff silty clay with boulders were encountered below the adobe
clay in some of the borings This soil generally has higher shear strength
and is more stable than the upper adobe clay soils
C Basalt Basalt rock formation was encountered at depths of 55 to 85 feet
below the street level
A generalized subsurface cross section through the site is presented on Plate 7
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The boring
logs are presented in Appendix A

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The major components in most landslides consist of
1 Poor weak soil conditions including clay soils or adverse geologic features
in the formation
2 The steepness of the slopes which is determined by the soil properties
and
3 An external source of water which saturates and weakens the soil as well
as provides additional destabilizing pressures
Our field exploration disclosed that these components are present at the site
Findings
1 The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by a thick layer of
highly plastic clay soils to depths of about 50 to 64 feet These clay soils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 13
which are classified as CH according to the Unified Soil Classification
System locally referred to as Adobe Clay exhibit high swelling shrinkage
potential and will lose considerable shear strength when saturated When
the CH clay soils experience significant movement shear slip surfaces
will then develop which will further reduce the shear strength along the slip
surfaces to a very low residual shear value
2 The existing ground surface in the area slopes down at an average overall
slope gradient of about 14 to 16 degrees 4H 1V and therefore could be
susceptible to slope movements
3 Based on the slope stability analysis the computed factor of safety against
an overall slope failure utilizing residual shear strength values and a
generalized surface and subsurface profile is about 1 3 which is
considered to be low for residential areas The factor of safety decreases
below 1 0 if a high water table is present indicating probable slope failure
4 A perched or trapped water level has been recorded by the piezometers
at a depth of about 30 feet below Leighton Street Only a small 1
fluctuation in water level has been recorded during recent rains although
the rain gauges indicate that rains of similar intensity as the January 1989
storm has occurred
5 Prior to the slide and repair of the upper interceptor ditch it was reported
by the residents that a significant amount of water flowed down from the
hillside above Leighton Street and at times water was visible bubbling
through the asphalt pavement The amount of water runoff appears to have
decreased substantially since the repair of the ditch
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 14
6 Residents along Ailuna Street particularly 809 through 841 Ailuna have
reported continuous seepage in the rear of their properties The source of
the seepage is presently unknown but believed to be natural hillside
seepage or sewer leakage
7 We were not able to install instruments within the deep portion of the slide
on private property to measure the actual depth of the slip surface
However based on the location of the top and toe of the slide and the
topography of the ground between Leighton and Ailuna Streets it appears
that the slip surface may be relatively deep Refer to Plate 7
8 The major ground movement areas continue to be situated primarily within
private property These areas could spread and extend further upslope if
major movements continue The continued movements could destabilize
upper areas underlain with adobe clay soils
9 Much of the area within the original slide limits were relatively stable during
the first six 6 months July to December 1989 of our study However
accelerated movements have been noted in the area including Lots at 880

be noted that the above lots along Ailuna area are those areas which have
previously reported continuous seepage in their backyards
Interpretation of Mechanism of Movement
Visual observations indicate that the ground movements are a result of a relatively
deep seated slide similar to those experienced in Palolo and Aina Haina Valleys in the
past
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 15
Due to the nature of the clay soils and the relatively steep natural ground slope
it is likely that even before residential development of the area the near surface soils were
slowly creeping downhill Based on the terrain and street configuration it generally
appears that the subdivision grading cut away some of the toe support of the slope and
added weight additional driving force to upper areas Site observation indicates that
subsequent individual house construction activities further cut into the toe of slope for
garages and added weight to the upper areas retaining walls with backfill Additionally
residential development generally increases the infiltration of water into the soil landscape
watering thereby contributing to weakening the underlying adobe clay soils Based on
the above observations we believe that these post construction activities increased the
potential for the subsequent ground movements
Generally slope creep movements are initially very slow and may go undetected
for many years However with time the clay soils eventually get saturated due to
continual infiltration of rainfall landscape water seepage etc and lose a portion of their
initial soil shear strength thereby increasing or accelerating downslope ground
movements Water infiltration then increases due to subsequent utility line leakages and
surface cracks in the ground and pavements caused by the previous movement Due to
the continued movement the soil strength continues to decrease to its lowest value
residual strength At this point the movements could accelerate to rates that are very
noticeable and measurable particularly if there is a build up of water pressure or
increased seepage The reported rapid movements at the site indicates a build up of
water in the soil due to the heavy rains and possibly other sources
The above description applies to the large scale movements covering tens of feet
in lateral extent On a smaller scale our observations indicate localized failures as
evidenced by cracked or toppled retaining walls and slope sloughing It is our opinion
that many of these smaller localized failures may be a result of insufficient structural
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 16
design to resist the very high lateral soil pressures developed by the clay soils and
over steepening of the ground slopes
Recommendations for Remedial Work
It should be realized that due to the presence of existing houses and utility systems
located in the affected area major remedial work would be difficult costly and would
create considerable inconvenience for the local residents
The major causes of the ground movements are
1 Poor subsurface soil conditions adobe clay deposits
2 Relatively steep hillside terrain and
3 Excessive water infiltration
Therefore remedial measures should concentrate on the improvement of these
three areas More detailed discussions to further address these issues are presented
below
Improve Poor Soil Conditions
Due to the extensive lateral and vertical distribution of the clay soils and the
existing residential improvements removal and replacement of all the clay soils is
considered to be an optimal solution but may not be a practical or acceptable
solution
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2332 00 Page 17
In principle arresting the ground movements in the affected area would require
extensive ground stabilization measures to reduce the driving force to restrain the
movement of the upper sliding hillside soils This would require extensive
regrading to flatten the slopes and or construction of massive stabilization buttress
fills at intermediate slope levels on the hillside private lot areas Due to the
existing facilities and structures it appears that this approach is not considered a
practical solution at the present time
Flatten Steep Ground Slope
The lateral ground movements at the site could be arrested if the ground slopes
could be flattened to ratios of about six horizontal to one vertical 6H 1V or less
However due to the existing housing development this does not seem practicable
and or feasible
Intercept Water Infiltration
Since water infiltration into the underlying clay soils would accelerate or increase
the potential for slope movement immediate remedial work to intercept and drain
the groundwater would minimize water infiltration into the weakened soil mass
This may help to increase the hillside stability
In order to effectively drain the hillside area it is important to identify the sources
and the depth of the water The piezometer readings indicate that the 30 foot
water level is fairly stable indicating that the influence of deep runoff water
infiltration is probably relatively small Therefore measures to intercept shallow
water infiltration in the upper 10 to 15 foot depths may be effective in an attempt
to reduce further ground movements It should be recognized that this method
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 18
may be only partially effective however it is more feasible and less costly than the
two 2 above approaches
A trench drain could be installed within the Leighton Street right of way and extend
down into the clay layer for a depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground
surface This deep trench drain would serve to intercept and drain the upper
subsurface water from runoff infiltration and utility lines A typical section of such
a trench drain is shown on Plate 8 The trench drain should be designed to drain
into a suitable outlet in the lower stable areas of the development The deep
trench drain would have to be constructed in small sections and backfilled on a
timely basis to permit through traffic and to minimize the potential for further
instability due to the open trench excavation The contractor should also be
required to provide proper shoring and bracing to protect the adjacent ground and
structures from lateral movements toward the open trench Shoring is also
required by governmental regulatory bodies to protect workers from cave ins
Repair Pavement at Leighton Street
The top or scarp of the slide has caused settlement and cracking of a portion of
the pavement from 880 to 892 Leighton Street
In order to restore the pavement level in this area a retaining structure is required
A flexible retaining structure is recommended to accommodate possible additional
movements A crib wall reinforced earth wall and a caisson wall were considered
for this area A caisson wall appeared to be most appropriate primarily since it
would create the least disturbance during construction A schematic section
illustrating the retaining wall system is shown on Plate 9 We recommend that
additional borings be made in this area to further delineate the vertical extent of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 19
slip surface and the location of an underlying more stable formation to support the
flexible retaining structure
Replace Relocate Sewer Line
The sewer line that extends along the houses between Ailuna and Leighton Streets
is situated near the middle of the slide and as such is susceptible to large earth
movement In light of this precarious location and reports of seepage by many of
the residents below the sewer line we recommend that this line be relocated if
possible or raised to the ground surface to allow monitoring and easy repair
Collect and Dispose Roof Water
A significant amount of water can fall on the houses and lots during heavy rainfall
if uncollected this water may infiltrate into the ground and add to the instability
Therefore we recommend that the residents be urged to collect roof runoff by
gutters which drain to the streets and then into the storm drain system
In some areas such as the houses on the lower side of Leighton Street the
elevations of the houses make it impossible to flow the gutter water back into the
streets These residents would need to drain their water through pipes passing
through the lots below and then into Ailuna Street
Additional Studies
The slope indicators and piezometers should be monitored on a long term basis
to evaluate the effectiveness of the initial remedial work and to aid in the planning
of additional remedial work

Additional borings will be required in selected locations to provide additional
subsurface data for use in the design of the initial remedial work
Topographic survey information is also required prior to preparation of the

LIMITATIONS
The general site is underlain by adobe clay soils which are subject to long term
creep movements The recommendations presented in this report should help to reduce
drastic movements Creep movements however may continue
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data collection points such as borings piezometers and
slope indicators Variations of conditions between the field data collection points may
occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data collection points were determined by measuring from
the attached Site Plan Plate 2 Elevations of the data collection points were not available
at this stage of report preparation The physical locations of the field data collection
points should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition The strata lines shown on profiles or cross sections are based upon
interpolation between borings and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2332 00 Page 21
Water level data from the boring points were collected at the times given on the
graphic representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed
and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the earth movement area under the current study
were explored by drilling ten 10 borings to depths of approximately 38 8 to 100 0 feet
below the existing ground surface by utilizing either truck mounted or portable drilling
equipment The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate
2
The subsoils encountered were classified by visual and textural examination in the
field by our engineer and geologist who continuously monitored the drilling operations
The classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory
All soils were classified in accordance with the Unified Soil Classification System USC
Graphic presentations of the soils encountered in the borings is presented on the Boring
Soil samples were obtained by driving a standard penetration sampler or a 3 0 inch
O D ring sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling a distance of 30 inches
Normally the blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches or shorter length when
hard driving or refusal is encountered are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Rock core samples and some soil samples were obtained by using a rotary wash
NX or wireline HQ core barrel The core recovery REC and or rock quality
designation RQD are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
sessereneeee
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample
depths
Nine 9 one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected natural samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plates
B 1 and B 2
Eleven 11 Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results are shown on Plates B 3 through
B 6
Two 2 sieve analyses were performed on selected samples to determine the
gradation characteristics of the soils The test results are summarized also on Plate B 5
Seven 7 direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
their shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 7 through B
13

APPENDIX D
Piezometer Data
Single and double level piezometers were installed in selected borings to monitor
groundwater seepage and or utility leakage at various depths below ground in the study
area Readings have been taken manually with water reading probes for a period of
about nine 9 months and automatically with electrical piezometer dataloggers for a
period of about eleven 11 months
The typical piezometer details are shown on Plate D 1 The piezometer readings
collected manually to date are presented on Plates D 2 The electrical piezometer
readings collected to date are plotted on Plate D 3 through D 64 Where readings are
not shown on the plots the electrical piezometer readings were not collected due to a
periodic maintenance and or occasional repair of the instruments The approximate
locations of the piezometers are shown on the Site Plan Elevations and depths of the
electrical instruments are also shown on the plots of readings Plates D 2 through D 64
The single level piezometer has a 3 4 or 1 inch PVC riser pipe connected to a 1
1 2 inch diameter slotted PVC piezometer tip lowered to a pre determined depth in the
boring Clean sand was used to backfill the annular space between the boring shaft and
the slotted tip Bentonite seals were placed above and below the granular backfill to
isolate the seepage interception zone Similarly the double level piezometer has two 2
of these piezometers installed at two 2 different levels in a selected boring
Where automatic monitoring of water level is desired electrical vibrating wire type
piezometer sensors were placed inside the slotted PVC piezometer tips and connected
to an automatic datalogger through special electrical cables The automatic data logging
systems used for this study are and GEOKON Model
4500H vibrating wire piezometers which can measure up to 50 psi water pressure

APPENDIX E
Slope inclinometer Data
Geolabs Slope Inclinometers
Slope inclinometers systems SI 1 through SI 3 were installed in three 3 selected
borings to monitor the subsurface lateral movements in the study area Readings have
been taken periodically from Slope Inclinometers SI 1 and Sl 2 for a period of about ten
10 months Readings were not taken on SI 3 since it was damaged during road repair
work The first and the last readings to date are presented on Plates E 1 through E 4
The slope indicator casings were left in place and readings were taken by means of a
separate deflection measurement system The approximate locations and depths of the
slope indicator are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 and the readings Plates E 1 through
E 4
After completion of a selected boring a 2 75 inch outer diameter ABS slope
inclinometer casing manufactured by Slope Indicator Co of Seattle Washington with
four 4 internal longitudinal grooves was installed in the borehole The annular space
between the casing and the borehole shaft was then grouted with a cement bentonite
mixture The casing grooves were generally orientated to provide displacement readings
in the downslope direction The top of the casing was generally cut at a suitable height
above the road pavement to facilitate subsequent readings and covered with a plastic
cap Where the casing is located in the public right of way such as SI 2 the casing was
cut below ground and protected by a steel cover
The deflection measurement system consists of a servo accelerometer probe
Model 50325 connected by special electrical cable to a digital readout unit The probe
has spring loaded guide wheels which fit into the casing grooves The probe is lowered
into the casing and readings of the casing deflection are made at 2 foot intervals After
readings are made in one direction the probe is rotated 180 degrees for a complementa
ry set of readings
The slope inclinometer readings provide a measurement of lateral displacement of
the casing relative to the bottom of the casing
Others Slope Indicators
Two 2 slope indicators OSI 1 and OSI 2 installed by others in the study area
were also made accessible for Geolabs Hawaii through the efforts of the Department of
Public Works City and County of Honolulu to make observations since late January
1990 The approximate locations of the slope indicators are also shown on the Site Plan
The observations to date are presented on Plate E 5
