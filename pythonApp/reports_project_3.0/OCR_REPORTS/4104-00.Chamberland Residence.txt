In accordance with our service agreement we have evaluated the backfill conditions
behind the existing retaining wall located at the rear of the subject residence in the Makiki
Heights area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
In general a site visit was conducted to evaluate the existing wall and surface
conditions at the subject residence In addition a limited subsurface exploration by hand
auger was performed on September 30 at the subject site This letter report presents our
findings and preliminary recommendations pertaining to the retaining wall deformation
experienced at the site and some preliminary geotechnical design parameters for evaluation
of a new wall and other wall systems at the site
Field Observations
The subject site is located at 2683 Aaliamanu Place in the Makiki Heights area of
Honolulu The existing wall located at the rear property line measures approximately
400 feet in length The exposed portion of the wall is approximately 10 feet in vertical height
The existing wall has been observed to move outward toward Tantalus Drive over a period
of years such that there are some noticeable cracks in the wall In addition the wall appears
to have moved slightly beyond the boundary of the property fronting Tantalus Drive The wall
is located above a relatively steep slope up from Tantalus Drive as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 1 Further details on the construction of the wall were not available at the time this
report was prepared

Field Exploration
A limited field exploration was conducted by hand augering two borings extending to
depths of about 8 3 and 13 8 feet below the existing ground level The borings were
terminated upon encountering refusal on hard obstructions which is believed to be boulder
or cobble material or potentially the footing for the existing retaining wall Disturbed
composite soil samples were collected at 1 to 2 foot intervals from the borings for soil
classification The soil samples were obtained by collecting the soil cuttings contained in the
hand auger bucket head The soil samples were bagged and transported to our laboratory
for visual classification
The two borings were located in the backyard area approximately 1 5 feet from the
back wall face and 35 feet toward the center of the yard from each side of the property
boundary The ground surface was measured to be about 8 5 inches below the top of the
wall elevation at each of the boring locations
Boring No 1 encountered generally medium stiff clayey silt backfill extending to a
depth of about 5 5 feet below ground level Below the clayey silt horizon clean cinder sand
with some silt was encountered extending to about 8 3 feet below ground level where auger
refusal was encountered on an unknown obstruction Some chips with the appearance of
concrete were recovered from Boring No 1 at a depth of about 6 feet below the ground level
The chips may represent concrete debris associated with the wall construction The cinder
material encountered in Boring No 1 below about 6 feet in depth appeared to be uniform in
character and density therefore it was interpreted as undisturbed native soil
Boring No 2 was extended until auger refusal was encountered at about 13 8 feet
below the ground level Boring No 2 encountered generally medium stiff clayey silt backfill
overlying a horizon of loose medium dense silty cinder sand Below the cinder a horizon
of stiff clayey silt with decomposed gravel was encountered extending to the maximum depth
explored The latter soil horizon did not correlate with the anticipated cinder soil stratigraphy
therefore it was interpreted to represent fill soil
Based on the soil cuttings retrieved from the borings it appears that the generally
medium stiff soils encountered represent backfill of varying character ranging from clayey silt
and silty clay to cinder sand with some silt _The wall footing was not conclusively
encountered in the hand augered borings Hard materials that resisted hand auger
penetration were encountered at the bottom of the borings however the depths do not
appear to correlate with the anticipated footing depth of the wall
Descriptions of the materials encountered in the two hand augered borings are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates 2 and 3

Preliminary Findings and Recommendations
Based on the results of the hand augered borings it appears that the existing
retaining wall may have been constructed without a typical concrete footing for foundation
support Considering the relatively steep slopes in front of the existing wall and the relative
age of the wall we believe that it would be prudent to abandon the existing retaining wall and
construct a new gravity retaining wall behind the existing wall The existing wall may be
retained as a fascia for the new gravity retaining wall
As an alternative the existing wall may be rehabilitated by the construction of a
deadman anchor system to resist the lateral loads and connected to the existing wall by
tie rods The existing wall would need to be rehabilitated to reduce the potential for the
existing wall to deteriorate and fall over onto Tantalus Drive with the passage of time
As part of this study of the wall we understand that three wall alternatives have been
evaluated for implementation The three wall alternatives are as follows
1 Remove the existing wall and replace it with a new retaining wall
2 Construct a new gravity wall behind the existing wall and retain the existing
wall as a fascia rehabilitate the existing wall as needed for a fascia
3 Rehabilitate the existing wall by constructing a deadman anchor system to
resist the lateral loads and rehabilitate the existing wall as needed
Based on the three wall alternatives to be evaluated the following preliminary
recommendations may be used in the preliminary design and cost estimation of the
alternatives
Retaining Structure
Based on the constrained working space at the location of the existing retaining wall
we envision that a gravity retaining wall may be used to retain the earth below the existing
residence The gravity wall may consist of either cement rubble masonry CRM wall or a
mechanically stabilized earth retaining wail In either case the existing wall may be re used
as a fascia and does not need to be demolished and removed However the existing wall
should be structurally connected to the new wail to reduce the potential for further lateral
movement of the existing wall The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary
design of the new gravity wall and or the new replacement retaining structure
Wall Foundations
In general we believe that the retaining structure foundation should be embedded a
minimum of 3 feet below the lowest adjacent grade in front of the existing wall If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials or the wall
foundation may need to be extended down to bear on firm and unyielding ground

For preliminary evaluation of the gravity retaining wall or cantilever retaining wall we
believe that an allowable bearing pressure on the order of about 2 500 pounds per
square foot psf may be used to evaluate the bearing conditions of the retaining wall
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil A coefficient
of friction of 0 35 may be used for the foundation bearing on the medium dense sandy
soils Due to the sloping ground conditions in front of the existing wall we believe that
the passive pressure resistance should be neglected for the wall

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations facing down at
the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should
also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of the on site cinder
sands or select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements
the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of non expansive impervious material
to reduce water infiltration behind the walls
Deadman Anchor System
As previously indicated we understand that a deadman anchor system may be
considered to resist the lateral loads imposed on the existing retaining wall For design of
the deadman anchor the deadman anchor should be located at a horizontal distance of at
least 14 feet behind the existing wall based on an average vertical height of 10 feet to
reduce the potential for the deadman anchor to impose additional lateral loads on the wall
For resistance to lateral loads a coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for the interface
between the top and bottom faces of the deadman anchor and the soil In addition the
passive pressure resistance of the wedge of soil between the deadman anchor and the
retaining wall may also be used for resisting lateral loads A passive pressure of up to 300
pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf may be used to evaluate lateral load
resistance acting against the vertical faces of the deadman anchor It should be noted that
the soil backfill around the deadman anchor should be compacted to a minimum of 90
percent relative compaction
