1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
engineering analyses performed for the Manoa Flood Control Study Phase project
located within the Mid Pacific Institute MPI campus at Manoa Valley on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based
on our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses performed The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of foundations for the
subsurface flood control project structures including site preparation and grading The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The area explored for the Manoa Flood Control Study Phase includes the
drainage alignment within the Mid Pacific Institute MPI campus near its entrance at the
end of Kaala Street to the existing drainage structure near the boundary of Mid Pacific
Institute lower campus and the University of Hawaii UH behind Gilmore Hall and the
Agricultural Engineering Institute Based on preliminary information provided we
understand that the proposed subsurface flood control drainage system along the
above described alignment will consist of 42 and 60 inch diameter drain pipes and
nine special drain manholes Based on the plan and profile provided the depth to invert
will vary from about 14 feet at the higher end to about 7 feet at the lower end of the
drain alignment

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The project site is on the
southern flank of the Koolau Volcano
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
northwest to southeast trending rift zones These lavas consist of interbedded
pahoehoe and a a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form A a is the
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flow typified by a rough jagged spinose clinkery
surface Pahoehoe is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth
billowy or ropy surfaces After the cessation of the main shield building phase of
Koolau Volcano there was a long period of erosion
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge on the
westerly side of Manoa Valley exists the Tantalus Round Top and Sugarloaf vents
When these vents were active lava spilled down the westerly wall of the valley and
moved across the valley floor The lava flows ponded and filled the lower portion of
Manoa Valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it cooled forming dense
massive rock The new lava flows from these vents blocked Manoa Stream and forced
the stream to the east between the East West Center and Waahila Ridge Subsequent
development has covered the site with man made fills

Foundations
As previously mentioned our borings encountered alluvial deposits from
approximately 3 to 21 5 feet the maximum depth drilled below existing ground
surface We did not encounter groundwater at the time of our exploration
However transient groundwater conditions could occur within porous zones during
and after periods of heavy precipitation
Based on the relatively competent subsurface conditions encountered within the
zone of the proposed drainage alignment we believe that the new drainage
structures may be adequately supported by the on site soils using conventional
construction methods An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be
used for design of drainage structure foundations bearing on the compacted on site
sandy silts and silty clay soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by
seismic and traffic loads
It should be recognized that it is common for localized soft soil pockets to occur
within alluvial deposits In general the bottom of foundation excavations should
be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement
of bedding material or reinforcing steel and concrete Soft and or loose materials
that are encountered at the bottom of pipe trench and manhole excavations
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials After
over excavation the bottom of the trench should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction with an appropriate compactor such as a roller
wheel or a remote controlled sheepsfoot or RAMMAX prior to receiving fill The
over excavated portion should be backfilled with select granular fill materials
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
For support of the new 42 and 60 inch drainage pipes we recommend granular
bedding consisting of a minimum of 12 inches of No 3B Fine ASTM C33
No 67 gradation The width of the bed course shall be equal to the diameter of the
pipe manhole plus 17 inches on each side for the 42 inch diameter pipe and

Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation directly around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base
of the walls discharging to an appropriate outlet or weep hole Weep holes should
be spaced not more than 6 feet apart As an alternative a prefabricated drainage
product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage
material The prefabricated drainage product should also be_ hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
As previously indicated the backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may
consist of on site soils or select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete
slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious
material such as the on site clayey soils to reduce the potential for significant
water infiltration behind the retaining walls

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable

Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential
for excessive ground movement
lt is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction The properly braced
shoring is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the
adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in potential settlement or
distress to adjacent structures or other improvements
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship
such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed the
subsurface conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical The adjacent ground should be continuously
monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with instruments
until the subsurface drainage pipes and manholes are finally backfilled

3 2 Site Grading and Backfilling
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly It should be noted that a substantial portion of the new
drainage alignment will be located within an existing paved road inside the Mid Pacific
Institute campus Grading operations for the installation of the new drain alignment
would involve saw cutting and removal of the existing AC pavement Concrete debris
vegetation tree roots deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should be

over excavated to expose firm natural material The exposed subgrade after
over excavation should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

be properly disposed off site

APPENDIX A
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 which were drilled to depths of
approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig CME 55 equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and
third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations
ASTM D 2937 were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1 Test results are also
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in situ soils The test results are
presented on Plate B 2 and B 3
Three Swells tests ASTM D 4546 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the swelling characteristics of the confined soils in relation to changes in
moisture content The test results are presented on Plate B 4
