SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the majority of the proposed roadway reconstruction 
project alignment generally encountered dense to very dense sands and silty sands 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 5.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the 
time of our field exploration. 
Based on the poor conditions of the existing pavements, we recommend 
reconstructing the entire Papa Avenue within the limits under consideration for this project 
with a new pavement section.  Because the project will involve the reconstruction of the 
existing roadway, we have performed a detailed economic comparison involving both 
flexible and rigid pavement sections in accordance with the latest guidelines provided by 
the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing 
and Research Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements will be constructed over the in-situ sandy soils in the shallow 
subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data provided for our 
pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction considerations, it 
is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway reconstruction project may consist of 
2.5 inches of AC over 4 inches of ACB on 6 inches of ASB.  It should be noted that CBR 
tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during 
construction to confirm that the above pavement design section is adequate. 
We recommend placing a layer reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, 
TX140 or TX5 or equivalent) over the recompacted existing base/subbase/subgrade below 
the new pavement structural section. The reinforcing geogrid would serve to provide 
stabilization and reinforcement to the underlying base/subbase/subgrade materials. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Papa Avenue Pavement Reconstruction 
(Kaahumanu Avenue to Puunene Avenue) project in Kahului on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Papa Avenue within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway between Puunene Avenue and Kea Street. The alignment then 
becomes a three-lane undivided roadway from Kea Street to the end of the project limits 
at Kaahumanu Avenue, except for the additional turn lanes at the intersection areas. 
The approximate limits of the Papa Avenue pavement reconstruction project are shown 
on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut 
grass, sidewalks at some locations, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils 
along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of tan to tannish brown 
coralline sands with silt. The existing Papa Avenue roadway alignment is relatively flat 
to a slight gentle slope towards the east from Puunene Avenue to Lono Street where 
the slope slightly increases between Lono Street and Kunu Place. The roadway then 
slopes down from south to north from Kunu Place to the project limit at 
Kaahumanu Avenue.

Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in December 2012 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under 
consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented below. It should 
be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to areas where the pavement 
deterioration are believed to be caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, 
and/or subgrade materials. The term “Ride Characteristic” refers to the response of a 
vehicle when passing at normal speed over the roadway. 
In general, the entire Papa Avenue from Puunene Avenue to Kaahumanu 
Avenue was observed to be in poor condition with moderately to highly distressed 
areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to be widespread and 
generally moderate to high in severity. The poor areas consist of elongated, severely 
distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of up to 2 inches, rutting 
and some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes. Potholes patched with AC 
material were observed at various locations along the alignment. The ride characteristic 
of the roadway was observed to be generally fair to irregular and bumpy in the areas of 
poor pavements. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling fifteen borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 15, at selected locations along the roadway 
reconstruction project extending to depths ranging from about 4.8 to 5.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. In addition, three bulk samples of the subgrade soils, 
designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-3, were collected for laboratory Resistance (R) Value 
tests.  The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.10. 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway reconstruction project encountered 
a pavement section consisting of approximately 5 to 6 inches of AC over dense to very 
dense sandy gravel materials.  Below the sandy gravel materials, the borings generally 
encountered dense to very dense poorly graded sands and silty sands along the 
majority of the roadway alignment under consideration for this project.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignments by drilling 
and sampling fifteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 15. The borings 
extended to depths of about 4.8 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.10. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-15. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Three laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D 2844) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 through B-8.
