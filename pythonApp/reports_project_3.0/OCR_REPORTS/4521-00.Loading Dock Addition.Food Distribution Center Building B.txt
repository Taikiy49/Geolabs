Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep vaileys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu The coastal plain
was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern two thirds of
the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium
derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains were developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below
the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations
The project site is generally underlain by deposits of calcareous sediments and
lagoonal deposits A surface layer of man made fill was placed over these deposits for the
development of the Kaka ako area within the last century In addition a former municipal
landfill is located within close proximity to the project site The former municipal landfill has
been closed and developed into Kaka ako Park

Surface and Subsurface Conditions
The project site is located in the Kaka ako area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The project site is located on the makai side of llalo Street The existing Food
Distribution Center is generally located in a commercial industrial area with a variety of
buildings and warehouses nearby The project site is generally level and the surface
consists of asphaltic concrete paved areas
As mentioned previously a field exploration was not conducted due to time
constraints However research and review of available information was made to obtain
general information regarding the subsurface conditions at the project site
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the project site is generally
underlain by near surface fills consisting of mixtures of sands and gravel extending to depths
of about 6 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface The relative density of the near
surface fill is likely to range from medium dense to dense with loose pockets in localized
areas It should be noted that near surface fills might contain refuse such as glass plastic
rubber and metal fragments due to the presence of the nearby landfill Our experience
indicates that the near surface fills are generally underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting
of silty sands and sandy silts In general lagoonal deposits may be considered to exist in a
loose and or soft condition
Based on the available information the existing ground surface at the project site is
generally about 6 to 8 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Groundwater levels are generally
encountered at elevations between about 1 and 2 feet MSL Therefore groundwater
should be anticipated at depths of about 4 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface It
should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations seasonal
rainfall time of the year and other factors
Discussions and Preliminary Recommendations
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site we believe that the loading
dock structure may be supported on a shallow foundation system bearing on crushed rock
Stabilization layer Because the near surface fill is likely underlain by soft and or loose
lagoonal deposits at the project site we recommend that a 24 inch thick stabilization layer
wrapped on all sides with a layer of non woven filter fabric be provided below the loading
dock foundations The 24 inch thick stabilization layer should consist of free draining
materials such as No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped in a layer of

Loading Dock
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project the medium dense to dense
surface fills at the project site may contain pockets of loose materials In addition the
near surface fills are likely to be underlain by soft and or loose lagoonal deposits Based on
the generally poor subsoil conditions anticipated at the project site we recommend that a
stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of clean granular fill material wrapped on all sides
with a layer of non woven filter fabric be provided below the loading dock foundations The
clean granular fill material should consist of free draining materials such as No 2 Rock
ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation In addition the stabilization layer should extend a minimum
of 2 feet laterally beyond the edges of the foundations The non woven filter fabric should
consist of Mirafi 180N or equivalent and is required to reduce the potential for migration of
the finer adjacent soils into the open graded clean granular fill materials resulting in future
ground settlement
Loading Dock Foundations
For design of the loading dock foundation bearing on the 24 inch thick stabilization
layer an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may
be used This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Where foundations are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities
the foundations should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane
projected upward from the bottom edge of the below grade structure
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein the total settlement of foundations is estimated
to be on the order of about 1 to 1 5 inches Differential settlements of the foundations
are estimated to be on the order of about 0 75 inches
Lateral loads acting on the loading dock structure may be resisted by frictional
resistance between the base of the foundation and the stabilization layer and by
passive earth pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded
portion of loading dock foundation A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for
foundations bearing on the stabilization layer Resistance to lateral loads due to
passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 150 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf for foundations embedded in the near surface
fills or soft loose lagoonal deposits Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be
neglected

much as 0 5 percent of the wall height
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a

considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side
of the structure a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 50 percent
of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the structure which
is restrained may be used in design For retaining structures that are free to deflect
cantilever a rectangular distribution equal to 33 percent of the vertical surcharge
pressure acting over the entire height of the structure may be used for design
Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects
of point loads and line loads

Wall Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system should consist of about 1 cubic foot of permeable material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped with non woven
filter fabric at each of the weephole locations The weepholes should be spaced not
more than 6 feet apart Unless covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper
12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials such as the
on site sandy soils to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the
walls
Site Preparation
We anticipate that only minor site grading work will be required for this project Site
grading and earthwork operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs
It is important that a representative from our office be present to observe the grading
operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation process and whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those assumed
in providing these preliminary recommendations
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris and other deleterious and organic
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Existing pavements that are
to be demolished should be completely removed The over excavation resulting from
demolition should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material Existing utilities
to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly
backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under
the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition areas at grade or are designated to receive
fill should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optinum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general the near surface excavated on site soils excluding the saturated materials
below the water table may be re used as a source of general fill and backfill materials The
excavated wet sands and gravels generated from the utility line excavation may also be
re used as a source of fill and backfill materials provided that they are aerated and brought
to a moisture content that will allow compaction of the materials to the specified
requirements Fill and backfill materials required for the project construction should be

Pavement Design
We understand that rigid pavements will be used for this project We have made our
preliminary pavement design assuming that the pavement subgrade soil will generally consist
of sands and gravel fills with a minimum California Bearing Ratio CBR of about 12 We
understand that the design vehicle for this project will be an semi trailer
The following rigid pavement section may be used for preliminary design purposes
Portland Cement Concrete Pavement
6 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The aggregate subbase materials should consist of crushed basalt
aggregates compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction CBR and density tests
should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm
the adequacy of the above section
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry
all surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction
