INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Ward Entertainment Center project to be located at the northwest corner of
the intersection of Auahi and Kamakee Streets in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade
site grading and pavements only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Range and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana area are dominated by
fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are intercalated
with terrigenous sediments laid during high stands of the sea The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or
lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast and
in areas that were formerly valleys and drainage ways Land development and reclamation
projects within the last 100 years have brought the project area to its present form

to high structural loads we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of driven
piles be used to support the proposed structures such as the theatres and commercial retail
areas We recommend that a combination of 12 inch square and 16 5 inch octagonal precast
prestressed concrete piles driven into the upper coral ledge starting at about Elevation

The 12 inch square and 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles may
be designed with an allowable compressive load capacity of 50 and 75 tons respectively The
allowable compressive load capacities are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces To reduce

of 4 feet from center to center The minimum pile spacing would also avoid reduction in
vertical capacity due to group action and also facilitate pile driving in groups In addition we
also recommend that the pile spacing be increased as appropriate to keep the bearing
pressures column load divided by the pile cap area on the upper coral ledge to about 12 kips
or less per square foot

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the proposed structures may be resisted by a combination
of the lateral load capacity of the driven piles and the passive pressure against the
vertical faces of the pile caps grade beams Passive earth pressure against the vertical
faces of the pile cap grade beam may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure
of 300 pounds per square foot per depth pcf above the groundwater level and 175 pcf
below the groundwater level The groundwater level at the site may be assumed to be
at about Elevation 2 feet MSL
The lateral load resistance of driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection at the top of the pile and
the induced moment in the pile The lateral load analyses were performed using the
program LPILE plus for Windows which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite
difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the University of Texas
at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a pile under
lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use of
non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then
computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths The analyses were
performed for the laterally loaded 12 inch square and 16 5 inch octagonal piles

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the
pile supported foundations to be less than 0 75 inches with differential settlements
between columns supported on piles not exceeding about 0 5 inches We believe that
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the range
of 25 000 to 30 000 foot pounds in the field Significantly higner energy hammers
hammers with energies greater than 45 000 foot pounds are not recommended for
this project The hammer should be equipped with an energy level control such that
driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile
stress development which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to
the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs should review the
pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of installing the
piles to the recommended capacity The contractor should forward pile and driving
equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to construction for evaluation Geolabs should
review the submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected
hammer

Based on our engineering analyses and the relatively light structural loads we

In general new footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
The soils at the footing subgrade level should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete In

the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are

ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results
of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Six sieve analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The tests were performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation C 136 and D 1140 Graphic presentation of the grain
size distribution is provided on Plates B 2 and B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the soils
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear tests are presented
on Plates B 4 and B 5
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 with time rates of consolidation was performed
on a sample of the soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered The results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 6
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the
near surface soils to determine the dry density and moisture content relationship Results of
the tests are presented on Plate B 7
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils Results
of the tests are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
