SIDEWALK LEVEL SURVEY
A sidewalk level survey was conducted to evaluate the magnitude of the slab
heaving phenomenon observed at the project site The level survey was performed on
the distressed sidewalk slab on using a computerized water level
equipment which measures the difference in head of the internal liquid to determine the
elevation difference between points A grid pattern was set up to collect data points
along both the longitudinal length of sidewalk and transverse width of sidewalk
directions of the distressed sidewalk Measurements were collected along the sidewalk
on the Sidewalk Level Survey Plan Plate 2 At each longitudinal point measurements
were collected across the entire width of the sidewalk in a transverse direction at
distances of 13 14 and 15 feet from the
outside edge of the concrete curb
The results of the survey are shown on the Summary of Sidewalk Level Survey
Measurements Plate 3 A three dimensional graphic presentation of the survey results
is shown on the Sidewalk Deflection Inclined Sidewalk Slab Plate 4 In general the
measurements were plotted against a base reference line that assumed the sidewalk
slab was constructed with a 2 percent slope maximum slope on plans Based on the
level survey measurements it appears that there is a distinct heaving of the middle
portion in the transverse direction of the sidewalk slabs on the order of about 0 5 to
1 5 inches above the base reference line The heaving phenomenon is generally
absent or minor in magnitude towards the curb line likely due to the doweling of the
sidewalk slab to the concrete curb thus preventing movements of the sidewalk slabs
In addition the heaving phenomenon appears to level off towards the open
undeveloped land likely due to the presence of a concrete encased electrical duct
bank resulting in lesser amount of underlying expansive clays under the sidewalk

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our geotechnical engineering exploration conducted for the Urban Core 3 Roads
project indicated that the on site clayey soils generally exhibit moderate to high
shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
Therefore the clayey subgrade soils were moisture conditioned to the wet side of the
optimum moisture content during the earthwork operations to reduce the potential of
swelling resulting from subsequent increase in the soil moisture content
Due to the extended time lapse from the completion of the site grading work to the
commencement of the sidewalk construction we speculated that the clayey subgrade soils
might have lost a substantial amount of the soil moisture through evaporation as the soils
started to dry out under the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei
area Subsequent introduction of free water such as those from the tree well irrigation
and rainfall events to the relatively dry clayey subgrade soils could cause swelling of the
expansive clays and resulted in lifting of the sidewalk slabs as observed at the site
Several options to remediate the sidewalk movement cracking were evaluated
Stiffening the sidewalks by adding reinforcement and or thickening the sidewalks were
considered However if the soils continued to heave the sidewalks would also lift
Although the concrete cracking would be reduced the finished grades may not meet
specifications for flatness or grade Removal of the clay soils under the sidewalks and
replacement with structural fill was also considered However due to the existing utilities
and road improvements this option was deemed too costly A third option considered was
in situ stabilization of the clay soils by injection of a chemical stabilizer The purpose of the
stabilizer is to reduce the swelling potential of the existing clay soils Generally the goal of
this treatment is to reduce the swelling potential of the clay soils to one percent or less
which is considered acceptable for most structures We have used this method previously
and achieved success in reducing the heaving of concrete slabs
GMS Electrochemical Soil Stabilization
The main cause of expansion in clayey soils is the tendency of individual soil
particles to attract and hold water Clay particles have a net negative electrical charge

and attract positively charged ions present in the soil water matrix In the attempt to
attract positive ions many layers of water molecules are held by the clay particle
These layers of adsorbed water force individual clay particles away from each other
resulting in soil expansion
We recommend considering an electrochemical stabilization system such as
GMS to reduce the swelling potential of the on site expansive soils underlying the
distressed sidewalk slabs GMS is a mixture of sulphonated oil and other additives
The ion exchange solution is mixed with water and introduced into the soils under
pressure through injection points drilled at a grid pattern of about 3 feet on center
GMS creates a cation rich environment in the injected zone and the individual
clay particle s negative charge is effectively neutralized The structure of the clay
particles is changed physically and chemically from an expanding crystalline lattice to a
non crystalline amorphous structure to greatly reduce the ability of the clay particle to
attract and hold water This results in a great reduction in the shrink swell potential of
the clay soils
Prior to the injection operation a grid pattern of 3 feet on center in a staggered
pattern should be laid out in the field extending a minimum of 3 feet beyond the footprint
of the sidewalk slabs Hand held injection rods are manually forced downward with a
hammer in approximately 12 inch vertical intervals up to a depth of about 7 feet below
the existing slab surface or upon refusal at the injection points
After completion of the soil stabilization work the non expansive select granular
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the pouring of the
new sidewalk slabs
ADDITIONAL RECOMMENDATIONS
Although trees along the sidewalks are aesthecally pleasing from a geotechnical
engineering point of view they are problematic due to the water that is introduced into
the soil The tree wells are generally quite deep about 4 feet below the ground surface
level This allows water to easily enter the subsurface and be absorbed by the soil If the
soils are expansive this added water can cause expansion and subsequent lifting of the
surface features When the sidewalks are installed next to something more rigid such
as the road curbing the surface movements are amplified
We understand that tree planting along the streets is required However when
possible we recommend that the sidewalks be placed several feet away from the curbs
This will allow the sidewalks to move more freely without creating a tripping hazard and
the movements will be much less noticeable
