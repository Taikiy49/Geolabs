Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of
the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused solely by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that they can only be detected by instruments However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanism the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground
shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The Island of
Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5
however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in
the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and
Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small landslides occurred on the
Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of Some houses on the

Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered medium dense to
dense coralline materials with localized zones of soft sandy silt deposits Based on the
average penetration resistance N values of the subsurface materials encountered
below the foundation subgrade level and the geology of the area we believe that the
project site may be classified as a Stiff Soil Profile Therefore we believe that the
seismic design of the building structure at the project site may be designed based on a
Soil Profile Type SD in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1997

Foundations
Based on the anticipated subsurface soil conditions at the project site we believe
that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used
for support of the proposed new gas storage tanks planned at the project site An
allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the foundation design of
the proposed new gas storage tank structures The bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces
However due to the potential presence of soft and or loose subsoils at shallow
depths as encountered in our field exploration a 2 foot thick layer of compacted fill
material should be provided below the bottom of footings for more uniform support The
over excavation for the compacted fill should extend beyond the edges of the footing a
minimum of 12 inches Over excavation for the 2 foot thick compacted fill below the
footing bottom may be terminated earlier if dense coral formation is encountered during
the footing excavation work Prior to placement of the compacted fill the bottom of the
footing over excavations should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose soils are encountered at
the over excavated subgrade levels deeper over excavation may be required to expose
the underlying firm material Alternatively the bottom of the over excavation may be
lined with a layer of filter fabric such as Mirafi 600X or equivalent prior to placement of
the compacted fill

Foundation Probing
Our previous experience indicates that cavities and or voids are commonly
encountered within the coral formations in Hawaii In addition the coral formation
typically encountered in the Barbers Point area is generally susceptible to the presence
of cavities and or voids Due to the critical nature of the gas storage tanks we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations be
implemented during construction to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from collapse of the cavities below foundations
For estimating purposes we recommend that probeholes be drilled at each
spread footing location one probehole for every 50 square feet of footing area and at
10 feet on center along wall foundations The probeholes should be at least 3 inches in
diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation
elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probeholes should be drilled at closer spacings to aid in delineating
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probeholes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout with a slump range
of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations In lieu of the sand cement grout
fluid lean concrete such as controlled low strength material flowable fill may also be
used for the grouting operation
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and

Site Preparation
As previously mentioned our field exploration disclosed that the project site may
be underlain by soft loose subsoils and cavities near the existing ground surface
Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation for the project
development
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris
deleterious material old pavements and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for
contamination of the excavated materials with the cleared and grubbed spoils Soft and
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to
expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted fill
The excavated subgrades underneath future slabs and pavements should be
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy equipment a
minimum of six passes to detect the presence of potential near surface cavities or loose
zones The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 200 to
300 feet per minute If the proof rolling operations detect a near surface cavity or loose
zone the area should be ripped open cleaned of debris and then backfilled with
compacted fill material or lean concrete
In general the excavated on site granular soils may be re used as a source of fill
material provided that the largest rock fragment is less than 3 inches in maximum
dimension and that the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious materials
Select granular fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed
coral and basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections may be required for the proposed
project In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be
used under the pipes Where soft and or loose soil deposits are encountered near the
pipe invert elevations we recommend that the utility pipe be supported on an additional
18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a
non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the
trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away
from the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the concrete to
reduce the potential for water infiltration

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the existing tank locations were explored by our
office in 2000 Our current field exploration at the proposed new tank locations were
explored by drilling and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through
103 to depths of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface A bulk sample
designated as Bulk 1 was obtained from the near surface soils to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics The approximate locations of the test borings
previous and current field explorations and bulk sample are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drill equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
