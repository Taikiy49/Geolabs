PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the surface and subsurface conditions to formulate geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the design of pavements and site preparation only To accomplish
this we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work
efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of five borings extending to depths of about 10 2 to
21 5 feet below the existing ground surface for a total of approximately
63 lineal feet of field exploration
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our
geologist
4 Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data to formulate geotechnical
engineering recommendations for the design of pavements and site
preparation
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by our senior engineer
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by our
principal engineer
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and the Logs of Borings are
presented on Plates 3 through 8 of this report The results of the laboratory tests
performed on selected soil samples are presented on Plates 9 and 10

FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of about 10 2 to 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers A bulk sample designated as
Bulk 1 was obtained from the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in our borings drilled were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our representative who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 4 through 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general
accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at appropriate sample
depths

Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our field exploration the proposed additional roadway alignment is
generally underlain by dense to very dense coralline sands and gravel at the
existing ground surface Laboratory testing performed on the surface coralline
materials indicated a CBR value of over 20 and swell of about 1 percent Based
on these assumptions and test results a design R value of 60 was adopted in
our pavement analyses for the coralline materials subgrade condition
Design Pavement Section
Based on our pavement analyses we believe that the following new pavement
structural section may be used for preliminary design and cost estimating
purposes
4 0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete
8 0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base 91 Percent Relative Specific Gravity
6 0 Inches Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
18 0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and proof rolled with a minimum
20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to detect the presence of
potential near surface cavities or loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be
operated at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute If the proof rolling operations
detect near surface cavities or loose zones the area should be ripped open cleaned of
debris and then backfilled with compacted fill material or lean concrete
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basalt
aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to greater than 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with ASTM D 2041 The aggregate subbase should consist of
crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction
CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered
during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section The recommended
section assumes that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the

pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soils below the base and subbase course layers to reduce the potential for
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a
subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from the landscaping
irrigation For long term performance we recommend considering a subdrain system
along the sides of the roadway
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades we
anticipate site grading work consisting of cuts and fills on the order of less than about
3 feet thick may be required to achieve the design finish grades for the project In
general the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and grubbed
thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site Soft and
yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or
future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm and or stiff material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated
soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed off site
After clearing and grubbing the existing ground should be over excavated
where necessary to the bottom of the aggregate subbase layer The over excavated
subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of 10 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory
drum roller a minimum of 6 to 8 passes to assist in detecting and collapsing
near surface voids and loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute The proof rolling operations should be performed in
the presence of a qualified geotechnical engineer or a Geolabs representative
Yielding areas which may be indicative of subsurface cavities and or voids
loose areas or cavities disclosed during the proof rolling operations should be
over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials The depth of
over excavation should be extended until dense underlying materials are exposed and
should be evaluated in the field by a qualified geotechnical engineer or a Geolabs
representative
The aggregate subbase materials should meet the requirements of Section 31 of
the City and County Standard Specifications September 1986 The materials should
be moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level
loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Imported fill materials below the subbase course layer should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension In addition the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883 The materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Geolabs should test imported fill materials
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Geolabs should observe site grading operations It is important that our office
representative observe the site preparation to evaluate whether undesirable materials
are encountered during the excavation and scarification process and whether the
exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration
Utility Trenches
Utilities for the additional roadway extension project may consist of water drain
and sewer lines In general good construction practices should be utilized for the
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities The contractor should
determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation subject to practical
limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the
applicable federal state and local safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears conventional excavation
methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site However some of
the utility trench excavations may encounter localized areas of hard to very hard coral
formation which will likely require the use of chipping with hoe rams
We recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement
and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils with a maximum
particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence
