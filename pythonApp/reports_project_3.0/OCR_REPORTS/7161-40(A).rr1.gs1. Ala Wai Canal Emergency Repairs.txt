SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings performed at the project site generally encountered a surface fill layer 
about 4 to 8.5 feet thick underlain by loose to very loose lagoonal deposits to about 26 to 
28 feet deep. The lagoonal deposits were underlain by coral formation and coralline 
detritus extending to the maximum depth explored of about 47.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths of about 4.9 to 
6.4 feet below the existing ground surface. The groundwater levels correspond to 
elevations ranging from about -0.6 to -0.2 feet MSL. 
Based on our site observations, the existing canal wall has settled causing distress 
to the CRM wall and sidewalk above the canal wall.  Large voids were noted in the CRM 
wall and cracks on the surface of the sidewalk. Based on the topographic survey provided, 
the top of the canal wall ranged in elevations from +1.7 to +3.1 feet MSL.  Therefore, it 
appears that the canal wall has settled about 1.4 feet.  
Based on our borings, it appears that the existing canal wall is bearing on very 
loose fill and lagoonal deposits. Therefore, we believe that the potential cause of the 
observed distresses is due to the settling of the very loose fill and lagoonal deposits below 
the wall from the loading of the canal wall.   
We understand that the intent is to preserve the existing canal wall and prevent 
further movements and settlements of the wall.  Several remedial alternatives for the 
distressed wall were evaluated and consisted of utilizing micropiles or jet-grouted columns 
extending to or into the underlying coral formation to support the canal wall.  Due to the 
need for shoring and dewatering for the micropile foundation installation and the limited 
work area, this alternative was not selected or recommended. The installation of 
jet-grouted columns was recommended since the columns could be installed without 
shoring and dewatering. 
The recommended remedial measure consists of supporting the existing canal wall 
with one row of 42-inch diameter jet-grouted columns spaced at 84 inches on centers.  
The jet-grouted columns should be installed adjacent to the front edge of the canal wall toe 
and extend from the bottom of the wall to the top of the underlying coral formation.  In 
addition, we recommend that a jet grouting test program be performed. 
The damaged CRM wall above the canal wall should be repaired using grout and 
new rocks to seal the existing voids.  The existing sidewalk should be removed, and the 
wall backfill should be removed down to firm material and replaced with compacted 
aggregate base course material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Subsequent to an acceptable aggregate base course installation, the 
concrete sidewalk should be restored.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Ala Wai Canal Wall Emergency Repairs project in the Honolulu area 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on 
the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and 
engineering analyses for the project. These findings and geotechnical recommendations 
are intended for the remedial design of the distressed canal wall only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is along the south bank of the Ala Wai Canal between McCully 
Street and Kalakaua Avenue in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The project involves the repair of about 150 linear feet of the existing Concrete 
Rubble Masonry (CRM) canal wall and sidewalk that are in distress.  It appears that the 
canal wall has settled leaving cracks and large voids beneath the grouted riprap slope 
and the existing concrete sidewalk. The limits of the distressed area are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Ala Wai Canal is approximately 2 miles in length and averages about 250 feet in 
width. The Ala Wai Canal was built between 1921 and 1928 to drain the swamps in the 
Waikiki area and provide a drainage corridor for the waterways from central and east 
Oahu.   
Due to the accumulation of sediments in the canal, the canal was dredged from 
about the Years 2002 to 2003.  In the repair area, the canal was dredged to a base 
width of about 150 feet at Elevation -10 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) with side slopes at 
five horizontal to one vertical (5H:1V) inclination.  We understand that re-dredging the

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct 
shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger shield, Koolau Volcano, forms the 
majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is buried 
partially below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the 
Honolulu Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by 
fossil Pleistocene Age coral-algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high 
stands of the sea, which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the 
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), there were many sea level 
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher 
and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively 
rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that rise, the valleys in the project area were 
drowned. In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. 
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy 
estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of loose and/or soft 
sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located on the south side of Ala Wai Canal near the Kalakaua 
Avenue Bridge in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The 
existing canal walls are made of CRM materials.  A concrete capping layer has been 
placed on top of most of the CRM walls.  
The existing canal wall has significantly settled in the remedial area.  Based on 
the topographic map provided, the top of the canal wall has settled up to about 1.4 feet.  
Elevations at the top of the canal wall range from about +1.7 to +3.1 feet MSL.  
Within the settled canal wall area, the existing CRM wall behind the canal wall 
has cracked and separated from the existing concrete sidewalk.  Large voids were 
observed at the cracks. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the canal wall repair areas by 
drilling and sampling three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 401 through 403, 
extending to depths of about 42.5 and 47.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a surface fill layer about 4 to 8.5 feet thick 
underlain by loose to very loose lagoonal deposits to about 26 to 28 feet deep.  The 
lagoonal deposits were underlain by coral formation and coralline detritus extending to 
the maximum depth explored of about 47.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The surface fill layer generally consisted of stiff to soft sandy/clayey silt and loose 
to very loose silty/gravelly sand.  The lagoonal deposits were composed of loose to very 
loose silty/sandy/clayey gravel and silty sand.  The coral formation was soft to medium 
hard.  The coralline detritus consisted of medium dense to very dense clayey/gravelly 
sand. 
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of about 4.9 to 
6.4 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The groundwater 
level encountered generally correspond to about Elevations -0.6 to -0.2 feet MSL. It

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a surface fill layer underlain by a thick deposit 
of very loose to loose lagoonal materials followed by coral formation and coralline 
detritus extending to the maximum depth explored of about 47.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of about 
4.9 to 6.4 feet at the time of our field exploration. 
The potential cause of the observed distress and the recommended remedial 
measure are presented below.  A detailed discussion of these items and our 
geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Potential Cause of Distressed Canal Wall 
Based on our borings and the existing canal wall elevations, it appears that the 
existing canal wall is bearing on very loose fill and lagoonal deposits.  Therefore, we 
believe that the potential cause of the observed distress is due to the settling of the very 
loose fill and lagoonal deposits below the wall from the loading of the canal wall. 
3.2 
Remedial Measures 
We understand that the intent is to preserve the existing wall and prevent further 
movements and settlements of the wall.  Several remedial alternatives for the distressed 
wall were evaluated.  These remedial measures consisted of utilizing micropiles or 
jet-grouted columns extending to or into the underlying coral formation for support of the 
wall. 
A micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 
inches), drilled and grouted, pile with steel reinforcing.  The micropile foundation 
typically is constructed by drilling a borehole, placing steel casing and reinforcing steel 
in the hole, and grouting the borehole.  The micropiles would need to be connected to 
the bottom of the existing wall.  Due to the need for shoring and dewatering for the 
micropile foundation installation and the limited working area, this alternative was not 
selected and recommended.

Jet-grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with 
radial horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil-cement columns.  The jet-grouting 
technique is a process that produces soil-cement columns by pumping neat cement 
grout slurry through horizontal jets injected at high pressures.  The horizontal jets of 
cement grout slurry cuts and mixes the surrounding in-situ materials with the neat 
cement slurry grout as the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil-cement 
column.  Since the jet-grouted columns could be installed from the top of the underlying 
bearing layer to the bottom of the wall without shoring and dewatering, the utilization of 
jet-grouted columns was recommended. 
3.3 
Jet-Grouting 
In order to provide support for the existing distressed canal wall, we recommend 
installing jet-grouted stabilized soil columns under the canal wall.  The jet-grouted 
columns will be supported on the underlying coral formation.  Items of the jet-grouted 
stabilized soil that are addressed in the succeeding subsections include the following:

3.3.1 Jet-Grouted Columns 
In general, the canal wall should be supported on one row of jet-grouted columns 
having a minimum diameter of 42 inches installed adjacent to the front edge of the 
wall toe.  The jet-grouted columns should be spaced at 84 inches on-center.  The 
columns should be from the bottom of the wall extending down to the top of the 
underlying coral formation.  Since most of the vertical loading is located near the 
wall toe, the jet-grouted columns are used to transfer the vertical loads to the coral 
formation.  The jet-grouted columns are shown on the Jet Grout Column Plan, 
Plate 3. 
The grout mix should have a specific gravity of at least 1.5 and should be able to 
produce jet-grouted columns with a 7-day unconfined compressive strength of at 
least 200 pounds per square inch (psi) and a 28-day unconfined compressive

3.3.2 Jet-Grouting Equipment 
The drilling equipment should be capable of advancing the jetting rods to the depth 
required for this project.  The drilling equipment should also be equipped with 
automated controls necessary to slowly rotate and withdraw the jetting rods at 
those rates determined necessary for the formation of the jet-grouted columns.  
Rates of rotation and withdrawal of the jetting rods for each column should be 
recorded by the contractor and confirmed by a Geolabs representative. 
Grout mixers, holding tanks, and associated equipment should be capable of 
continuously producing a uniform grout mixture required for the formation of the 
jet-grouted columns.  Uniformity of the grout mixture should be measured and 
recorded by the contractor by taking unit weight (density) measurements of the 
mixed grout by mud balance at least once every 2,000 gallons of grout mixed and 
pumped. 
High-pressure pumps for the jet grouting operations should be capable of delivering 
grout at a minimum pressure of 4,000 psi.  The high-pressure pumps should be 
equipped with the necessary gauges to measure and record grout pumping 
pressures, flow rate, and total grout used for each column.

the core samples should be tested for unconfined compressive strength in 
accordance with ASTM D1633 or ASTM D2850, as appropriate.  If the samples 
tested do not meet the specified strength requirements, additional replacement 
jet-grout columns may be required at no additional cost to the owner, or other 
provisions shall be implemented to compensate for the lower strength columns. 
The core holes should be backfilled with minimum 4,000 psi grout.

3.4 
CRM Wall & Sidewalk Repair 
The damaged CRM wall should be repaired using grout and new rocks to seal 
the existing voids.  The existing sidewalk should be removed and the wall backfill should 
be removed down to firm material and replaced with compacted aggregate base course 
material.  The aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Subsequent to an acceptable aggregate base course installation, the concrete sidewalk 
should be restored.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 401 through 403, extending to depths of about 
42.5 and 47.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2488 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-3.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material 
Appendix A 
Field Exploration 
W.O. 7161-40(A) 
GEOLABS, INC. 
JULY 2019     Page A-2 
Hawaii • California 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-
foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. It should be noted that RQD does not apply to coral material.  
However, the RQD values of the coral materials are included in the boring logs for rough 
characterization of the coral materials.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Nine Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Four Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) was 
performed on selected coral cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of 
the coral formation encountered. Test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of 
the soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as the 
applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  The test 
results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.
