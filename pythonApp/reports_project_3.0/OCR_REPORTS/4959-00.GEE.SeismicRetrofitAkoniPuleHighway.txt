2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
five shield volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had
activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna
Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials These flows are believed to be late Pleistocene in age and
belong to the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The lava formation at the project site appears to include both a a and pahoehoe
type lava flows A a lava flows are characterized as dense blocky lava with a rough
clinker surface while pahoehoe lava flows are characterized by a smooth rope like or
billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are
commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava flows These cavities were formed when the
lava was still in a molten state and they represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a
large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
In situ weathering of the lava formation at the project site has formed a mantle of
residual soils that grade to saprolite with increasing depth In general saprolite is
composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic
rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth
2 2 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily

during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that only sensitive instruments can detect the earthquakes However
some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to

the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of

2 3 Faulting
Based on our review of available geologic information Walaohia Bridge and
Aamakao Bridge are not located in the immediate proximity of mapped geologic fault
features It appears that there are no known faults within about 5 miles of the project
site Due to the proximity of the project site to active volcanoes and mapped fault
zones the project site is considered to be located in a seismic zone of potential
recurring activity Therefore earthquake activity associated with fault movement and
underground magma movement should be anticipated However it appears that the
potential for fault rupture at the ground surface of the project site is unlikely

3 3 Seismic Slope Stability Analysis
In 1965 Newmark proposed a method that uses both simplistic pseudo static
analysis and sophisticated finite element modeling The Newmark method of analysis
models a landslide as a rigid plastic friction block having a known critical acceleration
the acceleration required to overcome frictional resistance and initiate sliding The
analysis calculates the cumulative permanent displacement of the block as it is
subjected to the effects of an earthquake ground motion time history The calculation
consists of integrating those parts of the ground motion time history acceleration versus
time that lie above the critical acceleration resulting in a plot of velocity versus time of
the sliding block The velocity versus time profile is integrated further to provide a
cumulative displacement of the sliding block
A simplified method of the analysis recommended by Makdisi and Seed 1977
for the method suggested by Newmark also was used for the evaluation of the slopes of
Walaohia Stream The method is based on the concept that an earthquake produces
acceleration of the mass of a potential slide surface that causes shear stresses in
addition to those under static loading The additional shear stresses caused by the
acceleration of the mass are usually for brief periods of time This method assumes
that shear stresses developed that are less than the ultimate shearing resistance of the
W O INC Page 17
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
soil causes elastic deformation while the shear stresses that are greater than the
ultimate shearing resistance causes plastic deformation permanent deformation
Plastic deformation begins to occur at the horizontal acceleration that will
produce a factor of safety of unity The term for a horizontal acceleration
producing a factor of safety of unity is the yield acceleration It should be noted that
for each period of time when the ground acceleration exceeds the yield acceleration
permanent deformations of the potential sliding mass that occur are accumulated
The Newmark method of analysis is applicable to soils that exhibit no significant
loss of strength due to seismic loading strain softening The loss of strength known
as liquefaction is usually the result of the generation of pore water pressure resulting
from seismic loading This significant loss of strength usually occurs with loose
cohesionless soils such as fine grained sands Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered at the project site we believe that the generation of pore water pressure
during a seismic event should not adversely affect the shear strength of the subsurface
materials Therefore we do not anticipate a significant loss of shear strength during a
seismic event for the soils encountered at the project site As mentioned previously the
undrained shear strength for the clayey silt matrix within the boulder and cobble fill and
the clayey silt saprolite soils have been reduced by 10 percent during a seismic event
Yield Accelerations
Pseudo static slope stability analyses were performed to evaluate the seismic slope
stability for the project The pseudo static analyses calculate the yield acceleration
the average horizontal acceleration on a potential sliding mass that will produce a
factor of safety of unity The yield acceleration is expressed in units of gravity and
is assumed to act as a continuous horizontal force at the mid height of the slice
During a seismic event the seismic loading imposed on the slopes is rapid which
may not allow enough time for dissipation of excess pore water pressure For this

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the Walaohia Bridge were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 The borings generally
extended to depths of about 27 to 66 feet below the existing ground surface and were
drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 1 The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig and portable drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the basalt rock encountered at the project site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

The excavation characteristic of a rock mass is a function of the relative
hardness of the rock its relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense
rock formation with a high RQD value would be very difficult to excavate and probably
would require more time consuming or more arduous methods of excavation
