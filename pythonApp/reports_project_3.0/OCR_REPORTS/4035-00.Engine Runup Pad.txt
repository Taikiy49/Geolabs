REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4035 00 Page 4
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under
relatively high energy conditions
The project site is located off the coastal plain of southern Oahu The coastal plain
was built on the eroded flanks of Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern two thirds 2 3
of Oahu by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano
interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits In the early 1970 s the site was
brought to its present condition by construction of the reef runway at HIA with hydraulic fill
consisting of dredged calcareous materials from Keehi Lagoon

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered dense fill materials
consisting of coral sand and gravel Based on the subsurface conditions encountered
we believe that these materials will provide adequate pavement support The following
sections of this report provide discussion in the areas necessary for pavement design
and analyses
Pavement Design Methodology
The methodology used to determine the rigid and flexible pavement sections for
the engine runup pad and stub taxiway respectfully was based on methods presented in
the U S Department of Transportation Federal Aviation Administration Advisory Circular
No Airport Pavement Design and Evaluation dated
in the method outlined designs are based on the CBR method for flexible
pavements and a jointed edge stress analysis for rigid pavements Pavements designed
in accordance with these standards are intended to provide a structural life of 20 years
that is free of major maintenance if no major changes in forecast traffic are encountered
The pavement design method is based on the gross weight gear type and configuration
of the design aircraft The design aircraft is selected from the forecast traffic on the basis
of the design aircraft requiring the greatest pavement thickness Based on the
information provided the design aircraft for the proposed engine runup facility is the
B 747 400
Forecast Traffic
The forecast traffic was estimated from a report by Edward K Noda Associates
Inc dated January 1993 entitled A Study To Determine Requirements For An Aircraft
Engine Runup Facility at Honolulu International Airport Based on the report total yearly
GEOLABS HAWAII

runups are estimated to be approximately 1 330 for the year 2000 increasing to 1 448 in
the year 2010
Pavement Design Parameters
The design aircraft the B 747 400 is a wide body aircraft with a gross weight of
850 000 pounds This particular aircraft is not specified in the FAA pavement design
curves however we understand through conversations with the FAA that the design
curves for the B 200 B C F may be used Therefore the following
pavement sections were designed accordingly
Based on the subsurface conditions consisting of dense sandy and gravelly fill
materials a modulus of subgrade reaction of 300 pounds per cubic inch p c i was used
in the analysis In addition a concrete flexural strength of 650 pounds per square inch
p s i was assumed Laboratory results indicate the fill materials have a CBR value
ranging from about 57 to 68 as shown in Appendix B

Compaction Specifications
The crushed aggregate base course should be moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 6 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to at least 100 percent relative compaction Relative
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4035 00 Page 9
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to the maximum dry density
The ACB should be compacted to 97 percent of the Marshall Mix Design
Compaction should be accomplished by vibratory rollers multiple wheel
pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Compaction
testing should be performed per lot in accordance to FAA standards
Site Grading Subgrade Preparation
Site grading operations should be observed by a _ representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
grading operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation process and to confirm that the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to
those encountered in our field exploration
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should
be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 100 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks or the subgrade should be_ re scarified
moisture conditioned and recompacted Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4035 00 Page 10
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted select granular
fill
Pavement Drainage
The recommended sections assume that good drainage will be provided for the
paved areas Pavements should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement areas Surface water ponding should not be allowed
within the project site during or after construction Development of good shoulder
drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is
strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature
failure of the pavements
Utilities
We envision that new utility lines may be installed for the new engine runup facility
A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
Itis critical that free draining materials be used to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes which could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of granular materials generally
less than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill material should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction to reduce the
GEOLABS HAWAIl

upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
at least 100 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two sieve analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics
and to aid in soil classification The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation C 136 Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
