Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Freezer Room Addition Schofield Barracks Commissary project
within Schofield Barracks in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the freezer addition project within Schofield Barracks These recommendations are
intended for the design of foundations slas on grade retaining structures and
earthwork only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

installation in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The freezer room
addition is situated in the loading dock area on the eastern side of the existing

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The dips
of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees The lava flows on the plateau have
weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the
soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are the
result of this weathering
The central operations area of Schofield Barracks lies at the mouth of a broad
amphitheater headed valley where colluvial and alluvial materials intercalate with and
sometimes overlie the Koolau lavas These colluvial and alluvial materials weather to
darker brown and reddish brown soils and generally have higher clay fractions than the
more silty residual soils The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient
alluvial deposits overlying weathered volcanic rock

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 16 feet
below the existing ground surface One bulk sample designated as Bulk 1 was
obtained from the near surface materials to evaluate their swelling potential and
pavement support characteristics The approximate locations of the borings drilled and
the near surface bulk sample collected are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Boring No 1 which was located in an open lawn area generally encountered
hard to very hard clayey silt soils to the maximum depth explored of 16 feet below the
existing ground surface Boring No 2 which was drilled in the existing loading dock
area encountered about 5 5 feet of man made concrete and fill over hard to very hard
clayey silts Boring No 2 was located on the upper side of the existing loading dock
retaining wall therefore the fill was generally consistent with the grade difference
between Boring Nos 1 and 2

exploration However the Schofield Barracks area is an area of relatively high rainfall
Groundwater levels can occur and fluctuate depending on factors such as seasonal
rainfall surface runoff and other factors In addition there exists a potential for the
presence of local perched groundwater and seepage beneath the site

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
In the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu An earthquake of

Slabs On Grade
Laboratory tests indicate the on site clayey soils exhibit slight to low swelling and
shrinkage potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for
future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling
of the clayey soils we recommend capping the slab subgrade area with at least
6 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials The non expansive select
granular fill materials should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The non expansive select granular fill materials should be placed as soon
as possible after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the above discussions The non expansive select
granular fill materials and the underlying subgrade soils should be kept moist until the
final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks or the subgrades should
be prepared again Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill
We envision that concrete pads for mechanical equipment will be subjected to
equipment vibrations Therefore we recommend providing a 6 inch thick layer of
aggregate subbase course be provided below these slabs The aggregate subbase
course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The
6 inch thick layer of aggregate subbase course would serve to provide uniform support
of the slabs subjected to vibrations

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to a depth of about 16 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2

general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
