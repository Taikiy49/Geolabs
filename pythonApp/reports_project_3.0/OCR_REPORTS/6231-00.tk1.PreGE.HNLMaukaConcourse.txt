REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above its present level
Glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and
deposition The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under
relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial delta and fans seaward by deposition of alluvium over the previously
deposited lagoonal sediments
The geology of the project area has been further complicated by the deposition of
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of vents of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series which included the nearby Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa vent These
eruptions were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments
along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways Urban development
and reclamation projects have since altered the natural formation of the area

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Anticipated subsurface conditions were determined from a review of geologic
maps available subsurface information from past projects in the vicinity and limited
borings performed thus far for the field exploration program to obtain an indication of the
subsurface conditions at the new HNL Mauka Concourse development site
In general the project site is situated in a geologic transition area between two
stratigraphic units i e Coral Limestone Qls to the south and Honolulu Tuffs Qht to the
north The following sections briefly describe the general characteristics of each of the
above stratigraphic units based on our literature research and past experience with
similar materials The approximate delineation of these stratigraphic units at the ground
surface in the vicinity of the project site is presented on the Geology Map Plate 3
Coral Limestone Qls
This unit consists of fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and their related detrital
debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant portion of
any coralline rock assemblage The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly
cemented coralline debris consisting of gravel sand and silt sized clastic
fragments Some of the coralline fragments were dissolved and the solution cavities
filled with cemented and or uncemented fine sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal deposits and the
variable degree of cementation frequently present this formation generally has high
in situ strength characteristics Structurally the coral algal reef deposits are
capable of supporting heavy foundation loads with generally low settlement
characteristics when designed properly The coral limestone may consist of the
following subunits
Coralline Detritus
This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations The
soils generally consist of medium dense to dense sandy gravel and gravelly
sands Silts and clays are only present in minor or trace amounts Detritus
also may be weakly cemented with occasional moderate to strong
cementation
Coral Formation
This subunit is composed of coral and calcareous algae in their original
growth positions or detrital deposits which have become moderately to well
cemented

Honolulu Tuffs Qht
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consists of volcanic ash which
consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard rock that resembles siltstone or
sandstone locally called mudrock The tuff is frequently found overlying coralline
deposits or alluvium units
Since the formation is a medium hard rock it has very high strength characteristics
Structurally the tuff is very competent This formation has relatively low
permeability and groundwater is generally not encountered in this formation When
it does occur it is usually in the form of seepage along joints
Based on the limited borings drilled for the project it appears that the project site is
generally underlain by 3 feet of surface fills consisting of dense silty gravel placed over
stiff silty clays and dense silty gravel extending to depths of about 12 to 19 feet below the
existing pavement surface Medium hard to hard volcanic tuff formation locally known as
mudrock was then encountered and extended to depths of about 30 to 43 feet below
the existing pavement surface The thickness of the volcanic tuff formation generally
varies from about 15 to 27 feet across the project site Below the volcanic tuff formation
older alluvial deposits consisting of very stiff silty clays or coralline detritus materials
consisting of medium dense to dense silty gravel were then encountered to the maximum
depth explored of approximately 61 5 feet below the existing pavement surface
Groundwater was encountered in the borings drilled at depths ranging from about
15 to 17 feet below the existing pavement surface at the time of our field exploration Due
to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean water levels are expected to vary
with tidal fluctuations and storm surge conditions It should be noted that water levels also
may vary with seasonal rainfall time of the year and other factors

Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the

uplift load capacity should be used for transient loads only For sustained uplift

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the new concourse structure may be resisted by a
combination of the passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of
the foundation caps and or grade beams and the lateral load capacity of the drilled
shafts Passive earth pressure against the near vertical faces of the foundation
caps and or grade beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 and 150 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf for above and below
groundwater conditions respectively
In general lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft In general we recommend spacing the
drilled shafts a minimum of 2 5 times the diameter of the shaft from
center to center Therefore the effect of group action was considered in our lateral
load analyses by including an efficiency factor in the direction of loading
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for
Windows which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally
loaded drilled shaft program originally developed at the University of Texas at
Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a drilled shaft
under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use
of non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then
computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths
The lateral loads 50 75 and 100 kips acting at the top of the drilled shaft the
maximum induced moments and the depths at which the maximum moments
occur in the drilled shafts are presented in the Summary of Lateral Load Analysis
for Drilled Shafts Plates 4 1 through 4 4 The lateral load analyses were conducted
based on two boundary conditions where the tops of drilled shafts are either free or
fixed against rotation

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The following
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a significant extent on the
frictional resistance between the shaft and the volcanic tuff formation and or
surrounding soils Therefore proper construction techniques especially during the
drilling operations are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete into the drilled holes
Hard volcanic tuff and or coral formation should be anticipated within the depths of
the drilled shaft Difficult drilling conditions will likely be encountered at the site and
should be expected The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate
equipment and tools to drill through these types of natural obstructions where
encountered
The drilled shafts are designed to be embedded into the hard volcanic tuff and or
coral formations that are anticipated underlying the project site The drilled shaft
contractor will need to perform a trial shaft program to demonstrate that the
proposed drill equipment and coring tools where appropriate will be capable of
installing the drilled shafts to the recommended depths and dimensions
Encountering localized soft loose zones should be anticipated within the coralline
materials Due to the soft and or loose consistency of these materials caving in
and or sloughing of these materials will likely occur during the drilling operations To
reduce the potential for caving in temporary casing of the drilled holes may be
required during the drilled shaft installation or the use of drilling fluids mineral
and or polymer slurry may be necessary to maintain the integrity of the drilled hole
during drilled shaft installation
Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the
potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of
concrete above the bottom of the casing or adequate concrete head to counter the
hydrostatic pressures should be maintained during removal of the casing The
anticipated groundwater level at the project site may also pose construction
difficulties since proper observation of the sides and bottoms of the drilled shaft
may not be possible
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids may have a significant effect on the
supporting capacity of the drilled shaft therefore use of drilling fluids should be
specifically approved by the engineer upon evaluation of the type of drilling fluids
proposed If drilling fluids are proposed by the drilled shaft subcontractor the same

conducted on a 36 inch diameter concrete drilled shaft The results of the load test
will be used to confirm or modify the estimated tip elevations of the production
shafts
In general we recommend that the load test shaft is structurally reinforced and
instrumented with vibrating wire embedment strain gauges for load testing
purposes As a minimum two embedment strain gauges should be placed at each
level starting from the bottom at an elevation of about 5 feet above and below the
load cells and subsequently at about 8 foot intervals

end bearing and shear resistance

Non Destructive Integrity Testing
We recommend conducting non destructive integrity testing on the production
drilled shafts One of the non destructive integrity testing methods Crosshole
Sonic Logging CSL has gained widespread use and acceptance for integrity
testing of drilled shafts
Crosshole Sonic Logging techniques are based on the propagation of sound
waves through concrete In general the actual velocity of sound wave
propagation in concrete is dependent on the concrete material properties
geometry of the element and wavelength of the sound waves When ultrasonic
frequencies are generated Pressure P waves and Shear S waves travel
though the concrete If anomalies are contained in the concrete the anomalies
will reduce the P wave travel velocity in the concrete Anomalies in the drilled
shaft concrete may include soil particles gravel water voids contaminated
concrete and highly segregated constituent particles
The transit time of an ultrasonic P wave signal may be measured between an
ultrasonic transmitter and receiver in two parallel water filled access tubes placed
into the concrete during construction The P wave velocity can be obtained by
dividing the measured transit time from the distance between the transmitter and
receiver Therefore anomalies may be detected if they exist
To reduce the potential de bonding between the access tube and the
surrounding concrete we recommend that the access tubes consist of standard
steel pipe with a minimum inside diameter of 2 inches In addition the access
tube should be equipped with watertight couplings The access tubes should be

Pavement Deflection Measurements and Subgrade Modulus
A heavy falling weight deflectometer FWD was used to measure the pavement
deflections as part of the 2001 airport pavement management system APMS
study The FWD was capable of applying forces to the pavement that simulate
aircraft wheel loads The weight was dropped at each test location to generate
dynamic forces of approximately 40 kips In addition all pavement deflection data
were normalized to a 40 kip test load For the current preliminary pavement
design normalized FWD deflections 84th percentile of 27 mils were used in the
Interisland Terminal area
Deflection measurements were analyzed using the computer program NLAYER
based on the mechanistic elastic layer analysis approach to determine the elastic
modulus of the subgrade material The subgrade moduli were estimated to be
about 17 500 psi in the Interisland Terminal area The elastic moduli of the asphalt
concrete surface layer and the base course were selected from the APTI s April
2007 report The back calculated subgrade modulus values are more important for
overlay design than for new construction because the subgrade material properties
for new pavement construction can be controlled by material selection and
compaction requirements It should be noted that the estimated subgrade moduli
could change depending on the new FWD pavement deflection measurements
