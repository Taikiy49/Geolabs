INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Honouliuli Second Reservoir in the District of Ewa on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of the
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice
Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank
The project site is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense
vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of the
Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process
has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade with depth to saprolite The saprolite
grades with depth to basaltic rock formation In general saprolite is composed mainly of
silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site consist
generally of a surficial layer of compacted gravel fill overlying hard basalt formation at
shallow depths Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect to the
proposed development the principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the
proposed second water reservoir project include the following
1 foundation support for the proposed reservoir
2 probing and grouting for potential cavities and clinker zones beneath the
foundations and
3 site grading and pavement design for the access driveway
In general concrete reservoirs are highly sensitive to differential movements In
order to provide more uniform bearing conditions for the concrete reservoir foundations
we recommend that a shallow foundation system bearing on at least 6 inches of
compacted base course be used to support the reservoir The base course layer should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3486 00 Page 9
be provided below footings and the floor slab on grade since the footings and floor slab
will be structurally connected
Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic lava
flows we recommend that a program of probing and grouting the footing locations be
implemented during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe the
probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Special
attention should be given to the Foundation Probing section of this report
Based on our field exploration the proposed cut slope at the southeast side of the
reservoir will likely expose stiff residual soils at the upper 10 feet with moderately
weathered basalt formation at greater depths In general we believe that the entire cut
slope should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V However due to the potential
space limitations an inclination of 2H 1V and 1H 1V may be used for the design of the cut
slope in the stiff residual soils and basalt formation respectively For design purposes the
upper 12 to 15 feet of the cut slope may be assumed to be the stiff residual soil
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the surficial compacted gravel fill and underlying basalt rock would provide suitable
foundation support for the proposed water reservoir structure It is our opinion that spread
and or continuous footings with concrete slab on grade foundations may be used to
support the proposed reservoir structure
Since basalt rock formation was encountered at shallow depths in order to provide
more uniform support for the foundation and floor slab on grade and to reduce the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3486 00 Page 10
potential for foundation and slab distress resulting from differing subgrade conditions we
recommend that footings and concrete slabs on grade be supported on a minimum of
6 inches of base course material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
For foundations bearing on the compacted base course layer as described above
an allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 p s f may be used for design This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces For design of structural slabs a modulus
of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection p c i may be
used for the compacted base course
Prior to placement of the base course layer the footing and slab subgrades should
be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should
be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic lava
flows we recommend that a program of probing and grouting the footing locations be
conducted during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe the
probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Probing
and grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of this
report

herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of inch with differential

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance

Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present
in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss
of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and any cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3486 00 Page 12
pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part cement and
0 7 part water
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time
between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of
a representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence
of cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed

Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
It is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading to evaluate

process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in

At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site After clearing and
grubbing the reservoir area should be over excavated where necessary to a minimum
depth of 6 inches below the future footing and slab subgrade levels to provide the base
course layer The base course layer should extend a minimum of 12 inches laterally
beyond the limits of the reservoir structure Any loose or soft spots encountered at the
over excavated subgrade levels should be removed and replaced with compacted fill The
over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The 6 inch base course cushion recommended below the footing and slab areas
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate Fill materials required to raise the existing
grades up to the bottom of the base course layer should consist of imported granular
material such as select borrow crushed coral or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension
The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher
and a swell potential of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the
No 200 sieve Imported material if necessary should be observed and or tested by
Geolabs Hawaii for its suitability prior to being transported to the site Base course and
imported fill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines will be required for development of this project
It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in hard basalt rock
For utility lines located outside the reservoir limits a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the
pipes Self draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill
thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finish grade should consist of the on site fill soils excavated
basalt rock materials less than 6 inches in maximum size or select granular material The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about
8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Where trenches will be located in paved areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below
the road subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Where the utility line crosses beneath the footings the utility pipes should be concrete
jacketed
Since basaltic rock formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field
exploration the trench work for utility installation will likely involve excavation in the basaltic
rock formation and will require hard ripping or hoerams Care must be exercised by the
contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation
resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths ranging from
about 5 to 22 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by

Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance

Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow

on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock
material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
