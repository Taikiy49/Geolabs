2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene
Epoch The dips of the lava beds are generally near horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees
from horizontal The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering
extending to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil
At the project site in situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of
residual soils that grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly
of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks It should be
noted that spheroidally weathered boulders are typical in the Schofield Plateau The
saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth

In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity
therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic
eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground movement of

In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on

3 2 Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the project site is generally underlain by low to
moderately expansive clayey soils near the existing ground surface Unless
slabs on grade constructed above these expansive soils are properly designed there is
a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content To
reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils
we recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to fill placement In addition
a minimum of 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill material should be provided
below the slab cushion The non expansive select granular fill should also extend
beyond the perimeter of the slab a minimum of 24 inches or extend to the edge of the
concrete sidewalk walkway surrounding the slab if applicable
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery
vibration we recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting
of No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs The open graded
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform slab support and would serve as a capillary
moisture break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab
and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the No 3 Fine gravel cushion fill layer Flexible floor coverings
such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor
slab cracking It is also recommended to design interior walls to incorporate some
flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible ground movements
Where the slabs on grade will be subjected to vehicular or forklift traffic we
recommend providing a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the
4 inch thick gravel cushion fill layer The moisture barrier may also be omitted for slabs
subject to vehicular traffic The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic
aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction It should be

3 6 On Site Sewage Disposal System
We understand that on site sewage treatment and disposal by septic tank and
leach field method are planned at the proposed container storage area within the new
HARC Research Facility site Therefore we have conducted a field percolation test to
obtain data for the preliminary design of the sewage disposal system at the proposed
site The approximate location of the field percolation test designated as P 1 is shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
Field percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The percolation test was performed by drilling a
4 0 inch diameter borehole to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The percolation test was performed within the near surface sandy clay soils generally
encountered in the project area
Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole
to protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments During testing the hole
was carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack
The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which
calculation of the percolation test was based
Based on the test results a percolation rate of about 40 minutes per inch was
measured within the near surface sandy clay soils The results of the percolation test
are presented on Plate C 1 of this report Based on the relatively slow percolation rate
measured in the boring it is our opinion that the feasibility of using septic tank and leach
field method for sewage disposal should be carefully evaluated for the project site In
addition due to the variability of the subsoil conditions the absorption capacity of the
disposal system should be confirmed by additional percolation tests during construction

3 7 Utility Trenches
The following guidelines and recommendations may be used for the installation
of on site utility lines In general good construction practices should be utilized for the
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities Based on the results of
our field exploration it appears that conventional excavation methods may generally be
utilized for utility line excavation The contractor should determine the method and
equipment for trench excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations In
addition the excavations should comply with the applicable federal state and local
safety requirements The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design
and installation
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground
subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction

Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths of about 8 to 20 4 feet below the

augers
