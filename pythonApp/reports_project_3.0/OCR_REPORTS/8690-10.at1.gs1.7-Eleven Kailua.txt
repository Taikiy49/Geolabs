SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a surface layer of asphaltic concrete about 
6 inches thick underlain by fill consisting of medium stiff to hard silty clay to 2.5 and 4.5 
feet below the existing pavement surface.  Below the fill, residual soils consisting of 
medium stiff to very stiff silty clay/clayey silt were encountered, extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the project site at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
At the concrete slab openings within the store, reddish brown clay with traces of 
gravel and gray sandy clay with some gravel were encountered from about 0.4 to 0.8 feet 
below the top of the concrete slab. 
Based on our site observations and field exploration at the project site, we believe 
that the potential causes for the observed floor slab distress may be due to highly expansive 
clay soils below the concrete slabs-on-grade and poor drainage conditions that caused the 
infiltration of moisture below the slabs. 
The floor slab subgrade materials recovered from the field exploration indicate that 
the subgrade clay soils are highly expansive with a liquid limit of greater than 100 percent 
and ring swells of greater than 20 percent.   
Wet and soft ground conditions were observed behind the store building area. This 
area between the building and the existing CRM wall is currently open and is subjected to 
the elements.  In addition, we observed PVC drain pipes extending out from the back wall 
of the building and discharging into the narrow open area.  We suspect that moisture may 
have gotten below the store slab, which may have caused the expansive clay soils to heave 
and settle.  In addition, the moisture infiltration may have caused the heave observed in the 
freezer area. 
Based on the above, we recommend covering the narrow open area between the 
building and the existing CRM wall with a concrete pavement to reduce the water 
infiltration beneath the building.  The concrete pavement should be properly sloped to 
carry water away from the area. In addition, open cracks on the slabs inside and outside 
the store should be sealed to minimize water infiltration into the subgrade soils.  An 
additional floor level survey of the 7-Eleven store should be performed to evaluate if the 
movements are slowing or continuing.  Should the readings indicate that the subgrade 
clay soils have come to an equilibrium and the movements are minimal, permanent 
repairs may be made at that time.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the 7-Eleven Kailua project. The project location and general site 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These findings 
and geotechnical recommendations are intended to evaluate the probable cause of the 
observed concrete slabs-on-grade distresses. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at the corner of Kailua Road and Uluoa Street in Kailua 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 7-Eleven store is located at the north end of the 
existing one-story building. The 7-Eleven store is part of a single-story structure supported 
on a shallow foundation with concrete slabs-on-grade on the southern and western ends 
of the building.  
Geolabs previously conducted a visual observation of the distress as well as floor 
level survey of the store and the loading zone area. Numerous cracks were observed 
along the tile floor inside the store and the concrete slabs-on-grade at the trash bin area.  
A floor level survey was previously performed inside the store in open areas, along the 
sidewalk in front of the store, at the loading zone, and the trash bin area at the north end 
of the store.  Observations of distress at the project site and the results of the floor level 
survey are presented in our report entitled “Floor Level Survey, 7-Eleven Kailua, Kailua, 
Oahu, Hawaii,” dated July 31, 2023. Based on the observed distress and results of the 
floor level survey, additional field exploration was recommended to try and determine the 
possible cause of the observed distresses and to develop recommended remedial 
measures.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis for distress assessment of the building are 
the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the 
materials encountered, and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project 
site. Therefore, the following sections provide a description of the geologic setting of the 
project site and the surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, 
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These 
more recent series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic series and include some 
prominent landforms as the Diamond Head and Koko Head Craters. 
During the late stages of the Koolau Volcano eruption, the central Koolau Caldera 
was filled with thick ponded lavas and intruded by a myriad of narrow lava feeding volcanic 
dike structures, which have been collectively identified as the Kailua Volcanic Series. The 
Koolau Caldera structure is believed to have encompassed the entire Kailua area, 
extending roughly from the existing Koolau Pali northeastward toward the existing 
coastline and beyond. Following the deposition of the caldera filling lavas, the caldera 
structure subsided, and the basaltic rocks of the Kailua Volcanic Series were deeply 
altered by percolating underground fluids and gasses. In addition, streams were incised, 
and dissection and erosion of the exposed rock materials commenced. Thus, only 
scattered erosional remnants of the original caldera structure and lava filling deposits 
remain exposed as relatively low hills surrounding the Kailua area, which include Mr. 
Olomana, located in the near vicinity of the project site.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
With the exception of narrow areas behind the store, the project site is generally 
covered with concrete slabs and asphaltic concrete (AC) pavements. Based on our visual 
observation, the project site appears to generally slopes down towards Kailua Road to 
the north.  
In general, the distresses observed inside the store consisted of cracks along the floor 
tiles at the back aisle of the store and cracks on top of the concrete slab floor in the 
refrigeration room. We also observed distress outside on the front sidewalk of the store.  
Efflorescence was observed along the cracks near the front of the building.   
At the loading and trash area at the north end of the building, numerous cracks were 
noted on top of the trash area slab leaning of the concrete curb with separation from the AC 
pavement were observed at the loading area. It appears that the loading area may have been 
repaved. 
At the back of the store building, an existing Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) wall 
with a narrow open area between the CRM wall and the store building was observed.  Water 
downspouts were noted behind and on the northeastern side of the building, which 
discharged into the narrow open area behind the CRM wall. The narrow open area behind 
the building appears to have poor drainage and wet and soft ground conditions were noted 
in this area. Cracks in the building's Concrete Masonry Unit wall were observed at the back 
of the store. Distress to the existing CRM wall was not readily observed.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a surface layer of AC about 6 inches thick 
underlain by medium stiff to hard fill followed by medium stiff to very stiff residual soils 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration.  
At the concrete slab openings within the store, reddish brown clay with traces of 
gravel and gray sandy clay with some gravel were encountered from about 0.4 to 0.8 feet 
below the top of the concrete slab. 
The results of our previous floor level survey are summarized below.  The potential 
causes of the observed floor slab distresses are discussed and recommended remedial 
measures are provided in the following sections.  
3.1 
Summary of Previous Floor Level Survey and Findings 
The floor level survey readings ranged from -31.3 to +6.5 inches relative to the 
zero-datum location. The readings ranged from -0.8 to +6.5 inches inside the store with the 
lowest reading of -0.8 inches at the north corner of the store and the highest reading of +6.5 
inches within the freezer area at the southeast end of the store. Along the outside walkway, 
the survey readings ranged from +2.6 to 0 inches. The readings along the loading zone and 
the trash bin area ranged from -6.8 to -31.3 inches. The significant difference in elevation 
from the datum and the loading zone was partly due to the grade drop from the landscape 
planter to the loading zone. 
Based on the as-built drawings provided, the zero-datum location is about 
Elevation 114.25 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  In addition, the finished floor elevation of the 
store is 114.5 feet MSL. Based on the floor level readings, it appears that the freezer area 
has risen about 2 inches.  In addition, the north end of the store has settled about 3 inches.

of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Six DCP tests extending to depths 
of 3.1 to 13 feet were performed behind and within the building. In addition, two bulk 
samples were collected from the near-surface soils. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. The borings were 
drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist and field logger classified the materials encountered in the borings 
by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture content (ASTM D2216) and unit weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and a 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C-1. 
Five one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under 
surcharge loads. The results of these tests are summarized on Plate C-2. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils, two 
Unconfined Compression tests were performed on selected in-situ samples in 
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D2166. The test results are shown on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and presented on Plates C-3 and C-4. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate C-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates C-6 and C-7.
