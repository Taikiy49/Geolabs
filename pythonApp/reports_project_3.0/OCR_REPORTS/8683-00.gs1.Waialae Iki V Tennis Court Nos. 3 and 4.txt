SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered about 6 inches of AC pavement underlain by fill 
materials and placed over residual soil deposit. The fill materials encountered generally 
consisted of medium dense to loose silty sand and extended down to about 6 to 14 inches 
below the ground surface. The residual soil deposit generally consisted of stiff to hard silty 
clay extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 6.5 feet below the ground 
surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, 
time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on our site observations and field exploration, we believe that there are 
several potential causes of the observed distresses on the existing tennis court surface. 
One of the potential causes is age (about 20 years old), and the other potential cause is 
due to water entering the subgrade through the cracks on the court surface and/or the 
exposed base material at the tennis court edges. In addition, subsurface water can seep 
down the hillside and into the court clayey subgrade. 
Based on the above, we recommend that Tennis Court Nos. 3 and 4 be 
demolished and reconstructed. Drainage improvements to carry surface water away from 
the courts should be implemented. In addition, subsurface drainage should be provided to 
intercept and dispose of potential subsurface water that may enter the court subgrade. 
Based on pavement support characteristics of the existing clayey subgrade soils, a 
pavement section consisting of 1.5 inches of asphaltic concrete, Tensar Glasgrid 8511TF 
(or equal) and 1.5 inches of asphaltic concrete over 10 inches of aggregate base course 
material is recommended for the new tennis courts.  
In order to better control the surface and subsurface water flow in the tennis court 
area, we recommend installing a drainage system consisting of a concrete swale between 
the tennis court and the mauka slope and subdrains near the tennis court edges. The 
concrete swale should have a minimum slope of 2 percent, and the subdrains should 
consist of a 6-inch diameter perforated PVC pipe installed with a minimum slope of 
0.5 percent and connected to a nearby drainage structure or daylighted.   
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Waialae Iki V Tennis Court Nos. 3 and 4 in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field 
exploration and presents our geotechnical recommendations for the design of the 
project. 
This report summarizes our observations and findings and presents our 
discussions and recommendations with respect to the observed distresses in the 
existing tennis court areas based on our site reconnaissance, field exploration, 
laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is situated within the Waialae Iki V tennis facility at 1959 Laukahi 
Street in Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii. Pavement distresses were observed on the surface of 
the existing Tennis Court Nos. 3 and 4, located in the eastern part of the facility. 
Observed distresses include slight depressions and numerous cracks with crack widths 
of up to 3/8 inches on the surface of the courts. Based on the information provided, we 
understand that the courts are more than 20 years old, and surface water has been 
observed running on the court from the mauka slope area. Based on our observations, 
the existing tennis court surface is covered with asphaltic concrete (AC) and topped with 
an acrylic surface.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age (Ice Age) 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau volcano 
reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The Koolau Range and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly 
related to the glacio-eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice 
Age).  The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. The Koolau 
volcanic shield was built by thinly bedded lava flows during the late Pliocene and early 
Pleistocene Epochs.  The main shield-building activities ceased approximately 2½ 
million years ago.  In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are 
dominated by detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and the movement of 
the material downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits.  
Once the colluvium reaches the streams in the valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. Large areas of 
alluvium can be found in the lowland areas and areas bordering streams. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The project area is on the southern flank of Koolau and is composed of basaltic 
rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These rocks are 
generally characterized by flows of jointed, dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin 
clinker layers. For the last 1 to 2 million years, in-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau 
lava flows has occurred, forming a mantle of residual soils, which grade to saprolite with

Existing Site Conditions

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered about 6 inches of AC pavement underlain by 
fill materials and placed over residual soil deposit. The fill materials encountered 
generally consisted of medium dense to loose silty sand and extended down to about 6 
to 14 inches below the ground surface. The residual soil deposit generally consisted of 
stiff to hard silty clay extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 6.5 feet

the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are

The possible causes of the observed pavement distress and the recommended 
remedial measures are presented. Detailed discussions and recommendations for these 
items and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following

3.1 
Possible Causes of Observed Pavement Distress 
Based on our site observations and field exploration, we believe that there are 
several potential causes of the observed distresses on the existing tennis court surface.  
One of the potential causes is age and exposure to the elements for an extended 
amount of time.  For Court Nos. 3 and 4, we understand that the courts were constructed 
about 20 years ago. After the end of court construction, the asphalt is pliable and soft.  
As time passes, the asphalt cures and becomes harder.  The oil in the asphalt begins to 
dry out, making the asphalt less pliable, and shrinkage and cracking of the asphalt begin 
with the difference in temperature that the court experiences.   
The second potential cause of the observed distresses is due to water entering 
the subgrade of the tennis courts.  The water can enter the tennis court clayey subgrade 
by surface water traveling through the cracks on the court surface and/or the exposed 
base material at the tennis court edges.  In addition, subsurface water can seep down 
the hillside and into the court clayey subgrade.

3.3 
Tennis Court Pavement Design 
We understand the new tennis courts will be covered with asphaltic concrete (AC) 
with an acrylic surface. Based on the laboratory CBR value of 10.2 and swell of 1.72 
percent for the on-site clayey soils in the project vicinity, the following pavement section 
may be used for the preliminary design of the tennis courts until the finished subgrade 
material is verified and laboratory testing of this material is conducted:  
Tennis Court Pavement 
  1.5-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  Tensar Glasgrid 851TF (or equal) 
  1.5-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
10.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
13.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The subgrade soils under the tennis court pavement area should be scarified to a 
minimum depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative 
compaction. The subgrade soils should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until 
covered by the pavement structural section. We recommend performing CBR and 
density tests on the actual subgrade soils encountered during pavement construction to 
confirm the adequacy of the above section.

Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to re-compacting. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with

Aggregate base course should meet the material requirements for Aggregate 
Base Course as specified in Section 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. The aggregate base course 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level 
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
The recommended pavement section assumes that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas, which should be sloped, and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavements to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction.

entering the court area. The swale should have a minimum slope of 2 percent and

3.5.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a 
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated 
materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to 
expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be reused as fill materials 
and should be properly disposed of off-site or in landscaped areas, if appropriate.

After clearing and grubbing, areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry

recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and 
poor drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and replacing these areas 
with engineered fill. A Geolabs representative in the field should evaluate the need 
for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
3.5.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material, 
provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported fill materials should consist 
of crushed basalt or coral. The select granular fill should be well graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should 
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
less than 1 percent.  
3.5.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
In general, fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers,

3.5.4 Excavations

project will generally consist of excavations for subgrade preparation and drainage

excavation equipment. However, deep excavations may require the use of hoerams 
or trenching machines. We recommend encouraging contractors bidding on this 
project to examine the site conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation. 
3.6 
Underground Utility Lines 
We envision new underground utility lines consisting of electrical duct lines may 
be required. We anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in 
the compacted fill and the residual soil encountered in the drilled borings. 
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or S4C Sand should be provided below the duct lines for 
uniform bearing support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or S4C Sand, should be used for the initial trench backfill 
up to about 12 inches above the duct lines.  
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular 
materials less than 3 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose 
lifts, and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the drilled borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the drilled borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided 
herein. 
The boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been located 
using a handheld GPS device. Elevations noted on the logs of borings were estimated 
from Google Earth Pro imagery dated June 2019. The physical locations and elevations

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 
5 to 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk 
sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
portable drill rig with continuous flight augers.  
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B 2. 
Three One-Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed 
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-3. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils and potential borrow material to evaluate the dry 
density and moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test result is presented on Plate B-5.
