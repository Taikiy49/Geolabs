This technical memorandum provides our drilled shaft foundation recommendations,
pending our ongoing laboratory testing program. A detailed summary of our
recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report. The report
should be consulted when it becomes available.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling six borings (Boring Nos. 1
through 6) extending to depths of approximately 50.5 to 163 feet below the existing ground
surface.
Due to the locations of the proposed HECO steel poles, the subsurface conditions
vary greatly across the project alignment. The following subsurface descriptions are
separated by the proposed pole locations.
Boring No. 1 near the proposed P.321 — Kamehameha Riser generally encountered
a surface fill layer consisting of medium stiff clayey silt extending to about 6 feet. Underlying
the fill, medium stiff/medium dense alluvium consisting of clayey silt and silty sand was
encountered extending to about 19 feet below the existing ground surface. Below the
alluvium, a thick layer of very soft lagoonal deposit consisting of clays and silts with organic
material was encountered to a depth of approximately 126 feet. The lagoonal deposit was
underlain by a layer of cobbles and boulders and relatively dense coralline detritus extending
to the maximum depth explored of about 163 feet below the existing ground surface.
Boring No. 2 near the proposed P.241 — Mokauea Riser generally encountered a
pavement structure consisting of about 6 inches of asphaltic concrete (AC) overlying about
18 inches of sandy silt fill material. The pavement section was underlain by alluvium and
weathered sandstone to a depth of about 9 feet. The alluvium consisted of clayey silt, and
the weathered sandstone broke down to a dense silty sand. Below the alluvium, moderately
weathered, medium hard coral formation was encountered to 26 feet depth. Underlying the
coral formation, medium stiff to very stiff alluvium consisting of silty clay and sandy silt was
encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 52 feet below the existing ground
surface.

Boring No. 3 near the proposed P.13 – Kalihi Riser generally encountered a 
pavement structure consisting of about 5 inches of AC overlying 31 inches of sandy gravel

followed by alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of about 53 feet below the

Boring No. 4 near the proposed P.1I – Dillingham Pole generally encountered a 
pavement structure consisting of about 4 inches of AC overlying about 38 inches of sandy 
gravel fill material. The pavement section was underlain by very loose alluvium (clayey 
gravel) and very loose coralline detritus (sandy gravel and silty sand) to a depth of about 
27 feet. Below the coralline detritus, alluvium consisting of soft to very stiff sandy clay was 
encountered to about 38 feet depth.  The alluvium was underlain by medium hard to hard 
basalt formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 50.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
Boring No. 5 near the proposed P.1S – Dillingham Riser, P.1A – Iwilei Pole, 
P.1H - Iwilei Riser, and P.1SA – Iwilei Riser generally encountered 3 inches of AC pavement 
overlying about 6 feet of fill material consisting of soft sandy silt. Underlying the fill, very 
loose marsh deposits (clayey sand) and very soft lagoonal deposits (sandy clay) were 
encountered to a depth of about 29 feet. Below the lagoonal deposits, stiff to very stiff 
alluvium consisting of silty clay was encountered to a depth of about 55 feet, followed by 
hard basalt formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 76.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface.  
Boring No. 6 near the proposed CCTV camera location generally encountered a 
3.5-foot-thick surface fill layer consisting of medium stiff sandy silt and gravel overlying 
medium hard coral formation, extending to a depth of about 27 feet. Beneath the coral 
formation, alluvium consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey/sandy silt was encountered to 
about 74 feet. Below the alluvium, a relatively thin layer of stiff saprolite was encountered 
overlying the basalt formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 115.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The basalt formation was moderately weathered and 
hard.  
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of about 7.5 to 16.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be 
noted that water levels may vary with tidal fluctuations, seasonal rainfall, time of year, and 
other environmental factors.

Downdrag Loads 
Some of the drilled shafts will be subjected to downdrag loading due to the on-going 
settlement of the nearby area from prior fill placement and the significant thickness of 
underlying soft soil deposits. Where the soft soils are estimated to have undrained shear 
strengths less than 600 pounds per square foot (psf), we recommend using a permanent 
steel casing within the depths of the soft soils for the drilled shafts as required in the 
Compendium of Design Criteria. Due to the use of a permanent steel casing, we estimated 
a nominal adhesion value of 200 psf to estimate the magnitude of the downdrag loads. 
Downdrag loads were factored by 1.25 as required by AASHTO LRFD in our determination 
of the drilled shaft lengths.  
Lateral Load Resistance 
In general, the lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness 
of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable deflection at the top of the shaft, 
and induced moment in the shaft. The lateral load analyses were performed using the 
program LPILE v2019 for Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite 
difference, laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the University of Texas at 
Austin.

Foundation Settlements 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the underlying soil and rock 
deposits. Total settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be on the order of about 
0.5 inches. We believe a significant portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as 
the loads are applied. 
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
In general, the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the 
contractor's method of installation and construction procedures. The following conditions 
would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations. 
The load-bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on the 
friction between the shaft and the surrounding soils and/or formation. Therefore, proper 
construction techniques, especially during drilling operations, are important. The contractor 
should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and placing concrete into the drilled holes. 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions described above, some of the 
geotechnical considerations associated with drilled shaft foundations are discussed below. 
Soft Silts, Clays and Loose Sands 
Drilled shaft foundations are highly effective in soil and/or rock formations that will 
stay open after drilling until concrete placement. Unfortunately, materials such as the 
very soft to soft lagoonal deposits encountered during our field exploration will likely 
collapse following the drilling if it remains unsupported. Therefore, partial-depth 
temporary steel casing and/or the use of drilling fluids (polymer slurry) will likely be 
necessary to maintain the integrity of the drilled hole during the drilled shaft 
installation. This condition would increase the construction difficulty and costs of the 
foundations. However, this condition is common in Hawaii and has been addressed 
by local drilled shaft subcontractors for structures such as high-rise buildings and 
bridges. 
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids may have a significant effect on the 
supporting capacity of the drilled shaft; therefore, the use of drilling fluids would 
require prior evaluation and acceptance by Geolabs.  
Care should be exercised during the removal of the temporary casing to reduce the 
potential for "necking" of the drilled shaft. Therefore, a minimum 5-foot head of 
concrete above the bottom of the casing or adequate concrete head to counter the 
hydrostatic pressures due to shallow groundwater conditions should be maintained 
during the removal of the casing. The shallow groundwater conditions at the project

Obstructions, Boulders, and Basalt Formation 
Where obstructions, boulders, and/or hard basalt formation are anticipated, some 
difficult drilling conditions likely will be encountered and should be expected. The 
drilled shaft subcontractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to 
drill through these types of natural or man-made obstructions where encountered. 
The drilled shaft subcontractor will need to demonstrate that the proposed drilling 
equipment (and coring tools, where appropriate) will be capable of installing the 
drilled shafts to the recommended depths and dimensions. 
Shallow Groundwater Conditions 
Groundwater conditions are anticipated within the depths of the drilled shaft 
excavations and, therefore, concrete placement by tremie methods will be required 
during drilled shaft construction. The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner 
by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole. A 
low-shrink concrete mix with a high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should be used 
to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. The 
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for 
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix. 
In addition, the concrete should be placed promptly after drilling (within 24 hours after 
substantial completion of the holes) to reduce the potential for softening of the sides 
of the drilled holes. Furthermore, drilling adjacent to a recently constructed shaft 
(within three shaft diameters of the recently constructed drilled shaft) should not 
commence until the concrete for the recently constructed drilled shaft has cured for 
a minimum of 24 hours. 
It is imperative for a Geolabs representative to be present during construction to 
observe the drilling and installation of drilled shafts. Although the drilled shaft designs 
are primarily based on skin friction, the bottom of the drilled hole should be relatively 
free of loose materials prior to the placement of concrete. Therefore, Geolabs’ 
observation of the drilled shaft installation operations is necessary to confirm the 
assumed subsurface conditions.
