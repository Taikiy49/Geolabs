Preliminary Evaluation and Recommendations
Rehabilitation of 24 Inch Sewer Force Main
From Fort Shafter te Sand Island WWTP
Honolulu Oahu Hawaii
In accordance with our revised fee proposal dated we recently
completed a portion of field exploration work pertaining to the rehabilitation of the existing
24 inch sewer force main from Fort Shafter to Sand Island Wastewater Treatment Plant
WWTP This letter summarizes our work performed thus far on the project and presents
our preliminary geotechnical engineering evaluation and recommendations derived from
our analyses for the subject project
Project Considerations
Based on the information provided the existing 24 inch cast iron force main starts
at the Fort Shafter Sewage Pump Station Bldg 1605 and connects to the City and
County of Honolulu s Sand Island Wastewater Treatment Plant WWTP on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The force main is approximately 13 850 feet long between the sewage
pump station at Fort Shafter and the Hart Street junction box at Sand Island WWTP
The 24 inch force main was originally constructed in 1945 by the U S Army and
was subsequently modified by relocation and or replacement of the original line due to
line breakage Due to the deteriorated condition as the force main approached the end of
its useful life expectancy studies were conducted in 1975 1984 and 1996 to evaluate
the existing condition of the force main and to recommend various remedial alternatives
for implementation

Because of the various construction constraints associated with open trench
construction methods such as subsurface utility conflicts relocation surface disturbance
to affected property owners and traveled roadways shoring support dewatering
requirements and handling of potentially contaminated soils etc we understand that two
alternatives are being considered for the rehabilitation of the existing force main including
Cured In Place Pipe CIPP and Insertion Sliplining method
Both methods will require the construction of insertion points or pressure manholes
at selected locations along the force main alignment Subsurface information will be
needed at the insertion points to provide data pertaining to the design of the foundation
excavation and shoring system for the pressure manhole structures In addition we
understand that both methods will require full bypass of the existing system during the
rehabilitation process of the force main therefore a temporary bypass crossing over
Moanalua Canal will need to be constructed We understand that the exact location for
the temporary bypass crossing over Moanalua Canal has not been determined at the time
this letter was prepared Therefore geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertaining to the foundation design of the cross over at Moanalua Canal will be presented
in our report when the final location is selected
Insertion Points Pressure Manholes
Our field exploration disclosed that the existing force main is generally underlain by
a surficial fill crust over soft recent alluvial and loose lagoonal deposits over the majority
of the alignment Shallow groundwater conditions between depths of about 4 5 and
10 3 feet below the surface were encountered in the borings drilled along the existing
force main alignment
In general sheet piling may be considered for temporary shoring at the insertion
points especially where dewatering of the pits will be necessary Based on the
anticipated depths of the pressure manholes at the insertion points and the relatively
shallow groundwater table dewatering with cut off walls and or tremie concrete plugs
may be considered A monitoring program should be developed and implemented by the
Contractor to detect ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the manhole
excavations which may result in damage to the nearby structures and pavements
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the shaft pits based on the subsurface conditions encountered However it should
be noted that existing abandoned underground utilities and other below grade
concrete structures may exist at the site that would require heavy equipment for
excavation or removal The method and equipment to be used for excavation

Excavation Support
Based on the information provided to our office the insertion points pressure
manholes may extend to depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the existing ground
surface Based on the generally poor subsoil conditions encountered in our
borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the excavations will be necessary
to protect personnel working in the excavations Because a majority of the existing
force main alignment will be running along the existing roadway sloping of the
sides for an open cut excavation will not be possible
Temporary shoring of the insertion points pressure manholes using sheet pile
shoring especially where dewatering of the pits will be necessary may be
considered However it should be noted that the existing force main alignment
may likely traverse other utility lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid
truncating the existing utility lines if sheet piles are to be used as shoring If sheet
pile shoring is used it would also serve as a cut off wall for the dewatering
operations Therefore the sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to
reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of
dewatering within the excavations due to the high permeability of the subsoils A
minimum factor of safety of 1 5 should be provided in evaluating shoring stability
and bottom heave of the excavation
For preliminary design of cantilever sheet pile shoring it is our opinion that the
following recommended lateral earth pressures expressed in equivalent fluid
pressures may be used

Manhole Structures
In general we anticipate that the bottom of the pressure manhole structures will be
bearing on a tremie concrete plug over the in situ soft alluvial soils and loose
lagoonal deposits as encountered in our drilled borings Based on our engineering
analyses it is our opinion that the net increase in bearing pressure on the
underlying soil deposits resulting from the manhole construction would be minimal
Settlements on the order of less than 1 to 2 inches are anticipated for these
structures
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground manhole
structures will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the
compactive effort on the backfill material around the structures Therefore
granular material with less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve is
recommended for use as backfill around the structures to reduce the compactive
effort required which may cause additional lateral earth pressures We
recommend that the new manhole structures be designed to resist the following
lateral earth pressures from the adjacent soils

Backfilling of Insertion Points Pressure Manholes
We understand that the insertion points pressure manholes will need to be
backfilled If backfilling below the groundwater level is necessary we recommend
that No 2 rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation be used from the bottom of the
manholes up to an elevation of about 2 feet MSL The first 12 inches of backfill
above the No 2 rock should consist of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation After placement of the No 3B Fine gravel a layer of filter fabric such
as Mirafi 180 N or equivalent should be placed above the No 3B Fine gravel prior
to placement of soil backfill to reduce the potential for migration of fines into the
underlying open graded gravel fill Excavated on site sandy soils where
encountered may be used as backfill above the filter fabric up to about 3 feet
below the pavement elevation
The backfill should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness
with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 The trench
backfill from 3 feet below the finished grade elevation up to the subgrade elevation

Dewatering
Groundwater was encountered at depths ranging from about 4 5 to 10 3 feet below
the existing ground surface in the borings drilled Based on the groundwater levels
anticipated and the nature of the soils to be encountered during the manhole excavations
dewatering will be required to construct the shaft excavations at the insertion points
Therefore dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for
the proposed construction
We envision that interlocking sheet piles may be used as a groundwater cut off at
the insertion points However sumps will be needed to collect water that percolates up
into the base of the shaft or infiltrates through the sheet piles The sheet piles should be
driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to
reduce the amount of dewatering within the excavations in areas underlain by granular
subsoils with high permeability Use of an interlocking sheet piling shoring support
system with dewatering with tremie concrete plugs are relatively watertight which
should allow the groundwater levels outside the shafts to be maintained close to the
original pre construction levels Therefore some type of groundwater control requirement
should be specified in the contract documents
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the Contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the Contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the existing groundwater levels surrounding the proposed
excavation Dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is
pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or
heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
