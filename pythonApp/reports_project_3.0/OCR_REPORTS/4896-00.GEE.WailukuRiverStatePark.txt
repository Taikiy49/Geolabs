2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be dormant and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both are still active
The volume of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano extending from the ocean floor is
estimated to be on the order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest
volcanic mountain on earth Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that
Mauna Loa may have been forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is
actually composed of two separate shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter
having been covered by Mauna Loa lavas leaving only a few localized surface
expressions visible at present The Mauna Loa shield has been built by volcanic eruptions
along two principal rift zones that extend southwestward and east northeastward from the
summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consist of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Kau Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age
and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash
deposit identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and
interbedded within the rocks of the Kau Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically occur
as kipukas isolated deposits with some significant accumulations ranging up to about
30 feet in thickness
Pahala Ash is a low plasticity silt derived from the weathering of ash cinder and
Pele s Hair It generally has low shear strength and when the moisture content is high

2 2 Site Description
The project site is located within the existing Wailuku River State Park to the north
of Waianuenue Avenue in the Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii The existing Wailuku
River State Park is a long narrow parcel of land encompassing approximately 3 56 acres
in area The park premises generally extend from the intersection of Waianuenue Avenue
and Kaumana Drive in the east to a short distance beyond Pe epe e Falls Street on the
west The Wailuku River generally traverses the central portion of the park site in an
east west direction
The Rainbow Falls Lookout is located off Rainbow Drive at the eastern portion of
the park premises Based on our field observations the area surrounding the project site
is heavily vegetated with African Tulip Palm and Mango trees The existing pavement at
the parking lot has exhibited signs of distresses such as depressions and cracking An
existing restroom structure was observed to the south side of the parking lot Based on
our field observations and the topographic survey map provided the project site is slightly
sloping down from the south toward the north The existing ground surface elevations
generally range from about 385 feet Mean Sea Level MSL in the south to about
375 feet MSL on the north

3 2 Retaining Structures
As part of the park improvement project we understand that new moss rock walls
will need to be constructed to provide the necessary grade separation along the non slip

used for design of the retaining walls planned at the project site
Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Slab On Grade
Foundations section of this report The 12 inch thick compacted structural fill
recommended in the Slab On Grade Foundations section should also be provided
below the retaining wall footings The structural fill material should be compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction and should extend a minimum of
12 inches beyond the edges of the footing
In general retaining wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In
addition wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum
depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground
conditions the footing should be extended deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot
setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the
face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should
be constructed in stepped footings

lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
above fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling
and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In
addition slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
erosion of the finished slopes

3 5 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the
pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction
To reduce the potential for water infiltration into the pavement structural section we
recommend that a subdrain system be considered along the upslope boundary of the new
parking lot to intercept subterranean seepage from the sloping hillside The subdrain
should be setback away from the pavement edges to reduce the potential for water
infiltrating into the pavement areas In general the subdrain should be on the order of
about 3 to 5 feet and should consist of a 4 inch diameter PVC perforated pipe with

3 6 Utility Trench
A new sewer line will be required to serve the new comfort station planned for the
project In general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and
backfilling of the trenches for the new utility at the site The method and equipment to be
used for trench excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical
limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the
applicable federal state and local safety requirements Trench shoring design and
installation should be made the responsibility of the contractor
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support under the pipes
Where soft volcanic ash soil deposits are encountered near the pipe invert elevations we
recommend that the utility pipe be supported on an additional 18 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes A typical section of the
trench detail is presented on Plate 4 of this report

3 7 Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage at the site are especially
critical considering the sloping terrain and wet environment at the site Therefore the
finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and foundations and
to reduce the potential for ponding around the structures Excessive landscape watering
near the slabs and foundations should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface Drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from the slabs and foundations

three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about

approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3

30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third

Borings at the appropriate sample depths
