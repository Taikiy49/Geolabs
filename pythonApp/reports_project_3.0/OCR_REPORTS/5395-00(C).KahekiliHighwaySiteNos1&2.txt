PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project sites are situated along Kahekili Highway in the district of Kaneohe on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The two existing cut slopes approximately 480 and 190 feet
long are adjacent to the town bound lane of the highway as shown on the Site Plans
Plates 1 and 2 We understand that these two slopes have experienced near surface
erosion and sloughing of earth material forming rills and gullies Debris from the slopes
wash down into the drain system The slope sections are up to about 95 and 54 feet high
at Site Nos 1 and 2 respectively
It is desired to mitigate the continuous slope erosion and reduce sloughing of the
near surface earth materials onto the highway using Percussion Driven Earth Anchor
PDEA system It appears that the slope may consist of stiff to very stiff residual and or
saprolitic soils with some extremely weathered rock fragments and some talus deposits
near the lower portion of the slopes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene Epoch to early Pleistocene
Epoch Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Waianae Volcano became extinct while the Koolau Volcano remained active Therefore
the older Waianae Volcano s eastern flank was partially buried below the younger Koolau
Volcano lavas that banked against the Waianae s eastern flank
The Koolau Volcano produced a large caldera Koolau Caldera remnants of which
are along the northeastern side of the Koolau Mountains extending from about Waimanalo
to Kaneohe During the later stage of eruption the Koolau Caldera was filled with
significant thickness of basaltic lavas and breccia that were intruded by vertically oriented
dikes narrow bands of distinctively fine grained dense rock material that fed the extrusion
of the lava The lavas and associated dikes were intensively altered and decomposed by
rising hydrothermal fluids and gases which emanated from the active volcano As a result
of the accelerated rock decomposition induced by the hydrothermal activity much of the
original caldera structure has been eroded away by natural erosion processes
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project sites are along the western side of Kahekili Highway between the
intersection with Haiku on the south and Temple Valley on the north The existing cut
slopes were constructed in the 60 s during Kahekili Highway construction The existing
Kahekili Highway is two lane one lane at each direction roadway and about 44 feet wide
including 10 feet shoulder on either side of the highway
Site No 1 is comprised of upper and lower slopes separated by an 8 foot wide
bench Based on the topographic map provided the upper and lower slope heights are up
to about 52 and 43 feet respectively with slope inclination of about one horizontal to
one vertical 1H 1V The slope is approximately 480 feet long measured at the slope toe
Site No 2 is about 190 feet long measured at the slope toe and up to 54 feet high
with a slope inclination of approximately 1H 1V No middle bench was constructed on the
slope
Majority of the slopes were bare due to continuous erosion exposing reddish brown
stiff residual and or saprolite soil with some sparse grass and trees The slope erosion
caused some shallow slope failure and raveling forming rills and gullies Soil debris from
the erosion was accumulated near the slope toe Thick bushes were observed near the
beginning and end of the slopes

FIELD EXPLORATION AND LABORATORY TESTING
Our field exploration consisted of excavating eight shallow test pits five in
Site No 1 and three in Site No 2 Test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan Plates 2 1
and 2 2 The test pits were excavated using hand tools to about 1 foot depth Tube
samples were collected at the bottom of the test pits
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are present on Plate 3
Eight Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples from
the test pits to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The test results are presented
on Plates 4 through 11

EVALUATION OF ANCHOR MAT SYSTEM
Based on the current design concepts we understand that the Percussion Driven
Earth Anchor PDEA system will be installed on the slope to provide slope erosion
mitigation and reduce soil debris accumulation at the slope toe The PDEA system is a
slope treatment that involves the installation of geogrid panels anchored onto the slope
face with driven anchor tie down system in an effort to prevent the near surface soils from
moving falling A layer of erosion control matting with hydro mulch seeding is usually
placed between the slope surface and the geogrid

It should be noted that the PDEA system is primarily intented to reduce surface
erosion and to prevent shallow slide failure within the anchor depth It is not designed to
prevent slope slide failure at the greater depth
The PDEA system was modeled as a very stiff surface layer of about 5 to 10 feet
thick The combination of 5 and 10 feet long anchors in staggered pattern as shown on
Plates 13 1 and 13 2 is to increase the factor of safety against the slope from shallow
failure

To achieve the above design intent we recommend installing anchors in a square
pattern of 5 feet by 5 feet center to center along the slope surface with additional anchor at
the center of the square for good coverage The working loads for 5 and 10 feet long
anchors may be on the order of magnitude of 1 000 and 2 000 pounds respectively We
recommend locking all the anchors in place to the working loads after installation to
engage the geogrid panel with the erosion control matting tightly against the slope surface
We also recommend grading the existing slope surface to provide relatively smooth
surface by removing rills and gullies These grading requirements are intended to provide
a good contact between the firm ground surface and the PDEA system In addition the
geogrid and erosion control matting should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below
the lowest adjacent grade near the top edge of the slopes to prevent the erosion
extending beneath the matting
