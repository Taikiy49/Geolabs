SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 4, extending to depths of about 20.1 and 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Boring Nos. 1 and 4 generally encountered residual soil to about 7 to 19 feet deep, 
followed by saprolite and basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored 
of 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  In Boring No. 1, a surface fill layer about 
1 foot thick was encountered. 
The surface fill layer generally consisted of 5 inches of asphaltic concrete and 
medium dense silty sand. The residual soil was composed of medium stiff to very stiff 
clayey silt and silty clay.  The saprolite consisted of hard clayey silt with decomposed 
gravel.  The basalt rock formation was hard to very hard.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings during our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology are presented in 
Appendix A. Descriptions and graphic representations of the materials encountered in the 
borings are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were 
performed on selected soil samples, and the test results are presented in Appendix B.   
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a medium-density surface fill layer 
underlain by medium-stiff to hard residual and saprolite soils. This was followed by a basalt 
rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during 
our field exploration. 
We understand that new drainage structures will be installed for the project. Detailed 
discussions and recommendations for these structures are presented in the following 
sections. 
Excavation 
In order to install the proposed drainage structures, we anticipate that excavations 
below the existing ground surface will be required for the project construction. Based on

the planned bottoms of the drainage structures, we envision that temporary shoring of the 
excavations will be required for the project. 
Our field exploration at the project site encountered a surface fill layer, residual 
soils, saprolite, and basalt rock formation. It is anticipated that most of the material may 
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, deep excavations 
encountering cobbles and boulders and basalt rock formation may require the use of 
hoe-rams or chipping.  
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The contractor should 
be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make their own 
reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the excavation in order to 
reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement. In addition, the excavated soils 
should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from existing underground utilities to reduce the 
potential for appreciable ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the existing 
underground utility line. 
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole 
responsibility of the contractor. The contractor’s representative should be required to be 
continuously present on-site during the excavation and construction work. They will have 
the best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such as 
unforeseen subsurface soil conditions, unexpectedly high groundwater table, inappropriate 
construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may adversely affect shoring stability. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical, if necessary, and that the adjacent ground should be 
continuously monitored for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with 
instruments. 
Drainage Structure Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for the design of the 
drainage structures bearing on the anticipated medium stiff to stiff residual soil, saprolite 
and hard to very hard basalt rock formation at the project site. To provide uniform 
bearing support for the drainage structure, we recommend providing a 6-inch thick layer 
of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the bottom of the structure.  
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface 
prior to the placement of the No. 3B Fine gravel and reinforcing steel, concrete, or precast 
box structures. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the excavations 
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials or a maximum of 2 feet

Backfilling of Drainage Structure Excavations 
The drainage structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled. The excavated 
on-site soils or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, clay 
lumps, and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in the maximum dimension may be used 
as backfill above the cushion layer up to the finish grades.  
Backfills using on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where drainage 
structures are located below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the drainage structure 
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density.  
Underground Drain Lines 
We anticipate that new drain lines connecting to the new drainage structures will 
be constructed for the proposed project. The methods and equipment to be used for the 
excavation should be determined by the contractor, subject to practical limits and safety 
requirements. The excavation should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal 
safety requirements. Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility 
of the contractor. Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health 
and safety requirements. 
In general, for the support of the underground utility lines, we recommend that a 
granular bedding layer consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) be used under the pipes. The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above pipes 
should consist of free-draining backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the 
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill. It is critical that a 
free-draining granular material be used to reduce the potential for the formation of void 
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
The use of on-site soils as backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended.  
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the 
finished subgrade should consist of the approved on-site granular soils or select granular 
material. The backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
future ground subsidence. The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement 
subgrade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our field borings. Variations of subsoil conditions between and 
beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been located 
with a handheld GPS device. The elevations of the borings were estimated from spot 
elevations shown on the Drainage and Plan Profile Drawings received from WSP USA on 
October 1, 2020. The field boring location and elevation should be considered accurate 
only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the boring depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil types, and as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or in the text of this report. These data have been reviewed 
and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be noted that 
fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, temperatures, and other factors. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of WSP USA and other project 
subconsultants for specific application to the Kawa Watershed Drainage Structures, City 
and County of Honolulu SWQ PBMP project in accordance with generally accepted

This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design
