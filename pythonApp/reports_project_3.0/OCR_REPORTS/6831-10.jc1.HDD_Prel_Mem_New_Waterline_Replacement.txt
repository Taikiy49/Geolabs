This memorandum presents the results of our geotechnical engineering services
and engineering analyses performed for the New Waterline Replacement, Kapalama
Container Wharf and Dredging Design project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
A detailed summary of our findings and recommendations will be contained in our final
geotechnical engineering report. The final report should be consulted when it becomes
available.
HDD Considerations
The technical feasibility of the HDD method depends on the characteristics of the
materials at and along the depth of the pipeline profile, as well as the length of the drill
path, and diameter of the pipes to be installed.
One boring (B-301A) was drilled at the south side of the channel adjacent to the
existing Pier 51. In addition, three borings (B-6, B-16 and B-115) was drilled previously at
the north side of the channel within the University Marine Center facility. Approximate
boring locations are presented on the attached New Waterline Layout with Approximate
Boring Location Plan for reference.
Based on the borings drilled at the south and north sides of the channel (land
portions), the subsurface conditions along the HDD alignments consist of fill over soft
estuarine and/or loose lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 20 to 22 feet below
the existing ground surface. Below the estuarine/lagoonal deposits, coralline materials
were encountered, consisting of interbedded loose to dense sandy and gravelly coralline
detritus and soft to medium hard coral and sandstones. The results of our laboratory
testing from the previous borings drilled in the project vicinity indicate that the consistency
of the coralline materials may range from soft to hard with highly variable unconfined
compressive strength between about 40 and 2,600 pounds per square inch (psi).
Based on the electrical resistivity tomography conducted by Global Geophysics, the
subsurface condition anticipated along the waterline alignments within the channel
consists of coralline materials with localized alluvium materials. Silty/clayey alluvium
materials may likely be encountered between about Stations 15+60 and 16+25.
Sandy/gravelly coralline detritus may likely be encountered between about Stations 16+80

The results of the electrical resistivity tomography indicate that less competent coral 
and sandstone may be encountered at the northern half of the channel while relatively 
competent coral and sandstone may be present within the remaining portion of the 
channel along the proposed waterline alignments.  Due to the relatively hard coral and 
sandstone anticipated along the proposed waterline alignments, it is imperative that the 
contractor selects the appropriate HDD equipment, drilling tools, and reamers to drill 
through the coral and sandstone formations. 
A potential change in the drill path alignment may occur at the south side of the 
alignment at about Station 20+80.  If the pilot borehole drilling exits the sand/gravel and 
encounters coral and sandstone formations during the inclination to the ground surface, 
deflection and “skidding” could occur.  The skidding of the pilot drill bit is due to the 
difference in hardness between two subsurface materials. 
Gravelly soils will likely be encountered along the waterline alignments.  Soils with a 
high gravel content of 50 percent or more will generally be problematic, expected to cause 
difficulty in steering the pilot hole and borehole stability problems during the pilot borehole 
drilling and reaming stages.  The instability of the materials will result in caving in of the 
soils into the boreholes, which could obstruct or divert the reaming tools and result in a 
meandering drill path.  The resulting kinks in the drill path may substantially increase the 
pulling stresses on the HDPE pipes, and may impede pipe pulling or damage the pipe and 
other tools during the pullback stage. 
Pipe Size and Material 
The pipe installed by HDD must be able to safely handle the loads imposed on the 
pipe during installation and service life of the pipe.  The following items will need to be 
considered during the installation of the pipe by HDD methods. 
Installation 
Tension Force 
Tensile Stresses 
External Pressure Due to Drilling Fluid 
The tension (pull-back) force on the pipe must overcome several forms of resisting 
forces during the pullback sequence.  The resisting forces during the pullback sequence 
include the friction between the pipe and the borehole, friction between the pipe and the 
ground surface, “capstan” effect force which develops around bends, and hydrokinetic 
drag force of the drilling fluid.  The most important consideration during installation of the 
pipe by HDD methods is the tensile stresses imposed on the pipe when it is pulled into 
place through the drilled borehole.  HDPE pipes may be susceptible to failure during

During the drilling operations, water only or water mixed with bentonite and/or

The following items will also need to be considered during the service life of the 
pipe to ensure that the pipe will safely handle these loads. 
Service Life 
External Pressure Due to Earth and Live Loads 
Ring Deflection Due to Pipe Buoyancy 
Ring Deflection Due to Earth and Live Loads 
After installation of the pipes by HDD methods, the drilled borehole may deform 
considerably or even collapse.  Drilled borehole deformation normally is limited when 
arching in the soil is mobilized.  Where the arching in the soil develops, the earth pressure 
applied to the pipe is less than the overburden pressure.  Typically, a depth of cover of at 
least five pipe diameters above the top of the pipe would be sufficient to develop the 
arching in the soil. 
Ring deflection is caused by the non-uniform pressure acting on the pipe due to 
buoyancy or from earth and live loads acting on the pipe.  The ring deflection reduces the 
hydrostatic collapse resistance of the pipe and also produces tensile bending stresses in 
the pipe wall.  When the pipe is submerged in drilling fluid, there is a pressure difference 
resulting from the fluid head difference at the crown and invert of the pipe.  The larger 
pressure acting at the invert of the pipe causes an upward deflection toward the crown 
resulting in ring deflection.  Ring deflection due to earth and live loads occurs when soil 
deforms near the crown of the pipe, causing load to be applied to the crown of the pipe.  
An opposite reaction occurs at the invert of the pipe.  Because the drilling fluid does not 
provide side support for the pipe, there is no horizontal pressure to resist the vertical 
deflection of the pipe.  The ring deflection of the pipe should be limited to reduce the 
potential for instability of the pipe. 
Based on our preliminary analyses, we believe that the proposed 12 and 16-inch 
diameter waterlines installed by HDD method to an invert elevation of -105 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL) may consist of HDPE pipe with a Standard Dimension Ratio (SDR) of 9 or 
better.  The HDPE pipe with a SDR of 9 or better should safely handle the loads imposed 
into the pipe during installation and the service life of the pipe.

Drilling Fluid Pressure Versus Hydraulic Fracture Pressure 
Another important consideration during the HDD drilling operations is to determine 
if the drilling fluid pressure in the borehole will exceed the hydraulic fracture pressure of 
the in-situ soils.  The drilling fluid pressure is the sum of the hydrostatic pressure and 
circulation pressure required to overcome friction losses during flow.  If the drilling fluid 
pressure exceeds the hydraulic fracture pressure of the in-situ soil, there is a potential for 
heave and/or blowout of the ground surface.  The hydraulic fracture pressure of the soil is 
the sum of the horizontal in-situ stress of the soil and the shear strength of the soil. 
Based on our preliminary analyses, we believe that the drilling slurry pressure in the 
borehole would unlikely exceed the hydraulic fracture pressure of the in-situ soil formation 
when the waterline invert is set at an elevation of about -105 feet MSL or lower.
