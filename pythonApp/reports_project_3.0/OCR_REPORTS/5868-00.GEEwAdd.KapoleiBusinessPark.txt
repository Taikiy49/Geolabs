Soft Ground Stabilization

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling a total of

borings were drilled to 10 to 10.3 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous
basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on the Log Legend, Plate 3.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. Meanwhile, some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586,
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard

the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the

addition, core samples of the coral formation encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the coral formation. The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and classification.

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments.
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause
minor to moderate damage.
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over
90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The
Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5
(M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii.
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example,
several small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui
Earthquake of 1938 (M6.8). In addition, some houses on the Island of Oahu were
reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+).
Over the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of reported
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 occurring on the Island of Oahu. We
understand that an earthquake of Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond
Head Fault in 1948 on the Island of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken
store windows, ruptured building walls, and broken underground water mains.

Foundations
Based on the competent soil conditions, we envision that shallow foundations will
be used for the proposed structures. Due to the soil changes at shallow depth at the
project site, we believe that different design parameters should be used for above-ground
structures and below-ground structures.
Above-Ground Structure
Generally, stiff to very stiff clayey silt was encountered near the surface at the
project site during our field exploration. Based on the current design concept and
the generally competent subsoil condition encountered, we believe that spread
and/or continuous footings may be used for support of the various above-ground
improvements planned for the expansion project. An allowable bearing pressure
of up to 3,000 psf may be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the
stiff in-situ soils or new compacted fills. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those
caused by seismic forces.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades. Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement.
For footings bearing on new compacted fills, the bottom of the footing excavations
should also be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior
to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. We estimate that
footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings bearing on the
recompacted in-situ soils or new compacted fills as recommended herein to be less
than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of one-half (72) inch.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of
friction of 0.35 may be used for footing bearing on properly compacted fill materials.
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) assuming that
the soils around the footings are well compacted. The passive resistance in the
upper 12 inches of the soil should be neglected unless covered by pavements or
slabs.

Below-Ground Structure
The pump station is envisioned to be a wet pit — dry pit WWPS in order to mitigate
the odor released to the neighborhood. Based on available plans, the elevation at
the bottom of the wet well slab is -26.5 feet MSL, approximately 43.5 feet below the

Based on the field exploration, bottom of the below-ground structure may bear on
loose to medium dense calcareous sediment (coralline silty sand). In order to
reduce potential settlements, loose material should be removed or recompacted.
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be
used for the design of the concrete mat. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those
caused by seismic forces.
As mentioned, a deep excavation is anticipated for the wet well. Due to the limited
space of the project site, we envision that temporary shoring will be necessary in

shoring and should conform to the applicable health and safety requirements. We
estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings

1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of one-half (7) inch.

Dewatering
We encountered groundwater at about 13.4 to 13.6 feet below the existing ground
surface in the borings during our field exploration. Dewatering of excavations will be
necessary for the below-ground structure construction. Therefore, dewatering provisions
will need to be included in the contract documents for the proposed construction. Since the
excavation may involve discharge of groundwater, a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System (NPDES) permit may be required. The contractor should consult their
independent consultant for the latest regulations and information for the permit application.
Generally, the calcareous sediments encountered in the borings have moderate to
high permeability. Therefore, dewatering of the excavation may involve moderate to large
volumes of water. A cut-off wall system, such as interlocking steel sheet piles, could be
used to aid in dewatering the excavation. Sumps will be needed to collect water that
percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates through the sheet piles. The
sheet piles should extend to a sufficient depth below the bottom of cuts to reduce the
amount of dewatering within the excavation. However, hard limestone and sandstone
encountered in our borings may pose difficulties in driving sheet piles. As an alternative
construction method, the trench may be over-excavated to the sheet pile tip elevations.
After sheet piles are installed in place, the bottom of the trench may be backfilled with
grout.
We do not recommend dewatering by means of a well point system along the
outside of the excavations because the resultant areal depression of the natural
groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible subsurface soils
resulting in potential ground settlements, which could affect the existing structures. The
project site is next to the existing pump station. The potential ground settlements may
affect the existing utility pipes and pump station structures. Therefore, a well point system
should not be used for dewatering at this site. Prior to dewatering, the contractor must
carefully evaluate the potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing
the natural groundwater table and causing structural disturbance and/or damage to nearby
existing structures.
The contractor is responsible for construction dewatering. The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she

Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or stockpiled
in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials.
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill, provided that it meets fill material requirements.
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of
10 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture,
and compacted to a minimum of 90percent relative compaction. Relative
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight)
corresponding to the maximum dry density.
In the event shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction, the soil should be
thoroughly moistened to close cracks or prepared again as recommended above.
The excavated soft soils may be used as fill, provided that it meets fill material
requirements.

Fills and Backfills
General fill material should consist of materials less than 3 inches in largest
dimension, with a CBR swell of less than 1 percent. The fill material should have a
low expansion potential and should be free of vegetation and deleterious materials.
We believe that most of the on-site material generally may be suitable for fill
material within 1 foot of finish subgrade below slabs-on-grade.
Structural fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as crushed
coral, basalt or cinder sand. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.
Geolabs, Inc. should test imported fill materials for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use.
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, multiple-wheel
pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment.
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the clayey soils are critical
elements of earthwork, Geolabs, Inc. should perform observations and soil density
tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of
compaction and the proper moisture content. Where compaction is less than
required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content, as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. It should be noted that
the moisture requirement of the fills and subgrades (about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture) is an important requirement for the use of on-site clayey soils.
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed pump station site is underlain at
shallow depths by limestone and coralline sandstone ranging from soft to very hard. It is
anticipated that some of the limestone and sandstone may be excavated with normal
heavy excavation equipment, such as ripping with large bulldozers, where the hard
formation is near the existing ground surface. However, based on the location and scope
of the proposed project, we believe that a hoeram might be appropriate in order to
excavate the project site to the design grade. Based on limited space of the proposed
project, we envision that temporary shoring will be required for the project construction.

Utilities Lines
We envision that new utility lines and connections may be required. In general,
good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the
trenches for the new utilities at the site. The contractor should determine the method and
equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal,
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench
shoring design and installation.
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation. However, some of
the utility trench excavations will likely encounter the hard to very hard limestone and
sandstone as described on the Logs of Borings, which may pose some excavation
difficulties during the trench excavation as describe in the “Excavation” section above.
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes for support.
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, should also be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes. We believe it is critical to use a free-draining granular
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site soils as backfill
directly around utility pipes is not recommended and could result in pipe damage.
In general, the excavated on-site soil or select granular material may be used from
a level 1 foot above the pipes to the finish subgrade. The backfill material should be
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in
level lifts about 8 inches in loose thickness, and mechanically compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence.
Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill should
be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Mechanical compaction
equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project site. Water tamping,
jetting, or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill material.

Site Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is highly recommended to
install gutter systems around the structures and to divert discharge away from the
foundation and pavement areas. Blockage or clogging of drainage system could cause
ponding of water at the structure and foundations, resulting in foundation and structure
distress.
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils. Gravel strips adjacent to slabs and
foundations should be avoided.
Because ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent loss
of strength of the near-surface soils, we believe these drainage requirements are very
important for the proper performance of the proposed structures. Immediate backfill of the
foundation excavations after setting of concrete is strongly recommended to reduce water
infiltration. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface run-off away from foundations.
