REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 2 7 to 3 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age 2 2 to 2 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed
by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the
dome shaped Koolau Shield Volcano
Approximately 850 000 to years ago some limited volcanic activity
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents scattered
across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu The eruptions occurred along the
established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano Some of the most notable
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head Craters
including Aliamanu Crater were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to elevations above the present sea level

The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments filled pre existing
depressions and accumulated at higher elevations
The rock formations are mainly inclined downward towards the central volcanic vent
The volcanic tuff rock comprising Aliamanu Crater was created by the fall out deposition of
super heated volcanic ash and cinders containing some entrained blocks of basaltic lava
rock which were emitted from the Aliamanu volcanic vent
The northeastern part of the site lies near the southwestern flank of the Aliamanu
vent and Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As the molten magma
rose through these vents it reacted with the groundwater generating steam that
developed into explosive eruptions depositing low cones of volcanic ash which
consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid when the
seawater level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited
its material in alluvium that originated from Halawa stream
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated
over the Aliamanu tuff before the resumption of the next phase of volcanic activity The
Salt Lake eruptions occurred when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its
present level The Salt Lake eruptive events deposited its materials over earlier tuff from
the older Aliamanu events
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the Pearl Harbor area The sea
level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 100 feet
above the present sea level
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have
added man made fills bringing the site to its present configuration

The following sections briefly describe the general characteristics of each of the
above stratigraphic units based on our review of the available data and experience in the
vicinity of the proposed transmission pipeline limits The approximate delineation of these
stratigraphic units at the ground surface along the pipeline alignment is presented on the
Geology Map Plate 3
Older Alluvium Qa
Older alluvial deposits are generally very stiff to hard silty and clayey soils that
originally were deposited in alluvial environments buried over long periods of time by more
recent deposits to become somewhat consolidated and then subsequently uncovered at
the ground surface by erosion or other geologic processes This unit generally overlies the
Koolau Basalt and consists of terrigenous sediments which have been transported by
stream action from the upper reaches of the streams
Gravel lenses representing buried stream channels are sometimes encountered
Portions of the Older Alluvium unit contain some weathered basaltic cobbles and boulders
These coarse grained parts of the unit also may contain artesian groundwater from the basal
fresh water system or as a sub aquifer The deposits may resemble saprolite soil deposits
but are clearly differentiable by the layered characteristics
Older Alluvium materials generally exhibit low permeability with exception of the
coarse grained basaltic cobbles and boulders and high strength with generally low
settlement characteristics Frequently these materials show a moderate to high shrink swell
potential when subjected to fluctuations in moisture content
Coral Limestone Qls
This unit consists of fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and their related detrital
debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant portion of any
coralline rock assemblage The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coralline
debris consisting of gravel sand and silt sized clastic fragments Some of the coralline
fragments were dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented and or uncemented
fine sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal deposits and the variable
degree of cementation frequently present this formation generally has high to moderate
in situ permeability and strength characteristics The following subunits are typically included
in this unit

Coralline Detritus
This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations The soils
generally consist of medium dense to dense sandy gravel and gravelly sands Silts
and clays are only present in minor or trace amounts Detritus also may be weakly
cemented with occasional moderate to strong cementation
Coral Formation
This subunit is composed of coral and calcareous algae in their original growth
positions or detrital deposits which have become moderately to well cemented
Sandstone
It is a sedimentary rock composed mainly of cemented sand sized particles clasts
consisting of individual mineral particles or grains of the weathered parent rock The
sedimentary particles comprising sandstone typically are derived from the
breakdown of parent material into sedimentary particles The sedimentary particles
typically are eroded transported and sorted by water or air and deposited as layers
of sedimentary materials Over time the sediments may be buried by other
sediments or geologic layers and the burial pressure combined with a cementing
agent act to consolidate the grains and cement the pore space between the grains
to form hardened sandstone Typical cementing agents may be calcium carbonate
common in calcareous sand deposits of Hawaii or silica and some clays
Sandstones containing such clast inclusions commonly are referred to as beachrock
in Hawaii having been derived from available shoreline geologic materials
Quaternary Honolulu Tuffs Qht
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consists of volcanic ash which
consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard rock that resembles siltstone or sandstone
locally called mudrock The tuff is frequently found overlying coralline deposits or alluvium
units It should be noted that it is quite common to see a coarsening from accidental detritus
and or agglomeration toward the bottoms of the ash beds
Because the formation is a medium hard rock it has very high strength
characteristics Structurally the tuff is very competent This formation has relatively low
permeability and groundwater generally is not encountered in this formation When it does
occur it is usually in the form of seepage along joints
Recent Alluvium Ra
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low energy
environment such as an estuary or bay Frequently the soils assigned to this unit
accumulated very rapidly under water and might not have been exposed to air Therefore
there is a tendency to encounter under consolidated sediments which settle under the
weight of minimal overburden or structural loading Recent alluvium is generally found within
some stream channels and bordering some stream banks at the mouths outlets of
streams and within broad valley floors and basin features surrounded by hills These soils
also may be found in proximity to swamp and tidal flat environments

The soils assigned to this unit generally are considered to have low in situ
permeability and low strength characteristics and the tendency for excessive settlement to
occur in structures overlying soils of this unit The following subunits are typically included
in this unit
Recent Alluvium
This subunit generally consists of tan to brown silty clays with varying amounts of
sands and infrequent pockets of gravel and cobbles The consistency is generally in
the range of very soft to medium stiff
Lagoonal Deposit
This subunit generally consists of light gray to medium gray sandy silts or silty sands
in a very soft to soft consistency and or very loose to loose relative density
Gravel sized fragments are rare Frequently the silts are organic in origin and may
have a mild to strong organic odor
Estuarine Marsh Deposit
This subunit generally consists of dark gray and brown silts and clays with fibrous
organic materials in a very soft to soft consistency This soil unit contains traces of
fine to medium grained sands They are characterized by a moderate to strong
organic odor and may contain small fragile mollusk shells
Recent Fill Rf
This stratigraphic unit represents low lying areas which were filled during
urbanization The majority of these low lying areas is underlain at relatively shallow depths
by recent alluvium and were originally marsh areas Some of the filled lands may be
underlain by older alluvial soil deposits In areas underlain by the recent alluvium the fill
materials generally consist of clayey silts and sandy silts with stiff to hard consistencies The
fills only form a thin crust over the soft alluvial soils
The quality and composition of the fill materials are variable These fills were
frequently placed without proper compaction control Therefore it is difficult to make any
general statement regarding their engineering properties or construction in these areas
GROUNDWATER
Elevations taken from Google Earth show the lowest point on the project site and
appears to be at about 5 feet Mean Sea Level MSL near the intersection of Radford
Drive and N Road The project site slopes up to the northeast portions of the project site
to about 85 feet MSL
Groundwater is anticipated to be encountered at a shallow depth less than
20 feet as a basal water table near sea level through various parts of the site with the
exception of the northeastern side of the site Salt Lake Boulevard and Marshall Road
However groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on seasonal rainfall
groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
