Discussions
The project site is situated at the mouth of Makaleha Stream on the north shore of
the Island of Stream like most streams in Hawaii is an
ephemeral stream with a relatively well defined channel in its lower reaches Typically
major streamflow occurs only after a precipitation event however Makaleha Stream does
have a modest base flow as a result of artesian leakage in its upper reach
The area where Makaleha Stream discharges into the ocean is sheltered bya
patchy system of reefs forming a barrier to major wave action As is typical for similar
areas in Hawaii beach sand is transported along the beach by longshore currents At the
mouths of small streams like Makaleha when the rate of sand transport exceeds the
ability of the stream to erode the sand a littoral sand berm will build up and close the
mouth of the stream
The accumulated beach sand will impound stream water behind the littoral berm
until such time as a major precipitation event causes the berm to fail allowing the
impounded stream to rush out of the breach During such a breach the flow velocity is
very high causing both vertical and horizontal scour of the sand simultaneously deepening
and widening the opening until there is a balance between the elevations of the stream
water and the outlying ocean water
Our site reconnaissance indicates that the shallow subsurface conditions at the
mouth of Makaleha Stream generally consist of a surface layer of beach sand up to about
5 feet thick overlying clayey alluvial soils See Photograph 1 Laboratory testing indicates
that the beach sand is relatively uniform medium grain sand

Photograph 2 shows the mouth of Makaleka Stream a short time after a major rain

In Photograph 3 taken immediately after a second storm the berm is fully
breached and flood water is being discharged at full flow Within a few weeks the berm
will again close off the mouth of the stream
In summary the closing and opening of the mouth of Makaleha Stream by a littoral
accumulation of beach sand is a repetitive natural occurrence and does not greatly impact
the flooding conditions of Makaleha Stream It is important to note that the material is
easily eroded beach sand rather than more stable dune sand
