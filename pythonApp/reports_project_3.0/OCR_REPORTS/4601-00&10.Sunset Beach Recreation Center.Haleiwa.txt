REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level was
high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and the soil materials are
transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general stream
flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit large volumes
of water for very short duration Because of this transport of sediments is intermittent and
the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles
gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are
left as deposits
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 5
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin to
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of
the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously
erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This
continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older
alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project site is generally underlain by stiff alluvial deposits over coral detritus
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted silty clays or engineered fill materials may be used to support the proposed new
recreation center The new footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface silty clays exhibit a moderate
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To reduce
the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from the swelling
of the near surface soils we recommend that the on site silty clays be capped with a minimum
of 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below slab areas Our
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations
slabs on grade site grading pavements and leach fields are further discussed in the following
sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we recommend that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new
recreation center An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf
may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted silty clays or
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 8
engineered fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the silty clays or the engineered fills the bottom of footing
excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at
the bottom of footing excavations the material should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with select
granular materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In lieu of
backfilling the over excavation with compacted granular fill the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted silty clays or engineered fills
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located
on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches For footings
constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep
enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the
outside edge of the footings to the slope face
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below
a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is

loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
silty clays with a moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the silty clays due to changes in the moisture content we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches
of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below the slab cushion
The non expansive select granular capping fill should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation subsection of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the
soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with engineered fill
For the building slabs we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to
serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce
GEOLABS INC

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should
be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account
for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and demolition the areas within the future slab on grade should be
over excavated to provide the 12 inch thick layer of non expansive select granular
fill material Areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The compaction requirement should be increased to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in areas subjected to vehicular traffic
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close cracks or prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft
areas and replacement with well compacted fill The need for over excavation should
be evaluated by a representative from Geolabs in the field

Fills and Backfills
The on site silty clays generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of
general fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and
rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension Imported general fill
materials needed for the site filling should consist of materials with a low expansion
potential Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with particle
sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12 or higher and a swell potential
of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1883
The 12 inch non expansive capping layer recommended below the building and
walkway slabs should consist of select granular material such as crushed basalt or
coral The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger
than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent
Aggregate base course and select borrow subbase material should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate and should conform to the City and County of Honolulu Department
of Public Works Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction dated
September 1986
it should be noted that boulders were encountered in our borings Therefore some
over sized materials should be expected in the excavated materials and should not
be used as general fill materials

Leach Field
We understand that on site sewage treatment and disposal by the leach field method
is planned for the proposed recreation center Therefore field percolation tests were
conducted in our field exploration to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of the
sewage disposal system A total of three field percolation tests were performed at Boring
Nos 1 through 3 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 1
The field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State s Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The tests were performed by drilling 4 inch
diameter boreholes extending to depths of about 4 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Approximately 1 to 2 inches of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the
borehole to protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments During the test
the hole was carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel
The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the
calculation of the percolation test was based
Based on the percolation tests conducted the percolation rates at the site generally
ranged between 40 and 160 minutes per inch Note that 60 minutes per inch is the
minimum percolation rate allowed by the State of Hawaii Department of Health Results
of the individual percolation tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 3 of this report

Therefore additional percolation tests were performed at the park site across
Kalunawaikaala Stream north of the proposed recreation center as shown on Plate 2 2
and the area across of Kamehameha Highway west of the proposed recreation center
as shown on Plate 2 3 The tests were performed by drilling 4 inch diameter borehoies
extending to depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on our field exploration the site across of Kalunawaikaala Stream is underlain
by alluvial deposits consisting of very stiff to very hard silty clays with varying amounts of
sand and gravel extending to the maximum depths explored of about 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The site across of Kamehameha Highway is underlain by beach
deposits consisting of loose to medium dense fine coralline sand extending to the maximum
depths explored of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on the percolation tests conducted at the site across of Kamehameha Highway
the percolation rates were generally less than 1 minute per inch The percolation rates at the
site across of Kalunawaikaala Stream ranged between 3 and 15 minutes per inch The
results of the additional percolation tests are presented on Plates C 4 through C 7 of this
report
Based on the additional percolation test results we believe that both sites are suitable
for siting of the proposed leach fields Based on the results we recommend a percolation rate
of about 15 minutes per inch may be used for design of the proposed leach fields sited at the
area across of Kalunawaikaala Stream We recommend a percolation rate of 1 minute per
inch may be used for design of the proposed leach fields sited at the area across of
Kamehameha Highway Due to the variability of the subsoil conditions the absorption

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and
pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales should
be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from the slabs
and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings to depths ranging from about 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface
One bulk sample of the near surface materials was obtained to evaluate the pavement
support characteristics of the near surface soils The approximate locations of the drilled
borings and bulk sample are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 1 In addition four additional
borings were drilled for the alternate leach field locations The approximate locations of the
additional drilled borings are shown on Plates 2 2 and 2 3 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight solid stem and hollow stem
augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved
in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
