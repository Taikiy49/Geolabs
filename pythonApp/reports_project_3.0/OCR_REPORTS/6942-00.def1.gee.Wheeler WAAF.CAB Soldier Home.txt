SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Combat Aviation Brigade (CAB) Soldier Centered Medical Home 
project within Wheeler Army Airfield on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the CAB Soldier Centered Medical Home project within Wheeler Army Airfield. These 
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining 
structures and earthwork only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is in the northern portion of the Wheeler Army Airfield military 
installation in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The CAB Soldier 
Centered Medical Home project involves an addition connecting the existing Buildings 
680 and 681 along with other site improvements, such as sidewalks, parking stalls and 
an accessible curb ramp [compliant with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)]. 
Based on our field observations and the plans provided, site grading will be 
minimal and we anticipate cuts and fills of less than1 foot deep will be required. 
1.3 
Purpose and Scope

conditions at the project site to develop a soil data set to formulate geotechnical 
recommendations for design of the CAB Soldier Centered Medical Home project. In 
order to accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the 
following tasks and efforts:

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against 
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips 
of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees. The lava flows on the plateau have 
weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the 
soil. The deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalaua, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are the 
result of this weathering and erosion.  
Wheeler Army Airfield lies at the mouth of a broad amphitheater headed valley 
where colluvial and alluvial materials intercalate with and sometimes overlie the Koolau 
lavas.  These colluvial and alluvial materials weather to darker brown and reddish brown 
soils and generally have higher clay fractions than the more silty residual soils.  The brown 
and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits overlying weathered 
volcanic rock.  Residual soils derived from the in-situ chemical weathering of basaltic rock 
tend to be red to reddish brown in color. 
An available geologic map indicates that the surface geology at the site consists of 
the weathering products (i.e., residual soils, of Koolau Basalt).

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 26.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Two bulk samples, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, 
were obtained from the near-surface materials to evaluate their swelling potential and 
pavement support characteristics. The approximate locations of the borings drilled and 
the near-surface bulk samples collected are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The borings generally encountered hard to very hard clayey silt and silty clay 
soils to the maximum depth explored of 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. However, the Wheeler Army Airfield area is an area of relatively high 
rainfall.  Groundwater levels can occur and fluctuate depending on factors such as 
seasonal rainfall, surface runoff, and other factors. In addition, there exists a potential 
for the presence of local perched groundwater and seepage beneath the site. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered during our field exploration 
are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests 
performed on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by hard to 
very hard clayey silts and silty clays extending to the maximum depths explored. We did 
not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend utilizing shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the 
building addition planned for the Soldier Centered Medical Home project. Based on the 
anticipated site grading, the shallow foundations should bear on either recompacted 
on-site clayey soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished grades. 
Laboratory tests indicate the on-site clayey soils exhibit low swelling potential when 
subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for distress to the future 
slabs-on-grade (if required) resulting from the swelling of the on-site clayey soils, we 
recommend providing at least 6 inches of non-expansive select granular material below 
the slab cushion fill. The non-expansive select granular material does not need to be 
incorporated in the subgrade below the building foundations. 
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for 
design are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend utilizing a shallow foundation system to support the Soldier Centered 
Medical Home addition.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,500 pounds per 
square foot (psf) may be used to design the shallow foundations bearing on the stiff 
on-site soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing 
value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottoms of exterior footings should be embedded a minimum of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration into the footing subgrade soils. For interior footings, the footing 
embedment may be reduced to a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent

3.2 
Earthwork 
We anticipate that the majority of the site grading work will involve shallow cuts 
and fills on the order of less than 1 foot deep or thick. In general, a Geolabs 
representative should monitor the grading operations to observe whether undesirable 
materials are encountered during the excavation process, and to confirm whether the 
exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration. 
3.2.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, and other deleterious and 
organic materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and/or 
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to 
receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense materials, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed off-site and/or used 
in landscape areas, where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive 
and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to 
account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, existing structures and pavements that are to be 
demolished should be removed completely. Over-excavations resulting from 
demolition should be backfilled with compacted on-site soils or select granular fill 
materials. Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting 
excavation should be properly backfilled with excavated on-site soils or select 
granular fill materials placed in 8-inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.

After clearing, grubbing, and demolition, areas at grade or areas designated to 
receive fill should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned 
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Subgrades for areas subjected to

determined by ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
Based on our field exploration, the excavated on-site soils may be reused as a 
source of general fill and backfill materials.  Imported fill materials should consist of 
non-expansive, select granular fill materials, such as crushed coral and basaltic 
gravel. The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
greater than 3 inches in largest dimension. The materials should have a California 
Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less 
when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. The materials also should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirement 
Fill and backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The compaction requirement for the finished subgrade of pavement areas should 
be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate base and aggregate subbase materials required for the project should 
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsections 703.06 
and 703.17, respectively, of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Roads and 
Bridge Construction (2005). Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content,

3.3 
Slabs-On-Grade 
Laboratory tests indicate the on-site clayey soils exhibit low swelling potential 
when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for future distress to 
the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade (if required) resulting from shrinking and swelling of 
the clayey soils, we recommend capping the slab subgrade area with at least 6 inches 
of non-expansive select granular fill materials. The non-expansive select granular fill 
materials should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed as soon as possible after 
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in 
the above discussions. The non-expansive select granular fill materials and the 
underlying subgrade soils should be kept moist until the final placement of concrete.  
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks or the subgrades should 
be prepared again. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to 
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. 
We envision that concrete pads for mechanical equipment will be subjected to 
equipment vibrations. Therefore, we recommend providing a 6-inch thick layer of 
aggregate subbase course below these slabs. The aggregate subbase course should 
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The 6-inch thick layer of 
aggregate subbase course would serve to provide uniform support of the slabs 
subjected to vibrations. 
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at 
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Areas adjacent to the slab

maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed 
on-site during or after construction. If concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in 
or adjacent to the pavement area, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 
2 inches into the soil below the aggregate base course to reduce the migration of 
landscaping water into the pavement section. In addition, a subdrain system is 
recommended to collect the excess water from the landscaping for long-term usage. 
3.6 
Utility Trenches 
We anticipate that utility work will most likely be required for the project.  Based 
on our field exploration, utility trench excavation will encounter stiff to hard clayey 
material. We envision that conventional excavation methods may generally be utilized 
for the trench excavation.  The contractor is responsible for determing the method and 
equipment to be used for trench excavation and shall comply with the applicable 
federal, state, and local requirements. 
For support of the utility lines, we recommend using granular bedding consisting 
of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes. The 
initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free-draining material, 
such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for pipe damage from compaction of 
the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce the potential 
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for 
the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site soils as backfill directly around utility pipes is 
not recommended. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and utility 
pipe damage.

3.7 
Drainage 
The surface and subsurface drainage at the site are critical considering the 
nature of the soils at the site. We believe that the finished grades adjacent to structures 
should be sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential 
for ponding. It is also advised to install gutter systems around the structures and to 
divert the discharge away from the foundation and pavement areas. Excessive 
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided. Gravel 
strips adjacent to slabs and foundations should be avoided. Planters next to foundations 
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into 
the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near-surface soils. The foundation 
excavations should be backfilled properly against the footings immediately after setting 
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration. Drainage swales should be provided as soon 
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run-off away from walls and 
foundations.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 26.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer 
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
