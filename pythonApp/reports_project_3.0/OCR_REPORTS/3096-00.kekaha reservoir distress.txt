INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the existing 0 5 Million Gallon M G Kekaha Reservoir located in Paua
Valley north of Kekaha on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input of foundation
rehabilitation and may not be appropriate to form the basis for the preparation of
construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project consists of an existing 0 5 M G concrete water reservoir of approxi
mately 74 feet in diameter situated in the lower Paua Valley north of Kekaha on the
island of Kauai We understand that this tank was constructed in the mid 1970 s The
reservoir structure is constructed on a reinforced concrete slab with a thickened edge for
a continuous ring footing supporting concrete walls of about 20 feet high The perimeter
wall footing is shown on the plans to be 3 feet wide
We understand that shortly after the reservoir was constructed water leakage was
observed near the bottom of the tank along a portion of the wall foundation interface
situated on the eastern side of the reservoir The approximate location of the zone of
observed seepage at the wall foundation interface is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
We understand that attempts were made to seal the leaks from within the tank by
patching using epoxy however these attempts were met with limited success
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3096 00 Page 3
Therefore it is desired to evaluate the potential cause of the leakage and distress to the
reservoir and to repair the leakage

The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions near the location of the observed water leakage of the reservoir and also on
either side of this distressed zone The subsurface information obtained was used to
develop an idealized soil data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertinent to the design of the foundation rehabilitation

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located north of Kekaha in Paua Valley on the island of Kauai
Hawaii The site generally slopes down from the north to the south Access to the site
is provided by an unpaved roadway from the south of Old Mana Road The reservoir site
is enclosed by a chain link fence which surrounds two 0 5 M G reservoir tanks An
asphalt concrete perimeter roadway surrounds each of the water tanks One of the water
tanks located on the eastern portion of the site is a tank with a concrete wall
constructed in the 1970 s The other tank located on the southwest portion of the site
is a steel wall tank constructed in the 1980 s
The eastern concrete reservoir has experienced some structural distress and is the
subject of this report Along the eastern side of the concrete tank is a zone of slow
continual water seepage of approximately 30 feet wide where the tank wall meets the
perimeter foundation The asphalt concrete pavement fronting this zone of seepage
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3096 00 Page 5
appears to have settled differentially from adjacent areas as evidenced by the presence
of linear cracks in the pavement near the margins of the seepage zone Hairline cracks
are also present on the perimeter ring footing foundation in the leakage area The
surface soils in this leakage area appear to be loose
Plastic sheeting had been placed along the leakage zone attempting to shed the
seepage water away from the immediate area of the tank foundation This plastic
sheeting was noted to be deteriorating and was not effectively shedding the seepage
water Previous attempts to seal the seepage zone by mortar were noted but also
appeared to be deteriorating
The construction plans indicate that the entire water tank structure is situated on
cut However overlying the in situ cut formation a cushion fill layer consisting of 18
inches of sand fill is shown on the plans below the tank slab and the wall footing Based
on field observations and construction plans a 12 inch diameter steel washout pipe is
buried beneath the distressed pavement area and the washout pipe appears to center
beneath the zone of water seepage from the reservoir Based on the plans we
understand that this washout line was jacketed with concrete within the limits of the tank
This washout line discharges to a drainage ditch outside the fenced property
A brief reconnaissance of the reservoir tank interior was conducted on January 19
1994 to observe the interior conditions of the structure Several cracks were noted on
the floor slab of the reservoir tank The cracks appear to be along the area where
seepage water and distress were noted on the exterior tank perimeter

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject tank site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings to depths of about 8 to 17 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the three borings drilled we generally encountered fill soils consisting
of tan sand underlain by brown silty clays and clayey silts with gravel from below the
pavement section to approximately 4 to 8 feet below the ground level The borings
encountered increasing amounts of gravel and cobbles below the clayey materials until
encountering the in situ basalt rock formation at depths of about 5 to 8 feet below the
existing ground level Our borings were advanced to a maximum depth of about 17 feet
below the existing ground surface
Boring No 1 drilled adjacent to the buried 12 inch washout line revealed a zone
of loose sand cushion fill and stiff clayey backfill materials which appeared to be less
compact than those fills encountered in either of the adjacent borings Boring Nos 2 and
3 This loose fill Zone may be associated with an inadequately compacted trench backfill
around the 12 inch reservoir washout line or the upper 18 inch sand fill placed beneath
the slab and perimeter ring footing
In general the near surface soils appear to be damp and grade to moist with
increasing depth Based on laboratory swell tests the near surface soils appear to be
highly expansive when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to rainfall and the time of year

FIELD SURVEY
Two sets of spot elevation surveys of the tank perimeter were provided by the
Department of Water County of Kauai One set of elevation readings was taken in
January 1987 and the other set was taken in January 1994 Based on these two
separate elevation surveys it appears that the portion of the tank where seepage was
observed has an elevation differential on the order of approximately inch It was not
evident from the surveys if the settlement observed has ceased since only the
approximate locations of the previous survey points were available Exact locations of the
previous survey points were not available
Based on the available information and assuming that the tank was constructed
near level it appears that the portion of the tank where water seepage was observed has
probably settled an amount on the order of inch The other portions of the tank
perimeter did not appear to have significant elevation differences
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the field surveys provided our field exploration and site reconnaissance
it is our opinion that the zone of water seepage observed along the bottom of the tank
is likely related to the settlement of the footing in this area The cause of the settlement
may be attributed to one or more of the following factors
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3096 00 Page 8
1 The sandy and clayey materials encountered in Boring No 1 appeared to
be previous trench backfill which appears to be in a loose less compact
state than the tank s other structural fill materials as encountered in the
other two adjacent borings
2 The 18 inches of sand cushion fill beneath the slabs may have been placed
with inadequate compaction resulting in potential ground settlements
3 Confinement of the sandy cushion material was lost when the asphalt
pavement fronting the zone of observed seepage was removed
4 Infiltration of water into the foundation soils and subsequent saturation of
the fill materials could have resulted in tank settlement
In general we believe that the cause of the tank settlement and observed water
seepage could be related to the perimeter continuous footing foundation supported on
the sandy fill materials and the lack of confinement of these sandy soils Since the
reservoir tank is sensitive to foundation settlements we recommend that the portion of
the tank where water seepage and settlements were observed be underpinned with new
footings extending to the underlying basalt formation encountered in the drilled borings
at depths of about 5 to 8 feet below the surface For the other portions of the reservoir
tank we recommend that the entire tank perimeter area be paved with asphalt concrete
or concrete apron to provide confinement of the sandy soils beneath the other portions
of the foundation which will not be underpinned The reservoir tank should be drained
prior to construction of the new footings
Inside the reservoir tank cracks were noted on the slab It is recommended that
a new structural slab be provided above portions of the existing slab to span the areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3096 00 Page 9
where cracks were noted This would serve to strengthen the slab and to reduce leakage
from the reservoir tank The new structural slab may be constructed directly above the
existing slab provided that the existing slab surface is properly cleaned and that the new
concrete is properly bonded to the existing concrete In addition a new water tight
membrane should be provided in the interior of the reservoir tank to reduce future water
leakage
New Foundations
The zone of observed seepage is about 30 feet wide along the tank perimeter as
noted on the Site Plan Plate 2 For underpinning of the existing foundations with new
foundations we recommend that the underpinning sections extend at least 10 feet
beyond the area of the observed seepage to obtain a minimum of 50 feet of total
underpinning The new underpinning foundation sections under the reservoir tank should
excavate through the previous trench backfill or cushion fill materials and bear on the
underlying basalt formation Based on the borings drilled the depth to the basalt rock
formation is about 8 feet below the ground surface at the Boring No 1 area Based on
the sloping topography the depth to basalt formation may vary
Based on our field exploration we believe that an allowable bearing pressure up
to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of the new footings
bearing on the basalt formation The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by for transient loadings such as temporary wind and or
seismic forces We estimate that the total settlements of the new footings bearing on the
basalt formation to be very small probably on the order of less than inch
To reduce the load on the existing footings and to reduce water seepage at the
footing underpinning areas we recommend that the reservoir be drained prior to
construction of the new footings In addition we also recommend that the new
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3096 00 Page 10
underpinning footings be constructed in segments not exceeding 10 feet to reduce the
potential for significant ground movements during construction It should be noted that
shoring of the footing excavation will likely be necessary and is discussed in the
Excavation section of this report
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the new footing and the basalt formation and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of the
footings A coefficient of friction of 0 5 may be used between the base of the footings and
the supporting basalt formation Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f
for footings These values assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly against
well compacted soils Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in
the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected
It is important that a representative of Geolabs Hawaii observe the footing
underpinning excavations to evaluate the competency of the bearing materials and the
embedment depths If unsuitable materials are encountered in the underpinning footing
excavations these materials should be over excavated to expose the basalt formation
Excavation
Construction of the new underpinning footings will require excavations on the order
of 5 to 8 feet or deeper to extend the new underpinning footing segments down to the
basalt rock formation As previously indicated shoring of the footing excavations will be
necessary to reduce the potential sloughing of the sandy fill soils directly beneath the slab
and footings Therefore the back and sides of the excavation will need to be shored to
reduce the potential sloughing of the sandy cushion fill material from being undermined
by the excavation for the new footing Care should be exercised when excavating the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3096 00 Page 11
new footings at the ends of the underpinned area since the adjacent existing footings will
not be underpinned Undermining of the sand cushion fill beneath these footings will
need to be properly backfilled with lean concrete to reduce the potential for future
settlement
The appropriate method and equipment to be used for the underpinning footing
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavated soils should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal
distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce
the potential for ground movement All excavations should conform to apprioprate OSHA
Federal State and local requirements
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made
the sole responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the
best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during construction such
as unforeseen subsoils groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or
techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring
system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to estimate
accurately the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring supports are installed the
subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation

Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground should be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with
instruments
Fills and Backfills
In general the on site sandy soils may be reused as a source of fill material
however the on site clayey materials are highly expansive and should not be reused as
a source of fill material All fills and backfills should be properly moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
Any imported soil should be select fill Select fill snould contain no lumps
fragments or rocks greater than 3 inches in largest dimension with less than 20 percent
passing the No 200 sieve Fill materials should be tested and approved by Geolabs
Hawaii prior to delivery to the job site All surfaces should be finished to create smooth
unyielding subgrades and should be kept moist until covered by concrete slabs or
pavements
Pavement
Asphalt concrete pavements may be used for the on site roadway and around the
perimeter of the tank We anticipate tnat the vehicle loading for the asphalt concrete
pavements of this project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and trucks
Therefore we have assumed light traffic loading conditions for pavement design
purposes In general the near surface on site soils are highly expansive therefore
pavements constructed directly on the potentially expansive soils may undergo distress
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3096 00 Page 13
when subjected to changes in the moisture content The existing asphalt concrete paved
roadway appears to have undergone significant distress as evidenced by the extensive
cracking observed Therefore we recommend that the new pavement structural section
be supported on at least 18 inches of select borrow to reduce the potential of heaving
from the highly expansive subgrade soils Based on this we recommend the following
preliminary pavement design be used for cost estimating purposes
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 percent relative compaction
18 0 Inch Select Borrow 95 percent relative compaction
26 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The subgrade soil under the pavement structural section should be moisture
conditioned to between 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction CBR tests and or field observations should be
performed on the actual subgrade soil samples during construction to confirm that the
above design sections are adequate
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches
into the subgrade soil to reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend a subdrain system be
constructed adjacent to paved laridscaped areas

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depth of our borings ranged between about 8 to 17 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with 4 inch diameter continuous flight solid stem augers or coring tools as
appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation of the
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74

core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils The results of the swell tests are
summarized on Plate B 2
One consolidation test with time rates was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate B 3
