This report presents the results of our foundation investiga
tion for the proposed 18 story apartment building and ancillary
parking structure which is located between Ala Ilima and Likini
Streets in the Salt Lake district of Oahu Hawaii

SITE CONDITIONS
SURFACE
The site is bounded to the south by Ala Ilima Street and the
north by Likini Street A terrace approximately 5 feet high
running in an east west direction divides the site The
surface drainage on the lower portion of the site is directed
toward Likini Street and the upper portion of the site towards
Ala Ilima Street The surface elevation on the upper portion
ranges from approximately 33 to 30 feet above sea level and
on the lower from approximately 29 to 18 feet At the time
of this field investigation the site was used as a storage
area for construction materials

GEOLOGY
Geologically the site is underlain by some poorly bedded
volcanic tufts from the Salt Lake Crater These tufts are
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Late in Oahu s
geologic history hot magma rising in fissures along the coast
encountered sea water The subsequent hydromagmatic explosions
generated a series of tufts cinder and breccia volcanic cones
Salt Lake crater is one of the cones The ash and rock frag
ments were often welded together by heat to fairly resistant
rock which is locally known as mudrock Much of the original
pore space was blocked by welding and palagonization causing
Salt Lake to act as a large reservoir Rain water carrying
sediment and dissolved minerals were deposited in this basin
Dissolved carbonates and salts were generally deposited during
periods of dessication and possibly by calcareous algae
Carbonate minerals are mixed with land derived debris and are
normally consolidated Later a lakeside marsh apparently
covered the site as is evidenced by carbonized root and stem
fragments in some of the borings Within the last eight years
a man made fill consisting of silty sands and mudrock was
placed over the previous deposits The thickness of this man
made fill is approximately 20 feet The center of the site
is to be over the shoreline of the ancient Salt Lake

SUBSURFACE
In order to investigate the new building location and to
supplement the existing data three additional borings were
drilled Two of the borings were drilled on the apartment
building site and one boring was drilled in the parking struc
ture site The locations of these borings and the borings
originally drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 A
more detailed description of the field exploration and labora
tory testing is presented in the Appendix which also contains
the Logs of Borings and the results of laboratory tests
The site is immediately underlain by brown tuff or dark gray
cinder tuff that varies in thickness from approximately 18
to 26 feet below the existing surface The fill placement was
performed approximately 7 to 8 years ago We have obtained
copies of reports for the density tests that were taken at
various elevations throughout the fill by another firm The
placement of the fill was not continuously supervised and
therefore may contain local softer of less compacted areas
Underlying the man made fill is a 3 to 6 foot layer of clayey
silt that may be the lakeside marsh deposits This material
is located mainly above the old shore line which is located
in approximate center of the property below the existing
terrace Above the shoreline the marsh deposits are underlain

by layers of clayey and silty sands that are about 3 to 6
feet in thickness These deposits are mixed with carbonate

contains layers of a dark gray silt which may be decomposed

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
GENERAL
Our additional study indicates that the proposed high rise
structure and the subterranean parking garage may be supported
on shallow strip or spread footings bearing in the fill
FOUNDATIONS
Analysis indicates that the proposed high rise structure may
be constructed on footings founded a minimum depth of three
feet below final adjacent grade in the dense granular fill

Strip or spread footings may be designed for a total imposed
dead plus live load of 5 000 pounds per square foot Total
settlements are calculated to be on the order of magnitude of
about one to two inches The elevator shaft footings should
be kept as high as possible to reduce differential settlement
that may occur from inducing foundation pressures too close
to the compressible layer Due to the bridging effect of the
dense fill layer maximum differential settlements should be
on the order of about three quarters of an inch The proposed
parking structure placed on spread footings at a total dead
plus live load of 3 000 pounds per square foot and founded at
a minimum depth of two feet below adjacent grade will result
in total settlements on the order of two to two and one half
inches with differential settlements on the order of one inch
Calculations indicate that most of the settlement will occurr
during the time of construction
LATERAL PRESSURES
Lateral loads may be resisted by the passive resistance of the
soils and their friction A coefficient of friction of 0 4
may be used between the supporting soils and footings The
passive resistance of the compacted granular fill at founda
tion level may be considered at 300 pounds per cubic foot
equivalent fluid pressure

W
Basement retaining walls in the parking structure should be
designed to resist lateral active earth pressures Laterally
restrained walls that will be backfilled after being tied
to the structure should be designed to resist an equivalent
fluid weight of 55 pounds per cubic foot Yielding walls
or those backfilled prior to connection to the structure
should be designed to resist fluid densities of 35 pounds per
cubic foot
The backfill immediately against the wall should be an open
graded compacted granular material An adequate drainage
system should be provided behind the wall to prevent buildup
of excessive pore pressure
PAVEMENT
Pavements in parking and other areas should be placed on well
compacted granular fill similar to the surface soils existing
at the site Compaction should be a minimum of 90 percent of
and extend a minimum of 12 inches below finished subgrade
elevation

INVESTIGATION LIMITATIONS
The materials encountered on the project site and utilized in
our laboratory investigation are believed representative of
the total area however soil materials may vary in character
istics between boring locations Should other soils conditions
be encountered during construction a foundation engineer
should be consulted and appropriate construction modification
implemented if necessary Due to the possible variations
that may be encountered in the existing fill we recommend
that all foundation excavations be inspected and approved by
a soils engineer prior to placement of steel or concrete to
assure that the subsurface conditions encountered during
construction are of comparable competency as those encountered
during this investigation and upon which the recommendations
presented in this report are based If loose or debris
materials are encountered they may have to be replaced and
compacted with competent material It also recommended that
several elevation marks be established for the building columns
at grade and that settlement be monitored and recorded during

APPENDIX
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface soil conditions at the site were supplemented
by drilling three test borings utilized truck mounted rotary
equipment The depths of the borings ranged from fifty to
sixty one feet below the existing ground surface The boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 The drilling
operations were continuously supervised by our field engineer
who identified and classified the materials encountered in the
borings The classifications were later verified by inspec
tion and testing in the laboratory All soils were classified
in accordance with the Unified Soil Classification System and
rock with local geologic terminology Graphical representations
of the encountered materials are presented on the boring logs
Plates A 1A to A 3B Undisturbed soil samples were obtained by
driving a 2 4 I D sampler with 140 pound hammer free falling
a distance of 30 inches The blow counts required to drive
the sampler the final 12 inches are presented on the boring
logs at the appropriate sample depths

Appendix
LABORATORY TESTING
Attempts were made to determine the in place strength charac
teristics of the man made fill materials However due to the
high content of rock fragments it was difficult to obtain
adequate undisturbed samples for strength testings Correla
tions of strength with blow counts were made with data of
similar materials obtained from adjacent site investigations
by Geolabs Hawaii
Three consolidation tests were performed on the soft compress
ible cohesive soils Time rate measurements were taken on two
of the samples to aid in determining the duration of consoli
dation The results of these tests are presented on Plates
B 1 B 2 and B 3
To aid in the classification of the site materials six grain
size analyses were made of these samples The results of these
tests are shown on Plates C 1 and C 2 Grain Size Analysis
In place moisture and density determinations were made on
undisturbed samples where possible Only moisture determina
tions were made on disturbed samples The results of these
are presented on the enclosed boring logs Atterberg limits
were made on the fine grained materials to aid in the classi
fication and to determine other physical characteristics of
the site materials The results of these tests are presented
