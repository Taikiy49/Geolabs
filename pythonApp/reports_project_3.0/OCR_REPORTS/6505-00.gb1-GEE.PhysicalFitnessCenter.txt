Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the FY11 MCON P 006 Physical Fitness Center project within Camp
Smith in the Halawa Heights area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade retaining structures earthwork and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau Both the Waianae and Koolau
ranges have lost most of their original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges
shaped largely by erosion
The site is on a ridge in the upper portion of the Halawa Valley which is believed
to be a part of the old Koolau Range volcanic series The original Halawa Valley was a
youthful valley cut into the Koolau Dome during a lower stand of the sea Several
subsequent stages of alluvial depositional processes have filled the valley floor with
fluvial deposits The surface soil at the site is reddish brown clayey silt which originated
from the in place weathering of the underlying parent basalt rock on this ridge

Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the International Building Code 2009 Edition IBC 2009 the project
site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to
be addressed The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and soil
profile type for seismic design
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The
Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5
M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration generally encountered clayey soils below the existing
pavement surface Based on the laboratory test results the clayey soils exhibit a
moderate swelling and shrinking potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
However the laboratory test results also indicate that the moisture content of the in situ
clayey soils are above the optimum moisture content Therefore if the clayey soils are
not allowed to dry significantly the swelling potential of the clayey soils will be reduced
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils we recommend capping the
slab subgrade area with at least 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill
materials The non expansive select granular fill materials should be compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive select granular fill
materials should be placed as soon as possible after the subgrade soils are prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented herein The non expansive select
granular fill materials and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept
moist until the final placement of concrete It should be noted that the non expansive
select granular material does not need to extend below the building foundations
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend thoroughly moistening the soils to close the cracks or scarifying the soils to
a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioning to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill
For interior building slabs we recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer
of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the
slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To

Retaining Structures
We anticipate retaining structures such as retaining walls for grade separation
and elevator pit walls will be required for the project construction In general retaining
structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent
soil and surcharge effects The following guidelines may be used for design of the
retaining structures
Wall Foundations
In general we believe the retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the
Foundations section herein In addition retaining wall footings should have a
minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent grade
For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a
minimum 5 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of
the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of
the slope should be constructed in stepped footings

alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining structure
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on site
materials or free draining compacted fills where specified by the designer Unless
covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should also consist of
low expansion relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive
water infiltration behind the retaining structures

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 105 extending to a depth of about
16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown

drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 5

general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD

determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Ten one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples of the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of
the subsurface soils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Nine Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 8 through B 12
Two Expansion Index of Soil tests were performed on the bulk samples of the
near surface soils in general accordance with ASTM D 4829 The test is a standardized
test used to determine the expansive characteristics of the soil The Expansion Index of
Soil test is the same test UBC Standard 18 2 described in the Uniform Building Code
UBC to determine the expansive characteristics of soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 13
