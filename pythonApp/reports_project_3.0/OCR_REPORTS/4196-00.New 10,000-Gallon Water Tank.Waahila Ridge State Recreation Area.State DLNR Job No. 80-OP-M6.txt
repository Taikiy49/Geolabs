INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed 10 000 gallon water tank within the existing Waahila Ridge State Recreation
Area in the St Louis Heights area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed new water tank project These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations and site preparation only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Waahila Ridge State Recreation Area in
the St Louis Heights area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the
information provided the project will consist of the demolition and removal of the existing
10 000 gallon wooden water tank which measures approximately 12 feet in diameter 10 feet
in height and is supported on concrete piers We understand that the existing wooden water
tank will be replaced by a new 10 000 gallon water tank at the same location Based on the
information provided the new tank will be of steel construction measuring approximately
14 feet in diameter and will be supported on a concrete slab foundation In addition the
project will also include the abandonment and replacement of approximately 1 100 lineal feet
of the existing 2 inch waterline
Our previous experience in the area suggests that the project site may be underlain
by saprolite soils extending to great depths Special attention was given to the swelling
potential of the in situ soils and their effects on the foundation and slab on grade
construction for the proposed project The main objectives of our exploration program
included the following
provide design recommendations pertaining to the foundation of the proposed
water tank particularly the bearing pressure and footing embedment depth
provide estimates of foundation settlements

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava banking against its
eastern flank
The project site is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense
vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of the
Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has
formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite
is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks
The saprolite material grades with depth to basaltic rock formation

Water Tank Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally stiff subsoil conditions
encountered at the site we believe that shallow foundations may be used for support of the
proposed new water tank An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the design of the perimeter ring footing bearing on 6 inches of
compacted base course over the stiff clayey soils anticipated at the project site This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The base course below the footings should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction In addition the bottom of the footing excavations below the base course
layer should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively
firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of the base course material if soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the base course elevation they

should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
should be backfilled with on site soils up to the bottom of the base course elevation and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted base course
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for
foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground
the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility tench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that foundation settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils as recommended herein to be less than
1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of 0 5 inches
We recommend that foundation excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Slab On Grade
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a slight
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To reduce
the potential for future distress to the slab on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of
these clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we recommend that the subgrade
soils below the siab concrete be properly prepared and capped with at least 6 inches of base
course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The 6 inch compacted
base course would also serve to provide more uniform support for the tank slab
The 6 inches of compacted base course should extend a minimum of 12 inches
laterally beyond the limits of the tank structure The base course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design
of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per
inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted base course The area adjacent to
the tank slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with compacted on site soils and
graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around
the slab and foundation
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the current design concept we anticipate that
minimal site grading work will be required for construction of the new water tank At the
on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing
below areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted general fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site or used in
landscaping areas if appropriate
Slabs and foundations of the existing water tank to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with
W O 4196 00 Page 9
compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and
the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material
moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose
lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be
abandoned in place under the proposed tank structure should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
After clearing and demolition areas beneath future slabs should be over excavated
where necessary to provide the minimum 6 inch thick compacted base course layer below
the slab concrete Prior to placement of the base course layer the subgrade soils should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we recommend that the
soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted general
fill
The on site clayey soils generated from excavations may be re used as a source of
general fill material if needed General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types
of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be
allowed to compact the on site clayey and silty soils
Base course material should meet the material requirements for Aggregate for
Untreated Base Section 703 06 as specified in the Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Imported fill material if required
should consist of non expansive imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt
gravel or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no

particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested
in accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Base course and imported granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to
provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill
placement recommendations presented above Surface water should be diverted away from
the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the fill slopes are
completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction It is critical that the
1 inch minus granular fill materials be well compacted to reduce the potential for formation

be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete up to the bottom of the base course layer The
base course layer recommended below the tank floor should be placed above the concrete
to provide more uniform support for the tank slab The concrete around the utility lines below
the water tank structure should be placed neat against the sides of the excavation
Drainage
The finished grades outside the water tank structure should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided These drainage
requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above foundation
recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce

boring designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of approximately 16 5 feet below

testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil

depths

retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
