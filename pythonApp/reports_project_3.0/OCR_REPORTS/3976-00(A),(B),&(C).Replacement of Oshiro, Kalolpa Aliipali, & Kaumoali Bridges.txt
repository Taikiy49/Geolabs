The existing pavements at the three replacement bridge sites
were observed to be in relatively poor to fair condition with
distresses consisting of high severity raveling localized
potholes low to moderate fatigue severity cracking and
settlement of the pavement surface Due to the poor to fair
condition of the existing pavements we recommend that new
pavements be used for the transition sections from the existing
one lane roadway to the new wider bridge structure
Two alternative pavement sections incorporating asphalt
concrete as a wearing surface were considered for the bridge
replacement projects One alternative considered untreated
aggregate base course as the base material and the other
alternative considered asphalt treated base asphalt concrete
f base and aggregate subbase as the base and subbase
materials Selection of the preferred alternative was based on
preliminary analyses to evaluate the relative costs of the two
pavement alternatives
As a result of the preliminary cost analyses for a design life of
20 years the pavement section for the Oshiro Bridge project
La should include 2 5 inches of asphalt concrete over 7 inches of
aggregate base course Pavement sections for both the
Kalopa Aliipali and Kaumoali Bridges should include 2 5 inches
Ls of asphalt concrete over 10 inches of base course

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering analyses for design
of new pavements for the Replacement of and Kaumoali Bridge
projects Oshiro Bridge is located in the District of Puna on the Island of Hawaii and the
Kalopa Aliipali and Kaumoali Bridges are located in the District of Hamakua also on the
Island of Hawaii The general locations of the project areas are shown on the Project
Location Maps Plates 1 1 and 1 2
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the design of pavements The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided we understand that the one lane Oshiro
Kalopa Aliipali and Kaumoali Bridges will each be replaced with a new wider bridge
structure As a result new pavements will be required at each side of the bridge as the
existing roadway width transitions from the narrower roadway to the wider roadway at each
of the new abutments The required transition lengths vary from as little as about 35 feet
at the Kalopa Aliipali Bridge to a maximum of about 75 feet at the Oshiro Bridge The
pavements will be designed for a life of 20 years
Since the existing roadways are currently paved with asphalt concrete and the
volume of traffic is generally low recommendations for new pavements will be limited to
pavement sections with an asphalt concrete riding surface

The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during

EXISTING SITE AND PAVEMENT CONDITIONS
The existing roadways at each of the bridge site are paved with asphalt concrete AC
The overall conditions of the existing pavements were generally poor to fair with some areas
exhibiting high severity raveling of the AC surface Localized potholes areas of low to
moderate severity fatigue cracking and settlements of the pavements were also observed
At the time of our site visit traffic was very light and consisted primarily of passenger
vehicles and occasional school buses

Site Grading
The existing AC underlying base subbase materials and other unsuitable materials
should be completely removed and properly disposed of off site The exposed subgrade
materials in areas designated to receive fill prior to construction of the new pavements and
in cut areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches where possible
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 or ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density If pumping conditions are encountered during compaction of the
subgrade soils the subgrade soils may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the
potential for pumping subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for
50 square feet of subgrade area
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general fill
and backfill materials provided that they are free of organic materials and contain no lumps
or rock fragments greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The on site materials should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction The aggregate subbase and base course materials should consist of basalt
aggregates and should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Imported material should consist of select granular fill such as crushed mudrock
basalt or cinder sand Select granular fill materials should be well graded from coarse to fine
with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension Select granular fill should have
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more with a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 AASHTO T 193 In addition
select granular fill material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve Imported materials should be brought to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Surfaces should be finished
to create smooth unyielding subgrades and should be kept moist until covered by concrete

Pavement Surface Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the
pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed
on the site during or after construction Development of good shoulder drainage along with
a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly recommended to
reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of the pavements

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that Geolabs Hawaii be retained to provide geotechnical
engineering services during construction of the project The items of construction monitoring
that are critical include observation of subgrade preparation and placement and compaction
testing of the fill and base materials This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to expedite suggestions for design changes that may
be required in the event that subsurface conditions differ from those considered in this report
The recommendations provided in this report are contingent upon such observations If the
actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are different from
those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the design
should be made

A total of three California Bearing Ratio Tests were performed on bulk samples of the
near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils Each bulk
sample was remolded near the optimum moisture content of the soil Compaction of the
samples was performed by dropping a 10 pound hammer from a height of 18 inches The
samples were compacted in five equal layers with each layer receiving 10 blows of energy
Each sample was soaked in water prior to the penetration test The results of the tests are
U presented on Plates C 1 through C 3
