SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is in the Kalihi Valley area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii Kupehau Park is a City owned park at the end of Piliwai Street in Kalihi Valley
There are two existing culverts under Likelike Highway discharging into Kalihi Stream
through the park The culverts are located near Stations 72 50 and 74 50 Outbound
Lane of Likelike Highway Currently the discharge water has eroded two deep ditches
along the drainage path before exiting into Kalihi Stream The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
In general the Kupehau Park ground slopes down about four horizontal to

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu The higher sea level stands caused the
accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays
accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of
lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower
sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits In
addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited
terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium

In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When
the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
The project site is along the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano adjacent to Kalihi
Stream The subsurface conditions at the project site are composed of terrigenous alluvial
soils from Kalihi Stream deposited by surface water erosion of the Koolau volcanic dome
Based on our experience at this area the base of the stratigraphic section in this area is
Koolau Basalt
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of approximately 16 and 8 feet
below the existing ground surface respectively The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on the Log Legend Plate 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
In general our field exploration encountered stiff to very stiff alluvium deposit
containing clay with traces of sand and gravel The alluvium deposit extends to the
maximum depth explored Boring No 2 was terminated at 8 feet due to possible
encountering of a boulder Basalt rock formation was not encountered during drilling
Groundwater was not encountered at the time of our field exploration It should be noted
that groundwater levels may vary due to tides seasonal precipitation and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by stiff to
very stiff alluvium deposit with embedded boulders We understand new concrete culverts
will be constructed to connect the existing culvert to the Kalihi Stream We also understand
that intake box structures will be connected to the existing and new culverts A detailed
discussion and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in
the following sections of this report
Concrete Pipe Culvert
Based on the current design concepts we understand that two concrete pipe
culverts will be constructed to mitigate the erosion problem caused by the discharging
water We anticipate that a 48 inch and a 42 inch culvert will be used to connect the
existing southern and northern culverts respectively Both culverts will be approximately
150 to 190 feet long A headwall will also be constructed at the end of the culvert The
concrete pipe drain and headwall structures should be designed to resist lateral and
vertical earth pressures The following items are addressed in the succeeding
subsections

Foundations
Stiff to very stiff alluvium deposits are anticipated at the bottom of the new culvert
structures Based on the current condition of the existing ditch eroded by the
discharging water a stabilization layer consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel shall provide adequate support to the new culverts in this area An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used on the stabilization layer
The in situ soils below the 6 inch stabilization layer should be scarified to a depth of
about 12 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Soft and or loose
materials encountered should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
material The over excavation should be backfilled with imported select granular fill

Lateral Earth Pressure
The concrete box drain and headwall structures should be designed to resist the
lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects In general
an active condition may be used for gravity retaining structures that are free to
deflect by 0 5 percent of the structure height If the tops of the structures are not
free to deflect beyond this degree or are restrained the retaining structures should
be designed for the at rest condition These lateral earth pressures do not include
hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the
structures

Excavation Support
Based on our field observation the current ditches eroded by discharging water

Backfill
The backfill around the culvert structures should consist of structural fill Imported
structural fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed
coral or basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain less
than 15 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less
than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 The backfill
should be compacted to 90 to 95 relative compaction

Backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Compaction should be accomplished by suitable compaction
equipment i e sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel
pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed when compacting the
backfill
Because compaction of fills are critical elements of earthwork Geolabs should
conduct observations and soil density tests during backfill to assist the contractor
in obtaining the required degree of compaction Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of
moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction
Drainage
The headwall structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system should consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67
gradation placed near the bottom and along the length of the wall discharging to
an appropriate outlet or weepholes The weepholes should be spaced no more
than 8 feet apart In addition the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind
headwalls
Intake Box Structure
We understand that an intake box structure will be used to direct all the
discharging water from the existing culverts into the new culverts The detail design of
the intake box is not available at the time of the completion of this report However the
intake box structure should be designed to resist lateral and vertical earth pressure as
specified in the Concrete Pipe Culvert subsection
