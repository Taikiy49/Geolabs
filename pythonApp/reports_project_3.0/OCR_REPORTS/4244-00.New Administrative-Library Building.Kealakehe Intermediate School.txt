INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed New Administrative Library Building project at Kealakehe Intermediate
School in the Honokahau area of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Kealakehe Intermediate School in the
Honokahau area of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new
administrative library building as part of the development plan of the Kealakehe Intermediate
School
Based on the information provided the new administrative library building will be
located to the southwest of the existing kitchen dining facility and to the south of the existing
parking lot The new building will be a two story structure with CMU wall and slab on grade
construction The second floor will be of CMU wall construction with metal studs and precast
concrete plank flooring Roofing for the building will consist of steel framing Detailed
information pertaining to the structural loads of the new building were not available at the
time this report was prepared Based on our experience with similar projects maximum
column loads are estimated to be on the order of about 150 kips per column with wall loads
of up to about 10 kips per lineal foot
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finished floor elevation of
682 feet Mean Sea Level MSL for the new administrative library building cuts of about
5 feet and fills of up to about 2 feet thick are anticipated for the project grading work Our
previous experience in the area suggest that the project site may be underlain by a surficial

layer of fill soils over basalt formation at shallow depths Special attention was given to the
potential presence of near surface voids cavities in the basalt rock formation which may
collapse under structural loads

foundations site preparation and pavements for the proposed construction Our work was
performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated March 8 1999 The scope of
work included the following tasks and work efforts

project site and its vicinity

exploration

engineer from our firm

recommendations pertinent to the proposed building project

findings and recommendations pertinent to the design of foundations site

7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by a project engineer from our
firm

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which spread
and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like
or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten
state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as
blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains
from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
distance longitudinally along the fiow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal
extent

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to a depth of approximately 20 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a surface layer of silty sands and sandy
gravel of approximately 1 foot thick The surficial layer was underlain by hard basalt rock
formation interbedded with some thin clinker layers extending to the maximum depth drilled
of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as
seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
For detailed description of the materials encountered please refer to the Logs of
Borings presented on Plates A 1 and A 2 of Appendix A
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a very thin surficial layer of granular soils
overlying hard basalt rock formation at shallow depths Based on the relatively competent
subsoil conditions encountered in our borings and the anticipated structural loads it is our
opinion that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed administrative library building Based on the existing topography and proposed
finished floor elevation for the project we anticipate that the new administrative library
building may straddle across cuts of about 5 feet and fills of up to about 2 feet thick In order
to provide more uniform support for the foundation and to reduce the potential for foundation
distress resulting from differing subgrade conditions we recommend that the footings be
extended through the surficial granular materials and bear on the underlying basalt rock
formation for support of the proposed building For more uniform support of slabs on grade

we recommend that concrete slabs be supported on at least 12 inches of granular fill

Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic lava

Building Foundations
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed building Based
on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we anticipate that the new
building may straddle across cut and fill areas In order to provide more uniform support for
the foundation and to reduce the potential for foundation distress resulting from differing
subgrade conditions we recommend that the footings be extended through the surficial
granular materials and bear on the underlying basalt rock formation for support of the
proposed building
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 5 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation encountered
at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade Depending on the depths of the basalt rock formation deeper excavations
may be required at localized areas to provide a competent bearing surface for the footing

Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present
in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss
of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a footing we recommend
that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented during construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4244 00 Page 8
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 feet on center along wall foundations The probe holes should be
at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet below the planned bottom
of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probeholes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered
should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We
recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to
9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting operations
are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between probing grouting operations
and foundation construction might be required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and or voids and to allow for additional recommendations to be made if excess grout
take and or changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the proposed building may straddle cut and fill transitions To
reduce the potential for differential settlements at the cut and fill transition due to differing
subgrade materials we recommend that concrete slabs be supported on at least 12 inches
of granular fill material The intent of the 12 inch granular fill layer is to reduce the potential
for slab cracking due to differential settlements at the cut and fill transition Prior to
placement of the 12 inch granular fill the subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report
In addition to the 12 inches of granular fill a basaltic termite barrier BTB should be
incorporated into the slab on grade design of the proposed building in accordance with the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4244 00 Page 9
requirements of D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61 dated Therefore
for the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of BTB be placed below the slabs to
serve as a termite barrier and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of
the BTB cushion layer Two inches of BTB may be considered above the plastic membrane
to provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of BTB be provided below the
concrete walkway slab Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of
the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
relatively impervious soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the foundation

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide zone of permeable material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around a
perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to an
appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of select granular fill
material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of low expansion relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for
water infiltration behind the walls
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated finished floor elevation of the
proposed building we anticipate that site grading work consisting of cuts of about 5 feet and
minor fills of up to about 2 feet thick will be required for the project construction At the on set
of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site The surficial soils should be removed and may be stockpiled
for use in landscaped areas if appropriate Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with
compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and
the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material placed
in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities

to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping
lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition the areas within the proposed building limits
should be over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick capping
layer of granular fill material Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly
encountered in basaltic lava flows we recommend that the over excavated subgrades be
proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of 6 passes prior to
fill placement to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities Loose areas or
cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations should be excavated to expose
firm materials and backfilled with compacted select fill Therefore contract documents
should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities The
proof rolling operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to
evaluate the presence of voids and or cavities below the subgrade level
The on site excavated basalt rock materials may be used as fill or backfill materials
provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively well graded select granular material
with a maximum size of about 6 inches The 12 inch granular capping fill recommended
under the building slabs should consist of imported materials such as select borrow crushed
coral or basalt gravel The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no

California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain
between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Imported material should be
observed and or tested by Geolabs Hawaii for its suitability prior to being transported to the
site
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry

Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed building site is underlain at shallow
depths by volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard It is anticipated that the
volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping
with large bulldozers where the rock is near the existing ground surface However
excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams chipping or
blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on
our visual observation of the existing rock formations and field data from the borings drilled
at the site We recommend that all contractors proposing to bid on this project be
encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own
interpretation

Drainage

foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems

drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to a depth of about 20 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling
is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
