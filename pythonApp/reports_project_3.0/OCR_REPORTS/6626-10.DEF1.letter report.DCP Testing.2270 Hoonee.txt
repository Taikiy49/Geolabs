Dear Mr. Price: 
This letter report presents the results of our dynamic cone penetrometer (DCP) 
testing for the warehouse floor subgrade for the Graybar warehouse project at 
2270 Hoonee Place in the Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings from our field testing and engineering analyses 
for the proposed project.  The findings presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is an existing warehouse that was previously occupied by 
Longs Drugs/CVS. The existing floor in the main portion of the warehouse consisted of a 
combination of asphaltic concrete pavement and Portland cement concrete.  It appears 
that the original floor was paved with asphaltic concrete and Portland cement concrete 
was used to repair damaged areas. 
Previously, we conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration for this project.  
Based on the information provided for our exploration, the new intended occupant, 
Graybar, Inc., wished to evaluate the existing floor for the support of high stacking loads.  
Therefore, our exploration focused on the thickness and condition of the existing 
pavements and the underlying subgrade materials.

In general, our field exploration disclosed that the floor pavement in the existing 
warehouse consisted of 2 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by about 9 inches of 
basaltic base course.  Variations in the pavement section were observed at Boring No. 3 
(1.75 inches asphaltic concrete over 6.25 inches Base Course), Boring No. 5 (3 inches 
asphalt over 5 inches Base Course), and Boring No. 6 (4 inches Portland cement concrete 
over 2 inches asphalt over 5.5 inches of coralline base).  Boring No. 3 was terminated at a 
depth of 2.5 feet due to concerns about possible underground utilities at that location. 
Since the slabs would be subjected to equipment vibration and/or vehicular traffic, 
we recommended placing the structural floor slab over 6 inches of aggregate base course. 
For the design of structural slabs supported on the aggregate base course, a modulus of 
subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) was 
recommended.  On the basis of these recommendations, a final design of a 7-inch thick 
slab was developed for the new warehouse floor.   
It was requested that, since the existing pavement was underlain by granular 
material, would it be possible to leave this material in-place rather than over-excavating 
and importing base course material.  We agreed to test the slab subgrade using the DCP 
test method (ASTM D 6951) to estimate the CBR value of the subgrade soils and correlate 
the CBR to the subgrade modulus. 
The DCP test method is used to assess in-situ strength of undisturbed soil and 
compacted materials (or both). The penetration rate of the 8-kg [17.6-lb] DCP can be used 
to estimate in-situ CBR (California Bearing Ratio), to identify strata thickness, shear 
strength of strata, and other material characteristics. 
The 8-kg [17.6-lb] DCP is held vertically, and therefore, is typically used in 
horizontal construction applications, such as pavements and floor slabs.  Empirical 
correlation of the test indicates that a CBR value on the order of 20 to 25 is generally 
equivalent to a subgrade modulus of about 250 pci. 
This instrument is typically used to assess material properties down to a depth of 
1000 mm [39 in.] below the surface.  The 8-kg [17.6-lb] DCP can be used to estimate the 
strength characteristics of fine and coarse-grained soils, granular construction materials 
and weak stabilized or modified materials. The 8-kg [17.6-lb] DCP cannot be used in 
highly stabilized or cemented materials or for granular materials containing a large 
percentage of aggregates greater than 50 mm [2 in.]. 
FIELD DCP TESTING 
On July 16 and July 27, 2012, we conducted DCP tests on the prepared slab 
subgrade within the existing warehouse at 2270 Hoonee Place.  On July 16, a total of 
23 tests were conducted.  These tests were labeled as 1 through 23 inclusive.  Test 
Nos., 7, 8, 11, 12, 15, 16, 18, 19, 21 and 22 encountered areas where the subgrade 
contained excessive moisture and needed to be reworked.  All other locations (Test Nos.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings submitted herein are based in part upon information obtained from the 
field testing.  Variations of conditions beyond the test locations may occur. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Abbott Construction and 
their client, Graybar, Inc., for specific application to the 2270 Hoonee Place project in

No warranty is expressed or implied. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered.  Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft deposits, 
hard layers or cavities, may occur in localized areas and may require additional probing or 
corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to attain a properly 
constructed project.  Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is recommended to 
accommodate these possible extra costs.
