Project Considerations 
The project site is located along the mauka bank of the Ala Wai Canal in the 
Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  We understand that the project 
includes maintaining and preventing the existing canal wall located on the mauka side 
of the canal, west of the McCully Street Bridge, from further distress.  The existing canal 
wall is leaning towards the canal.  
The distressed wall section in this area is about 500 linear feet in length and 
extends from McCully Street Bridge to the Makiki Stream.  The canal wall repair area is 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Based on the as-built drawings, the existing canal wall is composed of cement, 
rubble, and masonry (CRM).  In the year 1999, a 2-foot wide concrete cap was 
constructed on top of the wall. It appears that the existing canal wall is about 9 feet in 
height within the repair area. 
An existing 72-inch sewer tunnel traverses the wall alignment.  The invert 
elevation of the sewer tunnel is about -37 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The top of the 
sewer tunnel is estimated to be about 36 feet below the existing ground elevation of 
about +5 feet MSL. 
An existing walkway is located beyond the canal wall.  In addition, a structure for 
a bus stop waiting area is located adjacent to the McCully Street Bridge. 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the canal wall repair area by drilling and 
sampling one boring, designated as Boring No. 201, extending to a depth of about 
52.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice 
for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), 
as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate 4.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in 
accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, 
Plate 4.2. Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description 
System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate 4.3. 
The boring drilled at the project site generally encountered a surface fill layer about 
6 feet thick underlain by very loose to medium dense lagoonal deposits to about 24 feet 
deep.  The lagoonal deposits were underlain by coralline detritus, and coral and limestone 
formation extending to a depth of about 52.5 feet below the ground surface, the maximum 
depth explored.

The surface fill layer consisted of medium stiff to stiff sandy silt and silty clay and 
loose silty gravel.  The lagoonal deposits were composed of very loose to medium dense 
sandy/silty gravel with some sand.  The coralline deposits consisted of medium dense to 
very dense silty gravel with sand and stiff sandy silt with gravel.  The coral and limestone 
formation were medium hard. 
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled boring at a depth of about 3.5 feet 
below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The groundwater level 
encountered generally correspond to about Elevation +1.0 feet MSL. It should be noted 
that groundwater levels may vary with tidal fluctuations and storm surge conditions (due to 
the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean and Ala Wai Canal), seasonal rainfall, 
time of year, and other factors.

coral samples are presented on Plates 6 through 9.  Photographs of the core samples are 
presented on Plate 10. 
Existing Wall Remedial Measures 
Based on our boring, a thick layer of highly compressible lagoonal deposits was 
encountered. Previously, the installation of a deep foundation system consisting of 
micropiles was recommended to support the canal wall.  Due to the presence of the large 
underground sewer tunnel and the wide easement, the installation of micropiles would not 
be allowed within the easement and would not be feasible to support the canal wall. 
Since the remedial measures for the existing wall need to cross over the sewer 
easement, a remedial measure that reduced the depth of disturbance within the sewer 
easement was considered.  The remedial measure that was developed consisted of 
utilizing light-weight concrete and anchors to restrain the existing canal wall. 
The existing material behind the canal wall would be excavated down to about 1.5 
feet above the bottom of the existing wall and back to about 5 feet.  The backslope of the 
excavation should have an inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. A 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equal) should be placed at the bottom of the excavation below 
water to reduce the potential of the backfill material moving into the underlying subgrade 
soils. 
The excavation should be backfilled with light-weight concrete with a unit weight of 
about 90 pounds per cubic foot (pcf).  The unit weight of the excavated material is greater 
than the unit weight of the light-weight concrete.  Therefore, additional loading to the 
underlying soft materials should not occur. Since the excavation will be underwater, the 
light-weight concrete should be placed by tremie methods.  Corrosion resistant anchors, 
such as glass fiber reinforced polymer (GFRP) anchors, embedded within the concrete 
backfill should be used to permanently support the existing wall. The existing canal wall 
should be temporarily supported during the remedial work.  The excavation should be

Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work will generally consist of cuts and fills on the 
order of about 7 feet or less in thickness relative to the existing ground surface for the 
remedial measures for the canal wall. 
Based on the boring, fill and lagoonal deposits are anticipated near the bottom of 
the existing wall footing.  To provide separation between the subgrade soils and the 
concrete backfill, we recommend that a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equal) be 
provided below the concrete backfill in areas where the subgrade is below the water. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading, excavation, and backfilling 
operations to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the 
excavation process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those 
encountered in our field exploration.  
Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field boring. Variations of subsurface conditions beyond 
the field boring may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, 
Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein. 
The field boring location indicated herein is approximate, having been estimated 
by taping from visible features shown on the Topographic Survey Plan transmitted by 
R.M. Towill Corporation on March 10, 2015. Elevation of the boring was interpolated 
from the spot elevations shown on the same plan. The field boring location and 
elevation should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods 
used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the boring 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the boring was measured at the time shown on 
the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors.

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer about 12 inches thick 
over highly compressible estuarine and lagoonal deposits extending to a depth of about 
24 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the highly compressible estuarine and 
lagoonal deposits, a 15-foot thick sandstone formation over an alluvial deposit was 
encountered extending to about 42.5 feet deep, the maximum depth explored.  The 
surface fill layer consisted of 2-inch asphaltic concrete and very dense silty gravel with a 
little sand.  The estuarine and lagoonal deposits were composed of very loose to loose 
silty sand, silty/sandy gravel with some sand, and gravel with little sand.  The sandstone 
formation was very soft and the alluvium consisted of stiff to very stiff sandy silt.  
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled boring at a depth of about 2.5 feet below the 
ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The groundwater level encountered 
generally correspond to about Elevation +1.5 feet MSL. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the boring drilled and other 
available boring information at the project site, we believe the new retaining wall structure 
may be supported by 7-inch diameter micropiles with a total embedment depth of 43 feet.  
The micropiles should be spaced at 3 feet on-centers or greater.  The allowable 
compressive load capacity of a single micropile is about 40 kips with a minimum bonded 
length of 18 feet.  The micropiles would derive support primarily from skin friction between 
the sides of the micropile and underlying sandstone and stiff alluvium. 
Since the micropile capacities are highly dependent on the drilling procedures and 
grouting methods employed by the contractor to install the micropile, we recommend 
implementing a micropile load test program for the project.  We recommend performing at 
least one load test on a pre-production sacrificial micropile situated near the wall site.  The 
load test on the pre-production micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 
percent of the allowable design load.  In addition, we also recommend performing pullout 
tests (proof tests) on 10 percent of the total number of micropiles.  The pullout tests should 
consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 133 percent of the design load.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Ala Wai Canal Wall Repairs project in the Honolulu area on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering

engineering analyses for the project. These findings and geotechnical recommendations

findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at

A geotechnical engineering study was performed for the conceptual design 
phase of this project and was entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Study, Ala Wai Canal 
Wall Repairs – Conceptual Design, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii” dated March 18, 2015.  
Several remediation alternatives for the distressed canal wall were presented and 
included supporting the new canal wall with shallow foundations, deadman anchors, 
micropiles, and helical anchors.  The use of micropiles to support the new wall structure 
was the preferred alternative.

Project Considerations 
The project site is along the mauka bank of the Ala Wai Canal in the Waikiki area 
of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project involves the repair of a portion 
of the existing canal wall, about 700 linear feet in length, adjacent to the Ala Wai Field 
and between the Ala Wai Community Park and Manoa-Palolo Drainage Canal.  The 
limits of the project site are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The Ala Wai Canal was built between 1921 and 1928 to drain the swamps in the 
area and provide a drainage corridor for the waterways from central and east Oahu.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct 
shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger shield, Koolau Volcano, forms the 
majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is buried 
partially below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the 
Honolulu Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by 
fossil Pleistocene Age coral-algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high 
stands of the sea, which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the 
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to present sea level on Oahu. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in 
sea level occurred. During that rise, the valleys in the project area were drowned. In the 
last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and 
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal 
environments resulting in thick deposits of loose and/or soft sediments along the coast 
in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer about 12 inches 
thick over highly compressible estuarine and lagoonal deposits to about 24 feet deep.

3.1.3 Micropile Foundations 
The new wall footing will be supported by micropiles extending below the very 
loose and highly compressible estuarine and lagoonal deposits.  In general, the 
micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter (usually less than 
12 inches), drilled and grouted, pile with steel reinforcing.  The micropile 
foundation is typically constructed by drilling a hole (with or without casing), 
placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the hole.  Micropiles are 
desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions.  In addition, 
installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent 
structures, the adjacent soils, and the environment. 
The critical loading condition for retaining walls is due to lateral loading. These 
loads will introduce moment and shear forces at the base of the retaining wall 
and the tops of the micropiles. Lateral load resistance for micropile is a function 
of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the micropile, allowable

of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the micropile, allowable 
deflection at the top, and induced moment in the micropile. The uplift and 
compressive loads are resisted by skin friction of the uncased portion of micropile 
that is embedded in the competent layer below the lagoonal deposit.   
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend using a cased micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of at least 
7 inches to support the new retaining wall.  The micropiles should be spaced at 
3 feet on-centers or greater.  The permanent casing for the cased micropile 
system should have an inside diameter (ID) of at least 7 inches. The minimum 
thickness of the permanent casing should be about 0.375 inches.  A reinforcing 
steel bar consisting of a Grade 75 No. 11 all-thread reinforcing bar with a 
cross-sectional area of 1.56 square inches may be considered.  Since the 
micropiles are used in a permanent application, epoxy coated or stainless steel 
bars should be used.

3.1.4 Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the compressive load capacity of the micropiles is highly 
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, the compressive load capacity of 
the micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile 
foundation systems.  In order to determine whether the contractor’s methods of 
micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate compressive 
load capacity, we recommend performing at least one static load test on a 
pre-production sacrificial micropile near the new wall site.  In general, the 
purpose of the pre-production load test on a micropile is to fulfill the following 
objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depth required 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grout injection

To confirm or modify the estimated minimum depth of the 
micropiles by determining the ultimate grout-to-soil bond stress 
In general, the pre-production load test should be performed in accordance with 
ASTM D1143.  Based on our experience, we believe that the load test should be 
conducted no earlier than 7 days after completion of the micropile installation to 
allow the grout adequate time to cure.  Two to four additional micropiles may be 
used for reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre-production load 
test micropile.  The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the 
load test micropile to provide an adequate reaction in uplift (to be determined by 
the contractor). 
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the 
allowable design load.  In addition, we recommend holding the micropile for a 
minimum of 4 hours at 200 percent of the allowable design load depending on 
the recorded movements of the load test micropile.  Please note that the 
pre-production load test is an integral part of the design of the micropile 
foundation system. 
In addition to the pre-production load test, we also recommend performing pullout 
tests (proof tests) on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the load 
carrying capacity of the installed micropiles.  We recommend testing a minimum 
10 percent of the total number of micropiles for pullout.  The pullout tests should 
consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 133 percent of the design load, and 
the maximum test load should be held for at least 10 minutes.  Pullout tests on 
the micropiles also are integral parts of the micropile foundation system design.

Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the generally permeable nature 
of the sandy/gravelly soils to be encountered within the excavations, dewatering will be 
required at the planned excavations. Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be 
included in the contract documents for the proposed construction. Because the 
excavation will involve discharge of groundwater, a National Pollutant Discharge

should consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations and information

Because of the granular nature of the subsurface soils, we anticipate the granular 
soils encountered near the ground surface are relatively permeable. Therefore, 
dewatering of the excavation may involve large volumes of water. Because of the 
presence of loose and/or soft soils, we do not recommend dewatering by means of a 
well point system along the outside of the excavations. The resultant areal depression 
of the natural groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible 
subsurface soils resulting in potential ground settlements, which could affect the 
adjacent existing structures. The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on 
depressing the natural groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor 
prior to dewatering. 
Our opinion is that a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking steel sheet piles, 
should be considered to aid in dewatering the excavation. However, sumps will be 
needed to collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates 
through the sheet piles. The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce 
the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering 
needed within the excavations in areas underlain by granular subsurface soils with 
generally high permeability. Use of an interlocking sheet pile shoring support system 
with jet-grouted soils (or tremie concrete plug) is relatively watertight, which should 
allow the groundwater levels outside the excavations to be maintained close to the 
original pre-construction levels. Therefore, some type of groundwater control 
requirement should be specified in the contract documents.

The contractor is responsible for dewatering. The selection of equipment and

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular soils may be 
susceptible to piping and “quick” conditions. The dewatering operations should be 
carried-out without creating a “quick” condition or softening at the excavation 
bottoms. Therefore, the project dewatering operations should be performed without 
pumping out soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should be coordinated with 
the shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected. 
Excessive pumping, which removes soil fines, may result in “blowing” or heaving of 
the excavation bottom or sides. 
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement 
resulting from consolidation of the very loose to loose compressible soils. 
Therefore, the use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the 
groundwater level should not be allowed. 
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected 
to the excavations. If this occurs, the granular bedding and/or backfill in the utility 
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater 
drawdown. Significant areal groundwater drawdown would result in appreciable 
ground settlements and potential damage to utility lines and/or other adjacent 
existing structures.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 101, extending to a depth of about 42.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2488 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-1.1 and A-1.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Boring indicate the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-1. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected in-situ soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of 
the soil.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results and the 
stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-2. 
One Uniaxial Compressive Strength test (ASTM D7012, Method C) was 
performed on a selected sandstone core to evaluate the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sandstone formation encountered. Test results are presented on 
Plate B-3.
