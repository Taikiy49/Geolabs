In accordance with our fee proposal dated October 28, 2019, we have reviewed 
the 60% Design Submittal grading plans, foundation plans, and technical specifications 
for the Maintenance Building, Septage, and Site Improvements (MBSSI) design package 
of the Sand Island Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) Secondary Treatment project. 
The following sections contain our geotechnical considerations and recommendations for 
implementation on the design drawings for construction of the MBSSI design package of 
the Sand Island WWTP Secondary Treatment project. 
SITE RETAINING WALL 
Based on our review of the grading plans, new fills up to about 8 feet in thickness 
are planned at the proposed MBSSI Maintenance Building site to achieve the design 
finished floor elevation of +16 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). We understand a new concrete 
retaining wall, on the order of about 9.5 feet high, is planned along the west side of the 
parking lot at the new Maintenance Building site to retain the new fills placed at the site. 
As mentioned in our report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Report, Sand Island 
Wastewater Treatment Plant, Secondary Treatment – Phase 1, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii” 
dated June 28, 2019 prepared for the project, the MBSSI site generally is underlain by 
thin surface fills overlying soft and/or loose compressible deposits at shallow depths. The 
substantial new fills planned likely will induce consolidation of the underlying 
compressible lagoonal deposits, resulting in potential areal settlement in the vicinity of the 
new fills.  
Therefore, the new concrete retaining wall planned along the west side of the 
parking lot underlain by compressible deposits likely will settle when the new fills are 
placed against it to raise the building site. To reduce the potential for distress to the new 
concrete retaining wall resulting from the fill induced settlements, we recommend using a 
flexible wall system, such as a Mechanically Stabilized Earth (MSE) retaining wall, at this 
location to better accommodate the expected ground settlements. The alternative would 
be to fill the site first to allow the settlement to occur and then excavate down to construct 
the concrete retaining wall. Geotechnical recommendations for the design of the MSE 
retaining wall are contained in the afore-mentioned geotechnical engineering report.

SETTLEMENT MONITORING 
As mentioned above, the new fills planned will induce consolidation of the 
underlying compressible lagoonal deposits, resulting in areal settlement at the 
Maintenance Building site. Based on our evaluation, we estimate potential ground 
settlements on the order of about 3 to 6 inches could occur at the building site. In addition, 
we estimate the majority of the primary settlement would occur in about 6 to 8 weeks after 
the new fills are placed. Therefore, a settlement monitoring program should be 
implemented, and construction of the new structures and other improvements atop of and 
within the new fill should be delayed to allow for the majority of the settlement to occur 
under the new fill load. For the soils anticipated at the Maintenance Building site, the 
estimated settlement period could vary by as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual 
settlement waiting period required. Therefore, the actual settlement rate should be 
monitored, and provisions should be made for potential delays in the project construction 
schedule if a longer period is required. 
To monitor the actual settlement rates, we recommend installing a minimum of five 
settlement gauges at the fill areas planned for the Maintenance Building site. The 
approximate locations of the settlement gauges are shown on the attached Proposed 
Settlement Gauge Location Plan, Plate 1. The number of settlement gauges and their 
locations should be confirmed after the final grading plans are reviewed and evaluated 
further. In addition, their locations may need to be adjusted to avoid potential conflict with 
the installation of the ACIP pile foundations for the Maintenance Building.  
The settlement gauges should be read optically by a professional surveyor, and 
the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs for review in a timely manner. The 
settlement gauges should be established on a prepared ground surface (after removal of 
the existing soil stockpiles) prior to the filling operations. A baseline elevation of the 
gauges should be established by two surveyed readings taken a minimum of 24 hours 
apart. Subsequently, readings should be taken once a week until the settlement rate has 
stabilized for at least 2 to 4 weeks. 
UTILITY LINE INSTALLATION 
Based on the 60% Design Submittal for the MBSSI design package for the project, 
we understand that various underground utility lines, including drain lines, sewer lines, 
water lines, fire lines, and fire hydrants, are planned for the project. Based on the piping 
profiles provided, some and/or portions of these utility lines may be subjected to new fills 
up to about 8 feet thick. As mentioned above, the new fills planned likely will induce 
consolidation of the underlying compressible lagoonal deposits, resulting in potential areal 
settlement at the MBSSI site. If the utility lines were installed prior to the fill placement, 
there exists the potential for settlement of these utility lines under the new fill loads and 
resulting in sags in the gravity sewer lines. Therefore, special attention should be given 
to the construction sequencing of the fill placement and infrastructure installation.

ABANDONMENT OF EXISTING OUTFALL 
We understand that it is necessary to remove the abandoned pipe from below the 
future Maintenance Building as the alignment of the pipe is in line with the new building 
column foundations. Furthermore, the abandoned pipe likely is supported on driven piles 
because most of the existing pipes of 60-inch diameter or greater on the site appear to 
be supported on a concrete cradle that is pile supported. The existing pile locations 
supporting the abandoned pipe should be surveyed during the demolition of the pipes to 
avoid encountering it during the new ACIP pile installation. Based on our experience with 
similar situations, that has caused a lot of problems and damage to equipment that 
becomes a change order for the Owner.  
DEWATERING EVALUATION 
Based on our review of the 60% Design Submittal drawings, we do not envision 
significant deep excavation, such as basement level construction and other substantial 
below-grade structures for the MBSSI design package. We envision that the majority of 
the excavation will be for the installation of various underground utility lines, including the 
following: 
1. 
Drain lines “DL-A” thru “DL-D” 
2.

5. 
Fire Hydrants “FH-1” thru “FH-3”
