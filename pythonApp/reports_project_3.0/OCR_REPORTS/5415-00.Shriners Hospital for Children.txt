2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The Waianae and Koolau Volcanic Shields
were built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western third of
the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island The main shield building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million
years ago It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu After a long
period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the
Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder
and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project is located on the southeastern flank of Punchbowl Hill
Punchbowl Hill Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the
end of a spur of the Koolau Range The tuff consists mostly of brown palagonitized
vitric ash and lapilli with scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC current code adopted by the
City and County of Honolulu the Island of Oahu is located within Seismic Zone 2A with
an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 15g Therefore the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be
addressed for the project The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity
of the Island of Oahu the soil profile for seismic design and the potential for
liquefaction at the site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they could only be detected by
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of high seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii

Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
Saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not
confined can result in significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine
grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to
liquefaction Generally liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is
deeper than 40 to 50 feet below the ground surface The greater overburden
pressure can be sufficient to prevent liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is mostly underlain by stiff to hard
volcanic ash and medium dense to dense cinder deposits Loose pockets of silty
sand were encountered in the borings however these loose pockets were
encountered at depths between 40 and 60 feet below the existing ground surface
Additonally groundwater table was encountered at depths of about 34 5 to 41 feet
below the ground surface Therefore we believe that there is no potential for
liquefaction at this project site

The on site soils generated from the cut areas may be re used as a source of

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings extending to depths ranging from about 30 5 to 102 feet below the existing
ground surface The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were advanced to the desired depths using truck mounted
drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the surface fill soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The
test results are presented on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples of the upper clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The test were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 117 and C 136 Graphic
presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plate B 3
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear tests
performed are presented on Plates B 4 through B 6
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 with time rates of consolidation was
performed on a sample of the soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered The results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 7
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on selected samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the
near surface soils to determine the dry density and moisture content relationship Results
of the test are presented on Plate B 10
