INTRODUCTION
We have performed a geotechnical engineering exploration at the proposed site
for the Royal Vista Estates Golf Course project The exploration was performed in
accordance with our proposal dated This report presents our findings and
recommendations
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface soils
and rock conditions within the proposed golf course grading area in order to provide data
for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design
of the mass grading roadway and associated infrastructure installation This exploration
was limited to the golf course grading The findings and recommendations presented
herein may not nor should they be considered applicable to proposed clubhouse
structures to proposed maintenance facilities or subdivision development related to the
golf course
The scope of our exploration consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization of a refraction seismograph with operators to and from the
project site
2 Performance of fifteen 15 geophysical surveys seismic refraction in
various areas throughout the golf course
3 Excavation and logging of nineteen 19 shallow test pits within the
proposed golf course grading limits using a combination loader backhoe
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2695 00 Page 3
4 Detailed site reconnaissance coordination of the field work and logging of
the test pits by a Senior Geologist from our firm
5 Engineering analyses of the exploration data for the formulation of
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design and
construction of the proposed access roads golf course grading and
appurtenant utility installation
6 Preparation of this report presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project and design team
consultation by a Principal Engineer of our firm
8 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The Royal Vista Estates Golf Course project site is located at the Puu Anahulu in
the district of North Kona on the island of Hawaii The area is situated to the north of
Mamalahoa Highway approximately midway between Kailua Kona and Waimea and
encompasses 190 acres The site is bounded to the east west and north by open ranch
land and to the south by the North Kona Belt Road Mamalahoa Highway
The terrain is generally rolling with some steep slopes locally approaching a ratio
of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V The terrain generally reflects the surface of the
original trachyte lava flow originating from Puu Waawaa The ground surface elevations
range from about 2200 feet above mean sea level in the southeast to about 1900 feet
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2695 00 Page 4
above sea level in the northwestern quadrant with drainage trending towards the
northwest
The site is vegetated with a thick cover of grasses with occasional stands of tress
particularly upon the upper elevations The area is traversed by ranch fences and a few
jeep trails
There are localized exposures of cobbles boulders and volcanic rock formation
In the southwestern quadrant of the site the upper surface of a younger lava flow from
Mauna Loa is exposed
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the project site as a twenty seven hole golf course
Although not included in this exploration it is anticipated that the development will also
include other site improvements such as one or more clubhouses maintenance facilities
and residential subdivisions
Based upon the topography and the preliminary grading plans provided for our
exploration it is anticipated that the golf course grading will involve cuts and fills of up to
twenty 20 feet in depth
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is situated on the northeastern flank of Hualalai which is the third
oldest of the five 5 shield volcanoes forming the island of Hawaii Hualalai was built
during the Pleistocene and Holocene Epochs by basaltic lava flows from the Hualalai
Volcanic Series
GEOLABS HAWAITI
W O 2695 00 Page 5
Typically the lava flows of the Hualalai series are composed of alkalic olivine
basaltic lava flows grading to hawaiite These lava flows are frequently mantled by a layer
of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash
During the later stages of the active phase of Hualalai the Puu Waawaa vent
opened This event extruding lavas of trachytic composition built a large cinder cone
now known as Puu Waawaa On the southerly side of this vent a thick trachyte lava
broke through the cone The flow which is on the order of about 700 feet thick turned
to the south and formed the long ridge now known as Puu Anahulu Subsequent to the
Puu Waawaa eruption younger lava flows from Mauna Loa overran portions of this ridge
During our field exploration it was noted that there is a relatively thick surface layer
of ash soil mantling the site and that many of the exposed cobbles and boulders
appeared to be a pumice material It is believed that this surface ash the boulders and
cobbles are original airfall deposits from the Puu Waawaa eruption
The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of
the island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is somewhat lower and the soil
exists in a friable state as opposed to the plastic soil found in areas such as along the
Hamakua Coast

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that development of the
proposed golf course is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view
The volcanic formation encountered at the project site should provide adequate
bearing for the proposed site improvements provided that our recommendations are
followed The dry friable ash soil encountered at the surface throughout most of the site
generally has low strength characteristics and is susceptible to erosion Therefore some
modification to standard grading design and construction procedures will be required for
this golf course project
Whenever possible the ash soil should be stockpiled for use as topsoil rather than
used as embankment fill material Efforts should be made to construct the majority of the
embankment of the more competent excavated basalt and clinker materials if reduced
to less than 12 inches in size
The excavated ash soils may be utilized as fill provided that special consideration
is given to its placement
Site Preparation
Prior to commencement of grading the areas to be graded should be cleared of
all vegetation and deleterious materials Due to the relatively high potential for erosion
of exposed ash soils clearing and grubbing should be scheduled as closely as possible
ahead of the grading work i e leaving the vegetation in place in given areas until shortly
before the grading in that area is to commence The resulting grub and spoil material
should be disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2695 00 Page 8
Ash soils generally have relatively low strength and if the in situ moisture contents
are high enough they are thixotropic i e they lose strength when remolded Therefore
it is Our recommendation that the ash soil should not be used as fill in roadway and
structural areas The ash soil may be either stockpiled for use as topsoil or thoroughly
blended into rockfill material as discussed below
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary It may be advisable to institute a dust control program during
grading operations
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to firm natural material and the
resulting depression backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site and in its vicinity
indicate that the site is most likely underlain by ledges of basalt at relatively shallow
depths
It is anticipated that some hard excavation and or ripping may be encountered in
localized cemented areas or when ledges are encountered
Cemented clinker and ledge basalt formation will most likely require some hard
excavation such as heavy ripping hoerams or blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor Excavation by
ripping is dependent upon the jointing of the formation the configuration of the rippers
and the skill of the operator Blasting should be considered to expedite the excavation
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2695 00 Page 9
process Generally consideration should be given to the economics of blasting to initially
reduce the size of the excavated rock material and to facilitate excavation as opposed to
extended periods of heavy ripping at high equipment rates However care should be
exercised to avoid overshooting which could disrupt the clinker or rock formation resulting
in a loss of strength
Fill Placement
The dry friable ash soil encountered at the surface throughout most of the site
generally has low strength characteristics and is susceptible to erosion Therefore some
modification to standard grading design and construction procedures will be required for
this golf course project
Whenever possible the ash soil should be stockpiled for use as topsoil rather than
used as embankment fill material Efforts should be made to construct the majority of the
embankment of the more competent excavated basalt and clinker materials if reduced
to less than 12 inches in size
In general the excavated soil and rock materials less than 12 inches in size may
be considered to be suitable for use as fill material The material if reduced to less than
six 6 inches in size should be adequate as a source of trench and structural backfill
if needed Imported material if required should be free of boulders organic debris or
other deleterious materials and be non expansive
The excavated rock material may not contain sufficient fines to produce a
well graded material therefore the surficial ash soil could be mixed into the rocky
excavated material to increase the percentage of fines and to create a better graded fill
material However where possible it is preferable to utilize the ash material as topsoil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2695 00 Page 10
in the event it is necessary to utilize the ash soils as fill the ash should be moisture
conditioned to within a few percent of its optinum moisture content and placed in the
upper two feet below finish subgrade and compacted to a minimum of 90 of its
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Whenever
feasible the ash should be mixed as thoroughly as possible with granular materials The
ash soils should not be placed as fill for constructed slopes nor in roadway and structural
areas
Generally embankment fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density as
established by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should be placed in level
lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet of
fills and backfills below road and structure subgrade level should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 maximum dry density
Alternately large boulders or rock fragments could be utilized as rockfills or
boulder fills provided that they are not nested and that they are well tracked with a heavy
bulldozer to lock the rock fragments and bouldery materials into grain to grain contact
A choking layer at least eighteen 18 inches thick for yard areas and a minimum of
twenty four 24 inches thick for road or structural areas should be used to cap the
rockfill to reduce the potential of loss of fines from the upper portion of the fill The
choking layer should consist of a relatively well graded select granular material with an
average maximum size of about six 6 inches and an absolute maximum size of twelve
12 inches Care must be exercised to avoid placement of any boulders in locations
which would conflict with future utility line excavations In general boulder or rock
fragments larger than 24 inches in size should be placed at depths greater that four 4
feet below finish subgrade level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2695 00 Page 11
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to determine the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate
compaction
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one
vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the underlying consolidated clinker
or basalt formation to lessen potential instability of the new fill see Plate 3 The keys and
benches should not be based in the upper ash soil or in loose clinker Ash soils should
not be utilized as fills in constructed slopes
Slopes
Provided that the above specified fill placement procedures and granular materials
are utilized design fill slopes of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter are
recommended For fills constructed of ash soils a maximum design slope ratio of three
horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V is recommended Fill slopes should generally be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio
For cut slopes the following ratios are recommended
a Ash Soil 3 1 H V
c Dense Basalt 1 1 H V

Any cuts or fills greater than 20 feet in height should have a bench at the midpoint
of the slope
It is recommended that cut slopes exposing ash soils be immediately protected by
planting or other means to reduce the potential of erosion of the exposed soils
Lake Embankments
Based upon the preliminary grading plans it appears that embankments of up to
twenty feet in height are to be constructed along lake edges These embankments may
function as dams and as such should be constructed in conformance with the
Department of Land and Natural Resources Regulations pertaining to such structures
Lake embankment fills should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry
density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Ash soils should not be utilized
as fill in such structures
Subdrains through the lake embankments should be considered to minimize the
potential of the build up of excessive pore pressures in the embankment
Lake Liners
It is Our understanding that it is planned to construct one or more lakes as water
features for the golf course Based on the materials encountered in our field exploration
it is our opinion that impermeable membrane liners will be required to reduce the potential
of excessive seepage losses through the bottom of the lake
Generally some type of cushion would be required to reduce the potential of rocky
subgrade material puncturing the liner The manufacturer of the liner material selected
for the project should be consulted for the actual details of subgrade preparation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2695 00 Page 13
Subdrains should be installed below the liner to intercept potential leakage through
the liner The installation of subdrains would minimize the potential of accumulation of
water in the lake subgrade which could weaken the subgrade soils or result in excess
pore pressures in the lake embankment The subdrains could also serve as an early
warning system to give indications of possible liner failure
Infrastructure Installation
It is anticipated that underground utilities will be installed as part of the
development work Based on the results of our test pit excavations and seismic surveys
it is anticipated that relatively easy excavation will be encountered in the upper ash and
clinker soils for utility line trenching grading to more difficult as the underlying rock layers
are encountered
Trench backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding ten 10 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density per ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet of backfills below road or structural
subgrade level should be compacted to a minimum of 95 maximum dry density
Foundation Support
It is anticipated that some form of structures may be needed as part of the
development It is our opinion that relatively shallow spread or strip footing foundations
may be used to support the structures
An allowable bearing capacity of up to 4 000 p s f may be used for the design of
foundations bearing on in situ basalt rock formation or dense rockfill clinker in level areas
An allowable bearing capacity of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the design of
foundations located in sloping areas An allowable bearing capacity of up to 2 500 p s f
may be used for the design of foundations bearing on in situ ash soils or on fill consisting
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2695 00 Page 14
of ash soil These values may be increased by one third when temporary wind and
seismic loads are considered
In order to provide uniform support for the foundations and to minimize the
potential of foundation distresses where foundations for a structure are to span both cut
and fill areas or areas of differing soils conditions we recommend that footing excavations
in the cut portions be over excavated one 1 foot below subgrade level The
over excavated area should be backfilled with select borrow or well graded clinker placed
in eight 8 inch loose horizontal lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum
dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of two 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Foundations located on sloping areas should have a minimum
set back of eight 8 feet from the outside edge of the footing to the adjacent slope face
This may require additional embedment to achieve the proper set back distance
Lateral Pressures
Lateral loads on proposed structures may be resisted by a passive resistance of
up to 300 p s f per foot of depth of the buried vertical foundation faces A frictional
coefficient of 0 45 may be used to calculate resistance to sliding

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the performance of fifteen
15 seismic refraction surveys at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate
2 The seismic refraction surveys were conducted using a 12 channel signal
enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound sledge hammer as a seismic energy source
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves These
waves are refracted at interfaces between materials
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by
the seismograph The recorded data is downloaded to a portable computer either for
processing in the field or for storage and later processing
This technique permits mathematical calculations which will yield approximate
depths or thicknesses of materials below the survey and give their approximate
compressional wave velocities The calculated velocities may be empirically related to the
excavation characteristics of the materials The following legend explains the terms
shown on the Seismic Refraction Survey Results Plates A 1 through A 15
Velocity Compression wave velocity in feet per second
Dip Dip of the top of the layer in degrees
Ha Thickness of the layer under the forward shot point
Hb Thickness of the layer under the reverse shot point
Da Depth to the top of the layer under the forward shot point
Db Depth to the top of the layer under the reverse shot point
In addition to the geophysical surveys the subsurface conditions at the site were
explored by the excavation of nineteen 19 test pits to depths ranging between 0 5 and
7 5 feet below the existing ground surface The test pits were excavated using a Case
S80E rubber tired backhoe mobilized to the site

Laboratory Testing

samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Two 2 Atterberg Limit tests and eight 8 sieve analyses were performed on
selected samples to aid the classification of the subsoils The test results are summarized
on Plates B 1 through B 3
Four 4 determinations of the Maximum Dry Density were performed in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 78 and four 4 California Bearing Ratios were performed
to determine the engineering characteristics of the near surface soils The test results are
summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
