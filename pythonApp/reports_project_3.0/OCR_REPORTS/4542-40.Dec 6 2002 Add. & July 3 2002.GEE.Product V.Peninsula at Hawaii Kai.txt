The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the
range of about 25 000 to 50 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer should be
equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the loose soft soils may be

cause cracking of the piles

Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete
piles pile driving may be terminated when the aggregated blow counts are at least

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions within the project area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in
sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
With the urbanization of the area man made fills were placed to reclaim marshy
areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands
with coral fragments The lower clayey material was deposited during periodic influxes of

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures As previously
mentioned the load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend primarily on the
end bearing resistance of the hard volcanic tuff formation The bottom of the drilled
shaft hole will need to be clean and relatively free of loose materials in order for the
drilled shaft to derive support from end bearing Therefore we wish to emphasize
that the contractor should take appropriate measures to remove loose materials
from the drilled shaft hole prior to rebar cage installation and concreting
The drilled shaft holes will need to be extended a minimum 3 feet into the hard
volcanic tuff formation The hardness and fracturing in the volcanic tuff formation
are likely to vary significantly by nature Therefore some difficult drilling conditions
may be encountered in these subsurface conditions In addition due to the
presence of groundwater table caving in of the materials may occur during the
drilling operation due to seepage of groundwater and the granular nature of the
subsurface materials To reduce the potential for significant caving in of the holes
temporary casing of the drilled holes will likely be necessary
In addition the presence of groundwater in the drilled shaft holes will require that
the concrete for the drilled shafts be placed by tremie methods A low shrink
high slump concrete mix 7 to 9 inch slump in wet holes should be used to provide
close contact between the drilled shafts and surrounding materials The concrete
should be placed promptly after the completion of drilling within 24 hours to
reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes The concrete should also be

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general

accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice

method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One one inch ring swell test was performed on a remolded sample to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the test are summarized on
Plate B 1
Two unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
core samples of the volcanic tuff formation to evaluate the unconfined compressive
strength of the volcanic tuff formation encountered The results of the unconfined
compression tests are presented on Plate B 2
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics and swelling potential of the soils Results of the tests are presented on
Plates B 3 thru B 5
