REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice
Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield
reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago As volcanic
activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded eastern
slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast These alluvial sediments accumulated and
interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary
wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of Interstate Route H 1 to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the
present
The project site is located on the northern half of the Waipio Peninsula and is
composed of a composite of alluvial sediments interbedded with marine sediments and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3980 00 and 3980 10 Page 7
coral algal reef formations as described above and of man made fills Man made fills
were generally placed in marshy areas to raise the grade and extend the limits of useable
land These man made fills generally consist of sandy clay material obtained from
dredging or excavations from nearby areas overlying garbage bagasse and slurry from
sugar mills The lower marshy soils generally consist of highly organic muck or peat
material that accumulated from organic marine sediments that deposited near coastal
areas due to the low energy environment

Fills and Backfills
Other than the on site dark gray clayey soils the on site soil materials may be
used as a source of general fill material provided that they are free of vegetation
and deleterious materials and have a maximum particle size of 6 inches In
addition the silty coralline sand material may also be used as select granular fill
material
Import select granular fill should consist of non expansive select granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1883

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill and select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
between about 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts
not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
Select borrow subbase and base course materials should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of clayey soils are critical elements
of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the
required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift
Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be
applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the on site fills
and subgrades between 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture is an
important requirement considering the moderate expansive nature of the on site
clayey soils

Slopes

heights in excess of 10 feet may require geogrid reinforcement or staged filling to
resist slope instability caused by surcharge loads imposed on the soft subgrade
soil Currently fill heights not including fill heights in existing unlined swales are
planned to be less than 10 feet If grading is altered from the current grading plan
to include fill heights in excess of 10 feet Geolabs Hawaii should be notified for
review and comments
Also due to the presence of soft soils within the southern half of the project site
we believe that cut slopes within the southern half of the project site should be
constructed at an inclination of 3H 1V or flatter Due to the constant presence of
water detention basins with a slope inclination of 3H 1V should be designed with a
slope height of 5 feet or less to reduce the potential for slope instability If the
slope inclination is reduced to 4H 1V the slope height may be increased to

be increased to 19 feet Based on the current design where one half of the slope
height is at 5H 1V and the other one half of the slope height is at 4H 1V the slope
height should be limited to 16 feet
Within the northern half of the project site it appears that most of the cut slopes
would expose soils consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey materials and

coralline silty sand or coral rock should be constructed at an inclination of 1H 1V or

In general pole foundations will be subjected to torsional moments resulting in
torsion on shaft foundations Torsion may be resisted by the side shear along the
shaft surface and adjacent soils In our foundation analyses we have evaluated
the shafts subjected to the torsional moments computed using the lateral force

diameter shafts extending to the lengths recommended should be able io resist
torsional moments without significant movements We wish to emphasize that the
sides of the holes should be relatively free of loose materials and the concrete for
the shaft should have close contact to in situ soils after the shaft has been
constructed to provide the torsional resistance required

Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by soils

a
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Three consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on site soils The test
results and consolidation curves are presented on Plates B 1 thru B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate their shear strength characteristics The test results and shear stress versus
normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Thirteen 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
and remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge
loads These tests were run on air dried and non air dried samples Results of these
swell tests are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
Four Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 8
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil
classification Results of these tests are presented on Plate B 9
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a
near surface soil Results of the test are presented on Plate B 10
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test was performed on a bulk sample of the
near surface soil to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade support
Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 11

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
For the off site facilities our field exploration encompassed 3 discrete
locations These locations include the proposed Waikele Stream Off Site
Pump Station the proposed Kapakahi Stream Utility Bridge and the
proposed Ted Makalena Golf Course Irrigation Lake and Irrigation Pump
Station
Our field exploration for the proposed Waikele Stream Off Site Pump
Station generally encountered dry medium stiff orange brown clayey silt fill
material to a depth of 5 feet followed by very stiff orange brown sandy silt
alluvium to a depth of 13 feet Below the sandy silt very stiff
reddish brown clayey silt residual material was encountered to a depth of
24 feet followed by very stiff gray and red sandy silt saprolite to a depth of
30 5 feet Below the sandy silt moderately hard to very hard gray basalt
rock was encountered to the final depth of the boring at 33 feet
Groundwater was encountered at 24 5 feet below the ground surface at the
time of our field exploration
Our previous field explorations near the proposed Kapakahi Stream Utility
Bridge generally encountered soft to medium stiff brown clayey silt to a
depth of 5 to 10 feet followed by very soft dark gray clay with organic
material to a depth of 100 feet Below the very soft dark gray clay the soil
graded to medium stiff to stiff to a depth of 200 feet followed by hard brown
silty clay saprolite to the final depths of the borings Groundwater was
encountered at 2 5 to 4 feet below the ground surface at the time of our
previous field explorations
Our field exploration for the proposed Ted Makalena Golf Course Irrigation
Lake and Irrigation Pump Station generally encountered stiff brown clay
within the upper 3 5 feet of the existing irrigation lake slope Below the
upper 3 5 feet of the existing irrigation lake slope and at the bottom of the
existing irrigation lake very soft to soft gray clay was encountered to the
final depths of the borings Groundwater was not encountered at the time
of our field exploration

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice
Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield
GEOLABS HAWAII
om
W O 3980 10 Page 6
reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago As volcanic
activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded eastern
slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast These alluvial sediments accumulated and
interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary
wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of Interstate Route H 1 to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the
present
The Waikele Stream Pump Station is located north of the Waipio Peninsula and is
composed of a thin layer of man made fill material underlain by alluvium and followed by
residual soils The residual soil material may be considered part of the Schofield Plateau
The Kapakahi Stream Utility Bridge and the Ted Makalena Golf Course Irrigation
Lake are located on the northern half of the Waipio Peninsula and are composed of a
composite of alluvial sediments interbedded with marine sediments and corail algal reef
formations as described above and of man made fills Man made fills were generally
placed in marshy areas to raise the grade and extend the limits of useable land These
man made fills generally consisted of sandy clay material obtained from dredging or
excavations from nearby areas overlying garbage bagasse and slurry from sugar mills
The lower marshy soils generally consisted of highly organic muck or peat material that
accumulated from organic marine sediments that deposited near coastal areas due to the
low energy environment

Downdrag Loads
When regional artesian head is lowered or fill is placed over a compressible soil
the soil consolidates and moves downward relative to the pile The downward
movement results in negative skin friction forces generally known as downdrag
forces on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the
thickness of the compressible layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the
pile perimeter area Due to the on going consolidation of the soft compressible
soils the estimated settlements will result in downdrag loading of deep foundations
extending through the thick layer of recent alluvial deposit Therefore the
allowable compressive load capacities of the piles will need to be reduced by the
expected downdrag loads The downdrag load on the pile shaft is estimated to be
about 80 kips for the 12 inch square pile Battered piles are not recommended
because of the additional bending forces imposed on the piles due to ground
settlements

Excavation and Shoring
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The side slopes of
excavations must comply with applicable federal state and local requirements In
general the excavated soils should not be stockpiled within the zone of a 45 degree
imaginary line projected upward from the toe of the excavation to reduce the potential for
ground movement or subsidence toward the excavation
Where space is available an open cut excavation may be used to construct the
various structures A slope inclination of 1H 1V or flatter may be used for temporary cuts
however it should be noted that sloughing of the sides of the excavation at the soil cuts
may still be experienced during construction This should be further evaluated by a
qualified geotechnical engineer retained by the Contractor during construction
Where an open cut excavation is not possible the excavation for construction of
the structures will need to be adequately shored to reduce the potential for adjacent
ground movement The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with
applicable safety requirements One possible method of shoring would be to use a
soldier pile and lagging system The lateral earth pressures expressed in equivalent fluid

pressures presented in the Retaining Structures section of this report may be used for
design of a cantilever soldier pile and lagging system It is recommended that a qualified

The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the Contractor His representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction such as

It should be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in the earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to estimate accurately the

conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation

Dewatering of the excavation for the proposed drywells will be necessary since
the existing groundwater level is above the bottom of the proposed excavation
Therefore we recommend that dewatering provisions be included in the construction
contract documents Since the excavation will involvé discharge of groundwater a
National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary
The contractor should consult their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii
Department of Health for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit
application

Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the Contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the Contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation In addition dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear
water is pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing
or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
It should be noted that the subsurface materials in the zone where dewatering will
take place consist of clay soil materials which may be considered to be relatively
impermeable and of basalt rock formation which may be considered to be relatively
permeable It should be noted that dewatering excavations within basalt formations
adjacent to the Pacific Ocean may involve large quantities of water due to the permeable
conditions of the basalt rock formations It is our opinion that the definition of
Dewatering in the contract documents should be written to include all works or systems
required to lower the natural groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the
excavations to allow construction of the proposed structures under safe and dry
conditions These works or systems may include but are not limited to well pumping
grouting cut off walls tremie concrete plugs or any combination of the above and or other
possible methods

b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation

groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the Contractor prior to

the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during

remedial measures including but not limited to slowing down or stopping the

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done
without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the use
of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level
should not be allowed Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering
utility trenches connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding
and or backfill in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant

Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
Contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should
comply with applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring
design and installation should be the responsibility of the Contractor Trench shoring and
bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material
be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill
immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
For utility excavations that reveal soft dark gray soils we recommend that the
No 3B Fine gravel bedding thickness be increased to 12 inches and wrapped with a
geotextile fabric AMOCO 4557 or similar
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade should consist of the approved general fills or select granular material
The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential
for future ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project
site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill
material considering the expansive nature of the on site soils
Drainage
The finish grades outside the proposed structures should be sloped to shed water
away from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters next to
foundations and slabs should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce
the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation and slab recomendations since ponded water could cause subsurface
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slabs immediately after setting the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should
be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off away
from slabs and foundations
