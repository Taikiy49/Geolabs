SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project site is underlain by alluvial soils generally consisting of soft to very stiff
silty clays and clayey sandy silts with gravel and cobbles and medium dense silty sand
extending to the maximum depth explored of 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of about 8 and 9 feet below
the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration The groundwater levels
encountered generally correspond to about Elevations 196 and 197 5 feet MSL It
should be noted that the groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall surface
water runoff and other factors
A soil nailed slope with gabion facing will be constructed for the project The soil
nailed slope and gabion facing will have a finished slope of about inclination with
a slope height of about 15 feet The soil nailed slope system consists of a series of
individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the slope Based on our
analysis the installation of minimum 5 5 inch diameter soil nails with a vertical spacing of
3 feet and a horizontal spacing of 6 feet is recommended for the 12 foot long gabion basket
Should it be desired to use 6 foot long gabion baskets the horizontal nail spacing should be
reduced to 4 feet to provide a minimum of two nails per basket A filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equal should be placed on the slope face prior to gabion placement The 15 foot long soil
nails should extend through the stone filled gabion baskets and locked against the gabion
basket with an anchor plate
A soil nail testing program should be implemented consisting of performing a
verification load test on a sacrificial nail and proof testing a minimum of 10 percent of the
production nails
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific
geotechnical recommendations

Project Considerations
The project is located along a section of Manoa Stream between residential
properties flanked by Poelua Street and Pamakani Place in central Manoa Valley on the

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions These later eruptions are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosion processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
The project site is located within the central valley floor Basalt rock formation of
the Koolau Volcanic Series forms the stratigraphic base of the region and is believed to
underlie the near surface deposits of mixed alluvial and colluvial materials

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is along the Manoa Stream behind the property at 2748 Poelua
Street in Manoa Valley in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This portion of the
stream is flanked by residential properties on both the east and west banks An existing
drainage easement about 7 feet wide is located on the north side of the property at
2748 Poelua Street The rear of this property is enclosed with a chain link fence mounted
on a Concrete Masonry Unit CRM wall that sits along the crest of the slope about 15 feet
above the stream The opposite bank of the stream is overgrown with vegetation and
trees
The stream bed at the drainage outfall is lined with a 10 foot wide concrete spillway
that connects the drainage apron from the culvert to the opposite east bank of the
stream Water running over the spillway apparently eroded the stream bed creating a
5 to 6 foot deep depression on the downstream side of the spillway The scouring
migrated under the spillway slab and portions of the wingwall and apron of the drainage
outfall structure including the toe of the slope of the adjacent downstream property
Immediately downstream of the concrete spillway about 20 feet of the east bank
of the stream about 250 square foot eroded and receded precariously close to and
threatened to undermine the existing wall of the property at 2748 Poelua Street
Based on the topographic survey plan provided the existing elevations behind the
existing wall ranged from about 205 to 206 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Between the
stream and the bottom of the existing wall the elevations ranged from about 190 to
204 feet MSL

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs
before or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does
not erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year
but most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the International Building Code 2006 Edition the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project
site will need to be evaluated
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by
earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected
to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease
in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the
pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows

Soil Nailed Slope with Gabion Facing
The soil nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes used to stabilize the steep stream slope We understand that
the installation of gabion facing is desired The reinforcing bars provide both additional
tensile and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the slope The
reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a
footing We understand that the face of the soil nailed slope system will have a finished
slope of about one half horizontal to one vertical inclination with a slope height
of about 15 feet A typical cross section of the soil nailed slope system is presented on
Plate 3
The soil nailed slope with gabion facing consist of the installation of 5 5 inch diameter
soil nails with a vertical spacing of 3 feet and a horizontal spacing of 6 feet The soil nail
spacing was developed to anchor each of the 3 foot wide by 3 foot high by 12 foot long
gabion baskets If the use of 3 foot wide by 3 foot high by 6 foot long basket is desired the
horizontal spacing should be reduced to 4 feet to provide a minimum of 2 soil nails per
gabion basket A filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equal should be placed on the slope face prior
to gabion placement The soil nails will extend through the stone filled gabion baskets and
locked against the gabion basket with anchor plates We understand that this alternative

Soil Nails
Design of the soil nailed slope system will need to consider both the internal and
external stability of the reinforced mass The design of the internal stability includes
establishing the size spacing orientation and length of the grouted reinforcing bars The
external stability includes slope stability of the reinforced mass
Surcharge loading due to the existing residential structure was considered in the
analyses Estimated distributed loading of 150 pounds per square foot was used
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a 5 5 inch diameter hole with inclination
of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal as shown on Plate 3 The soil nail bar should
consist of ASTM A615 Grade 60 threaded bar with minimum bar diameter of 1 125 inches
We anticipated that the subsoil may be very corrosive Therefore we recommend using a
double corrosion protection system for the nails Galvanized coated bars surrounded by
neat cement grout or sand cement mixture with a minimum 28 day compressive strength of
4 000 pounds per square inch psi may be considered
Based on our soil nail analysis design embedment nail length of 15 feet long is
recommended To accommodate anchoring the 12 foot long gabion basket facing a
horizontal nail spacing of 6 feet and a vertical spacing of 3 feet are recommended For 6 foot
long gabion baskets the horizontal spacing should be reduced to 4 feet

Soil Nail Testing
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nailed slope we
recommend performing a minimum of one pre production verification test on
sacrificial soil nail In addition proof tests should be performed on at least 10 percent
of the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in
the design
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load for
the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent of
the design load In addition the load at every loading increment should be held for
at least 10 minutes and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes The proof
tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the design
loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes until stable The proof
test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nailed slope provided the nail
satisfies the test criteria Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are integral
parts of the design of the soil nailed slope system Therefore a Geolabs
representative should observe the pullout tests and creep tests

Gabion Facing
The gabions should be flexible double twisted hexagonal woven steel wire mesh
zinc coated and PVC coated The nominal minimum diameter of the steel wire core used
in the fabrication of the gabion basket shall be 0 106 inches with a PVC coating extruded
onto the wire core having a nominal thickness of 0 02165 inches with a minimum thickness
of 0 015 inches The overall nominal minimum diameter should be 0 146 inches
The stone used to fill the gabions should be between 4 and 8 inches and have a
minimum unit weight of 140 pounds per cubic feet pcf The stone should consist of hard

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to a depth of about 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a portable drill rig with continuous flight augers
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard Practice
for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and
by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Deviations made to
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil
Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials encountered
are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586 Penetration
Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second
and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the
Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used
The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT
blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results
is provided on Plate B 1
One Sieve Analysis test was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 2
One Unconfined Compression test ASTM D2166 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soil The test results are
presented on Plate B 3
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 4
through B 6
