SECTION 1.  
INTRODUCTION 
As part of the City Center Section of the Honolulu Rail Transit Project (HRTP), existing 
utilities along the proposed 4-mile-long guideway need to be relocated. We understand that the 
existing overhead 138 kV lines along Dillingham Boulevard will be relocated underground. At ten 
locations, the lines will transition from underground to overhead, in which two of the ten poles will 
be standard poles. The different locations and poles are summarized below. 
1. 
Oahu Community Correctional Center – 1 Standard and 1 Riser Pole 
2. 
Mokauea Street – 1 Riser Pole 
3. 
Kalihi Street – 1 Riser Pole 
4. 
Kokea Street – 1 Standard and 1 Riser Pole 
5. 
Iwilei Substation – 4 Riser Poles 
We understand that the design of the pole structures will be performed by Hawaiian 
Electric Company (HECO). The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This Geotechnical Planning Report (GPR) addresses the geotechnical engineering tasks 
that will be performed for our field exploration program.  
In addition, Geolabs has developed the approach described in the following sections to 
further characterize the geologic conditions, to select the appropriate foundation type, and to 
design the foundations based on the geologic conditions for the proposed HECO utility poles 
that will be constructed for the project. A Geotechnical Engineering Report (GER) will be 
prepared by Geolabs for the foundation design of the proposed utility poles.  
Specifically, this GPR addresses the geotechnical engineering tasks, including field 
explorations and testing, laboratory testing, and geotechnical engineering analyses that will be 
performed for the HECO steel pole and riser pole foundations.

SECTION 2.  
GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION AND TESTING 
The geotechnical investigation and evaluation will include exploratory borings near the 
HECO steel pole and riser pole foundation locations and geotechnical laboratory testing on 
selected soil and rock core samples retrieved during the field exploration. The proposed 
exploration locations, methods, and sampling techniques to be utilized for the explorations are 
described in the Geotechnical Investigation Work Plan (GIWP). 
Geotechnical laboratory testing will be performed to supplement the geotechnical data 
provided in the GER. Results of the laboratory test data will be used in conjunction with the results 
of the field exploration and testing to evaluate and develop design parameters for the drilled shaft 
foundations that will be constructed to support the utility pole structures to be installed as part of 
the contract. Statement of qualifications for geotechnical testing laboratories is submitted under 
separate cover in the Geotechnical Laboratory Testing Contractor Qualifications. 
2.1 
Subsurface Explorations 
A GIWP describing the borehole locations, drilling depths, proposed exploration 
methods, and other pertinent information will be prepared for the fieldwork program. The 
exploration will be observed by field representatives from Geolabs. Each representative will log 
the explorations and collect geotechnical soil and rock samples in a consistent manner. This 
work will be accomplished in general accordance with the “Geotechnical Quality Assurance 
Plan” and the “Site-Specific Health and Safety Plan” (for geotechnical investigations) submitted 
under separate cover. 
Explorations to be completed for the project will be conducted in general accordance 
with the American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO) Load 
and Resistance Factor Design (LRFD) Section 10.4.2 (Subsurface Exploration) and Federal 
Highway Administration (FHWA) HI-97-021 (Subsurface Investigations – Geotechnical Site 
Characterization) to explore and characterize subsurface conditions along the project 
alignment. Depending upon site access issues and the presence of existing utilities, the 
explorations generally will be accomplished as close as possible to the proposed foundation 
location. In areas where this cannot be accomplished readily by a truck-mounted drill rig, we

2.1.1 Borehole Drilling 
The soil borings typically will be advanced with a combination of 4-inch diameter 
continuous-flight solid-stem augers and continued with PQ-size wireline coring methods to 
advance the PQ size casing. Sampling of the soils will be performed using the modified 
California sampler (ASTM D3550) and the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) split spoon 
sampler (ASTM D1586). In the softer soils, “relatively undisturbed” and “undisturbed” 
samples will be obtained for testing by driving a ring-lined modified California split-barrel 
sampler (ASTM D3550) and/or pushing a 3-inch outside diameter (OD) thin-walled steel 
Shelby tube (ASTM D1587) into the soil formation. 
Where the borehole will be advanced utilizing the PQ core barrel in the soil formations, 
samples of the soils recovered from the PQ core barrel also will be obtained and retained 
in core boxes for visual observation. In some cases, the borehole will be advanced utilizing 
the PQ casing only as a rotary wash drilling method. Therefore, there will be no soil 
samples retrieved between the soil sampling points (soil sampling by the modified 
California sampler or the split spoon sampler for the Standard Penetration Test). The 
borings will be advanced into the rock formations with the PQ size wireline coring 
methods. The PQ size wireline coring methods will produce rock core samples that are 
3.35 inches (or 85 mm) in diameter. 
In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings will include the SPT, which will be 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. Standard Penetration Test sampling provides 
a measure of the resistance or consistency of a soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-
value, which is defined as the number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon 
sampler 12 inches with a 140-lb. hammer after 6 inches of initial seating. The penetration

2.2 
Laboratory Testing 
Laboratory testing will be performed on soil and rock core samples to determine the 
physical and engineering properties. Soil and rock core samples collected from the explorations 
for geotechnical testing will be stored in bags, jars, or tubes, and in core boxes at the testing 
laboratories. The objective of the laboratory testing program is to develop a soil and/or rock 
dataset for the design of the HECO steel pole foundations. 
In general, index testing will be accomplished on samples retrieved from the 
explorations drilled at/near the proposed utility pole foundation locations. Index testing will 
include natural water content determinations, unit weight determinations, Atterberg limits, and 
grain size distribution. 
In addition to the index tests outlined above, strength tests (including unconsolidated-
undrained [UU] triaxial compression and direct shear on relatively undisturbed samples, and 
unconfined compression tests on intact rock samples) will be performed on samples retrieved 
from the explorations. Furthermore, corrosion potential tests (pH, minimum resistivity, chloride

3.1 
Design Criteria 
The basic design criteria that will be used for structures designed and constructed for 
this project will comply with AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications, 9th edition, 2020, 
including all subsequent interim revisions as amended by the State of Hawaii Department of 
Transportation (HDOT) Highway Division Design Branch, Design Criteria for Bridges and 
Structures dated August 8, 2014.  
Structural-geotechnical design will satisfy all applicable portions of the State of Hawaii 
general laws and regulations and the current editions of the code manuals. Unless specifically 
noted otherwise in the design criteria, the latest edition of the code, regulation, and standard 
that is applicable at the time the design is initiated will be used. If a new edition or amendment 
to a code, regulation, or standard is issued before the design is completed, the design shall 
conform to the new requirement(s) to the extent practical or required by the governmental 
agency enforcing the code, regulation, or standard changed; and as agreed to by HART. 
Computation of seismic forces will be based on seismological input and site soil 
response factors. The seismological inputs are the peak ground acceleration (PGA) coefficient, 
short period spectral acceleration (Ss), and spectral acceleration at the 1 second period (S1).  
The site factors are based on determination of the Site Class definitions as presented in 
Section 3.10.3.1 of the AASHTO specification. The Site Class will be determined based on the 
results of borings and laboratory tests (shear strength estimates) made at/near each utility 
foundation location and using the appropriate equations in Table C3.10.3.1-1 of the AASHTO 
specification. 
The site factors for peak ground acceleration (Fpga), short-period range (Fa), and long 
period range (Fv) for the determined Site Classes will be based on Section 3.10.3.2 of the 
AASHTO specification. Site Classes will be determined for selected intervals along the project 
alignment for the seismic design of the facility.

3.2 
Foundations 
Foundation design and construction will be impacted by the variable subsurface 
conditions between each of the planned utility pole locations. Therefore, the engineering 
analyses and design will be performed for each foundation location. In addition to the geologic 
conditions, we have identified several geotechnical and structural issues that will need to be 
addressed during foundation design and construction: 
1. 
Deep foundation installation may encounter boulders and variable bedrock 
conditions. 
2. 
The length of the deep foundations will vary. 
3. 
In some areas, the Recent Alluvium formation is very soft to soft and will require 
special attention during both design and drilled shaft installation. 
4. 
The foundations will be installed in areas of limited access. 
5. 
The footprint of the foundations should be limited. 
6. 
The environment and traveling public must be protected. 
7. 
Commitments in the FEIS will be followed. 
The AECOM Team of geotechnical and structural engineers has considered these issues 
along with the geologic conditions to evaluate a suitable foundation type for the project. Based 
on this evaluation, we selected single, concentrically aligned drilled shafts as the most suitable 
foundation to support the utility poles. 
Design of the foundations to support the poles will be based on the design criteria 
referenced in Section 3.1 of this report. Foundations designed using the LRFD method will be 
proportioned so that the factored axial resistance provided by the soil and/or rock is at least 
equal to the factored axial load applied to the foundations for the service, strength, and 
extreme event limits states. The factored axial load demand is defined as the nominal load 
multiplied by a load factor that would increase the ultimate load. The factored axial resistance 
is defined as the nominal resistance provided by the soil multiplied by appropriate resistance 
factors for each limit state to account for uncertainties in determination and variability of the

3.2.1 Axial Resistance 
3.2.1.a Design Approach 
The drilled shaft foundations will be designed to resist axial compression loads 
by combining the shaft side resistance with its end-bearing resistance. The axial 
resistance analyses include evaluating nominal side and base resistance values 
for the subsurface conditions. 
For the conceptual design, nominal side resistance along the sides of the drilled 
shafts was estimated using the “” method, which calculates the nominal unit 
side resistance, fx, of a cohesive deposit by multiplying the cohesive soil’s 
undrained shear strength, Su, by an “” value. The upper 5 feet of soil have been 
neglected in evaluating side resistance. 
Nominal unit base resistance values for cohesive material were estimated by 
multiplying the Su with an “Nc” value. The Su values used in the analyses are 
those obtained from laboratory strength tests (UU). 
For drilled shafts in granular soils, the nominal side resistance along the sides of 
the drilled shafts was estimated using the “β“ method, which calculates the 
nominal unit side resistance, fx, of a cohesionless deposit by multiplying the 
vertical effective stress, σv’, by a “β“ value. The end-bearing resistance, qp, is 
estimated by multiplying σv’ with the bearing capacity factor, Nq.  
For geologic units composed of rock, the unit side and base resistance were 
correlated with parameters developed from coring and measurements of 
recovery, rock quality designation, unit weight, and compressive strength of the 
rock cores.

3.2.1.b Downdrag Loads 
Based on local experience, the Recent Alluvium deposits are assumed to be 
under-consolidated and are therefore expected to settle under existing fill loads. 
The settlement is assumed to be sufficient to result in the development of 
downdrag loads on the drilled shafts. Typically, downdrag loads are considered 
fully developed when the settlement of the soils surrounding the shaft is about 
0.4 inches greater than the settlement of the shaft itself. 
The laboratory testing program will include consolidation and organic content 
testing of the Recent Alluvium to further evaluate the stress history and 
consolidation properties of the material. Based on the results of this testing, the 
potential to develop downdrag loads on the shafts due to continued settlement 
of the Recent Alluvium will be evaluated further. 
Where downdrag is anticipated, nominal unit downdrag loads will be determined 
considering the shafts will be installed with a permanent steel casing in areas 
and to depths where, based upon subsurface investigation and laboratory 
testing programs, that the soil strength is anticipated to be 600 psf or less, as 
required by the project specifications. The unit side resistance values measured 
along the casing of the test shafts installed in the Recent Alluvium deposits also 
will be reviewed and considered for estimating the nominal unit downdrag loads. 
The nominal pile resistance available to support structure loads will be estimated 
by considering only the positive side and base resistance below the lowest layer 
contributing to downdrag.

Where downdrag loads are determined to exist as a result of settlement of the 
Recent Alluvium due to historical fill placement, the drilled shafts will be 
designed to consider downdrag force effects under both the Service Limit and 
Strength Limit states. In accordance with AASHTO Section 3.11.8, the length of 
the shaft subjected to downdrag is taken to be any portion of the shaft for which 
settlement in the soil layer adjacent to the shaft is estimated to be 0.4 inches or 
greater relative to the downward movement of the shaft at the Service or 
Strength Limit, as appropriate. For the Strength Limit state condition, the force 
effects due to downdrag will be considered using a load of factor of 1.25, and the 
axial resistance below the length of shaft subject to downdrag will be considered 
with the appropriate resistance factor for the Strength condition (0.45 plus a 
20 percent reduction for non-redundant foundations). For Service Limit state 
conditions, load and resistance factors are equal to 1.0.  
3.2.1.c Scour Considerations 
Based on the locations of the proposed utility poles, scour is not a design 
consideration for the utility pole foundations. 
3.2.2 Foundation Settlements 
Settlement analyses will be performed for the drilled shafts. The magnitude of the 
settlement will depend on the subsurface conditions along the side and underlying the 
base of the drilled shaft, in addition to the magnitude of the applied loads. 
The equivalent footing method and elastic theory will be used to estimate foundation 
settlements. For settlement calculations, the equivalent footing will be assumed to be 
located at a depth equal to about two-thirds of the foundation penetration depth below 
the top of the foundation. A stress distribution analysis will then be performed using an 
assumed stress distribution with an approximate Two Vertical to One Horizontal (2V:1H) 
prism method below the assumed equivalent footing location. Appropriate moduli will be 
selected for the different soil units that underlie the foundation location. The selected

3.2.3 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral resistance of drilled shaft foundations will be evaluated for geotechnical strength 
and stability by the project geotechnical engineer to establish minimum embedment 
requirements. In addition, the project geotechnical engineer will provide soil properties for 
use as input parameters for analyses by the project structural engineer. Evaluation of 
deflections for serviceability and extreme event loadings will be performed by the 
structural designer for each foundation using this model. Analyses for structural design will 
utilize the minimum foundation embedded length, which is determined by the project 
geotechnical engineer and may be controlled by either lateral and overturning or axial load 
considerations. 
In order to establish the minimum embedment for lateral and overturning forces at the 
geotechnical strength limit state, the project geotechnical engineer will perform analyses 
using the computer program LPILE (Ensoft 2019). These analyses will be performed for the 
guideway foundations using factored loads. To provide geotechnical strength and ductility, 
the foundation will be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide an equivalent resistance 
factor of 0.67 for Strength and 0.80 for Extreme Limit States. This design approach requires 
that the foundation provide nominal lateral and overturning resistance in excess of 1.5 
times the factored lateral and overturning forces for the governing strength load cases and 
in excess of 1.25 times the factored lateral and overturning forces for the extreme event 
load cases. 
It is noted that the requirements for geotechnical strength described above exceed the 
current requirements of the AASHTO design code, which does not adequately address this 
issue. This approach is implemented in the interest of sound engineering practice to ensure 
ductility in the design for the maximum lateral and overturning force effects.  
Soil properties for use in all lateral analyses will be determined at the location of each 
guideway foundation based on the results of the exploration and laboratory testing

3.2.4 Resistance Factors 
AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications (2020 AASHTO LRFD), Section 10.5.5.2.4 
indicates that axial resistance factors up to 0.45 and 0.35 for Strength Limit State may be 
used for design in compression and uplift, respectively, for drilled shafts installed in clay. 
This resistance factor will be reduced 20 percent for non-redundant foundations (three or 
fewer shafts per foundation), as specified in Section 9.6.1 of the Design Criteria. 
Based on these considerations, axial resistance factors of 0.36 and 0.28 will be used for 
design of the drilled mono-shaft foundations for compression and uplift in the Strength 
Limit State, respectively. In accordance with 2020 AASHTO LRFD, for the Extreme Event 
Limit State, axial resistance factors of 1.0 and 0.80 will be used for design in compression 
and uplift, respectively. 
As described for the lateral resistance analyses, equivalent resistance factors of 0.67 for 
strength and 0.8 for extreme event limit states will be used for geotechnical strength 
evaluation by the project geotechnical engineer. A resistance factor of 1.0 on the lateral 
soil resistance will be used by the structural design in computing deflections for both 
service and extreme event limit state conditions, as well as for structural strength limit 
state.
