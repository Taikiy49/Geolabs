INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kokua Kalihi Valley Community Health Center located at the
northwestern corner of North School and Ahonui Streets in the Kalihi district on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site consists of two lots and is located at the northwestern
corner of North School and Ahonui Streets in the District of Kalihi on the island of Oahu
Hawaii It is proposed to demolish the abandoned one story building and related facility
of the former service station on the eastern lot and deteriorated asphalt concrete
pavement on the western lot The project site will then be developed as a new facility for
the Kokua Kalihi Valley Community Health Center consisting of two levels of
office medical facility one basement parking level and adjacent on grade parking facility

geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of foundations and
site preparation for the subject project The work was performed in general accordance
with our fee proposal dated Our scope of work included the

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3871 00 Page 4
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
The project site is located in the lower part of Kalihi Valley which was formed by
stream erosion of Tertiary Age Koolau basalt flows This initial period of erosion and
valley carving was followed by several periods of relative rise and fall of sea level which
permitted valley filling by alluvium followed by subsequent periods of erosion During the
Quaternary Age Honolulu Volcanic Series lava flowed down the valley and was
subsequently partially covered by Quaternary Age alluvium Continued recent alluvial
processes have covered part of the site with black to dark gray clay

The proposed project site consists of two lots and is located at the northwestern
corner of North School and Ahonui Streets in the Kalihi district of Honolulu on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The eastern lot is occupied by an abandoned one story building and

appears that both lots are currently being used for temporary parking
Based on our field observations and the topographic survey map provided the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff adobe clay with gravel Based on
the subsurface conditions encountered it is our opinion that shallow foundations
consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
health center facility An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per square
foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on the stiff clay and gravel
encountered near the proposed footing depths
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3871 00 Page 6
Laboratory tests indicate that the near surface clay soils exhibit high shrink swell
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for distress to
the future slab on grade resulting from the swelling of the near surface soils we
recommend that concrete slab on grade required for the project be supported on at least
36 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials Detailed discussion of these
items and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in the
following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the relatively competent subsoil conditions encountered at the project
site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support
of the proposed new health center building An allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 500 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on the in situ stiff clay and or silty gravel with sand This bearing value is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
The bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent finish grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep
as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
For footings bearing on the in situ clayey soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be lightly compacted to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing
surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft and or loose materials
are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3871 00 Page 7
select granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
lf foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential
settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f provided non expansive imported fills
are placed under slabs This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted A
coefficient of friction of 0 25 may be used for footings bearing on the in situ clay soils
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
The project site is generally underlain by adobe clays which are considered to be
highly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless concrete slabs
supported by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to
the lightly loaded slab on grade construction resulting from the shrinking and swelling of
these soils due to changes in moisture content To reduce the potential for structural
distress resulting from the swelling of the on site adobe clays we recommend that
non expansive select capping fill material be provided for a minimum depth of 36 inches
below the bottom of the slab s bedding material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3871 00 Page 8
The non expansive select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete The
select capping fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil above the
underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from
the subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the
subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab
Since the proposed slabs on grade will be subjected to vehicular traffic a 6 inch
layer of base course is recommended as bedding material over the 36 inches of
non expansive select granular capping fill for more uniform support The base course
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction We recommend that the concrete slab be designed by a structural
engineer for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a structural slab to
accommodate some amount of differential settlement For design of structural slabs a
modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the
compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the
slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The subgrade soils beneath the
basement parking level and driveway slabs should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report
The thickened edges of slab adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded
at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert
water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations

Exterior concrete sidewalks around the building structure may be required for the
proposed project If sidewalks are planned around the building structure the capping fill

accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations
section of this report Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be
embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade In addition wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall Since the
drainage pipes behind the basement walls will be well below the outside grade
special provisions should be made to provide positive drainage of seepage water
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of select granular fill
material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the
walls
Excavation Support
In order to construct the proposed basement parking level we anticipate that an
excavation on the order of 8 feet below the existing ground surface will be required for the
project construction Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we anticipate that
conventional excavation equipment may be used for the proposed excavation However
it should be noted that hard weathered basalt boulders were encountered in our borings
Therefore heavy equipment may be needed at localized areas to facilitate the proposed
excavation The method and equipment used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated
materials should be stockpiled no closer from the edge of the excavation than a horizontal
distance equal to the depth of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground
movement

Safety considerations require that the anticipated 8 foot deep excavation be either

estimate the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground movements also
depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring supports
are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation

properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent ground should be

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and current grading concept we anticipate that
site preparation work for the proposed construction will consist of cuts of about 8 feet
deep for the proposed basement parking level
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed The existing one story building concrete slabs islands
and asphalt concrete pavements should be demolished and disposed of properly off site
All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and
grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose
firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
Excavated on site clay soils generated from construction of the proposed
basement parking level should not be used as backfill It is anticipated that underground
fuel tank s will be encountered during excavation The removal and disposal of the
underground fuel tanks should be handled properly and with care If the excavated soils
are contaminated with hydrocarbon or other toxic materials the project environmental
consultant should be consulted for proper handling and disposal of the contaminated
soils
After clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs should be over
excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 36 inch thick capping layer of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab subgrade level i e bottom of

Utility Trench
New utility connections will be required for the proposed new health center
building A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the
trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around
the pipes and to reduce the compactive effort on the backfill thus reducing the possibility
of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement could result
in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select fill material The backfill
should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground

subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into
the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings to a depth of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight and hollow stem
augers casing and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of

Laboratory Testing

bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the quality and swelling potential of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Three 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented
