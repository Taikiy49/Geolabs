1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Kapa a Water System Stable Tank Water Main project in the Kapa a
area on the Island of Kaua i Hawai i The general location and project vicinity are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of the new water system
improvements and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located along Ka apuni Road in the Kapa a area on the Island
of Kaua i Hawai i Based on the provided information it is proposed to install about
2 500 lineal feet of 12 inch diameter waterline and about 500 lineal feet of 16 inch
diameter waterline along Ka apuni Road The 12 inch diameter waterline will extend
from the Stable Tank reservoir toward the intersection of Ka apuni and Olohena Roads
Connections to some existing water mains and a new PRV Station are included in the
new construction package
Based on the available plans the majority of the waterline inverts range between
about 4 0 and 4 5 feet below the existing roadway surface Localized deeper deflections
are planned where the new waterline traverses other existing utility lines The deeper
waterline inverts range between about 6 and 8 feet below the existing ground surface
We understand that the new waterlines will be installed along the existing roadway by
utilizing cut and cover methods

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
which was built by the extrusion of lavas from the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series that
began about 5 to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic
Series ended about 2 million years ago followed by a long period of erosion
Renewed volcanic activity followed the cessation of this main shield building phase
about 1 million years ago This renewed activity consisted of the extrusion of basaltic
lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the
thick alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The Koloa Volcanic Series is generally characterized by thick dense basaltic lava
flows extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales on
the eastern half of the Island of Kauai Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava
flows are some localized volcanic deposits consisting of pyroclastic materials volcanic
ash and cinders which are usually encountered as surficial mantling deposits and as
accumulations surrounding the cinder cone vents Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series
cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project site
The project site is located inland on a broad sloping plateau region that is
underlain by weathered soils and basaltic rock belonging to the Koloa Volcanic Series
Multiple meandering stream channels which drain towards the ocean located easterly
of the project site incise the inland plateau region The near surface soils at the project
site consist of residual and saprolitic soils completely weathered rock derived from the
deep in situ weathering of the Koloa Volcanic Series igneous rocks In general these
residual and saprolitic soils typically grade with increasing depth to highly and
moderately weathered basalt rock formation Therefore occasional hard basaltic rock
boulders embedded within the deeply weathered soils may be encountered

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by asphaltic
concrete pavement followed by medium dense silty gravel with sand base fill material
Below the pavement section is generally stiff to very stiff residual and saprolitic clayey
silt material extending to the maximum depth explored
We understand that conventional trench excavation will be the method of
construction for the new 12 inch and 16 inch waterlines At the southeastern end of the
project site a new PRV station is planned for the proposed new waterline A 2 Detailed
discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations in

3 1 Trench Excavation
Based on the provided information we understand that the inverts of the
proposed 12 inch and 16 inch diameter waterlines will range from about 4 to 4 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Two deeper invert deflections are planned in areas
where the waterline will traverse existing utilities Our field exploration generally
encountered stiff to very stiff clayey silts within the elevation of the proposed pipe
inverts
We envision that most of the new waterline trenches will likely be excavated in
the residual and saprolitic soils that consist of stiff to very stiff clayey silt material
In general the contractor should determine and select the method and
equipment subject to practical limits and safety consideration to use for trench
excavation To reduce the potential for new appreciable ground movement the
excavated materials from the trench excavation should not be stockpiled from the
excavation closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation The
trench excavation should comply with all applicable local state and federal safety
requirements

3 2 Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration A minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided below pipes that
bear on the residual and saprolitic clayey silts A typical section of the trench backfill is
presented on Plate 4
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe diameter Before placing the bedding material a
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm whether
firm materials are exposed or the bedding should be extended deeper due to the
exposed subsurface conditions
3 3 Trench Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water
System Standards State of Hawaii 1985 Volume 1 The first zone of backfill extending
from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes initial trench
backfill should consist of free draining granular materials to reduce the required
compaction effort and the resulting pipe stresses
The granular material should have a maximum particle size of 1 inch and should
have less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve It is critical to properly place and
compact the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill around the pipes could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the top of the pipes to about
2 feet below the pavement subgrade may consist of the excavated on site silty and
clayey soils provided they are free of deleterious materials vegetation and are
screened of particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The backfill should be
placed in thin lifts normally 6 to 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative

3 4 Thrust Blocks
At selected water line locations we envision that thrust blocks will be required to
provide resistance to the thrust forces due to bends in the pressurized pipes In general
thrust forces acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the thrust block Based on the available information
the planned inverts are anticipated at about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground
surface with deeper inverts planned at about 6 to 8 feet below the existing ground
surface
Based on our field exploration stiff to very stiff clayey silt materials were
encountered near the planned waterline invert depths Based on these materials we
recommend using a lateral bearing pressure of 2 000 pounds per square foot psf for
the design of thrust blocks

Station and Valve Vaults
We understand that a new PRV station is planned at the new Waterline A 2 site
Based on the provided information the valve vault will extend to a depth of about 6 feet
below the existing ground surface Furthermore we understand that some new valve
vaults are planned in the Stable Tank vicinity Based on our field exploration we
anticipate that the valve vault structures will bear on the very stiff clayey silt material that
was encountered in our borings An allowable bearing pressure of up to may
be used for design of the valve vaults bearing on the very stiff clayey silt material
Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are anticipated for the valve vault
structures
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed valve vaults will depend on
the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the compactive effort on the backfill
material around the structures Therefore to reduce the required compactive effort that
may cause additional lateral earth pressures granular fill material is recommended for
use as backfill around the valve vault structures We recommend designing the valve
vault structures for an equivalent fluid pressure of 60 pounds per cubic foot per foot of
depth pcf The recommended lateral earth pressure assumes that the backfill around
the structures is properly placed and compacted
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges traffic loads line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structures should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the valve vault
structures a rectangular distribution having a uniform pressure equal to 53 percent of
the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire depth of the structure may be
used in the design To evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
additional analyses during design may be needed
Before placing the valve vault structures a Geolabs representative should be
contacted to visually confirm that firm materials are exposed at the bottom of the
structures

3 6 Settlements
Primary settlement of the new waterline is normally caused by the difference in
the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill
material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will cause minor settlement
of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Considering the generally stiff to very
stiff subsurface soils below the inverts of the waterline small primary settlements of less
than 1 inch is anticipated for the project The above estimate assumes that proper
construction procedures and good workmanship good compaction of the trench
backfill will be engaged during construction
3 7 Pavement Restoration
It is anticipated that asphaltic concrete pavement will be used to repave the
waterline trenches or roadway lane along the waterline alignment Select borrow
subbase material should be placed in the trench backfill within the upper 2 feet below
the pavement subgrade elevation and should be placed in 8 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
In general the new pavements along the waterline trenches should match the existing
pavement sections or consist of at least 2 inches of asphaltic concrete placed over 6
inches of base course whichever is greater
