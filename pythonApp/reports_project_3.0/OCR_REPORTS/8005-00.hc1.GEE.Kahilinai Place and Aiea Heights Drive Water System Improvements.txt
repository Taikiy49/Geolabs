SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 
2 to 6 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying 3 to 12 inches of aggregate base course 
and/or subbase course consisting of medium dense sandy gravel, silty gravel and gravelly 
sands. The pavement structure was underlain by residual soils and saprolite. A thin fill 
layer was revealed in about half of the borings drilled.  The thin fill layer that generally 
consisted of stiff silty clays with sands and gravel was about 1 to 3 feet thick. Residual 
soils and saprolite consisting of stiff silty clays and dense silty sands and sandy gravel 
were encountered below the pavement structure to the maximum depth drilled of about 
16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Weathered basalt formation was 
encountered in Boring No. 7 at a shallow depth of 5.5 feet and in Boring Nos. 1, 2, 8, 11 
and 13 at depths of about 8 to 13 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that the groundwater level will fluctuate based upon rainfall, 
surface water runoff, seasonal precipitation, and other factors. 
We anticipate that the majority of the water main inverts will be on the order of about 
4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. However, the pipe invert will be extended 
to a depth of about 10 feet below the pavement surface where the new water main traverses 
under other existing utility lines. Installation of the proposed water mains will involve 
excavation into the underlying fills, residual soils, saprolite, and weathered basalt formation. 
In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignments 
may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, saprolite and 
weathered basalt formation typically contain localized hard zones with boulders.  Therefore, 
we anticipate that some difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas during 
construction.  It is possible that excavations will require the use of hoerams or chipping.

of shoring during construction. For shallow excavations, we envision that trench shields may 
be used for the shoring of the excavated trenches. For deeper excavations, one possible 
method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring.

materials may be used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice 
Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. As volcanic 
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its 
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, known as Schofield Plateau. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally characterized by flows of 
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers.  In-situ chemical 
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years.  The 
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils.  In general, 
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material while residual soils are more clayey.  Both 
residual and saprolitic soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The 
residual soils and saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increased depth. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is in the Aiea area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. It is planned to 
install water mains, fire hydrants, service laterals, pipe locator devices, thrust blocks, 
concrete valve vault, and other appurtenances along Kahilinai Place, Aiea Heights Drive, 
Aheahe Street and Hakina Street. Aiea Heights Drive is the main thoroughfare through 
the well-established Aiea neighborhood, while Kahilinai Place, Aheahe Street and Hakina 
Street are connecting roadways. In general, all the roadways are on sloping grounds with 
gradients varying from about 18 Horizontal to 1 Vertical (18H:1V) to 7H:1V. The existing 
roadways are paved with asphaltic concrete pavements and appeared to be in fair 
conditions. The top of pavement elevation varies from about +160 to +291 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL).

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Deeper excavations 
up to about 10 feet below the pavement surface may be required where the water main 
traverses under an existing utility. Where deeper excavations (greater than about 5 feet 
in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented 
for the trench excavations. Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut 
slopes for open-cut excavations are not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to 
be cut vertically necessitating the use of shoring during construction. 
For shallow excavations, we envision that trench shields may be used for the 
shoring of the excavated trenches. For deeper excavations, one possible method of 
shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring. The sheet piles should be driven 
with a suitable hammer to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground 
subsidence.  
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain 
it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as roads or utilities. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative, 
who should be required to continuously present on-site during excavation and 
construction works, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the in-situ stiff silty clay soils, a minimum of 6 inches 
of compacted granular cushion material such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) should be provided below the pipe.  
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater, and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should 
be provided below the cushion layer.

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the 
trench, the unit weight of trench backfill material and the friction resistance between the 
backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried pipes, 
we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic feet (pcf) for the 
trench backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3. Earth forces acting upon the 
pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the 
trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be 
considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select 
free-draining granular material such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) 
to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it 
is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids 
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill 
materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay 
lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be 
noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may 
be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field observations on the 
available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations.

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the in-situ soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. Imported fill materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The upper 3 feet of trench backfill below the finished pavement grade should 
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the on-site soils or imported fill materials. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.

3.6 
Valve Vault Foundations 
We envision that concrete valve vault structures may be required for the proposed 
waterlines. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) be used for the 
foundation design of the concrete valve structures. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by 
seismic forces. 
If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the valve structure 
excavations, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 24 inches of open-graded 
gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the valve vault structure to be less 
than 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the valve vault structure may be resisted by friction between 
the base of the foundation and on-site soils and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded of valve vault.  A coefficient of friction of 
0.35 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted on-site soils.  Resistance to 
lateral loads due to passive pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure 
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  Unless covered by pavements or 
slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be 
neglected. 
The valve vault structure should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressure 
due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects. The following lateral earth pressure, 
expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf), 
may be used in the design of the valve vault walls.

3.8 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the new water mains. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.

3.10 Corrosion Potential 
Fifteen sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, consisting of pH (ASTM G51) and 
Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57), were performed on selected soil samples obtained from 
our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-5 of Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regards to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure will have. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects.  Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils have 
resistivity values of about 380 to 5,800 ohm-cm. It should be noted that only two resistivity 
test results are higher than 3,000 ohm-cm.  Therefore, the majority of the on-site near-
surface soils may be considered as extremely corrosive to very corrosive (Corrosion Rating 
of 1 to 2) based on the Board of Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu Water System 
External Corrosion Control Standards dated 1991.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 15 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 15, to depths of about 9.5 to 16.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the General Site Plan, 
Plate 2 and the Site Plans, Plate 3.1  through 3.7. We used a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers to advance the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The 
Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the 
Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for 
Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-15. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used.  The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
One Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) test (ASTM D2850) 
was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was 
used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Fifteen sets of pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57) tests were 
performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration.  Results of the corrosivity tests are presented on Plate B-5.
