SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the site consists of a 1-foot thick layer of dense gravel and cobbles 
underlain by hard to very hard basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depths 
explored of about 21 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface.  We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the tower site and the 
structural loading conditions provided, we recommend using a shallow mat foundation to 
support the proposed antenna tower structure.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
6,000 psf may be used for the design of the mat foundation bearing on the hard to very 
hard basalt rock formation.  A modulus of subgrade reaction of 300 pci may be used for 
the mat foundation bearing on the hard to very hard basalt rock formation. 
Alternatively, consideration may be given to the use of drilled shaft foundations for 
the support of the antenna tower structure.  A single cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft 
may be used to support each leg of the antenna tower. The cast-in-place concrete drilled 
shafts would derive vertical support from friction between the concrete shaft and the 
surrounding basalt rock formation.  A 2.5-foot diameter drilled shaft extending to a 
minimum depth of 15 feet into the hard to very hard basalt rock formation should be used 
to support each leg of the antenna tower. 
The reinforced concrete building structure should be supported on shallow 
foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings. As an alternative, concrete 
slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge footings may be used to support the building 
structure.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 psf may be used for the design of 
the building foundations bearing on the hard to very hard basalt rock or compacted fills. 
Cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in basalt rock formation. Due to 
the potential presence for cavities and/or voids at the project site, there is a potential for 
loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities.  We recommend 
implementing a cavity probing and grouting program for the antenna tower foundations 
during construction to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the 
collapse of cavities below foundations. We believe the potential for loss of foundation 
support resulting from collapse of cavities below the foundations supporting the building 
structure is relatively small, and the probing and grouting program may be omitted if this 
risk is considered tolerable.  However, if it is desired to further reduce the potential for loss 
of foundation support, then a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation of

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles.  This island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct); Mauna Kea (activity during recent 
geologic time); Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803); and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano, 
which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials.  The near-surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano 
overlying the Mauna Kea Shield.  The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed 
to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the 
Hamakua Volcanic Series.  The near-surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may 
be on the order of only 750 to 1,500 years old. 
The lava formation at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow, which is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of 
vesicular (porous) rock.  Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava.  
Cavities are formed when lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes 
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities).  Lava tubes are formed 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow.  Inter-flow cavities are 
generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
Blisters and pockets are more common in spatter deposited lavas since the 
accumulation of the spatter material can be discontinuous.

In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in 
the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our exploration, the site is underlain by hard to very hard 
basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depths explored.  We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the tower site and the 
structural loading conditions provided, we recommend using a shallow mat foundation

given to the use of drilled shaft foundations for support of the antenna tower structure.

3.1 
Antenna Tower Foundation 
We understand a new four-legged antenna tower is planned at the project site. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loading information 
provided, we recommend using a shallow mat foundation to support the proposed 
antenna tower structure.  Alternatively, consideration may be given to the use of drilled 
shaft foundations for the support of the antenna tower structure. Detailed 
recommendations for each foundation option are presented in the following subsections. 
3.1.1 Mat Foundation 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend an allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 6,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for design of a mat 
foundation bearing on the hard to very hard basalt rock formation. This bearing 
value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.  
A modulus of subgrade reaction of 300 pounds per square inch per inch of 
deflection (pci) may be used for the mat foundation bearing on the hard to very hard 
basalt rock formation. The subgrade modulus is the modulus of vertical subgrade 
reaction for a 1-foot square test plate and must be adjusted for the actual size of 
mat. 
The bottom of the mat foundation should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finish grade. Deeper embedment may be required to 
provide resistance to uplift and moment reactions at the base of the tower.  
The dead weight of the structure and the mat foundation may be used to provide 
the required uplift load capacity. Additionally, the weight of the backfill material 
placed above the foundations may be used to provide resistance to uplift loads, if 
applicable. A unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) may be used for 
structural fill material.

The bottom of foundation excavation should be firm and unyielding prior to 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Loose materials encountered at the 
bottom of the foundation excavation should be over-excavated to expose the 
underlying hard to very hard basalt rock formation. Concrete or Controlled Low

Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from the 
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less 
than 1 inch. Differential settlements between the center and the corner of the mat 
may be on the order of about 0.5 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system and by friction 
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil. A coefficient 
of friction of 0.5 may be used between the foundations and the hard basalt rock 
formation. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of about 450 pounds per square foot per foot of depth 
(pcf).  This assumes that structural fill around the mat footing are well compacted 
(minimum of 90 percent relative compaction).  Unless covered by pavements or 
slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be 
neglected.

A 2.5-foot diameter drilled shaft extending to a minimum depth of 15 feet into the

3.1.2.a Uplift Load Resistance 
In general, uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead 
weight of the drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the 
adjacent basalt rock formation. Considering that the drilled shafts are 
designed based on adhesion between the shaft and the surrounding 
basalt rock formation, we believe that an ultimate uplift load capacity of 
400 kips may be used in the design. This value includes the weight of the 
drilled shaft and should be used for transient loads only.  
For sustained uplift loads acting on the foundations, the provided uplift 
load capacity should be reduced by a factor of safety of 2.0. The project 
structural engineer should check the structural capacity of the shaft 
member in tension.

3.1.2.c Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic 
compression of the shaft and subgrade response of the foundation 
embedded in the rock formation encountered at the site. The total 
settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be on the order of 
0.5 inch and the potential differential settlements between adjacent drilled 
shafts may be on the order of 0.25 inch. We believe that a significant 
portion of the settlement will be elastic and should occur as the loads are 
applied.

subcontractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to 
drill through hard to very hard basalt rock formation. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) 
should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the 
surrounding rock formation. Concrete should be placed in a suitable 
manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the 
concrete mix. In addition, concrete should be placed promptly after drilling 
(within 24 hours after drilling of the holes) to reduce the potential for 
softening/unraveling the sides of the drilled holes. 
A Geolabs representative should be present at the project site to observe

3.3 
Foundation Probing 
Based on our experience, voids and/or cavities should be anticipated in the 
basalt formation. In order to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting 
from collapse of cavities below the mat foundation supporting the antenna tower, we 
recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the foundations 
during construction. We believe the potential for loss of foundation support resulting 
from collapse of cavities below the foundations supporting the building structure is 
relatively small due to the light loads, and the probing and grouting program may be 
omitted if this risk is considered tolerable.  However, if it is desired to further reduce the 
potential for loss of foundation support, then a program of cavity probing and grouting at 
the foundation of the building structure should be conducted during construction. 
If a mat foundation is selected to support the tower, we recommend drilling 
one probe hole per 100 square feet for the mat foundation.  If it is desired to conduct 
foundation probing for the building structure, probe holes should be drilled at 10-foot on 
centers for continuous footings. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each 
isolated spread footing location.  The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in 
diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of 
foundation.

the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities 
discovered should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM). The 
CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pump pressures. As an alternate to CLSM,

3.5.3 Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces 
Because the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii, forces 
due to dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of 
retaining structures. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures associated 
with seismic loading (amax=0.40g) may be estimated using 16H2 pounds per foot of 
wall length for level backfill conditions, where H is the height of the wall in feet. For 
a sloping backfill condition (up to 3H:1V), a dynamic lateral force of 28H2 pounds 
per lineal foot of wall (H in feet) may be used in design. The dynamic lateral earth 
force generally would act at the mid-height of the wall. 
It should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the 
wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in the event of an 
earthquake. If this amount of lateral movement is not acceptable, the retaining wall 
should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a semi-restrained wall 
condition. For a semi-restrained wall condition, dynamic lateral forces due to 
seismic loading may be estimated using 28H2 pounds per lineal foot of wall for level 
backfill conditions. 
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the 
mid-height of the wall. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented 
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures. An appropriately reduced 
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted 
for in the design of the retaining structure. It should be noted that due to relatively 
high dynamic lateral forces, sloping backfill behind the retaining structures should 
be limited to an inclination of 3H:1V or flatter.

speed of about 300 feet per minute. The roller should pass over the fill surface at

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, to depths ranging from about 20 to 51 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.

For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound rock material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to 
describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D 2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 7012 Method C) were performed on 
selected core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-1. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D 1883) was 
performed on a bulk sample of the near-surface volcanic ash soils to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics of the soils.  The test results are presented on 
Plate B-2.
