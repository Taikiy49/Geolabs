DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The secondary clarifier tanks will need significant dead weight to counteract
buoyant uplift force generated from deep submergence below groundwater Due to the
poor subsoil and shallow groundwater conditions we recommend that end bearing piles
be used to support the heavy tanks
It appears that the on grade biotower tanks may be underlain by highly varying
foundation conditions ranging from fairly thick compressible sediments to more competent
saprolite subsoils Differential settlement of the biotowers would thus be a critical design
consideration In addition lowering the groundwater level by future dewatering within the
plant site may cause the biotowers to settle if pile support is not provided Therefore we
recommend that pile foundations be also used to support the biotowers
Alternately mat foundations may be used for the biotowers provided that the site
is surcharged to reduce the amount of differential settlement that would otherwise be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2651 00 Page 10
anticipated and that future dewatering is prohibited or closely controlled A non expansive
select capping backfill is recommended over the expansive surface soil encountered at
the site
Based on the design bottom elevations it appears that the solids contactor solids
reaeration tanks and biotower pump station do not need a heavy dead weight to
counteract buoyant uplift force We recommend that these light weight underground
structures be supported on mat foundations provided that the groundwater level at these
structure sites are not allowed to be lowered by future dewatering However if the
treatment plant site may be dewatered in the future due to construction or other activities
we recommend that pile foundations be also considered for these underground structures
to reduce the possibility of future settlement of these structures
The proposed light weight structures on the existing grade including the one story
blower building the on grade partitions of the biotower pump station and the odor control
system pads may bear directly on a non expansive capping backfill over the stiff subsoils
It is imperative that adequate shoring and proper dewatering be employed for the
excavations in soft and loose subsoils below high groundwater table Adjacent ground
to the excavations should also be closely monitored for signs of instability to enable timely
implementation of remedial measures
Secondary Clarifiers
Due to the deep bottom elevations at about 15 feet below MSL the proposed
tanks will be subjected to a heavy buoyant uplift force equivalent to a hydraulic head of
as much as 18 feet plus the thickness of the tank bottom Therefore the tanks must be
designed to resist the uplift force under an empty tank condition This may be done by
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2651 00 Page 11
using a heavy dead weight for the tanks and also by depending on the uplift capacity of
pile foundations
It is our understanding from the project design team at GMP Associates Inc that
the dead weight of the three 3 secondary clarifier tanks may be increased by using thick
tank bottoms or by constructing a thick tremie concrete pad attached to each tank
bottom An oversize tank bottom slab may also be used to mobilize the earth overburden
on top of the protruding slab edge around the tank to resist the uplift force
Due to the heavy weight of the tanks significant settlements are anticipated due
to compression of the underlying soft loose subsoils if the secondary clarifier tanks are
supported on a thick concrete mat or tremie concrete pad without pile support
Assuming that a 9 foot thick tank bottom would be required to resist uplift we estimate
that the tanks may settle as much as 10 inches
Therefore we recommend that pile foundations be used to support the secondary
clarifier tanks The piles may be driven from the existing ground surface prior to the tank
excavations and cut off below the tank bottoms after the tank excavations
Depending on the construction method used a stable working platform may be
required over the saturated subgrade soils at the bottoms of the tank excavations We
recommend that a two 2 foot thick cushion of 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67
gravel be used if necessary Mechanical compaction should not be used for placement
of the self compacting gravel since compaction vibration may disturb the underlying
compressible sediments and induce undesirable settlements

A pile hammer capable of delivering a minimum of 30 000 foot pounds of energy

Excavation
Based on the bottom elevations of the proposed underground structures and the
foundation design concepts discussed it is anticipated that deep excavations up to about
34 feet below the existing ground surface may be required for the project construction
Excavation Method
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations Excavation in
the soft loose subsoils utilizing conventional mechanical earthmoving equipment
should not be difficult
In order to reduce the potential of ground movement or subsidence which may
damage the existing adjacent treatment plant facilities we recommend that the
excavated soils be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from any excavation in all time
Excavation Support
Due to the anticipated soft loose subsoil conditions below high groundwater it is
imperative that adequate shoring be provided in the deep excavations in order to
reduce the potential of adjacent ground loss or movement We recommend that
continuous steel sheetpile walls properly braced with horizontal wales and or
struts be used
It is essential that properly interlocked steel sheet piles be installed prior to
excavation and maintained tight against the excavation faces by horizontal bracings
GEOLABS HAWAIll
W O 2651 00 Page 27
progressively installed during excavation The shoring support should not be
removed until completion of the new structures inside the excavations
The steel sheeting should be driven properly with a suitable sheetpile hammer to
a sufficient depth in order to improve the stability of trench bottom and to reduce
the possibility of bottom heaving or piping quick conditions which may be
created in the soft loose subsoils by upward groundwater seepage during
dewatering
Assuming use of sump pumping inside the excavation and no groundwater
drawdown outside of the excavation we estimate that the steel sheeting should be
driven to a minimum of 17 feet below the excavation bottom for the proposed
solids contactor and solids reaeration tanks and a minimum of 27 feet below the
excavation bottom for the proposed secondary clarifiers The use of a deep well
system outside the excavations to draw down the natural groundwater level in
order to reduce the required sheeting embedment is not recommended

The excavation shoring system used must comply with all applicable safety
requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site will have the best opportunity to promptly observe
any changing conditions during construction such as unforseen subsoils
GEOLABS HAWAII

unexpectedly high groundwater table unsuitable construction sequence or
techniques etc which might affect the shoring stability
However it must be noted that some movement of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground might still occur because of changes in earth stresses during
excavation The magnitude of movement is difficult to accurately predict due to the
complexity of the stress changes It depends greatly on workmanship e g how

continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
with instruments such as settlement markers and inclinometers Such monitoring
is particularly critical in areas adjacent to the existing plant facilities

Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
It is Our Opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit
construction of the proposed underground structures under safe and dry
conditions These works or systems should therefore include but not be limited
to pumping only
We suggest that the following three 3 basic criteria be considered in selecting a
suitable method of dewatering
a The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the existing
plant facilities
b The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and dry
working conditions in the excavations
C The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Based on these criteria it aoppears that sump pumping inside the excavations may
be used if the steel sheetpile shoring is driven sufficiently deep to prevent piping
and or heaving of the excavation bottoms
GEOLABS HAWAIi

Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations
is generally not recommended even though this method could reduce the required
sheetpile penetration The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater
table could induce consolidation of subsoils resulting in potential ground
settlements affecting the existing adjacent structures
lf however the use of a well point system is elected recharging by timely injecting
water back into subsoils should be considered to balance the groundwater table
and minimize areal drawdown in areas near the existing adjacent structures We
believe however that the risk of causing settlements to adjacent buildings will
generally be higher by using the well point method than using the sump pumping

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected
soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample
depths
Eight 8 one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the subsoils under both undisturbed and remolded
conditions The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
One 1 Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate its liquid and plastic limits The test results are shown on Plate B 3 and also
on the Boring Log Plate A 5 1 at the appropriate sample depth
Five 5 pH tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the pH
values The test results are also presented on Plate B 3
Five 5 electrical resistivity tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the electrical resistivity values The test results are also presented on Plate
B 3
Two 2 consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the compressibility of the subsoils The test results are plotted on Plates B 4
and B 5
