SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the site generally 
consist of 6 to 7 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by fill.  The fill consists of coralline 
gravel with some sand.  The relative density of the fill was generally dense to very dense 
to depths of about 2 to 6 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The relative density of 
the fill then changed to very loose to loose to depths of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. Below the fill, loose to medium dense coralline detritus was 
encountered to the maximum depth explored. Dense coralline detritus was encountered 
between depths of about 43 and 61 feet below the existing ground surface. We 
encountered groundwater at depths of about 6 to 8.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  
Due to the relatively light column loads, we believe that shallow foundations 
consisting of spread and/or continuous footings bearing on recompacted fill may be used 
for support of the proposed structure planned at the project site.  An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the foundation 
design.  The bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Based on our analyses, the loose soils encountered below the groundwater level 
are potentially liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6 with an associated peak 
ground acceleration of 0.36g.  Due to the potential liquefaction of the loose soils 
encountered, the site could be subjected to appreciable ground settlement (on the order of 
about 10 inches or more).  Therefore, a shallow foundation support system may 
experience appreciable settlement, and the new structure may sustain significant damage. 
Due to the potential structural damage that may occur due to ground settlement caused by 
liquefaction, driven precast, prestressed concrete piles will be used to support the new 
structure.  The concrete piles should consist of 16.5-inch octagonal piles with an allowable 
compressive load capacity of up to 20 kips per pile.  The driven piles would derive its 
support primarily from end bearing on the dense coralline detritus encountered at about 
43 feet below the existing ground surface.  Since it is not practical to conduct a test pile 
program for the project, we recommend casting the production piles at least 55 feet long to 
account for potential variation in the subsurface conditions. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,030 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes:  Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is within the Hilo Harbor area. Hilo Harbor lies at the intersecting 
slopes of the Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea Volcanoes. The lava flows of the Mauna Loa 
Volcano are relatively “young” in terms of geologic time and may be on the order of only 
750 to 1,500 years old. Below the basaltic lava flows of the Mauna Loa Volcano, the 
underlying lava flows of the Mauna Kea Volcano are believed to be Pliocene to 
Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series. 
The land surrounding the Hilo Harbor area was reclaimed from the bay by the 
placement of fill over the lagoonal deposits and/or coralline detritus. The project site is 
generally underlain by lagoonal deposits and deposits of calcareous sediments 
(also known as coralline detritus). Alluvial deposits associated with the Wailuku River 
underlie the lagoonal deposits and calcareous sediments. Basalt formation from the 
recent Mauna Loa lava flows may be encountered at greater depths below the alluvial 
deposits.

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
Earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the Island of 
Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island 
of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are 
related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the active volcanoes in the southern 
portion of the Island of Hawaii. 
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 6 (M6+) including the October 15, 2006 earthquakes. Based on

2.4.2  Liquefaction 
Based on the International Building Code, the project site may be subjected to 
seismic activity and should be evaluated for soil liquefaction potential. Soil 
liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore 
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In 
this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration 
(such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume 
causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly 
enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. It is generally acknowledged that 
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the 
ground surface. In deeper deposits, the greater overburden pressure is generally

sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows: 
1. 
Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more 
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands. 
2. 
Initial Relative Density - Loose sands and silts are most susceptible 
to liquefaction. Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to 
relative density. 
3. 
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration - Liquefaction potential is 
directly proportional to the magnitude and duration of the 
earthquake. 
In general, the subsurface information obtained from the boring indicates that the 
site is underlain by loose to very loose fill and coralline detritus to a depth of about 
43 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Based on our analyses, the loose to 
very loose fill and coralline detritus are potentially liquefiable during a seismic event 
of Magnitude 6 with an associated peak ground acceleration of 0.36g.  
It appears that the very loose to loose fill and coralline detritus below the 
groundwater level have a factor of safety of 0.3 to 1.0 against liquefaction. 
Therefore, it is our opinion that the project site could be subjected to appreciable 
ground settlements (on the order of about 10 inches or greater) in the event of 
liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+). The effects of potential 
liquefaction at the site should be taken into consideration when designing the 
proposed new structure.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the site generally consist of 6 to 7 inches of 
asphaltic concrete underlain by coralline gravel fill.  The relative density of the fill was 
generally dense to very dense to depths of about 2 to 6 feet below the existing 
pavement surface.  The relative density of the fill then changed to very loose to loose to 
a depth of about 21.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Coralline detritus was 
encountered below the fill and extended to the maximum depth explored.  The coralline 
detritus consisted of loose to medium dense sand and gravel.  A dense layer of coralline 
detritus was encountered between depths of about 43 and 61 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. We encountered groundwater at depths of about 6 to 8.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the end wall may be supported 
on shallow spread footings.  The total footing settlements under the anticipated design 
loads (static conditions) may be on the order of about 1 inch for footings supported on 
shallow spread footings. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported 
on the recompacted fill under static loading conditions should be on the order of about 
0.5 inches.   
It should be noted that our engineering analyses indicates that the site could be 
subjected to appreciable ground settlement (on the order of about 10 inches or more) in 
the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+) with a peak ground 
acceleration of 0.36g.  Therefore, the shallow foundation support system may 
experience appreciable settlement, and the new end wall may sustain significant 
damage.  Due to the potential structural damage that may occur due to ground 
settlement caused by liquefaction, driven precast, prestressed concrete piles will be 
used to support the new structure.  The concrete piles should consist of 16.5-inch 
octagonal piles.  The driven piles would derive its support primarily from end bearing on 
the dense coralline detritus encountered at about 43 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Since it is not practical to conduct a test pile program for the project, we 
recommend casting the production piles at least 55 feet long to account for potential 
variation in the subsurface conditions.

Foundation System Evaluation 
Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer, the 
maximum column load for the proposed new structure is approximately 12 kips.  Based

damage.  The structural engineer should check the integrity of the new structure against 
collapse due to potential ground settlement in the event of liquefaction.  The owner 
should assess the potential risks of a seismic event. If the risks associated with potential 
ground settlements during a seismic event is not acceptable to the owner, the new 
structure should be supported on a deep foundation system, such as driven precast, 
prestressed concrete piles.

In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades.  In addition, foundations next to other 
foundations, utility trenches, easements or retaining walls should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or 
utility trench, or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility 
lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement. 
Soft and/or loose soils, if encountered at the bottom of footing elevation, should 
be over-excavated to expose firm/dense materials and the resulting over-excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted fill material. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system.  A coefficient 
of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted fill.  Resistance 
due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  This assumes that the soils around 
the footings are well compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. 
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads (static 
condition) for footings bearing on the recompacted fill as recommended herein to be on 
the order of about one (1) inch total with differential settlements on the order of 
one-half (½) inch.   
We recommend that a Geolabs representative observe the footing excavations 
prior to placement of reinforcement steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing 
conditions and the required embedment depths.

3.3.1 Downdrag Loads 
Due to the potential for liquefaction of the very loose to loose fill and coralline 
detritus at the project site, settlement of the loose soils will result in downdrag 
loading of the pile foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable 
materials. Therefore, the allowable compressive load capacities of the piles have 
been reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses. 
Based on our foundation analyses, we estimate that the downdrag load imposed on 
the piles due to the potential for liquefaction of the very loose to loose soils may be 
on the order of about 40 kips for the 16.5-inch octagonal piles. The estimated 
downdrag loads has been accounted for in the recommended allowable 
compressive load capacity; therefore, the allowable compressive load capacity 
does not need to be further reduced for the downdrag loads. 
3.3.2 Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the driven pile 
and shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils.  The recommended uplift 
load capacity (ultimate capacity) for the 16.5-inch octagonal pile is 25 kips per pile. 
The uplift load capacity provided above includes the weight of the pile.  The uplift 
load capacity of the piles provided is intended for transient load applications. 
Therefore, the uplift load capacity should not be further increased for transient load 
applications.  For sustained uplift loads, we recommend reducing the uplift load 
capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads.  The capacity 
of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project structural engineer.

3.3.4 Pile Foundation Settlements 
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of 
the pile member and subgrade response.  We estimate the total settlement of the 
pile-supported foundations to be less than 0.5 inch with differential settlements 
between columns supported on piles not exceeding about 0.25 inches.  We believe 
that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are 
applied. 
3.3.5 Pile Hammer Selection 
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the 
range of about 10,000 to 15,000 foot-pounds in the field.  The hammer should be 
equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the loose soils may 
be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development, which could 
damage or cause cracking of the piles. 
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into 
consideration the “matching” of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be 
driven.  This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy 
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles.  Geolabs should review 
the proposed pile driving equipment to evaluate whether the equipment is capable 
of installing the piles to the recommended capacity.  The contractor should forward 
pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs for evaluation prior to 
construction.  Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the driveability of 
piles with the use of the selected hammer.

Underground Utilities 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the pipes for uniform support. 
Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a 
stabilization layer consisting of additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding 
layer for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, 
No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes.  It is critical to 
use free-draining materials around the pipes to reduce the potential for formation of 
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around the sides of 
the pipes. Improper trench backfill around the pipe could result in backfill settlement and 
pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade may consist of the excavated on-site soils, provided that they 
are free of deleterious materials and over-sized materials (greater than 6 inches in 
maximum particle size).

placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to a minimum

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
four borings extending to depths of about 21.5 to 100 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with augers and rotary coring 
equipment.

were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as 
shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4.

California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Twelve Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plates B-1 through B-3. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D 1883) was performed 
on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The 
test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-5.
