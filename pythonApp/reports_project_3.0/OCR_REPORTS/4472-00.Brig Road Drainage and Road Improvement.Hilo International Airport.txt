INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Brig Road Drainage and Road Improvement project at the Hilo International
Airport in Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of pavement rehabilitation reconstruction and
surface water disposal system only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of approximately
4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield volcanoes which
include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano overlying the Mauna
Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to be Pliocene to
Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series The
near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the order of only 750 to
1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is characterized by
a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities were formed when the
lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow
cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from
the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the aggregate base subbase and or subgrade soils In addition free water
voids in the pavement section repeated heavy wheel loads and an erodible base can also
cause pumping Therefore the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients
should be maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage
structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction Development of good shoulder drainage with a greater frequency of drywells
and disposal conduits along with a program to maintain the drainage structures to be free of
obstructions is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or
premature failure of the pavements

Stormwater Runoff Disposal Systems
Based on the information provided flooding of the existing roadway was observed in
localized areas especially along the portion of roadway fronting Aloha Air Cargo and the
FedEx Building after heavy rains As previously indicated one of the primary distress
mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to saturation of the aggregate base
subbase and or subgrade soils We believe that the standing water after heavy rains has
caused saturation of the base materials and or subgrade soils and has likely resulted in the
severe pavement deterioration observed at the site Therefore we recommend that additional
drywells be installed for stormwater runoff disposal at selected locations along this portion of
the roadway alignment
Two percolation tests were performed in Boring Nos 9 and 10 in the area fronting
Aloha Air Cargo and the FedEx Building to evaluate the subsurface permeability conditions
where ponding water was observed The constant head method was used for the percolation
testing The results of our percolation tests and the calculated permeabilities are presented
on Plates C 1 and C 2 of Appendix C The tests indicate that the subsurface soils have a
coefficient of permeability k of 7 6 x 10 and 3 6 x 10 feet per minute This coefficient of
permeability generally corresponds to a water run off disposal capacity of 0 02 and 6 5 cubic
feet per second cfs for a 5 foot diameter 20 foot deep drywell Due to the highly variable
disposal rates of the subsurface conditions across the project site we believe that an average
disposal capacity on the order of about 1 to 2 cfs may be used for the preliminary design of
a typical 5 foot diameter 20 foot deep drywell
Drywells on the island of Hawaii are typically 5 feet in diameter and 20 feet in depth
The actual size and depth of the stormwater runoff disposal system will depend on the design
quantity of the surface runoff Extension of the drywell from the planned 20 foot depth may
be required depending on the specific site conditions at the proposed drywell locations
Therefore we recommend that unit prices for additional drilling be obtained during bidding
Provisions should be incorporated into the construction documents to extend the drywell
depths from the planned 20 feet to about 60 feet below the ground surface using a minimum
15 inch diameter extension hole or 5 foot diameter drilling equipment The unit prices for

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the Brig Road alignment for this project were
explored by drilling and sampling 10 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10
extending to depths ranging from about 2 5 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the test borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drill equipment with continuous flight augers and
coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our project geologist who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook

the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to base the calculation
of the subsurface material permeability The results of the field permeability tests are
presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
