Project Considerations

Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity

by our office for the proposed development
Seismic Cone Penetration Testing SCPT provides near continuous soil profiling at
the site in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the
project site The SCPT method involves inserting a sensitive probe into the soil at a
constant speed Various sensors embedded in the probe continuously measure the tip
resistance side friction and induced pore pressures as the probe is systematically pushed
through the soil layers
In general subsurface exploration consisting of SCPT yields more accurate results
for highly sensitive materials such as the loose granular soils at the site because the data
is Obtained directly from the in situ soils Therefore SCPT with seismic shear wave
velocity profiling is considered to be the industry standard for higher order analysis such
as for detailed seismic analyses especially in these subsurface conditions

Field Exploration
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered medium dense sand
of about 7 feet thick The surface fill was generally underlain by soft lagoonal deposits
coralline detritus to depths of approximately 30 feet below the existing ground surface
Below the lagoonal deposits the CPT generally encountered alluvial deposits consisting
of stiff to very stiff silts and clays and loose to medium dense silty sands The alluvial
deposits extended to the maximum depth explored of about 104 feet below the existing
ground surface

Due to the nature of the subsurface conditions at the site it was desired to perform
seismic shear wave velocity profiling using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing SCPT in
an effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the site Shear
wave velocity test and cone penetration test were performed extending to a depth of
approximately 104 feet below the ground surface using seismic piezocone SCPT at the
approximate location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In the piezocone penetration test the seismic piezocone penetrometer is
hydraulically pushed at a rate of approximately 20 mm second 5 mm second through
the subsurface soil The seismic piezocone consists of a 10 ton capacity steel electronic
subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1 55 square inches
10 square centimeters connected to a friction sleeve with an area of 23 25 square inches
150 square centimeters The tip resistance side friction resistance and pore pressure
induced immediately behind the cone tip designated as the U2 parameter during
penetration are measured by pressure transducer and strain gages within the instrument
and are recorded versus the depth of penetration in approximately 2 inch 50 mm
intervals
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear
wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are made when
the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A shear wave is
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
Log of the seismic cone penetration test is presented on Plate 3 of this letter
report In general the plots include corrected cone tip resistance friction ratio sleeve
friction over tip resistance pore pressure differential pore pressure ratio shear wave
velocity soil behavior based on the R zone and interpreted soil description The values
of the tip resistance friction ratio and pore pressure may be correlated to soil strength
and classification The center of the friction sleeve is approximately 4 inches 100 mm
behind the cone tip To calculate the friction ratio the average friction resistance and tip
resistance are compared at the same depth This involves an offset of the friction
resistance by the physical distance of 4 inches
Pore pressure is presented in terms of equivalent height of water head
Differential pore pressure ratio is calculated as a ratio of excess pore pressure to cone tip
resistance The excess pore pressure was calculated by subtracting estimated

Soil Profile Type
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average shear wave
velocity for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the location is presented on Plate 4 The
weighted average shear wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear wave
velocity method of the Uniform Building Code Section 1997 Edition
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration the
weighted average shear wave velocity was measured at about 920 feet per second
Based on the shear wave velocity test results we believe that the project site may be
classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a Stiff Soil site corresponding to an
Sp soil profile type based on the Uniform Building Code Table No 16 J 1997 Edition
Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based in
part upon information obtained from the cone penetration test probes It should be noted
that the cone penetration testing does not actually obtain a soil sample As a result it is
not possible to precisely identify exactly every type of soil encountered in the SCPT
probes therefore the term SOIL BEHAVIOR TYPE is used on the logs In general the
soil is behaving like one soil type based on the SCPT data It may not be precisely the
soil described on the logs but it is behaving as such Variations of subsurface soil
conditions and shear wave velocities between and beyond the test probes may occur and
the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is
underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate the
recommendations provided in this letter report
The location of the field cone penetration test indicated in this report is
approximate having been estimated by taping from the features shown on Site and Utility
Plan transmitted by Hida Okamoto Associates Inc on Elevation of
the cone penetration test was interpolated from the elevation points shown on the same
plan The physical location and elevation of the cone penetration test should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
