INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed future building at northern corner of the Ewa Beach Shopping Center located
in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The shopping center is located west of
Fort Weaver Road north of Papipi Road south of the Ocean Pointe subdivision and east of
the Ewa Beach Elementary School and the Ewa Beach Post Office The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the structure foundations
slabs on grade pavements utilities excavation and fill placement only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age As voicanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau or Central
Oahu
GEOLABS INC
W O 4207 00 Page 4
Weathering and erosion of this plateau generated sediments which were transported
to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south these alluvial sediments
accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to form
a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over
1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining
formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of
these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the growth
and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water was
available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the current sea level As the glaciers
melted and retreated more water became available and sea levels rose to and often above
the current level When the stand of the sea was high and prolonged marine deposits
accumulated at elevations above the current sea level
At the project site below the near surface fill materials are primarily coral algal
deposits which are composed of medium hard limestone with an open lattice like structure
It is believed that these coral deposits were laid during the Kaena 95 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL stand of the sea and subsequently eroded to near their current elevation during the
Waimanalo 25 feet MSL stand The coral formation has also been subjected to subaerial
weathering
Coral algal materials are chemically composed of calcium carbonate During subaerial
weathering slightly acidic rain and groundwater can slowly attack the carbonate dissolving
the weaker portions of the formation This creates solution cavities ranging from small pores
to large caverns

Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration cavities and or loose zones appear to be present in
the coral formations encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities or compression of loose zones
below foundations and structures we recommend that a program of probing and grouting
be conducted The probing program should be conducted prior to excavation to allow for
easier access of the probe drill rig Otherwise special small drills may be required to
perform the probe drilling within the excavation The purpose of the probing and grouting
program is to locate and fill possible voids and loose zones
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each new
isolated spread foundation location and at 10 foot on centers for the new continuous and mat
foundation locations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should
extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation If cavities voids or loose
zones are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe holes
should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity
void or loose zones All probe holes should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected
at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand
1 part cement and 0 7 part water with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches
Because of the potential for encountering cavities voids and loose zones at the site
we recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting operation
is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be required in the construction
schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
voids and loose zones and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout
take and or changed conditions are observed

Fills backfills and base course should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent

thickness and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction

percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with low to very high swelling potential Unless floor slabs are
properly designed and constructed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from swelling of these soils due to increases in the moisture content
To reduce the potential for distress to slabs on grade resulting from swelling of the sandy
clay materials we recommend that the material be removed from beneath slabs on grade
and replaced with non expansive select granular fill material The on site
coralline materials may be used as non expansive select granular fill material
Conventional slab on grade design 4 inch cushion and vapor barrier may be utilized above
the select granular fill or on site coralline soils
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding around the
foundation
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed below the
slab The cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or
Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB should provide more uniform support of the slabs and should
serve as a capillary break To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to

Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface
water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after
construction Due to the fairly level grade of the area construction of drainage inlets to a
subsurface drainage system may be necessary to accommodate proper drainage of surface
water
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation and pavement recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 20 4 and 21 5 feet below
the existing ground elevation at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment

examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2

ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
