REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.   
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea level fell below its present stand. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the 
sea level rose to above its present level. 
The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of the western third of the island, 
while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the 
island. After the main shield-building phase of Koolau, the volcanic dome became 
deeply eroded. On the windward side of the island, combined wind, rain, and wave 
erosion cut the coastline back while forming steep cliffs.

Glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and 
deposition. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly 
lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea 
level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at 
higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
a detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls, and the soil materials are 
transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. 
Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial 
processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as 
alluvium. In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations. Because of this, 
the transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load 
consists of a poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When 
the erosional base levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of Koolau, and its subsurface 
conditions are directly related to this deep erosion and littoral processes at the coast. 
The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS 
The proposed asphaltic concrete paved driveway site is located near the base of 
the adjacent mountain to the west. Several single-story structures and work trailers on 
relatively flat areas of the site were observed. At the western end of the project site 
beyond the existing structures, the ground surface generally slopes up towards the 
mountain at a relatively gentle gradient that grows steeper as it approaches the 
ridgeline.  
Based on our field observations, the slope is heavily vegetated with medium to 
large trees and tall grass. A few rock outcrops were visible higher up the mountain near 
the ridgeline. However, generally no significant evidence of past rockfall activity, such 
as rock debris and fallen boulders, was observed at the bottom of the slope 
(Photograph No. 3, Plate 2.2).  
Based on the topographic survey plan provided, the lateral distance between the 
edge of the planned driveway to the base of the moderate to steep slope face ranges 
from about 30 to 75 feet. The inclinations of the lower part of the slope range from about 
two  horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) on the north end of the project site up to about 
1.5H:1V behind the existing structures. The slope higher up the mountain is steeper 
with a few visible rock outcroppings. However, a topographic survey of the upper 
sections of the slope was not available at the time of this report, and it is not clear how 
far the property line extends up the slope.

SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
Site reconnaissance of the project site was conducted by two of our engineers on 
March 24, 2023, to evaluate the existing site conditions. Due to the heavy slope 
vegetation, steepness of the hillside, and being unable to access the slope from the top, 
only limited on-foot reconnaissance was performed. Therefore, some of the existing 
slope conditions were observed via drone. Site reconnaissance photographs depicting 
the existing site conditions from the ground and in the air are presented on Plates 2.1 
through 2.5.  
Access to the project site is gained through the gate on Hauula Homestead 
Road, located on the north side of the property. Based on the provided grading plan, the 
proposed driveway begins on the north end of the project site at Sta. 3+00 and 
terminates on the south end at Sta. 16+57 for a total of about 1,357 linear feet of paved 
driveway.  
In general, the entire slope was highly vegetated, which made visual 
observations of the slope soil and rock conditions difficult. The slope was generally 
observed to be covered with thick California grass, palm trees, Albizia trees, and other 
surface vegetation (Photograph No. 1, Plate 2.1).  
At the north end of the project site around Sta. 4+75, the existing ridgeline toe 
slopes down close to the planned driveway alignment. Although the slope is at its 
closest distance to the proposed alignment at about Sta. 4+75, the slope inclination is 
relatively gentle and no exposed rock hazards was observed near the toe of the slope. 
In addition, the geometry of the slope would cause the potential hazards to fall away 
from the driveway (Photograph No. 2. Plate 2.1). 
As the proposed driveway moves south, a relatively consistent large setback 
between the slope and the proposed driveway remains (Photographs Nos. 3 and 4, 
Plate 2.2). As shown in Photograph No. 5 (Plate 2.3), there are existing structures and 
work trailers within the setback. However, the slope inclination behind these structures 
appears to be relatively gentle and heavily vegetated.  
Near-vertical rock outcroppings were observed close to the ridge line at multiple 
locations (Photograph No. 6, Plate 2.3). The outcroppings were observed to be slightly 
to moderately weathered. Based on what could be observed via our drone, we did not 
observe unstable rock blocks or severe undermining that could cause an immediate 
potential rockfall hazard (Photographs No. 7 and 8, Plate 2.4). In addition, at the south 
end of the project site near the location of the planned detention basin, the heavily 
vegetated slope appears to retain its gentle inclination (Photograph No. 9, Plate 2.5). 
The setback distance between the base of the slope and the proposed detention basin 
and the driveway is the largest at this point.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Near-vertical rock outcrops were observed close to the ridgeline. These outcrops 
appear to be slightly to moderately weathered with no significant undermining. The rock 
outcroppings are separated from the planned driveway by a long and relatively gentle 
slope with dense vegetation that would largely dissipate the energy of fallen rocks 
before the material reaches the driveway. Therefore, we believe that the observed 
outcrops present low rockfall hazard potential to the project site. 
The setback distance between the base of the slope and the planned driveway is 
the least near about Sta. 4+75. Because the primary slope at this location is generally 
facing away from the planned driveway and no rock hazards were observed at the base 
of the slope, we believe that there is a low potential for rockfall hazards in this section. 
During our site reconnaissance, we did not observe signs of previous rockfall 
events at the project site, such as the accumulation of detached cobble and 
boulder-sized rock blocks near the base of the slope. In addition, we generally did not 
observe signs of recent rock detachment from the visible rock outcrops on the slope via 
our drone photographs.  
Based on the considerations above, we believe there is a low potential for falling 
rock material to impact the planned driveway. Therefore, we do not believe that rockfall 
hazard mitigation measures are needed for the driveway project at this time. 
It should also be noted that we did not observe signs of past slope failures at the 
time of our site reconnaissance. However, due to the dense vegetation, observation of 
the entire slope surface was not possible. Subsurface information for the adjacent 
slopes was not available at the time this report was prepared. However, we anticipate 
that the relatively steep portions of the slope shown on the provided grading plan are 
largely composed of basalt rock formation. We anticipate the gentler slopes at the base 
of the mountain to be composed of relatively stiff clayey alluvium and colluvium. Based 
on these assumptions and our engineering analysis, we do not believe there are slope 
stability concerns in the vicinity of the proposed driveway.
