SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the majority of the proposed roadway reconstruction 
alignment generally encountered stiff to very stiff clayey silts and sandy silts extending to 
the maximum depth explored of approximately 6.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our 
field exploration. 
Based on the relatively poor conditions of the existing pavements, we recommend 
reconstructing the entire Kaupakalua Road within the Phase 2 limits under consideration 
for this project with a new pavement section.  Because the project will involve the 
reconstruction of the existing roadway, we have performed a detailed economic 
comparison involving both flexible and rigid pavement sections in accordance with the 
latest guidelines provided by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, 
Highways Division, Material Testing and Research Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements will be constructed over the in-situ clayey silt and sandy silt soils 
in the shallow subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data 
provided for our pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction 
considerations, it is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway reconstruction project 
may consist of 2.5 inches of AC over 4 inches of ACB on 6 inches of AB.  It should be 
noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual 
subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design section is 
adequate. 
A layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 or equivalent) on a 
layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the 
new pavement structural section.  We believe the reinforcing geogrid would serve to 
provide stabilization and reinforcement to the underlying subgrade materials that exhibit 
relatively high in-situ moisture contents. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Kaupakalua Road Pavement Reconstruction, 
Phase 2 (East Kuiaha Road to Hana Highway) project in Makawao on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway reconstruction project is along Kaupakalua Road from East Kuiaha 
Road to Hana Highway for a distance of approximately 3.2 miles. The approximate 
limits of the roadway reconstruction project are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
We understand that the proposed pavement project will be federally funded. 
Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to the requirements and 
guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will be required. The 
new pavements will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement 
Design Manual dated March 2002, prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
the roadway under consideration. The findings obtained from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing programs will be used as a basis to formulate geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the pavement reconstruction of the project roadway.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Kaupakulua Road within the Phase 2 limits under consideration for 
this project is generally a two-lane undivided roadway except for the additional turn 
lanes at the intersection areas.  The approximate limits of the Phase 2 portion of the 
roadway reconstruction project extend from the intersection with East Kuiaha Road to 
the south and to Hana Highway to the north as shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of cut grass 
and brush, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils along the shoulder areas 
below the vegetation generally consist of reddish brown clayey silts with sand and are 
generally in a moist to saturated condition. The existing Kaupakulua Roadway 
alignment generally slopes down from west to east between the project limits. 
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in August and September 2016 to 
collect general information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the 
roadway under consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented 
below. It should be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to areas where the

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

recommend reconstructing the entire Kaupakalua Road within the Phase 2 limits under 
consideration for this project with a new pavement section in accordance with guidelines

Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the Kaupakalua Road 
alignment under consideration for this project is underlain by clayey silt and sandy silt 
soils.  We understand that placement of asphalt concrete base (ACB) or Portland 
Cement Concrete (PCC) directly on the clayey silt and sandy silt subgrade is not 
recommended.  Therefore, the pavement section should consist of an aggregate 
subbase (ASB) layer between the upper ACB or PCC and the underlying clayey silt and 
sandy silt subgrade soils. 
Due to the long length of the roadway alignment, we envision that new 
pavements for the project would need to be constructed in sections.  Because of the 
poorly graded nature of the permeable base material, we envision that the material 
could not be compacted into a dense and stable mass.  Our experience suggested that 
the permeable base material would tend to unravel or spill out of the completed 
pavement section when excavation is made for the adjacent section. Therefore, 
incorporation of a permeable base layer for drainage purpose would not be practical 
from a construction standpoint and was not included in our pavement analyses.

we have determined the following flexible and rigid pavement design sections for the

Due to the relatively high moisture content of the in-situ soils, we recommend 
incorporating a layer of triaxial geogrid between the upper aggregate subbase and 
underlying clayey silt and sandy silt subgrade soils for the flexible pavement section.  The 
reinforcing geogrid would serve to provide stabilization and reinforcement to the underlying 
subgrade materials. 
To maximize the benefits of the triaxial geogrid, we understand that aggregate 
base course with a nominal maximum size of 1.5 inches should be used in lieu of the 
aggregate subbase. In general, the triaxial geogrid will interlock with the aggregate base 
course, resulting in two benefits during initial construction and for the life of the project: 
1) lateral confinement – increasing the modulus of the aggregate base course, and 
2) subgrade bearing capacity enhancement.  Based on the use of aggregate base 
course and reinforcing geogrid, it is our opinion that the following flexible pavement 
design section may be used for the Kaupakalua Road – Phase 2 Pavement 
Reconstruction project.  Detailed calculations are presented on Plate C-3.2 in

The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes. The 
vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform 
to Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling 20 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 13 through 32. The borings extended to 
depths of about 5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. The rock material left in the 
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock 
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-20. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

percent of the soil mass. Primary constituents are
capitalized and bold (i.e., GRAVEL, SAND)
PRIMARY constituents are based on plasticity. Primary
constituents are capitalized and bold (i.e., CLAY, SILT)
SECONDARY constituents are composed of a
percentage less than the primary constituent. If the soil
mass consists of 12 percent or more fines content, a
cohesive constituent is used (SILTY or CLAYEY);
otherwise, a granular constituent is used (GRAVELLY
or SANDY) provided that the secondary constituent
consists of 20 percent or more of the soil mass.
Secondary constituents are capitalized and bold (i.e.,
SANDY GRAVEL, CLAYEY SAND) and precede the
primary constituent.
SECONDARY constituents are composed of a
percentage less than the primary constituent, but more
than 20 percent of the soil mass. Secondary constituents
are capitalized and bold (i.e., SANDY CLAY, SILTY

constituent.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on the bulk samples 
collected to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Six laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by 
Ninyo & Moore on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 through 
B-7.
