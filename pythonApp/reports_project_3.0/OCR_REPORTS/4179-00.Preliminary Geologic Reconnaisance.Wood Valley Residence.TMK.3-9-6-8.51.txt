PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The subject parcel is a partially developed 34 acre residential site located in the
upper Wood Valley area of Pahala in the District of Ka u on the Island of Hawaii We
understand that the parcel may be further developed for use as a residence and small
farm The subject parcel is elongated and narrow in shape and extends upslope from the
existing North Road access The parcel encompasses the lower and middle slopes of the
northwestern valley wall of Wood Valley
Based on the topographic map provided to us the ground surface elevations
appear to range on the order of about 2 500 feet Mean Sea Level MSL to about
3 200 feet MSL Portions of the site had once supported sugar cane crop production
therefore some relict access trails exist that generally contour the slope as terraced
benches

The volume of Mauna Loa extending from the ocean floor is estimated to be on
the order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest volcanic mountain
on earth Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that Mauna Loa may
have been forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is actually composed of
two separate shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter having been almost
completely covered by Mauna Loa lavas to leave only a few localized surface
expressions visible today The Mauna Loa shield was built by volcanic eruptions along
two principal rift zones that extend southwestward and east northeastward from the
summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consists of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Ka u Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene
age and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional
ash deposit identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying
and interbedded within the rocks of the Ka u Volcanic Series The ash deposits
typically occur as kipukas isolated deposits with some significant accumulations
ranging up to about 30 feet in thickness
The project site and its vicinity are located on land that has been mapped as
Quaternary Age landslide deposits The landslide materials were deposited following
the earthquake of 1868 as a catastrophic mudflow resulting from a combination of a high
rainfall event and gravitational failure of basalt rock overlying wet volcanic ash deposits
on the volcanoes steep flank Stearns and Macdonald 1946 The landslide deposits
cover much of the ground surface of Wood Valley and generally consist of a mixture of
rapidly transported volcanic ash alluvial soil and blocks of basalt rock During our site
visit some relatively fresh landslide scars were observed on the distant very steep upper
mountain slopes located to the north and southwest of the subject site
Based on the available geologic map of the area the landslide deposits are
believed to be underlain by volcanic rock of the Ka u Volcanic Series The Ka u
Volcanic Series essentially consists of layered basalt rock representing multiple lava
flows erupted from the Mauna Loa Volcano

The project site is located in a topographic valley on the lower southeastern flank
of Mauna Loa Volcano Due to the relative elevation and local topographic effects the

In general the site slopes moderately at about a 20 percent grade down toward
the southeast The ground surface is typically obscured by vegetative growth and
presents a rolling topography with slight undulations Some poorly developed drainage
features were observed as broad dips in the ground topography Flowing streams
springs or deeply incised gullies were not observed within the estimated property limits
However some localized erosional features interpreted as formed by the convergence of
flowing water were observed These localized features were observed as having
exposed approximately 3 to 6 feet of the surficial soil profile The exposed near surface
soils consist of brown silty and sandy volcanic ash soils with some embedded cobbles
and boulders
Several areas of the site were observed as clearings of tree growth that harbor
generally flatter grades typically located at the crest of slight hills within the gently rolling
topography The areas were identified by the client as considerations for possible
building sites Soil samples collected from the site consist of brown to reddish brown
clayey silts with some sand and organic matter possessing relatively high moisture
contents
An existing one story wood framed structure was observed on the lower portion of
the parcel The structure was observed to have experienced significant structural distress
causing the building to lean in a down siope direction The cause of the distress appears
to be related to the surface construction of the pier footings with minimal embedment in
the subsurface The movement of the foundation and building structure may also have
been influenced by seismic earthquake activity

The occurrence of the landslide and mudflow of 1868 suggests that the area may
be susceptible to similar future events emanating from the steep upper slopes of Wood
Valley However because such a large scale event has occurred the valley may be
considered less susceptible to a repeat occurrence of that magnitude It is likely that the
soil deposits related to the event have been removed from the source and transported
therefore the valley may presently exist in a slightly more stable condition However it
should be noted that a repeat occurrence remains a possibility since the rainfall
characteristics and steep upper slopes of the valley remain in existence
Mauna Loa Volcano is classified as an active volcano with multiple summit
eruptions occurring within the recent past of about 50 to 100 years Most of the recent
lava flows have moved toward the northwest and west of the summit and have remained
on the upper slopes of the volcano However some recent lava flows have reached the
ocean in the area spanning between South Point and Hookena on the south and the west
side of Mauna Loa Volcano In addition some recent flows from Mauna Loa have
reached the Hilo area in the middle to late 1800s The direction of lava flow travel is
largely affected by the location of the eruption along the elongated rift summit Therefore
it is possible that future eruptions could affect the Wood Valley and Pahala area should
the source vent be situated in a proximal area to these locations
The Island of Hawaii experiences frequent earthquakes associated with the
underground movement of molten lava and isostatic adjustment of the mass of the island
of Hawaii Typically the earthquakes are centered beneath and around the eastern half
of the island including the offshore areas The earthquakes are usually small events
producing little if any noticeable damage and are generally more concentrated preceding
a volcanic eruption Some of these seismic events are larger in magnitude and produce
strong to severe ground shaking throughout the Island of Hawaii
Based on the available geologic map there are a number of small fault zones
mapped on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa Volcano including some located to the north
and south of Wood Valley The faults may have the potential to produce earthquakes
associated with ground movement along the fauit planes

GEOTECHNICAL CONSIDERATIONS
Based on our preliminary reconnaissance of the site and from a geotechnical
engineering point of view we believe that development of the site for low density
residential use may be feasible provided the potential risks associated with volcanic
activity mentioned above are considered to be acceptable
During the field reconnaissance several areas were indicated as potential building
sites Based on our visual observation of the ground surface conditions the locations
selected should not present overt difficulties for the construction of a one story residential
dwelling provided that proper drainage of the site by means of grading and building
foundation design is achieved
It was observed that the on site clayey silt MH soils have relatively high in situ
moisture content on the order of over 100 percent moisture content which generally
decreases the suitability of the silty volcanic ash soils for use as engineered fill If the
high moisture soils are used for construction the potential exists for shrinkage of the soil
mass to occur as the material dries out The shrinkage of the soils may induce some
settlement and foundation distress Furthermore the relatively high soil moisture content
and moist upland climate may present difficulties in achieving the required compaction of
the soils without significant air drying of the materials In consideration of the selection of
a building site the lower elevation locations may provide a somewhat drier and more
accessible environment to promote the workability of the on site soils during earthwork
operations
It should be noted that the selection of a building site should take into
consideration the natural drainage features and topography of the site to avoid low lying
potentially flood prone locations It is anticipated that site grading will be required to
provide for positive drainage of the site to channel surface water runoff away from the
building footprint and surrounding areas
