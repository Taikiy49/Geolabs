SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Generally, the subsurface conditions across the project site may be categorized 
into two zones.  Zone A generally is underlain by soft to medium stiff clayey soils between 
depths of about 2.5 and 9 feet below the existing pavement surface. Zone B generally is 
underlain by medium dense coralline sands and gravel extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 12.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. We encountered 
groundwater at depths ranging from about 4 to 6.8 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. The groundwater levels encountered at the project site generally correspond to 
about Elevations +0.5 to +1.4 feet MSL.  
Based on our laboratory CBR test results and the design guidelines of 
UFC 3-260-02 Pavement Design for Airfield, dated June 30, 2001, we believe design 
CBR values of 3.5 and 30 may be adopted for the pavement subgrade soils at Zone A 
and Zone B, respectively. In accordance with the design guidelines of UFC 3-260-02 and 
our evaluation, we recommend the pavement section at Zone A consists of 4 inches of 
AC on 8 inches of aggregate base course (CBR >= 80) over 10 inches of subbase 
(CBR >= 30) underlain by a layer of reinforcing geogrid (Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 or 
equivalent) on a layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) over the 
recompacted subgrade soils. The pavement section at Zone B should consist of 4 inches 
of AC on 8 inches of aggregate base course (CBR >= 80) over the recompacted subgrade 
soils. 
Based on the anticipated poor subsurface conditions and the subgrade 
stabilization measures recommended for Zone A, the aggregate base course may be 
compacted to 100 percent relative compaction. The top and bottom lifts (5 inches each) 
of the aggregate subbase should be compacted to 100 percent and a minimum of 95 
percent relative compaction, respectively. In addition, the subgrade soils for Zone A 
should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Based on the guidelines presented in UFC 3-260-02, the aggregate 
base course and subgrade soils at Zone B should be compacted to 100 percent and a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction, respectively. The text of this report should 
be referred to for detailed discussion and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
engineering analyses performed in support of the FY20 HI1422M Repair Helo Parking 
Area (Asphalt Pavement) Southeast of H101 project at Marine Corps Base Hawaii 
(MCBH) at Kaneohe Bay on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the pavement design of the helicopter parking area. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed project site is located to the southeast of Hangar H101 in the 
southwestern portion of the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based 
on the information provided, we understand it is desired to reconstruct the existing 
asphaltic concrete pavement section, designated as PAH-A09B, PAH-A11B, and 
PAH-A12B, for a total area of approximately 33,275 square yards. Subsequent to the 
completion of our field exploration program, two additional repair areas were identified 
near the southwestern side of the project site. As part of our scope of work for the project, 
we understand it is desired to drill or core through two presumed old concrete pads to 
determine the type and thickness of the pavement sections and underlying subgrade 
materials. 
Our experience in the project vicinity suggests that the subsurface conditions 
across the project area likely will consist of surface fills placed over lagoonal deposits and 
coralline deposits at relatively shallow depths. We envision groundwater levels likely will 
be encountered at shallow depths of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. 
Based on the design guidelines outlined in the Department of Defense (DoD) 
Unified Facilities Criteria (UFC) 3-260-02 (“Pavement Design for Airfields”), dated

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis for design of this project are the subsurface 
materials encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials 
encountered, and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. 
Therefore, the following subsections provide a description of the geologic setting of the 
project site, the surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site, and a 
discussion on the items needed for pavement design, such as existing pavement 
thickness, subgrade soil conditions, etc.  
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried 
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. The Koolau Volcano reached the 
end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The project is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and is composed of 
basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These rocks 
generally are characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded 
thin clinker layers. In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for 
the last 1 to 2 million years. The weathering process has formed a surface mantle of 
residual soils, which grade to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is 
mainly composed of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic 
rocks. Saprolite grades to basaltic rock with increasing depth.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling fourteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 14, extending to depths 
of about 11.5 to 12.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Fourteen bulk samples,

underlain by variable materials consisting of fills, lagoonal deposits, and coralline detritus 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 12.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.

3.2.2 
Fill and Backfill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site granular soils should be suitable for use as fill 
materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and are 
screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension. However, it 
should be noted that the on-site clayey soils, especially at Zone A, may consist of 
soft and wet soils due to shallow groundwater conditions at the project site. The 
soft and wet  clayey soils should not be used as a source of fill or backfill materials.  
Imported materials should consist of structural fill material, such as crushed coral 
or basalt. The structural fill should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should have a CBR 
value of 30 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. The material also should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. Imported fill materials should be 
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project 
site for the intended use.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
fourteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 14, extending to depths of about 
11.5 to 12.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, two shallow borings, 
designated as C-1 and C-2, were cored/drilled at the two presumed old concrete pads to 
determine the existing pavement sections and underlying subgrade materials. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-16. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits to aid in soil classifications. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Thirteen Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plates B-2 through B-4. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 
and B-6. 
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D2435) was performed on a sample 
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
material encountered. Consolidation test results are presented on Plate B-7. 
Twenty-four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on composite bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-8 through 
B-31. 
Six Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on 
composite bulk samples obtained to evaluate the relationship between the moisture 
content and the dry density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-32 through B-37. 
Two sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity, 
(ASTM G57) were performed by Geolabs, Inc., and Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and 
Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized 
on Plate B-38.
