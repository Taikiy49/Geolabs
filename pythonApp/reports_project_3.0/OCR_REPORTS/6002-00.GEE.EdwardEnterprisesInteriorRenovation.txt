Our borings indicated that the Portland cement concrete slab may range in
thickness from about 7 to 12.3 inches. The subsurface conditions below the concrete

2,500 psf, respectively. These bearing values are for dead-plus-live loads and may be

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Edward Enterprises — Interior Renovation, 240 Puuhale Road project
in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and recommendations resulting from our
field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These recommendations
are intended for the design of foundations and site preparation only. The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The interior renovation project of an existing warehouse building is located at
240 Puuhale Road in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The interior renovations
are planned on the southwest end (last bay) of the street level warehouse building. We |
understand that the planned interior renovations will be conducted to accommodate
printing equipment within the warehouse.
Based on the information provided, two large printing presses are planned to be
housed within the warehouse. Due to the size of the printing presses, the large printing
presses will have to be supported on new foundations.
In addition, a new mezzanine floor will be constructed to provide additional office
space. The new mezzanine floor is planned to the east of the large printing presses.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1. Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the extinct
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the
eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials. The project site is situated in an area where
the subsurface soil deposits are dominated by calcareous reef deposits. Land
development and reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project
area to its present form.

2.3. Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 19.5 and 21.0 feet
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 2.
Our borings generally encountered Portland cement concrete slab about 7 to
12.3 inches thick. Below the Portland cement concrete, fills were encountered to
depths of about 4 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface. The fills consisted of
dense to very dense silty and clayey gravel and sand. Alluvium, which consisted of stiff
silt and clay was encountered below the fills and extended to depths of about 5 to 8 feet
below the existing ground surface. Below the alluvium, coral formation was
encountered to the maximum depths explored of about 19.5 to 21 feet below the
existing ground surface.
We encountered groundwater in the borings during our field exploration at a
depth of approximately 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. It should be noted
that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal precipitation, storm
surge conditions, and other factors.
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Borings in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples
obtained during our field exploration, and the test results are presented in Appendix B.

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our borings generally encountered dense to very dense fills below the concrete
slab-on-grade. The fills extended to depths of about 4 to 7 feet below the existing
ground surface. A 1-foot thick layer of stiff alluvium was encountered below the fills.
Below the alluvium, coral formation was encountered to the maximum depths explored.
We encountered groundwater in the borings during our field exploration at a depth of
approximately 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we
recommend supporting the proposed new printing presses and mezzanine floor on mat
foundations and spread/continuous footings, respectively.
Since the printing presses will generate vibration, we recommend placing a
minimum 12-inch thick layer of aggregate subbase (compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction) below the mat foundation to provide uniform support.
The spread and/or continuous footings supporting the mezzanine floor may be placed
directly on the recompacted in-situ fills.
Detailed discussion and recommendations for the design of foundations, site
preparation, and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following
sections herein.
3.1 Printing Press Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, we recommend
using mat foundations to support the proposed new printing presses. Since the printing
presses will generate vibrations during operation, we recommend placing a minimum
12-inch thick layer of aggregate subbase course below the mat foundation to provide
uniform support. The aggregate subbase course should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.
Hawaii * California
SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may
be used in the design of footings bearing on the compacted aggregate subbase course.
This bearing value is dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) when
considering transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
We recommend embedding the foundations a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade. Foundations next to utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below an imaginary 45-degree plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
appreciable foundation settlement.
If the mat foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundation to be on the order
of about 0.5 inch or less. Differential settlements between the center and corner of the
mat foundation supported on similar materials should be on the order of about
0.25 inch. .
Lateral loads acting on the printing press structures may be resisted by friction
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A
coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used. Resistance due to passive earth pressure
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per
foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted.
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected.
A Geolabs representative should monitor the footing excavations prior to placing
the reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the
required embedment depths.

3.2 Mezzanine Foundations
We recommend using a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or
continuous footings to support the proposed new mezzanine floor. An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 2,500 psf may be used in the design of footings bearing on the
recompacted in-situ fills. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be
increased by one-third (1/3) when considering transient loads, such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces.
We recommend embedding the footings 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade. Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below an imaginary 45-degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench, or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement. —
If the shallow foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of footings to be on the order of
about 1 inch or less. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on
similar materials should be on the order of about 0.5 inch.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of
friction of 0.30 may be used. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pcf. This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the
passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.
A Geolabs representative should monitor the footing excavations prior to placing
the reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the
required embedment depths.

3.3 Site Preparation .
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared thoroughly. Construction debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Materials generated from
the demolition should also be removed and disposed properly off-site. The excavated
on-site soils encountered in the cut areas may be stockpiled and re-used as a source of
general fill materials, provided they meet general fill requirements presented later in this
section. Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over-excavated
to expose firm material. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed
properly off-site, and may not be used for fill materials. The resulting excavation should
then be backfilled with engineered fills. Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over-excavation and fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation quantities.
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with general fill material moisture-conditioned
to above optimum moisture content, placed in 6 to 8-inch level loose lifts, and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Utilities to be abandoned
in-place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete
under low pressure.
After clearing and demolition, the areas designated to receive fills should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the
water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
The on-site soils may be used as general fill materials provided that they are free of
vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum
dimension. Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should meet the
material requirements as specified in Section 703.17 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications

information obtained from our field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions between

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of approximately 19.5 to
21.0 feet below the existing ground surface. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools to drill the boreholes. The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist and engineer, who monitored the drilling
operations on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples to aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1.
Eight Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plates B-2 and B-3.
