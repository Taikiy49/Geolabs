FIELD EXPLORATION
We explored the subsurface conditions for earthwork related considerations by
drilling and sampling eight test borings designated as Boring Nos 201 through 208 In
general the borings drilled extended to depths of about 27 to 46 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
rotary wash coring tools The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 3 1 through 3 4
Materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate 4 Graphic representations of
the materials encountered and water levels observed in the borings drilled are provided on
the attached Logs of Borings Plates 5 through 12
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTMD 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered were obtained using diamond core
drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for
Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut

into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
The rippability or excavation characteristics of a rock mass is a function of the
relative hardness of the rock its relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics
Dense basalt formations with high RQD values would be very difficult to rip and probably
would require more arduous methods of excavation
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
As indicated we explored the subsurface conditions along the new roadway by
drilling and sampling eight borings designated as Boring Nos 201 through 208 extending
to depths ranging from about 27 to 46 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 4
The borings generally encountered a surface soil layer about 1 to 10 5 feet thick
consisting of fill and or residual soils overlying a basalt formation with clinker layers
extending to about 46 5 feet the maximum explored depth In one of the borings a cinder
deposit was encountered below the basalt formation

The fill materials generally consisted of stiff to hard clayey silts with gravel and
cobbles and dense cobbles and gravel The residual soil layer generally was composed of
very stiff clayey silts The basalt formation was composed of highly weathered to relatively
unweathered basalt with clinker layers within the formation
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of the field
exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels may vary with seasonal rainfall
time of year and other factors

The site grading work generally will consist of cuts up to about 35 feet deep and fills
up to about 25 feet thick Several of the large cut areas are on the northern slopes near
the Future Keawe Street Extension at the northern end of the Honoapiilani Highway
between Kahoma Stream Bridge and Lahainaluna Road Bridge and for excavation of the
Lahainaluna Road Grade Separation Structure Items of site grading that are addressed in

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill and or
future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm and or dense materials and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
Prior to filling the existing ground exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches where possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks
are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend thoroughly moistening the soil
to close the cracks or preparing the subgrade again Saturation and subsequent yielding of

Where scarification of the subgrade is not possible especially subgrades exposing

with compacted fill materials The depth of over excavation should extend until dense
underlying materials are exposed and should be evaluated by our representative in the
field
Fills and Backfills
In general we anticipate the excavations will likely encounter fill materials
residual soils clinker and basalt rock with the majority of the excavation encountering
the underlying basalt rock formation and clinker The excavated basalt rock materials
and clinker may be used as a source of general fill and backfill materials within the
upper 2 feet of the embankment below the finished subgrade provided that the material
contains rock fragments no greater than 6 inches and contains between 10 and
30 percent passing the No 200 sieve
Excavated on site materials including the fill materials and residual soils
consisting of boulders less than 3 feet in maximum dimension may be used as rock fill
provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 2 feet below the finished
subgrades In general the placement of boulders in fills should be performed in
accordance with the procedures recommended in the following Boulder Disposal
subsection
Select granular fill materials will be required for backfill within the retaining wall
mechanically stabilized earth retaining wall backfill below bridge foundations and
other selected areas Select granular fill materials should be well graded from coarse to
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more with a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less and contains
between 10 to 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The fill material should
also be free of vegetation and deleterious materials

Boulder Disposal
Boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less
than 3 feet in largest dimension encountered during site grading may be used in rock
fills provided that the following recommendations are followed The boulder disposal
recommendations also apply to cobble sized materials materials larger than 6 inches in
maximum dimension but less than 12 inches in largest dimension
1 Boulders less than 3 feet in largest dimension may be utilized as rock fills
provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 2 feet below the
finished subgrades Boulders larger than 3 feet in maximum dimension
should not be used as rock fill unless reduced in size
2 Boulders greater than 12 inches in largest dimension should not be
exposed at the fill slope face Boulder placement should be set back a
lateral distance of at least 6 feet from the edge of the fill slope face
3 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the proposed
utility line alignment roadways and within the depths of the proposed
utility lines to reduce the potential for encountering the boulders during
excavation for the utility trenches
Compaction of the boulders in the rock fills should be performed in accordance with
the recommendations presented in the following Fill Placement and Compaction
Requirements subsection It should be noted that disposal of boulders in the general fill
material will require that the entire fill mass be compacted and properly choked off at the
surface and each layer of rock fill to reduce the potential for migration of fines through the
voids between boulders
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should consist of
crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Roads Bridges and Public Works Construction dated 2006
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement of
the fills within 3 feet of the finished grades should be increased to at least 95 percent
relative compaction
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 3 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be by a large bulldozer to facilitate seating of the

rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks
traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during
placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction
should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes to be made to
achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading by the
performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests Subsequent layers of
rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of
smaller sized boulder materials or cobbles to fill in the voids between the boulders of
the preceding rock fill layer
Aggregate base course aggregate subbase and select granular fill materials
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during site
grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the
proper moisture content Where compaction is less than required additional compactive
effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the
specified compaction
Conventional compaction testing generally is not practical in fills composed of
rock fragments and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes by a specific compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a Geolabs representative Based on this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose basalt rock formation In general we believe that
cut slopes into the basalt rock formation may be excavated at an inclination of
one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter
Fill slopes constructed of the processed on site materials should be designed
with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter For fill slopes greater than 30 feet in vertical
height we recommend providing slopes at maximum 30 foot vertical height intervals
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V such as at the gullies should be keyed and

benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The
filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should be constructed by overfilling and
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face
Track rolling of slopes in excess of 5 feet in height is not acceptable In the event that
over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching requirements should be
implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided
as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes
Excavation
Based on our field exploration the project site generally is underlain by hard basalt
rock formation near the ground surface Therefore the contractor will likely encounter
some difficult excavation conditions during construction where the hard basalt rock
formation is encountered
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and earthwork recommendations submitted herein are based in part
upon information obtained from the field borings and site reconnaissance Variations of the
subsurface conditions between and beyond the field borings may occur and the nature
and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations
presented in this letter report
The locations of the field borings indicated herein are approximate having been
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the topographic plans downloaded
from Wilson Okamoto Corporation ftp site on Elevations of the borings were
interpolated based on the contours and spot elevations shown on the topographic survey
maps The locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered accurate
only to the degree implied by the methods used
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict
the approximate boundaries between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual
transition Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or presented in the text of this report These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall perched groundwater
conditions groundwater withdrawal and other factors
