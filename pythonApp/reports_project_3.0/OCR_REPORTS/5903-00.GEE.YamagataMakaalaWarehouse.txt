1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Yamagata Makaala Warehouse project in District of South
Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade retaining structures site grading and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

A new cold storage warehouse development identified as the
Yamagata Makaala Warehouse is planned in the existing industrial area along West
Makaala Street in the town of Hilo in the District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii

2 1 Regional Geology
Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area of
approximately 10 360 square kilometers 4 000 square miles The island was formed
by the activity of the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea
activity during recent geologic times Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both still active
The site is situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa on rocks of the
Prehistoric Member of the Kau Volcanic Series In the area of the site these rocks are
typified by thick a a basalt lava flows with a surface of coarse spiny fragmental clinker
generally overlain by a thin mantle of mucky organic soil
The U S Department of Agriculture classified the surface soils in the area as
Papai Extremely Stony Muck rPAE These soils are extremely thin organic soils
developed over fragmental a a lavas They are very well drained Runoff is slow to
medium and the erosion hazard is slight to moderate At the site previous grading work
appears to have removed the topsoil layer

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the preliminary grading and drainage plan provided for the preparation
of this report we understand that the site grading will consist of very thin cuts and fills of
about 1 to 2 feet from the existing grade to level the site for the proposed warehouse
and pavement area
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration we believe that shallow
spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the new building structure at
the site An allowable bearing pressure of 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of foundations bearing on the compacted subgrade
The bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should
be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavated area
may be backfilled with new fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on competent
materials at a greater depth
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of
the slope Footings adjacent to planned or existing retaining walls should be
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the
planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent below grade utility lines or structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement

Lateral loads acting on the building structure may be resisted by friction
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the dense on site soils

3 2 Foundation Probing
Voids and or cavities were not encountered in the borings However based on
our experience in the area there is a potential for presence of lava tubes blisters and or
cavities in the basalt formations In order to reduce the potential for structural damage
due to collapse of voids cavities a probing and grouting program is often conducted
below structures However due to the relatively light structure planned we believe that
the potential for loss of foundation support resulting form collapse of the cavities below
the foundation is relatively small and the probing and grouting program may be omitted
if this small risk is tolerable However if it is desired to further reduce the potential for
loss of foundation support then a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations should be conducted during construction

After clearing and grubbing subgrades finished subgrades and areas designated

minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry

retaining wall should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a
semi restrained wall condition For a semi restrained wall condition the forces due

foot of wall for level backfill conditions

3 8 Underground Utility Lines
We anticipate that the underground utilities for the proposed development will
primarily consist of water drainage and sewer Good construction practices should be
utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities The
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for excavation
subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the trench excavation

In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used under the pipes for support of the utility
lines The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining granular backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
pipe damage from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular
material as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes We
do not recommend the use of clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around utility
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill
materials with a maximum particle size of 6 inches or less The backfill material should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper
3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less
than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used
to compact the backfill materials at the project site Compaction efforts by water
tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed at the site

3 9 Leach Field Design
We conducted a falling head percolation test in one of the borings at a depth of
about 5 feet below the existing ground surface This test indicated that the shallow
subsurface permeability at the site is extremely rapid i e the time required for one inch
of water to percolate from the boring was considerably less than one minute Generally
this rate is considered to be too rapid and unsuitable for a leach field However if the
bottom of the leach field trench is over excavated and replaced with 3 feet of soil such
as medium to fine sand wastewater effluent could be applied at about 1 gallon per day
per square foot of leach field trench bottom
3 10 Drainage
As previously indicated proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations and slabs Therefore the finished grades outside the structure should be
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding
It is also advised to install gutter systems around the building and to divert the discharge
away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided
The foundation excavations should be backfilled properly against the walls or
slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water
infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of
approximately 11 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We used a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with rotary coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
