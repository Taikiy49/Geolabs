PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at the base of Puu o Hulu Kai and Puu o Hulu Uka in
the Waianae District on the leeward coast on the island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed
to subdivide and develop approximately 182 acres of the site for the construction of
approximately 1000 single family and duplex units in both the affordable and market price
ranges
We understand that it is desired to develop the site by grading to provide sloping
lots The proposed grades for the sloping lots would have slope inclinations similar to the
existing ground surface Based on this preliminary grading concept it appears that fills
on the order of up to about 14 feet in vertical height and cuts of up to about 10 feet in
depth are planned We anticipate that the future residences would be constructed as split
level homes supported by post and beam foundations or other feasible construction
techniques

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built up mainly by two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae
and Koolau Waianae which forms the west side of the island is the older of the two and
was built during the Pliocene Epoch by the extrusion of the lavas of the Waianae Volcanic
Series
The rocks of the Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into Lower Middle and
Upper Members The lavas of the Lower Member consist largely of thinly bedded
pahoehoe The Middle Member basalts closely resemble thcse of the Lower Member
except that the Middle Member contains more a a flows The Upper Member consists
largely of massive a a flows
Erosion has deeply dissected Waianae and removed most of the western slope of
the shield forming the Waianae Range Following a great amount of erosion large
accumulations of alluvium and colluvium were deposited in the deep amphitheater headed
valleys of Lualualei and Waianae
Puu o Hulu Kai and Puu o Hulu Uka are two peaks on an erosional remnant ridge
from the Waianae Range These promontories are composed of Lower and Middle
Member basalts with numerous dike intrusions

During the Late Pleistocene Epoch emerged coral reefs were formed when the sea
stood about 100 feet higher than it does at present These deposits of coral limestone
extend approximately two 2 miles inland and underlie the project site

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Deposits of expansive clay soils adobe are frequently found at the bases of ridges
and steep slopes in Hawaii These adobe clay soils have low shear strength values and
are often subject to down slope creep movement if the soils become saturated or have
surcharge loads placed on them Such down slope creep movement has resulted in
significant property damages at locations such as Aina Haina Palolo Manoa and
Kuliouou At all of these sites the subdivision developments were underlain by thick
deposits of adobe clay soils on sloping terrain and the adobe clay soils were left in place
during the development of the site
At the Maili Kai project site our field exploration indicated that the deposits of
adobe clay soils are generally relatively thin Based on the findings from our analyses of
the data from the exploration and laboratory testing removal and replacement of the
adobe clay soils would reduce the potential of down slope creep movement Since the
layer of adobe clay is relatively thin and relatively large quantities of coral materials are
available in the lower portions of the site we believe that it is economically feasible to
remove the adobe clay soils from the development area and grade the site with select
material generated from the excavation of the coral materials
Therefore it is our opinion that development of the entire proposed residential
subdivision area up to Elevation 200 feet MSL is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view provided that the recommendations presented in this report are
incorporated into the design of the project Due to the expansive nature of the surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2716 10 Page 10
soils the relatively low shear strengths of the adobe clay soils and the potential for slope
movement it is essential that the site preparation and grading recommendations
presented herein be closely followed
Grading Design
In the early part of our exploration and analyses we had felt that it would be
possible to remove and replace only part of the adobe clay soils to obtain stable slopes
In our Preliminary Findings and Recommendations letter dated we
presented a grading scheme which involved the removal and replacement of the clay soils
down to a slope of ten horizontal to one vertical 10H 1V and subsequent construction
of embankment fill slopes over the remaining adobe clay soils This scheme was based
on the assumption that the lots would have slopes of about 20 to 25 percent slope ratio
to limit the amount of fill on the relatively steep terrain
At this time we understand that the grading concept consists of sloping lot pads
The maximum height of cuts is up to about 10 feet with maximum fills of up to about 14
feet Due to the high shrink swell potential and low shear strength characteristics of the
adobe clay our analyses indicate that there is a strong potential for future movement of
the adobe clay soils when wetted and subjected to surcharge loading from the overlying
fills Therefore we strongly recommend the use of the following grading concept
Grading Concept
The current preliminary grading concept for the southern portion of the site is to
grade the area to create sloping residential lots with maximum fills on the order of
up to 14 feet in height Maximum cuts of up to 10 feet may also be required at
some locations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2716 10 Page 11
Our field exploration indicates that the expansive adobe clay soils are present at
the surface of most of the southern portion of the site The adobe clay soils
generally form a relatively thin mantle of approximately three 3 to four feet 4
over more competent materials In a few areas the adobe clay soils may be as
thick as 16 feet
Due to the shrink swell potential and the potential for long term down slope creep
movement of the wetted adobe soils under the surcharge loads from the fills
planned we strongly recommend the removal of the adobe clay soils and
replacement with on site select materials which would reduce the potential of both
structural distresses from the swelling of the expansive soils and long term down
slope creep movement of the clay soils
Our exploration indicates that the northern portion of the site is underlain at shallow
depths by competent coral formation and coralline deposits This material is non
expansive has good strength characteristics and if excavated and properly
processed could meet the requirements for Select Borrow for Subbase Course as
stipulated in Section 16 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared of trees vegetation debris rubbish and other deleterious materials These
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
In areas to receive fill the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of six

moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry
density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 Soft or
yielding areas should be over excavated and stabilized by backfilling with select
material placed in ten inch 10 thick loose lifts compacted to 90 percent of its
maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method
D 1557 91

Fill Material and Placement
The adobe clay soils to be removed from the upper slopes and placed in the lower
level area of the site generally have high swelling potential depending on the initial
compaction density and molding moisture content prior to wetting In order to
reduce the swelling potential of the soil the adobe fill material should be placed at
moisture contents above the optimum moisture content The adobe clay soils
should be placed a minimum of three feet 3 below the finished subgrade and
only on the lower level portion of the project site northern portion
Materials used for all general site embankment filling and the adobe clay capping
fills placed within the top three feet 3 of finisned subgrade should be non
expansive select material generally less than three inches 3 in maximum
dimension and should have a plasticity index not exceeding 20 as determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 The on site coralline materials
if properly processed after excavation are considered suitable for this purpose
Fill material should be placed in level lifts with a maximum loose thickness of ten
inches 10 and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry
density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 Each
layer should be spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain uniformity of the
material and water content Additional fill material should not be placed on any fill
layer which has not been properly compacted
If additional off site borrow soil is required it should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii
to evaluate its suitability as use for select fill prior to its delivery to the project site

Boulder Fills
Many surface boulders were noted over most of the sloping hillsides In addition
large quantities of boulders will be generated during the removal of the adobe
soils Boulders may be used in deep fills provided that the following
recommendations are followed
1 Boulders must not be nested together They should be placed such that
compaction equipment would be able to compact around them
2 Large boulders should not be used within five feet 5 from the finish
subgrade
3 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the proposed
utility alignments and proposed depths to prevent difficulty in the later
excavation of the utility trenches
Boulders may also be used for upper slope buttress fills For any remaining
boulders provision should be made to properly dispose of them off site
Based on the assumption that the surface adobe clay soils will be removed
completely from slope areas and that properly constructed fill slopes will consist
of select material cut and fill slopes up to two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V
may be used for the project grading design

W O 2716 10 Page 16

horizontal to one vertical may be used in stiff colluvium and up to one
horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V in weathered basalt formation depending on its
thickness Adobe soils encountered at the top of the cut slope should either be
sloped back to a gentle cut slope of about four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V

Therefore we anticipate that post and beam foundations will be used to support the
proposed residences It is our opinion that shallow spread or strip footing foundations
bearing on the on site coralline soils or bearing on fill placed and compacted in
accordance with our recommendations may be used to support the proposed houses

Proper footing embedment will also be necessary to reduce the potential

Boulder Protection
The upper hillside lots may be subjected to boulders rolling down the hillside
Falling boulders is a general phenomenon occurring along all ridge slopes on Oahu
Various methods of boulder barriers or catchments may be used to reduce the hazard
of falling boulders For the project site there are various attempts or efforts which may
be considered to reduce the impact of falling boulders into the development area It is
our opinion that the following may be considered
1 A boulder catchment area about 10 to 15 feet wide with a fence or wall
could be utilized near the property boundary to catch rolling boulders An
interceptor ditch along the upper boundaries of the site could be used in
this capacity
2 Boulder sweeps along the upper boundaries of the site could be performed
periodically to identify and remove loose boulders that appear likely to roll
down the slopes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2716 10 Page 18
3 The natural trees and vegetation along the upper portions of the site should
be retained to act as a natural barrier against the boulders and to reduce
erosion
4 Where practicable structures should be set back away from the toe of the
hillside slope to create an additional boulder catchment area
It must be pointed out that whatever methods or combinations of efforts used
there is still an inherent risk of damage due to falling boulders for hillside lots The state
of geotechnical engineering practice is not at the point that an absolute solution is
available for this concern It is our opinion that no matter what actions or precautions
are taken there is always the risk of boulders rolling down the hill if not from areas near
the house from areas higher up on the slopes Whatever measure is chosen it only
reduces the risk We would like to point out that no guarantee against future falling
boulders is possible due to changes in soil and rock conditions with time weathering
seepage and erosion earthquake and construction vibration and improvements and
construction activities at and around the site
The potential purchasers or owners of these hillside lots should be made aware
of the aforementioned limitations and cautioned against future improvements in the upper
areas of the lots which may disturb the stability of the existing boulders

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling 16 borings to depths
of 4 to 41 feet below the existing ground surface utilizing truck mounted drilling
equipment and by excavating and sampling 27 test pits with a track mounted backhoe
to depths of 2 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the borings and test pits are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 4
The soils were classified by visual and textural examination in the field by our
geologist who monitored the drilling operations and by our field engineer who monitored
the test pit excavations on a near continuous basis The classifications were made in
general accordance with the Unified Soil Classification System The soils encountered are
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving either 2 inch O D standard
split spoon sampler or a 3 inch O D split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
a distance of 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches
of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Soil samples were obtained from the walls of the test pit excavations and by
pushing a 3 inch O D shelby tube sampler with the bottom of the backhoe bucket

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs and Test Pit Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to assess the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs summarized on Plates
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to evaluate the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in
the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering
properties The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate B 6
Proctor ASTM D 1557 78 tests and California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883 87
tests were performed on selected samples as an aid to evaluating their embankment fill
and pavement support characteristics The results of these tests are summarized on
GEOLABS HAWAII
Appendix B Continued Page 2
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to evaluate the angle
of internal friction and the coefficient of cohesion in a soil sample These data are used
in slope stability calculations and other engineering calculations The results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 11 through B 19
