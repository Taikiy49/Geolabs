1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for five slope locations along Likelike Highway located between Nalanieha Street and the
Wilson Tunnel in the District of Koolaupoko on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Piate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for design associated with slope stabilization which includes
the design of retaining wall foundations mechanically stabilized earth slopes GRP slopes
excavation and backfill placement only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the late Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the glaciers
melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to
above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials
caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered the
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes consisting of
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered the natural condition
of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of the

Retaining Wall Backfill
Backfill behind the retaining wall should consist of select granular fill material
compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 91
Over compaction of the backfill should be avoided The upper 3 feet of existing clay
soil on the slope is wet and soft and therefore should be removed prior to backfilling
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Imported fill should consist of non expansive select granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and
should contain between 10 and 25 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883

Mechanically Stabilized Earth Slopes
Mechanically stabilized earth slopes generally utilize polymer grid structures
geogrids with high tensile strength These systems generally provide a

cost effective solution to slope stability problems which may include the following
insufficient right of way high surcharge loads poor quality fills high seismic forces
steep slopes or difficult landslide repairs When reinforced slope geogrids are
placed in soil the grid geometry interlocks with adjacent soil creating a soil geogrid
composite with greatly enhanced engineering properties Different grid
configurations are available to provide optimum soil grid interaction for a range of
soil types and slope reinforcement applications Reinforced slope geotextiles work
in a similar manner to that of reinforced slope geogrids The construction
methodology is briefly described as follows
As the slope is constructed horizontal layers of reinforced slope geogrids or
geotextiles are placed in the compacted fill at predetermined levels The lengths of
the reinforced slope geogrid or geotextile layers are designed to anchor potential
failure zones into stable interior sections of the embankment or hillside As forces
develop within a soil mass the high modulus reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles
are immediately pulled into tension The reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles
transfer this tensile force from the unstable soil back into less stressed portions of the
slope and stability is thus maintained

Gabion Wall Structure
Gabion walls are cellular structures formed of rectangular cages made of
zinc coated steel wire mesh filled with rocks of the proper size and mechanical
characteristics The individual units are firmly tied to each other with zinc coated
wires to form a monolithic structure The wire mesh is hexagonal and double
twisted and the openings are about 3 to 4 inches in size

contact both horizontal and vertical Once the filling is completed the gabion is
closed by lacing the lid along all edges and the internal cell diaphragms

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling eight
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 and 4A to depths ranging from about 15 to

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the maierials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Aiterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on a selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Seven direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate their peak and residual shear strength characteristics The test results and
shear stress versus normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 2 through B 9
Three residual direct shear tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their residual shear strength characteristics The test results and shear stress
versus normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 10 through B 12
