In general accordance with our fee proposal dated March 4th we have conducted
a geotechnical engineering exploration of the distressed area observed at the service
entrance of the Ewa Villages Golf Course Clubhouse in the District of Ewa on the island
of Oahu Hawaii A meeting was held on April 17th with your office to discuss the findings
from our field exploration and laboratory testing and to present our geotechnical
engineering recommendations derived from our evaluation of the subject project This
letter report serves to summarize our meeting discussions and provide additional
information pertaining to the remedial work for the subject project
PROJECT BACKGROUND
The project site is located at the Ewa Villages Golf Course Clubhouse in the
District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on the information
provided the service entrance area of the clubhouse was generaily underlain by
approximately 15 feet of fill materials placed by Oahu Construction Company during mass
grading of the area Pacific Rim Consulting and Inspection Corporation PRCIC was
contracted by Oahu Construction Company to perform the field compaction testing during

We understand that distress of the service entrance area located on the east

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the service area and the clubhouse parking lot
were explored by drilling ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 6B and
7 through 9 extending to depths of about 7 to 26 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Continuous sampling was performed during our field exploration to obtain a
detailed subsoil profile for evaluation purposes In general our borings at the service
entrance area encountered medium stiff silty clay and clayey silt soils throughout the
depths explored In general the silty clay and clayey silt soils encountered were
primarily fill materials placed during construction Subsurface water was encountered in
some of the drilled borings at depths ranging from about 12 to 16 feet below the
existing ground surface Stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts were encountered in
the borings drilled in the parking lot area which does not appear to have been
subjected to the subsoil saturation by the irrigation process In addition groundwater
was not encountered in the drilled borings at the parking lot area at the time of our field
exploration
DISCUSSIONS
Based on the findings of our field exploration and laboratory tests we believe
that several conditions had to be present in order for the significant distress observed at
the service entrance area to occur These conditions include the following
presence of clayey and silty soils which are susceptible to significant
volume changes when subjected to moisture fluctuations
presence of dry soils which would readily absorb significant amounts of
excess water resulting in significant volume changes

W O 2819 50 C Page 3
presence of a water source to infiltrate into the subsurface and to saturate
the subsoils and
e presence of poorly compacted fill soils which would settle readily upon
saturation
It is apparent that the on site clayey and silty soils may be classified as
moderately to highly expansive soils Therefore the on site soils are susceptible to
significant volume changes when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content
In order for the subsoils to undergo the fluctuations in the moisture contents we
believe that the subsoils had to be initially dry followed by saturation of the subsoils
Based on our review of the compaction test records performed by PRCIC the clayey
and silty fill soils placed at the site may be considered to be dry based on the moisture
contents of the fills taken during fill placement
Infiltration of water into the subsoils was also evident at the site especially since
the distress at the service entrance area was noted shortly after the activation of the
automatic water sprinkler system In addition the moisture contents of the soil samples
retrieved from our borings drilled in the area also indicate high moisture contents of the
fill soils in areas where the automatic sprinkler system was activated
Based on our research of documents field exploration and laboratory testing
the first three conditions cited above were evident at the site However we can only
speculate and surmise that some of the fills may have been placed with inadequate
compaction The following subsections discuss the presence of dry soils and poorly
compacted fill soils in further detail
Lack of Moisture Conditioning
Based on our review of the available limited compaction data performed by
PRCIC for Oahu Construction Company during mass grading of the service entrance
area it appears that the fill materials were not moisture conditioned to the degree
specified in the Earthwork specifications for the subject project According to Section
02210 Paragraph 3 01 Item D of the project specifications Each layer of soils shall
be brought to moisture content sufficiently close to optimum moisture to permit the
required degree of compaction the optimum moisture being determined by
ASTM D1557 If the soils moisture content is too high or too low it shall be adjusted by
suitable means before placing

As shown on the attached compaction test data sheet Plate 6 the compaction
test results performed by PRCIC for Oahu Construction Company indicated that the
moisture contents of the fill soils at the time of placement are about 7 to 10 percent dry
of the optimum moisture content Our review of other compaction data performed by
PRCIC for the Ewa Villages Revitalization project also suggests similar practice by the
contractor during the fill placement operations It appears that the mass grading
contractor Oahu Construction Company did not moisture condition the fill soils as
required in the project specifications We believe that the relatively low moisture
contents of the fill soils in relation to the optimum moisture content have contributed to
the significant volume changes in the fill soils when saturated resulting in appreciable
ground subsidence
Inadequate Compaction
Our review of the limited compaction data provided to us suggested that the fill
soils were compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction based on a
modified proctor of 106 3 pounds per cubic foot p c f The modified proctor of
106 3 p c f was used by PRCIC to determine the degree of compaction of the fill
materials in the service entrance area during mass grading We also performed a
modified proctor in accordance with ASTM D 1557 on the fill soils obtained from the
drilled borings in the service entrance area The modified proctor that we performed on
the fill soils at the service entrance area yielded a higher maximum dry density of
114 p c f The modified proctor used by PRCIC to determine the relative compaction of
the fill soils at the service entrance area does not appear to be representative of the
actual fill soils used in this area Therefore compaction test results that indicated
90 percent relative compaction by PRCIC would only translate to approximately
84 percent relative compaction based on the modified proctor performed on the actual
fill soils at the service entrance area Therefore it is possible that some of the fills
placed test results indicate 90 to 96 percent relative compaction by PRCIC would be
considered inadequately compacted in accordance with the specified requirements
It should be noted that we only located six field compaction tests performed by
PRCIC in the service entrance area in the documents we reviewed All six field density
tests were taken at an elevation of about 1 foot below the finished subgrade level for
the approximately 15 feet of fill placed in this area We believe that further review of
any additional field compaction test data performed at the clubhouse area should be
conducted to provide further details on this aspect If only six field compaction tests
were taken for the approximately 15 feet of fill in this area it is our opinion that the
quality control of the fill placement in the service entrance area appears to be lacking
Based on our laboratory simulation of the field conditions if the on site silty clay
and clayey silt fill soils were compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction at or
near the optimum moisture content the fill soils do not appear to result in significant
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 50 C Page 5
ground subsidence when subjected to saturation However if the on site fill soils were
inadequately compacted about 80 percent relative compaction and placed at a
moisture content significantly dry of the optimum moisture the fill soils can be
susceptible to significant ground settlements when wetted
Relatively low blow counts were observed in the borings drilled in the service
entrance area The relatively low blow counts could suggest that the fill soils were
inadequately compacted during the fill placement operations or that the subsoils were
softened due to saturation from the landscape irrigation However based on the
laboratory simulation of the field conditions it is likely that inadequately compacted fill
soils have contributed to the distress observed at the service entrance area
PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our evaluation we believe that landscape irrigation from the automatic
sprinkler system has infiltrated into the clayey subsoils below the asphalt concrete
pavements at the service entrance area Due to the clayey nature of the subsoils at the
subject site the soils have become very soft and lost their bearing strength when
saturated In addition because the fills at the site were placed with little moisture
conditioning very dry of optimum moisture and possibly inadequate compaction these
conditions may be the cause of the distress observed at the subject site Infiltration of
water into the subsurface was the trigger mechanism
Based on our field exploration it appears that the clayey subsoils have softened
substantially under the infiltration of water from the automatic sprinkler system We
believe that the softened soils are not capable of supporting the pavements for the
heavy vehicular loads anticipated at the service entrance area Therefore it is our
opinion that remedial work at the service area should be concentrated in restoring the
bearing strength of the pavement subsoils In order to reduce the potential for the
observed distress from recurring it is our opinion that water infiltration into the
pavement subsoils should also be mitigated One solution to the observed distress
would be to completely remove the soft soils below the service entrance pavements up
to about 10 feet below the surface and replace it with new fill material However this
alternative would be costly and time consuming Therefore as a minimum we
recommend that the following measures be implemented at the subject site to restore
the pavements in the service entrance area

1 The existing pavement within the service entrance area should be
removed and the subsoils below the pavements should be over
excavated to a depth of 3 feet below the finished grades The
approximate extent of over excavation and replacement is shown on the
Remedial Site Plan Plate 3 To reduce the potential for undermining the
CRM wall foundations we recommend that the over excavation and
replacement of soft loose subsoils be limited to areas a minimum of
18 inches from the wall face At the bottom of the over excavated
subgrade level the subgrade should be scarified brought to a moisture
content between 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture content
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction A
layer of biaxial geogrids such as or equivalent should
be placed at the bottom of the over excavated subgrade level prior to
backfilling with engineered fill material
The engineered fill material should consist of select material with a CBR
value of 20 or more and a swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883 Granular soils generated from the
excavations may be reused as a source of engineered fill material
provided that they meet the above requirements The engineered fill
material should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction within 2 feet of the finished subgrade below pavement
areas A layer of biaxial geogrids such as or equivalent
should be placed at the bottom of the base course layer Another layer of
biaxial geogrids should also be placed at the mid depth level of the
engineered fill Details of the remedial work are shown on the attached
schematic sketches Plates 4 and 5
2 We also recommend that a subdrain system be constructed along the
edges of the pavements to reduce the potential for water infiltration into
the clayey subgrade soils thus improving the long term performance of
the asphalt concrete pavements The subdrain system should consist of a
geocomposite fin drain hydraulically connected to appropriate drainage
outlet structures for proper discharge The approximate alignment of the
proposed geocomposite fin drain is shown on the Remedial Site Plan
Plate 3

3 We believe that some of the pavement distress observed at the service
area could be the result of a lack of lateral confinement of the pavement
section Under traffic loads the softened subsoils will have a tendency to
squeeze out into the adjacent landscape area due to lack of lateral
confinement along the edges of the pavements This has resulted in
some of the distress observed at the subject site Therefore we
recommend that concrete curbs be installed along the edges of the
pavements to provide the necessary lateral confinement Generally the
curbs should extend a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below
the aggregate base course to reduce migration of landscape water into
the pavement section
4 We also recommend that some form of barricade such as concrete
blocks or pilings be provided along the edges of the parking lot to reduce
the potential for the asphalt paving and underlying base course from
squeezing out into the adjacent landscape areas due to the lack of
lateral confinement along the pavement edges
5 Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and pavement areas
should be avoided In addition automatic sprinkler systems should be
properly controlled to reduce over watering with the system left
unattended
These drainage requirements and recommendations are essential for the proper
performance of the pavement bearing soils since ponded water could cause subsurface
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength We believe that the
potential for distress should be significantly reduced if these measures are properly
implemented and maintained
The probable causes and cost effective remedial measure for the observed
distress at the service entrance area are complex issues We would be glad to meet
with you again if needed to provide further information and assistance in this matter

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between
the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The location of the borings indicated in this report are approximate having been
estimated by taping from the features shown on the Golf Course Clubhouse Grading
and Drainage Plan by R M Towill Corporation dated Elevations of
the borings were estimated by interpolation from the contour lines shown on the same
plan The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used
Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or in the text of this report These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tidal fluctuation temperature
and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
ea ee a
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling ten
borings to depths ranging from about 7 to 26 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Fifteen 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples of the fill
materials to evaluate the relationships between the moisture content degree of
compaction and the shrinking swelling behavior of the fill soils The results of these
tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Nine sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 3 and
B 4
One Modified Proctor test ASTM D 1557 91 was performed on bulk sample of
the fill soils to evaluate the maximum dry density and optimum moisture content
relationship The test results are presented on Plate B 5
