SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration encountered about 3 inches of asphaltic concrete 
pavement underlain by residual and saprolitic soils consisting of medium stiff to very stiff 
clayey silt and very dense silty gravel extending to the maximum depth explored of 15.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Boulders were encountered in Boring No. B-2 between 
7 and 9 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Groundwater levels were encountered in the drilled borings at depths between about 
6.3 and 7.8 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The 
groundwater levels measured generally correspond to about Elevations +593.7 to 
+559.2 feet MSL.  However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We anticipate that concrete box culvert and headwall structures will be bearing on 
medium stiff clayey silt with high natural moisture content and with high groundwater 
conditions encountered in the boring.  We recommend providing a bedding layer consisting 
of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) on top of a subgrade 
stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation) 
wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the bottom of the box 
culvert and headwall structures for uniform support. For footing foundations bearing on the 
recommended bedding and subgrade stabilization layers, bearing pressures of up to 6,000, 
2,700 and 2,000 psf may be used for the extreme event limit, strength limit, and service limit 
states, respectively.  The foundations should be embedded a minimum of about 2 feet below 
the lowest adjacent finish grade.  
The project site is located in a high rainfall environment throughout the year; 
therefore, the in-situ soils will constantly be in a very moist to wet condition and drying or 
aerating the excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as general fill. 
However, it may not be feasible and/or practical to aerate the in-situ soils adequately enough 
to achieve the normal 90 to 95 percent relative compaction requirements. Therefore, we 
recommend reducing the relative compaction requirement to at least 85 to 95 percent 
relative compaction.

design sections for the proposed project are presented below.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano built 
by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 5 to 
6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 2½ million 
years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the cessation of this 
main volcano shield-building phase about 1½ million years ago, there was a renewed 
volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic 
Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of the Palikea 
Formation. 
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick 
lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north-south 
trends at various locales. Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava flows are some 
deposits of pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which usually form cinder 
cones surrounding the vent. Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of 
the eastern half of the Island of Kauai, including the project site. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental 
glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose 
because more water was available. The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable 
during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher and lower relative to the 
present sea level of the Island of Kauai. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series. The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa area 
appears to be representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type, which spread and ponded 
as it approached the ocean from inland areas. Pahoehoe lavas are typically characterized

by a smoother, billowy surface and internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock with limited 
clinker materials.  
In some places, in-situ chemical weathering of the Koloa lava flows has formed a 
mantle of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material, while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical 
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to a 
basaltic rock formation with increased depth.  
The project site is situated in the southern portion of the island. Based on a review 
of available geologic information, the project site is generally underlain by a thick 
sequence of residual soils and extremely weathered basaltic rock (saprolite) with volcanic 
cinder and ash interbeds. The lavas and interbeds belong to both Waimea and Kōloa 
Volcanic Series. The soil materials generally consist of in-situ basalt rock and cinder that 
have been deeply weathered to form silty and clayey residual and saprolitic soils with 
embedded decomposed rock material. These residual and saprolitic deposits extend for 
a considerable depth below the upland ground surfaces. Due to the high rainfall and runoff 
experienced at this upland portion of the Island of Kaua`i, the in-situ residual and saprolitic 
soils have high moisture content. In addition, groundwater seepage and high-level 
perched groundwater are commonly encountered in the subsurface materials. These 
groundwater conditions contribute to the formation of spring discharge at lower elevations 
and near-continual stream discharge, which occurs in the larger drainage ravines.

and saprolitic soils consisting of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silts and very dense silty

influenced by seasonal precipitation, runoff, storm surge conditions, and other factors.

0.5 percent of the structure height. For design in accordance with the AASHTO LRFD 
methods, the force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures associated with seismic 
loading (PGA = 0.160g) may be estimated using 2.6H2 pounds per linear foot of wall 
length for level backfill conditions. It should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth 
forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 
1 to 2 inches in the event of an earthquake.  
If the estimated amount of lateral movement is not attainable or the retaining structure 
is restrained, the retaining structure should be designed with higher dynamic lateral 
forces for a restrained condition. For a restrained condition (less than 0.5 inches of 
lateral movement), dynamic lateral forces due to seismic loading may be estimated 
using 4.4H2 pounds per linear foot of wall (H measured in feet) for level backfill 
conditions. 
The resultant force should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall. The 
above dynamic lateral earth forces are in addition to the static lateral earth pressures 
provided above. An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when 
dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the retaining 
structure.

3.2.4 Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the potential for the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of permeable 
material, such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation material, placed directly around a 
perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall discharging to an 
appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, 
such as MiraDrain, may be used instead of the drainage material.  The prefabricated 
drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the 
base of the wall.  
Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements, the upper 12 inches of backfill 
should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for water 
infiltration behind the walls. In addition, the backfill below the drainage outlet (or 
weepholes) should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential 
for water infiltration into the footing subgrade.  The relatively impervious material 
should be compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction.  
3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of the pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on medium stiff to stiff residual soils, a minimum of 
6 inches of compacted granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM 
C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipe. The subgrade beneath the 
pipe cushion should be recompacted to at least 85 percent relative compaction. Where 
soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization 
layer consisting of an additional 12 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine 
gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be 
provided below the cushion layer for uniform support.   
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the 
trench, the unit weight of the trench backfill material, and the friction resistance between 
the backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried 
pipes, we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) for 
the trench backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3. Earth forces acting upon the 
pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the 
trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be 
considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
In general, the first zone of backfill extending from the cushion material to at least 
12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above the groundwater level, whichever 
is higher) should consist of select free-draining granular material, such as No. 3B Fine 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and 
resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to 
reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes (or 
12 inches above the groundwater level, whichever is higher) to the pavement subgrade 
should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials that are free of 
vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension.  It should be noted that the fill requirements 
presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on 
additional laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials 
conducted by Geolabs during the project construction. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill, if required, should consist of select 
granular fill materials.  Select granular fill materials should be well-graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and contain between

At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 
14 and 15.5 feet below the existing pavement surface, respectively. The borings were drilled 
with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. In addition, one 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk 
sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of

summarized on Plate B-3. 
One Unconfined Compression Strength test (ASTM D2166) was performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the compressive strength of the soil.  The test results are 
presented on Plate B-4.

sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
