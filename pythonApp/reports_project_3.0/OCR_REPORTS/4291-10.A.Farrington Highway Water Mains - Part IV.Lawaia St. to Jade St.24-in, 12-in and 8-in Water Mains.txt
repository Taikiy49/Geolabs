performed for the Farrington Highway Water Mains Part IV Lawaia Street to Jade
Street project located in the Waianae area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general

the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of trench
excavation and shoring pipe support and trench backfilling only and are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the west side of the Waianae Mountain Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and
reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present
form

3 1 Bridge Crossings Trenchless Methods
We understand that alternative construction methods including microtunneling
horizontal directional drilling HDD and conventional open trench Cut and Cover were
considered for the installation of the water mains below the two stream crossings
Based on our field exploration alluvium coralline detritus coral formation and
sandstone were encountered in the borings drilled at the stream crossings It should be
noted that boulders and cobbles were encountered within the alluvium soil layers
Horizontal Directional Drilling
Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using a
multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole The pilot hole is
bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical cutting or
combinations of both The pilot hole is reamed to a diameter much larger than the
outside diameter of the pipe as necessary to accommodate the pipe Then the
pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling machine
The advantage of an HDD installation on this project would be the elimination of the
deep jacking and receiving pits needed for microtunneling installation This would
greatly reduce or eliminate the shoring and dewatering requirements at the jacking
and receiving pits However due to limitations on the drilling angles at the entry
and exit points at the ground surface HDD would require a much longer installed
length of pipe Additionally the accuracy of HDD is less than that of
microtunneling therefore care must be taken when traversing under the stream
crossings to provide the proper clearance between the new pipe and bottoms of the
streams
Typically HDD is well suited for clayey soils although it is possible to use HDD in
granular soils by adding bentonite into the drilling fluid Our borings encountered
gravel cobbles and boulders which may present difficulties in steering control and
penetration of the drill head and stability problems of the hole such as caving in of
the pilot or reamed out hole

Microtunneling
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine Entry and exit pits will be
required These pits are generally rectangular in shape to accommodate the length
about three to four tunnel diameters or two pipe lengths and width about two
tunnel diameters of the tunnel boring machine the jacking frame reaction block
and pipe sections to be jacked into place During the microtunneling process the
excavated material is removed by using a mixture of water and slurry pumped
through a pipe balanced screw auger or a screw conveyor system
In microtunneling selecting the right machine cutter head for the ground condition
is critical Different types of cutter heads are used for various types of soil and or
rock As mentioned previously loose gravel and sands very stiff clays and silts
with the cobbles and boulders and dense coral formation were encountered in the
borings drilled at the stream crossings We anticipate that the cutter head will bore
through different types of soil in the same run creating a mixed face condition
which may impede tunneling
Difficulty may be encountered during the excavation of the jacking and receiving
pits due to the presence of the dense coral formation encountered during our field
exploration In addition installation of shoring for the jacking and receiving pits may
encounter some difficulty due to the presence of the dense coral formation

3 3 Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the very stiff to hard silty clays and the medium
hard coral formation a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material
such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided below
the pipe For pipes with inverts in soft and or loose soils an additional 18 inches of
open graded gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be
provided below the bedding layer for uniform support A typical section of the trench is
presented on Plate 3
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the
pipe Before the placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom should be
observed by qualified technical personnel to confirm that firm materials are exposed at
the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper as recommended
above
3 4 Backfill
In general the first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at
least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select free draining
granular material to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the
pipe The granular material should be well graded less than 1 inch in maximum size
and should have less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water
ponding or jetting may be allowed provided the granular material is free draining such
as open graded gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet
below the finish subgrade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the

3 6 Settlements
Primary settlement of the new water main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will
cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlement of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur if
improper trench supports are used Therefore we believe that the proposed water
mains should be designed with sufficient flexibility to accommodate some possible
settlements

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 16 test borings The borings drilled extended to depths ranging from about
20 4 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring
equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 16
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The Pocket Penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the Pocket Penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of coral rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation The coral rock
material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of coral rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Six Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 4318 The results of the tests
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Seventeen Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils
to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation C 117 and C 136 Graphic
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B 2 through B 5
Five Hydrometer tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics below the No 200 sieve and to aid in soil
classification The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 422 Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution below the No 200 sieve is
also provided on Plate B 4
Four Direct Shear tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics The tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3080 The results of the tests are presented
on Plates B 6 through B 9

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
Five Percolation tests were performed in the drilled borings Boring Nos
13 through 16 by using both the falling head and constant head methods The drilled
borings had diameters of about 4 inches and depths ranging from about 20 7 to
22 8 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of testing
The Falling Head Method was tested by filling the boreholes with water and
allowing the water to percolate into the ground The water levels and corresponding
times were recorded for the calculation of subsurface permeability
The Constant Head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time
The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured
The head levels within the test borings were monitored by an electric water level
sounder during the test The results of the percolation tests conducted are presented
on Plates C 1 through C 5
