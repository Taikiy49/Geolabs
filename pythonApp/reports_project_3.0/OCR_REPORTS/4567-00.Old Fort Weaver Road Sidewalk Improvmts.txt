INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the Old Fort Weaver Road Sidewalk Improvements project in the District of Ewa on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of retaining walls
box culvert support AC sidewalks and site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided it is desired to improve the existing sidewalk along
the makai side of Old Fort Weaver Road in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
As part of the sidewalk improvement project a new retaining wall will need to be constructed
to provide the necessary grade separation between the new sidewalk and the adjoining
properties

and end at about adjacent to the parcel designated as

minor cuts on the order of about 2 to 5 feet deep for the wall footing excavation Backfills on
the order of about 3 to 9 feet thick are anticipated behind the retaining wall to raise the
ground surface to the new sidewalk elevations

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials
caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects within the last
century have brought the project site to its present form

drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed CRM wall alignment is generally
underlain by stiff to very stiff sandy and clayey silts extending to depths of about 13 feet
below the existing ground surface Soft organic silts were encountered at greater depths and
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 16 5 feet below the ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on our field exploration we believe that the stiff to very stiff sandy and clayey
silts encountered in the shallow subsurface should provide adequate foundation support
for the proposed CRM wall An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used
for the foundation design of the CRM wall bearing on the near surface soils recompacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The bottom of wall foundations located on relatively flat areas should be embedded
a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground
conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum horizontal setback
distance equal to the width of the footing Wall footings that are oriented parallel to the
direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings Wall footing excavations
should be backfilled as soon as possible after the wall construction to reduce the potential
for disturbance to the existing slope resulting from the traffic loads Detailed discussions
and recommendations pertaining to these items are further presented in the following
sections of this report

Wall Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe
that shallow continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new
retaining wall An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
The bottom of the wall footing excavations should be recompacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface
prior to construction of the wall If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the
bottom of the footing excavations the materials should be over excavated to expose
the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select
granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or
the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent materials
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
in situ soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure
should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
GEOLABS INC
W O 4567 00 Page 8
Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches Wall foundations on
relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions the footing should
extend deeper to obtain a minimum horizontal setback distance equal to the width of
the footing A schematic illustration of this requirement is presented on Plate 3 of this
report Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills as
recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the
order of one half 7 inch
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site silty soils or
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches
of backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls
Existing Box Culvert
Based on the preliminary drawings provided we understand that a portion of the new
CRM wall will traverse an existing underground box culvert at about The existing
box culvert is about 6 feet wide by 4 feet deep with a top slab thickness of about 7 inches
Based on our review of the available drawings we understand that a concrete slab bridge
spanning over the existing box culvert will be used for the base support of the CRM wall at
this location It is our opinion that the concrete slab bridge may be designed as a
simply supported beam with shallow foundations supporting the structural slab The
concrete slab should be reinforced as a structural slab and should be designed by the project
structural engineer
In order to avoid surcharging the existing box culvert with additional loads structural
loads from the bridge foundations should be transferred to the underlying soils at or below
the bottom of the box culvert Therefore we recommend that the foundations of the concrete
slab bridge extend to a depth at or below the bottom of the existing box culvert As an
alternative the excavation may be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM

up to the bottom elevations of the concrete slab bridge footings A flexible material such as
neoprene should be provided between the CLSM or concrete backfill and the adjacent box
culvert walls such that loads from the new structural slab bridge are not transferred onto the
box culvert The parameters for foundation design presented in the Retaining Walls section
may be used to design the foundations for the concrete slab bridge
AC Sidewalks
We understand that asphalt concrete paved sidewalks are planned for the proposed
sidewalk improvement project Based on our field and laboratory test results we believe that
new sidewalks may be constructed on grade provided that the subgrade is properly prepared
in accordance with the recommendations presented below and in the Site Preparation
section of this report
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick base course layer compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction be provided below the asphalt concrete
paving Sidewalk subgrades should be prepared by scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth
of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement should be increased to 95 percent relative compaction for the base course and
underlying subgrades that will be subjected to future vehicular traffic such as the driveway
areas Subgrades should be kept moist until covered by base course Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be
thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to
base course placement Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due
to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris rubbish
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or ina
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials with the
cleared and grubbed spoils Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and
grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures and pavements to be demolished
should be completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be
removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill
material moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch level
loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition the over excavated subgrades or areas
designated to receive fills should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 to 95 percent relative compaction Backfills behind the
retaining wall should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 to 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the

Imported fill materials required for the project construction should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral and basalt The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of one percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 Imported fill material should be tested for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Aggregate base course underneath the AC sidewalks should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate and should conform to Section 703 06 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Aggregate base course materials
should be moisture conditioned to near the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to
provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill
placement recommendations presented above Excessive water should be diverted away
from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the fill slopes are
completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
eee
The subsurface conditions along the proposed CRM wall alignment were explored
by drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to
depths of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations
of these borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of these tests are
summarized on Plate B 3
