Dear Mr. Inouye: 
In general, the Kapiolani Residence project involves the construction of a new 
45-story, high-rise building with about 484 residential units and an eight-story parking 
garage with 855 parking spaces on two parcels (designated as TMK: 2-3-041: 006 & 
009) situated between Kapiolani Boulevard and Kona Street in Honolulu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
We have performed a geotechnical engineering exploration in support of the 
foundation design of the high-rise condominium development and our recommendations 
were presented in the report entitled, “Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, Samkoo 
Pacific Condominium, 1631 Kapiolani Boulevard, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii”, dated 
November 5, 2015.  
Based on the structural drawings, we understand that the new high-rise building will 
be supported by 24-inch diameter augered cast-in-place (ACIP) piles.  A bi-directional 
axial load test using an expandable load cell (Osterberg Load Cell) was conducted on a 
sacrificial 24-inch diameter ACIP pile by LOADTEST, a division of Fugro USA Land, Inc.  
on January 8 and 9, 2017.  The test results are presented in their report entitled “Report 
on CFA Pile Load Testing (Osterberg Method), TP-1 – 1631 Kapiolani Blvd., Honolulu, 
HI (LT-1544-1)” dated January 16, 2017.   
We understand that the new parking garage will be supported by 18-inch diameter 
ACIP piles with an allowable compressive load capacity of 500 kips per pile.  In 
accordance with the project design requirements, a static compressive pile load test was 
conducted on a sacrificial 18-inch diameter ACIP pile on February 7, 2017 by our office. 
This letter report presents the results of our engineering analysis, field observations, and 
test data obtained from the static compressive pile load test.

The pile load test was conducted on an 18-inch diameter ACIP pile with a length of 
approximately 78.4 feet relative to the existing ground surface. The load test pile was 
installed by Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company (HDCC) using a crane-mounted 
drill rig equipped with automatic monitoring equipment to provide the drilling depth, drilling 
rate, auger torque, stroke count, etc., on a near-continuous basis. HDCC used continuous 
flight augers to drill the pile.  As the augers were removed, the hole was grouted from the 
bottom upwards. After sound quality grout (based on visual observation) appeared at the 
top of the pile, a reinforcing cage was inserted into the grouted hole. The 18-inch diameter 
load test pile location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  Installation records for the load 
test pile are presented on Plate 5.  
To provide support for the load test, two 18-inch diameter ACIP reaction piles, 
approximately 65 feet long, were installed in a similar fashion as the load test pile with 
2.5-inch diameter center bars in lieu of a rebar cage.  The reaction piles and load test pile 
were completed on January 27 and 30, 2017, respectively. 
To better understand the load transfer mechanism at the pile-soil interface, the load 
test pile was instrumented with seven vibrating wire embedment strain gages (Model 
No. VCE-4200) manufactured by Geokon. The strain gages were connected using a 
multiplexer to a Geokon Micro-10 data acquisition system to record the strains during the 
compressive load test.  The embedment strain gages were installed in the load test pile at 
the following depths below the existing ground surface.

The load test pile was loaded and reloaded in general accordance with the Quick 
Load Test Method (ASTM D1143) and modified by our office to suit the subsurface 
conditions at the project site. In general, the test pile was loaded to about 1,000 kips in 
approximately 62.5-kip load increments. Each load increment was held for at least 
15 minutes or until the rate of the pile head movement generally was stable. In addition, 
the static load test was held for approximately 2 hours at the 500-kip and 750-kip load 
increments, and 3 hours at the 1,000-kip load increment to evaluate the effects of creep. 
After loading, the load test pile was unloaded in approximately eight equal decrements and 
held for at least 5 minutes at each decrement. The results of the compressive pile load test 
are summarized below.

on Plates 3 and 4, respectively. Based on the information collected from the load test and 
our engineering analyses, it is our opinion that the recommended design pile length of 
65 feet below the pile cap bottom elevation should be adequate to achieve an allowable 
compressive load capacity of 500 kips per pile.
