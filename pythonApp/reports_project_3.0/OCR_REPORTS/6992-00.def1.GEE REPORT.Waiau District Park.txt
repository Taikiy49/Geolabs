SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicates that the near-surface soils at 
the site are very stiff to hard in consistency; and, have low expansion potential when 
subjected to cyclical drying and wetting.  
The primary structural element for the site improvements is a relatively low retaining 
wall to divert surface flow away from the existing fire station retaining wall.  Based on the 
subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend using an allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf to design the new retaining wall foundations bearing 
on the hard to very hard on-site silty soils. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-
live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those 
caused by wind or seismic forces. 
The toe of the new wall should be either situated horizontally or embedded 
vertically below a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward 
from the heel of the existing wall. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging the 
existing wall with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation 
settlement for the new wall. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed NPDES Erosional Area Improvements project at the 
Waiau District Park in Waiau on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is in the existing Waiau District Park located at the intersection of 
Kaahumanu Street and Komo Mai Drive in Waiau on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The 
project involves making drainage site improvements to divert storm water runoff from 
the top of the retaining wall surrounding the Waiau Fire Station, which is situated at the 
lower portion of the park.  The overall project involves the installation of a new retaining 
wall upslope of the existing fire station wall, site grading, erosion control and other 
drainage improvements. 
Based on the plan provided for the preparation of this report, it is planned to 
install a relatively low retaining wall, about 5 feet in height, about 10 feet behind 
(upslope) of the existing fire station retaining wall; and to regrade the area above the 
new retaining wall to divert the surface water flow to an adjacent drain inlet in the west 
side of the site.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Site Description 
The project site is upslope of the existing Waiau Fire Station located at the 
intersection of Kaahumanu Street and Komo Mai Drive in the Waiau portion of Pearl 
City on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The site consists of a relatively narrow strip of 
grassed park area above the retaining wall surrounding the fire station.  The existing fire 
station retaining wall is about 6 to 7 feet in height and retains soil for its full height. 
The “erosional” area is open park area extending about 20 feet from the top of 
the wall with the ground surface sloping at about two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) 
up from the top of the wall. 
Based on our field observations and elevation data from the plan provided, the 
site slopes down slightly towards the south with existing ground surface elevations 
ranging from about +263 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) along the northern part of the site 
at Kaahumanu Street to about +250 feet MSL on Komo Mai Drive. 
2.2 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Koolau 
Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The site is located on a planeze, or a triangular relatively uneroded intravalley 
ridge, on the southwest facing flank of the Koolau dome.  The soils on the ridge are 
generally residual soils, derived from the in-situ weathering of basaltic rock.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicates that the near-surface soils at 
the site are very stiff to hard in consistency; and, have low expansion potential when 
subjected to cyclical drying and wetting.  
The primary structural element for the site improvements is a relatively low 
retaining wall to divert surface flow away from the existing fire station retaining wall.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend 
using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) to 
design the new retaining wall foundations bearing on the hard to very hard on-site silty 
soils. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design 
recommendations are presented in the following sections.  
3.1 
Site Grading 
Grading plans were not available at the time this report was prepared. Based on 
the existing topography of the site, we anticipate that the majority of the site grading 
work will involve shallow cuts and fills on the order of less than 1 foot deep or thick.  
Since the near-surface soils have a low potential to shrink when allowed to dry 
and to expand when re-wetted, moisture control measures are recommended during the 
earthwork portions of the project.  Generally, the soils should be maintained at a field 
moisture content that is about 2 percent above optimum moisture content as 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Generally, a Geolabs representative should monitor the site grading operations 
to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification 
process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those 
anticipated. Items of grading addressed in the subsequent subsections include the 
following:

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Existing asphalt pavement material, vegetation, 
debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
disposed of properly at an off-site location.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should 
be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated soft soils should 
be properly disposed off-site and/or used on landscaping areas, where appropriate. 
Prior to filling and backfilling, the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 
12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where shrinkage 
cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend preparing 
the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and subsequent 
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may 
require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. 
The excavated soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used on 
landscaping areas, where appropriate. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
Based on the topography at the project site, we anticipate minor cuts and/or fills 
may be required for the proposed retaining wall construction. 
General fill material, if required, should consist of low-expansive materials less than 
3 inches in largest dimension. The material should have a laboratory California 
Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 12 or higher and a swell potential of less than 
1 percent in accordance with ASTM D1883. The fill material should also be free of 
vegetation and deleterious materials. It should be noted that the general fill 
requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be 
modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field observation on 
the available fill materials.  
Where fill is required under structural areas, we recommend placing select 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material 
should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in 
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing 
the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more 
and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use. 
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Base course and select fill 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in 
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum 
of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.2 
Foundations 
The primary structural element for the future site improvements is a relatively low 
retaining wall to divert surface flow away from the existing fire station retaining wall.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend that 
an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used to design the shallow 
foundations bearing on recompacted on-site silty soils. This bearing value is for 
supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
For footings that will bear on recompacted on-site soil, the bottom of the footing 
excavations should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide 
a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or 
concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation 
should be backfilled with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction, or the footing bottom may be deepened to the 
underlying firm materials. 
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade. Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet

The values provided above assume that select granular fill will be used to backfill 
behind the walls.  It is assumed that the backfill behind retaining walls will be 
compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction.  Over-compaction of 
the retaining wall backfill should be avoided.  In general, an active condition may be 
used for gravity walls and walls that are free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of 
the wall height.  If the tops of the walls are not free to deflect beyond this degree, or 
are restrained, the walls should be designed for the at-rest condition.  These lateral 
earth pressures do not include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by 
groundwater trapped behind the walls. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the 
design.  For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a 
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 31 percent of the vertical 
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall, that is free to deflect 
(cantilever), may be used in design.  For walls that are restrained, a rectangular 
distribution equal to 47 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the 
entire height of the wall may be used for design.  Additional analyses during design 
may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads.  
The above lateral loads acting on a retaining wall structure may be resisted by 
friction developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by 
passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation 
system. A coefficient of friction of 0.4 may be used for footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils.  Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot 
of depth (pcf) assuming that the soil around footings are well compacted.  The 
passive pressure should be reduced for foundations on slopes.  Unless covered by 
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be 
neglected.

3.2.2 Retaining Wall Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall 
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material.  The prefabricated drainage product should also 
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of select granular fill 
material.  Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavement, the upper 12 inches of 
backfill should consist of low-expansion, relatively impervious material to reduce the 
potential for water infiltration behind the walls. 
A Geolabs representative should observe footing excavations prior to placement of 
reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm foundation bearing conditions and the 
required embedment depths. Observation of the foundation excavations and 
preparation operations should be considered to be “Special Inspection” items in 
accordance with Section 1701 of the International Building Code (2012).

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by about and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of 16.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The borings and test holes were drilled using a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  The approximate boring and test hole 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
