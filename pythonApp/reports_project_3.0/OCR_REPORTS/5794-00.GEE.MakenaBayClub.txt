2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older Tertiary Epoch
West Maui and the more recent Pleistocene Epoch East Maui also known as
Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain
between these two volcanoes The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from
Haleakala Volcano ponding against West Maui Volcano The gently sloping plains were
subsequently filled with alluvium washed down from the slopes of West Maui and
East Maui Haleakala Volcanoes The erosional processes are dominated by the
detachment of soil and rock masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials are
transported downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
Haleakala Volcano was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows
The lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene early Pleistocene Epoch
consists of thinly bedded tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a a These lavas tend to be very
permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series
early Pleistocene Epoch primarily composed of thicker alkalic a a type flows which may
contain many inter stratified thin ash soil layers A long quiescent period followed during
which time canyons were carved in the volcano although a few eruptions may have
occurred during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes
higher and lower stands of sea level At the end of the rest period copious flows erupted
along the southwest and east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit known as the
Hana Volcanic Series Pleistocene Epoch The lavas range in composition from
ultra basic olivine porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts
The Makena Bay Club project site is on the lower southwest slope of
Haleakala Volcano The subsurface soils consist of volcanic ash material overlying cinder
sands and a a basalt formations of the Makena and Oanapuka Series In general the a a
basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite readily

2 2 Site Description
The Makena Bay Club project site is approximately 0 5 miles south of the Maui
Prince Hotel on the west side of Makena Keoneoio Road in the Makena area on the
Island of Maui Hawaii The project site is roughly rectangularly shaped and
encompasses approximately 11 27 acres Generally the project site is bounded by
Makena Keoneoio Road to the east and by the fairways of the Makena Golf Course to
the south and west
The project area was heavily forested at the time of our field exploration and
required substantial efforts of trail clearing for access to the proposed boring locations
Vegetation consisted of mostly arid land varieties of scrub brush cactus and
Kiawe trees Several archeological sites are present throughout the project area The
majority of the project site is generally blanketed by a surface layer of dry ashy soils
with basaltic rock outcrops exposed at the ground surface
The topography of the site is gently sloping with ground surface elevations
ranging from about 70 feet MSL in the southeastern corner to about 30 feet MSL at
the northern end of the property In addition one large ravine occupies the central
portion of the project site where steeper topography exists

2 4 Seismic Design Considerations
We understand that the structural elements of the project will be designed based
on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC Based on the 1997 UBC the Island of Maui
is located within Seismic Zone 2B with an effective peak ground acceleration of 0 20g
Therefore the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and seismic design
considerations will need to be addressed for the project The following sections provide
discussions on the seismicity of the Island of Maui and the soil profile for seismic design
at the project site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii over
90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The
Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5
M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii
It should be noted that some earthquakes in Hawaii are caused by the burden of
the island masses overlying the lithosphere which cause the lithosphere to sag
beneath the immense weight The isostatic adjustment of the Pacific lithosphere

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 25 borings designated as Boring Nos C 1 through C 10 and S 1 through
S 15 The borings were advanced to maximum depths ranging from about 23 to
36 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools The
approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 25
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Eight one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples
retrieved to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
One Sieve Analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected
soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification The grain size distribution is graphically presented on Plate B 2
Six sets of Corrosion tests including Minimum Resistivity
E 120 1 Chloride Content and Sulfate Content were
conducted on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The tests were
performed by TestAmerica Analytical Testing Corporation and the results are
summarized on Plate B 3
Four Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt
formation encountered at the project site Results of the unconfined compression tests
are presented on Plate B 4
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture
content and saturated The test results are presented on Plates B 5 through B 7
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 8 through B 10

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the upper subsurface materials was evaluated by performing
C 10 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The tests were performed at depths ranging
from about 6 5 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface
To conduct the constant head permeability tests we pumped clear water into the
boreholes until the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient
period of time We then measured the water height and the rate of pumping to provide
data upon which to base calculation of the subsurface material permeability Results of the
field permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 7
