EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This basis of design report was developed in support of the preliminary design of
the slope scaling and installation of draped wire mesh over the slopes of the four
designated rockfall hazard areas within the limits of the Route 1 Reconstruction and
Shoreline Protection Phase V project The hill slopes extending up from the roadway at
the four designated rockfall hazard areas consists of a lower slope cut face and the upper
slope native mountainside The hillside consists of layered basalt rock containing
alternating seams of weathered clinker and hard basalt rock materials The basalt rock
hillside is eroding and producing falling rock ranging from boulders to small stones In
addition there has been a fatality due to rockfall in October 2001 within proximity of the
southern portion of the project
Based on our observation of the existing site conditions we believe that the
potential exists for falling rock to occur at the four designated rockfall hazard areas
Therefore priority should be assigned to the implementation of a rockfall protection system
for the portions of Route 1 at the four designated rockfall hazard areas We understand
that budget constraints preclude the construction of a comprehensive rockfall protection
system for the four designated rockfall hazard areas at this time Therefore we
understand that it is desired to implement a temporary rockfall protection system to reduce
the rockfall hazard on the Route 1 roadway
Based on reconnaissance of the rockfall hazard areas it appears that there are
three main mechanisms generating rockfall events at the site The three mechanisms
related to rockfall are raveling failure boulder fall and differential erosion of the alternating
volcanic rock layers Based on the three mechanisms of rockfall observed at the project
site we believe that one of the most viable alternatives for a temporary rockfall protection
scheme for the site would consist of scaling the rock slope and installation of a draped wire
mesh to control falling rock from the slope face
Based on the exposed conditions we believe that a rockfall protection system
consisting of major scaling of the loose rocks from the slope face and installation of draped
wire mesh on the prepared slope surface may be considered a viable rockfall mitigation
measure and is the preferred alternative Because the weathering of rock is an on going
process of erosion rockfall events will continue to occur with the passage of time With
the implementation of scaling and installation of draped wire mesh the useful life of the
improvements may be on the order of about 10 to 15 years
The text of this basis of design report should be referred to for detailed discussions
on the intent of the preliminary design of the rockfall protection system for the project

In general the purpose for the installation of the rockfall protection system at the
rockfall hazard areas is to mitigate the potential hazard of falling rock from the existing
steep rocky slopes located adjacent to the roadway Because the project addresses the
potentially hazardous condition of rockfall activity a definition of rockfall is needed Where
cliffs and steep slopes composed of rock material occur rockfall is a common naturally
occurring process of erosion and mass wasting Rockfall is the detachment and
transportation downslope of individual rock fragments showers of rock fragments or large
boulders of rock material as a result of geologic weathering or disturbance to the slope
Talus deposits are the direct result of the accumulation of rockfall material either from
large scale catastrophic slope failure events or the fall of individual rock fragments
Due to the close proximity of Route 1 to the steep rocky mountain slopes the
rockfall source falling rock that bounces rolls or falls directly onto the roadway is a real
problem with a significant potential to cause personal injury and or traffic accidents
Accidents involving personal injury or even death occur as the result of a direct impact
from falling rock and collisions with other vehicles and objects as a result of vehicles
swerving to avoid fallen material in the roadway
Because there have been documented cases of personal injury and vehicle
accidents resulting from rockfall activity on this stretch of Route 1 it is necessary to
implement some form of rockfall protection to reduce the present hazardous condition at
the various sites Therefore it is desired to construct some improvements on the slopes to
mitigate potential future rockfall that may pose a hazard to traffic along the roadway
In general the existing two lane roadway that traverses the base of the rockfall
hazard areas has limited roadway shoulder width and limited vehicle sight distance
because of the existing horizontal roadway curves and the close proximity to the shoreline
Therefore adequate rockfall catchment areas adjacent to the roadway may be difficult to
construct Furthermore the significant height and steepness of the existing slopes
generally preclude other remedial alternatives such as slope setback by excavation of a
new cut slope face or the deployment of roadside rockfall barriers

SECTION 3 0 REGIONAL GEOLOGY
American Samoa is an island archipelago located approximately 2 500 miles
toward the south of Honolulu Hawaii at about 14 degrees south latitude from the
equator The total land area of American Samoa consists of about 76 square miles and
includes five volcanic islands Tutuila Aunu u Ofu Olosega and Ta u and two remote
atolls Rose and Swains The Samoan archipelago was formed by submarine volcanic
activity that built up to form the volcanic mountains which emerged from the ocean
about 1 5 million years ago during the late Pliocene Epoch Stearns 1944
3 1 Island of Tutuila
The Island of Tutuila is the largest island of the archipelago with a land area of
about 53 square miles and a maximum elevation of just under about 3 000 feet MSL
The Island of Tutuila consists of an elongated east west trending island shape with a
prominent sharp crested mountain ridge spine spanning the length of the island The
primary mountain slopes are very steep with limited flatter terrain encompassing the
island with the exception of the coastal regions and the vicinity of the lava terrace
identified as the Tafuna Leone Plain
The Island of Tutuila was formed by the coalescence of the five volcanoes
situated on two separate submarine volcanic rift zones The bulk of the Island of Tutuila
is composed of basaltic and andesitic lava flows and volcanic tuff and cinder deposits
Other volcanic rocks make up a small fraction of the geology and coralline reef and
sand deposits contribute to the coastal lagoons and shoreline areas
The American Samoa island archipelago formed as an elongated island chain
that grew as the Pacific Plate moved westward in relation to a nearly stationary hot
spot of thermal activity located deep in the earth s mantle As the island archipelago
moves westward on the Pacific Plate it interacts with the adjacent Australian Plate ata
junction located approximately 100 miles toward the south of American Samoa This
proximal plate interaction is believed to be responsible for some earthquakes and
renewed volcanic activity in the area

Tutuila American Samoa The project site consists of four separate hillside locations

4 1 Climatic Considerations
The climate generally is classified as tropical and is affected by the close
proximity of the inter tropical convergence zone ITCZ which is characterized by
weaker wind fields higher air temperatures and increased humidity Rainfall averages
about 125 inches per year at the lower elevations increasing to as much as about
300 inches per year at the inland mountain summit regions Thunderstorm activity
commonly is accompanied by briefly heavy rainfall rates with sudden and intense
surface water runoff generated from the steep mountain slopes The island may be
affected by hurricanes and other tropical weather systems which typically approach
from the north
The average annual air temperature is near 80 degrees Fahrenheit with
maximum temperatures recorded in the lower 90s and minimum temperatures near
60 degrees Fahrenheit The island is densely vegetated with tropical flora with many
perennial streams flowing from the typically small and steep drainage basins located
within the interior sections of the island
Due to the high rainfall and humid climatic environment groundwater seepage
from the slopes is anticipated to occur Groundwater seepage may be responsible for

4 3 Rockfall Mechanisms
Based on our reconnaissance of the four designated rockfall hazard areas along
Route 1 it appears that there are three main mechanisms generating rockfall events at
the site The three mechanisms related to rockfall are raveling failure boulder fall and
differential erosion of alternating volcanic rock layers
Raveling of one or several rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of
causes The hazard develops when the raveling rock has sufficient velocity and volume
to reach the roadway surface Raveling may be caused by water pressure behind the
rock ice jacking not possible at this location differential weathering or erosion along
faults or shear zones weakening of rock due to temperature and moisture cyclic

fluctuations and loosening by animals or trees and wind Raveling also may result from

pasic first aid course or equivalent and should have experience or training in the use

Each slope scaler is an individual who is engaged in accessing the slope face

It should be noted that rock slope scaling work may be considered hazardous

attribute to the successful performance of rock slope scaling by the rock slope scaling
crews Therefore night work for rock slope scaling at the site should be discouraged for
safety reasons of the scaling crews In addition the difficulty in maintaining proper
and complete illumination of the work site makes night work for rock slope scaling
undesirable and virtually impossible Furthermore it should be noted that night work for
rock slope scaling should be avoided because of potentially poor visibility and shadow
conditions which may reduce the ability of the rock slope scalers to properly assess the
rock slope conditions and the presence of loose materials and precariously located
rocks
The rock slope scaling will require temporary closure of the roadway during the
time that rock is being scaled or removed from the slope We recommend that all the
Hawaii California

rock slope scaling work be conducted in the day with some temporary roadway closure
In addition we envision that access for emergency vehicles would be provided at all
times during the scaling work
Due to the presence of inter bedded layers of weak clinker materials scaling with
the use of a backhoe may be too aggressive to control the breakage of rock along
existing fractures In addition scaling with the use of a backhoe may unnecessarily
over excavate clinker materials and cause potential new slope instability Therefore we
believe that the use of backhoe or other mechanized equipment to assist in the
excavation and scaling work in an effort to speed up the scaling work should be
performed with extra care in areas exposing the weaker rock materials such as the
clinker materials and highly weathered basalt rock formation
5 2 Draped Wire Mesh Installation
As mentioned previously the draped wire mesh cover should assist in the
controlled release of loose rock that is shed from the slope face during weathering and
erosion Therefore it is not the primary intent to closely pin the wire mesh to the slope
face as a retention system The draped wire mesh should provide positive rockfall
control for the useful life of the wire mesh material We anticipate the majority of the
large blocks of rock and boulders would have been removed following the slope scaling
effort Therefore we envision that some loose rocks that may be retained by the
draped wire mesh would be generally less than 3 feet in diameter The following
paragraphs provide a general description of the draped wire mesh installation
procedure
Once the rock slope scaling work has been completed installation of the draped
wire mesh may proceed Grouted rock anchors of about 5 feet in depth are grouted into
the solid rock located at the crest of the rock slope Rockfall Hazard Area 1 and Area 3
The grouted rock anchors are generally installed at about 20 feet on centers along the
slope crest Galvanized steel wire rope 0 75 inch diameter with a black colored PVC
coating is strung between the grouted rock anchors

For Rockfall Hazard Area 2 and Area 4 the natural slope above the roadway cut
slope continues up to several hundred feet in elevation Furthermore the slopes at
Rockfall Hazard Area 2 and Area 4 are heavily vegetated making the removal of the
heavy vegetation growth very difficult and possibly increase surface erosion of the
slopes following the clearing of the vegetation As a result we believe that a modified
draped wire mesh system with support posts at the top of the draped wire mesh should
be provided to reduce the rockfall hazard above the area above the termination of the
draped wire mesh system
The wire mesh which typically comes in rolls of about 6 to 13 feet wide by about
200 feet long up to 300 feet long is possible is attached and draped from the
0 75 inch cable ropes to cover the entire rock slope In general a minimum of about
1 foot of overlap between adjacent rolls of wire mesh is provided vertical seams For
the horizontal seams we recommend that the minimum overlap between rolls be
increased to 2 feet The mesh overlaps should be secured using stainless steel ring
fasteners with a minimum of six stainless steel ring fasteners for each square foot of
mesh overlap It should be noted that the wire mesh should be colored black to
approximate the general coloration of the natural slope
The rolls of wire mesh are usually draped starting from the top of the slope The
wire mesh is hung to within about 1 foot minimum to 2 feet maximum above the base
level to allow for removal of loose rock that may fall from behind the draped wire mesh
during installation The draped lengths of wire mesh are then tied together by the rock
slope scalers to complete the installation It should be noted that the new roadway does
not afford much room for the shoulders The new edge of roadway will be separated
from the base of the cliff by a drainage ditch At locations the minimum width of the
drainage ditch is about 6 feet with a typical width of about 10 feet Even with the limited
catchment maintenance of loose rocks that fall behind the draped wire mesh still will be
required
To provide a neat appearance at the ends of the draped wire mesh a 0 5 inch
diameter stability cable should be used to tuck the edges of the draped wire at the sides

670 01 Description This section is for providing the minimum requirements for designing and
constructing a rockfall protection system draped wire mesh and modified draped wire mesh on the slope
according to the Contract Documents
670 02 Rockfall Protection Netting Rockfall protection netting draped wire mesh and modified
draped wire mesh and the required fittings and hardware shall conform to the details shown in the
Contract Documents or shall conform to the design drawings prepared by the Contractor s U S licensed
Civil Engineer experienced in the design of rockfall protection systems and accepted by the CO for
implementation
The rockfall protection netting draped wire mesh and modified draped wire mesh shall be of non
raveling construction and shall consist of a uniform triple twisted hexagonal mesh of zinc coated steel
wire The zinc coated wire core shall have a diameter of approximately U S Gage 12 The wires used in
the fabrication of the netting shall comply with or exceed Federal Specifications QQ W 461H possessing
soft tempered tensile strength of 70 000 psi as specified in ASTM A 641 Class 3 coating and a PVC
coating extruded onto the wire core with zinc coating before weaving the coated wire into a triple twisted
hexagonal mesh having uniform approximate openings of 3 inches by 4 4 inches or smaller
The overall diameter zinc coated wire core plus PVC coating of the rockfall protection netting draped
wire mesh and modified draped wire mesh shall be 0 1363 inches Selvedge wire shall be of heavily
zinc coated wire core of 0 1338 inches in diameter approximately U S Gage 10 coated with PVC and
having an overall diameter zinc coated wire core plus PVC coating of 0 1368 inches Lacing wire
where required shall be of heavily zinc coated wire core of 0 0866 inches in diameter approximately
coating of 0 1166 inches The above wire diameters are subject to a tolerance limit of 0 004 inches
according to ASTM A 641
