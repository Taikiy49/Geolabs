SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway rehabilitation project generally 
encountered medium dense to dense poorly-graded sands with silt to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface along the roadway 
alignment under consideration for this project.  We did not encounter groundwater in the 
drilled shallow borings at the time of our field exploration, except towards the intersection 
of Wakea Avenue and Puunene Avenue.  In Boring No. 101, groundwater was 
encountered at a depth of about 4.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
Because the project will involve the reconstruction of the existing roadway, we have 
performed a detailed economic comparison involving both flexible and rigid pavement 
sections for the project in accordance with the latest guidelines provided by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements will be constructed over the in-situ sandy soils in the shallow 
subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data provided for our 
pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction considerations, it 
is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway rehabilitation project may consist of 4 
inches of AC over 6 inches of ACB on 4 inches of ASB.  It should be noted that CBR tests 
and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during 
construction to confirm that the above pavement design section is adequate. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Wakea Avenue Pavement Rehabilitation project 
in Kahului on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located 
between these two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting 
the two volcanic domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Wakea Avenue within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway between Puunene Avenue and Kaahumanu Avenue except for the 
additional turn lanes at the intersection areas.  The approximate limits of the Wakea 
Avenue roadway are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut 
grass, sidewalks at some locations, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils 
along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown sandy silts 
and tan coralline sands with silt. The existing Wakea Avenue roadway alignment 
generally slopes up from the eastern terminus at Puunene Avenue to the intersection 
with Kea Street.  The roadway then slopes down from the intersection with Kea Street to 
the western terminus at the intersection with Kaahumanu Avenue. 
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in March 2012 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under 
consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the portion of Wakea Avenue from 
Puunene Avenue to the intersection of Kamehameha Avenue within the existing 
pavements under consideration falls into this category and appear to be generally 
fair with minor signs of structural defects observed. Overall pavement deterioration 
is restricted to light alligator cracking of low to moderate severity, as well as a few 
rough AC patches along utility trenches. The ride characteristic of the pavement 
was generally fair.  
2.2.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
In general, the remaining alignment from Kamehameha Avenue to Kaahumanu 
Avenue falls under this category.  The existing asphaltic concrete pavements were 
observed to be in poor condition with moderately to highly distressed areas. 
Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to be widespread and generally 
moderate to high in severity. The poor areas consist of elongated, severely 
distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of up to 1.5 inches, 
rutting and some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes. Potholes patched 
with AC material were observed at various locations along the alignment. The ride 
characteristic of the roadway was observed to be generally fair to irregular and 
bumpy in the areas of poor pavements.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway under 
consideration is underlain by sandy soils with little or no cohesion.  As a result, the 
subgrade may not be able to compact into a dense mass to receive placement of the 
asphalt concrete base (ACB) or Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) layer.  Therefore, we 
recommend providing a 4-inch layer of aggregate subbase between the upper ACB or 
PCC layer and the underlying sandy subgrade soils.  The aggregate subbase will serve 
to provide confinement of the sandy soils and a compacted surface for the placement of 
the ACB or PCC.  Based on this construction consideration, we recommend using the 
following flexible and rigid pavement design sections for the roadways under 
consideration.

An economic analysis was performed on the two pavement design options 
presented above to evaluate the initial construction cost and life cycle cost of the 
pavement structural sections. Based on our cost comparison of the pavement design 
options, it appears that the flexible pavement structural section (Option A) would result 
in the most economical pavement design. The economic analysis results are presented 
on Plates C-6 through C-8 in Appendix C. 
The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes to help 
detect and collapse near-surface loose zones. The vibratory drum roller should be 
operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate subbase materials required should consist of crushed basaltic 
aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard 
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase material 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D 1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D 2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments.

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Construction Monitoring 
Geolabs should be retained to provide geotechnical engineering services during 
the project construction. The critical items of construction monitoring include 
observation of the subgrade preparation and observation of the placement and 
compaction testing of the fill and subbase materials. A Geolabs representative should 
also monitor other aspects of earthwork construction to observe compliance with the 
intent of the design concepts, specifications, or recommendations and to expedite 
suggestions for design changes that may be required in the event that subsurface 
conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared. The 
recommendations provided herein are contingent upon such observations. 
If the actual soil conditions encountered during construction are different from 
those assumed or considered in this report, then appropriate design modifications 
should be made.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the test borings, bulk samples, and laboratory test data. 
Variations of conditions between and beyond the test borings and bulk samples may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein. 
The test boring locations are approximate, having been estimated by taping from 
reference points and visible features shown on the Topographic Survey Plans provided 
by the County of Maui - Department of Public Works, Engineering Division on 
May 11, 2012. Elevations of the field borings were estimated by interpolation from the 
contour lines shown on the same plan. The physical locations and elevations of the 
borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made to formulate this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, temperature, and other 
factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignments by drilling 
and sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 106. The borings 
extended to depths of about 5 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Result of the pocket penetrometer test is 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-1. 
Two laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D 2844) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3.
