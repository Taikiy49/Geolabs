INTRODUCTION
This report presents the findings of our injection well performance evaluation for the
Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant in Kahuku on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
findings and recommendations presented herein are based on observations recorded
during our site visit the performance of short term injection testing of the wells and
communication with the operators and manager of the treatment plant facility The
approximate location of the treatment plant facility and the injection well field are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated
and the State of Hawaii Department of Health Underground Injection
Control UIC Injection Well Status Report Guidelines dated October 1999 This injection
well performance evaluation report covers the current monitoring period which extends
from June 2008 through May 2009

SECTION 1 GENERAL
1 1 Project Considerations
The Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for
the disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Turtle Bay Resort
development The majority of the effluent treated at the plant may be used for irrigation
of the resort golf courses within the Turtle Bay Resort complex Therefore the wells are
used for the injection of treated effluent on a stand by basis during periods of time when
the effluent is not being utilized for irrigation purposes
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit UIC Permit
No UO 1532 issued for the facility it is required that a professional geologist or
engineer that is familiar with injection well operations perform routine well monitoring
Geolabs Inc has prepared this injection well evaluation report to provide the owner the
operator and the regulatory agency with information regarding the performance of the
injection wells to aid in the prevention of adverse public health effects from an injection
well system malfunction or failure This evaluation is intended to assist in ascertaining
that the well system remains operational
1 2 Purpose and Scope

2 2 Well Field Description
The wells are on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of Golf
Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and the
moat around Punahoolapa Marsh The topography of the well field area is generally
level with some topographic mounds and depressions associated with the adjacent golf
course terrain Based on a topographic map of the well field and vicinity ground surface
elevations range from about 5 to 6 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults of approximately
4 feet by 4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The concrete vaults are
covered with hinged steel plate doors and are approximately flush with the adjacent
grass covered ground surface
Inside the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with flange plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant personnel for the well inspection and performance of the
injection tests In 1994 two inch diameter inspection ports were installed in the well
covers to eliminate the need for removal of the entire top plate during future
inspection testing However to allow complete access to the well interior for the
performance of the short term injection tests and visual inspection of the exposed well
casing the top plate of each well was removed

Typically only two of the three wells are placed in use for effluent disposal when
the well field system is activated The third well is typically off line on a rotating basis so

3 2 Injection Well Re Development
Based on information provided by the treatment plant operator the three injection
wells were aerated and pumped out re developed once during the past 12 month
operational period The wells were aerated May 19 through May 22 2009 in a routine
effort to maintain the disposal capacity of the system
A 130 cubic foot per minute cfm air compressor was utilized to blow out the
injection wells by lowering the air line into the bottom of the well The discharge of well
water produced by the airlift cleaning was contained within the well vault and pumped to
a tanker truck for proper disposal Based on our discussion with the treatment plant
operator during the aeration of the wells two tanker truck loads of effluent
about 5 000 gallons were pumped and hauled from each well

3 5 Short Term Injection Tests
The short term injection tests were performed for each of the three wells by
diverting the available effluent into each well separately Each well was isolated from
the others for the duration of each test by adjusting the valves controlling the effluent
flow to individual wells The following is a generalized summary of the injection testing
procedure
Prior to the injection testing all three wells were isolated off line The individual
tests were performed by opening only the control valve for the well being tested The
valve was opened slowly while observing the head level in the well The control valve
was slowly opened until the head level rose to near the top of the well at which time
opening of the valve was discontinued to avert a well overflow The injection well control
valve was not adjusted for the duration of the injection testing unless the head level
approached the well top and needed to be reduced The short term injection testing of
each well averaged about 40 minutes
During injection testing the head level response was continually measured and
recorded using an electronic piezometer pressure transducer and data logging
recorder linked to a portable computer Head level readings were also recorded at
selected intervals by manual measurement and recording for verification Thus the
head level response could be monitored continuously to record the fluctuation of head
level within the wells Manual readings collected from the totalizing flow meter were
recorded during the short term injection test to obtain the total quantity of injected
effluent and to estimate the average rate of flow into each well The duration of the
short term injection tests was about 40 minutes The average incoming flow rate to the
well field from the WWTP was calculated to range between about 500 gallons per
minute and 560 gallons per minute during the injection testing According to the plant
personnel the incoming flow rate to the well field is mainly controlled by the volume of
effluent flow passing through the WWTP sand filtration system The flow rate normally
fluctuates as the various sand filters operate in a switching cycle

as the individual injection well is opened or closed some delay and surge of flow is
encountered which affects the short term head level response In addition the
effluent flow from the WWTP typically fluctuates as the sand filters operate on a
cyclic basis Based on our observations the average incoming flow rate from the
WWTP during the current injection testing was about 400 to 600 gallons per minute
which was less than the available flow from the plant during last years injection
testing
Based on our observations of the static water level in the wells measured with the

disposal capacity of Well Nos 1 and 2 has substantially decreased relative to the

extended periods of time and the effects of reduced program of well

3 6 Recommendations
Based on the short term injection test results and the available well usage data
we believe the disposal capacity of the three injection wells has decreased relative to
the previous well performance evaluation conducted in May 2008 To maintain the
optimal disposal capacity of the well system we recommend well re development and
cleaning twice per year continued well operation and rotation of well usage and well
field usage at least once per week to reduce possible bio fouling It is our opinion that
W O INC Page 21
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS
the wells should be activated at least bi weekly with a minimum average monthly well
field flow of about 200 000 gallons or greater to maintain good operational capacity of
the well field system
Injection wells sustain losses in capacity over time due to natural biological and
chemical fouling processes or other introduced factors such as suspended solids or
debris contained in the effluent In addition extended periods of well inactivity and the
introduction of poor quality effluent containing excessive suspended solids may cause
acceleration of the normal losses in capacity It should be noted that some loss in
capacity may be permanent and irreversible however it has also been proven that
redevelopment of injection wells can aid in the recovery of lost capacity provided that
the proper methods are utilized on a regular basis
Based on our injection well evaluation we recommend that the owner operator
continue to operate the injection wells on a routine rotating basis in an attempt to
maintain the disposal capacity of the system In addition we recommend that the
owner operator closely monitor and document the operation of each of the wells to
assure that overflows or back ups related to a well failure are detected prior to their
occurrence In the event an overflow of a well or other failure occurs and the situation
cannot be remedied the owner operator should promptly notify the Department of
Health Safe Drinking Water Branch
We believe that by maintaining the use of the wells on a rotating basis especially
during periods of low effluent availability the current operational performance of the
wells could be maintained Keeping the wells operational is not normally a problem
however we believe it is crucial to maintain rotating operation of all the wells during the
periods of time when low effluent flow are experienced We suggest that the actual well
usage rotation be recorded in the Daily Operating Logs to show the pattern of individual
well usage
We recommend that the owner operator continue recording field observations
such as_ the following 1 notes regarding the overall system operation
W O INC Page 22
Hawaii California

2 documentation of well maintenance performed including well cleaning and
replacement of faulty parts and instruments 3 observation of the head levels within
the wells prior to and during injection events 4 daily flow volumes 5 a log of individual
well usage and 6 notes pertaining to the increase or decrease of the total flow to the
plant for treatment This information would be used to evaluate the future performance
of the injection wells In addition the results of our short term injection testing of the
wells would be used to evaluate the operation of the wells with respect to prior well
inspection data
Generally injection well re development should be considered if head levels
continue to increase over time or when well failures such as overflow or system back
ups may be anticipated However when the wells begin to fail it may often be too late to
restore the capacity Therefore based on the effectiveness of the current well
re development schedule we believe it may be prudent to continue well cleaning at
biannual intervals
Well re development should be performed by a professional experienced with
coastal injection well construction and rehabilitation The selected re development
method should be carefully evaluated based on the existing well design proper
containment and disposal of wastewater generated by the activity and other factors
specific to the site and operation of the wells
Based on our observations the valves that control the flow to each injection well
appear to be working satisfactorily however the valves are not visible due to their
continual submergence below water Therefore the valve cannot be visually inspected
In addition the long term and continual submergence of the valve may accelerate
corrosion of the hardware We do not believe that this is a critical concern however
consideration may be given to reconstructing the valve vault so that it is sealed from the
infiltration of groundwater to facilitate inspection for signs of wear
In consideration of other possible long term improvements that could facilitate
monitoring of the injection well performance it is suggested that individual flow metering

devices be installed between each well control valve and well head The installation of
additional flow meters would provide direct readout of flow data for each individual well
which would improve the direct correlation between the flow rate and the head level
response in the well As a result changes in the well flow rate could be monitored and
adjustments to the control valve could be made to achieve the desired flow rates that
can be compared from year to year Currently only the total flow to the well field is
known therefore when more than one well is operating simultaneously it is not possible
to determine the actual flow rate into the individual wells This scenario occurs when the
actual capacity of a well is lower than the flow entering the well field from the plant and
a second well must be opened to avoid overflow at the well being tested Currently the
only means to vary the individual well flow rate is to make adjustments to the total flow
entering from the treatment plant weir However due to the significant distance between
the treatment plant and the well field a 15 to 20 minute delay is typically experienced
before the flow adjustment is observed at the well field In addition individual well flow
meters may be used to detect possible leaks and could be used to record the total flow
to each well for comparison between well usage
Finally based on our understanding of the requirements of the facility UIC permit
the annual injection well inspection should be performed in the month of May since the
annual monitoring period is set to run from June through May The owner and facility
operator is reminded to periodically review the requirements of the UIC permit set forth
by the State of Hawaii Department of Health Safe Drinking Water Branch to assure
that the operational procedures and reporting requirements are adhered to
