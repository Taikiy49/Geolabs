As requested we have made a study of the near surface soil conditions
below the above referenced lot The purpose of the study was to eva
luate the suitability of the near surface soils as foundation support
for the proposed house The investigation consisted of drilling and
sampling three 3 borings at the proposed building location
performing laboratory soil tests and reviewing the grading and
compaction test records pertaining to the project site This report
summarizes our findings and recommendations

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The review of grading plan and compaction test records and the results
of site reconnaissance indicate that the portion of the lot where most
of the house is planned is underlain by a layer of grayish brown
expansive clay and boulders at least 5 feet thick
The remaining portion of the lot behind the house area is probably
underlain by natural colluvial deposits transported from the upper
hillside area These deposits are typically heterogenous and vary
from clayey silt low expansion potential to clay high expansion
potential
Detailed descriptions of the soils encountered in the borings are
presented on the attached logs Plates A 1 through A 3 The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the attached
Site Plan Plate 1

soil can be classified as clay CH according to the Unified Soil
Classification System Laboratory one inch ring swell tests indicate
that the soil has high shrink swell potential The laboratory test
results are summarized on Plate B 1l

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that most of the lot is underlain by
more than 5 feet of grayish brown clay and boulders The laboratory
test results on this material generally indicated that it has a high
expansion potential Therefore for Lot 33 the slab on grade and
foundation construction would require special procedures to reduce the
shrink swell effect of the subsoils
Foundation
Post and beam construction is highly desirable and recommended due to
the sloping terrain and expansive soil conditions The post and beam
construction generally results in lower foundation costs and the
individual footings can be readily adjusted should the need occur
Where utilized concrete slab on grade should also be properly
designed for expansive soil conditions
Due to the expansive soil and sloping grade we recommend that the
individual post footings be extended a minimum depth of 36 inches
below the ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 865 71 Page 5
The footings should be poured in place and the excavation backfilled
and compacted to provide an impervious layer over the footing
An allowable bearing value of 2500 p s f may be used for footings
bearing on the stiff natural ground or compacted fill The bottom of
the footing excavations should be neat recompacted and free of loose
soils or debris
If a footing is located next to a utility line it should be deepened
to the level of the adjacent utility trench bottom
Surf ace water should be diverted away from the foundations This is
especially important in the rear portion of the house to reduce
ponding of surface run off water and possible saturation and
settlement or heaving of the foundation soils

Slab On Grade
Concrete slab on grade is planned for the garage area It
should be noted that the on site clay soils are expansive and
unsuitable as slab subgrade soils In order to reduce the
expansive effects of the clay the expansive clays should be
overexcavated down for a depth of 3 feet below the slab
subgrade and then replaced with non expansive borrow soils
such as select borrow crusher waste or coral compacted to a
minimum of 90 of the maximum dry density as determined in
accordance with the ASIM D 1557 78 test procedure
A 4 inch layer of cushion should be provided below the slab and
compacted to 90 of its maximum dry density In order to
protect against moisture infiltration a moisture barrier
membrane may be incorporated below the slab
GEOLABS HAWAII
Concrete Driveway and Sidewalks
A layer of more than 5 feet of expansive soils was encountered in the
front portion of the lot driveway area Since the driveway can
tolerate some amount of heaving the clay overexcavation and
replacement can be reduced to 2 feet below the driveway subgrade level
Where 2 or more feet of fill is planned select non expansive borrow
material should be used to cap over the expansive clay Where less
than 2 feet of fill is planned overexcavation and replacement of the
expansive clay with select non expansive material is required The
above capping requirements should be followed for a minimum distance
of 3 feet beyond the driveway
For the driveway planned joints are recommended at about 8 to 10 foot
intervals to allow for some relative movement of the concrete
For future sidewalks the capping of the clay by over excavation and
replacement similar to the driveway is recommended to reduce the
potential heaving from the underlying expansive clay soil

The existing terrain at the site generally slopes down at about 30
gradient

Preliminary plans indicate that most of the ground area below the
house will be cut to a relatively level ground and no fill is
anticipated
Fill if any and backfill should be a select non expansive borrow
material placed in maximum 8 inch lifts and compacted to a minimum of
90 of its maximum dry density established by ASTM D 1557 78 The
on site clay soil should not be used for fill material below
structures and behind retaining walls
Generally cut slopes should not exceed 4 1 H V in the expansive
clay and boulder soils therefore retaining structures may be
required to minimize the cut
The finished grade outside the slabs pavements and foundations
should be shaped to avoid ponding conditions and to shed water away
from the structures

CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the soils engineer be present during earthwork
construction particularly the fill placement slope cuts and to
monitor the contractor s grading operations
It is also recommended that the footing excavations be observed by a
soils engineer during construction to see that adequate bearing has
been obtained and that the subsurface conditions are consistent with
the finding of our subsurface investigation
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon the above
inspections
