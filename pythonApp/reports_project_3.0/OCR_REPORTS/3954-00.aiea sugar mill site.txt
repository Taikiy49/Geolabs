INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Aiea Sugar Mill Site located northeast of Ulune Street northwest of
Kulawea Street and southeast of Hakina Street in the District of Aiea on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design associated with the mass
grading construction which includes the design of bridge abutment foundations road
pavements utilities excavation and fill placement only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
REGIONAL V V
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae Is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range and is
composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava from the Koolau Volcanic Series
These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt
interbedded with thin clinker layers The lava flows have weathered in place and are
characterized by the red colors of the soil The brown soils are generally ancient alluvial
deposited over the surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that the proposed bridge
abutments may be supported on the underlying highly weathered basaltic rock at a depth
of 15 feet below the existing grade
Due to the variable expansion potential of the on site material it will be difficult to
perform selective grading to create a capping layer of low expansive material within the
upper 3 feet In general the site contains moderate expansive material with some areas
containing low to very high expansive material We recommend that the expansive soil
situation be handled individually for each lot on a case by case basis Future
slab on grade design should account for the on site low to very high expansive material
Based on the low CBR value of the on site material and the anticipated traffic
loading the recommended pavement section consists of 2 inches of asphaltic concrete
6 inches of base course and 18 inches of select borrow subbase material
Due to the erosion potential near the steep stream bank we recommend that the
pavement and sidewalks be set back a minimum of 5 feet from the tops of slopes
Based on the tests performed for a soil corrosion study a soil corrosion rating of 2
is recommended for design of water utilities

Bridge Abutment Foundations
Based on the following conditions we recommend that the bridge abutment
footings be deepened to the underlying soft basalt rock with a minimum embedment
depth of 15 feet below the existing grade These conditions are
1 Presence of poorly compacted fill material in the upper 6 to 8 feet below
existing grade
2 Potential for loss of lateral support and undermining from scouring
An allowable bearing pressure of 5 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be
utilized for the design of bridge abutment foundations bearing on the underlying highly
weathered basalt rock These bearing values are for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of 15 feet below the existing grade
We estimate that footing settlements bearing on the underlying highly weathered
basalt rock as recommended herein to be less than one half inch total Differential
settlement is estimated to be less than one fourth inch
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
the footings should be deepened to expose the underlying firm materials
The bridge abutments should be designed assuming scour will remove the
material above the footing thus creating a retaining wall condition for the full height of the
GEOLABS HAWAII

abutment therefore no passive earth pressure should be considered Lateral loads
acting on the abutment may be resisted by frictional resistance developed between the
bottom of the foundation and highly weathered basalt rock Resistance due to friction
may be estimated using a coefficient of friction of 0 5 This value assumes that the
concrete for footings is placed directly against the footing excavations
For free standing wall conditions with level backfill an equivalent fluid pressure of
45 p c f may be used as the active pressure for the on site soils For restrained
conditions with level backfill an equivalent fluid pressure of 65 p c f may be used Note
that free standing walls are defined as walls that are allowed to rotate about 0 5 percent
of the wall height Surcharge and hydrostatic loads are not accounted for in the above
values Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges live loads and point loads should
be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 40 percent of the vertical surcharge
pressure acting on the entire height of the wall which is free to deflect cantilever may
be used in design For walls that are restrained a rectangular distribution equal to
55 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may
be used for design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the
surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
Backfill behind bridge abutments should consist of select granular fill material
compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over compaction of the
abutment backfill should be avoided
Bridge abutments should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable

the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to an

Sidewalk Design
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by soils
with moderate to very high swelling potential Unless sidewalks constructed above these
expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the
sidewalks resulting from shrinking and swelling of the soils due to changes in the moisture
content To reduce the potential for distress resulting from the swelling of the subgrade
soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with 6 to
12 inches of select borrow material The thickness of select borrow required will depend
on the expansion potential of the soil For soils with a CBR swell in excess of 6 percent
12 inches of select borrow material should be used

moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils The prepared select
borrow and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final
placement of concrete The subgrade soil under the sidewalk area should be
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction The select borrow material should be
moisture conditioned to slightly above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill and select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
Fill material with more than 30 percent oversized material normally cannot be
tested using conventional testing procedures For these layers of fill compaction
should continue until the mass does not weave or creep under the weight of the
compactor
Select borrow subbase and base course materials should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be
performed by Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the Contractor in
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on
each fill lift Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the
specified compaction It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the
GEOLABS HAWAII

The finished grades adjacent to the bridge abutments should be sloped to shed
water away from the walls and to reduce the potential for ponding Unless covered by
concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill behind the bridge abutment should consist of
impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the walls
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause hydrostatic
pressures Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from walls and foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Nine resistivity tests ASTM G 57 78 nine pH tests ASTM G 51 four sulfate
tests and four chloride tests were performed by Environmental
Laboratory of the Pacific on selected samples of the soils to evaluate the corrosion
potential of the soil Results of these tests are presented on Plate B 1

soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification
Results of this test are presented on Plate B 2
Four Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 3
Twelve 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
and remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge
loads These tests were run on air dried samples Results of these swell tests are
presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of
near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade support
Results of the CBR test are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a
near surface soil Results of the test are presented on Plate B 8
