As requested we have made a preliminary geologic reconnaissance of
the near surface soil conditions below the above referenced lot The
purpose of the reconnaissance was to evaluate the suitability of the near
surface soils for design of site grading and building foundation support
for the proposed house
The reconnaissance consisted of a review of available soils and
geologic maps and literature concerning the area b a general geologic
reconnaissance of the project area including the inspection of the local
road cuts and conversations with local contractors c a walk through
geologic reconnaissance of the proposed house location and d reviewing
the lot site plan This report summarizes our findings and recommend
ations

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed single family residence consists of a one or two story
wood structure approximately 70 by 170 feet in plan dimension Based on
the preliminary plans provided to us the house will be set back about
200 feet from Lanikeha Way
Concrete slab on grade and post and beam construction is contemplated
for the house
Cuts up to about 7 feet deep are planned for the northerly and
southerly sides of the house Some shallow fills are proposed over
portions of the site
CMU retaining walls less than 6 feet in height are contemplated
CLIMATIC CONDITIONS
The average annual rainfall at the project site is between 5 to 10
inches and the average temperature varies between 75 and 85 degrees F
The area is subjected to strong tradewinds forced through the wind gap
between the Kohala Mountains and Mauna Kea

GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the
activity of 5 shield volcanoes These are Kohala which is long
extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during recent geologic times
Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both
still active
The site is located on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea mountain
which makes up the northern part of the island and consists largely of
the older Hamakua Volcanic Series The lava flows at the site are AA
basalt types with dense zones of hard rock and loose zones of clinkers
The clinker phase is highly permeable Lava tubes are generally not
encountered in AA flows
A thin dry desiccated and erodible layer of volcanic ash locally
referred to as Pahala Ash covers the surface of the flows
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
Lot 17 is a flag lot located in the central eastern portion of the
Fairways at Mauna Kea North development west of the end of Lanikeha Way
and about 400 feet north of the Mauna Kea Resort entry road
A house is currently under construction at Lot 19 situated about 200
feet east of the site

Lot 17 generally slopes downward towards the west at about 10 to 30
per cent gradients with steeper slopes along the northerly and southerly

The existing elevation of the site generally varies from about 240 to

The topography is characterized by fairly smooth rolling hills that

Soil Conditions
From visual observations of the surface exposures inspection of
excavations for Lot 19 nearby and discussions with local contractors it
appears that most of the area is covered with a thick probably less than
3 foot thick surface soil layer of dry desicated sandy silt ash mixed
with cobbles and boulders over AA lava flows

General
The field engineering and geological reconnaissance indicates that
the site is suitable for the proposed house construction
The competent volcanic materials should provide adequate bearing for
the proposed structure and related utilities
Site Grading
Site Preparation
Prior to commencement of site grading surface vegetation and organic
material should be removed and wasted off site In areas to receive
fill or at grade construction tree roots should be removed to a
minimum depth of 3 feet and the resulting excavation backfilled and
compacted to 90 of its maximum density as established by ASTM D
1557 78
In fairly level areas prior to receiving fill the existing ground
should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches moistened and compacted
to 90 maximum density as per ASTM D 1557 78
Loose ash and clinker below the house area should be removed

In general the excavated rock material less than 12 inches in size
may be used as general fill material If the excavated rock material
does not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material
the surficial ash soil may be mixed into the excavated rocky material
to increase the percentage of fines to provide a better graded
material for compaction and increase the stability of the fills
Large boulders should be wasted off the site

Embankment fills should be placed in horizontal loose lifts up to 18
inches thick and compacted to its maximum density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in
excavated volcanic rock material due to the large particle size
Instead a testing program consisting of visual observations and
test strips to determine the lift thickness and number of passes of a
compactor required to achieve the desired compaction should be
conducted at the start of the project The number of passes can then
be used as the criteria for compaction
Fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than 5 1 H V
should be keyed and benched into the underlying consolidated clinker

Excavation
The field reconnaissance indicates that the site may be underlain at
fairly shallow depths by dense AA basalt mantled with partly
consolidated clinker and a thin layer of ash soil
It is anticipated that the clinker may be excavated with normal
excavating methods Much of the upper fractured rock layers can be
ripped with heavy equipment Some hard digging may require the use
of a hoe ram and even some blasting if large accretionary lava balls
boulders are encountered Also much of the upper fractured rock
layers can be ripped with heavy equipment
The more massive basalt will likely require special excavating
procedures such as hoe ram or blasting to make the proposed cuts or
utility trenches
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface
materials are based on our visual observations of the existing road
cuts nearby and excavations for the house at Lot 19

In general two horizontal to one vertical fill slopes are

Foundations
We believe the on site rocky ground and compacted granular fill
resulting from the above site grading can provide suitable foundation
support for the proposed building
Spread and wall footing foundations may be used for the proposed
structure
All exterior footing bottoms should be embedded a minimum of 24
inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade The minimum embedment
for interior footings may be reduced to 18 inches below the finished
grade
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1497 00 Page 9
For the footing bearing on the compacted clinker rock fill an
allowable net soil bearing pressure of 3 000 p s f may be used The
recommended soil bearing could be increased for footing bearing on the
natural clinker densely backfilled with a choking fill material to fill
the near surface voids in the natural formation We believe if
required an allowable bearing value up to 4 000 p s f could be used
These values may be increased by one third when temporary wind or seismic
loads are considered
Slabs and spread foundations located close to the tops of the fill
slopes should have sufficient embedment to obtain a minimum horizontal
set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outer edge of the footing
to the slope face
The ground surface should be shaped to divert surface runoff away
from building foundations and slabs
It is anticipated that the footings if constructed in accordance
with the above recommendations will have negligible settlements
On Grade Slab
Based on a non expansive slab subgrade condition anticipated at this
site on grade slab should be placed over a 4 inch layer of base course
compacted to 90 of its maximum density as established by ASTM D 1557

78 The thickened edge around the on grade slab should extend a minimm
of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished ground surface
Depending on the structure of the underlying rock formation a
choking layer consisting of well graded rock materials may be required
between the underlying rugged rock surface and the upper cushion fill or
concrete slab
To reduce differential settlement where the slab is partly on cut in
rock and partly on fill the cut portions should be over excavated about
one foot and then recompacted to match the adjacent subgrade density
Utilities
It is anticipated that most of the trenching will be done in the
unconsolidated clinker material However some boulders or areas of
dense basalt may be encountered especially in the deeper trenches
Select on site material generally less than 6 inch in size may be
used as backfill to 2 feet above the top of culvert and could be
compacted by water tamping The ash soils are not suitable for water
tamping and would require mechanical tamping if this soil is used as
trench backfill Above this level general embankment fill of 12 inch
minus may be used in accordance with our recommendations for fill up to
within 2 feet of finished subgrade

Retaining Walls
It is our understanding that retaining walls generally less than 8
feet high may be required The guidelines for the retaining wall designs
are listed below
Based on visual observations consolidated clinker material or
basalt rock is anticipated For the foundations of the
retaining walls the footings should be embedded a minimum of 2
feet into the consolidated clinker material or 6 inches into
dense basalt whichever occurs first
An allowable bearing value of 3000 p s f may be utilized for
the filled areas or clinker material and 6000 p s f may be
utilized for dense basalt These values are for total dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third when temporary wind
and seismic loads are considered
For lateral earth pressures the following can be used
Walls unrestrained at the top 35 p s f equivalent
active condition level fluid pressure
backfill per foot of depth
Wall restrained at the top 50 p s f equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure
level backfill per foot of depth
Additional loads due to traffic or surcharge should be included
where applicable

Clean granular materials such as the on site gravel and
sand sized clinker materials should be used as backfill
directly behind the walls This would reduce the compactive
effort required for the backfill and the build up of additional
lateral pressures on the walls Impervious on site silty
material should be used for the top 2 feet of the backfill
A friction factor of 0 5 between concrete footing and basalt
rock or clinker may be utilized to determine the sliding
resistance of the wall
A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth may
be used for the wall footing on level areas The passive
pressure should be ignored for walls on slopes
A subsurface drainage system subdrains or weepholes should be
utilized behind the walls Subdrains should be properly
constructed to drain water away from the wall
