SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
engineering analyses performed for the Main Road Lighting Replacement, 
Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus, project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based 
on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The 
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of light standard 
foundations, trench excavation and backfilling, and pavement restoration. The findings 
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of 
this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the replacement of the existing main road lighting system at 
the Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The planned improvements will involve installation of new light standards, 
electrical duct lines and other appurtenances.  
1.3 
Purpose and Scope 
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration is to obtain an overview 
of the surface and subsurface conditions along the proposed lighting replacement 
alignment to develop a generalized soil/rock data set to formulate geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the design and construction of the new lighting 
system. In order to accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program, in 
accordance with our fee proposal dated June 12, 2014, generally consisting of the 
following tasks and work efforts:

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is situated on the southerly facing slope of an intra-valley ridge 
on the southwestern facing flank of Koolau.  The geologic conditions underlying the site 
are the result of in-situ chemical weathering of basaltic lava flows.  The shallow soils are 
generally residual soils consisting of reddish to brown silty clays which grade with depth 
to silty sand saprolitic soils which, in turn, grade with depth to highly weathered basaltic 
rock.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by stiff 
clayey residual soils overlying silty saprolitic soils.  Weathered basaltic rock formation 
was encountered in the deeper portion of some borings. 
It is anticipated that the new electrical ductlines would have invert depths on the 
order of about 2 feet to 3 or more feet below the existing ground surface. The borings 
generally indicate stiff clay soils within this zone. Based on the relatively competent 
subsurface soil conditions encountered, we believe that conventional excavation 
methods may be used for installation of the electrical ductlines.  However, there is a 
potential that remnant boulders or isolated ledges of rock may be encountered at 
shallow depths at the site. 
For minor structures, based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the 
project site, we recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf to 
design the shallow foundations bearing on the stiff to hard on-site silty and clayey soils 
or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing value is for 
supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
It is anticipated that the vertical axial loads for the light standards will be minimal.  
The principal considerations for these foundations will be lateral resistance to sliding 
and resistance to overturning as a result of wind loads on the vertical poles. 
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration, it is our opinion that 
lateral sliding forces may be resisted by friction developed between the bottom of the 
foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure against the near-vertical 
faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings 
bearing on the in-situ clayey soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished 
grades. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This 
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless covered by 
pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil

3.1 
Foundations 
The primary structural element will be foundations for the new light standards. 
Other structural considerations will be handholes and other minor structures. 
For the minor structures, based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the 
project site, we recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf to 
design the shallow foundations bearing on the stiff to hard on-site silty and clayey soils 
or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing value is for 
supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Foundations located next to utility trenches and/or easements should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge 
of the utility trench, or the footings should be embedded to a depth as deep as the 
inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent 
below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for 
appreciable foundation settlement. 
The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be recompacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Soft and/or loose materials that are 
encountered at the footing subgrade level should be over-excavated to expose the 
underlying firm or dense materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled with 
imported select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative

3.1.1 Light Pole Foundations 
It is anticipated that the vertical axial loads for the light standards will be minimal.  
The principal considerations for these foundations will be lateral resistance to 
sliding and resistance to overturning as a result of wind loads on the vertical poles. 
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration, it is our opinion that lateral 
sliding forces may be resisted by friction developed between the bottom of the 
foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure against the near-
vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used 
for footings bearing on the in-situ clayey soils or new compacted fills needed to 
achieve the finished grades. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pcf. This assumes that the soils

supported by shallow circular shafts with a diameter of about 18 inches and 
depth of about 7 feet.

3.3 
Trench Excavation Support 
Deeper excavations may be required where the electrical lines and ducts 
traverse under existing underground utilities.  Where excavations greater than 5 feet in 
depth are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be used.  Based 
on the existing site conditions (i.e., active roadways in an educational and residential 
campus), we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations are not 
practical.  On this basis, trench walls would have to be cut vertically requiring the use of 
shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative (i.e., competent person 
as defined in OSHA regulations) should be on-site at all times during excavation and 
construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe changing or unforeseen 
conditions, such as, high groundwater, inappropriate construction sequence or 
techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential 
for excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the

3.4 
Pipe or Duct Line Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe or duct line has a significant 
effect on the load supporting capacity of the pipe or duct line.  Therefore, the pipe 
bedding is an important design consideration.  For this project, a minimum of 4 inches of 
compacted granular bedding material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should be provided below the pipe or duct line.  
3.5 
Trench Backfill 
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches 
above the top of the pipes or duct lines should consist of select free draining granular 
material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and 
resulting stresses on the pipe or duct.  In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining 
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and 
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes or duct line. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and damage to the piping or duct. 
The trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes or duct lines to 
18 inches below pavement subgrade may consist of excavated on-site material less 
than 3 inches in size. Backfill within 18 inches of the pavement subgrade should consist 
of imported non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coral, basalt or recycled 
asphalt. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger 
than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should also contain less than 
25 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 25 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less.

3.6 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfills should be moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  The upper 2 feet below the pavement grade 
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.  Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the 
specified compaction.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and bulk samples. Variations of conditions 
between and beyond the field borings and bulk samples may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If 
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the layout plans transmitted by 
Bennett Engineers, Inc. on or about November 14, 2014. Elevations of the borings were 
obtained based on their relative position as plotted on Google Earth™. The field boring 
locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by 
the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, which were drilled to depths of 
approximately 15.2 to 16.3 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.4. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in 
the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 
through A-4.  
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch OD split-barrel sampler with a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and 
third 6 inches (a total of 12 inches) of an 18-inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. Rock cores were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
