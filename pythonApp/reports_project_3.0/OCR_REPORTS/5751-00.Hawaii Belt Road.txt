This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering services performed
for the drainage improvements project in the vicinity of the Hakalau Bridge in the District of
North Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on
the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. These recommendations are intended for design input only. The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report.
Project Considerations
The project site is along Hawaii Belt Road (Route 16) between Mile Post (MP)
15.7 and 16.0 in the vicinity of Hakalau Bridge. We understand that off-site storm water
runoff from the mauka slopes and on-site storm water runoff along the shoulders of the
highway accumulates near the north end (Hamakua end) of Hakalau Bridge before it
discharges into the Hakalau River Basin.
To correct the drainage problems at the site, the existing flume (about 600 feet
north of the north abutment of Hakalau Bridge) will be removed and replaced with a
riprap channel. A stilling basin will be constructed at the shoulder of the highway below
the new riprap channel. A drain line will extend from the stilling basin to the north
abutment of Hakalau Bridge. The drainage water will be diverted onto the existing
service road, located below the bridge, and into existing drainage features. The portion
of the existing service road that will be exposed to the drainage water will be lined with
grouted rubble paving (GRP).

The portion of Hawaii Belt Road within the project limits is a two-lane asphaltic
concrete roadway. The site is situated between two cut slopes. The drainage
improvements are planned along the west side (mauka) of the highway. Below the bridge

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions by hand augering eleven borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 11, extending to depths of approximately
1.0 to 6.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operation on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate 3. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates 4 through 14.
The borings drilled at the proposed riprap channel generally encountered sandy silt
extending to depths of about 1.0 to 1.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Hand
auger refusal was encountered below the sandy silt layer.
The borings drilled along the proposed drain line alignment generally encountered
silty and clayey soils extending to depths of about 3.0 to 6.0 feet below the existing ground
surface. Hand auger refusal was encountered below the silty and clayey soils layer.
Based on our observations of the existing cut slope and the geology of the area, we
anticipate that basalt rock formation may be encountered below the silty and clayey soils.
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of the field
exploration. However, groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on seasonal
precipitation, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and other factors.

Discussion and Recommendations
Detailed discussions and recommendations for the project design are presented
in the following sections herein.
Riprap Channel
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by
near-surface sandy silt soils. Due to the high rainfall environment and the presence of
near-surface soils with high in-situ moisture contents (40 percent or greater), a 6-inch
thick layer of aggregate subbase course should be provided below the riprap channel to
serve as a protective cover or working platform.
Due to the presence of soils with high in-situ moisture contents, we do not
recommend scarifying the subgrade soils below the riprap channel. Therefore, we
recommend proof-rolling the riprap channel subgrade to provide a firm and unyielding
condition prior to the placement of the aggregate subbase course.
Drop Inlet Structures
We recommend placing a minimum 6-inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the inlet structures. An
allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for the
structures bearing on the cushion layer. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads
and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces.
Drain Line
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, it is likely
that hard basalt formation may be encountered during the trench excavations. It is
anticipated that conventional excavation techniques may be used for the proposed
excavations. However, excavation in the basalt formation may require the use of
hoerams or chipping. In addition, it should be noted that existing abandoned
underground utilities and other below grade concrete structures may exist at the site
and would require heavy equipment for excavation. In general, the contractor should
determine the method and equipment used for excavation, subject to practical limits and
safety considerations.

In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes for uniform support.
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel should also be used for
the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill
settlement and pipe damage.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade may consist of the excavated on-site soils with a maximum
particle size of 3 inches. The backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content, placed in about 8-inch loose lifts, and mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence. Where trenches are located below pavement
areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography, we envision that the site grading work for the
project will generally consist of minor cuts and fills.
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared thoroughly. Debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing
below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated
to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly
off-site. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation quantities.
After clearing and grubbing, the areas designated to receive fills and finished
subgrades in cut areas should be proof-rolled to provide a firm and unyielding subgrade
due to the high moisture soils at the site.
The materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of general
fill material, provided that they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be noted that the
on-site soils have high in-situ moisture contents (in excess of 40 percent). Therefore,
the contractor should anticipate difficulties in achieving the specified compaction
requirement using the on-site soils considering the relatively wet conditions at the site.

Therefore, consideration should be given to disposing the excavated on-site soils
(with high moisture contents) and importing the fill materials needed for the project.
Imported fill should consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as
crushed basalt or coral. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent.
Geolabs should test imported fill material for conformance with these recommendations
prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use.
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Compaction by water tamping, jetting,
or ponding should not be allowed for the on-site soils.
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe
whether undesirable materials are encountered and to confirm whether the exposed
soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration.

Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of subsoil conditions may occur,
and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is
underway. If variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate
the recommendations presented herein.
The field boring locations are approximate, having been estimated by taping from
the features shown on the Topographic Survey Map provided by Earth Tech, Inc. on
December 6, 2006. Elevations of the field borings were interpolated from the elevation
points and contour lines shown on the same map. The location and elevation of the field
boring should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the boring depict
the approximate boundaries between the soil and rock types, and as such, may denote a
gradual transition. Water level data from the boring was measured at the times shown on
the graphic representations and/or in the text of this report. These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, temperatures, and other
factors.
