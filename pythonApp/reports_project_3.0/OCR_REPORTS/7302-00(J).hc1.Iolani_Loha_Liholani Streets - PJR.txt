SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway reconstruction alignments 
generally encountered soft to very stiff clayey silts and silty clays extending to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 6 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the relatively poor conditions of the existing pavements, we recommend 
reconstructing the entire roadways within the project limits under consideration for this 
project with a new pavement section.  Because the project will involve the reconstruction 
of the existing roadways, we have performed a detailed economic comparison involving 
both flexible and rigid pavement sections in accordance with the latest guidelines provided 
by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing 
and Research Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements will be constructed over the in-situ clayey silt and silty clay soils in 
the shallow subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data 
provided for our pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction 
considerations, it is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway reconstruction project 
may consist of 2.5 inches of AC over 4 inches of ACB on 6 inches of AB.  It should be 
noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual 
subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design section is 
adequate. 
A layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 or equivalent) on a 
layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the 
new pavement structural section.  The reinforcing geogrid would serve to enhance the 
R-value of the subgrade materials by providing stabilization and reinforcement to the 
base/subbase/subgrade materials. 
An existing portion of Iolani Street, approximately 300 feet long, was constructed as 
concrete pavement.  It is our opinion that the existing concrete pavement may be 
reconstructed with 10.5 inches of PCC over 6 inches of ASB, 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Iolani Street, Loha Street, and Liholani Street 
Pavement Reconstruction project in Makawao on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway reconstruction project is along the entire Iolani Street, Loha Street 
(from Iolani Street to Old Haleakala Highway), and Liholani Street (from Pukalani Street 
to Aina Lani Drive) for a total distance of approximately 2.4 miles. The approximate 
limits of the roadway reconstruction project are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
We understand that the proposed pavement project will be federally funded. 
Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to the requirements and 
guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will be required. The 
new pavements will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement 
Design Manual, dated March 2002, prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
the roadways under consideration. The findings obtained from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing programs will be used as a basis to formulate geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the pavement reconstruction of the project roadways.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Iolani Street, Loha Street and Liholani Street within the project limits 
are generally two-lane undivided roadways, except for the additional shoulder parking 
lanes. The approximate project limits of the roadways are shown on the General Site 
Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut 
grass, paved asphalt concrete shoulders, concrete sidewalks, traffic signs and utility 
poles. The surface soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist 
of brown sandy silts. The existing Iolani Street roadway alignment slopes downwards 
from southeast to northwest throughout the limits of the roadway. Loha Street, from the 
intersection with the Old Haleakala Highway to where the road becomes Iolani Street, 
slopes downwards from northeast to southwest. The existing Liholani Street within the 
project limits slopes downwards westerly from where Liholani Street intersects with 
Pukalani Street down to Hokulani Street. The Liholani Street then meanders through the

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, Loha Street within the project limits, Iolani Street 
from the end cul-de-sac to the intersection of Alohilani Street, and Liholani Street 
from the Haulani Street intersection to the intersection of Aina Lani Drive fell into 
this category and appeared to have generally minor to moderate structural defects 
observed, except for random occasional severe spots of subgrade fatigue.  Overall 
pavement deterioration was moderate longitudinal and transverse cracking and 
moderate cracking along paving cut joints. Also, potholes and trenches patched 
with asphaltic concrete (AC) material were observed at some locations along the 
alignments. The ride characteristic of the pavement was generally moderate, with 
exception to occasional severe subgrade fatigue and trench patch locations.  
2.2.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
In general, Iolani Street from the intersection with Alohilani Street to where the 
roadway becomes Loha Street, and Liholani Street from the Pukalani Street 
intersection to Haulani Street intersection, the existing asphaltic concrete pavement 
fell under this category and was observed to be in poor condition with moderately to 
highly distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to be 
widespread and generally high in severity. The poor areas generally consisted of 
elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of 
1 to 3.5 inches, rutting, stripping and some shoving of the pavement within the tire 
lanes. Locations where subgrade fatigue cracking and tire rutting were prevalent

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 14 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 14, at selected locations along the roadway 
reconstruction project extending to a maximum depth of about 6 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. In addition, five bulk samples of the subgrade soils, designated as 
Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, were collected for laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests.  The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 
through 3.3. 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway reconstruction project encountered 
a pavement section consisting of approximately 2 to 8 inches of AC over 2 to 15 inches 
of fill/base course materials consisting of medium dense silty gravel and gravelly sands.  
Below the existing pavement section, the borings generally encountered residual soils 
consisting of soft to very stiff clayey silts and silty clays extending to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 6 feet below the existing pavement surface.  In Boring 
No. 12, weathered basalt formation was encountered at an approximate depth of 2 feet 
below the existing pavement surface.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

3.4 
Design Pavement Sections 
Based on the relatively poor conditions of the existing pavements, we 
recommend reconstructing the entire roadways within the project limits under 
consideration for this project with a new pavement section in accordance with guidelines 
provided in the pavement design manual, prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department 
of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research Branch. 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway alignments 
under consideration for this project are underlain by clayey silts and silty clays.  We 
understand that placement of asphalt concrete base (ACB) or PCC directly on the 
clayey silts and silty clays subgrade is not recommended.  Therefore, the pavement 
section should consist of an aggregate subbase (ASB) layer between the upper ACB or 
PCC and the underlying clayey silt and silty clay subgrade soils. 
Due to the long length of the roadway alignments, we envision that new 
pavements for the project would need to be constructed in sections.  Because of the 
poorly-graded nature of the permeable base material, we envision that the material 
could not be compacted into a dense and stable mass.  Our experience suggested that 
the permeable base material would tend to unravel or spill out of the completed 
pavement section when excavation is made for the adjacent section. Therefore, 
incorporation of a permeable base layer for drainage purpose would not be practical 
from a construction standpoint and was not included in our pavement analyses. 
An approximately 300 feet long portion of Iolani Street, located northwesterly of 
the intersection of Iolani Street and Pukalani Street, was constructed as concrete (rigid) 
pavement.  The existing concrete pavement appeared to be aged and deteriorated.  
There was corner break patched with asphaltic concrete and failed section covered with 
steel plate.  We understand that it is desired to reconstruct the existing concrete 
pavement section with a new rigid pavement section.

The thickness of the asphalt concrete base layer for the flexible pavement 
section may be reduced by incorporating a layer of triaxial geogrid between the 
aggregate subbase and the underlying clayey/silty subgrade soils. To maximize the 
benefits of the triaxial geogrid, we understand that aggregate base course with nominal 
maximum size of 1.5 inches should be used in lieu of the aggregate subbase. In 
general, the triaxial geogrid will interlock with the aggregate base course, resulting in 
two benefits during initial construction and for the life of the project: 1) lateral 
confinement – increasing the modulus of the aggregate base course, and 2) subgrade 
bearing capacity enhancement.  Based on the full-scale performance test results by 
Tensar as shown on Plate 4, we believe that the R-value of the subgrade soils may be 
enhanced from a value of 6 to 35 (conservative side) with the incorporation of a layer of

that the following flexible pavement design sections may be used for the pavement

The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes. The 
vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform 
to Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments.

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and the drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignments by drilling 
and sampling 14 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 14, extending to a 
maximum depth of about 6 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.3. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock formations were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-14. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test result is summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by 
Ninyo & Moore on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 through 
B-6.
