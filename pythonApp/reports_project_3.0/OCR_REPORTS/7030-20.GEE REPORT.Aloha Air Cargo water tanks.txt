This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Two Water Tanks for Aloha Air Cargo Maintenance Hangar at the 
Honolulu International Airport in Honolulu on the island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vincinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
In accordance with our fee proposal dated August 18, 2014, we summarized our 
findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations resulting from our field 
exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
letter report.  
We previously issued a geotechnical report for the new maintenance hangar 
foundation design entitled, Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, Aloha Air Cargo 
Maintenance Hangar, Honolulu International Airport, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii, dated 
July 15, 2014. This current report for the water tanks is intended to supplement our 
previous report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
This project involves two water tanks for the new maintenance hangar for Aloha Air 
Cargo. The steel water tanks will each be approximately 21.5 feet in diameter and 44 feet 
high with a capacity of about 113,500 gallons. The steel tank structures will be supported 
on a ring footing approximately 4.25 feet wide and 4.25 feet below the tank bottom. The 
interior tank bottom will consist of 4 millimeter polyethylene sheets over 0.5 inch fiber 
expansion boards over 3 inches of clean dry sand on 6 inches of base course. We 
understand that the steel tank structure weighs approximately 30 kips.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The new Aloha Air Cargo Maintenance Hangar site is northwest of the existing 
Aircraft Rescue Firefighting Facility (ARFF) near the end of Elliot Street within the Honolulu 
International Airport on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project site is bordered by the 
existing Cargo Warehouse to the east,  Hickam Air Force Base to the north and west and 
by Taxiway “A” and concrete hardstand to the south as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The two new water tanks will be located on the northwestern portion of the new 
maintenance hangar. 
FIELD EXPLORATION 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. Generally, the borings were extended to depths of 30.5 and 
32 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate 3. Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered are provided on the attached Logs of Borings, Plates 4 
and 5. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by about 7 feet of fill over 
3 feet of soft lagoonal deposits underlain by dense coral formation. Settlement will be 
induced from consolidation of the lagoonal deposit when subjected to structural loads of 
the water tanks and weight of the water stored. For the anticipated loads, the consolidation 
settlement is estimated to be on the order of about 2.5 inches. Differential settlements are 
anticipated to be on the order of 1 inch between the perimeter ring footing and center of 
the tank. 
Provided the water tanks can tolerate these settlements, shallow foundations 
consisting of ring footings may be used to support the project. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used to design shallow 
foundation on the re-compacted fills. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and 
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grades. The bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction prior to placing reinforcing steel and/or concrete. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the footing subgrade level should be

over-excavated to expose the underlying firm or dense materials. The over-excavation 
should be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to no less than 95 percent 
relative compaction or the footings may extend to bear on the firm or dense materials. 
Foundations next to utility trenches and/or easements should be embedded below 
a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench 
or embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is 
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural 
loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
Lateral loads acting on the tanks may be resisted by friction developed between the 
bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting against 
the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.4 may be used 
for footings bearing on the compacted fill materials. Resistance due to passive earth 
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square 
foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soils around the footings are well 
compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the 
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. It should be emphasized that surface water 
should be diverted away from the foundations. 
If the structure cannot tolerate the estimated settlements, the soft lagoonal deposits 
below the tanks may be over excavated and replaced with compacted structural fill. This 
would reduce the potential settlements to less than one half inch total and differential.  
LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions between and 
beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided in herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having 
been taped from features shown on the General Site Plan provided by Rim Architects on 
September 12, 2014. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides, rainfall, temperature, and other 
factors.
