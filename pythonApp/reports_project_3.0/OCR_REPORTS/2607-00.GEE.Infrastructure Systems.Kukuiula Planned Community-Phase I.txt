INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Infrastructure Systems Kukuiula Planned Community Phase
The general location of the project site and its vicinity is
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the exploration These recommendations are intended for use in project
planning and to provide engineering design input The recommendations and findings
presented herein are not intended for other uses nor are they intended to form the
basis for preparation of construction cost estimates or bidding purposes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
soils and rock conditions at the site in order to provide data for the formulation of
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design and construction
of the proposed site grading infrastructure and foundations for the project elements
The scope of our exploration consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and operators
to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of twenty nine 29 borings to depths ranging
between 10 0 and 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface

of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the proposed

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
epoch more than 2 3 million years ago Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of the basaltic
lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the
alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation Our exploration of the site and its vicinity
indicates that the area is underlain by a very thin surface layer of residual soil derived
from the in situ weathering of Koloa Basalts Under the surface soil and exposed in
areas of the project site is a basaltic rock formation of the Koloa Volcanic Series

Blasting Considerations
It is our Opinion that although some of the rock at the site can be excavated
by heavy ripping and the use of hoerams blasting will probably be found to be more
efficient for excavation of the massive rock that is more dense and relatively free of
fissures
There is a potential for objectionable impacts or hazards resulting from blasting
operations These generally originate from one or more of the following factors
1 Flyrock
2 Ground Vibration
3 Air Blast
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2607 00 Page 16
These factors are discussed in the following paragraphs
Flyrock is the blaster s term for flying rock or other debris which is launched
into the air during open blasting by the force of the detonating explosives
These projectiles often have tremendous kinetic energy and can crush or
destroy objects in their flight path or at their point of impact
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced and possibly eliminated by increasing
the confinement of the blast or the depth of the explosive charges and or by
using safety mats
Ground Vibration
With respect to the subject project ground vibration is probably the most
critical potential hazardous factor to be anticipated during blasting operations
When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense stress waves
in the surrounding material These waves crush the rock immediately adjacent
to the explosion and permanently distorts and cracks the rock for some
distance
Beyond the area in which the intensity of the stress waves attenuates to such
a degree that there is no longer any permanent deformation of the rock the
stress is further propagated through the rock as elastic waves These elastic
waves create ground vibration
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2607 00 Page 17
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of the
intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure According
to the DuPont Blaster s Handbook a formula has been developed to relate
the intensity of ground vibration at a given point in terms of particle
displacement velocity and acceleration to the weight of the explosive charge
and the distance between the explosion and the point of interest
Vibration would be a consideration in the vicinity of developed areas such as
the residential development situated along the southern and eastern borders of
the proposed site
Air Blast
s e
The air blast from an explosion is a compression wave in air Noise is the
portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and
concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20 Hz The DuPont Blaster s
Handbook also sets forth a scaled distance relationship for air blast
The effects of air blast which depends on atmospheric and or other
considerations can be a consideration for much greater distances than for
vibration
If blasting is to be used for rock excavation we recommend that the scaled
distances of the explosive charges be limited to those that will generate peak
particle velocities of 1 5 inches per second or less at any nearby structure
Blasting should be performed in accordance with the applicable statutes rules
and regulations and only by a qualified operator with a proven record We

on the project
if blasting is to be utilized for rock excavation we recommend a program of

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Load on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of
the pipe the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance
between the backfill material and the trench side walls To compute the
vertical loads on the pipes an average unit weight for backfill material of 120

the pipe increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the width of
the trench should be kept to a minimum

and backfill will be very small Significant settlements due to sewerline
construction are not anticipated if proper construction techniques are utilized

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an
important design consideration
For pipes bearing on the dense rock formation a minimum of six 6 inches of

granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of one
fourth the outside pipe diameter and it also should extend half way up the pipe
sides The bedding material should consist of clean granular material such as
No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of twenty nine 29 borings to depths ranging between 10 0 and 26 5 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Truck mounted rotary drilling equipment was utilized to drill the borings This
equipment was mobilized to Kauai from our Honolulu office
Drilling in softer materials was accomplished by using continuous flight augers
to advance the borings Soil samples were obtained by using drive penetration
samples driven by a 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The number of blows
required to drive the sampler one 1 foot or a fraction thereof are presented at the
appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 29
The drive samplers used were 2 0 inch O D Standard Penetration Samplers and
samplers to obtain and preserve relatively undisturbed samples for laboratory testing
Samples of the rock material encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses
a hollow bit to cut into the formation The material left in the annulus of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing The recovered
cores were logged by an Engineer or Geologist from our firm Descriptions of the
recovered cores are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 29
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring run vs the total length of the run e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown
on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation ROD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is
sound material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any
discontinuities discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of
sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the ROD would be 50 and
would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 50

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to identify appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content Determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and
to aid in determining certain engineering properties The moisture content expressed
as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate
location on plates summarizing test results and at the appropriate sample depths on
the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests are used to determine the plasticity of the fine grained
portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used to classify the soil under
the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests are shown on Plate
B 1 and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve Analysis Tests ASTM Test Method D422 63 are performed to determine
the grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for
classification in the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other
engineering properties The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
