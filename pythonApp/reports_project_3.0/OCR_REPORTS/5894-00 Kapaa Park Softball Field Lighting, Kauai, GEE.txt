PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions to develop a soil rock data set to formulate geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the design of floodlight poles To accomplish this we conducted an
exploration consisting of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and two operators
to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of three borings to depths of about 41 feet below the
existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our field
geologist
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their engineering
properties
5 Analyses of field and laboratory data to formulate geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the project design
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
f Coordination of the work on the project by our project engineer
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by our
principal engineer
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support

FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers Generally the borings were extended to depth of about 41 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic representations
of the materials encountered are provided on the attached Logs of Borings Plates 4
through 6
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are
summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by stiff clayey silt
extending to about 5 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface The stiff clayey silt was
underlain by beach deposit overlying coralline detritus consisting of medium dense sand
and gravel extending to depths of about 10 5 to 15 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Below the sand and gravel our borings encountered lagoonal deposit consisting
the existing ground surface The lagoonal deposits were underlain by alluvial deposits
consisting of medium stiff to stiff silty clay clayey silt extending to the maximum depth
explored of about 41 feet
We encountered groundwater at about 4 6 to 5 5 feet below the existing ground
surface in the three borings at the time of our field exploration It should be noted that

Laboratory Testing
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained
from the field exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating the
engineering properties

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the available plan seven 60 foot floodlight poles will be installed to
replace the existing floodlights at the Kapaa Park Softball Field site Based on the loading
conditions provided by the project structural engineer and subsurface soil conditions
encountered during our field exploration we believe a cast in place drilled shaft foundation
system will be the most appropriate to support the new floodlight poles Detailed
discussions and recommendations for foundation designs are presented in the following
sections herein
Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the load information provided the maximum structural loading at each
pole foundation will consist of a compressive load of approximately 5 6 kips a shear load
of approximately 3 3 kips a torsion load of approximately 1 1 kips feet and an overturning

shafts would derive both vertical and lateral support from the friction between the concrete

Based on the structural loads and subsurface conditions encountered we

drilled shafts is for dead plus live loads The compressive load capacity may be increased

factor of safety of 2 5 was used for the allowable compressive load capacity
Lateral Load Resistance
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the drilled shaft allowable deflection at the top of
the drilled shaft and the induced moment in the drilled shaft The lateral load
analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded deep foundation
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral
loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use of non linear
o y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then computed
using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths

Foundation Settlement
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered
We anticipate that the total settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be less
than 0 5 inch We believe that a significant portion of the settlement will be elastic
and should occur as the loads are applied
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
The load carrying capacity of the drilled shaft depends on both the friction between
the shaft and the surrounding soil and the bearing over the soil at the bottom of the
shaft The performance of the shaft will significantly depend upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling or
excavating the shaft hole and in placing concrete in the hole As a result it is
important that Geolabs representative be present to observe the drilled shaft
installation during construction
Based on our field exploration and the above shaft parameters soft to very soft
lagoonal material will probably be encountered during the shaft excavation at the
project site Due to the nature of lagoonal material caving in will likely occur during
the drilling operations if encountered To reduce the potential for caving in of the
drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled holes is recommended during the
drilled shaft installation Temporary casing may extend to the full depth of the shaft
or to suitable depth to be determined by the Contractor to protect the shaft concrete
from caving in of surrounding soil The casing shall be continuous between the top
and bottom elevations and shall be advanced through the ground by twisting
driving or vibration before shaft or before cleaning the shaft
We encountered groundwater table at approximately 5 feet below the ground
surface therefore the shaft concrete should be placed by tremmie methods During
concrete placement a minimum of 5 feet of concrete head should be maintained
above the bottom of the tremmie pipe A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch slump
range concrete mix should be used to provide close contact between the drilled
shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed promptly after
drilling within 24 hours after drilling of the holes to reduce the potential for
softening and or caving in of the sides bottom of the drilled holes Please note that
vibration effort shall not be applied to the shaft concrete during and or after the
concrete pour since it tends to segregate the high slump concrete In addition
although the drilled shafts are primarily designed based on skin friction the bottom
of the drilled hole should be relatively free of loose materials prior to concrete
placement

Utility Trench
We envision that electrical lines will also be installed to serve the new floodlighting
system We recommend placing a minimum 6 inch granular bedding consisting of clean
sand or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the lines Free draining
granular materials such as clean sand or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches above the
lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of
the backfill thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material
and placement procedures could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to
the maximum dry density
