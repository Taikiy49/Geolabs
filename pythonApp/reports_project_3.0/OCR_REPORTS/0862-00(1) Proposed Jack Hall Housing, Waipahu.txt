PURPOSE SCOPE
The purpose of the investigation is to determine subsurface
soil conditions beneath the proposed apartment buildings
and parking area We were also to provide recommendations
for foundations pavement design and grading recommendations
for general site development
The scope of the investigation included five 15 foot deep
borings within the proposed building areas and one 5 foot
boring in the vicinity of the proposed parking Soil
sampling standard penetration tests and a reconnaissance
of the elderly housing portion of the site was performed
under the observation of our engineer Additional laboratory
testing was performed on selected samples to help provide
the basis for our analyses The boring information from the
adjacent Co operative Ownership Development was also utilized
in this investigation This report summarizes our conclisions
and recommendations

SITE CONDITIONS
The site was formerly occupied by residential structures
There are existing shrubs trees driveways there is
also a former garden area At the time of our field explora
tions loose piles of fill were observed near Waipahu Street
some of these loose fill also contain boulders and pieces of
concrete Piles of trash and organic matter had been dumped
in other areas closer to Hapapa Street
The ground surface in the Elderly Housing Project is relatively
uniform The surface elevation rises from 40 feet at the Waipahu
Paiwa Streets intersection to 60 feet just west of the elderly
apartments Existing slopes are roughly four per cent two
degrees
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Minor shallow fill was encountered in several exploratory
borings indicating that the fill soils are localized sbhe
dominant natural soil consists of stiff silt The upper
stratum brown and gray color is generally sandy friable
and only slightly to non expansive The underlying red brown
clayey strata are also generally stiff and are slightly expansive
change in volume with changes in moisture content

CONCLUSIONS RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of the field investigations laboratory
testing and engineering analyses the following conclusions
and recommendations are presented
The use of localized loose fill as not recommended for supporting
foundations or floor slabs The underlying natural soils are
suitable for supporting conventional spread type footings We
have provided recommendations for the excavation of the existing
fill and the degree of compaction required for new fill in order
to provide adequate floor slab support

Foundations
Spread footing foundations established in the natural soils
may be used to support the proposed apartment buildings
The spread footing foundations may be designed using a
net allowable bearing capacity of 3 000 pounds per square
foot Above value of bearing capacity is for total of dead
plus live loads and may be increased by 33 when temporary
lateral loads wind and seismic are considered

Lateral Loads
Resistance to lateral loads may be provided by a combination
of passive pressure and friction Passive pressure may be
taken vas that exerted by a fluid of density equal to 300
p c f Friction may be taken as normal dead loads times
a coefficient of friction equal four tenth
A combination of friction and passive pressure may be used
provided that passive pressure is reduced by one third

FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by
drilling six borings to depths from 6 to 15 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled and sampled
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate l
The soils encountered in the borings were Classified by
visual and textural examination in the field by our engineering
geologist who continuously monitored the drilling operations
The classifications were reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System
A graphic presentation of the soils is presented on the Boring
hogs Plates A 1 through A 6
In the borings undisturbed and disturbed samples were
obtained and standard penetration tests were performed Either
ring sampler was driven using an 140 pound hammer falling 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches
are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate depths
