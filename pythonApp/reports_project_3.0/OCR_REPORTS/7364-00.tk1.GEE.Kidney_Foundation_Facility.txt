SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to hard silty clays, clays, and clayey 
silts extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 22 feet below the existing 
ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the design finished grades 
for the new facility, we recommend supporting the new building on shallow foundations 
consisting of spread and/or continuous footings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 psf may be used to design the shallow foundations bearing on the new compacted 
fills needed to achieve the finished grades. The bottom of footings should be embedded a 
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
Based on our laboratory test results, the on-site clayey soils exhibit moderate to 
high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture 
contents. Therefore, we recommend providing at least 36 inches of non-expansive select 
granular fill materials below the building slab, exterior flatwork, and pavement areas due to 
the expansive nature of the on-site clayey soils.  The non-expansive select granular 
materials should also extend beyond the building footprint a minimum of 24 inches, or to 
the outside edge of the sidewalks, if applicable. 
Shrinkage cracks are commonly encountered at the ground surface of the 
expansive clayey soils in the Kapolei area due to the dry weather characteristic of the Ewa 
Plains area. If left unattended, the shrinkage cracks will likely increase further in size under 
prolonged dry weather conditions. Shrinkage cracks adjacent to the building foundations 
are potentially detrimental because water can readily infiltrate through the crack openings 
and soften the subsurface soils around the building foundations, thus affecting their 
performance. As a result, special attention should be given to the recommended site 
preparation procedures presented in the “Site Grading” section prior to the fill placement 
operation and/or construction of improvements on top of the existing ground surface. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. As the 
volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked 
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield 
Plateau. The project site is on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments that were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the project 
site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile 
type for seismic design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can be detected only by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii.

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils located near the ground surface undergo a substantial 
loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from 
cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose 
saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the 
soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. 
Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the 
overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and 
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a 
mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not 
confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to 
hard silty clays, clays, and clayey silts extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 22 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was not 
encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our laboratory tests, the on-site clayey soils exhibit moderate to high 
shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. 
The shrink/swell characteristics of the surface clay soils are exacerbated by the 
prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area resulting in large 
fluctuations of the soil moisture content. Therefore, special attention should be given to 
the recommended procedures for subgrade soil preparation below the slabs and 
pavements in order to reduce the potential shrink/swell effects of the surface clayey 
soils on the new structures and improvements. 
Based on the anticipated finished grades for the development and the subsurface 
conditions encountered, we recommend supporting the new building structure on 
shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings bearing on the new 
compacted fills needed to achieve the finished grades. The footing subgrade soils 
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively 
firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placing the reinforcing steel and/or concrete. 
In addition, we recommend supporting the building slab, exterior flatwork, and 
pavement areas on at least 36 inches of non-expansive select granular fill materials to 
reduce the potential for distress to the future slabs-on-grade and pavements resulting 
from swelling of the clayey soils. The non-expansive select granular materials should 
also extend beyond the building footprint a minimum of 24 inches, or to the outside 
edge of the sidewalks, if applicable. In order to reduce the potential for appreciable 
distress due to differential movements between the heavier foundations and the lighter 
building slabs, we recommend using free-floating concrete slabs-on-grade with no 
structural connections to the wall and column foundations.

finished grades at the project site. Based on the anticipated grading concept and the 
generally stiff to hard subsurface conditions encountered, we believe shallow spread

recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot

The bottom of footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent

down to bear directly on the underlying competent materials. 
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades. Footings constructed near tops of slopes or 
on sloping ground conditions should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 
horizontal setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to 
the face of the slope. Footings adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should 
be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the 
planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads. 
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench 
or the footing should be embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures

3.4 
Shrinkage Cracks 
Our field observations indicated that substantial shrinkage cracks are present at 
the existing ground surface of the project site.  In general, shrinkage cracks will start to 
develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the 
soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions.  The development of 
shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic 
conditions in the Kapolei area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content.  
Detailed discussions of the shrink/swell behavior of the on-site soils and the mechanism 
of shrinkage crack development are presented below. 
3.4.1 Shrink/Swell Behavior of On-Site Soils 
Based on the results of laboratory tests performed for the project, the on-site clayey 
soils generally have a moderate to high shrink/swell potential when subjected to 
fluctuations in the moisture content and may be classified as expansive soils.  In 
addition, it will likely lose substantial strength when saturated and will shrink 
appreciably when subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks.  In general, the 
mechanism of shrink/swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is 
influenced by a number of factors, including soil characteristics and environmental 
factors. 
Expansive soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering properties 
such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink/swell potential of soils.  In 
general, the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the soil to 
retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure.  Therefore, a 
highly plastic soil, such as those encountered at the project site, will have a greater 
potential for volume change (shrink and swell) due to the loss or absorption of 
moisture under cycles of drying and wetting.  In addition, increased soil densities 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 7364-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 19 
Hawaii • California 
through compaction or natural depositional history (such as desiccation) generally 
have greater shrink/swell potential. 
Environmental factors include the initial moisture content of the soil and moisture 
variations over time.  The initial moisture content generally impact the potential of 
the soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system.  In general, a dry soil will 
have a higher capacity to absorb water and swell, while a wet soil will lose water 
more readily upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more.  Moisture 
variations can generally be caused by climate, surface drainage, vegetation, 
permeability, etc. 
3.4.2 Shrinkage Crack Development Mechanism 
Due to the high plasticity of the on-site clayey soils, shrinking of the near-surface 
soils starts to develop as soil moisture evaporates from the soil system under the 
prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area.  These 
shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils 
expand during periods of wet weather or heavy rains.  However, shrinkage of the 
on-site soils and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will recur when the dry 
weather returns.  Therefore, the development of shrinkage cracks will recur when 
the on-site clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry climatic conditions, which 
causes large fluctuations in the moisture content, unless some measures are taken 
to maintain the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level. 
In areas where ponding water occurs, the wet ground will have a higher potential 
for shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily upon 
exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more.  Therefore, it should be recognized 
that this phenomenon will recur and continue unless measures are taken to 
maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant in order to reduce the 
shrink/swell behavior of the on-site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations. 
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time 
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface, the ground cracks could 
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations.  The ground cracks

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 8 to 22 feet below the 
existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample, designated as Bulk-1, of the near-
surface soils was obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics. The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill equipment equipped with continuous flight 
augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-1. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
of the clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits for soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils, 
four Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected in-situ 
samples.  The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. Individual stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are 
presented on Plates B-3 through B-6. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested.  Results of the direct 
shear test are presented on Plate B-7. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.
