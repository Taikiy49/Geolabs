SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by a surficial fill over stiff 
and/or dense alluvial soils, and relative permeable coralline deposits extending to the 
maximum depth explored of about 86.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater 
was encountered at about 17 to 36 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our 
field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water run-off, and other factors. 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would involve the placement of general fill 
materials. General fills may consist of the on-site soils with the maximum particle size of 
6 inches or less and should be free of deleterious materials. In general, the excavated 
on-site materials may be reused as a source of general fill material, provided they are free 
of vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials. General fill 
materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
In general, cut slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may 
be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope 
to provide stability of the new fill embankment against sliding. Based on the results of the 
slope stability analyses, it is our opinion that the proposed new fill slope constructed in 
accordance with the recommendations presented in this report would have a factor of safety 
for global stability of at least 1.5 against the slope instability. 
Because the near-surface clayey alluvial soils present poor subsurface water 
infiltration characteristics, our field exploration was focused on the relative permeable 
coralline materials at about 45 to 86 feet below the existing ground surface. These 
permeable coralline layers generally correspond to elevations of about +10 to -50 feet MSL. 
To evaluate the subsurface permeability of the coralline materials, percolation tests were 
conducted in the boreholes for the infiltration characteristics in support of the planning and 
design of stormwater run-off disposal systems.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the project design.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in the vicinity of the Ho’opili Off-Site Channel and Basin project in the Ewa 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, field permeability testing, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the project. These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended 
for the design of site grading and box drain retaining structures only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed Ho’opili Master Plan Development project is located to the south of 
the Interstate Route H-1 Highway and west of Fort Weaver Road in the Ewa area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed development encompasses approximately 
1,500 acres. The project site is gently sloping and is currently either vacant or for 
agricultural cultivation use. 
As part of the development, it is planned to divert the stormwater run-off generated in 
the Ho’opili subdivision area to a permanent basin in a vacant/open area located on the east 
of Fort Weaver Road and south of Arizona Road. The previous exploration within this open 
area indicated relative permeable coralline material may be located between depths of about 
45 and 90 feet below the ground surface. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to refine the 
permeability characteristics within the previously identified relative permeable zones in 
support of the permanent basin design. In addition, we understand the underground concrete 
box culverts will be installed between Ho’opili Parcel 114 and the planned permanent basin.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, 
lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a 
broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the 
southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project site vicinity and 
to the south, these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is situated across Fort Weaver Road from the main Ho’opili area 
and is a roughly triangular area delineated by Fort Weaver Road and an unnamed 
plantation road to the west, Arizona Road to the north, and West Loch Branch of Naval 
Magazine Lualualei Explosive Safety Quantity Distance (ESQD) arc to the south and east. 
This area is sometimes locally referred to as the “Blast Zone”. 
Based on our field observations, the project area generally is blanketed with wild 
grasses, some bushes, and scattered trees on the northern portion of the project site. The 
one-third northern portion of the site was used as a temporary detention basin during the 
West Loch subdivision construction with relatively lower elevations at the ground surface 
comparing to the remaining southern portion of the site. Based on our site observations 
and topographic surveying, the project site generally slopes gently down towards the 
north with elevations ranging from +38 to +18 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions within the project site by drilling and 
sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 110, extending to depths 
of about 21.5 to 86.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils were obtained to characterize the pavement subgrade design 
parameters of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The subsurface conditions at the project site area 
are illustrated on the Generalized Geologic Cross-Section A-A’, Plate 3. 
In general, the project site is underlain by surficial fill, alluvial soils, and coralline 
deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of 86.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The surficial fill consisted of stiff to very stiff clayey/silty soils and dense to very 
dense sands/gravels about 0.5 to 21.5 feet thick. In general, the alluvial soils consisted 
of stiff to hard silts and clays and/or medium dense clayey gravel ranging from about 21 
to 33 feet below the existing ground surface. Underlying the alluvial soils were

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or 
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing operations 
should be over-excavated to expose stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill materials. The excavated soft 
soils may be used as a source of general fill material, provided that it meets the fill 
material and placement requirements. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill at no cost to the owner. A Geolabs 
representative should evaluate the need for over-excavation in the field.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve primarily the placement 
of general fill materials. General fills may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum 
particle size of 6 inches and should be free of deleterious material. In general, the 
excavated on-site soils may be reused as a source of general fill material, provided 
they are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious material and other unsuitable 
materials. 
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
We wish to emphasize that moisture-conditioning of the fill materials is an integral 
and critical part of the earthwork recommendations provided herein. Compaction 
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be 
allowed to compact the fills. 
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical 
elements of earthwork, Geolabs should provide observations and perform soil 
density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the appropriate 
degree of compaction and proper ranges of moisture content. Where moisture and/or 
compaction are less than required, additional moisture-conditioning and compactive 
effort should be applied with adjustment as necessary.

3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it appears most of 
the cut slopes would expose soils consisting of stiff clayey alluvial soils. In general, 
we believe cut slopes into the alluvial soils may be constructed at an inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Slope 
benches, 8-feet wide, are recommended at a maximum 25-foot vertical height 
interval. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched 
into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill embankment against sliding. 
The toe of the bench should be at least 12 feet wide. The key should be constructed 
at the toe of the new slope a minimum of 2 feet deep and 12 feet wide. Detail is 
presented on the Slope Detail, Plate 4.  
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above general fill placement 
recommendations.  
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of fill slopes should not be 
accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching 
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills.  Surface water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes, 
and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
erosion of the finished slopes.

3.1.7 Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
In general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance 
of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather 
conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently 
intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Kapolei/Ewa area resulting 
in large fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to 
reduce the potential for significant soil erosion soon after the completion of the site 
grading. The relatively dry climatic conditions of the Kapolei/Ewa area likely will 
require controlled irrigation of the graded surfaces to maintain the moisture 
contents and to keep graded surfaces free of large shrinkage cracks. However, 
over-watering of the graded surfaces should be avoided to reduce the potential for 
saturation and softening of the surface soils.

3.2 
Subsurface Permeability 
We understand it is planned to divert the stormwater run-off generated in the 
Ho’opili subdivision to a permanent basin in the “Blast Zone”. Because the near-surface 
clayey alluvial soils experience poor subsurface water infiltration characteristics, our field 
exploration was focused on the relative permeable coralline materials at about 45 to 
85 feet below the existing ground surface, corresponding to elevations of about +10 
to -50 feet MSL. 
To evaluate the subsurface permeability of the “Blast Zone”, we conducted a total 
of 40 percolation tests in the boreholes generally at 10-foot intervals from depths of 45 to 
85 feet below the ground surface for infiltration characteristics in support of the planning 
and design of stormwater run-off disposal systems. In general, the permeability tests were 
performed by advancing the boreholes to the designated test depths using core drilling 
techniques. For some percolation tests, a section of steel casing was removed to expose 
subsurface materials within the testing interval. 
Field permeability testing, utilizing constant and/or falling head methods, were 
performed to measure the permeability of the exposed subsurface materials. Results of 
the field permeability tests are summarized in Appendix C. 
The field permeability testing indicates that the coralline materials have a 
coefficient of permeability, k, on the order of about 10-1 to 10-2 centimeters per second 
(cm/s). Based on our analyses, an infiltration trench about 500 feet long with a width 
ranging from about 5 to 15 feet may achieve infiltration capacities between 10 to 100 
million gallons per day. We recommend bottom of the trench to be at about -5 feet MSL 
with a hydraulic head at about +25 feet MSL. It should be noted that this calculated value 
is based on testing conducted in undeveloped boreholes and does not allow for head 
losses associated with infiltration screen clogging or piping losses. 
The actual production disposal trenches should be thoroughly developed and 
should consider screen losses in the design. A regular maintenance program should be 
developed and implemented to address long term clogging losses.

3.3 
Box Drain/Retaining Structures 
We understand that concrete box drain culverts will be required for the project. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the following general guidelines may 
be used for design of the retaining structures at the project site. 
3.3.1 Concrete Box Drain/Retaining Structure Foundations 
In general, we believe that an allowable bearing capacity of 3,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf) may be used to evaluate the concrete box culvert and retaining 
structure foundations bearing on the existing clayey soils. To provide uniform 
bearing support for the concrete box culvert, we recommend providing a 6-inch 
thick layer of aggregate subbase material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction between the new box culvert slab and the underlying subgrade 
soils. Prior to placing the aggregate subbase material, the subgrade should be 
scarified to a depth of about 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 
4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. 
Retaining wall footings should be at least 18 inches wide and should be embedded 
a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. For sloping 
ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot 
setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the 
face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should 
be constructed in stepped footings.

3.3.3 Drainage 
The retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the potential for build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No 67 
gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the 
base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weephole. As an alternative, 
a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or 
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of 
the retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to 
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure 
unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface. 
3.4 
Corrosion Potential 
Three sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), and/or Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300.0), were performed (by our office and Test America Laboratories, Inc.) on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the 
on-site soils. Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from approximately 440 to 5,300 ohm-cm 
(based on the soluble soil sample prepared in accordance with Standard Methods for the 
Examination of Water and Wastewater SM 2510B) and pH values of between 8 and 9

within the upper 5 feet of soils, corresponding to a corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely 
Corrosive) based on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of 
Water Supply.  
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a corrosion 
rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of 
metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, should consider 
the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Levels of the chloride content of the on-site soils encountered near the existing 
ground surface are relatively low, on the order of 110 parts per million (ppm) or less, while 
the sulfate content is relatively low, on the order of about 140 ppm or less. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the soil. In 
general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate content 
of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered “non-corrosive” to 
underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the relatively low values of pH, 
chloride content, and sulfate content tested on the in-situ materials, we believe the 
near-surface soils at the project site generally may be considered “non-corrosive” and either 
Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the concrete in contact with the ground. 
It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test 
results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

3.5.1 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
The near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry and friable and exhibit 
relatively moderate to high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations 
in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 6-inch thick layer of select granular 
fill and the layer of Geogrid, we recommend that the subgrade be properly scarified 
to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above 
the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to recompacting. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. 
3.5.2 Select Granular Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell 
of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
3.5.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches 
in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance

3.5.4 Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to isolate 
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the 
curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base or subbase 
layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water into the 
pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be constructed to collect 
the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term performance, we 
recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the paved/landscaped 
areas.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 110, extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 21 to 86.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Two bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils were obtained to characterize the pavement subgrade 
design parameters of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock formations were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that

Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties 
of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
Nine Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plates 
B-2 and B-3. 
Four Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-7. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, two Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil 
samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-8 and B-9. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
