Dear Ms. Hayashi: 
In accordance with your request, we performed a geotechnical engineering 
evaluation of several trenchless methods to install a water main below the existing double 
box culvert for the Kanunu Street and Makaloa Street, 8-Inch Water Main Replacement 
project located in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1.  
Our evaluation is based on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and 
engineering analyses.  Our field exploration and laboratory testing are contained in our 
report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, Kanunu Street and Makaloa 
Street, 8-Inch Water Main Replacement, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii,” dated October 20, 
2023.  Our findings presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves the installation of approximately 840 linear feet of 8-inch water 
main along Kanunu Street, from Keeaumoku Street to Kaheka Street, and approximately 
1,520 linear feet of 8-inch water main along Makaloa Street between Keeaumoku Street 
and Poni Street.  In addition, approximately 144 linear feet of water main will be installed 
on Amana Street and Keeaumoku Street. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that trenchless methods of 
installation are being considered for the water main crossing below an existing 3-foot by 
7-foot double box culvert at the intersection of Makaloa Street and Kaheka Street.  The 
trenchless methods that are being considered include auger bore pipe jacking, 
microtunneling, pilot tube microtunneling or guided auger boring, and horizontal 
directional drilling (HDD).  The feasibility of these trenchless methods was evaluated for 
the water main crossing the existing 3-foot by 7-foot double box culvert.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the double box culvert crossing were determined using 
the borings designated as Boring Nos. 5 and 6.  The borings extended to a depth of about 
41.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan and Profile, Plate 2.  A generalized geologic profile at the box culvert crossing 
is also shown on Plate 2.   
The borings drilled near the box culvert crossing encountered about 6 inches of 
asphaltic concrete underlain by medium dense to very dense silty sand and sandy gravel 
fills extending to depths of about 2 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface.  The granular 
fill on the west side of the box culvert was underlain by soft to very stiff clayey/sandy silt fills 
extending to a depth of about 6 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft to soft clayey silt and very loose to loose 
silty sand and gravel were encountered below the fills, extending to depths of about 28 and 
32 feet below the existing ground surface.  A thin layer of coral about 1.5 to 2 feet thick was 
encountered below the lagoonal deposits.  Below the coral layer, coralline detritus 
consisting of medium dense to very dense silty sand and gravel was encountered and 
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 41.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.

GEOLOGIC UNITS 
The various geologic deposits encountered in the borings drilled near the double 
box culvert crossing may be grouped into the following three generalized types of geologic 
materials. 
1. 
Fills 
2. 
Lagoonal deposits 
3. 
Coralline deposits 
Further discussions on the general characteristics of the geologic units are 
presented in the following subsections. 
Fills 
The fills consist of variable soils, which were placed during the development of the 
area. The fills may be encountered as both unconsolidated and semi-consolidated 
deposits having variable density resulting from man-induced compaction of the 
fills. The fills generally consist of medium dense to very dense silty sand and sandy 
gravel and soft to very stiff clayey/sandy silt soils. In addition, the thickness of the 
fill could vary along the alignment. Localized areas of deeper fills may be 
encountered as utility trench backfill. 
Lagoonal Deposits 
Lagoonal deposits generally consist of variable light gray calcareous sediments 
(clays, silts, and sands) mixed with broken granular fragments of coralline reef 
materials (sandy and gravelly coralline detritus). The materials generally are 
deposited in low wave energy, shallow marine environments such as bays and 
lagoons that are commonly protected by an outer fringing coral reef and an inner 
shoreline environment.

The lagoonal deposits were comprised of granular material consisting of sands 
and gravel with varying amounts of silt and cohesive soils consisting of clayey silt. 
The deposits encountered in our borings are typically very soft/very loose to 
soft/very loose. The N60 values of the granular lagoonal deposits encountered in 
the borings ranged from 3 to 7 blows per foot.  The N60 values of the cohesive 
lagoonal deposits encountered in the borings ranged from 0 (weight of hammer) 
to 4 blows per foot.  The lagoonal deposits were encountered from Elevations -1 
to -23 feet MSL on the west side of the double box culvert and from Mean Sea 
Level to -19 feet MSL on the east side of the box culvert. 
Coralline Deposits 
Coralline deposits consist of fossil remnants of coral-algal reefs and their related 
detrital debris deposits. In Hawaii, the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant 
portion of any coralline rock assemblage. The detrital deposits are weakly to 
strongly cemented coralline debris consisting of gravel, sand, and silt-sized clastic 
fragments. Some of the coralline fragments were dissolved, and the solution 
cavities were filled with cemented and/or uncemented fine sands and silts. 
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral-algal deposits and the 
variable degrees of cementation frequently present, this formation generally has 
high to moderate in-situ permeability and strength characteristics. The following 
subunits are typically included in this geologic unit: 
Coralline Detritus – This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from 
reef formations. The soils generally consist of medium dense to very dense 
silty sands and gravel. The presence of silts and clays in this unit can vary 
significantly. Detritus also may be weakly cemented with occasional 
moderate to strong cementation. The N60 values of the coralline detritus 
encountered in the borings ranged between 33 and 46 blows per foot.  
Coral Formation – Coral formation (or coral-algal reef) is another subunit 
that is composed of coral and calcareous algae in their original growth, 
which has become cemented with secreted calcium compounds during the 
growth and expansion of the marine organisms. The joined skeletons form 
a structural framework, often containing variable interstices within the 
framework. The interstices may be void space or filled with other cemented 
or loose calcareous debris (detritus). The coral formation may range from 
loose to very dense in consistency depending on the type of cemented coral 
and algal skeletons and the degree of cementation. The relative hardness 
of the coral formation encountered in the borings was generally medium 
hard.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions encountered at the 
double box culvert crossing consist of fills over lagoonal deposits.  The lagoonal deposits 
were underlain by coral formation and coralline detritus.  We encountered groundwater in 
the borings at depths of about 5.5 and 6 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to about Mean Sea Level and 
Elevation of +0.5 feet MSL.   
As mentioned previously, trenchless methods are being considered for the 
installation of the water main below the existing double box culvert located at the 
intersection of Makaloa Street and Kaheka Street.  The trenchless methods being 
considered are auger bore pipe jacking, microtunneling, pilot tube microtunneling or guided 
auger boring, and HDD.  We anticipate that the 8-inch water main crossing below the 
existing double box culvert would have to be installed in a larger casing about 30 inches in 
diameter.   
Due to the presence of groundwater and very soft/very loose lagoonal deposits 
along the anticipated alignment of the water main that will be installed by trenchless 
methods below the double box culvert, we believe that the auger bore pipe jacking method 
may not be a feasible trenchless installation method.  This method utilizes an auger to 
remove spoils while simultaneously jacking the casing.  This method does not provide 
continuous and full support of the excavation face and is not suitable below the groundwater 
level and in running, flowing, or squeezing soils.  Therefore, the auger bore pipe jacking 
method is not further discussed in this letter report.   
Because of the relatively high cost of microtunneling versus the pilot tube 
microtunneling or guided auger boring method, microtunneling is also not further discussed 
in this letter report.  Detailed discussions and evaluation of the trenchless methods 
consisting of the pilot tube microtunneling or guided auger boring method and HDD are 
presented in the following sections. 
Pilot Tube Microtunneling 
The pilot tube microtunneling or guided auger boring method employs the pipe 
jacking and guidance system used for microtunneling, the steering method used for 
horizontal directional drilling, and the cuttings removal system used in auger boring for 
the installation of pipes up to 48 inches in diameter.  The maximum drive length using this 
method is about 400 feet and will depend on the ground conditions and the pilot tube 
operator’s skill.  The pilot tube microtunneling method may not be feasible where the 
depth of the pipes to be installed is deeper than 10 feet below the groundwater level. 
The pilot tube microtunneling method is generally a two or three-stage process.  
This method typically consists of drilling a pilot hole about 4 inches in diameter.  A target 
with electric light emitting diodes is mounted in the steering head, and the head position 
is monitored using a camera-mounted theodolite or laser and TV monitor.  The slanted

surface of the drill head is used for steering.  After completion of the pilot hole, the bore 
hole is enlarged by a reamer and auger casing with augers while the casing is thrust 
forward. Typically, steel, reinforced concrete, and clay pipes may be installed using the 
pilot tube microtunneling method.  We believe that steel casing should be used as a jacket 
for the water main.  The top of the steel casing installed by the pilot tube method should 
be a minimum of at least 5 feet below the bottom of the box culvert.   
Evaluation of the subsurface conditions is important in assessing the viability and 
feasibility of pilot tube microtunneling techniques and in determining appropriate 
construction techniques.  For the design and cost estimation pertaining to the pilot tube 
microtunneling method, special attention should be given to the characteristics of the soils 
that are likely to be encountered and the presence of groundwater.   
Based on our borings drilled near the double box culvert, we anticipate that the 
over-sized casing will traverse within the lagoonal deposits.  The lagoonal deposits 
consist of very soft to soft clayey silt and very loose to loose silty sand and gravel.  
Because of the presence of very soft clayey silt and very loose silty sand and gravel 
along the pilot tube alignment, there is a potential for squeezing or flowing ground 
conditions.  Squeezing ground conditions may occur where the soil is observed to 
advance slowly into the tunnel excavation without any signs of fracturing.  Squeezing 
occurs without an increase in the water content or a volume change in the soil and is 
governed by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden pressure.  Squeezing 
ground may include very soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on the overburden 
pressure at the tunnel depth.  Flowing ground conditions generally occur in silt, sand, or 
gravel material located below the water table without high enough clay content to develop 
cohesion in flowing-type soils.  Flowing-type soils generally advance like viscous fluid into 
the heading.  Significant loss of ground due to squeezing and flowing ground conditions 
may occur at the heading of the auger casing, resulting in potential surface and culvert 
settlement unless appropriate guided boring equipment is used.  Stabilization of these 
soils adjacent to the box culvert at and around the depth of the casing may be required.  
These soils may be stabilized by using jet grouting.    
Jacking and receiving pits are required.  To accommodate the pilot tube 
equipment, the jacking pit length would need to be at least 2 times the length of the pilot 
tube equipment.  The typical width of the jacking pit would be about 10 to 15 feet.  The 
receiving pit is generally smaller in size and would need to accommodate the size of the 
auger casings.  Although the width of the pits would likely require closing one lane, there 
appears to be sufficient room on the roadway to still provide two lanes of travel.  In 
addition, the pit that will be on the east side of the intersection should not be located in 
front of the fire station.   
Since the top of the casing would need to extend at least five feet below the bottom 
of the box culvert, substantial shoring and groundwater control of the jacking and 
receiving pits are required.

HDD 
Horizontal directional drilling (HDD) is a trenchless, steerable installation method 
using a multi-axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface. The 
pilot hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid-assisted mechanical cutting, 
or a combination of the two methods. Depending on the subsurface conditions, water only 
or water mixed with bentonite and/or polymer can be used as a drilling fluid in a directional 
drilling application. The pilot hole is reamed, as necessary, to accommodate the pipe(s) to 
be installed. The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling machine. Most 
HDD systems use slurry to stabilize the walls of the borehole and to reduce the frictional 
drag on the pipeline being installed. Steering corrections are made in the subsurface 
materials by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired orientation and then advancing 
the shoe and string without rotation. 
In general, the HDD pilot hole is drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit angles 
between 8 and 15 degrees measured from horizontal. However, steeper entry angles 
(about 20 degrees) may be required to avoid underground utilities and/or obstructions. 
Shallow working pits may be required at each end of the bore to allow placement of the drill 
pipe and to allow transition to the conventional pipe trench installation. The product pipe 
should be installed in one continuous operation, preferably without pause, and should 
protrude a sufficient distance beyond the entry and exit points to allow proper termination 
or connection at a later time. Typically, high-density polyethylene (HDPE), steel, and 
polyvinyl chloride (PVC) pipes may be installed using HDD methods.  We believe that either 
HDPE or fusible PVC should be used as a jacket for the water main.  Steel casing should 
not be considered since the steel casing will not bend through tight vertical curves required 
to cross this area. 
The very soft clayey silt and very loose silty sand and gravel would make steering 
a drill hole in these subsurface materials more difficult and complicated. There is also a 
potential that ground heave or hydraulic fracturing of the very soft and very loose soils 
may occur during the drilling and/or pullback. The unconsolidated subsurface materials 
generally have short stand-up times and tend to have viscous fluid properties when 
disturbed by vibrations. Therefore, proper drilling mud properties and/or casing of the hole 
will be required in order to maintain the open hole integrity of the pilot hole and/or reamed 
hole. Otherwise, ground heave or hydraulic fracturing of the soil formations may occur 
during pullback if the reamed hole is not properly flushed of debris and/or caved-in 
materials or if excessive drilling slurry pressure conditions are not controlled during the 
HDD installation. 
The water main could extend deeper into the coralline detritus, which could make 
the steering of the drill hole less difficult. In addition, the deeper HDD installation depths 
could reduce the potential for ground heave or hydraulic fracturing. 
Staging areas will be required at the entry and exit locations for the HDD installed 
pipelines. Staging area requirements at the entry locations will depend on the size of the

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been estimated 
by taping from visible features shown on the Plan & Profile drawings prepared by Austin, 
Tsutsumi & Associates, Inc., dated August 30, 2022. Elevations of the borings were 
estimated from spot elevations shown on the same drawings. The field boring locations and 
elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be noted that 
the groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuation, rainfall, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

Associates, Inc. and their project consultants for specific application to the planning and

Street, 8-Inch Water Main Replacement project in accordance with generally accepted 
geotechnical engineering principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in the preliminary planning and design of the trenchless installation of the water

The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly

probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to attain a 
properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is recommended to 
accommodate these possible extra costs.
