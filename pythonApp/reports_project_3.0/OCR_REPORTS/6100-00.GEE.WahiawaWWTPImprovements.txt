2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger Koolau Shield Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae
Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was
partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank The banking of the
Koolau lavas against the Waianae Volcano formed a broad plateau in the central portion
of the island which is now known as the Schofield Plateau The site is situated in the
central portion of this plateau
In situ chemical weathering of the Koolau Volcano lava flows has occurred during
the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has transformed the surface lavas
into a deep mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general residual soils and saprolite
are composed mainly of silty materials and are typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to less weathered basaltic rock formation with
increasing depth

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are
so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some
of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to
moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in
Hawaii over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from
the Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not
confined only to the Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a

3 1 Foundations
We understand the new structures planned will be constructed at depths ranging
from at or near existing grades to approximately 15 feet below the existing ground
surface Based on the generally stiff subsurface conditions encountered at the project
site we recommend using shallow spread and or continuous footings or mat
foundations to support the new structures planned A net allowable bearing pressure of
up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used in the design of the shallow
footings and mat foundations bearing on the recompacted on site clayey soils This net
bearing value is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be increased by up to
one third 1 3 when considering transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces It should be noted that the recommended allowable bearing pressure is
a net value therefore the allowable bearing pressure should not be increased further
for increased width and depth of footing embedment A minimum footing size of 3 feet
by 3 feet should be used in the design of spread footings Continuous footings should
be designed using a minimum width of 18 inches for foundation settlement
considerations
For the mat foundations we recommend placing the foundation mat on a
minimum 6 inch thick layer of aggregate subbase to provide for uniform support The
aggregate subbase layer should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction A modulus of subgrade reaction of up to 200 pounds per square inch per
inch of deflection pci may be used to design the mat foundations Although
groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration it should be noted that the groundwater level at the project site could
fluctuate due to the proximity of the adjacent Wahiawa Reservoir Where waterproofing
will be provided for the mat foundation and or below grade floors we recommend
providing a minimum 6 inch thick layer of mud slab below the foundation slab in lieu of
the 6 inch thick aggregate subbase
For footings bearing on the on site soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively
firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete

To reduce the potential for structural distress to lightly loaded slabs we
recommend providing a minimum of 6 inches of aggregate subbase material below the
slab cushion fill The aggregate subbase material should extend beyond the footprint of
the new slab on grade areas by a minimum of 24 inches Prior to the placement of the
aggregate subbase the subgrades for slabs on grade should be scarified to a depth of
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
For interior slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic or vibrations
such as pedestrian traffic we recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick cushion fill
layer consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs
to provide for uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a capillary moisture break To
reduce the potential for excessive moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion
fill layer Consideration should be given to providing flexible floor coverings such as
carpet or sheet vinyl on the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab
cracking
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled
tightly against the edges of the slabs with low expansion relatively impervious soils
on site clayey soils These areas also should be graded to divert water away from the
slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs and foundations

bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation or similar free draining type material below the pipes for support The
granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of
one fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or 12 inches whichever is greater

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads acting on the
buried pipes an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot pcf may be
used for compacted backfill material within the trench excavation and above the
pipes In addition a coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used to calculate the
frictional resistance between the compacted backfill and the sidewalls of the
excavated trench If the trench excavations are shored using steel sheet piles or
similar type shoring the frictional resistance between the backfill material and the
sidewalls of the trench should be neglected
For underground pipes earth forces acting upon the pipe increase rapidly with the
width of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum
Traffic loads on the buried pipes also should be considered for the portion of the
pipes located in roadways In general the traffic load may be modeled as 2 feet of
surcharge fill or a vertical surcharge pressure of 240 psf
Trench Backfill
In general we recommend using free draining granular materials such as
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for the initial trench backfill
from the pipe bedding up to about 12 inches above the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material around the pipes and placement could result in backfill
settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or imported
non expansive select granular fill materials The trench backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than

In general the excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal

Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 15 feet may be required for the

Based on the stiff subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated depth

considered for temporary shoring of the trench excavations for the new
underground piping planned for the project For the deeper excavations and

APPENDIX B
Geotechnical Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Geotechnical Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
One set of corrosion test including pH ASTM G 51 and In Situ and Saturated
Resistivity ASTM G 57 was performed on a bulk sample obtained from our field
exploration The test results are provided on Plate B 1
Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the on site clayey soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 2
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 3
Three Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 136 including hydrometer tests
ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the
test results are provided on Plate B 4
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey soils
three Unconfined Compression tests were performed on selected in situ samples in
accordance with ASTM D 2166 The individual stress strain curves of the unconfined
compressive strength tests are presented on Plates B 5 through B 7
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The Direct Shear test results are
presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 10
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship The test results are presented on Plate B 11
