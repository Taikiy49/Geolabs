EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at 51-484 Kamehameha Highway in Ka’a’awa on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The new septic tank and seepage pit are located on the east side of the parking 
lot.  The parking lot consists of both Portland cement and asphaltic concrete (AC) surfaces 
that are generally in fair condition. An existing concrete rubble masonry wall was 
observed along the east side of the property line. Based on the topographic survey, the 
site is relatively flat with an existing ground surface elevation of about +8 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL).  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program at the site consisted of drilling and sampling two 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of about 5 and 17 feet below the 
existing ground surface, respectively.  Boring Nos. 1 and 2 were drilled near the proposed 
septic tank and seepage pit locations, respectively.  The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration encountered 3 and 4 inches of AC underlain by 
about 6 inches of base material consisting of medium dense silty gravel.  Below the base 
material, fill materials on the order of about 1.5 to 2 feet thick were encountered. The fill 
materials consisted of medium stiff to stiff clayey silt and loose silty sand in Boring Nos. 
1 and 2, respectively. The fill materials were generally underlain by loose to medium 
dense silty sand extending to a depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Older alluvium consisting of medium stiff silty clay was encountered below the silty sand 
and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 17 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  
We encountered groundwater in Boring No. 1 at a depth of about 7.1 feet below 
the existing ground surface. It should be noted that groundwater levels may change due 
to tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS 
We understand that a new septic tank and seepage pit are planned for the 7-Eleven 
Ka’a’awa store located at 51-484 Kamehameha Highway in Ka’a’awa on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. Therefore, we have conducted a field percolation test to obtain data for the design 
of the sewage disposal system.  Detailed discussions of our geotechnical recommendations 
for the design of the project are presented in the following sections. 
Field Percolation Testing 
A field percolation test was performed in general accordance with the State of 
Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater 
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the 
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal.  The percolation test was performed by drilling 
a 4.5-inch diameter borehole to a depth of 4.7 feet below the existing ground surface.   
Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole to 
protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments.  During testing, the hole was 
carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack.  The time 
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of 
the percolation test was based.  Field percolation test results are summarized in the 
following table. In addition, the completed Site Evaluation/Percolation Test Form is 
presented as Plate 3.

A percolation rate on the order of about 12.5 minutes per inch was obtained for the 
test conducted in the silty sand encountered near the proposed seepage pit location.  
Based on the State of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 
11-62-34, seepage pits should be constructed in soils having a percolation rate faster 
than 10 minutes per inch.  Based on the information provided, the existing cesspool will 
be converted to a seepage pit by cleaning the existing cesspool of debris and backfilling 
with ½ to ¾-inch washed gravel to above the groundwater level.  We believe that the 
washed gravel should have a percolation rate faster than 10 minutes per inch. 
Septic Tank 
We anticipate that the bottom of the new septic tank will be about 7 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  Installation of the new septic tank will involve the removal of the 
existing pavement structure and excavation into stiff to medium stiff clayey silt and loose

To provide uniform support and a firm bearing condition for the septic tank, we

per square foot (psf) may be used to design the septic tank foundations.  This bearing value 
is for dead-plus-live load and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by seismic forces.

obtained from the field borings and percolation test.  Variations of subsoil conditions

approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition.  
Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the graphic 
representations and/or in the text herein.  These data have been reviewed and 
interpretations made in the formulation of this report.  However, it must be noted that 
fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall, tides, temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 5 and 17 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength parameters.  The test results are presented on Plate B-2. 
One set of Corrosivity test, including pH (ASTM G51), Chloride Content (EPA 
300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed by Geolabs, Inc. and Eurofins 
TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3.
