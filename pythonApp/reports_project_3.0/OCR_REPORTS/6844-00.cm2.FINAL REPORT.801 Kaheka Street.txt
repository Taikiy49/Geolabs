This letter report presents the findings from our due diligence study conducted for 
the proposed development at 801 Kaheka Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. 
This letter report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical 
recommendations based on our review of readily available subsurface information in our 
files for the project site.  Field exploration, including borings, were not conducted for this 
study but should be performed prior to design to verify and confirm the assumptions of the 
subsurface conditions below the site. These preliminary recommendations are intended to 
inform the owner about potential geotechnical risks involved and the geotechnical 
considerations that may need to be addressed for development of the proposed project. 
The findings and preliminary recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
additional services and limitations noted at the end of this letter report.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island.  It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct while 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below 
Koolau Volcano lavas in Central Oahu. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its geomorphology 
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea 
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain.  The 
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are 
intercalated with terrigenous sediments.  The base of the stratigraphic section in this area 
is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as 
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world.  These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level on the Island of Oahu.  About 15,000 years ago, a 
relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred.  During that sea level rise, the valleys in the 
project area were drowned.  In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its 
present stand.  Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in 
low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft and/or 
loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainage 
ways. 
In the early part of this century, the Waikiki-Ala Moana area consisted of low, 
marshy wetlands.  As the City of Honolulu grew and the area was urbanized, man-made 
fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons for development.  The fill 
materials placed on the lagoonal deposits consisted of silty sands with coral fragments. 
The lower clayey materials were deposited during periodic influxes of non-marine alluvial 
debris, which apparently smothered the coral reef development.  Land development and 
reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki area to its present 
form.  It should be noted that much of the resulting fill materials placed are of poor quality 
in terms of supporting heavy structural loads.

ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The project site is in an area of complex geology. Based on our experience and a 
review of the general geology of the area, portions of the site may be underlain by a 
relatively hard near surface coral layer. However, the competency and distribution of this 
upper coral layer may be erratic due to an ancient stream channel that may be present. An 
ancient alluvial stream channel (known as Kaheka Channel) is located in the vicinity of the 
Ewa side of the project site near Kaheka Street.  We understand the buried alluvial stream 
channel may be on the order of about 200 to 300 feet wide and is generally oriented 
(longitudinally) in a northeast-southwest direction.  The “upper” coral ledges located within 
the channel have been eroded away and replaced with thick sequences of alluvial soils 
extending to depths of about 110 to 150 feet below the existing ground surface, or more.   
Therefore, the site may contain at least two distinct subsurface conditions. Where 
the ancient stream channel cut through the site (probable in the western portion), the 
conditions may consist of a thin layer of fill about 5 feet thick over soft/loose lagoonal 
deposits to depths of about 20 to 35 feet. Below the lagoonal deposits, variable deposits of 
alluvium mixed with coralline sand and cinder may be encountered to depths of up to 
about 150 feet underlain by coral detritus. 
In the eastern portion of the site, hard coral layers may be encountered near the 
ground surface and possibly at depths of about 20 to 30 feet below the ground surface. 
These “upper” coral ledges may be underlain by coralline detritus materials interbedded 
with alluvium, coral formation, and dense sandstone ledges extending to depths of over 
100 feet below the ground level. 
In general, we envision that the groundwater levels will be near sea level, ranging 
from about -1 to +2 feet MSL.  It should be noted that groundwater levels can fluctuate 
depending on tidal fluctuations (due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific 
Ocean), seasonal precipitation, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and other factors. 
Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the 2006 International Building Code (IBC) (current code adopted by the 
City and County of Honolulu), the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and the 
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be evaluated.  Based on the 
anticipated subsurface conditions and a design peak ground acceleration of 0.15g, 
liquefaction of the loose lagoonal deposits may occur, resulting in appreciable settlements

In general, the project site is underlain by soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits 
overlying thin coral ledges and medium dense coralline detritus deposits at greater depths. 
The coral ledges and medium dense coralline detritus are generally not susceptible to

Therefore, the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground settlements in 
the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+) with a peak ground 
acceleration of 0.15g.  
Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design 
As indicated previously, the subsurface conditions may consist of thin surface fills 
over loose lagoonal deposits (about 25 feet thick) overlying coral ledges or alluvium 
extending to over 100 feet below the existing ground surface.  Based on the geology of the 
area, the site may be subject to liquefaction. Therefore, the seismic design of the building 
structures at the project site may need to be designed based on a Soil Profile Type SF in 
accordance with the International Building Code (2006). 
These seismic related issues will be critical to the structural design of the high rise 
structure and should be further evaluated. Therefore, we strongly recommend conducting 
seismic shear wave velocity profiling using Seismic Cone Penetrometer Testing (SCPTu) 
equipment as part of the design-level geotechnical engineering exploration in addition to 
preparation of a site specific response spectrum.

Seismic Design Considerations 
As mentioned previously, the project site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils. 
Therefore, the effects of a potential seismic event will need to take into consideration the 
design of the new condominium project planned at the site.  We recommend conducting a 
site-specific seismic response evaluation, including: (1) seismic shear wave velocity 
profiling using SCPTu equipment; and (2) a site-specific response spectrum analysis, for 
the seismic design of the high-rise structure.  We anticipate deep foundations may be 
utilized to mitigate the liquefaction concerns. 
Foundation Considerations 
Variable subsurface conditions may be encountered due to the ancient stream 
channel crossing adjacent to the site. Although hard coral formation may be encountered 
in portions of the site, it is our opinion that the coral may be too erratic and discontinuous 
to provide suitable support for the high rise structure planned. Therefore, for the 
preliminary purposes of this study we assume the presence of the lagoonal and alluvial 
deposits.  
Based on the compressible lagoonal deposits anticipated and significant structural 
demands on the high-rise condominium foundations, we believe a deep foundation 
support system, such as pile foundations or drilled shaft foundations, would be required for 
support of the proposed structure.  We envision these deep foundation support systems, 
on the order of about 80 to 120 feet deep, would be similar to the designs for high-rise 
buildings in the Waikiki/Ala Moana areas.  Evaluation of the feasibility of pile and drilled 
shaft foundation alternatives to support the proposed building structure should be 
conducted for the development.  Based on our recent experiences on high-rise building 
developments, we believe that drilled shaft foundations would likely be more efficient when 
compared to piles.  Detailed discussion for the preliminary design of the building 
foundations utilizing drilled shaft foundations is provided below. 
In general, we believe each column location for the building structure may be 
supported by one drilled shaft to reduce the size of the footing at each column location. 
Based on the anticipated structural loads (on the order of about 1,000 to 3,000 kips per 
column), the proposed building structure would likely be supported by a combination of 
30 and 42-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts, depending on the vertical 
and lateral load demands on the foundations.  The drilled shafts would extend through the 
loose and/or soft lagoonal deposits and would embed into the coral ledges and medium 
dense coralline detritus deposits.  The drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily 
from adhesion between the shaft and surrounding soils.

Based on preliminary analyses, the 42-inch diameter drilled shafts on the order of 
about 100 to 120 feet deep would be required to develop allowable compressive loads of 
about 2,000 to 3,000 kips per drilled shaft to support the structural loads anticipated at the 
column locations of the proposed building structure.  For the lighter structural loads 
(less than 1,000 kips per column), we recommend utilizing drilled shafts with a smaller 
diameter, such as 30 inches, to support the columns with lighter structural loads. 
It should be noted some difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during 
installation of the drilled shafts due to the presence of loose granular soils and shallow 
groundwater levels anticipated.  To reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, 
temporary casing and/or use of drilling fluids will likely be necessary to maintain the 
integrity of the drilled holes during drilled shaft installation.  Furthermore, placement of 
concrete by tremie methods will be required for the drilled shafts because groundwater will 
be encountered within the depths of the drilled shafts. 
It should be noted that cavities and voids are commonly encountered in the 
coralline materials in the project vicinity.  Therefore, the actual volume of concrete required 
to fill the drilled shaft foundation may be appreciably more than the theoretical concrete 
volume.  Based on our experience at the Waikiki/Ala Moana areas, we envision that 
average concrete overpour on the order up to about 50 to 60 percent over the theoretical 
concrete volume may be required for the drilled shaft foundations. 
Ground Settlement 
Based on the existing topography, we anticipate minimal site grading work would 
be required to achieve the design finished grades at the site.  Due to the presence of loose 
and/or soft lagoonal deposits underlying the site at shallow depths, we believe new fills, if 
required, should be placed as soon as practical to induce the anticipated ground 
settlements to occur prior to construction of structures, slabs-on-grade, and pavements. 
Settlement monitoring should be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the 
estimated settlements during the waiting period prior to foundation construction over the 
fills. 
Ground Floor Slabs 
We anticipate that the ground floor slabs for the new building construction will 
consist of reinforced concrete slabs-on-grade.  Based on the existing topography and the 
anticipated finished floor elevation, we envision that the slabs-on-grade for the proposed 
building structure will be supported either on new compacted fills placed to raise the 
existing ground surface to the finished subgrades or on the existing ground conditions. 
As mentioned previously, the project site could be subjected to ground settlements 
in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event (M6+).  Seismically 
induced ground settlements on the order of about several inches may be anticipated, and 
the building slabs-on-grade may sustain some damage.  Therefore, there exists the 
potential for damage and distress to the building slabs-on-grade (not structurally

As discussed above, concrete slabs-on-grade may be subjected to ground

anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to slab-on-grade construction. 
Dewatering of Below-Grade Excavations 
Dewatering is not anticipated because a basement level is not planned.  However, 
some dewatering may be required during excavation and construction of the foundation 
caps, elevator pits and other below-grade structures.  Special attention should be given to 
the effects of dewatering on the adjacent properties and beyond. 
Based on our initial evaluation of the dewatering requirements of projects in the 
Waikiki area, we believe soil stabilization using jet-grouting or similar methods may need 
to be implemented for groundwater control of deeper excavations, if planned.  In addition, 
the excavation support system for the below-grade construction will have a significant 
effect on the dewatering considerations.  The selected shoring system for the below-grade 
excavations will need to be carefully evaluated to reduce the potential impacts of the 
shoring on the adjacent properties and travelways. 
Due to the shallow groundwater levels anticipated, dewatering of planned 
excavations, such as the elevator pits and other below-grade structures, will be required 
for the project construction.  Therefore, we recommend installing groundwater monitoring 
wells during the field exploration program for the performance of field permeability tests, 
collection of groundwater samples for National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System 
(NPDES) permit application, and groundwater level monitoring purposes.
