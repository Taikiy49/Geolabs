INTRODUCTION
This report represents the results of a subsurface investiga
tion undertaken at Pier 1 and at the proposed extension of
Substation J 15 at the U S Naval Submarine Base Pearl Harbor
Oahu Hawaii The purpose of the investigation was to provide
soil data for the dredging of the Pier 1 area and for the
design of the extension of Substation J 15
The scope of this investigation includes the drilling of five
exploratory borings field reconnaisance evaluation of exist
ing geologic and soils literature made available laboratory
testing and engineering evaluation to develop information and
recommendations for design

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project sites are located on the southwestern end of the
U S Naval Submarine Base at Pearl Harbor The general area of
the project sites is shown on the Project Vicinity Map Plate
1
Pier 1 is an existing reinforced concrete pier 18 feet wide
and 250 feet long and is supported by 16 inch square concrete
piles There is a timber pile fender system along the edges of
the pier This pier provides Berthing positions S2 and S3
The substation extension site abuts the existing Substation
J 15 Building S637 at its southwestern side The project
area is currently paved with asphalt concrete and used for
automobile parking and as a work area for the adjacent rigging
shop
The general layout of Pier 1 is shown on Plate 2 1 and that of
Substation J 15 is shown on Plate 2 2

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a
complex deposition and erosion brought on by glacioeustatic sea
level changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the
Pleistocene Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau
Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or es
tuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300
feet below and rising about 95 feet above the present sea level
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during late Pleistocene
era eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits
of volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1146 00 Page 4
locally called mudrock layers Subsequent erosion dis
sected and thinned those mantles of tuff
During the Recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand
the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine depo
Sits Those deposits were subsequently covered by man made
fill placed during the middle portion of this century for land
reclamation projects during the construction of the new exist
ing Naval Submarine Base
SOIL CONDITIONS
General
The subsurface conditions at the project sites were explored by
drilling 3 borings along Pier 1 and 2 borings at the proposed
Substation J 15 expansion site The locations of borings are
shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The soils condi
tions encountered are described in the following sections under
each site
Pier 1
In the outboard area of the pier our Borings 1 and 2 encoun
tered soft or loose marine deposits to 100 feet at Boring 1 and
56 5 feet at Boring 2 the maximum depths drilled Boring 3 on
the inboard end of the pier encountered loose marine deposits
approximately 8 feet thick overlying approximately 10 feet of

boring penetrated volcanic tuff to the maximum depth drilled of
56 8 feet
Substation J 15
At the proposed Substation J 15 extension location Borings 4
5 encountered 8 to 12 feet of man made fill The fill was
dense granular coralline material near the surface but graded
looser with depth to the range of medium dense at or near the

Piling Demolition
Demolition of Pier 1 will include removing the decking and
the piling According to the pile records shown on US Navy
PACDIV Plan File No Z N15 127 the piles on the outerboard
half of the pier were driven to 40 to 90 feet below the
harbor bottom Some of the inboard piles are not as deeply
embedded and even have pile tips above the design dredge
bottom elevation A cross section showing the range of
pile tip penetrations and generalized subsurface condition
is shown on Plate 3
Judging from the pile embedment depths we believe that
most outboard piles from Pile Bent No 7 108 feet from
the concrete bulkhead line to the end of pier will be very
difficult to extract and they must be cut off at some depth
below design dredge bottom However some of the inboard
piles may be extracted particularly those tipped above the
dredge bottom or with very shallow embedment beneath the
design dredge bottom The extraction may only be accon
plished after materials around the piling are excavated
sufficiently to unfreeze the piles

Generally piles should be cut off a few feet below the
design dredge bottom The depth of pile cut off should
consider the type of dredging equipment to be used to
maintain the harbor bottom most often a suction dredge
This may call for cutting the piles 2 to 5 feet below the
design depth of dredging
The piles within 10 feet outboard of the concrete bulkhead
line should be cut off at the existing mudline We believe
that these piles if extracted will tend to reduce lateral
earth support to the bulkhead piles and sheetpiling There
fore these piles should be cut off at mudline instead of
being extracted
Dredging
Based on the boring data and pile record we believe that
most materials to be dredged consist of soft gray silt and
loose sand and gravel of lagoonal deposits from outboard of
the pier to about 70 feet from the concrete bulkhead line
From about 70 feet the material at design dredge bottom
will include dense sand gravel or stiff Clay and harder
volcanic tuff The excavation of volcanic tuff may require
special dredging tool such as drop chisel prior to clam
shell or dragline dredging

To minimize difficult underwater excavation of tuff the
dreding limit may be set at 70 feet from the bulkhead and
from thence maintain a 2 1 Slope until the slope daylights
on the existing harbor botton
However if a larger dredged area is desired the toe of
slope should not be located less than 40 feet outboard from
the bulkhead line The tuff or coral rock may be trimmed
to a near vertical slope of horizontal vertical to
the top of the tuff or coral at about elevation 75 US
Navy Datum The overlying stiff silty Clay and dense
gravel be cut at 1 1 slope till the Slope daylights to the
harbor bottom Although sloughing of soft and or loose
harbor sediments overlying the stiff soils and volcanic
tuff may develop we believe such amount will be relatively
small and will not substantially affect the slope
configuration
The slopes recommended above are based primarily on slope
stability of the underwater cut Slope We believe that the
dredge slope configuration discussed will not significantly
affect the present stability of the bulkhead sheetpile
Dredging closer than 40 feet will result in reducing the
sheetpile and bulkhead soil embedment and affect their
stability The closer the dredged toe line to the concrete
bulkhead line the more critical the condition will become

Substation J 15 Addition
As discussed previously the subsurface conditions at the pro
posed addition to Substation J 15 consist of 7 to 12 feet of
coral fill underlain by somewhat compressible loose silty sand
and medium dense sands on volcanic tuff at about 28 feet
According to File Plan No Z N26 195 Basement Foundation
Plan for Quarry Lock Substation Building S637 is supported on
wooden piling However an early 1960 addition as shown on
File Plan Nos 917253 and 917254 is supported on shallow
continuous footings 4 8 to 5 0 wide with a structurally
Supported floor
If the proposed new addition to Substation J 15 cannot tolerate
any significant differential settlement a pile foundation
should be used However if the new addition can be struc
turallly separated from the existing building and tolerate some
settlement a shallow continuous foundation may be utilized
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1146 00 Page 10
For pile foundation predrilling will be required to minimize
ground vibration that may affect the existing structures and
other facilities nearby The predrill depth should be at least
10 feet below the existing grade to penetrate through the dense
surface fill For low capacity piles 10 to 12 inch square
prestressed concrete piles are commonly used However the
drill holes are typically 18 inches in diameter To provide a
better fit of pile in a predrilled hole it would be desirable
to use 16 5 inch octagonal piles Piles should be inserted in
the drilled holes 18 inches in diameter and driven to the
volcanic tuff at a depth of about 28 feet The actual pile
length should be so casted that the tip would be able to reach
a depth of 33 feet to account for possible minor variations in
tuff depths and minimize splicing or build up of pile
Piles may be driven with a hammer delivering at least 15 000
ft lb of energy per blow For piles up to 40 tons each the
pile may be stopped at a penetration resistance of 6 blows per
inch for the last 3 inches of penetration using the above
hammer For a heavier hammer the driving criteria may be
modified accordingly by a soils engineer Since the proposed
addition is relatively small no test piling is deemed
necessary
If a settlement of one half to three fourths of an inch can be
tolerated a continuous footing under thick grade beam may be
utilized
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 11
For such foundation design an allowable soil bearing of 2000
psf at a depth of 1 5 feet below the lowest adjacent grade may
be used The bottom of footing should be recompacted to 95 of
the maximum density determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Any weak or soft areas under the footing should be overexca
vated and backfilled with compacted coral fill
Generally the settlement will develop rapidly and the majority
of settlement will be completed probably within 2 months of
construction To raise the grade the floor should be struc
turally supported to avoid filling because the increased fill
weight may include larger settlements and would adversely
affect the adjacent ground and structures
