INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kailua Kona Swimming Pool Complex at the existing Kailua
Kona Gymnasium in Kailua Kona on the island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that a new swimming poo complex is planned for the existing
Kailua Kona Gymnasium in the Kailua Kona area on the island of Hawaii Based on the
information provided the new swimming pool complex will be located to the northwest of
the existing gymnasium and bounded by existing parking areas on the other three sides
The project site will cover an area of approximately 200 by 200 feet in plan
dimensions The new facility will consist of a 50 meter swimming pool and a one story
building housing the locker rooms office and storage The new building will be of CMU
construction with mineral fiber slate roofing covering approximately 10 000 square feet in
area Based on the information provided to us the finish floor elevation of the locker
room building is set at 20 feet Mean Sea Level MSL with the bottom of the swimming
pool at approximately 8 feet MSL Therefore the majority of the grading work will be in
the swimming pool excavation with minor grading work anticipated for the building
construction Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer
maximum column loads are estimated to be on the order of 55 kips per column with wall
loads of approximately 2 5 kips per lineal foot

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3617 00 Page 5
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which
spread and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities
are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the
lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and
inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a
large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizonta extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the
porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed locker room
building In order to provide more uniform support for the foundation and to reduce the
potential for foundation distress resulting from differing subgrade conditions we
recommend that the footings be extended through the surficial fill materials and bear on
the underlying basalt rock formation for support of the proposed locker room building

An allowable bearing pressure of up to 6 000 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation
encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Depending on the depths to the basalt rock formation deeper
footing embedments may be required at localized areas for the footings to bear on the
basalt rock formation If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements
they should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 60 may be used for footings bearing on the underlying basalt
rock formation Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be calculated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f
These values assume that the concrete for footings is cast neat against the basalt rock or
embedded in structural fill Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area and the highly vesicular nature of the basalt
rock encountered voids and or cavities may be present in the basalt rock formation
encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from collapse of the cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during
the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help
delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with
a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule

representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of

Static Lateral Earth Pressures
All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due
to adjacent soils and surcharge effects The recommended lateral earth pressures
for design of retaining walls expressed in equivalent fluid pressures are presented
below
Walls unrestrained atthe top 35p s f equivalent fluid pressure
active condition level backfill per foot of depth
Walls restrained at the top 50 p s f equivalent fluid pressure
at rest condition level backfill per foot of depth
The values provided above assume that on site granular fill and or select granular
fill less than 3 inches in maximum dimension will be used to backfill behind the
wall It is assumed that the backfill behind retaining walls will be compacted to
between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over compaction of the retaining
wall backfill should be avoided In general an active condition may be used for
walls that are free to deflect by as much as 0 5 percent of the wall height If the
tops of the walls are not free to deflect beyond this degree or are restrained the
walls should be designed for the at rest condition These lateral earth pressures
do not include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by groundwater
trapped behind the walls
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures Because of the relatively permeable site conditions a

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
