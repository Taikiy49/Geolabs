The following contains our thermal curing plan results of concrete thermal 
analyses, thermal monitoring plan, and qualification of inspection personnel as required 
by the contract documents for the above project. Specification Section 31 63 30 (Drilled 
Concrete Shaft Foundations) requires that drilled shaft measuring 5 feet in diameter or 
greater be considered mass concrete and subject to the requirement of Section 03 70 
00 (Mass Concrete). Specification Section 03 70 00 requires monitoring and controlling 
the internal temperature and temperature differential of mass concrete during the heat 
dissipation period. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the approved 
4,500 pounds per square inch (psi) concrete mix design (dated October 18, 2014) 
contains 540 pounds of cement per cubic yard with 25 percent of Class F Fly Ash for 
the drilled shaft construction of the above project. The concrete mix design submittal is 
attached in Appendix A for reference. The length of the drilled shafts varies from about 
72 to 82 feet with an 8-foot diameter of lower shaft and a 10-foot or 9-foot diameter of 
upper shaft. Detailed dimensions of the drilled shafts are presented in the approved 
Reinforcing Steel Shop Drawings, dated December 1, 2014, as attached in Appendix B 
for reference. 
Specification Section 03 70 00 requires an analysis of the anticipated thermal 
developments in the drilled shafts for temperature ranges using the selected mix design, 
casting procedures and material. It requires that the maximum temperature and 
temperature differential should not exceed 160°F and 35°F, respectively.

The concrete behavior was evaluated using the program ConcreteWorks, 
originally developed by Texas Department of Transportation (TxDOT), using a 
non-linear regression analysis to model the heat of hydration development induced by 
activation energy of concrete cementitious component. Based on our experience, we 
modeled ground temperature to be about 78°F (Fahrenheit) with concrete placement 
temperature ranging from 70°F to 80°F. 
Based on the above information, the maximum temperature in the specified 
drilled shaft was predicted to be approximately 145°F to 157°F with the maximum 
temperature differential to be around 23°F to 27°F, depending on the concrete 
placement temperature. Results of the concrete temperature prediction are presented 
on Plate 1 for the maximum temperature and Plate 2 for the maximum temperature 
differential. Based on the information provided, the concrete placement temperature will 
be around 75°F by mixing with chilled water. Thus, the maximum temperature and 
temperature differential may be approximately 151°F and 25°F, respectively, meeting 
the specified temperature requirements. 
It also requires using thermistor devices to measure temperature development 
between the interior core center and exterior surface. We propose installing three 
thermistor probes (Geokon Model 3800) at the middle of the drilled shaft (between top 
of drilled shaft and bottom of drilled shaft). Location of the thermistor probes consists of 
one near the center and two at opposite sides of the rebar cage, as illustrated on Plate 
3. The concrete temperature will be measured at intervals of one hour and recorded 
continuously by Geokon LC-2 Datalogger. The concrete temperature measurement will 
begin upon completion of concrete placement and terminate after the measured 
temperature near the shaft center is about 130°F, within 50°F of the ambient 
temperature (about 80°F), or for a maximum of two weeks. Details of specifications for 
thermistor probe and datalogger are included in Appendix C for reference. 
In accordance with Specification Section 03 70 00, a specialty engineer with a 
minimum of 5 years of experience should inspect and approve the installation of 
monitoring devices and verify effective temperature recording process for the first drilled 
shaft installation. We propose the following personnel to perform the concrete temperature 
monitoring. 
Provide experienced engineers, Mr. Tim Lin and Mr. Jason Seidman, who 
have 8 years of instrumentation monitoring experience, to inspect and verify 
the specified thermistor probe installation and recording process. Mr. Lin and 
Mr. Seidman are licensed professional civil engineers in the State of Hawaii 
and are familiar with instrumentation data acquisition system. 
Provide a senior geotechnical engineer, Mr. John Chen, who has over 14 
years of licensed experience in the State of Hawaii and deep foundation 
instrumentation/testing experience, to provide supervision to the field 
personnel and consultation during concrete temperature sensor installation 
and monitoring.

stability over a long life span. Because their resistance
change is so great, it is unusual for cable effects to be
significant. However, for high accuracy work, and when
long cables are used, the cable resistance must be taken
into account, unless the Model 3810A is used. Standard

The correspondence between the resistance output in
ohms and the equivalent temperature in degrees Celsius
is presented in tabular form with each thermistor or
thermistor string. The high resistance of the thermistor
affords it a distinct measurement advantage inasmuch
as a four-wire resistance measurement to compensate
for cable effects is not required, as may be the case with
RTDs (Resistance Temperature Detectors). Thermistor
output is nominally 3000 ohms at 25°C and around this
temperature the rate of change of resistance is approxi-
mately 130 ohms/°C. For greater accuracy the resistance
of the cable leading to the thermistor can be taken into
account (with non-addressable sensors).
Readout
Thermistors can be read using either a GK-404 or GK-405
Readout, which both display the temperature directly in
: degrees Celsius. Alternatively, a digital ohmmeter can be
used in conjunction with look-up tables. For remote unat-
tended applications, thermistors can be connected to the
 Micro-1000 Datalogger (via the Model 8032 Multiplexer)
: or the Micro-800 Datalogger, both of which can provide
automatic data collection at pre-determined intervals,
and data transmission via wireless methods. For these
applications, connectors can be attached to the thermis-
tor strings to facilitate rapid connection.
