SECTION 1.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The new fuel storage tanks project site is located on Kwajalein Island in the Kwajalein 
Atoll of the Republic of the Marshall Islands. We understand it is desired to gather additional 
subsurface information at the Tank #8 site to supplement the field data performed by others in 
the surrounding area.  
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Area Map with Inset, Plate 1. 
In addition, the project location within Kwajalein Atoll is presented on the Atoll Map (Plate 2) 
and the Kwajalein Island Map and Inset (Plate 3) for ease of reference.  
Based on the agreed upon scope of services requested of Geolabs, Inc., our geotechnical 
field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling four borings to depths of about 62.5 to 
102.5 feet below the existing ground surface at the locations provided. As requested, we 
performed seismic shear wave velocity profiling in the 102.5-foot deep boring using our seismic 
cone penetration testing (SCPT) equipment conducted within a backfilled borehole. In addition, 
we also performed one exploratory SCPT probe adjacent to each drilled boring for a total of 
four CPT probes extending to depths of about 50.7 to 61.4 feet below the existing ground 
surface.

SECTION 3.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to geotechnical engineers for design are the subsurface materials 
encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, and 
the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following 
sections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site, the surface and 
subsurface materials encountered at the site, and the groundwater conditions encountered. 
3.1 
Regional Geology 
Kwajalein Atoll, part of the Republic of the Marshall Islands, is a pistol-shaped coral atoll 
located in the Ralik (Sunset) Island Chain at approximately 8 degrees North latitude. The origin 
of coral atolls was first postulated by Charles Darwin in 1842. His theory was that an atoll began 
as a fringing reef around a volcanic island. The island begins to subside tectonically at a slow 
rate so that the coral growth maintains a reef-line. As the island subsides and becomes smaller, 
the reef grows larger and becomes a barrier reef. When the island finally disappears below sea 
level, the upward-growing reef encloses only a lagoon and becomes an atoll. This basic theory 
has been somewhat modified by other considerations, such as eustatic sea level changes and 
cooled waters brought on by glacial activity.  
The detailed geology of Kwajalein Atoll is not nearly as well established as compared to 
Bikini and Enewetak Atolls and is primarily based on shallow boring logs prepared by the USACE 
and drilling logs prepared during the installation of monitoring wells. The seismic-refraction 
studies of Bikini, Eniwetok, and Kwajalein Atolls, by Raitt, Dobrin, and Perkins, have confirmed 
and extended the results obtained by drilling, which show that several thousand feet of 
relatively unconsolidated calcareous materials overlie a volcanic core in these atolls.  
The subsurface conditions in a coral atoll vary substantially in both the horizontal and 
vertical dimensions. Much of the large-scale vertical variation is the result of worldwide 
glacio-eustatic sea level changes that occurred during the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age). One 
significant low stand of the sea was a stand of relatively long duration at about 60 feet below 
the present sea level; however, the uppermost unconformity on Bikini and Enewetak Atolls 
appears at depths of approximately 26 to 40 feet below sea level. Throughout the world, many

The islets of Kwajalein Atoll are essentially accumulations of unconsolidated calcareous 
materials on the reef surface and/or hard erosion elements of higher reef surfaces. The ocean 
side reef is typically a flat, continuous, hard coral limestone reef with the surface elevation 
generally above Mean Low Water (MLW) elevation. The hardness of the coral limestone reef 
rock generally decreases with depth.  
The extension of the coral limestone reef rock from the ocean side through the land 
mass typically decreases in thickness and hardness and generally found at greater depths. Reef 
rock is not often found beneath the island surface several hundred feet from the ocean side 
shoreline. The subsurface material within the island mass consists of unconsolidated clastic 
limestone sediments ranging in size from silts to cobbles that typically classify as poorly-graded 
sand (SP), interrupted with occasional existence of coral limestone rock lenses.  
During World War II, bomb and shell craters were filled expeditiously with coconut 
trees, vegetal waste, and construction debris prior to backfilling with coralline sands. In 
addition, large amounts of hydraulic fill were placed to expand the land area of the Kwajalein 
Atoll. The approximate limits of the hydraulically placed fill are shown on the Kwajalein Island 
Map and Inset, Plate 3. Grading of the island over the past 70 years has brought the project site 
to its current state.

The following subsections briefly describe the general characteristics of each of the two 
primary stratigraphic units above based on our literature research, past experience with similar 
materials, and the field exploration and laboratory testing program.  
3.2.1 Recent Fill 
This stratigraphic unit represents the upper layer of soil material across the project site. 
As shown on the Kwajalein Island Map and Inset (Plate 3), the Defense Fuel Supply Point 
site is located outside of the Original Pre-1944 Island limits. As part of the United States 
development of the Kwajalein Atoll, areas of the atoll were built out using uncontrolled 
hydraulic fill consisting of dredged coralline materials.  
The quality and composition of the fill materials are variable. These fills were placed 
without proper compaction and quality control and may consist of broken coralline 
limestone, sandstone, and reef formations as well as coralline sands and gravel. The size 
of the coralline particles may vary widely and may consist of a mixture of silts, sands, 
gravel, and cobbles.  
3.2.2 Coralline Detritus 
This unit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations. The soils generally 
consist of uncemented loose to dense silty/sandy gravel and silty/gravelly sands. 
Coralline Detritus also may be weakly cemented with occasional moderate to strong 
cementation. Coralline Detritus materials were encountered extensively in the borings 
drilled for this project. 
3.2.3 Groundwater 
Groundwater level measurements were taken during advancement of the boreholes 
drilled for the project during our field exploration program. We encountered 
groundwater levels in the borings drilled at the project site at depths ranging from 
about 2.2 to 4.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The groundwater levels 
measured generally correspond to about Elevations +3.5 to +5.5 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) datum. The water level measurements collected are shown on the Logs of Borings 
in Appendix A. Due to the geology and size of Kwajalein Atoll, it should be noted that

SECTION 4.  
GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM 
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the Defense Fuel Supply Point – 
Tank #8 project generally consisted of the following field and laboratory work components. 
A brief description of each work component is presented below. Detailed discussions, findings, 
and test results of the various components are provided in the respective appendices herein. 
4.1 
Soil Borings and Associated Field Testing 
The soil borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig utilizing 4-inch diameter 
continuous-flight solid-stem augers (SSA) to advance the borings until groundwater was 
encountered and then continued with PQ-size wireline coring methods to advance the PQ-size 
casing from the ground surface. Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the 
borings by visual and textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and 
laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), 
as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in 
accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the drilled borings are presented on 
the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. 
Sampling of the soils generally was performed at about 5-foot intervals. Drive samples 
were obtained using the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) split-spoon sampler (ASTM D1586) 
and the modified California sampler (ASTM D3550). 
In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the SPT, which was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. SPT sampling provides a measure of the resistance 
or consistency of a soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-value, which is defined as the 
number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon sampler 12 inches with a 140-lb 
hammer falling 30 inches, after 6 inches of initial seating.

4.2 
Seismic Piezocone Penetration Testing 
Seismic piezocone penetration testing (SCPTu) was performed adjacent to each of the 
four drilled and sampled boring locations using seismic piezocone testing equipment, also 
referred to as Seismic CPT (SCPTu). The test procedures are discussed in more detail in 
Appendix B. Generally, conducting SCPTu involves the use of a steel cone connected to a 
friction sleeve that is hydraulically pushed into the soil. The tip resistance, sleeve friction, and 
piezometric pore pressures are measured, providing information that can be correlated to the 
soil type and behavior in a virtually continuous profile with depth. SCPTu can be performed only 
to the depths to which the cone tip can be hydraulically pushed with our truck-mounted drill rig 
set up. Four seismic piezocone penetration tests were performed adjacent to each of the drilled 
and sampled soil boring locations and are designated as Location Nos. 2019-CPT-01 through 
2019-CPT-04. At the locations tested, the SCPTu were terminated at depths ranging from about 
50.7 to 61.4 feet below existing grade. 
The seismic cone tip contains a geophone sensor. At each depth interval (approximately 
1 meter or 3.28 feet), the advancement of the cone tip was halted, a seismic pulse was 
generated at the ground surface by striking a loaded plate with a switched hammer, and the 
resulting seismic shear wave was detected at the geophone. The distance from the source and 
the wave arrival time allow for determination of the average shear wave velocity of the soil 
material over a depth interval. The seismic piezocone penetration test records are presented in 
Appendix B. 
4.3 
Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Testing 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at one completed borehole 
location (Boring No. 2019-TB-02) using SCPTu equipment. Procedures for the tests are 
discussed in more detail in Appendix C. In brief, the completed borehole was tremie-filled with 
time-release bentonite pellets in order to provide a medium through which the seismic cone tip 
could be pushed. The sleeve and tip resistance through the bentonite-filled borehole are not 
meaningful because the cone is pushed through the bentonite backfill; however, the seismic 
wave travels from the ground surface through the surrounding soils, allowing measurement of 
the average seismic shear wave velocity in the same manner as described above for the SCPTu.

LIMITATIONS

Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the FY17 Replace Fuel Storage Tanks, Defense 
Fuel Supply Point – Tank #8 project site by drilling and sampling four borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 2019-TB-01 through 2019-TB-04. The borings were extended to depths ranging 
from about 62.5 to 102.5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous-flight augers and rotary coring tools. The drilling and sampling work 
was conducted from May 14, 2019 through May 17, 2019. The boring locations are shown on 
the Kwajalein Island Map and Inset, Plate 3.  
Soil borings were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
(SSA) to the first encounter with groundwater and then continued with PQ-size wireline coring 
methods to advance the PQ-size casing from the ground surface. After the borings were 
advanced to the maximum depth of the boreholes, the bored holes generally were backfilled 
with soil cutting materials generated by the drilling work. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the 
laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for 
Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on 
the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered and other observations, such as groundwater 
levels, etc., are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled at the depths indicated in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were 
obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D3550, Standard Practice 
for Thick Wall, Ring-Lined, Spilt Barrel, Drive Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the harder materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond Core 
Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet 
of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would 
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality of 
rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound material in 
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, mechanical, 
and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the 
rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by 
Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Seismic Piezocone Penetration Tests 
Geolabs, Inc. conducted cone penetration tests using the seismic piezocone penetration 
testing equipment at four probe locations, designated as Location Nos. 2019‐CPT‐01 through 
2019‐CPT‐04, as shown on the Kwajalein Island Map and Inset, Plate 3. 
The  tests  were  performed  in  general  accordance  with  ASTM  D5778  –  Standard  Test 
Method for Performing Electronic Friction Cone and Piezocone Penetration Testing of Soils. The 
probes  extended  to  depths  ranging  from  about  50.7  to  61.4  feet  below  the  existing  ground 
surface  using  seismic  piezocone  penetration  testing  equipment  (SCPTU)  before  refusal 
conditions to our system were encountered. 
The seismic piezocone penetration test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10‐ton steel 
electronic subtraction cone (Serial Number DSG0824), manufactured by Vertek with an apex 
angle of 60 degrees and an area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) connected to a 
friction sleeve with an area of 23.25 square inches (150 square centimeters), into the ground at 
a rate of approximately 20 mm/second (+5 mm/second) using a CME‐75D truck‐mounted drill 
rig. A catalog cut of the 10‐ton electronic subtraction cone and the manufacturer’s calibration 
report  are  presented  in  Figures  B‐1.1  and  B‐1.2  and  Figures B‐2.1  through  B‐2.3  for 
illustration/information purposes only. 
The tip and side friction resistance and pore pressure induced immediately behind the 
cone tip (designated as the U2 parameter) during penetration are measured by strain gauges 
within  the  instrument  and  are  recorded  versus  the  depth  of  penetration  at  about  2‐inch 
(50‐millimeter) intervals. When the material encountered was too hard for further penetration 
by  the  SCPTU  equipment,  which  might  cause  potential  equipment  damage,  penetration  was 
halted, and the cone was withdrawn. 
The  cone  carries  a  uniaxial  horizontal  geophone  with  an  embedded  inclinometer  to 
detect  the  arrival  of  a  shear  wave  propagated  from  the  ground  surface.  The  seismic 
measurements are made when the SCPTU has stopped, and a seismic shear wave is sent into 
the subsurface. A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plate in opposite 
directions  with  a  switched  hammer  at  approximately  1‐meter  (3.28  feet)  intervals.  The 
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each depth 
interval.  The  vector  difference  of  these  depths  divided  by  the  time  difference  provided  the 
average seismic shear wave velocity of the subsurface material over the depth interval. 
The field data collected was subsequently processed in the office using the computer 
software program – CPT Coneplot developed by Vertek. Graphic representations of the seismic 
piezocone  penetration  test  data  are  presented  on  Plates  B‐1.1,  B‐2.1,  B‐3.1,  and  B‐4.1.  In 
general, the plots include cone tip resistance, friction ratio (sleeve friction over tip resistance),

• Pore Pressure

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Tests 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was performed using 
Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. Seismic shear wave velocity testing using 
our seismic cone penetration test equipment was performed at one selected boring location, 
designated as 2019-TB-02(SW), as shown on the Kwajalein Island Map and Inset, Plate 3. 
The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at approximately 1-meter 
(3.28 feet) intervals extending to a depth of about 99.9 feet below the existing ground surface. 
In order to conduct the shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test boring generally was 
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. Logs of the 
materials encountered in the boring are presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. After 
the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the bored hole was backfilled 
with 0.25-inch diameter time-release bentonite pellets. The temporary casing from the coring 
operation was used as a tremie pipe to place the bentonite pellets starting from the bottom 
and advancing upward. When the bentonite pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the 
borehole, the pellets start to slowly hydrate. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell and 
soften. The probe was then pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a depth of 
about 99.9 feet below the existing ground surface using the seismic cone penetration testing 
(SCPT) equipment. 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton steel 
electronic subtraction cone into the bentonite backfilled borehole. The cone has an apex angle 
of 60 degrees and a projected surface area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) 
equipped with an electronic depth sensor attached to the cone rod. The seismic cone carries a 
uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave generated and propagated 
from the ground surface. Seismic measurements are made when the SCPT has stopped and a 
shear wave is sent into the subsurface. A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a 
loaded plate with a switched hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow 
to the cone was measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths 
divided by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths provided the 
average shear wave velocity of the subsurface material over the depth interval. 
The shear wave velocities measured and the average shear wave velocity calculated for 
the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the boring location are presented on Plates C-1.1 through 
C-1.5. The weighted average shear wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear
wave velocity method described in Section 20.4 of ASCE Standard ASCE/SEI 7-10 referenced by
the International Building Code (2015 Edition).

Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our geotechnical field exploration program performed for the 
FY17 Replace Fuel Storage Tanks, Defense Fuel Supply Point project were visually examined and 
classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected samples were tested to evaluate the 
physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered. The laboratory 
testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, and 
gradation of the soils encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural 
moisture content, sieve analysis, and fines content tests. Results of the tests performed are 
presented on Plates D-1 through D-8. Detailed descriptions of the various tests performed for 
this exploration are presented below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture content and dry density tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid 
in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between test borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general accordance with 
ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, 
thirty-one (31) Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics 
and to aid in soil classification. The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C117 and 
C136. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates D-1 through D-7. In 
addition, twenty-three (23) tests were conducted on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
amount of fines content. The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D1140. The test 
results of the fines content are summarized on Plate D-8.
