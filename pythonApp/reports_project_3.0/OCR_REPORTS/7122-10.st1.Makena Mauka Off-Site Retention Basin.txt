SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered fill materials consisting of dense gravel and 
cobbles from the ground surface extending down to about 1 to 5 feet below the ground 
surface.  The fill materials are generally underlain by interbedded layers of a’a clinker 
and dense basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 
41.8 feet below the ground surface.  The basalt formation encountered was generally 
moderately weathered and hard.  The clinker materials generally consisted of medium 
dense cobbles and gravel. 
The majority of the retention basin will be constructed in cut condition.  In general, 
we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose dense basalt formations. In general, 
we believe the cut slopes planned at the site may be designed with a slope inclination of 
1H:1V or flatter. In areas where loose clinker materials and/or highly fractured basalt 
formations are exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes may need to be flattened to 
inclinations of 2H:1V or flatter. Therefore, provisions to flatten the cut slopes and/or 
construct retaining walls should be considered in the project design. For cut and fill slopes 
steeper than 3H:1V with vertical heights greater than 30 feet, we recommend providing a 
minimum 8-foot wide bench at the mid-point of the slope or at every 30-foot height interval 
to reduce the potential for significant erosion due to surface water runoff. 
Portions of the retention basin slopes will be constructed in a fill condition with a 
slope inclination of 2.5H:1V.  Retention basin embankment fill should consist of particles 
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and contain at least 60 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve.   
Global slope stability of the retention basin slopes was evaluated for both static 
and seismic conditions. Based on the results of the global slope stability analyses, the 
retention basin slopes have a minimum factor of safety of 7.5 and 2.9 for static and 
seismic conditions, respectively. Based on our analysis, we believe that the retention 
basin slopes generally has adequate factors of safety from global slope stability 
considerations. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Makena Mauka Off-Site Retention Basin project in Makena on the 
Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field 
exploration and laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations for 
design of the proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of the 
retention basin, retaining structures, and site grading only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The Makena Residences & Beach Club project is in the District of Makawao on 
the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project generally consists of renovating the existing 
hotel development into a luxury condominium complex. As part of the project, an off-site 
retention basin will be constructed. 
The retention basin will have a bottom elevation of about +200 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL).  A 36-inch diameter outlet drain and cement rubble masonry (CRM) lined 
inflow ditch and overflow spillway will be provided for the retention basin. 
Based on the preliminary plans, construction of the retention basin may require 
cuts of up to about 20 to 40 feet in depth to achieve design grades.  Minor fills of less 
than about 5 feet in height may be required in some areas.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older Tertiary Epoch 
(West Maui) and the more recent Pleistocene Epoch (East Maui), also known as 
Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain 
located between these two volcanoes. The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows 
from Haleakala Volcano ponding against West Maui Volcano. The gently sloping plains 
subsequently were filled with alluvium washed down from the slopes of West Maui and 
East Maui (Haleakala) Volcanoes. The erosional processes are dominated by the 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are 
transported downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
The project site is on the western flank of the East Maui Volcano, which forms 
the east side of the Island of Maui. The East Maui Mountain is primarily composed of 
lavas from the Kula and Hana Volcanic Series. The basalt formations at the project site 
may be part of the Kula Volcanic Series, which consist of a thick series of alkalic a`a 
lava flows.

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is situated about ½-mile mauka (east) of Makena-Alanui Road in 
Makena on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. An unpaved access road leads to the site from

The project site is currently vacant, undeveloped land.  Vegetation consists of

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic 
design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of

In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. Due to the relatively short period of documented 
earthquake monitoring in the State of Hawaii, information pertaining to earthquakes 
on the Island of Maui may not be complete. However, the following are 
two examples of reported earthquakes within the County of Maui. A Magnitude 6.8 
earthquake occurred on the Island of Maui in 1938, and the Lanai Earthquake of 
1871 was estimated at Magnitude 7 or greater. Based on these two reported 
earthquakes, the Island of Maui may be considered seismically active.

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of 
strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose 
saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the 
soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. 
Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the 
overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and 
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a 
mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not 
confined, will result in significant deformations.

3.1 
Retention Basin 
The retention basin will generally be constructed in a cut condition with a bottom 
elevation of about +200 feet MSL.  We envision that the cut slopes will generally expose 
interbedded layers of basalt formation and clinker materials.  A portion of the Makai 
(west) side of the retention basin will be constructed in a fill condition.  The fill slopes will 
have an inclination of 2.5H:1V. 
Global slope stability of the retention basin slopes were evaluated using the 
computer program Slide from RocScience, Inc. Both static and seismic conditions were 
taken into consideration for our slope stability analysis. 
For the seismic analysis, the maximum considered earthquake spectral response 
acceleration at short periods (Ss) was estimated at 0.974g. Based on Ss of 0.974g, a 
peak ground acceleration of 0.299g (amax = 0.299g) was estimated and used for the 
slope stability analysis. The maximum considered earthquake is based on an 
approximately 2,500-year return period. 
Based on the results of the global slope stability analyses, the retention basin 
slopes have a minimum factor of safety of 7.5 and 2.9 for static and seismic conditions, 
respectively. The Engineer Manual “Design and Construction of Levees” by the U.S. 
Army Corps of Engineers (EM 1110-2-1913) generally requires a minimum factor of 
safety of 1.4 for long-term slope stability. In addition, the minimum factor of safety 
required for short-term slope stability generally is between 1.0 and 1.2 based on 
EM 1110-2-1913. Based on our analysis, we believe that the retention basin slopes 
generally have adequate factors of safety from global slope stability considerations.

3.2.1 Retaining Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
using continuous strip footings to support the new retaining structures planned. 
We recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per 
square foot (psf) for design of footings bearing on the re-compacted on-site 
materials or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design finished grades. 
This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased 
by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
The bottom of foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade.  The bottom of footings constructed near the 
tops of slopes or on slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the 
horizontal distance measured from the outside edge (at the base of the footing) 
to the face of the slope is no less than 6 feet. 
In addition, foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, easements or 
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane 
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the 
footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for 
appreciable foundation settlement. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated until stiff/dense materials are exposed in the footing 
excavation. The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill 
materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the 
bottom of the footing may be extended down to bear directly on the underlying 
competent material. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less

It is assumed that the backfill behind retaining structures will be compacted to

needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads. 
3.2.3 Drainage 
In general, retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of 
permeable material, such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation material, placed near 
the bottom and along the length of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or 
weep holes. 
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or

prefabricated drainage product should also be connected hydraulically to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall. Unless covered by concrete or 
asphaltic concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively 
impervious materials, such as well-graded soils, to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the walls.

3.3.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, 
existing structures, and other unsuitable materials, should be removed and 
disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with well-compacted fills. The excavated loose and/or soft soils should be 
disposed of properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.3.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, fill materials required for retention basin embankment should consist of 
relatively impermeable materials.  Retention basin embankment fill should consist 
of particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and contain at least 
60 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
Other fill materials required for the project may consist of general fills.  General fill 
should be non-expansive with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The general fill material should also contain between 10 and 60 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
Imported fill materials should be free of organics and deleterious materials and 
should be suitable for the intended use. In addition, imported fill materials should 
be observed and/or tested by Geolabs prior to being transported to the site for 
the intended use. 
3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials required for the project should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or 
ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.3.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose dense basalt 
formations. In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site may be 
designed with a slope inclination of 1H:1V or flatter. In areas where loose clinker 
materials and/or highly fractured basalt formations are exposed at the cut slope 
faces, the cut slopes may need to be flattened to inclinations of 2H:1V or flatter. 
Therefore, provisions to flatten the cut slopes and/or construct retaining walls 
should be considered in the project design. A Geolabs representative should 
observe and map the cut slopes during construction to evaluate whether the 
exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our assumptions. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed herein may be designed with a slope inclination 
of 2.5H:1V or flatter. In addition, fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should 
be benched.  The fill slope face should be finished to a relatively smooth and 
well-compacted surface. The fill slope face should be free of voids, which would 
allow erosion and migration of fines to occur. 
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting 
back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In addition, 
slope planting or other means of slope protection should be provided as soon as 
possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes. 
For cut and fill slopes steeper than 3H:1V with vertical heights greater than 30 feet, 
we recommend providing a minimum 8-foot wide bench at the mid-point of the 
slope or at every 30-foot height interval to reduce the potential for significant 
erosion due to surface water runoff.

3.4 
Underground Utility Lines 
We envision new underground utility lines will be required for the project 
development. We anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in 
the basalt formation and clinker materials encountered in our borings. 
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches 
above the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement 
of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular fill 
materials less than 3 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose 
lifts, and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.

3.5 
Permeability Testing 
Four constant head permeability tests were conducted in selected borings drilled 
to provide data for design of the retention basin planned at the project site. The 
permeability tests were performed at depths of about 16.5 to 31.5 feet below the ground 
surface. 
Based on our field permeability tests, the subsurface materials generally have 
coefficient of permeability (k) of 2.3 x 10-1 to over 1 foot per minute at the locations 
tested. Based on the field permeability test results, we believe an average hydraulic 
conductivity of about 0.5 feet per minute may be assumed in the subsurface materials 
encountered at depths of about 16.5 to 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
hydraulic conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 
5,400 gallons per day per square foot of surface area at those locations. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is 
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava or the 
presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal capacity of the 
retention basin.  In general, the greater the number of clinker seams and/or voids 
encountered at the retention basin, the higher the overall permeability and disposal 
capacity.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 41.5 and 
41.8 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made 
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. 
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings 
indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow 
counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow 
counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2.  
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted four permeability tests in 
selected borings using the constant head test method. In general, the borings had a 
diameter of about 4 inches and extended to depths of about 16.5 to 31.5 feet below the 
ground level at the time of the test.  
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. 
The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which 
to base calculation of the subsoil permeability. Results of the permeability tests conducted 
in selected borings are presented on Plates C-1 through C-4.
