Project Considerations 
The project involves developing a stream stabilization and erosion protection 
plan for Kawa Stream and Kawa Ditch in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The 
project limits are from Kaneohe Bay Drive to the vicinity of Mokulele Drive. 
Kawa Stream within the project site is approximately 4,000 feet long and 
traverses an area of approximately 6 acres. Kawa Ditch within the project site is 
approximately 1,000 feet long and traverses an area of approximately 1.3 acres.  Based 
on the existing surface  conditions along Kawa Stream and Kawa Ditch, we evaluated 
the use of concrete lining or other alternative stream stabilization and erosion control 
measures.   
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to

late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. The project site is at the eastern flank of the 
Koolau Mountain Range. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of the valley, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited 
as alluvium. 
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations.  Kawa Stream is a 
perennial stream and flashy.  Due to the flashy nature of the stream, transport of 
large-sized sediments is intermittent.  In addition, the bulk of the stream’s hydraulic load 
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. 
When the erosional base levels change, these sediment loads are left as 
deposits.  When these deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical 
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or 
weathering of the material. 
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, the newer sediments are 
generally transient in terms of geologic time.  In addition, their consistency and density 
are generally less than those of the older, partially consolidated  deposits.

Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on our site reconnaissance, significant erosion of the stream bed due to 
stream water flow were observed in the outer bend of the meandering portions of the 
stream.  Some minor erosion was observed at the toe along several areas of the stream.    
In addition, erosion of the stream banks appears to be from surface water runoff 
originating from the private properties located above the stream banks.  The erosion 
observed along the stream bank adjacent to James B. Castle High School appears to be 
due to surface water runoff originating from the school campus.  Due to surface water 
erosion, the top of the stream banks appear to be encroaching towards the private 
properties. 
We observed undermining at the downstream ends of the drop structures located at 
both Kawa Stream and Kawa Ditch.  We also observed significant erosion at the toe of 
several concrete linings resulting in undermining of the concrete linings.  These concrete 
linings that have been undermined could potentially slide down the stream bank since 
there is no support at the toe of the linings.  
 We believe that the stream banks that have eroded significantly from stream water 
flow should be protected with a lining (concrete or grouted rubble paving) to reduce the 
potential for future erosion.  Lining should be placed on stream banks having a slope 
inclination no steeper than one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V).  Where stream banks 
have a slope inclination steeper than 1H:1V, a retaining wall should be used in lieu of 
lining.  Concrete rubble masonry (CRM), gabion, and cast-in-place reinforced concrete 
retaining walls are being considered.  Since CRM and gabion retaining walls are 
considered gravity type walls, these types of wall should be located in areas with sufficient 
room so that construction of these retaining walls do not result in narrowing the stream 
width.  Detailed discussions and recommendations for preliminary evaluation of the 
project are presented in the following sections herein. 
Stream Bank Lining 
Based on our observations, we recommend placing a protective lining along the 
stream banks that have eroded significantly from stream water flow.  The protective lining 
may consist of concrete or grouted rubble paving (GRP) to reduce the potential for future 
erosion of the stream banks.  The protective lining should be placed on stream banks 
having a slope inclination no steeper than 1H:1V.

The stream bank lining should consist of a drainage system behind the lining to 
reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures.  In addition, we recommend providing a 
cutoff wall at the toe of the stream bank lining to reduce the potential for undermining of 
the stream bank lining.  The cutoff wall should extend a minimum of 48 inches below the 
bottom of the stream bed.  If medium hard to hard basalt rock is encountered within the 
cutoff wall excavation prior to the minimum 48-inch embedment depth, then the cutoff wall 
may extend a minimum of 12 inches into the medium hard to hard basalt rock. 
Retaining Walls 
Where stream banks have a slope inclination steeper than 1H:1V, we 
recommend using retaining walls to reduce the potential for future erosion from stream 
water flow.  The retaining walls should bear on basalt rock to reduce the potential for 
foundation scour.  In addition, retaining walls should consist of a drainage system 
behind the lining to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures.  We understand that 
CRM, gabion, and cast-in-place reinforced concrete retaining walls are being 
considered. 
CRM walls are considered gravity type walls and typically, the base of a 
CRM wall is about 60 percent of the height of the wall.  CRM walls will need to be 
relatively large to provide stability from sliding and overturning; therefore, sufficient room 
is needed so construction of the wall does not result in a narrower stream width. We 
believe that CRM walls may be used to reduce the potential for erosion of the stream 
banks, provided that construction of the CRM walls does not result in a narrower stream 
width.  CRM wall foundations should be embedded a minimum of about 12 inches into 
basalt rock.  In addition, we recommend placing a cutoff wall at the toe of the CRM wall 
that extends a minimum of about 12 inches below the bottom of the foundations. 
Gabions consist of rectangular wire mesh baskets filled with stones to form 
flexible, permeable, monolithic structures.  The gabions can be tied together vertically 
and horizontally to form a retaining wall.  Gabion retaining walls are considered gravity 
type walls. Typically, the base of a gabion wall is about 70 percent of the height of the 
wall.  Similar to CRM walls, gabion walls will need to be relatively large to provide 
stability from sliding and overturning. There should be sufficient room so the gabion 
walls do not protrude into the current path of the stream flow, which could disrupt the 
stream flow.  Gabion wall foundations should be embedded a minimum of about 
24 inches into basalt rock.  
The stability of gabions depends on the integrity of the wire mesh. The wire mesh 
may be damaged due to abrasion and corrosion.  The wire mesh may be abraded in 
streams with cobbles and boulders moving along the bed of the stream.  Over time, the 
wire mesh will eventually fail. The gabion will no longer behave as a single unit but 
rather as individual stones and will be washed away from the stream flow.   Based on 
our site reconnaissance, scattered boulders, cobbles, and large concrete fragments 
were observed along the stream bed.  Where the stream flow and velocity are relatively 
low, the cobbles, boulders, and concrete fragments within the stream bed are generally

cutoff wall at the toe of the reinforced concrete wall that extends a minimum of about 
12 inches below the bottom of the foundations.

Existing Stream Bank Lining 
Based on our observations, the existing CRM linings encountered along 
Kawa Stream and Ditch appears to be in relatively good condition.  We observed some 
undermining in localized areas. The undermined areas should be reconstructed with CRM. 
In addition, some existing concrete linings encountered along Kawa stream appear 
to be in relatively good condition.  Some minor undermining was observed along the 
existing concrete linings.  If the existing concrete linings are left in place, we recommend 
placing boulders and cobbles to fill the undermined areas.  The remaining void spaces 
within the boulders and cobbles should be filled by using high slump concrete.  After filling 
the undermined areas, we recommend providing a cutoff wall at the toe of the existing 
concrete lining to reduce the potential for future undermining.   
Erosion Control 
As previously mentioned, there are some areas along Kawa Stream and 
Kawa Ditch with minor to moderate soil erosion due to stream water flow and surface 
water runoff originating from the private properties located above the stream banks.  The 
erosion due to stream water flow has resulted in a near-vertical slope about 2 to 3 feet 
high.  The slope inclination above the near-vertical slope ranges from 1H:1V to 2H:1V. 
To reduce further erosion of the stream bank toe, interlocking concrete armor units, 
such as A-Jacks, may be used.  The interlocking concrete armor units reduce potential 
erosion by dissipating the energy of the stream water flow.  The slope above the 
near-vertical slope may be protected from further erosion by placing a turf reinforcement 
mat on the exposed slope face and landscaped.
