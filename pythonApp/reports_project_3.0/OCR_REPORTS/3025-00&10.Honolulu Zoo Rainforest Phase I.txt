performed for the Honolulu Zoo Rainforest Phase project located in the district of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda

be appropriate to form a basis in the preparation of construction cost estimates nor for
construction bidding purposes

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 7
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Koolau Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 5 million
years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has subsided as
much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and
Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O oe Page 8
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period
In an unpublished masters thesis Ferrall mapped discrete coral ledges in the
Waikiki area at elevations of feet MSL and at greater depths These ledges
are found in most coastal areas and are generally used as the end bearing layers for
driven pile foundations except in areas where alluvial action has removed the ledge by
cutting a channel through the reef formation
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the valleys in the project area were drowned in the last 10 000 years or so sea
level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 9
thick deposits of soft loose harbor sediments along the coast in the areas which were
formerly valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms
of supporting heavy structural loads

Groundwater Conditions
The project area is generally located at elevations between about 4 to 6 feet
MSL and is underlain by shallow groundwater as a basal water table at or near sea level
This groundwater is usually brackish or saline Any excavations extending to near or
below sea level will most likely encounter groundwater
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of approximately 214
to 6 feet below the existing ground surface These depths generally correspond to
elevations of approximately 2 to 2 feet MSL The groundwater levels were observed
during our exploration on an intermittent basis and the groundwater levels measured are
shown on the boring logs
Due to the proximity of the shoreline and the Pacific Ocean it is likely that the
groundwater levels measured in the borings during our field exploration may be affected
by the tidal fluctuations of the sea levels In addition the groundwater levels at the project
site may be influenced by seasonal precipitation and storm surge conditions

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our exploration encountered 2 to 5 feet of near surface fill crust
overlying highly compressible lagoonal deposits which consist of very loose to loose silty
sands gravelly sands and sandy silts Below the compressible soils a coral formation
was encountered at depths of about 22 to 47 feet below the surface At the location of
Boring No 11 the coral formation was encountered at a depth of approximately 17 feet
below the existing ground surface Based on data obtained from Boring No 15 the coral
formation extends to a depth of about 47 feet From 47 to 58 feet medium dense sandy
coral gravel was encountered Below 58 feet soft to medium hard coral formation
overlying hard basalt formation was encountered
Because the proposed project consist mainly of lightly loaded structures with
column loads of less than 10 kips per column these lightly loaded structures may be
supported on a shallow foundation system bearing on the near surface fill crust provided
that these structures are constructed within one foot 1 of the existing ground surface
We believe that the recompacted near surface fills could provide adequate support for the
light structural loads of generally less than 10 kips per column However if these
structures are planned to be embedded into the ground by more than one foot 1 the
foundation level will be closer to the compressible soils Therefore we recommend that
the embedded structures be supported on a mat slab foundation with reduced bearing
pressures
Due to the presence of the compressible soils and high groundwater levels
appreciable total and differential settlements would occur if conventional shallow
foundations are used to support the heavily loaded structures As a result of the heavy
loads anticipated for the elephant holding building we recommend that special foundation
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 12
design be taken to support the heavily loaded structures without undergoing significant
settlements
We have considered special mitigation measures such as in situ densification of
the very loose to loose soils to improve the settlement characteristics and strength of the
subsoils and surcharge program to induce a significant portion of the anticipated
settlements prior to the construction of the proposed structures
It is Our opinion that in situ densification methods and placement of new fills will
induce settlements of the ground surfaces existing underground utilities that traverse
these locations would be subjected to ground surface settlements on the order of several
inches which could cause significant distress to the existing structures Based on the
final design concepts these underground utilities will be left in place Therefore to
reduce the potential of distress due to ground settlements during the construction period
we recommend that pile foundation be considered for the support of the elephant holding
building However future dewatering of the area should be avoided or closely controlled
to reduce the potential for inducing additional settlements

Pile Foundations
As previously indicated structures with heavy column loads and or structures that
are sensitive to differential settlements such as the elephant holding building may not be
supported on a conventional shallow foundation system unless significant settlements can
be accommodated As a result we recommend that pile foundations be used to support
GEOLABS HAWAII

these structures The piles will derive support from end bearing on the underlying coral
formation encountered at about 25 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface
corresponding to elevations of about 20 to 25 feet MSL
Pile Capacities
Pile foundations should be driven through the surficial fill and develop its
supporting capacity from end bearing on the coral formation encountered at
approximately 25 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface We recommend
that either 12 inch square or 1612 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete
piles be used to support the elephant holding building
An allowable compressive load capacity of 20 and 35 tons may be used for the 12
inch square and 16 inch octagonal piles respectively When piles are driven in
groups they should have a minimum center to center spacing of 3 times the width
of the pile to avoid capacity reduction due to group action
An allowable uplift capacity of 12 kips and 15 kips may be used for the 12 inch
square and 16 inch octagonal pile respectively
Pile Length
For initial design and construction cost estimating purposes a pile length of about
30 feet may be used to support the pads The pile lengths are estimated by
assuming that the piles will be driven from the existing ground surface at about 5
feet MSL

program However due to the limited number of piles to be installed for the new

Therefore we believe that an additional 5 to 10 feet of pile length may be added
to the piles to account for variability in subsoil conditions if a test pile program is

Splice cans should be provided at the site during the pile installation Splice cans

Foundation Settlements
Settlements of pile foundations will result primarily from the elastic compression of
the pile member and subgrade response We estimate that the total settlement of
the pile supported foundation would be less than 14 inch with differential
settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that amount We believe
these settlements would be essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are
applied

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
at least 26 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy level
control so that driving within the loose soft soil may be controlled at a lower
energy to reduce the tensile stress development within the piles which could
damage or cause cracking of the piles
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs
Hawaii prior to construction for evaluation Geolabs Hawaii should review the
submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained
during bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
GEOLABS HAWAIl

when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop in the pile
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This can be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the

For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the pile has
been driven to its full length or the blow counts steadily increase with depth and

effect

Grid Foundation
For structures that are planned within one foot of the existing ground surface with
column loads of less than 10 kips per column a grid foundation system consisting of
inter connected grids of reinforced continuous footings may be used to transmit the
building loads to the supporting subsoils The grid footing should bear on the near
surface fill crust of recompacted fill material Grid footings bearing on the recompacted
near surface fill crust may be designed with allowable net bearing pressures of up to
1 500 pounds per square foot p s f The allowable net bearing pressures are for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient applications such as wind
or seismic loads The weight of foundations may be discounted since allowable net
bearing pressures are provided
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 20
Footing Embedment
Footings should be embedded a minimum depth of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade with a maximum embedment of 18 inches Footings should not
exceed 4 feet wide to limit the depth of influence and to reduce potential
settlements
Where footings will be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plan projected
upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility line trench This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential of excessive foundation
settlement
Where footings will be located adjacent to slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance between the outside
edge at the base of the footing and the edge of the slope is no less than 10 feet
Subgrade preparation for footings should be performed in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report

Mat Slab Foundation
For structures that are planned to be embedded more than one foot 1 below the
existing ground surface we anticipate that the bearing soils for the structures would
consist of the compressible lagoonal deposits encountered in our borings Because of
the low shear strength characteristics and the compressible nature of these materials we
recommend that the proposed below grade structures be supported by a mat slab
foundation system bearing on a two foot 2 layer of gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel
The gravel layer would provide more uniform support for the mat slab and would serve
as a working surface during construction Prior to placement of the gravel layer a
reinforcing fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed at the bottom of
the mat excavation to serve as a separation layer and provide additional strength to the
subgrade The mat slab foundation may be designed with allowable net bearing
pressures of up to 1 000 p s f
The allowable bearing pressures are for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by for transient applications such as wind or seismic loads The foundation settlements
are estimated to be on the order of 11 inch total with differential settlements on the order
of inch

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory tests were performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 90 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample and dry unit weight are shown at the appropriate sample depths on
the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limit tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to evaluate the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Liquid Limit and Plasticity Index of the
material are used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The
results of these tests are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs and
are summarized on Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
An Unconfined Compression test ASTM Test Method D 2166 85 was performed
on a selected cohesive soil sample The compressive strength is presented on Plate B 1
A Soil Compaction test ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the degree of compaction of the existing fill
material The results of the compaction test are shown on Plate B 1

Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 were performed on samples
of granular material to evaluate its grain size distribution Graphic presentations of the
results are shown on Plates B 2 through B 12
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to estimate the angle
of internal friction and cohesion in a soil sample Results of the two 2 direct shear tests
are presented on Plates B 13 and B 14
