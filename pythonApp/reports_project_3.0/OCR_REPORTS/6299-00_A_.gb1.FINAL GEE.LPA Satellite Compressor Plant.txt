performed in support of the P 089 Construct LPA Satellite Compessor Plant project
within Joint Base Pearl Harbor Hickam in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield
reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level
changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch Following
the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials were
deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 100 meters 1 000 feet
below and rising about 30 meters 100 feet above the present sea level Concurrent
with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene era eruption of the
Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium
with thick deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally
known as mudrock layers Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles
of volcanic tuff
During the recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits These deposits were
subsequently covered with man made fills placed during the middle portion of this
century for land reclamation projects during construction of the existing Pearl Harbor
Naval Shipyard The project site is at the edge of the former shoreline of Pearl Harbor
The subsurface conditions of the project site generally consist of fill materials overlying
weathered volcanic tuff materials coralline formation and alluvium deposit at greater
depth

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can be detected only by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than

Liquefaction
Based on the International Building Code the project site may be subjected to
seismic activity and should be evaluated for soil liquefaction potential Soil
liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing
an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength
of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up
could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil
acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if
not confined will result in significant deformations

Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 12 to 15 meters 40 to
50 feet below the ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden
pressure is generally sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major
factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to
relative density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is
directly proportional to the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the boring indicates that very
loose to loose silty gravel was encountered between 11 and 12 5 meters 36 and
41 feet below the existing concrete slab surface The very loose silty gravel is
potentially liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6 with an associated
peak ground acceleration of 0 19g
Based on our analyses it appears that the very loose to loose silty gravel between
11 and 12 5 meters 36 and 41 feet below the existing concrete slab surface have
a factor of safety of 0 8 to 1 0 against liquefaction Since a relatively thick volcanic
tuff layer was encountered near the existing concrete slab we believe that the
volcanic tuff would bridge over the liquefiable soils during a seismic event
resulting in relatively small settlement of the existing concrete slab

concrete slab surface using SCPTu equipment After drilling coring at the boring
location the bored hole was backfilled with bentonite pellets When the bentonite pellets
come in contact with the groundwater the pellets swell soften and conform to the

The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a
9 000 kilogram 10 ton steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of
60 degrees and an area of 10 square centimeters 1 55 square inches into the softened
bentonite backfill The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of
a shear wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are made
when the SCPT has stopped advancing and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface
A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a
switched hammer The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone
was measured at each depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by
the time difference provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval

approximately 30 9 meters 101 3 feet below the existing concrete slab surface is

Underground Utilities
New utility lines and connections will be installed for the project In general we
recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 150 millimeters 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the pipes for uniform support
Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes or about 300 millimeters 12 inches above the groundwater
level to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use the free draining
materials around the pipes to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill around the pipe could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 millimeters 12 inches
above the pipes or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade may consist of the
excavated on site soils provided that they are free of deleterious materials and
over sized materials greater than 150 millimeters 6 inches in maximum particle size
The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in maximum 200 millimeters 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches will be located below areas
subjected to vehicular traffic the upper 0 9 meters 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of approximately
6 1 and 30 8 meters 20 and 101 feet below the existing concrete slab surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary
coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as
shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 75 millimeters 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram 140 pound hammer falling
760 millimeters 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled
borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 millimeter 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 300 millimeters 6 inches of a 450 millimeter 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was performed
using seismic Cone Penetration Testing SCPT equipment Shear wave velocity testing
using seismic cone penetration testing equipment was performed at one selected boring
location designated as B 2 SW as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at approximately 6 foot
intervals extending to depths ranging from about 30 9 meters 101 3 feet below the
existing concrete slab surface In order to conduct the shear wave velocity test in the
boring the test boring generally were advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the
maximum depth of the boring A log of the materials encountered in the boring is
presented in the Logs of Borings in Appendix A After the boring was advanced to the
maximum depth of the borehole the bored hole was backfilled with bentonite pellets
The temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the
bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward When the bentonite
pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the borehole the pellets started to hydrate
slowly As the bentonite pellets hydrate they swell and soften The probe was then
pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a depth of about 30 9 meters
101 3 feet below the existing ground pavement surface using seismic piezocone
penetration testing equipment SCPTU
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a
10 ton steel electronic subtraction cone into the bentonite backfilled borehole The
cone has an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected surface area of 10 square
centimeters 1 55 square inches equipped with an electronic depth sensor attached to
the cone rod The seismic cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the
arrival of a shear wave generated and propagated from the ground surface Seismic
measurements are made when the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the
subsurface A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a
switched hammer The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone
was measured at each discrete depth interval The vector difference of these depths
divided by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths provided
the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
