This report summarizes our geotechnical engineering observations and testing 
services performed during site grading for Lot Nos. 10 through 13 at the U/AA Subdivision 
– Phase 2 of the Kukui’ula Development.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The planned Kukui’ula Resort Community development encompasses about 
1,045 acres in Koloa on the south shore of Kauai and includes hotel, vacation ownership, 
commercial park, golf course and residential uses. The project is a part of the 
improvements for Kukui’ula Resort Community.  
The U/AA Subdivision is located in the east-central portion of the 
Kukui`ula Development in the Koloa area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii.  The project 
involves developing 20 individual home sites and two large multi-family sites (Lots 21 and 
22). A general grading plan is attached for reference on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The general area was formerly used for sugar cane cultivation. During the previous 
mass grading of the development site the U/AA Subdivision site was used to produce 
crushed rock material from the boulders generated by the site grading operations. The 
crusher machinery was removed; however, boulder stockpiles remained from the 
previous construction activity. The boulder stockpiles were found to be thickest in the 
southeastern portion of the site, generally within Lots 12 through 21. Exploratory trenches 
into the existing boulder stockpiles indicated that the material consisted of boulders, 
cobbles, and gravel in a silty clay matrix in a moderately dense state. Therefore, long 
term settlement due to compression of the boulder fill was not anticipated. Since the 
development plan called for significant fills to raise the lot grades above the existing

This report addresses our field observations and compaction testing services for

SITE GRADING  
Earthwork at the subdivision site generally consisted of fills up to about 15 feet high. 
Prior to filling, the existing ground surface was cleared and grubbed of vegetation and 
deleterious material, then proof-rolled with a compactor. Earthworks Pacific performed the 
grading work.   
During the site grading operations, samples of the fill materials utilized as fill were 
obtained and transported to our office for testing. The maximum dry densities and 
optimum moisture contents were established in our laboratory in general accordance with 
ASTM D1557 (Modified Proctor), and the test results are summarized on Plate 4. 
The fill materials were moisture-conditioned to near optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry density as determined in 
accordance with ASTM D1557.  
During the site grading operations, periodic field density tests were performed in 
general accordance with ASTM D6938 (Nuclear Gauge Method). Compaction test results 
are summarized on Plates 5.1 through 5.58. Test locations and elevations were estimated 
by pacing and interpolating from on-site survey stakes. These locations and elevations 
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. Areas 
assessed as unsatisfactory were reworked until the specified minimum compaction was 
obtained and/or approved by our field representative based on visual observation and 
hand probing. 
Our services consisted of full-time and part-time field observation of the contractor’s 
fill placement, subgrade preparation, and compaction procedures and confirmation of 
subgrade soil conditions exposed during the above grading operations.

HOUSE CONSTRUCTION CONSIDERATIONS 
The finished grades adjacent to the house structures should be sloped to shed 
water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also 
advised to install gutter systems around the house structures and to divert the discharge 
away from the foundation and pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering within 
3 feet of the foundations and slabs should be avoided. Gravel strips adjacent to slabs 
and foundations should be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or 
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
house foundations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and 
subsequent loss of strength of the near-surface soils. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the 
concrete to reduce water infiltration. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as 
possible and should be maintained to drain surface run-off away from foundations.  
Homeowners should also be advised not to block or alter surface drainage. 
Professional consultation (including a licensed geotechnical engineer) should be 
obtained in the planning, design and construction of houses and improvements.
