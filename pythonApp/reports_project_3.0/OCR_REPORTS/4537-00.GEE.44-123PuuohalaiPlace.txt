INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the two story wooden house structure located at Place in the District
of Kaneohe on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the remedial work design of the house structure only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The existing house is a two story wooden structure situated on an approximately
20 foot high slope with a two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V slope ratio The front of the
house western end is supported by a short retaining wall approximately 3 feet high while
the remainder of the house is supported by wooden columns on individual spread footings
The spread footings were embedded into the slope and did not show signs of settlement or
movements
Visual observations indicate that the house is tilting away from the street Bowing and
signs of distress were noted in some of the timber supports particularly in the northern end
of the house facing Kaneohe Bay There is a separation of about 2 inches between the
concrete driveway and the sidewalk Large cracks were observed in the ground adjacent to
the short retaining wall It is desired to evaluate possible causes of the distress and remedial
measures to stabilize the house

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located near the base of the northeastern slope of Puu Papoa
which is essentially an erosional remnant of caldera filling lava flows of the Koolau Volcano
Upon cessation of activity from the Koolau Volcano a period of erosion and
subsequent submergence followed during which time most of the eastern flank of the
volcano was removed Later glacio eustatic sea level changes during the Pleistocene
resulted in the accumulation of several hundred feet of marine and non marine sediments

from the intersection of Kaneohe Bay Drive and Puuohalai Place

Large cracks were observed in the ground adjacent to the short retaining wall Also

The following summarizes our pertinent observations at the site

concrete driveway and the sidewalk It appears that the driveway has moved
away from the sidewalk toward the downslope direction
2 The driveway slopes down toward the house from the road level Surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The site observations indicate that the house is being pushed in the downslope
direction It appears that the portion of the house at the street level is moving relative to
the post footings on the slope causing distress to the structural members The boring data
indicate that the subsoils are generally competent and do not exhibit overt signs of
instability Based on the above observations it is our opinion that the cause of the
movements are related to the low retaining wall which supports the front portion of the
house It was noted that the soils near the base of the wall were very moist and the wall
did not seem to have a definite subdrainage system to drain subsurface water build up
Since the wall is located at a lower elevation than the road and sidewalk subsurface water
could accumulate behind the wall causing additional build up of lateral pressure Since
retaining walls are normally designed to resist only the soil loads the wall would not be
able to resist the additional water pressure resulting in lateral movements
We recommend that remedial measures be directed toward elimination or reduction
of surface water into the subsoils interception of subsurface seepage water behind the
wall and increasing the lateral resistance of the retaining wall
Surface Water Interception
The large separation between the driveway and sidewalk should be sealed with
grout or joint filler to prevent water infiltration into the subsoils
The trench drain in front of the house should be checked to verify that there are no
cracks or open joints that could allow water infiltration behind the wall Additionally the
drain outlets from the trench drain should be checked to ensure that water is drained away
from the house foundations
Subsurface Water Interception
Weepholes were not visible in the retaining wall face Also subdrain pipes were not
observed at the ends of the wall Therefore the retaining wall may have been built without
a subdrainage system In order to prevent the build up of water pressure behind the wall
GEOLABS INC
W O 4537 00 Page 7
we recommend that a subdrain system be installed Ideally a perforated subdrain pipe
should be installed along the back of the retaining wall and daylighted to drain away from
the house foundations However this would require demolition of the concrete pavement
and trench drain behind the wall An alternative would be to drill 3 inch diameter holes
though the wall at a spacing of about 8 feet on centers to act as weepholes PVC pipes
should be inserted through the drilled holes to allow seepage water to flow out The interior
end of the PVC should be covered with a geotextile material to prevent soil from washing
out Splash blocks or other coverings may be required over the ground immediately below
the pipe outlets to prevent erosion if significant amounts of seepage water is encountered
Additional Retaining Wall Support
We recommend that mechanical supports be installed under the retaining wall to
provide additional vertical and lateral support A system such as the Atlas helical tieback
and resistance pier would increase the ability of the existing wall to resist lateral
movements and settlements We estimate that four Atlas piers may be required at this
time
Installation of the Atlas piers will likely require over excavation of about 2 feet laterally
beyond the Atlas pier location to allow installation of the bracket and pipe pile jacking
assembly The excavation will also extend laterally about 1 foot beneath the footing The
footings appear to be large enough that enlargement of the footing for proper support and
resistance during installation will not be needed The Atlas piers should be jacked downward
until a bearing capacity of 20 000 pounds based on a 3 5 inch outside diameter steel pipe
is obtained and to a minimum depth of 15 feet below the existing foundations
Helical tiebacks should extend a minimum of 10 feet behind the wall and should
provide at least 10 kips of lateral resistance per location

report

borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 20 5 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a hand carried portable drill rig equipped with auger equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System Descriptions of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs
of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
