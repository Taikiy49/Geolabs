Geolabs, Inc. is pleased to submit our letter report entitled "Geotechnical

This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations 
derived from our field exploration, field infiltration, and field permeability testing only.

infiltration and permeability characteristics of the existing detention basin at the project 
site. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at the northern end of the existing Maui Veterans 
Cemetery in the Makawao area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  Based on the information 
provided, we understand that standing water is present after heavy rainfall within the 
completed detention basin for the Phase 1 expansion and improvements work for the 
cemetery. The elimination of the standing water in the basin is desired.  
Based on the provided grading plan, the existing detention basin measures 
approximately 235 feet in length by 120 feet wide in plan dimension. The rim of the 
detention basin sits at an elevation of approximately +1,514 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL), sloping down at an inclination of about four horizontal to one vertical (4H:1V) to 
the bottom of the basin that sits at an elevation of about +1,509 feet MSL.

The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and 
subsurface conditions to develop an idealized soil and/or rock data set to formulate

Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.

The bulk of the Haleakala shield was built during the late Pliocene and early 
Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic 
Series.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, the characteristics of the lavas changed to very 
hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition.  These lavas have been grouped as 
the Kula Volcanic Series.  Typically, the basalt consists of thinly to thickly bedded a’a 
and pahoehoe type flows. 
These lava flows have undergone in-situ weathering over time.  This in-situ 
chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade to 
saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The project site is located on the 
western slopes of the Haleakala shield. 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling four borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. L-1, L-2, P-1 and P-2, extending to depths ranging from 
about 5 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface.  We also conducted field infiltration 
tests in Boring Nos. L-1 and L-2 and two field percolation tests each in Boring Nos. P-1 
and P-2 for a total of two field infiltration tests and four field percolation tests.  The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the existing detention basin is underlain by residual soil to a depth of 
approximately 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface followed by saprolite 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 15 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The residual soil generally consists of soft to very stiff sandy silt with varying 
amounts of decomposed gravel. The saprolite generally consists of stiff sandy silt with 
traces of gravel. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4, of Appendix A.  Laboratory test results are presented in 
Appendix B, Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Field Infiltration Testing 
The infiltration tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures 
in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with 
other states’ procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed in Boring Nos. L-1 and L-2 by augering 
boreholes to the selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.

Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom

casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a one-hour trial period. 
The infiltration rate is calculated based on the result of the fourth and the last trial for 
each test location. The results are summarized in Table 1 below. Detailed information of

Based on the infiltration testing, the infiltration rates ranged from about 5.1 to 
7.3 inches per hour with an average rate of about 6.2 inches per hour. This corresponds 
with a drainage rate of approximately 93 gallons per square foot per day. It should be 
noted that the infiltration tests were conducted through a 4-inch diameter borehole, 
which may not represent the actual infiltration conditions within the finished detention 
basin.

15 feet below the ground surface, in support of the evaluation of the detention basin.

Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our field borings and in-situ testing. Variations of subsoil 
conditions between and beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent 
of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations 
then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided 
herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the grading plan transmitted by 
Warren S. Unemori Engineering, Inc. on February 1, 2018. The locations of the field 
borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
