SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling 7 borings within the planned 
mass grading development. Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered 
surficial fill overlying clayey and silty alluvial soils. It should be noted that a layer of 
cobbles/boulders underneath the surficial fills and/or alluvial soils was encountered at varied 
depths ranging from about 1 to 7.5 feet below ground surface. The thickness of the 
cobble/boulder layer varied from about 3 to 9 feet. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, 
time of the year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.  
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey/silty soils exhibit moderate 
to high shrink-swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture 
contents. Depending on the type of commercial structures planned for the Ho’opili Gateway 
development, we believe that structural distresses could occur to the new commercial 
buildings and pavements due to the moderate to high shrink-swell potential of the on-site 
soils unless special subgrade preparations are implemented during mass grading 
construction. Therefore, special attention should be given to the site grading preparation 
currently planned for the mass grading operation and future foundation design to reduce the 
risk of structural distresses due to the moderately to highly expansive soil conditions. 
We recommend implementing the following site grading preparation procedures to 
create a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits at the project site. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near-surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These soils 
should be disposed of properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, if 
appropriate. 
2. 
Over-excavate and remove additional 12 inches of soil and moisture-condition 
to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture content for use as 
general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials. 
3. 
Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as general fill, the bottom of 
the over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction.

The intent of the above site preparation is to create a 2-foot thick reworked soil 
layer within the entire grading limits. It would reduce the future soil swelling/heaving 
potential by preparing the clayey soils with elevated moisture contents and reduced 
compaction requirements. In addition, it decreases the affinity of the clayey soil for water 
and creates a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil. It 
should be noted that the 2-foot thick reworked soil layer should also be constructed below 
the finished subgrade and pad grade in cut areas.  
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. 
General fills may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum particle size of 6 inches and 
should be free of deleterious materials. In general, the excavated on-site materials 
(not including the 12 inches of soil materials removed after clearing and grubbing) may be 
reused as a source of general fill material provided they are free of vegetation, debris, 
deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials. 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive select granular fill 
materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished subgrades subjected to 
vehicular traffic). 
The on-site clayey soils should be protected to retain the moisture by keeping it moist 
or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks 
develop, the soils should be re-scarified, moisture-conditioned, and recompacted again. 
Field infiltration tests were performed at a depth of about 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Based on the results of the field infiltration testing, the infiltration rates at 
these locations are highly variable, ranging between zero (no infiltration) and about 
17 inches per hour. Due to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the absorption 
capacity of the selected stormwater disposal system should be confirmed by conducting 
additional infiltration tests during construction.

recommendations for the project design.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, 
lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a 
broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the 
southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project site vicinity and 
to the south, these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions within the project site by drilling and 
sampling seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 107, extending to depths

subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed.

3.1.2 Site Perimeter Cut-Off Trench 
Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa/Kapolei 
area during prolonged dry weather conditions. Since shrinkage cracks tend to be a 
conduit for water infiltration into the subsurface soils, we recommend implementing 
special site preparation procedures consisting of installing a cut-off trench, especially 
along the southern and western perimeter of the grading limits at the project site. We 
recommend a cut-off trench at least 3 feet deep and 5 feet wide consisting of the 
on-site clayey soils placed and compacted with elevated moisture contents, as 
illustrated on Plates 5.1 and 5.2. As an alternative, the cut-off trench may be 
backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM). 
The on-site soils used for this cut-off trench should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The intent of this cut-off trench is to create a barrier to reduce water 
infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil from the shrinkage cracks on the 
adjacent properties.  
3.1.3 Fills and Backfills

1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.

and should be free of deleterious material. In general, the excavated on-site soils (not

3.1.7 Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
As indicated previously, shrinkage cracks are anticipated at the ground surface of the 
project site. In general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of 
maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the sun 
and dry weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground is 
expected to be intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Kapolei/Ewa 
area resulting in large fluctuations in the soil moisture content.  
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to reduce 
the potential for significant soil erosion soon after completion of the site grading. The 
relatively dry climatic conditions of the Kapolei/Ewa area likely will require controlled 
irrigation of the graded surfaces to maintain the moisture contents and to keep 
graded surfaces free of large shrinkage cracks. However, over-watering of the 
graded surfaces should be avoided to reduce the potential for saturation and 
softening of the surface soils. 
3.2 
Corrosion Potential 
One corrosivity test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57, 
Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. Results of the corrosivity test 
are presented in Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure.

Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have a resistivity value on the order of about 1,200 ohm-cm and a pH value 
of 7.66, corresponding to a corrosion rating of 2 (Very Corrosive) based on the guidelines 
provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply. 
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity test, we believe that a corrosion 
rating of 2 (Very Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of 
metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes and other metallic piping, should consider 
the effects of the corrosive environment of the substructure.  
Levels of the chloride content of the on-site soils encountered near the existing 
ground surface are relatively low, on the order of about 15 parts per million (ppm), while 
the sulfate content is relatively low, on the order of about 55 ppm.  
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, we 
believe that either Type I or Type II cement may be used for the concrete in contact with 
the ground planned for the project. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional 
corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for 
corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 107, extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Two borings, 
designated as I-1 and I-2, were drilled to about 5 feet below the existing ground surface 
for infiltration testing. In addition, two bulk samples of the near-surface soils were obtained 
to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The 
approximate boring, infiltration and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.  
Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
Five one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and/or 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, two Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil 
samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method A) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-8 and B-9. 
One Corrosivity test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57), 
Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed by our 
office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-10.
