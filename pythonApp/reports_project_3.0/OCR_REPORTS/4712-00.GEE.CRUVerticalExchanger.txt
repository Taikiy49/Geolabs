SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the CRU Vertical Exchanger project within the Tesoro Hawaii Refinery located in the
Campbell Industrial Park area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the
CRU Vertical Exchanger project These recommendations are intended for the design of
drilled shaft foundations and site preparation only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located within the existing Tesoro Hawaii Refinery site in the
Campbell Industrial Park area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii We understand that a new
CRU Vertical Exchanger is planned at the project site The new vertical exchanger will have
a diameter ranging from about 4 7 to 6 feet The vertical exchanger will be approximately
80 feet in height We understand that the vertical exchanger will have an operating weight of
about 275 200 pounds Based on the information provided the bottom of the foundation cap
will be set at 4 feet below the existing pavement elevation
In general we anticipate that construction of the proposed vertical exchanger structure
will involve only minor site preparation consisting of cuts of up to about 3 feet deep and fills on
the order of about 1 to 2 feet

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from the Koolau Volcano
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield
Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary
wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at
Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or
caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has
continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at correspondingly
higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by
fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials

Uplift Load Resistance
Based on our foundation analyses each of the four drilled shafts may be subjected to
uplift loads on the order of about 270 kips Uplift loads may be resisted by a
combination of the dead weight of the drilled shaft and shear along the drilled shaft
surface and the adjacent subsurface materials In general an ultimate uplift load
capacity of up to 550 kips per drilled shaft may be achieved for the drilled shafts
minimum 22 foot deep below the footing The uplift load capacity provided above
includes the weight of the drilled shaft
The uplift load capacity of the drilled shaft provided is intended for transient load
applications Therefore the uplift load capacities should not be further increased for
transient load applications For sustained uplift loads we recommend that the uplift
load capacity provided above be reduced by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads
The capacity of the drilled shaft member in tension should be checked by the project
structural engineer

capacities of drilled shafts depend on the frictional resistance between the shaft and
the surrounding soil Therefore the contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft
holes and in placing concrete into the holes
The subsurface conditions at the project site generally consist of coral formation The
hardness and fracturing in the coral formation are likely to vary significantly by nature
with localized soft and caving in layers and relatively hard zones Therefore some
difficult drilling conditions may be encountered in these subsurface conditions In
addition due to the presence of groundwater table caving in of the materials may
occur during the drilling operation due to seepage of groundwater and the granular
nature of the soils within the coral formation To reduce the potential for significant
caving in of the holes temporary casing of the drilled holes may be necessary
In addition the presence of groundwater in the drilled shaft holes will require that the
concrete for the drilled shafts be placed by tremie methods A low shrink high slump
concrete mix 7 to 9 inch slump in wet holes should be used to provide close contact
between the drilled shafts and surrounding materials The concrete should be placed
promptly after the completion of drilling within 24 hours to reduce the potential for
caving in of the drilled holes The concrete should also be placed in a suitable manner
to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix
As mentioned previously placement of concrete by tremie methods will be required
because groundwater will be encountered in the drilled holes During concrete
placement a minimum of 5 feet of concrete head should be maintained above the
bottom of the tremie pipe at all times Considering the relatively high structural loads
imposed on the drilled shafts and the subsurface conditions at the site a representative
from Geolabs should be present at the site to observe the drilling and installation of
drilled shafts during construction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 20 5 and 51 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools The approximate locations of
the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Core samples of the coralline materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method
that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The coral material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50

Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
