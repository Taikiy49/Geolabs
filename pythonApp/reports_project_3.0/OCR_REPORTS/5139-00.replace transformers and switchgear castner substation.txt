SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Replace Transformers and Switchgear project for Castner Substation
located at Schofield Barracks on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses pertaining to the design of
foundations slabs on grade site preparation and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
Based on the information provided we understand that it is desired to construct
an addition to the existing Castner Substation within Schofield Barracks on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project site is located near the Macomb Gate along Wilikina Drive
In general the project consists of constructing new transformer pads and
electrical structures Several one story storage and shelter buildings also will be
constructed as part of the project In addition an access driveway likely will be required
for the new substation A perimeter wall extending from the project site to the existing
Castner Gate also will be constructed Based on the information provided we envision
that earthwork consisting of minor cuts and fills of less than about 2 feet in height will be
required to achieve the design grades for the project

information obtained was used to develop a soil rock data set for the formulation of

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to
be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield Volcano volcanic
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene
Epoch as volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased The dips of the lava beds
generally range from about 3 to 5 degrees near horizontal The lava flows on the
plateau have weathered to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the
reddish colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa
and Kaukonahua Gulches are the result of this weathering The brown and reddish
brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits and residual soils overlying the
weathered rock

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the borings drilled at the project the site generally is underlain by
alluvium consisting of medium stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts extending to
the maximum depth explored of about 21 5 feet below the ground surface It should be
noted that varying amounts of cobbles and boulders were encountered in the borings at
various depths Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of
our field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are expected
to vary with seasonal rainfall time of the year and other factors
Based on the generally stiff subsurface conditions encountered at the project
site we recommend that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support
the new structures planned at the project site As an alternative foundations for support
of the structural loads of the one story building structures planned at the site may
consist of thickened edge slab footings It should be noted that footings for the
structures should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
exterior grade
The surface soils encountered in the borings generally consist of silty clays
which exhibit a slight expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for structural distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade we
recommend that a minimum of 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials
be provided below the concrete slabs on grade in addition to the 4 inch thick cushion
fill materials normally provided below concrete slabs on grade The non expansive fill
should extend a minimum of 3 feet laterally beyond the edge of the slab Detailed
discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for design are
presented in the following sections of this report

3 4 Pavement Design
We understand that flexible pavements will be used for the access road planned
at the project site In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed
access road for this project would consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light
trucks with some occasional heavy trucks Therefore we have assumed generally light
traffic loading conditions for pavement design purposes
For pavement design we have assumed that the pavement subgrade soils will
be similar to the stiff silty clay soils encountered during our field exploration On this
basis we recommend that the following preliminary pavement design be used for this
project
Flexible Pavement Section
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The subgrade soils under the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted
to at least 95 percent relative compaction CBR tests and or field observations should
be performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above
design section is adequate The aggregate base course should consist of crushed
basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavements Surface water ponding should not
be allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs
extend a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils to reduce the potential for
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section

3 5 Underground Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines and utility line connections may be required for
development of this project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be
excavated in the fills and or on site clayey soils
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided for support below the
pipes Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of the pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes
and improper placement of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site
materials with a maximum particle size of 6 inches The trench backfill material should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Where trenches will be located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It also is
advised that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations and slabs also should be avoided Planters next to foundations within
3 feet should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential
for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 The borings were
advanced to the maximum depths of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
auger tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized
on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture content Results
of the test are presented on Plate B 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the test are presented on Plate B 4
