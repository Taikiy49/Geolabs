the existing Hart Street WWPS located in the Kapalama area of Honolulu on the island of

extends from Salt Lake to Downtown Honolulu The WWPS pumps wastewater
generated in the Hart Street subdistrict to the Sand Island Wastewater Treatment Plant
WWTP through a force main which crosses under Honolulu Harbor
The existing Hart Street WWPS was built in 1950 and has been in continued
service since its construction Due to age probable corrosion out dated equipment and
design we understand that the facility has exhibited building and equipment problems

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes came to an
end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our knowledge and past project experience at the subject site we
anticipate that the site is generally underlain by a surficial fill layer consisting of medium
dense to dense sandy and gravelly soils extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Below the near surface fill layer loose to very loose sandy
gravels and gravelly sands interbedded with occasional layers of very soft clayey silts are
anticipated to depths of about 25 feet below the existing ground surface Alluvial deposits
consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays are anticipated at depths of about 25 feet or
greater below the existing ground surface
A shallow groundwater table is anticipated at a depth of about 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The ground water level corresponds to an elevation of about
1 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Due to the proximity of the site to Honolulu Harbor the
groundwater level will likely be affected by tidal fluctuations and other factors such as
seasonal precipitation and storm surge conditions
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous
footings bearing on the recompacted near surface fill soils may be used to support the
lightly loaded buildings provided that some settlements are tolerable Based on the
subsoil conditions and structural loads anticipated for the proposed project footing
settlements on the order of about 1 to 1 inches may be anticipated for the foundations
of the lightly loaded structures We recommend that the foundations be structurally
connected with tie beams to reduce the effects of potential total and differential
settlements In addition the footings should be placed at a level as high as possible to
take advantage of the fill crust thereby reducing the footing load applied onto the
underlying compressible deposits Efforts should be made to reduce the building loads in
order to use shallow foundations for support otherwise a driven pile foundation should
be considered if the anticipated settlements are of concern

Shallow Foundations
It is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous
footings bearing on the recompacted near surface fill soils anticipated at shallow depths
may be used to support the proposed buildings provided that some ground settlements
are tolerable Based on the subsoil conditions and structural loads anticipated for the
proposed buildings settlements on the order of about 1 to 1 inches may be anticipated
for the footing foundations with differential settlements of approximately one half the
amount Efforts should be made to keep the building loads light in order to utilize shallow
foundations otherwise driven pile foundations should be considered if these anticipated
settlements are of concern
We understand that the proposed electrical building will be located immediately
adjacent to the existing pump station with basement walls Due to the location of the new
electrical building the shallow building foundation will impose a surcharge load on the
adjacent basement walls We recommend that the potential impacts of the additional
surcharge loads on the existing pump station basement walls resulting from the new

Underground Fuel Storage Tank
We anticipate that the top of the proposed underground fuel storage tank structure
may be embedded about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore
construction of the underground fuel storage tank may involve an excavation on the order
of about 10 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface Because construction of the
underground fuel storage tank will replace the soil with lighter material fuel there is a net
decrease in stresses on the underlying soils On this basis we recommend that a
concrete mat be used to provide the necessary bearing support and uplift resistance for
the tank structure A preliminary allowable bearing pressure of up to 1 000 p s f may be
used for the design of the concrete mat bearing on a minimum 2 foot thick gravel layer
wrapped in a filter fabric This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third for transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic
forces
Due to the presence of very soft loose subsoils near the foundation level we
recommend that a minimum 2 foot gravel layer wrapped with a layer of filter fabric such
as Mirafi 140N or equivalent be provided below the concrete mat for more uniform
bearing support and to serve as a working platform during construction The gravel layer
should consist of clean granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation The filter fabric should be lapped a minimum of 24 inches at the ends
of each roll and along the sides
Due to the high groundwater levels anticipated at the project site it is anticipated
that the proposed underground fuel storage tank will be subjected to buoyant uplift forces
Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the dead weight of the tank structure the
concrete mat and the backfill material above the concrete mat Contribution of dead
weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of 105 p c f above the
groundwater table and 41 p c f below the groundwater For evaluating uplift forces due
to hydrostatic pressures a high groundwater level elevation of 3 feet MSL may be used
for design to take into consideration tidal fluctuations
Settlements of the new tank structure may result from the compression of the
soft lioose subgrade soil layer due to the placement of the heavier backfill material and or
disturbance of the soft loose subsoils during construction We estimate the total
settlement of the tank structure to be on the order of 1 inches We believe that most of
the settlements should occur during the construction period

Underground Utilities
We understand that new underground utilities will be installed for the subject
project For the large diameter underground utility lines anticipated for the subject project
such as the 42 inch sewer force main 48 inch overflow pipe and 54 inch primary influent
sewer we believe that driven piles or jet grouted columns as used in the Nimitz Highway
Relief Sewer project should be considered for pipe support to reduce the potential for
differential settlements along the pipeline
For support of other smaller utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes Where soft and or loose soil deposits are encountered near the pipe
invert elevations we recommend that the utility pipe be supported on an additional
18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a geotextile
fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes or
1 foot above the groundwater level should consist of free draining material such as
No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the
backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or the
approved on site soils The soft and or wet excavated soils should not be used as trench
backfill The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction
Due to the upper soft loose subsoil conditions and possible settlements of the
existing utility lines under the impact of dewatering operations we recommend that
flexible pipe joints be used to connect the new and existing utility lines to allow for some
amount of differential movement

Corrosion Potential
Our past project experience in area suggest that the subsoils at the project site
generally exhibit relatively low resistivity values indicating that the subsoils may be highly
corrosive to buried metallic structures Based on our experience in the area the subsoils
have minimum resistivity values in the range of less than 100 ohm cm Therefore we
recommend that substructures especially metallic substructures be properly designed
for protection against potential corrosion
Shoring and Dewatering
In order to install the underground fuel storage tank and other utilities we
anticipate that relatively deep excavations below the existing ground surface will be
required for the project construction In general the method and equipment to be used
for excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations Our previous field exploration at the subject site encountered a
surface layer of man made fill underlain by loose to very loose sandy soils Therefore we
anticipate that conventional excavation techniques may be used for the excavation
Because of the soft subsoil conditions and high groundwater levels anticipated at
the project site it is our opinion that continuous shoring support such as interlocking steel
sheetpiles will be needed for the project construction The selected excavation shoring
system should comply with all applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety
of the shoring installation should be the sole responsibility of the contractor
Dewatering will likely be necessary due to the shallow groundwater table
anticipated at the project site It should be noted that water from the dewatering
operations would likely be discharged to a City and County storm drain system A
National Pollution Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit will be required for this
discharge
Because of the granular nature of the subsoil conditions it is anticipated that the
granular soils encountered are relatively permeable Therefore dewatering of the
excavation may involve large volumes of water It is our opinion that a cut off wall
system such as interlocking sheetpiles should be considered to aid in dewatering the
excavation Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the
excavations is generally not recommended The resultant areal depression of the natural
groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible subsoils resulting in
potential ground settlements which would affect the existing structures The potential
impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table
must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering

We understand that asphaltic concrete pavement is planned for the new driveway
at the east side of the pump station We anticipate the vehicle loading for this project will
consist primarily of light trucks and maintenance vehicles We have made our preliminary
pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will consist of the surficial sandy

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations
