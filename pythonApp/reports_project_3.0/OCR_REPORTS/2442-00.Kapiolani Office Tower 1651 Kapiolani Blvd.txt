Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located along the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorpholo
gy and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plane The subsurface
conditions below the site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits
and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2442 00 Page 7
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted in
deposits of coral algal limestone and maris within the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary
unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2442 00 Page 8
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the deep valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so
sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in
thick deposits of soft estuarine sediments along the coast in the areas which were
formerly valleys and drainageways
Available evidence indicates that there are several of these buried drainage
channels traversing the Ala Moana and Waikiki area Based on mapping by C Ferrall in
a Master s thesis the project site is situated near a buried channel known as the Kaheka
Channel
Subsoil Conditions
In general the field exploration encountered a surface fill crust about 4 to 6 feet
thick consisting of medium stiff brown to reddish brown clayey silt overlying lagoonal and
alluvial deposits varying from loose to very loose silty sand with coral gravel to medium
dense weakly cemented coral detritus to medium stiff clayey silt
It appears that the site is underlain by a relatively competent coral ledge at depths
of approximately 25 to 45 feet below the existing ground surface However the coral
ledge becomes erratic and grades to weakly cemented and loose coral sand and gravel
detritus in the western portion of the site These detritus materials are not sufficiently
competent to provide end bearing support for high capacity piles The complex and
variable subsurface geological conditions may be due to the close proximity of the site
to the previous buried Kaheka alluvial channel C Ferrall

Groundwater Level
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths ranging from 4 8 to
7 9 feet below the existing ground surface during our field exploration Based on the
topographic map provided to us it appears that the groundwater level fluctuates between

tides and the groundwater levels during construction may be influenced by other factors
such as tidal storm surges higher seasonal tides and pumping stresses

needed to support the anticipated heavy structural loads of the proposed 20 story office
tower building and the 5 level parking structure

The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be modified when the
driving process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose sandy soil the
hammer stroke should be carefully controlled reduced energy so that
excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
For short piles of about 30 feet in length and end bearing on the upper
coral ledge the pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts
steadily increase with depth and reach 10 blows per inch for the last three
inches or when aggregated blow counts exceed 100 blows for the last foot
For long piles of about 130 to 150 feet in length and end bearing on the
underlying cemented coral sand and silt layer the pile driving may be
terminated when blow counts steadily increase with depth and reach 8
blows per inch for the last three inches or when aggregated blow counts
exceed 70 blows for the last foot

xcavation Support
Based on the soft subsoil and high groundwater conditions it appears that
adequately designed and constructed continuous interlocking steel sheetpile walls
could be utilized to support the deep excavation
It is critical that the sheeting be driven with a suitable sheetpile driving hammer
prior to excavation It is our opinion that cantilever free standing sheetpiles
without a lateral support system may not be adequate to support the excavation
side walls The sheeting should be maintained tight against the excavated earth
faces to reduce the potential of ground loss or movements which may jeopardize
existing adjacent structures
Lateral support by horizontal struts or bracing appears neither sufficient nor
practical due to the anticipated width of the excavation Therefore other forms of
lateral support such as rakers should be considered to brace the sheetpile walls
The braced steel sheeting should also be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the
possibility of bottom heaving or quick conditions which may occur during
dewatering of the excavation due to upward seepage pressure
Based on our preliminary estimate the braced sheeting should be driven to a
minimum embedment of 10 feet below the excavation bottom to reduce the
potential of subgrade heaving or quick conditions
The actual required depth of sheetpile penetration will also be affected by the
method of dewatering used The above recommended sheetpile penetration depth

is based on the assumption that there is no groundwater drawdown outside of the
sheeting
If the dewatering system s used create groundwater drawdown outside the

Construction Precaution and Monitoring
The sheeting and the bracing support system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the sheeting and lateral support
system should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor His representa
tive who is continuously present on site will have the best opportunity to observe
any changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsoils
unexpected high groundwater level unsuitable construction sequence or
techniques etc which might affect the excavation support stability
Even with good construction procedures some movement of the sheetpile walls
may still occur because of changes in earth stresses during excavation This
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2442 00 Page 21
depends greatly on workmanship for example how tightly the sheetpiles are to the
exterior soils and how quickly the lateral braces are installed It also depends on
the types of subsoils the depth and width of excavation and the rate of excavation
In view of the complexity of the problem it is difficult to accurately predict
movement of a retaining wall system and its adjacent ground Therefore it is
important to realize that the excavation lateral support should be installed as early
as practicable and that vertical and horizontal ground movements outside of the
excavation should be continuously monitored and controlled with installed
settlement markers piezometers and inclinometers etc
Generally a damage survey of all the existing buildings and structures adjacent to
the project site should be conducted including photographs and crack surveys
prior to the commencement of construction
During construction the ground adjacent to the excavation should be continuously
monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of movement such as deflection
of the ground surface adjacent to the excavation Such monitoring is particularly
critical where existing adjacent structures and utilities are not supported by pile
foundations

We suggest that the following three 3 basic selection criteria be considered in
determining a suitable method of dewatering
a The method should impose the least disturbance water level drawdown or
damage to existing adjacent developments outside the braced excavation
b The method should maintain adequate excavation stability and provide safe
working conditions
Cc The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modification to accommo
date various ground conditions
It appears that sump pumping inside the adequately braced excavation may be
used if the sheeting is driven sufficiently deep to prevent piping and heaving at the
bottom of the excavation
Dewatering by means of a deep well or well point system along the outside of the
excavation should be avoided since the resulting areal depression of the
groundwater table could induce consolidation of subsoils and or settlements below
the existing adjacent structures In addition the amount of water which would
have to be pumped out may be excessive if a deep well penetrates through the
upper coral ledge layer
Recharging injecting water back into the subsoils to balance the natural
groundwater levels and minimize areal drawdown in areas near the existing
adjacent structures should be evaluated to reduce the risk of settlements to these
buildings

Construction Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to operation
The impact and safety of the dewatering system operations should be made the
sole responsibility of the contractor His representative who is continuously
present on site will have the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of
dewatering during construction and to implement immediately precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations as the situation deems necessary
Where encountered at the excavation bottom the relatively permeable granular
subsoils may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the
project dewatering operations should be done without pumping out soil fines
pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with the excavation support
installation so that the excavation is not jeopardized The dewatering operations

Caution should also be taken during dewatering operations not to dewater utility

the trench es and could cause settlements and damages to the utility lines and or
other existing structures

deleterious materials The resulting grub and spoil material should be disposed of

compactive effort required

APPENDIX C
Wave Equation Analysis of Pile Driving
The wave equation analysis of pile driving was performed using Program WEAPG
which is a microcomputer adaption of the program WEAP originally written by Goble and
Associates for the Federal Highway Administration to solve pile drivability problems
The program models hammer pile soil system by representing the pile and driving
system by a series of masses and springs The soil is modeled by a spring and dashpot
attached to each mass The program output provides ultimate resistance and maximum
compressive and tensile stresses in the piles as a function of hammer blow count
The primary objectives of the analysis are
1 to analyze the driving stresses in the pile
2 to determine the suitable driving system to be used for the pile driving and
3 to predict the ultimate pile capacity of the pile
The analysis was performed for driving inch octagonal prestressed concrete
piles with a DELMAG D 30 hammer The relationships of ultimate capacity and hammer
blow counts commonly known as the bearing graph are presented on Plate C 1 together
with input information The maximum compressive stresses are presented on Plate C 2
