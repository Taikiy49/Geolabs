REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two 2
extinct shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the
bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located at the base of the northeastern slope of Puu Papoa
which is essentially an erosional remnant of caldera filling lava flows of the Koolau
Volcano
After the Koolau Volcano ceased its activity it was followed by a period of erosion
and subsequent submergence during which most of the eastern flank of the volcano was
removed Later glacio eustatic sea level changes during the Pleistocene resulted in the
accumulation of several hundred feet of marine and non marine sediments
Data obtained from the exploration for the secondary clarifier tanks of the plant
indicated that the northwestern and central portions of the plant are underlain by
interbedded sediments of both alluvial origins and detrital marine over tuff to basalt
formation at greater depths The current Phase Ill exploration for the HECO electrical
substation located at the northwestern corner of the plant is consistent with the subsoil
conditions previously encountered at the central and northern portions of the site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The project site is underlain by a layer of fill material overlying alluvial and coral
deposits to a depth of approximately 115 feet below the existing ground Saprolite
overlying tuff formation was encountered from about 115 to 133 feet the maximum depth
explored Some of the fill encountered may have been the result of placement of surplus
material generated from the adjacent construction
Based on the moderate to heavy structural loads 80 to 90 kips provided to us
we believe appreciable total and differential settlements would occur for the proposed
transformer and switchgear pads if shallow foundations are used We understand that
very small settlements are required for the transformer and switchgear foundation pads
Therefore deep pile foundations deriving support principally from a combination of the
friction between the pile shaft and the surrounding soil and end bearing on the tuff
formation are recommended to support the pads
For the lightly loaded switch frames it is our opinion that the medium stiff fill may
provide support for the anticipated structural loads of up to three 8 kips per column and
shallow spread footing or drilled pier foundations may be utilized

Transformer and Switchgear Pads
Due to the anticipated moderate to heavy structural loads 80 to 90 kips at the
transformer pad and the relatively light structural loads 10 to 15 kips at the switchgear
pad appreciable differential settlements are anticipated for shallow foundation support
We have also evaluated the option of using a mat foundation to support both pads
However the anticipated total settlement for the mat foundation would be on the order
of about 1 inch with differential settlements not exceeding about that amount
Because of the rigid interconnection between the equipment we understand that
stringent differential settlement between the two pads are required As a result pile
foundations are recommended to support the transformer and switchgear pads located
above the poor subsoil conditions
Pile Foundations
Due to the anticipated significant total and differential settlements of a shallow
foundation system we recommend that pile foundations be used to support the
transformer and switchgear pads The piles will derive support from a combination

Pile Capacities
Pile foundations should be driven through the surficial fill and develop its
supporting capacity from a combination of friction between the pile shaft and the
surrounding soil and end bearing on the tuff formation at approximately 120 feet
below the existing ground surface We understand that the existing structures of
the Phase lil modifications are supported by 15 inch square piles During our
exploration a coral ledge was encountered at about 51 to 59 feet below the
ground surface It is our opinion that hard driving in localized coral ledges may
produce high tensile stresses in the square pile resulting in potential damage to the
piles particularly the corners of the square piles As a result we recommend that
16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concretes piles be used to support the
pads
An allowable compressive load capacity of 50 tons may be used for the 16 4 inch
octagonal piles When piles are driven in groups they should have a minimum
center to center spacing of four feet 4 to avoid capacity reduction due to group
action

Downdrag Loads
Where fill is placed over a compressible soil the latter consolidates and moves
downward relative to the pile The downward movement results in negative skin
friction forces generally known as DOWNDRAG forces on the pile shaft The
magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the compressible
layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the pile surface area
Due to the anticipated fill placement onto the slopes to the north of the project site
consolidation of the subsoils at the northern portion of the substation site would
occur The consolidation will result in downdrag loading of deep foundations
extending through the recent alluvial and coral deposit Therefore the allowable
compressive load capacities of the piles located in and near the fill areas will need
to be reduced by the anticipated downdrag loads The approximate area where
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3072 00 Page 11
pile foundations would be subjected to downdrag forces is shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The downdrag load on the pile shaft is estimated to be about 50 kips for
the 16 inch octagonal pile Therefore we have recommended higher pile
Capacities to account for the combined downdrag and structural loads
To reduce the downdrag forces coating of the portion of the pile shaft within the
compressible layer with bitumen coating may be considered The bitumen coating
should only be applied to the portion of the pile which will be embedded in the
negative skin friction zone or compressible soil layer and above However due
to the anticipated hot temperature and high humidity of this area depending on
the time of year placement of the bitumen coating may be difficult during pile
installation and is not recommended

Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four 4 hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop in the pile
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This can be accomplished by using a driving
resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the
more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the pile has
been driven to its full length or the blow counts steadily increase with depth and
reach four 4 blows per inch for the last six inches 6 of final pile penetration or
when aggregated blow counts are 45 blows for the last foot
Appropriate driving criteria will be developed after selection of the pile driving
equipment

Test Pile Program
As previously indicated a test pile program may not be feasible considering the

thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation rubbish old pavements
construction rubble and other deleterious materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site
Where the proposed fill will encroach into the ditch area stabilization of the
anticipated soft soils in the drainage channel will be required A working platform
or stabilization layer should be installed to provide a firm working surface on which
to place fill The stabilization layer should consist of a minimum 36 inch layer of
gravel No 3B Fine gravel or equivalent with a geotextile fabric such as Mirafi
600X or equivalent placed at the bottom of the gravel layer The gravel layer
should be embedded at least three feet 3 below the lowest adjacent grade to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3072 00 Page 20
provide a key for the proposed fill A typical cross section of the stabilization layer
is shown on Plate 3
Due to the presence of soft subsoils at the toe of the proposed fill slope a slope
inclination of approximately four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V or flatter may be
used for design of the fill slope Installation of utility lines at the new fill
embankment is not recommended due to the long term settlement effects of the
underlying compressible soil which may result in ruptures of the utility lines
Fill Placement
As previously indicated cuts on the order of up to eight 8 to ten feet 10 will be
required to construct the proposed electrical substation Based on our borings
the excavated material would consist generally of clayey soils with sandy gravel
It is our understanding that the excavated material are to be reused as fill materials
for the fill proposed to the north of the substation Since structural improvements
are not planned above the fill slope the on site excavated material may be reused
as fill material with the exception of highly expansive adobe soils if present
lf import fill materials are required import fills should consist of non expansive
select granular material less than three inches 3 in maximum dimension with
less than 25 percent passing the No 200 sieve and with a Plasticity Index less
than 20 The material should be free of organic matter debris and other
deleterious material The material should be tested and approved by the Geolabs
Hawaii prior to delivery to the project site
Where the existing ground surface is steeper than five horizontal to one vertical
5H 1V the new fill should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3072 00 Page 21
remove loose soils on the slopes and to aid in proper bonding of the new fill to the
existing soils
We recommend that a key be established with an embedment depth of three feet
3 below the lowest adjacent grade and a minimum base width of 15 feet as
shown on Plate 3 at the toe of the proposed fill slope A layer of geotextile such
as Mirafi 600X or equivalent should be placed at the base
After placement of the stabilization layer fill placement at the northern slope of the
project site should be done in near level layers starting at the lower end and
working upward A filter fabric such as Mirafi 140 or equivalent should be
provided above the stabilization layer as shown on Plate 3 to reduce potential
migration of fines into the gravel layer
Fill material should be placed in horizontal loose level lifts of up to a maximum of
eight inches 8 thick moisture conditioned to at least two percent 2 above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative
compaction As the fill is brought up it should be continually keyed into stiff
ground by cutting steps in the slopes and compacting the fill into these steps
We recommend that the new fill be placed at a very slow rate overlying the existing
weak crust and high compressible soils The rate of new fill placement should not
exceed two feet 2 per day
Fill Settlement Monitoring
We recommend that a number of settlement gauges be installed on the proposed
fill area to monitor the fill settlements and to observe the rate of future
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3072 00 Page 22
embankment fill settlements due to the consolidation of the underlying soft
compressible soils
Slope ratios of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter may be used for the
design of cut slopes Care should be exercised during construction to avoid over
cutting of cut slopes in order to avoid the placement of sliver fills i e thin sloping
fills on the slope faces in an attempt to repair the over excavated area In the
event over cutting occurs consideration should be given to leaving the slope rather
than attempting to backfill the slope to design grade If backfilling of an over cut
slope is required keying and benching requirements should be implemented
Inclinations of four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V or flatter may be used for the
design of fill slopes Fill slopes should be constructed by over filling past the
design slope and then cutting back
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should
be utilized to reduce the potential of erosion and or instability of the finished
slopes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of two
2 borings with depths ranging between 17 and 133 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary and
core drilling tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D
2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD
50

In general accordance with your request we have observed the pile driving

utilized A single acting diesel hammer with a rated energy of 49 500
foot pound was used to drive the piles at the locations shown on the Pile Location Plan

recommended that piles be cast to 60 and 65 foot lengths
A total of 14 piles including 2 replacement piles were driven to depths ranging from
about 23 feet to 123 feet below the existing site ground surface The locations of the
driven piles are shown on Plate 1 A summary of the pile driving data is attached for your
reference

driving operation Their replacement pile Pile No 12B was driven at about 3 feet to the

horizontal to 3 feet in vertical Based on the pile driving records it is our opinion that Pile
No 12B will still be able to provide the allowable pile capacity of 50 tons Our telephone
conversation with the HECO s structural engineer indicated that no additional replacement
pile will be needed for Pile No 12

Based on our site reconnaissance house foundation excavation observation and
our experience at the vicinity of the project site the following are our recommendations
for the geotechnical engineering design parameters
Foundations
Post and Beam
According to the architectural plans the proposed two story residence addition will
be supported by a foundation system consisting of posts and beams Based on
information obtained during our site reconnaissance an allowable bearing capacity
of up to 2 500 pounds per square feet p s f with a minimum footing embedment
of 36 inches is recommended The allowable soil bearing pressures is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient applications such
as temporary wind and or seismic loads
If footings are located near the top of slopes or on slopes the footing should be
embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance from the outside edge
at the base of the footing to edge of slope is not less than eight feet 8
Footing Subgrade Preparation
The foundation subgrade should be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM
Test Method D 1557 91
GEOLABS HAWAIl
Page 3
W O 3071 00
May 10 1993
Footing excavations should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations the soil should be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks and recompacted prior to concrete placement Also
where utilities cross perimeter footing lines the utility trench backfill should consist
of a vertical barrier of impervious type material or lean concrete
The individual footing bottom preparation should be monitored by representative
of Geolabs Hawaii during the construction to evaluate if adequate bearing would
be obtained prior to placement of reinforcement and concrete
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral load acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
developed against the buried vertical faces of the foundation system and by
frictional resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the soil
subgrade
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth This assumes that the soil around
the foundation is well compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 85 may be used to
calculate frictional resistance between the bottom of the foundations and the
bearing soils Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in
the upper 12 inches of soils should be neglected The passive earth pressure
should be reduced for footings located on slopes

The estimated footing settlements should be on the order of 1 inch total and

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3070 00 Page 5
The project site is underlain by Hamakua Volcanic Series which typically
composed of basaltic lave flows which are mantled by a surface layer of Pahala ash The
basaltic lava flows were laid down in the a a phase characterized by a clinkery surface
and a core of dense rock The Pahala Ash is an air laid deposit of volcanic ash from the
Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa mountain during the Pleistocene Epoch The
Pahala Ash has high in situ moisture contents and low in situ densities and is
characterized as having thixotropic properties i e it is susceptible to significant loss of
strength when remolded under equipment vehicular or seismic loads

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Footing Foundations
It is our opinion that the proposed projects are feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view The clinker basalt formation encountered at relatively shallow
depth below the surface of the project site should provide adequate foundation support
for the proposed buildings Shallow spread or continuous footings and slab on grade
construction may be used to support the proposed buildings bearing on the underlying
clinker basalt formation
An allowable bearing capacity of up to 4 500 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the underlying dense clinker
basalt formation Based on data from Boring and 5 soft and friable clinker
basalt is encountered down to a depth of approximately five feet 5 Therefore it
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3070 00 Page 7
appears that footing foundations may have to be embedded in the order of about five feet
5 below the existing ground surface The allowable bearing pressures are for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third for transient applications such as
temporary wind and or seismic loads
If soft loose pockets are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation they
should be over excavated to competent in situ basalt material and replaced with lean
concrete or structural fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D
1557 91 test procedures
if footings are bear on two different types of materials such as in situ hard basalt
and unconsolidated clinker we recommend that the portion of footing subgrade in loose
clinker be over excavated down to match the adjacent hard basalt The over excavation
should then be backfilled with lean concrete This will reduce the potential for differential
settlement across the footing span due to its bearing on two types of rock with different
bearing capacities
The individual footing excavation and preparation should be observed during
construction by representative of Geolabs Hawaii to evaluate if adequate bearing capacity
would be obtained prior to placement of reinforcement and concrete
It is our opinion that total settlement for footings bearing on the underlying
competent clinker basalt formation should be less than 12 inch with differential settlements
not exceeding about half that amount
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3070 00 Page 8
Slab on Grade
We recommended a minimum four inch 4 thick layer of cushion material such
as base course and select borrow material be installed below the slabs to provide uniform
support for the slabs Due to the anticipated seismic activities poorly graded backfill
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation are not
recommended
The above slab cushion layer can bear on the recompacted in situ clinkery
material It is recommended that after complete removal of the soft volcanic ash soil the
entire subgrade should be scarified a minimum of 10 inches and recompacted to 95
percent relative compaction to obtain a more uniform dense subgrade surface to receive
the overlying four inch 4 thick of cushion material
A moisture barrier membrane covered with a one inch 1 layer of sand is also
recommended between the cushion layer and the slabs
Lateral Resistance
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated with an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 p s f
per foot of depth This assumes that soils around footings are well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on
the in situ clinker basalt formation
Cavity Probing
In order to reduce the possibility of footing settlement resulting from collapse of a
unconsolidated clinkers cavities or voids in the underlying basalt rock formation we
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3070 00 Page 9
recommend that a cavity probing program be implemented The cavity probing program
may also be used to confirm the embedment depth of the footings
We recommend that ail of the footing excavations be probed at approximately 10
feet on centers for continuous footings and with a minimum of one probe drilled at each
column footing location The probes should consist of a 3 inch minimum diameter hole
drilled to a minimum depth of 10 feet below the bottom of footing
If a cavity is located below a footing additional probe holes should be made to

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of 11
borings to depths ranging between 51 and 20 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 The borings were
drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary core
drilling tools as appropriate
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the boring logs
at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based
on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
