2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from
Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as
the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank of the
Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness
of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1
Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern
Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the
present Within portions of the caprock wedge are volcanic deposits from late stage
post erosional vents of Waianae such as Puu Makakilo and Puu Kapolei In the region
of Puu Kapolei some of these deposits occur at very shallow depths
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to continental
glaciation cycles As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the
sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We envision concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
structures The near surface soils encountered at the project site consist of clayey
soils Based on the laboratory tests and our site observations the clayey soils may
exhibit a moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for structural distress to lightly loaded slabs a 24 inch thick
layer of non expansive select granular fill material may be required below the smaller
retail building and main retail building concrete slabs The select granular fill should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Prior to placement of the select granular fill material the slab subgrades should
be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that
a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill would
serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of the slabs
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as trucks and or forklifts we
recommend that a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase be provided below the slabs
in lieu of the 4 inch cushion fill layer The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Building slabs subjected to vehicular traffic such as forklift traffic should be
designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a
structural slab to accommodate some amount of differential settlement For design of
structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per cubic inch pci may
be used for the compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load
transfer across the slab joints subjected to vehicular traffic

3 7 Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not
confined can result in significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally
liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the
ground surface The greater overburden pressure can be sufficient to prevent
liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is mostly underlain by stiff
cohesive soils Therefore we believe there is no potential for liquefaction at this project
site

addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods or longer duration of excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Thirteen one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under a
surcharge pressure of 55 psf The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1
and B 2
Three sets of corrosion tests including Minimum Resistivity
ASTM G 57 Chloride Content and Sulfate Content
were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field
exploration The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 3
Seven Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 4
Three Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on
Plate B 5
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the Direct
Shear tests are presented on Plates B 6 through B 9
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of in situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates B 10 through B 13
Two Modified Proctor Compaction tests ASTM D 1557 A were performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 14 and B 15

APPENDIX C

APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
The permeability of the on site soils was evaluated by performing four falling
head permeability tests at depths of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface
The field permeability tests were performed at Boring 212 and 215 The
locations of the above mentioned borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top
of the hole The time intervals and water drops in the borehole were then recorded to
provide data upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface
soils The test results of the field permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1
through C 4
