Dear Mr. Myers:
In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 6, 2008, we performed a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the Kapolei City Urban Core 8 Roads project in
the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This letter report summarizes the
findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents our
recommendations for pavement design, below-grade structures, and site grading. It should
be noted that the recommendations provided are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this letter report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided, it is desired to construct new roadway
extensions as part of the Urban Core Roads project in the Kapolei area on the Island of
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map, Plate 1. The work under this project involves construction of the proposed street
extension for Road “B” as described below. The approximate limits of the roadway
construction are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Road “B” extends from the intersection with Kapolei Parkway to the intersection
with Kamokila Boulevard. Based on the information provided, Road “B” is a 60-foot
right-of-way roadway measuring approximately 1,000 feet long. Minimal fills and cuts of up
to about 6 feet thick are anticipated to achieve the design finish grades for the roadway. In
addition, new utilities, such as water, drain, and sewer lines, are planned for the project.

program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts:

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 46 through 50, each extending to depths ranging from about
20 to 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, a bulk sample of the
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-16, was collected for laboratory CBR test to
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
In general, our field exploration disclosed that Road “B” alignment is generally
underlain by variable subsurface conditions ranging from very stiff silty clays, medium
dense silty sands, to medium hard to hard coral formation from the existing ground surface
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 26.5 feet below the existing
ground surface.
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration.
However, groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, seasonal precipitation,
surface water runoff, and other factors. Descriptions and graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5 of
Appendix A.
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface ‘conditions encountered in the borings, the principal
geotechnical considerations for the design of this project include proper site preparation
requirements to take into consideration the expansive nature of the near-surface clayey
soils and the relatively dry condition of the soils and the site. Detailed discussion of our
geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of pavements, below-grade
structures, and site preparation are presented in the following sections herein.

Methodology of Pavement Design
The Kapolei City Urban Core 8 Roads (including Road “B”) is classified as high
volume road and the pavement structural section is generally determined based on
the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated
March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation,
Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. The pavement design
methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by
the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans).

Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our field exploration, the proposed new roadway alignment may be
underlain by variable subsoil conditions ranging from very stiff silty clays, medium
dense silty sands, to medium hard to hard coral formation at the pavement
subgrade level. Laboratory testing performed on the clayey soils at the project
vicinity generally indicated a CBR value of about 4 and a swell of around
5 percent. Based on these assumptions and test results, a design R-value of 9
was adopted in our pavement analyses for the clayey soils subgrade condition.
Design Pavement Section
Based on our pavement analyses, we believe that the following new pavement
structural section may be used for preliminary design and cost estimating
purposes:
Road “B” (between Kapolei Parkway and Kamokila Boulevard)
3.5 Inches Asphaltic Concrete
6.0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity)
18.0 Inches Aggregate Subbase (95 Percent Relative Compaction)
27.5 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
As previously mentioned, medium hard to hard coral formation may be exposed at
the pavement subgrade elevation. It is our opinion that the 18-inch aggregate subbase
recommended above may be eliminated under such condition. Therefore, CBR and field
density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section. The recommended section
assume that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas.
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction.
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches below the
asphalt concrete base layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades for the
proposed roadway project, we anticipate that site grading work consisting of minimal fills
and cuts of up to about 6 feet thick will be required for the project construction. In
general, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and grubbed
thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Soft and
yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or
future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or stiff material, and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated
soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site.
Existing structures and pavements to be demolished should be completely
removed. The over-excavations resulting from demolition should be backfilled with
compacted granular fill material. Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed,
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled properly with granular fill material
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level
loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Utilities to
be abandoned in-place should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low
pressure.
Our field observations indicate that shrinkage cracks and ground depressions are
present at the existing ground surface along the roadway extension alignment. In
general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of
the soil moisture content as the surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry
weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently
intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area
resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content.
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface, the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future roadway. The ground cracks could serve
as conduits for water to percolate into the pavement subgrade soils and cause loss of
strength of the subgrade soils resulting in potential settlement of the pavement
structure. Therefore, remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed new
roadway.
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled with a
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting

and collapsing near-surface voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks and
ground depressions. The exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled without
moisture-conditioning of the subgrade soils (at the pre-existing moisture contents).
Loose areas disclosed during the proof-rolling operation should be backfilled with
general fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The
proof-rolling operations should be conducted under the near-continuous observation by
our representative.
Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, the subgrades should then be
scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we
recommend scarifying the soils and preparing again as recommended above.
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation quantities.
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with ASTM D 2041. The aggregate subbase material should
meet the requirements of Section 31 of the City and County Standard Specifications
(September 1986). The material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction.
In general, the excavated on-site soils generated from cut areas may be re-used as
a source of fill materials to raise the existing ground surface to the bottom of the subbase
course layer. Additional imported fill materials required for the project should consist of
3-inch minus materials free of vegetation, adobe clays, and other deleterious materials. In
addition, imported fill materials should also have a laboratory CBR value of 12 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883. It should be noted that the imported fill requirements presented herein are
intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on additional laboratory testing
and field observations on the available fill materials conducted by Geolabs. Fill materials
should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of

We envision that the planned cut slopes will generally expose stiff clayey soils,
medium dense silty sands, or hard coral formation as encountered in the borings. Based
on this assumption, we recommend designing the cut slopes with a slope inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Permanent fill slopes constructed using
the excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials may be designed with a slope
inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against
sliding. Construction of earth berms, interceptor ditches, and the use of geotextile
fabrics over the fill slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for
significant erosion, thus enhancing the long-term stability of the fill slopes. Appropriate
slope planting or other permanent erosion control measures to reduce the potential for
significant erosion of the exposed slopes (including a permanent irrigation system)
should be implemented as soon as possible after the finished slope faces are
completed.

Below-Grade Structures
Based on the information provided, we understand below-grade structures, such
as special drain manholes, will be installed as part of the roadway extension project. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
design of the drainage structures bearing on the stiff/dense in-situ soils. Lateral loads
acting on the drainage structures may be resisted by friction developed between the
bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.3
may be used for footings bearing on the in-situ soils. Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes the soil around footings is well-compacted.
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected.

hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the
walls.
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structure should be considered in the
design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the
structure, a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 33 percent of the
vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire structure height, which is free to
deflect (cantilever), may be used in design. For structures that are restrained, a
rectangular distribution equal to 50 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting
over the entire structure height may be used for design. Additional analyses during
design may be needed to evaluate surcharge effects of point loads and line loads.
Utility Trenches
We anticipate that the utilities will primarily consist of water, drain, and sewer lines.
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the new utilities at the site. The method and equipment to be used for
trench excavation should be determined by the contractor, subject to practical limits and
safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable
federal, state, and local safety requirements.

Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site. The
clayey soils encountered are generally very stiff to hard. However, some of the utility
trench excavations may encounter hard coral formation, which may pose some excavation
difficulties during the trench excavation.
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support under the pipes.
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on-site soils (with a maximum
particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in
maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence.
Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction.

LIMITATIONS
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information
obtained from the field borings and bulk sample. Variations of subsurface conditions
between and beyond the borings and bulk sample may occur, and the nature and extent of
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided
herein.
The boring and bulk sample locations indicated herein are approximate, having
been estimated by taping and pacing from the features shown on the Base Drawing
provided by Engineering Concepts, Inc. on February 11, 2009. Elevations of the field
borings were estimated by interpolation from the contour lines and spot elevations
shown on the same plan. The physical locations and elevations of the borings and bulk
samples should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods
used.
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition.
As previously mentioned, groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the
time of our exploration. However, it must be noted that groundwater level changes may
occur due to seasonal precipitation, surface runoff, and other factors.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 46 through 50, each extending to depths of about 20 to
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight, solid-stem augers. One bulk
sample, designated as Bulk-16, was obtained from the near-surface soils to evaluate
the pavement support characteristics. The approximate boring and bulk sample
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. The materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
3-inch outside diameter (OD) Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
One Atierberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. Test results are presented on Plate B-1.
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C 117 and 136) was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.
Test results are presented on Plate B-2.
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-3.
One Modified Proctor Compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on a bulk
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship. The test results are presented on Plate B-4.
