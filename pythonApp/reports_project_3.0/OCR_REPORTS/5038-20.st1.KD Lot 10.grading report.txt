Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago covering an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai last erupted in Loa and Kilauea both still
active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation observed at the
project site prior to site grading activities appears to consist of both a a and pahoehoe
type lava flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is
typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders A a lava is generally
present at the ground surface of the project site Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a
smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially the pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets are generally
smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers Because the permeability of the clinker and cavities is high they
serve as the primary water transmission features
Subsurface Conditions
As previously mentioned the Kaupulehu project site is located in the Kaupulehu
area in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii In general the original
topography of the project site prior to site grading activities consisted of irregular a a lava
flow terrain at the ground surface
Based on observations during the mass grading operations the project site is
generally underlain by a a basalt at the original ground surface and medium hard to hard
basalt formation at greater depths It should be noted that lava tubes cavities and
blisters are commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows in the project vicinity In
addition lava tube features were observed at the project site during the mass grading
operations It should be noted that near surface lava tubes detected within the limits of
grading for the mass grading operations were generally collapsed and the loose
materials from within the collapsed lava tube were removed The resulting excavation

Discussion and Recommendations
Based on the grading plan the original ground surface elevations ranged from
about 26 to 35 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at Lot No 10 Lot No 10 was generally
graded to a finished pad elevation of about 34 5 feet MSL Mass grading at Lot No 10
generally consisted of excavations of less than about 1 foot deep and new fills of up to
about 8 5 feet high to achieve the design grades
During the mass grading operations extensive subgrade preparation procedures
were employed within the limits of grading at the commencement of the mass grading
operations to reduce the potential for collapse of near surface cavities and or voids within
the lots The intent of the subgrade preparation procedures is to assist in detecting and
collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the porous near surface
materials It should be noted that the subgrade preparation procedures employed for this
project are consistent with the standard of practice for similar type projects and similar
subsurface conditions along the Kohala Coast on the Island of Hawaii
In general the subgrade preparation in fill areas consisted of ripping the original
ground to a depth of about 3 feet or until refusal using a ripper tooth of a large bulldozer to
assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids contained in the
porous near surface materials After the fill surfaces have been ripped the ground was
generally leveled with the use of a Komatsu 375 and or Komatsu 475 bulldozer working
the surface for about four passes to provide a relatively level surface After leveling of the
rugged surfaces with the bulldozer the fill subgrades were generally proof rolled with a
Caterpillar 825 sheepsfoot roller compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to
help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids
Fill materials consisting of processed on site materials including a a basalt and or
dense pahoehoe basalt were placed in the fill areas of the project site Structural fills
consisted of well graded granular materials generally smaller than 6 inches in dimension
In general the fill materials within the top 2 feet of the lots within the pad areas consisted
of structural fill materials It should be noted that general fills consisting of cobbles and
boulders of up to about 2 feet in dimension generally were placed at least about 2 feet
below the finished grades within the pad areas in fill areas and choked off with finer sized
materials In addition boulders of up to about 3 to 5 feet in dimension were generally
placed at least about 4 feet below the finished grades within the pad areas and choked off
with finer sized materials Boulders larger than 18 inches in dimension generally were
placed about 6 feet or more horizontally from the face of the fill slopes
Subgrades in the cut areas were generally proof rolled with a Caterpillar 825
sheepsfoot roller compactor minimum 20 tons for about eight passes to help detect and
collapse near surface cavities and or voids It should be noted that many areas of
