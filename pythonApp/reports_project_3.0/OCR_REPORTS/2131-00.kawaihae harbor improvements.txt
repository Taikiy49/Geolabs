INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Kawaihae Harbor Improvements in
South Kohala Hawaii The general location of the site is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed in general
accordance with our proposal dated May 11 1988
This report summarizes the findings from the field exploration and
our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
based on data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and are not intended for construction bidding
estimation purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to improve the harbor facilities at the subject site
A major portion of these improvements will be the construction of a
container handling facility which includes container yards and access
roads Based on the plans supplied to us the proposed pavement area is
approximately 7 5 acres
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2131 00 Page 3
It is our understanding that both asphaltic concrete and Portland
Cement concrete pavements may be considered for the facility The
pavements will support heavily loaded straddle trucks and or forklifts
The development also includes the construction of high light poles
The existing soil structures located at the northern portion of the
site will be demolished Minimum site grading is anticipated for the
project
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface soil conditions at the site in order to develop a generalized
soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertinent to the pavement and foundation design of the
proposed improvements
The work was performed in general accordance with our proposal to
Okahara Associates Inc dated May 11 1988 The scope of work
consisted of the following
1 Drilling and sampling thirty four 34 borings to depths of 10 to
30 3 feet below the existing ground surface and obtaining surface
samples
2 Reconnaissance of the project site and vicinity to make visual
observations of the surface soil conditions
3 Review of available soils and geologic information pertinent to
the project site and its vicinity
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field
exploration to determine the engineering properties and
classification of the materials encountered

5 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the
purpose of developing pavement and foundation design
recommendations
6 Preparation of this report presenting the design recommendations
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and laboratory testing
are presented in the appendices of this report
SITE DESCRIPTION
The subject site is located at Kawaihae in South Kohala Hawaii The
general area is bounded by Kawaihae Road to the east Kawaihae Harbor to
the west a drainage ditch to the north and an access road to the south
The area is currently being used by Matson and Young Brothers Companies
Existing silo structures are present in the northern portion of the
site The site is generally level with surface materials consisting
primarily of silty sand with coral gravel The existing ground elevations
vary from Elevation 15 at the eastern corner down to about Elevation 9
near the edge of pier area
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Thirty four 34 borings were drilled and sampled at the site to
depths of 10 0 to 30 3 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings and surface samples are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The field exploration generally encountered a surficial fill layer of
about 10 feet in thickness consisting primarily of dense to medium dense
tan white coralline silty sand with gravel Underlying the surficial
layer were lagoonal and beach deposits consisting of loose to medium dense
silty sands sandy silts with some clayey silts

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration generally encountered competent subgrade
material near the surface of the site The laboratory tests indicated
that the on site silty sand fill material can provide relatively high
subgrade strength values CBR and Modulus of Subgrade Reaction values
Based on the information provided to us the pavement should be
designed to support Hyster 920 forklifts with an anticipated maximum axle
load of 292 5 kips Both asphaltic concrete and Portland Cement concrete
pavements were considered for design lives of 10 000 and 20 000 load
repetitions
Pavement Design
Representative bulk samples of the upper sandy fill material were
obtained during our field exploration Five 5 laboratory CBR tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1883 on the samples
The laboratory CBR values obtained generally ranged from 37 to 85
with an average of 55 for specimens compacted to a minimum of 95
compaction per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 In general coral materials
often produce high CBR test values under static test loading condition
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2131 00 Page 6
owever due to the long term dynamic loading effects of the large
forklifts and the tendency of coral materials to break down under repeated
dynamic loadings it is recommended that a reduced CBR value be used in
the pavement design A design CBR value of 40 was selected in our
analyses to account for some localized soil variability and long term
strength degradation Based on previous experience and approximate
relationships between CBR values and modulus of subgrade reaction a value
of 450 p c i was selected as the design modulus of subgrade reaction
The pavement design parameters may be summarized as follows
1 Subgrade Soil Tan White Coralline Silty Sand With Gravel
2 Design Vehicle Hyster 920 Maximum Axle Load 292 5 Kips
3 Design CBR Value 40
4 Design K Value 450 p c i

Strength 650 p s i
6 Design Traffic 10 000 and 20 000 Load Repetitions

The above recommendations assume a jointed non reinforced concrete

thickness may be reduced The degree of reduction depends on the

The thick pavement sections recommended above are based on the very
high stresses induced by the heavily loaded Hyster forklift If the
traffic lanes can be delineated from the storage areas then the thick
pavement sections can then be limited to the traffic lanes and a thinner
pavement section may be considered where only storage loads are
anticipated

Concrete Pavement for High Stress Areas
Previous experience has indicated that structural failures in
asphaltic concrete AC pavements are common in high stress areas where
the heavily loaded vehicles make sharp turns In these areas it may be
more desirable to use Portland Cement concrete PCC pavements to
accommodate the high load concentrations
Therefore if asphaltic concrete pavement is planned for the
container yard a combination of AC and PCC pavements may be considered
with PCC pavement constructed for certain traffic paths particularly along
the areas where sharp turns of the heavily loaded vehicles are
anticipated Asphaltic concrete pavement may be used for the remainder
areas
This method would only be effective if definite traffic lanes can be
established so the high stress areas can be identified We would be glad
to provide additional design details for this approach should it appear
desirable
Pavement Joints
Joints in concrete pavements should be provided to 1 allow
contraction and expansion caused by temperature and moisture changes in
the pavements 2 relieve warping and curling stresses caused by
temperature differential within pavements 3 prevent irregular or random
cracking of pavements from unequal subgrade support or other causes 4
control shrinkage and cracking and 5 expedite construction
Joints should be provided in general accordance with the
recommendations of the Portland Cement Association
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2131 00 Page 9
Pavement Drainage
Surface run off from the pavement areas should be collected and
drained to a suitable discharge point Water should not be be allowed to
pond immediately adjacent to the pavement areas The subject site should
be graded during construction to provide positive drainage away from the
pavement sections
Site Grading
The finish pavement grades were not available at the time of report
preparation However it is anticipated that no major site grading is
required
For pavement construction the area within the contract limits should
be thoroughly cleared at the on set of earthwork All existing old
pavements debris and unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
properly off site in order to prevent contamination of the excavated
material It appears that conventional equipment may be used for
excavation The excavated soil can be used as a source fill and
backfilling materials up to the subgrade elevation The compaction
requirements are detailed below
Subgrade Preparation
The surface of the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of 12
inches and recompacted to a minimum of 100 of its maximum dry density per
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 A vibratory compactor is recommended to
facilitate the compaction
Soft loose spots encountered at the subgrade level should be removed
and replaced with on site sandy soils compacted to a minimum of 100
maximum dry density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2131 00 Page 10
Fill if required should consist of select on site coral sand or
off site select borrow material with a minimum CBR value of 55 when tested
per ASTM D 1883 Test Method laboratory CBR test Fill should be placed
in 8 inch compacted lifts to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density
per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The compaction should be increased to
100 relative dry density for the last 12 inches below the pavement
subgrade elevation
Groundwater level was generally encountered lower than 6 feet below
the existing ground surface during our field exploration In addition
the sandy subgrade soils generally fall into the category of SM and SP SM
based on the Unified Soil Classification System and may be considered as
relatively permeable Therefore subsurface drainage problems are not
anticipated
Utility Trenches
New drain and utility lines may be planned with the pavement
construction A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of ASTM C 33 No
67 gravel 3B Fine is recommended under the pipes
Granular material such as on site silty sands should be used for
the trench backfill The backfill up to about one foot above the pipes
should be lightly tamped to reduce the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot
above the pipes to the subgrade or finished grade should be placed in
maximum 8 inch horizontal lifts mechanically compacted to a minimum of
95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper
portions of the backfill below the pavement areas should be compacted in
accordance with the pavement subgrade requirements

Pier Foundations
It is our understanding that high light poles will be constructed for
the project We recommend that drilled pier foundations be used to
support each light pole
Since the piers would be subjected to repeated wind loads a critical
consideration would be to limit permanent lateral deflections of the
piers This may be achieved by sufficient embedment of the piers
The vertical compressive load on the poles is relatively light and
may be supported by the frictional and end bearing resistance between the
piers and the subsoils
It is our opinion that a 24 inch diameter drilled pier embedded 5
feet below the existing ground surface into the on site sandy fill
material would be adequate to support each light pole
Drilled Pier Construction
The drilled pier construction should be closely monitored by the
geotechnical engineer in order to see that adequate foundation support is
achieved Since a portion of the loading capacities come from the
frictional resistance between the shaft and the in situ soils extra care
should be applied to the drilling of the pier hole selection of a
non shrinkage concrete mix design and the placement of concrete
Prompt placement of concrete in the prepared hole as soon as it is
completed is recommended to reduce caving in of the hole The concrete
mix should be designed to minimize shrinkage in order to maintain high
frictional contact between the in situ soils and pier shaft The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to avoid segregation of the
aggregates

Building Foundations
Miscellaneous service structures may be planned at the site Shallow
spread footing foundations bearing on the on site sandy fill material may
be used to support these very light structures
An allowable bearing value of 3000 p s f with a minimum footing
embedment of 12 inches may be utilized A minimum footing width of 12
inches is recommended Estimated settlements are on the order of less
than one half of an inch total and one fourth of an inch differential
Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be
forwarded to the geotechnical engineer for review and written comments
prior to construction This review is needed to determine adherence to
the earthwork pavement and foundation recommendations given herein If
this review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot assume
responsibility for misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to
provide geotechnical engineering services during earthwork and drilling of
the pier holes prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete This
is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications or
recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface
conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such
observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site

construction are different from those assumed or considered in this
report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
and surface samples Variations of conditions between the field data
points may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction If variations then appear evident it
will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this
report
The location of the field data points were approximately determined
by taping from reference points indicated on the general plan dated
March 1988 provided by Okahara Associates Inc The physical
locations of the field data points should be considered accurate only to
the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field
data points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
