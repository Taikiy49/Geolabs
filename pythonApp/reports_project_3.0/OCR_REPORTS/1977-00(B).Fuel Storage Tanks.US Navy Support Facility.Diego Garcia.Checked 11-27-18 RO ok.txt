SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration generally encountered dense to
medium dense silty sand and gravelly sand deposits to
depths of about 40 to 75 feet below the existing
ground surface
Loose sand layers were encountered generally at depths
of about 25 to 35 feet in the borings The loose sand
layers have a critical effect on the foundation design
of the tanks since they have a potential for
liquefaction settlements during a large earthquake
The following three alternatives to mitigate the
liquefaction potential were evaluated
1 densification of the loose sand layer by special
deep densification methods to allow the use of
conventional spread or ring foundations for the
tank
3 relocation of the tanks to a site with better
subsurface conditions and
2 use of a pile foundation to derive support from
below the loose liquefiable sand layers
It appears that a pile foundation system would involve
more conventional construction techniques which have
been used on the island previously However the pile
foundation system compared with deep densification
methods and spread footing could be several times
more costly We recommend further studies and
consultation with specialty contractors to more
accurately define the cost effectiveness of the
methods

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
Diego Garcia is a footprint shaped atoll of the Chagos Archipelago
The atoll is about 850 miles south of India in the Indian Ocean
The origin of coral atolls was first postulated by Charles Darwin in
1842 His theory was that an atoll began as a fringing reef around a
volcanic island The island begins to subside tectonically at a slow rate
so that the coral growth maintains a reef line As the island subsides
and becomes smaller the reef grows larger and becomes a barrier reef
When the island finally disappears below sea level the upward growing
reef encloses only a lagoon and becomes an atoll
This basic theory has been somewhat modified by other considerations
such as eustatic sea level changes and cooled waters brought on by glacial
activity
Generally the ocean side of an atoll is built up of larger pieces of
rock ripped off the hard reef and worked up during storms while the lagoon
side is composed of finer material
The usual atoll island stands between 5 to 30 feet above sea level
The reef rock platform is generally about the low tide level The island
detritus may spill off the reef rock platform and build up towards the
lagoon side of the island
Visual observations confirm the presence of hard reef rock along the
ocean shoreline west of the site

Liquefaction Dynamic Settlements
The borings disclosed that the project site is underlain by loose
silty fine sand The thickness of this layer varies from about 10 to 15
feet When a loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough due to a large areal extent of the sand deposit to
W O 1977 00 B Page 8
dissipate the build up of pore pressure the effective stress internal
strength of the soils is reduced Under sustained vibration the pore
water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a
viscous fluid This phenomenon is called liquefaction The major factors
affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands are more prone
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional
to relative density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is
directly proportional to the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
The field exploration confirmed the existence of conditions 1 and 2
above However to our knowledge a seismicity study has not been
conducted for Diego Garcia therefore the magnitude of a design
earthquake necessary to evaluate condition 3 above has not been
established

Dynamic Compaction Heavy Tamping This method utilizes high
energy impacts on the ground by dropping heavy weights generally
ranging from 10 to 40 tons from heights of 50 to 130 feet The
dynamic impact of the heavy weights transmits shock waves
downward through the soil which rearranges the soil particles
into a denser configuration This method is generally effective
for sandy or gravelly soils with limited silt content as found
at the site Additional fill material is required to compensate
for the ground settlement caused by the densification This
method is generally the least costly however the effectiveness
of this procedure may be limited by the relatively deep zone of
loose sand
Vibro Compaction A special vibrator which produces high
centrifugal forces in the horizontal direction is used to
densify clean sand deposits The action of the vibrator is
usually accompanied by water jetting which reduces intergranular
forces between the soil particles causing localized liquefaction
and densification of the sands The vibrator is generally
suspended from a crane and is lowered into the ground at a
predetermined pattern generally 6 to 14 feet on centers Sand
or gravel is added as a settlement crater forms around the
probe This method is effective for clean sands with less than
about 15 percent passing the 200 sieve Much of the soil at the
site appears to meet this criteria
Vibro Replacement Stone Columns A vibrator is used as a means
of forming a hole and densifying the granular soils around the
hole Gravel is then introduced into the hole and compacted in
stages as the vibrator is removed In this way the granular
subsoils are densified and columns of stone are created which
increase the shearing resistance decrease the compressibility
W O 1977 00 B Page 11
and allow the rapid dissipation of the excess pore pressures
induces by earthquake vibrations The stone columns are
generally installed about 4 to 10 feet centers This method is
generally applicable to fine grained as well as granular soil
The applicability of this method depends primarily on whether a
source of gravel is readily available
d Compaction Piles Displacement piles are driven into the ground
at close spacing Granular backfill is then introduced into the
hole and compacted as the pile is withdrawn Alternatively the
piles may be left in the ground
We believe that the densification method selected would depend
primarily on the cost effectiveness of each alternative as well as the
availability of specialty contractors equipment at the site and the
results of a field test program to verify the effectiveness of each
method Our analyses indicate that densification of the underlying loose
sand deposit to about 70 percent relative density would provide a factor
of safety against potential liquefaction of about 1 1 In our estimation
each of the above densification methods or a combination of methods could
increase the relative density of the loose underlying sand layer to about
70 percent
The normal procedure for implementing a densification program would
be to specify a performance standard in the contract i e 70 percent
relative density A contractor would then be allowed to select the method
for densification to achieve this performance standard It is recommended
that a test program be conducted in the field at the start of the
densification program to verify that the contractor s methods selected can
achieve the required densification The field test program would consist
of densifying several locations on the site and then conducting standard
penetration tests in drilled borings or cone penetration tests to verify
the increase in density

Tank Foundation Alternatives
Based on the field exploration and conditions discussed above
several alternative foundation designs were evaluated These alternatives
are briefly described below
Footing Foundation With Densification
In light of the many large earthquakes which have occurred in the
vicinity of the site it is our opinion that the potential for
earthquake induced liquefaction settlements is relatively high
Therefore we recommend that shallow footing foundations be used for
the new tank structures only if the underlying sands are densified
first to mitigate the liquefaction hazard If the underlying loose
sands are adequately densified then the subsequent static and
dynamic settlements would be reduced to tolerable limits
An allowable bearing value of up to 4000 p s f may be used for
design of spread or ring footings bearing on the dense sand and
gravel material A minimum footing embedment of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade is recommended
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive
earth pressure developed against the buried vertical faces of the
foundation system and by frictional resistance developed between the
bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
W O 1977 00 B Page 13
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 p c f This assumes that the soil
around the foundation is well compacted as specified under Site
Grading A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used
It should be noted that the effectiveness of this method would depend
on the availability of specialty contractors in the area Also
there is some uncertainty regarding the time required for completion
of the densification program as field adjustments are commonly
required

Pile Foundation With Structural Slab
In light of the potential static and dynamic settlements at the site
pile foundations may be utilized to support the structure to limit
the effect of the ground settlement due to liquefaction
Since a hard bearing layer was not encountered in the borings a
friction pile design is recommended
W O 1977 00 B Page 14
Based on the boring data a pile length of about 75 feet below the
existing ground surface is anticipated
If the site is subjected to a strong earthquake settlement of the
loose sand may occur The downward movement would result in negative
skin friction forces generally known as downdrag force on the
pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force generally depends on
the thickness of the compressible layer adhesion between the soil
and pile and the pile perimeter area
Calculations indicate that a 75 foot long 16 5 inch octagonal
prestressed concrete pile bearing into the underlying sand may be
designed for a 75 ton net allowable load carrying capacity after
taking the downdrag force into account The design pile capacity
maybe increased or decreased by adjusting the pile length
We anticipate that predrilling would be required through 6 to 8 feet
of new fill as well as 10 to 20 feet of dense sand below the site
The lateral resistance of the pile can be analyzed by using the
appropriate soil modulus values of the subsoils at various depths
below the existing ground surface A lateral pile analysis was
carried out with the use of internally generated non linear P Y
curves to represent the soil modulus values Since the soil modulus
varies with the deflection experienced the computerized analysis
first assumes a modulus value then calculates the pile deflection
based on the assumed modulus The calculated deflection is then used
to determine the modulus from the non linear P Y curves The
calculated modulus and the assumed modulus are then compared The
procedure is repeated until the new assumed and calculated moduli are
essentially the same Based on the analysis we estimate that the
lateral capacity of the pile foundation would be about 8 kips per
pile

monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils
