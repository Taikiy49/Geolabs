SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, it appears that the hard/dense in-situ coral materials encountered at the 
site can provide adequate support for future development at the site. Based on the 
generally hard shallow subsurface conditions, we recommend using shallow spread and/or 
continuous footings to support new construction.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
4,000 pounds per square foot may be used for the preliminary design of shallow 
foundations bearing on hard in-situ coral or compacted fills. This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
It has been our experience in other projects on the Ewa Coastal Plain that coral 
formations are generally highly variable in terms of hardness, weathering, and 
fracturing.  In addition, cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in coral 
formations.  In many cases, cavities and voids in the coral formation may be filled with 
loose coralline deposits.  In areas where natural coralline deposits are anticipated near 
the ground surface, special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to 
reduce the potential for distress to the future improvements, such as foundations, floor 
slabs and pavements. 
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the 
cavities and/or voids below foundations, a program of cavity probing and grouting of the 
foundation locations may be necessary during construction depending on the building 
loads.  In addition, due to the high organic matter content of the surface soils, 
over-excavation of these materials may be required during construction.   
The potential need for over-excavation and probing and grouting under the 
structure foundations should be further evaluated based on the actual subsurface 
conditions at the structure locations.  In addition, the limits and extent of the surface fill, 
alluvium, and coralline deposits should be delineated as much as possible.  Therefore, 
we recommend evaluating the subsurface conditions by conducting a formal field 
exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling borings during the design phase 
when the type and distribution of the proposed structures are better defined. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of weathered remnants of the extinct 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active 
and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu. 
The project site is located on the Ewa Coastal Plain off the southern flank of 
Waianae and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of 
the coastal plain.  The subsurface conditions are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea during 
the Pleistocene Epoch which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments.  The base of 
the stratigraphic section in this area is Waianae Basalt. 
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene 
Epoch and the early Pleistocene Epoch by thin bedded basaltic lava flows.  The main 
shield-building activities ceased approximately 2.5 – 3.5 million years ago.  Evidence 
from drilled wells indicates that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1,200 feet 
since the cessation of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across the mouth of the bays.  The 
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence.  A series of 
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments 
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world.  As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins.  Conversely, as the glaciers receded, or melted, 
global sea levels rose because more water was available.  The land mass of Oahu

remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in 
nature.  These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both 
higher and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs. 
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted 
in deposits of coral-algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal 
sedimentary unit. 
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused 
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.  In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the 
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy 
conditions.  
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial channels, deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over 
the reefs and lagoonal sediments.

Sea Level (MSL) in the southeastern portion to about +34 feet MSL along the northern 
limits.  Groundwater is anticipated to occur at about Elevation +1 to +3 feet MSL 
throughout most of the project area.

2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is north of Campbell Industrial Park in the Kapolei – Kalaeloa 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project site is about 23 acres of fallow former 
agricultural land that is planned to be redeveloped as an industrial/commercial 
development, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field observations, the project site is presently vacant land which is 
covered with grasses with isolated stands and thickets of sub-tropical dry land 
vegetation, such as kiawe (prosopis sp.) and haole koa (leucaena sp.).  Based on our 
field observations and the topographic plan provided, most of the project site gently 
slopes down toward the southwest with existing ground surface elevations ranging from 
about +34 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the northern end of the site to about +12 feet 
MSL in the southwestern portion of the project site.   
There are three piles of coralline boulders in the northwestern portion of the site; 
and, there has been some minor amount of grading along a drainage swale on the 
northern boundary of the property.

Site Grading 
Our exploration indicates that competent coralline deposits underlie the site at 
relatively shallow depths below the existing ground surface.  These coralline materials,

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be 
backfilled with compacted fills. The cleared and grubbed material should be 
properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we believe that excavated coralline materials (without deleterious 
materials and/or over-sized materials) may be reused as a source of general or 
structural fill materials, if processed to a well graded material with a maximum 
particle size of 3 inches in greatest dimension.  If required, imported general fill 
materials should consist of materials with particle sizes of 6 inches or less in 
maximum dimension and should be free of deleterious materials. The material for 
general fill should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 8 or 
higher and a swell potential of 2 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D 1883.  
Geolabs should test imported fill materials a minimum of 10 days prior to being 
transported to the project site for the intended use. Imported materials, such as 
crushed coral or basalt, may be used for structural fill. The material should be well 
graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have a laboratory CBR 
value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than 1 percent when tested 
in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements

compaction requirements for subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such as 
pavement subgrades, should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  We

3.1.4 Cavities and Voids 
Native coralline materials and coral formations generally are highly variable in terms 
of hardness, weathering, and fracturing. In many cases, cavities and voids in the 
coral formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits and/or clayey alluvial 
soils. In areas where coralline formation is anticipated near the ground surface, 
special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the 
potential for distress to the future improvements. 
Based on the field exploration and our experience in the area, cavities and/or voids 
are commonly encountered in the coralline deposits in the project vicinity. To 
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities 
below foundations, we recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and 
grouting of the structure foundations during construction. 
For estimating purposes, we recommend drilling probe holes at each isolated 
spread footing location (one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area) 
and at 10 feet on-center along wall foundations or continuous strip footings. The

3.1.5 Excavation 
The coral limestone formation at the project site appears to be well-cemented and 
very hard in nature. Therefore, we anticipate some difficult excavation conditions 
may arise during construction. It is possible that the excavation for a portion of the 
utility line trenches will require the use of hoerams or chipping. The contractor must 
exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the coral 
formation, resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for improvements in the 
vicinity. 
It is believed that, for grading work, the coralline limestone may be ripped using 
large bulldozers, such as the CAT D-9 and D-10 series.  The ripped material will 
most likely be blocky in nature and will probably require crushing or other 
processing to be usable as fill material. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
visual observation of the existing coral formation and field data from the test pits 
excavated. We recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions 
and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
3.1.6 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe that cut slopes in coral may be cut at an inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Fills 
placed on sloping ground with an inclination of 5H:1V or steeper should be keyed 
and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations.

3.2 
Foundations 
Based on our exploration results and review of the available soils information, we 
recommend using shallow foundation systems, such as spread footing foundations, to 
support structures planned for the proposed development.  For preliminary design 
purposes, an allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 psf may be used for the design 
of the footings bearing on hard in-situ coral materials or select fill placed and compacted 
in accordance with the recommendations presented herein.  The bearing value 
recommended above is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) 
for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.   
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grade on relatively level ground.  Thickened edge slabs should be 
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. For 
footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on sloping ground conditions, the 
footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback 
distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the 
slope.  
The bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface 
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be over-excavated to 
expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with the 
on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the bottom

3.3 
Slabs-On-Grade 
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery 
vibration), we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting 
of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The 
open-graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would 
serve as a capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future moisture 
infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, we recommend 
placing an impervious moisture barrier, such as a plastic membrane, on top of the 
open-graded gravel cushion fill layer. Flexible floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet 
vinyl, should be considered because they can better mask minor slab cracking. In 
addition, we recommend designing interior walls to incorporate some flexibility in 
accommodating a small amount of possible ground movements. 
If the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic, such as trucks and forklifts, or 
subjected to machinery vibration, we recommend increasing the slab thickness to a 
minimum of 6 inches and providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs 
in lieu of the 4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer. The moisture barrier may be omitted 
for these slabs. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled 
tightly against the slab edges with low expansion, relatively impervious soils. These

materials or select non-expansive materials as recommended herein.  Therefore, these 
materials will have high CBR values with low expansion values. Considering the heavy 
industrial traffic anticipated for the new pavement areas, we believe that the following 
preliminary pavement design may be used for planning and budgeting purposes: 
Flexible Pavements 
  4.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  8.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
12.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.  Subgrades should be 
thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered with the pavement surface course. 
We recommend performing CBR and density tests on the actual subgrade soils 
encountered during roadway construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section.  
The recommended section also assumes that adequate drainage will be provided for 
the paved areas.

3.6 
Utility Connections 
We anticipate that new utility connections for the project will consist primarily of 
water, sewer, and drain lines.  In general, good construction practices should be utilized 
for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities at the site.  The 
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench 
excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations.  In addition, the 
excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety 
requirements.  The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation. 
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that most of the utility 
trench excavations will likely encounter the coral formation as described in the test pit 
logs, which may pose some excavation difficulties during the trench excavation. In 
addition, the contractor should anticipate significant over-break of the sides of the trench 
excavations considering the nature of the materials at the site.  This over-break 
condition may be minimized by the use of trenching machines. 
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended below the pipes.  Free-draining 
granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), should 
be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide 
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill, thus 
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material 
generally less than 3 inches in maximum size compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction.  Where trenches will be located in paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the 
trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to not less than 
95 percent relative compaction.  Where the utility line crosses beneath the footings, the 
utility pipes should be concrete jacketed.

Site Drainage 
The finished grades outside of buildings should be sloped to shed water away
