PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this geotechnical engineering investigation was to
explore the subsurface soil and groundwater conditions along the proposed
waterline alignment and at the two proposed reservoir sites in order to
provide soil engineering recommendations for use in the design and
construction of the waterline and foundations of the reservoir
structures The general project layout is shown on Site Plan 1
Plate 2
The work was done in general accordance with our proposal dated
May 22 1986

used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report

2 3 and 4 The boring logs and information regarding the field
explorations and laboratory testing are presented in the appendices

SITE CONDITIONS
Site Description
Clark Air Base is located about 50 miles northwest of Manila on the
Island of Luzon in the Republic of the Philippines
The project site generally covers the western half of the base Most
of the area has been developed for housing and schools
The proposed waterlines are primarily located along existing streets
The Lower Reservoir site is located in a vacant gently sloping
grassy area near the Sapang Bato Gate The ground elevations range from
655 to 660 feet above Mean Sea Level
The Upper Reservoir site is located on a ridge north of the
existing POL Storage Area The topography is hilly with an average
gradient of about 4 1 H V The general area is covered with grass and
trees There is an existing 40 000 gallon metal water tank next to the
proposed reservoir The ground elevations at the site generally vary from
880 to 920 feet above Mean Sea Level

Geology
From available geologic literature the project site is located
within the central volcanic province of the Zambales Bataan Volcanic
Highlands a subprovince of the Western Highlands Physiographic Province
Clark Air Base is situated on the lower flank of the Zambales Range
between two large streams the Bamban River along the north and Abacan
River along the south side
Examination of the topography of the area leads to the conjecture
that the base is generally located on outwash deposits derived from the
weathering of volcanics from the Zambales Range to the west of the base
These unconsolidated gravelly sands formed a deep deposit over the area
studied except for some isolated topographic highs that appear to be
composed of lithic tuffs and agglomerates
Several faults associated with regional tectonic mountain building
activities are located near the base and are probably associated with the
major river trends
Geological materials encountered in the borings include
1 Terrace Sand Deposit Alluvium
This unconsolidated granular deposit is composed mostly of fairly
clean fine to coarse sand size grains with occasional gravels
and cobbles Silt size particles within the alluvial deposit
generally comprise less than 10 percent of the soil below the
upper 1 to 2 feet of the ground surface

Upper Reservoir Site
This material consists of grayish brown very stiff cemented
silty sand tuffaceous matrix containing some subrounded to
angular completely to slightly weathered rock fragments which
vary from tuffs to relatively weathering resistant andesite and
dacite
FIELD EXPLORATION
Drilling and Sampling
A total of seventeen 17 borings were drilled at the site to explore
the subsurface soil and water conditions and to obtain disturbed and
undisturbed samples for laboratory testing
Twelve borings were drilled to depths of about 10 feet below the
existing ground surface along the waterline alignment
Two borings were drilled to depths of 50 feet each at the Upper
Reservoir site
Two alternative sites were evaluated for the Lower Reservoir A
total of three borings each to a depth of 46 to 50 feet were drilled at
the two sites
Details of the drilling and sampling are described in Appendix A of
Soil Conditions
The site investigation revealed that the subsurface materials below
the waterline alignment areas as well as the Lower Reservoir site
consisted almost entirely of well graded gravelly sand deposits to depths
of at least 10 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface These soils
can be considered as non expansive granular material with good bearing
qualities

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed relatively competent soil conditions
below the proposed improvements From a soil engineering point of view
the sites are suitable for the proposed improvements
Waterline
Medium to dense sandy soils were encountered along the proposed
waterline alignment These soils can generally provide good bearing
support for the proposed waterline therefore special subgrade
preparation is not required
Water level was not encountered in the borings therefore dewatering
is not anticipated for the waterline construction

Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The medium dense to dense sandy soils which were encountered along
the alignment have good bearing capacity
The new waterline construction in general will not materially
increase the vertical loading upon these underlying soils if no
additional fills are placed upon the existing ground surface The
resulting net loading between the weight of the excavated material
and backfill will be essentially small therefore significant
settlements are not anticipated if proper construction techniques
are utilized
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great
effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe therefore the
pipe bedding is an important design consideration
A minimum of six inches of compacted granular bedding material should
be placed below the pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond
the pipe sides a minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe
diameter and should extend halfway up the pipe sides

Trench Wall Supports

The sandy soils have minimal cohesion and it is our opinion that the
trench walls will require shoring
The excavation should be provided with proper bracing in accordance
with applicable safety requirements The design and safety of the
trench supports should be the responsibility of the contractor
If sufficient space is available the excavation may be cut back at a
temporary slope of H V in lieu of the above wall supports
However it should be taken into consideration that a greater amount
of vertical pressures will be transmitted to the pipe by widening of
the trench
Heavy equipment and stockpiles should be kept away from the tops of
the open trenches
The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should be
continuously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of
movements and possible sloughing of the trench walls Such
monitoring is particularly critical in areas where existing
structures are adjacent to trench excavation

Backfill
a at
Granular material such as sand should be used with light tamping or
water jetting to backfill the utility trench up to about one foot
above the pipe to reduce the compactive effort required and possible
damage to the pipe
The excavated on site soil can be reused in the upper portion of the
trench backfill from above one foot over the pipe to finished grade
Backfill should be mechanically compacted to a minimm of 90 of its
maximum dry density established by ASTM D 1557 78

Upper Reservoir
Foundation
Spread and continuous wall footings bearing on the dense weathered
tuff formation is recommended for the reservoir An allowable
bearing value up to 5000 p s f may be used for footings bearing on
the above undisturbed material A one third increase of the above
bearing value may be used for temporary wind and seismic loads
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 30 inches below the
adjacent finished grade
Footings located next to utility trenches should extend down to the
bottom of the utility trench and bear on the stiff undisturbed soil
or the trench backfill should be well compacted
Site drainage should be designed to divert surface water away from
the reservoir foundation

Fills placed on slopes having grades steeper than 5 horizontal to 1
vertical 5 1 should be keyed and benched into the existing stiff
ground
