INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have conducted a preliminary geotechnical
and geological reconnaissance of the East Mamala Bay Facilities Plan area This report
summarizes our work on the project and presents our findings
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop a facilities plan for the East Mamala Bay regional sewer
system A facilities plan is a preliminary planning document to provide guidelines for
establishing regional compliance to the enforceable portions of the Clean Water Act
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 2
A facilities plan examines the feasibility of installation of new wastewater
conveyance and treatment facilities in terms of economics water quality objectives
environmental compatibility and the comparison of cost effectiveness of alternatives As
an aid in evaluating the potential costs of any new conveyance facilities for which a need
may be identified it is desirable to have preliminary information pertaining to the general
subsurface conditions in the area of the proposed new facilities
The area being considered under the East Mamala Bay Facilities Plan extends from
Moanalua to Kuliouou in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
This preliminary report was prepared solely for project planning purposes and
therefore does not contain sufficient data nor appropriate recommendations for detailed
design of any new wastewater facilities
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our reconnaissance was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
soil conditions within the facilities plan area to develop an up dated generalized soils data
set for use in the Environmental Impact Statement for proposed upgrades to the
sewerage systems within the area It should be recognized that this report is preliminary
in nature and is not intended for use in cost estimation and design for any new sewer
system improvements
The scope of our work on this project included but was not limited to the following
work efforts
1 A review of some of the available soils and geological literature maps and
soils reports published and unpublished pertinent to the facilities planning
area

2 The drilling and sampling of five 5 borings at selected locations within the
project area to provide additional subsurface data at areas where
subsurface data was lacking

provide information on their engineering properties
4 Preparation of this report
SURFACE PHYSIOGRAPHIC REGIONS
Costal Plain Region
This region typically has low topographic relief sloping gently from inland areas
toward the sea with elevations typically less than about 50 feet above Mean Sea Level
MSL This physiographic region is found along the southern coastline and extends
inland for varying distances before it terminates at the Inland Mountain and Inland Valley
Regions The watertable is found close to the ground surface due to the proximity of the
ocean and MSL The shallow groundwater in this area is generally brackish Beach
sands coral limestone reefs recent alluvium and man made fills are the most common
formations and earth materials encountered in these areas
Central Mountain Regions
This physiographic region generally comprises the lower slopes of the Koolau
Mountain Range in the project area Elevations range from approximately 50 feet to in
excess of 200 feet above MSL This region may be found close to the ocean and coastal
regions as in the case in the far eastern portion of the subject area Kuliouou to Waialae
Kahala or may exist several miles inland as is the case in the areas from Nuuanu to Halawa
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 2909 00 Page 4
Groundwater in this region is generally found at greater depths except where
localized perched aquifers exist
The mountain regions are erosional remnants of volcanic shield building which
originally formed the island of Oahu Basalt rock formation and associated residual and
Saprolitic in situ soils are the most common geologic units encountered in this region
The basalt rock formations exist in a wide range of weathered states and physical
competencies
Central Valley Regions
This physiographic region comprises the elongated valleys which have been
eroded into and are bounded on either side by the Koolau Mountain ridges They
generally trend from the northeast down to the southwest from the central portion of the
island Geologic materials encountered in these valleys are generally alluvial and colluvial
materials derived from the weathering of up slope landforms The materials covering the
valley floors and lining the lower portions of the valley walls are generally silts and clays
with abundant cobbles and boulders
The existence of groundwater in this region is highly variable due to the range of
geologic factors governing the flow paths and barriers to groundwater flow Generally the
watertable can be found at the level of water observed in streams in the valley floors
This valley region with its associated alluvial soils spill out onto the Coastal Lowland
Region forming broad alluvial fans near the mouths of most of the valleys
Volcanic Crater Features
These physiographic features are found scattered throughout the project area The
dominant geomophic feature associated with this region is a volcanic crater or eruptive
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 5
center formed by the accumulation of basalt tuff and cinder deposits These features
are found as isolated landforms which are anomalous with the surrounding geologic
materials and are generally circular in shape reflecting their eruptive character
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield volcano Koolau forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project area is located along the southern flank of Koolau and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions along the majority of the routes are
dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid
during high stands of the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The
base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates
that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this
early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 6
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted in
deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary
unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 7
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
post erosional i e following the cessation of shield building events were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyrociastic material predominantly ash and cinder These deposits of
pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock One
of these eruptions Aliamanu caused a diversion of Halawa Stream to the north of its
original location into the area of what are now the Southeast and Magazine Lochs of Pearl
Harbor during the Kaena 95 feet high stand of the sea
The eruptions of the blanketed much of what is now
the metropolitan Honolulu area with a fine black cinder locally known as black sand
These eruptions also produced a thick lava flow which filled the bottom of Manoa Valley
Extrusion of lavas from Kamanaiki and other vents created flows which covered the
floors of Kalihi and Nuuanu Valleys
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the deep valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 8
sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in
thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in the areas which were formerly
valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Honolulu area to its present form A great many of those projects were originally
constructed done for agricultural residential and or military development Many of the
resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural loads

DISCUSSIONS
General
Our reconnaissance indicates that the facilities plan area encompasses a wide
variety of subsurface conditions Therefore it is critical that detailed geotechnical
engineering exploration and analyses be conducted as part of the design efforts for any
new sewer systems or improvement projects
Subsoil Characteristics
Based on our review of the available soils and geologic information and based on
previous experience in the facilities plan area the following discussions summarize the
general characteristics of the materials which we anticipate will be encountered within the
planning area
1 Koolau Basalt Tkb
The Koolau basalt is the oldest geologic unit within the project area It
generally consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a a flows of basaltic lavas
and may have a mantle of cobbles and boulders or residual soil overlying
the top of the rock
It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock sub components such
as Clinker layers and lava tubes could also be encountered
The Koolau basalt is the aquifer material of the Pear Harbor and Honolulu
Artesian Basins which are the major developed groundwater bodies
supplying fresh water for the Island of Oahu
The aquifer is the permeable lava rock while the upper confining member
caprock is the relatively impermeable alluvial and lagoonal deposits There
is no lower confining member as is the usual case in most aquifers At the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 10
bottom the fresh water level is confined by salt water according to the
Ghyben Herzberg Principle which is based on observations that fresh
groundwater floats on saline groundwater in coastal regions The ratio of
densities between fresh and salt water causes the salt water to be displaced
downward about 40 feet below sea level for every foot that the fresh basal
water table or potentiometric head rises above sea level
Structurally the Koolau basalt and its residual soils are among the strongest
of all the units encountered within the study area Therefore the basalt and
related residual soils saprolites make excellent bearing formations due to
their high strength characteristics in an undisturbed state
However its high strength characteristics can present problems in
excavation for trenches or substructures In situ basalt formation may
require hard excavation techniques such as hoerams or blasting These
conditions could increase the difficulty in construction and consequently
increase project costs
2 Older Alluvium Qa
The older alluvium is assigned the position of being the second oldest unit
in the stratigraphic column of the project area even though some of the
coral algal reef deposits may be intercalated with the alluvial deposits
This unit generally overlies the Koolau basalt and consists of terrigenous
sediments which have been transported by stream action from the upper
reaches of the streams The materials consist mainly of very stiff to hard
brown clayey silts and silty clays Gravel lenses representing buried stream
channels are sometimes encountered Portions of the older alluvium unit
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 2909 00 Page 11
contain some weathered basalt boulders and cobbles These coarse
grained parts of the unit may also contain artesian water of the basal fresh
water system or as a sub aquifer
The old lagoonal deposits of the older alluvium unit consist of marine or
lagoonal sediments which have been desiccated and consolidated into very
stiff to hard gray clayey silts
The old lagoonal deposits consist almost exclusively of clayey silts of rather
uniform texture and grain size with occasional shell fragments
The older alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength values which
leads to a relatively high bearing capacity Frequently these materials show
a moderate to high shrink swell potential however this is not considered
a constraint for the type of structures proposed on this project Structurally
the older alluvium materials are considered to be competent
In some areas such as along valley walls the older alluvium may contain
large percentages of boulders and cobbles These could cause difficulties
in excavation
3 Coral Algal Deposits Qls
This unit consists of fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and their related
detrital debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the
dominant portion of any coral rock assemblage
GEOLASS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 12
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral debris
consisting of gravel sand and silt sized clastic fragments Some of the
coral fragments were dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented
and or uncemented fine sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal deposits and the
degree of cementation frequently present this formation generally has high
in situ strength characteristics Structurally the coral algal reef deposits are
competent and capable of supporting heavy loads Hard ledges of coral
may present difficulties in excavating for trenches and substructures
4 Honolulu Basalts Qhb Qhp
The Honolulu Basalts generally consist of interbedded pahoehoe and a a
flows of basalt lavas and may have plastic alluvial clays with cobbles and
boulders overlying the top of the rock Also included are isolated deposits
of pyroclastics Qhp which are not related to the younger Black Sand
deposits Rtsp
It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock sub components such
as clinker layers and lava tubes could also be encountered
The basalt frequently acts either as a perching or confining member for
groundwater in the Honolulu Aquifer system Therefore groundwater may
be encountered in excavations located within this unit
Structurally this basalt is also one of the strongest of the units encountered
within the study area As with Koolau Basalt hard excavation techniques
may be required for excavation of trenches and for substructures
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2909 00 Page 13
5 Honolulu Tuffs Qht
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consist of volcanic ash
which consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard rock that resembles
siltstone or sandstone locally called mudrock The tuff is frequently found
overlying coralline deposits or alluvium units
Since the formation is a medium hard rock it has very high strength
characteristics Structurally the tuff is very competent and may present
some difficulty in excavation
This formation has relatively low permeability and groundwater is generally
not encountered in this formation When it does occur it is usually in the
form of seepage along joints
6 Black Sand Rtsp
These soils were developed from volcanic cinder sands laid down by
eruptions of the vents of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series They generally form a relatively thin less than 10 feet
mantle over coral tuff or basalt and consist of a surface layer a few feet
thick of brown sandy silt silty sand underlain by relatively unweathered
cinder sand The sand is usually of medium dense to dense consistency
and is frequently weakly to moderately cemented
Groundwater is usually encountered as a basal water table in low elevations
Groundwater is also occasionally encountered at higher elevations as
perched groundwater The cinder sands generally have rapid permeability
and therefore dewatering may be difficult
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 14
7 Recent Alluvium Ra
The recent alluvium consists mainly of very soft to medium stiff brown to
dark gray clayey silt and organic silt with varying amounts of sand and
infrequent pockets of gravel and cobbles
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low energy
environment such as an estuary or bay Frequently these soils assigned to
this unit accumulated very rapidly under water and have never been
exposed Therefore there is a tendency to encounter under consolidated
sediments which settle under the weight of a minimal overburden
Included in this unit are subunits of localized thin layers about 10 feet of
lagoonal marine sediments consisting mostly of very soft to medium stiff
gray clayey silts These gray silts are identified as lagoonal facies subunits
of the young alluvial unit
The soils assigned to this unit are generally considered to be poorly to very
poorly suited for use as support for foundation loads due to their low in situ
strength characteristics and the tendency for settlements to occur in
structures overlying soils of this unit
The soft consistency of these soils generally makes for easy excavation
However the excavation sidewalls generally must be shored to minimize the
potential of collapse
In addition these soils are usually encountered at low elevations
Groundwater is frequently encountered at shallow depths and excavations
must be dewatered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 15
8 Recent Sand Qa
This mapping unit represents recent deposits of beach and near shore
marine sands These sands are generally composed of medium grained
fragments derived from the coral algal reefs which have been reduced in
size and rounded by water action The consistency of the sands is
generally on the order of medium dense to dense There are some areas
where the sands have lithified to poorly consolidated sandstone Qd
These recent sands are generally capable of supporting moderately heavy
loads in their natural state Densification of the sands usually improves their
bearing capacity
These soils generally have easy excavation characteristics However due
to the granular nature of the soils the excavation sidewalls generally must
be shored to minimize the potential of collapse
In addition these soils are usually encountered at low elevations
Groundwater is frequently encountered at shallow depths and excavations
must be dewatered
9 Man Made Fill Rf
This mapping unit represents low areas which were filled during
urbanization The majority of these low areas are underlain at relatively
shallow depths by Recent Alluvium and were originally marshy areas A
small part of the filled lands are underlain by coral
The quality and composition of the fill is variable These fills were frequently
placed without control Therefore it is difficult to make any statement
regarding construction in these areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 16
Groundwater in the fill areas is generally expected to occur as a basal water
table near sea level However higher perched water tables may be
encountered
Along most of Nimitz Highway in the Downtown area and in parts of
Waikiki it appears that the man made fills are underlain at relatively shallow
depths by coral algal limestone The fill materials range from clays to
gravels and intermediate mixtures of soils The consistencies range from
soft or loose to hard or very dense
The capability of these man made fills to support structural loads is partly
dependent upon the design loads and the quality of the fill and its
placement and partly dependent upon the materials underlying the fills The
actual fill materials may be of suspect quality
At Keehi Lagoon in lwilei and at the mouths of major streams man made
fills were generally placed over soft sediments for development along the
shoreline areas The fill materials range from clays to gravels and
intermediate mixtures of soils The consistencies range from soft or loose
to hard or very dense
Anticipated Surface Conditions
The majority of any new sewer lines or improvements will be situated in developed
or urbanized areas Therefore surface conditions may be congested but are generally
underlain by relatively competent materials or at least a relatively stable crust overlying
poor subsoil conditions In most areas the surface soils would be sufficiently competent
to support construction activities
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 17
Critical surface conditions are identified as those areas where the surface soils may
have insufficient strength to support the loads of heavy construction equipment Critical
surface areas are situated in the Keehi Lagoon Mapunapuna area and along Nimitz
Highway at lwilei Stabilization of the ground surface may be needed in these areas to
allow access for heavy equipment If stabilization is required existing underground
utilities should be located It should be determined whether the stabilization may have
potential impacts on any of these buried utilities or on existing structures adjacent to the
work
Excavation
Excavation will be required to allow installation of any sewer lines or underground
structures such as pump stations Based on our review of available data it is anticipated
that both soft ground and hard excavation conditions will be encountered within the
facilities plan area
The method and excavation techniques should be left to the discretion of the
contractor within safe construction practices and the applicable regulations It is
anticipated that the cut and cover method would be used for most facilities which would
be installed under the facilities plan For large capacity sewer mains consideration could
be given to tunneling
It is anticipated that most of the excavations in areas underlain by coral deposits
recent alluvium and recent sands will have to be braced shored or otherwise stabilized
to reduce the potential of movement of the excavation walls Such movement could
destabilize the subsoils supporting adjacent facilities which could potentially cause
settlements and or other damages The possible stabilization techniques include but are
not limited to the installation of temporary sheeting and shoring the sloping of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 18
excavation sidewalls for temporary stability and or grouting of the subsoils to create a
competent stable mass
The method and techniques of stabilization should be left to the discretion of the
contractor within safe construction practices and the applicable regulations It is
recommended that a preconstruction damage survey be conducted to establish
preexisting conditions along the excavation alignment to reduce the potential for claims
against previous conditions and or damage It is further recommended that a surveillance
program be implemented during construction to monitor for potential signs of instability
It is strongly recommended that additional geotechnical engineering exploration be
conducted with particular emphasis on the potential stability of the excavations during
construction
Groundwater Conditions
The project area is located within the Pearl Harbor and Honolulu artesian aquifer
basins As previously discussed artesian groundwater occurs within the Koolau Basalt
The potentiometric head of this aquifer varies spatially temporally and in response to
pumpage Generally at any location where excavations encounter Koolau Basalt at an
elevation of less than 20 feet there is a significant chance of encountering artesian
groundwater
In addition groundwater is also encountered in the other geologic units described
within the project area Generally this groundwater occurs as a basal water table at or
near sea level This groundwater is usually brackish or saline In some isolated areas
perched groundwater and or sub artesian groundwater may be encountered Any
excavations which penetrate to or below sea level will most likely encounter groundwater
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 19
Dewatering
Based on our review of the available data it is our anticipation that most
excavations in areas underlain by coral deposits recent alluvium recent sands and
man made fill will encounter groundwater levels within their excavation limits
Therefore dewatering of the excavations will be required to allow construction to
be performed in dry conditions
It is anticipated that excavations in areas underlain by coral formation will
encounter highly permeable subsoil conditions which will require extensive
pumping to dewater the excavations unless precautions are taken to limit the flow
of groundwater into the open excavation
Dewatering in soft soil conditions may cause an areal drawdown of the
groundwater levels in the area surrounding the excavation This areal drawdown
could result in a loss of the buoyant forces which are partially supporting the soft
subsoils and any facilities situated in or on the surface of the soft soils This loss
of support could potentially cause settlement of the soft soils and potentially result
in damages to the existing improvements
The techniques and methods of dewatering should be the express responsibility
of the contractor However we recommend that further geotechnical engineering
exploration be conducted to emphasize the groundwater conditions and potential
dewatering parameters for any sewer construction work
Foundation Support
Currently there are no definite plans to install new pump stations or other
structures under the facilities plan However it is anticipated that some structures may
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2909 00 Page 20
be required as the plan develops The following discussions are general in nature and
are intended to provide an overview of possible foundation conditions
Spread Footing Foundations
In general we anticipate that spread footing foundations may be used to support
the proposed structures in the areas mapped as Koolau Basalt Honolulu Basalt
Older Alluvium Honolulu Tuff and Black Sand Depending upon site specific
conditions and structural loads it may be feasible to utilize spread footing
foundations in areas mapped as Coral Algal Deposits Recent Sands and some
areas of Man Made Fill
Pile Foundations
In the areas mapped as Recent Alluvium and in most areas mapped as Man made
Fill we anticipate that pile foundation systems will be required to support all but the
lightest structural loads Based upon our study we anticipate that in many of
these areas bearing strata may be encountered at depths ranging from about 50
to 100 feet below the existing ground surface
Ideally the piles would develop their vertical load capacity primarily from end
bearing on the competent bearing strata generally at great depths However it is
our opinion that where the bearing strata is very deep friction piles may be driven
to a sufficient depth that they could develop sufficient vertical load capacity from
adhesion between the piles and any medium stiff medium dense or stiffer denser
subsoils encountered before end bearing is reached
