REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea which has formed
the west central portion of the island The project site appears to be underlain by basaltic
lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the
Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are
mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The
ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the Island of
Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a dry
friable state The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has resulted
in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This desert pavement is a
surficial layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away
The lava formation at the site appears to be of a a and pahoehoe basalt type flows
which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized
by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock
material is contained within the lava flow core Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
GEOLABS INC
W O 4459 00 Page 5
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are
commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lavas were
still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and
pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the
cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

Fills and Backfills
As indicated previously we recommend that fill areas be capped with up to 2 feet of
structural fill materials to serve as a choking layer for the larger sized general fill
below and also to facilitate construction of the houses and improvements above
Structural fill materials should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of
voids in the compacted mass If the excavated materials do not contain sufficient
fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock crushing of
large sized rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required
gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material Imported material if
necessary should be observed and or tested by Geolabs for its suitability prior to
being transported to the site for the intended use A typical section showing the
recommended types of fill materials is provided on Plate 3 of this report
Where appropriate such as within utility corridors the thickness of the structural fill
layer may be increased to a minimum of 4 feet or 1 foot below the invert elevation of
utilities to facilitate construction of the underground utilities It should be noted that
the requirements for trench backfill materials call for utilizing 6 inch minus materials
such as structural fill material to backfill the trenches
General fill and backfill materials below the 2 foot structural fill capping layer may
consist of well graded granular materials of which the majority portion are less than
12 inches in size with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches The on site cut
and or stockpiled material if less than 18 inches in maximum dimension may be
used as general fill or backfill materials other than for trench backfill provided that
they are processed to meet the above gradation requirements

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
GEOLABS INC

at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be by bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rock
The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing
in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during placement
Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction
should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes to be made to
achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading by the
performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests Subsequent layers
of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of
smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the boulders of the
preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For

Excavation
Based on our field exploration at the site the project site is generally underlain by
medium dense to dense basalt cobbles and gravel with volcanic ash at the existing
ground surface The basalt cobbles and gravel with volcanic ash is underlain by
medium hard to hard basalt formations with occasional clinker layers It is anticipated
that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the existing ground

The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials is based on

Cut and Fill Slopes
We understand that cut and fill slopes of up to about 45 feet in vertical height are
planned at the project site Based on the available subsurface information it appears
that most of the cut slopes would expose the basalt cobbles and gravel with volcanic
ash material and slightly to closely fractured basalt formations ranging from medium
hard to hard with occasional clinker layers and potential small voids
In general we believe that the cut slopes planned at the site may be designed with
a slope inclination as steep as 2H 1V or flatter It should be noted that the on site
volcanic ash soils are highly susceptible to erosion by wind and surface water runoff
GEOLABS INC
W O 4459 00 Page 14
Therefore freshly graded cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope
planting or other means to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed materials
as soon as possible after the slope faces are exposed It is generally difficult to
provide a smooth cut slope face when excavating in basalt formations If over cutting
of the cut slopes occurs consideration should be given to flattening the slope or
leaving the slope as is Patching of the over cut areas with sliver fills is not
recommended due to the potential for erosion of the fill materials in the patched
areas
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and structural fill materials
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched The fill
slope face should be finished to be relatively smooth and well compacted The fill
slope face should be free of large boulders greater than 18 inches in largest
dimension and also should be free of voids which would allow erosion and migration
of fines to occur
In general slope benches may be omitted for cut and fill slopes up to 40 feet in
vertical height on this project For cut and fill slopes with a vertical height of 40 feet
or greater appropriate measures should be considered to reduce the velocities of the
surface water runoff and potential erosion of the slope face One method to reduce
the potential for erosion due to surface water runoff is to provide a minimum 8 foot
wide bench at the mid point of the slope The cut slope bench may be omitted for cut
slopes exposing dense to hard basalt rock formation
Where slope benches are provided especially for fill slopes swales should be
provided on the benches to collect and divert surface water away from the slopes to
appropriate discharge outlets In addition construction of earth berms interceptor
ditches and the use of geotextile fabrics over the fill slope face may be considered
to reduce the potential for erosion thus enhancing the long term stability of the
planned fill slopes Appropriate slope planting or other erosion control measures to
GEOLABS INC
W O 4459 00 Page 15
reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed slopes should be implemented as
soon as possible after the finished slope faces are completed

Retaining Structure Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions at the site an allowable bearing pressure of up
to 3 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing
on compacted structural fills or general fills This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Where footings are located
adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should be embedded to a sufficient
depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least 6 feet measured
from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope If foundations are
located next to utility ttenches or easements they should be embedded below a
1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or embedded as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated light design loads for
footings bearing on structural fill materials to be less than 1 inch total with differential
settlements on the order of about 0 5 inches It should be noted that if significantly
differing subgrade soils hard basalt rock and friable volcanic ash are encountered
at the retaining wall foundation elevation these materials should be over excavated
by at least 12 inches and replaced with compacted structural fill to provide a more
uniform foundation for the wall Therefore it is important that a representative from
GEOLABS INC

Geolabs observe the footing excavations to evaluate the competency of the bearing
materials and the required embedment depths
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth

footings A coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on the
compacted structural fill Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated

against in situ materials Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii Seismic
Zone 4 in accordance with the Uniform Building Code dynamic lateral earth forces
will need to be considered in the design of retaining structures The dynamic lateral
earth force due to seismic loading may be estimated using
16H pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions where H is the height

may be used when dynamic lateral earth forces are accounted for in the design of the

Underground Utilities
We envision that new underground utility lines will be required for development of this
subdivision project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for the utilities will be excavated
in the basalt rock formation or fill material placed for the proposed construction As
previously recommended placement of boulders within the utility trench corridors and within
the anticipated depths of the utility lines should be avoided to reduce the problems
associated with excavating boulders from within a utility trench
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended below the sewer and drain pipes for support For utility
lines that extend into the boulder rock fill we recommend that a 12 inch thick structural fill
layer in lieu of the 6 inch granular bedding be placed below the utility pipes to reduce the
potential for migration of the trench backfill through the rock fill voids This structural fill layer
when used as pipe bedding should be compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction

Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes The upper portion of the trench backfill

In general the purpose of the subbase course required in the County of Hawaii
standard section is to serve as a foundation or stabilizing course such that the subsequent
base and surfacing layers may be constructed on top of an inferior subgrade soil The
standard pavement section does not take into consideration good quality subgrade
conditions Since the subgrade conditions along the proposed roadway alignment at the
proposed subdivision project will consist primarily of basalt cobbles and gravel with some
volcanic ash compacted granular fill material or basalt rock formation it is our opinion that
a subbase course for stabilization foundation purposes is not necessary due to the high
quality of the subgrade materials anticipated at the subject site If the roadway subgrade
exposes poorer subgrade soils volcanic ash at the site we recommend that the poorer
subgrade soils volcanic ash be removed and be replaced with structural fill material 6 inch
minus material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

the aggregate base course layer can be considered as a subbase material since its
pavement support characteristics with CBR value over 60 would be equal to or better than
that of the select borrow subbase course required by the County of Hawaii standard section

A total of four falling head percolation tests were conducted in four drilled borings
Boring 4 and 6 to provide data for design of the proposed seepage wells and

in an effort to test the boreholes that appeared to encounter varying degrees of rock fractures
and embedded clinker seams The results of our percolation tests are presented in
Appendix D of this report
The percolation tests indicated that the subsurface materials generally have
coefficients of permeability k in the range of about 7 0 x 10 to 1 2 x 10 feet per minute
which corresponds to about 3 5 x 10 to 7 7 x 10 centimeters per second

It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava

seepage well or seepage pit In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or
voids encountered by the seepage well excavation the higher the overall permeability and
disposal capacity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depth of our borings extended to about 15 to 20 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Tests
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Tests
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 D 1140 was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils Results of the test are presented on Plate C 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the test are presented on Plate C 3

APPENDIX D
Percolation Tests
APPENDIX D
Percolation Tests
Percolation tests were performed by using the falling head method in four of the drilled
borings Boring 4 and 6 The drilled borings had a diameter of about 3 5 inches
and depths of about 5 to 6 feet at the time of testing The falling head method was tested
by filling the boreholes with water and allowing the water to percolate into the ground The
water levels and corresponding times were recorded for the calculation of subsurface
permeability The head levels within the test borings were monitored by an electric water
level sounder during the test The results of the percolation tests conducted are presented
on Plates D 1 through D 4
