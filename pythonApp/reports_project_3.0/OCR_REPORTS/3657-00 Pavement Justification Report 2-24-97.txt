REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of
Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3657 00 Page 5
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3657 00 Page 6
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in areas which were formerly
valleys and drainageways
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
post erosional i e following the cessation of shield building events were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in the expulsion of steam under extremely
high pressure together with large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash
and cinder These deposits of pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally
referred to as mudrock The eruptions of the vents
blanketed much of what is now the metropolitan Honolulu area with a fine black cinder
locally known as black sand

Mooheau Avenue from Kapahulu Avenue to Sixth Avenue
Mooheau Avenue is a two lane roadway located in the Kapahulu area of Oahu
The road alignment trends uphill toward the northeast from the intersection at Kapahulu

In general the existing asphalt concrete pavement was observed to be in fair
condition with a few significant areas of cracking related to underground utility
installation Based on our observations pavement deterioration appears to consist of
surficial longitudinal and alligator cracking with some isolated alligator block cracking
The more significant alligator block cracking was observed to correlate with locations
where buried utility trenches may exist however surface asphalt patching was not
observed at these locations The buried trench locations are reflected in the linear
crack patterns and weakly developed depressions observed at the pavement surface
as shown on Photograph No 1 Plate 4 The southwestern portion of the road
appeared to have sustained less cracking and fatigue than the greater sloping
northeastern portion of the road

relatively good surface drainage The ride characteristic of the pavement was generally
fair to good with a few noticeable surface irregularities

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should
be performed from an engineering point of view Existing pavements with minor distress
of low severity may be rehabilitated with an asphalt concrete AC overlay with or
without cold planing However reconstruction of the existing pavements would be
prudent for those areas where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the
underlying base subbase and or subgrade materials As a result the existing pavement
section requiring reconstruction should be completely removed to expose the underlying
subgrade soils and be replaced with a new pavement section Appropriate information
pertaining to the following areas would be required in order to provide input design
parameters for the pavement design and analyses

Methodology of Pavement Design
In general the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should
be performed Existing pavements with minor distresses of low severity are generally
rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold planing The procedures used in determining
the AC overlay thickness generally follow those provided by the State of Hawaii
Department of Transportation In the method outlined for AC overlays the strength of the
existing AC is evaluated according to the type and severity of the distresses observed
and is either reduced to that of asphalt concrete base ACB Option 1 Untreated Base
UTB Option 2 or remains unchanged Option 3
Reconstruction of the existing pavements is usually performed for those areas
where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base
subbase and or subgrade materials For the reconstruction option the existing
pavement sections requiring reconstruction should be completely removed and replaced
with a new pavement section The procedures used in determining the new pavement
section generally follow those described in Chapter 3 of the Draft Pavement Design
Manual prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation The design
procedures are based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the
California Department of Transportation

density of the same soil established in accordance with or
AASHTO T 180 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density If pumping conditions are
encountered during compaction of the subgrade soils the subgrade soils may be
stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping subgrade conditions
As a guide one sack of cement may be used for 50 square feet of subgrade area
The asphalt concrete base material should consist of asphalt treated basalt
aggregate placed in loose level lifts of approximately 6 inches in thickness and

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed anywhere during or after construction Development of
good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or
premature failure of the pavements
