INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a geotechnical engineering
evaluation performed for the proposed Bachelor Enlisted Quarters
Project P 976 at Subic Bay in Luzon Republic of the
Philippines
The general location of the project site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 and Site Plan Plate 2
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
The purpose of our exploration was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the project site to provide pertinent soil data such as
shear strength compressibility swelling potential and soil
classification for the site grading and foundation design of the proposed
Bachelor Enlisted Quarters BEQ structure and related site improvements
The scope of the investigation was performed in general accordance
with our proposal dated and included a review of
available aerial photographs geologic literature and previous soil
reports of the general area field reconnaissance drilling of seven 7
new borings and two 2 probings laboratory soils testing engineering
analysis of the field and laboratory data and report preparation
The seismic response study as described in our proposal of February
17 1987 was also performed for the project site The results of the
study are presented in a separate report
The logs of borings as well as the results of the field and
laboratory test data are presented in the appendices of this report

STTE CONDITIONS
The BEQ project site is located at the northern portion of the Naval
Station at Subic Bay in the Republic of the Philippines The principal
area included in this study is bounded by Binictican Drive to the south a
drainageway and unimproved high ground area to the north and east and a
lowlying unimproved area proposed Hospital site to the west
Almost the entire site is located within a lowlying marshy area which
is at about elevation 5 to 10 feet
Beyond the northerly and easterly boundaries of the site above
elevation 30 the ground rises steeply up toward the higher ground
hillside The elevations along the higher ground area vary from 30 to
more than 100 feet
The entire project area is densely vegetated with native and exotic
tropical and sub tropical vegetation This forested area provides a
habitat for a variety of native and feral animals including monkeys
pigs lizards and snakes
Several large boulders were noted at the toe of the steep hillsides
along the northern portions of the site
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located within the Western Volcanic Intrusive
Province of Luzon and comprises Recent to Pliocene Quaternary Volcanic
deposits The E W Trending San Antonio Fracture Zone is probably the
closest identified major fault zone about 8 to 10 miles northerly of the
site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1852 00 Page 6
Dk No 35
At the project site the volcanic deposits are primarily of
pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs volcanic breccia
agglomerate and volcanic mudflows lahar believed to have been
generated by explosive eruptions from Mount Natib Mount Santa Rita and
Mount Balakibok three of the major volcanic centers which comprise the
Zambales Bataan Volcanic Highlands
Examination of the geomorphology of the area leads to the conjecture
that the project site is located on a marine deltaic flat located at the
toe of a rugged mountain terrain The lowlying area was cut during low
stands of the sea by streams originating in the mountainous areas to the
east of the site
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass that produced soil deposits at various
levels both above and below the present sea level
The site exploration carried out revealed that the surface materials
in the lowlying portions of the study area are mainly of a low energy
depositional estuarine or lagoonal environment which would be encountered
in a sheltered bay area such as Subic Bay
Owing to the low energy estuarine or deltaic environment of
deposition these soft and highly compressible sediments would tend to be
coarser sand and gravel deposits close to the hillside and grade to
poorly consolidated clays and silts containing traces of organic matter
away from the hillside These soft deposits are underlain at depths by
more granular deposits of ash flow tuffs and mudflow debris from the
eruption of Mount Natib These deposits were probably contemporaneous
with the Pleistocene sea level changes

Soil Conditions
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by loose
estuarine deposits to a depth of about 20 to 30 feet below the existing
ground surface The estuarine deposits consisted primarily of silty sands
with generally less than 30 fines However the clay content in the soil
deposit increased with increased distance away from the hillside This
appears consistent with the finding at Boring 1 which has more clay than
those encountered in the other borings closer to the hillside
The loose silty sands were underlain by stiff residual soil over
lithic tuff rock formation at a depth of about 30 to 55 feet The tuff
was generally hard but did contain some softer weathered zones
Based on the information developed from the seven 7 borings and
geologic reconnaissance two generalized geologic soil profiles through
the site are presented on Plates 3 and 4
Groundwater
The borings in the lowlying swamp area of the site generally
encountered groundwater at about 3 to 4 feet elevation Generally this
represents the water level during dry weather A later visit to the site
following the start of the rainy season encountered a much higher water
level The measured depths to the groundwater from the existing ground
surface in each boring are shown on the logs of borings The groundwater
level will fluctuate due to seasonal monsoon rains and tidal influences

Building Siting
Early in the project design the siting of the BEQ Building was
discussed with the design team It was recognized that pile foundations
would be required due to the anticipated soft ground conditions at the
site Also it was anticipated that the soft soils would probably thin
out in the upslope direction Therefore if the building was situated
closer to the upslope area shorter pile foundations could probably be
used thereby reducing foundation costs On the other hand previous
experience and our geologic reconnaissance of the area disclosed that the
steep upper hillside areas may be prone to shallow landslides
Additionally large boulders were noted along the toe of the hillside
indicating the potential for future rockfalls
Based on the above discussion it was decided that the building
should be sited away from the hillside area to reduce the landslide and
rockfall hazard It was the design team s opinion that a building
location closer to Binictican Drive would provide a safer site although
slightly deeper piles would be required

Site Preparation
Prior to site clearance and surface vegetation removal we recommend
that off site drainage water around the lowlying marshy area should
first be diverted to permit the existing soft area to develop a
firmer soil crust
Clearing and grubbing should consist of removing the small trees and
brush above the ground surface Tree stumps and roots larger than
about 1 inch should also be removed Due to the very soft soil
condition we anticipate that most of the clearing would be done by
hand Light construction equipment could be used to perform the
grubbing provided a stabilization berm is placed first to support the
grubbing excavation and hauling equipments
The existing near surface marshy soil in the lower swampy areas

Support heavy construction equipment Therefore after clearing and

Geotechnical Instrumentation
We believe geotechnical instrumentation should be installed and
monitored by qualified field personnel at the site during the grading
operation The following are recommended
1
Inclinometers These instruments are installed to monitor
lateral movements of the underlying soft clays and fill
embankment during embankment filling They can help to determine
if the rate of filling at the site can be increased and also it
can provide early warning signs to possible slope instability
lateral ground movements and ground heaving below the embankment
fill
Piezometers The purpose of installing piezometers within the
soft soil is to monitor the rapid build up of pore pressure as
the fill lifts are being placed in order to avoid creating a
quick condition which can lead to slope instability It could
also help to indicate the degree of consolidation that has
occurred on the compressible layers

Liquefaction Evaluation
When a loose saturated sand deposit is subject to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough due to the large extent of the sand deposit to
dissipate the build up in pore pressure the effective stress internal
strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibration the pore
water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to act as a
viscous fluid This phenomenon is called liquefaction The major factors
affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain size distribution Fine and uniform sands are more prone
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial relative density Loose sands are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential decreases with increased
relative density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1852 00 Page 18
Dk No 35
3 Magnitude and duration of vibration Liquefaction potential
increases with increases in the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
Our borings disclosed that the project site is underlain at shallow
depth by a layer of very loose silty fine sand The thickness of this
layer varies between 20 to 30 feet As discussed in our seismic site
response study the Bachelor Enlisted Quarters structure will be designed
to resist a design earthquake which has a 50 probability of excedence in
50 years This design earthquake has been determined to have a probable
magnitude of 6 5 on the Richter Scale Because of the very shallow water
table almost at the ground surface and the extremely loose consistency
of the sand layer we believe that it is very likely that during a strong
earthquake M 6 5 occurring at close distance to the site 10 to 20
miles this layer of loose sand will liquefy

Relatively high column loads are anticipated for the 3 story concrete
building Additionally the subsoils were found to be very loose
Therefore pile foundations are recommended for the new BEQ structure
The piles should extend through the newly placed fill and soft deposits
and bear on the hard tuff formation Based on our borings the
anticipated pile length varies from about 50 to 70 feet with an average
cast length of about 60 feet

The piles should be driven after the site fill has been placed first
to its finish grade and allowed to settle Therefore the piles must
penetrate through the compacted new fill layers We anticipate that
pre drilling through the fill will be required
If the site is subjected to a strong earthquake settlement of the
loose sand may occur The downward movement would result in negative skin
friction forces generally known as downdrag force on the pile shaft
The magnitude of the downdrag force generally depends on the thickness of
compressible layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the pile
perimeter area At the lowlying area of the project site the average
downdrag force is estimated to be on the order of 30 tons per pile
Calculations indicate that a 14 inch square prestressed concrete pile
bearing into the moderately hard tuff rock may be designed for 100 ton
total load carrying capacity However after taking the downdrag force
into account the net allowable pile supporting capacity is reduced to 70
tons each
Pile Driving
The piles should be driven with a hammer with a maximm rated energy
of at least lbs The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control so that driving within the soft deposits can be
achieved at a reduced energy level to minimize the tensile stress
within the pile which could cause damage to the pile
The recommended pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The piles should be casted in one continuous section
2 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for
each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity
modified when the driving process is interrupted for more than 4
hours
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1852 00 Page 21
Dk No 35
During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft
soil the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled reduced
energy so that excessive tensile stresses are not transmitted to
the pile
The piles must be driven to the bedrock layer which is located
about 30 to 55 feet from the ground surface within the lowlying
project area
The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts are
steadily increasing with depth and reaching 10 blows per inch for
each of the last 3 inches or aggregated blow counts of 120 blows
for the last foot
This criteria is based on the use of a diesel driving hammer with
a rated energy of lbs to drive a 14 inch square
concrete pile with a design load of 100 tons This driving
criteria should be modified according to the actual rated energy
of the hammer used

To examine the drivability of piles with the use of one or a few
selected hammers
to determine if a driving shoe is required when the pile is
driven to the stiff to hard tuff layer
to verify if pre drilling through the upper fill layer is
required

It is our understanding that drainage channels will be constructed
around the site to intercept surface runoff

In light of the anticipated poor subsoil condition thick layer of
compressible material in the lower portion of the site and the design
difficulties for a rectangular concrete channel under earthquake
condition we recommend that the channel be designed with sloping banks
covered with riprap We believe the riprap design can provide the
following advantages over the concrete channel
1 The riprap channel is less likely to be damaged due to settlement
of the underlying soil
2 The use of riprap embankment eliminates the need of a heavily
reinforced channel wall to resist high lateral soil and
earthquake loading
3 The cost of repair to the riprap embankment in case of damage
will be less than that for a concrete channel
Portions of the new channel will be located in the very soft swampy
soils Due to stability considerations we recommend that the side
slopes be constructed at a slope ratio of 2 1 H V or flatter
In soft ground areas a filter fabric should be placed over the slope
prior to placement of the riprap The riprap should be carefully
placed to minimize the voids between rocks If the riprap is
grouted sufficient openings should be left in the facing to allow
free drainage of water through the riprap

Utility Construction
In order to reduce damages to the underground utilities due to
excessive ground settlement we recommend that utility line construction
be delayed as much as possible until the majority of the ground
settlements due to site filling has been completed The settlement gauge
readings should be utilized to determine when the utility construction
could be initiated
Utility Trench Backfill
To minimize the required compactive effort and possible damage to the
pipe the backfill around and up to 1 foot above the pipe should
consist of clean granular material such as sand and fine gravel with
less than 5 fines lightly tamped or jetted as appropriate The
remaining portion of the upper utility trench backfill should be
performed in accordance with the recommendations presented under the

Settlement of Utility Line
We estimate post construction utility line settlements of up to about
1 inch may occur The majority of this settlement is a result of
secondary consolidation of the underlying clayey material In light
of this amount of settlement we recommend that the utility line be
designed with flexible joints to accommodate this amount of
settlement in the future
We recommend sufficient gradient be provided for the utility lines
which will be buried within the lowlying marshy subsoil area
Proper type of flexible utility line connections should be considered
particularly at the connection to the structures on piles and
transition between marshy fill and upper stiff grounds

APPENDIX B
The moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit determinations
were performed on selected sample as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of the moisture content and
unit weight tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The Atterberg Limits are summarized on Plate B 9
In light of the fact that substantial amount of granular materials
were encountered during our soil investigation an extensive sieve
analysis program was performed Altogether 36 sieve analyses were
performed The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 8
Four hydrometer tests were performed to determine whether the fines
in the loose sands were predominantly clay or silt to aid in the
liquefaction analysis The test results are presented on Plates B 10
through B 13
Two direct shear tests were performed on samples of the subsoils
The purpose of the tests were to determine the shear strength parameter of
the soil The test results are presented on Plates B 14 and B 15
Two unconsolidated undrained triaxial shear tests were performed on
the soil samples from the loose sand layer The purpose of the tests was
to evaluate the shear strength parameters of the soils The test results
are presented on Plates B 16 and B 17
One consolidation test was performed on a sample obtained from the
loose layer The purpose of the test was to evaluate the settlement
characteristics The results of the test are presented on Plate B 18
