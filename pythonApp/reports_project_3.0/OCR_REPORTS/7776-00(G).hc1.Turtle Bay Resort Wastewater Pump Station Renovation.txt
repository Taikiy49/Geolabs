REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island.  It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still 
active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu.

During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the 
sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and 
induration of the calcareous reef materials.  
The project site lies on the Kahuku Coastal Plain and the pediment of the northern 
end of the Koolau Mountain Range. The project area is generally classified from a 
geological standpoint as ‘Qa’ for Quaternary Alluvium (Sinton, et al.) which consists of 
alluvial soils deposited since the Pleistocene Epoch.  Experience at the Turtle Bay Resort 
complex indicates that this alluvial material forms a thin mantle over residuum from 
Koolau Basalt at the southern side of the resort and over coralline and dune sand deposits 
to the north of Kamehameha Highway. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is situated at and next to the existing main wastewater pump 
station, near the northern end of Kuilima Drive, of the Turtle Bay Resort in the Kahuku 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The existing main wastewater pump station is a 
one-story building of approximately 18 feet by 22 feet in plan dimension.  The wastewater 
pump station is enclosed with existing CMU perimeter walls on the northerly and easterly 
sides and with wooden fences on the southerly and westerly sides.  The proposed 
emergency generator pad will be located on the southerly side of the pump station, which 
is currently covered with grasses or gravel. Based on our field observations, the project 
site is relatively flat with existing ground elevations between about +12 and +15 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL).

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the existing main wastewater pump station will 
be renovated in anticipation of the proposed development of the Turtle Bay Resort 
Improvements – Phase 1 project.  Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered 
during our field exploration, we recommend supporting the emergency generator with 
concrete slabs-on-grade foundations. Detailed discussions and recommendations for 
foundation design and earthworks are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Foundation System 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread footing/concrete slabs-on-grade (reinforced concrete pad) foundation to 
support the proposed emergency generator. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the concrete pad bearing on recompacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills needed to achieve the design finished grades. The bearing value 
recommended is for dead-plus-live-loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of the pad should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent exterior grade.  Foundations located next to retaining walls, other 
foundations, utility trenches, or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench (or retaining 
wall). Alternatively, the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the 
utility lines or bottom of the retaining wall.

Our field exploration and experience in the area indicate that the near-surface silty 
and clayey soils may exhibit low to moderate shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents.  To reduce the potential for appreciable structural 
distress resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly preparing the 
subgrade soils prior to fill placement and/or construction of improvements on top. In addition, 
we recommend providing a minimum 6 inches of non-expansive select granular fill materials 
below the concrete slabs-on-grade. The non-expansive, select granular fill should also 
extend beyond the perimeter of the pad a minimum of 12 inches. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material such as crushed 
coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no larger than 3 inches in its largest dimension. The material should also 
contain between 10 and 30 percent fines (particles passing the No. 200 sieve). Select 
granular fill should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more 
and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
We recommend properly preparing the footing subgrades by scarifying to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where soft or 
yielding ground conditions are encountered at the footing subgrades, the footing subgrades 
should be over-excavated until firm materials are encountered or to a maximum depth of 
about 2 feet deep and backfilled with compacted select granular materials (3-inch minus). 
The select granular material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. As an alternative, the footings 
may be extended down to bear directly on the firm materials. 
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend 
thoroughly moistening the soils to close the cracks or preparing the subgrades again. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and 
poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than 
1 inch.  
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by a combination of frictional 
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing material and by passive 
earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the 
footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on compacted 
select granular fill materials. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated 
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). The 
passive earth pressure value assumes the concrete footings will be cast neatly against the

Existing Pump Housing 
Based on the information provided, we understand it is desired to abandon the 
existing pump housing in place.  Any liquid or sludge in the existing pump housing should 
be properly removed and disposed of and all mechanical equipment in the pump housing 
should also be properly removed and salvaged prior to backfilling.  In addition, any opening 
or conduits should be plugged and sealed as necessary to prevent CLSM (Controlled Low 
Strength Material) from flowing into the opening or conduits. 
The empty pump housing may then be backfilled with properly compacted select 
granular fill materials, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.   As an alternative to placing and compacting select granular 
fill materials in lifts inside the confined space of the pump housing, flowable fills consisting 
of CLSM can be used.  The CLSM should be proportioned to produce a backfill material that 
is self-compacting and able to be excavated with conventional excavation equipment.  The 
28-day compressive strength of CLSM should be between 50 to 150 pounds per square 
inch (psi). 
Utility Trench 
We envision that new electrical duct and waterlines will be required for the proposed 
project. In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or No. 3B Fine 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the utility lines.  If soft/loose 
soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an 
additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N 
or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as clean sand or No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes, and to reduce 
the compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is critical

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines

subsidence. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to the maximum dry 
density.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one boring, 
designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 52.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The 
Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the 
Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for 
Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-1.1 and A-1.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Boring indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Boring at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentation of the test results is provided 
on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a remolded sample to evaluate the 
swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-3. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on selected in-situ soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
clayey soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plate B-4. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on selected sample to evaluate 
the shear strength characteristics of the sandy materials tested.  The test results are 
presented on Plate B-5.
