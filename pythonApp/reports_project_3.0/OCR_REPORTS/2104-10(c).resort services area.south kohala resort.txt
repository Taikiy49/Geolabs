subsurface soils and rock conditions at the proposed site in order to

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The South Kohala Resort project site is located in the Ouli section
of the District of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii and encompasses
approximately 485 acres The site is divided into two areas by the Queen
Kaahumanu Highway The mauka area is bordered to the north by Waiulaula
Gulch to the south and east by raw undeveloped land and to the west by
Queen Kaahumanu Highway The makai area is bordered to the north by the
golf course of the existing Mauna Kea Beach Hotel to the south by Hapuna
State Beach Park to the west by the northern part of Hapuna Bay and to
the east by Queen Kaahumanu Highway
The terrain is generally rolling with some steep slopes locally
approaching a ratio of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V The
terrain typically reflects the original surface of thick basaltic lava
flows in the A a form The ground surface elevations range from about sea
level to approximately 600 feet above sea level with drainage trending
towards the west
The site is vegetated with a thin cover of dry grasses with kiawe
algaroba trees occurring in the lower areas near Hapuna Bay and in
drainageways
Most of the ground surface is covered with basaltic cobbles and
gravel mantling an ashy soil There are localized exposures of basalt
clinker and rock formation
The proposed Resort Services Area is situated in the mauka portion of
the South Kohala Resort site to the south of an existing open reservoir
The area is raw undeveloped land vegetated with a thin cover of ary
grasses

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the South Kohala Resort project site into a
planned resort residential and recreational development The resort
portion of the development is currently planned to be a hotel facility in
the makai portion of the site on a low bluff overlooking Hapuna Bay The
remainder of the makai area is planned as golf course It is our
understanding that the mauka area is planned primarily for residential and
golf course development with other recreational facilities and maintenance
support within the area
It is proposed to develop the Resort Services Area as a central
location for support services and other facilities such as administration
offices a sewage treatment plant grounds maintenance yard buildings and
a small water reservoir A preliminary layout of the proposed structures
is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Preliminary plans indicate proposed grading consisting of cuts of up
to about ten 10 feet deep and fills of up to about ten 10 feet thick
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene
Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally
referred to as Pahala Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from
Pahala Ash found in other portions of the island of Hawaii in that the
in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists ina ary
friable state
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2104 10 C Page 5
The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has
resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the
site This desert pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel
which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away
At and in the area of the project site the lava flows are in the A a
form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of
fragmental clinker underlain by a dense core of massive basalt which
frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of clinker below the
massive core The clinker surface of the lavas at the site have been
mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed above
In parts of the main drainageways of the site the ash soil and
clinker have been stripped by erosion exposing the underlying dense rock
Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the proposed project site were explored
by the drilling and sampling of three 3 borings to depths ranging
between 16 0 and 20 0 feet below the existing ground surface Our
exploration encountered a surface layer approximately two 2 feet thick
of brown silt Pahala Ash intermixed with gravel and cobble sized basalt
fragments Clinker Below this surface layer our borings encountered
in situ volcanic formation consisting of interbedded dense basalt and
Clinker to the maximm depths explored
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during our
exploration
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration along with the boring
logs are presented in Appendix A of this report

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that
development of the proposed Resort Services Area is feasible from a
geotechnical engineering point of view The dense clinker and competent
basalt formation encountered at the site should provide adequate bearing
for the proposed facilities With the exception of the ash soil which
should be stockpiled for use as topsoil excavated volcanic materials
should provide a suitable source of fill material if reduced to less than
12 inches in size
Site Grading
It appears that the site grading work may consist of cuts and fills
geneally less than 10 feet in height
Prior to commencement of grading surface vegetation and organic
matter within the grading limits should be cleared and grubbed The
resulting spoils should be disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for
later use in landscaping
Ash soils generally have relatively low strength therefore it is
our recommendation that the ash soil be removed from fill roadway and
structural areas The ash soil may be either stockpiled for use as
topsoil or thoroughly blended into rockfill material as discussed below
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture
conditioning of fill material will be necessary It is also advisable to
implement a dust control program during grading operations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2104 10 C Page 7
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below
any areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to firm
natural material and the resulting depression backfilled with well
compacted fill
In general the excavated soil and rock materials if reduced to less
than 12 inches in size may be suitable as fill material The material
if reduced to less than six 6 inches in size should be adequate as a
source of trench and structural backfill if needed Imported material
if required should conform to the requirements stipulated in Section 30
of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Select
Borrow for Subbase Course dated September 1986
Consideration should be given to mobilizing a crusher to the site to
aid in reducing the size of large boulders and fragments It is possible
that portions of the dense ledges could yield aggregate quality material
if properly segregated and crushed
Alternately large boulders or fragments ranging from 18 to 24
inches in size could be buried in the deepest portions of the fills
provided that they are not nested and an adequate choking layer of 18 inch
minimum thickness is used to cap the rockfill to reduce the potential of
loss of fines from the upper portion of the fill The choking layer
should consist of well graded 6 inch minus granular material Care must
be exercised to avoid placement of any boulders in locations which would
conflict with future foundations or utility lines In general boulder or
rock fragments larger than 24 inches in size should be placed at depths
greater than four 4 feet below the finish subgrade level
The excavated rock material may not contain sufficient fines to
produce a well graded material therefore the surficial ash soil should
be mixed into the excavated rock material to increase the percentage of
fines and to create a better graded fill material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2104 10 C Page 8
Generally fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve
12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimm of 90 maximm
dry density as established by ASIM Test Method D 1557 78 Trench and
structural backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimm of 90 maximm
dry density per ASIM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet of
fills and backfills below road subgrade level should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 maximm dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills
composed of excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to
determine the number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the
desired level of compaction should be conducted at the start of the
grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing program the
mumber of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate
compaction
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than 5 1
horizontal to vertical should be keyed and benched into the underlying
consolidated clinker or basalt formation to lessen potential instability
of the new fill see Plate 3 The keys and benches should not be based
in the upper ash soil or in loose clinker
Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site and in
its vicinity indicate that the site is most likely underlain by dense
clinker with ledges of massive dense basalt at relatively shallow depths
It is anticipated that most of the ash and clinker materials may be
conventionally excavated by ripping Some hard excavation may be
encountered in well cemented areas or when large lava balls boulders or
ledges are encountered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2104 10 C Page 9
The underlying basalt formation will most likely require hard
excavation such as heavy ripping hoerams or possibly blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor
Blasting should be considered if it will substantially expedite the
excavation process However care should be exercised to avoid
overshooting which could disrupt the clinker or rock formation resulting
in a loss of strength
Slopes
Provided that granular materials 12 inch minus are utilized design
fill slopes of 2 1 H V are recommended
For cut slopes the following recommended slope ratios may be used
a Ash Soil 3 1 H V
c Dense Basalt 1 1 H V
Any cuts or fills greater than 20 feet in height should have a bench
at the midpoint of the slope
Surface water should be diverted away from the tops of Slopes to
reduce erosion of the slope face
Foundation Support
It is our opinion that spread or strip footing foundations may be
used to support the proposed structures It is anticipated that
foundations will bear on either in situ basalt rock formation on
rockfill or on clinker
An allowable bearing capacity of up to 5 000 p s f may be used for
the design of foundations bearing on in situ basalt rock formation or
dense rockfill clinker in level areas These values may be increased by
one third when temporary wind and seismic loads are considered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2104 10 C Page 10
In order to provide uniform support for the foundations and to reduce
the potential of foundation distresses resulting from voids which may
exist at the footing subgrades we recommend that the footing excavations
be over excavated one 1 foot below subgrade level The over excavated
area should be backfilled with well graded granular material placed in
8 inch loose horizontal lifts and compacted to a minimm of 95 of its
maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of two 2 feet below the
lowest adjacent finished grade Foundations located on sloping areas
should have a minimm set back of eight 8 feet from the outside edge of
the footing to the adjacent slope face This may require additional
embedment to achieve the proper set back distance
Lateral Pressures
Lateral loads on the proposed structures may be resisted by a passive
resistance of up to 300 p s f per foot of depth of the buried vertical
foundation faces in compacted fills A lateral bearing pressure of up to
3 000 p s f can be used for foundations embedded in dense basalt
formation A frictional coefficient of 0 45 may be used to calculate
resistance to sliding

III Site Preparation
A
Clearing and grubbing shall be performed and debris shall be
disposed of in accordance with Section 10 CLEARING AND
GRUBBING of the Standard Public Works Specifications
All vegetation and deleterious materials encountered within the
Grading Limits shall be cleared and disposed of off site This
removal shall be completed prior to excavating and filling
Organically contaminated topsoil and ash soil shall be stripped
prior to actual grading Materials determined as being
unsuitable for placement in compacted fill shall be removed and
wasted off site Any material incorporated as a part of the
compacted fill must be approved by the Geotechnical Engineer
Any abandoned underground structures such as cesspools
cisterns tunnels septic tanks wells pipelines or others not
located prior to grading are to be removed and the resulting
depression backfilled and compacted in accordance with these
specifications
Loose surface soils encountered in areas to receive fill shall
be removed to expose competent natural soils Localized soft
pockets shall be undercut and backfilled with compacted select
materials
All surfaces shall be observed and approved by the Geotechnical
Engineer prior to the placement of any fill
After approval the surface shall be scarified to a depth of six
inches brought to near optimum moisture content and compacted
as herein specified

V
Placement and Compaction of Fill
A
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers which when
compacted will not exceed twelve inches 12 in thickness
Each layer shall be spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain
uniformity of material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer
which has not been properly compacted Subsequent removal of
any unacceptable layer shall be done at the Contractor s
expense
All fill shall be compacted to a minimm of 90 percent of the
maximm dry density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
unless otherwise specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s
Report
Rock fragments or boulders larger than twelve 12 in diameter
may be utilized provided they are not concentrated in any one
area and that there is sufficient fine grained material to fill
the voids between the rocks
Boulders ranging in size from 18 to 24 inches encountered in
excavations may be used in the deeper fills provided that the
following are followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested
together They should be placed so that compaction
equipment will be able to compact around them

2 No part of the boulder should encroach any closer to
finished subgrade than the boulder s largest dimension and
in no case be closer than 4 feet to finished subgrade
3 No boulder larger than 5 feet in its largest dimension
should be placed in the fill It may be necessary to reduce
the size of larger boulders or dispose of them off site
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the
proposed utility alignments to prevent difficulty in the
later excavation of the utility trenches
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in
excavated volcanic rock material due to the large particle
size Instead a testing program consisting of visual
observations and test strips to determine the lift thickness and
number of passes of a compactor required to achieve the desired
compaction should be conducted at the start of the project
The number of passes can then be used as the criteria for
compaction
The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or
pneumatic tire rollers suited to the soil type and capable of
producing the specified results in an expedient and efficient
manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
The compaction shall be accomplished in a systematic and uniform
manner over the entire fill area to produce a uniformly
compacted fill
The Contractor shall be required to obtain the above specified
levels of compaction out to the finish slope of fill slopes
This may be achieved by either overbuilding the slope and

VI Unforeseen Subsurface Conditions
A If any conditions not described in the contract documents such
as perched water seepage lenticular or confined strata of a
potentially adverse nature are encountered during grading these
conditions shall be immediately brought to the attention of the
Project Engineers so that supplemental recommendations may be
made to treat these problems
B Should excavations encounter loose or unstable conditions the
Contractor shall notify the Geotechnical Engineer and Project
Civil Engineer immediately so that supplemental recommendations
can be given
