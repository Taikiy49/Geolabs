SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings for the project generally encountered 15 to 16 inches of Portland 
Cement Concrete (PCC) pavement underlain by 10 to 18 inches of the aggregate base 
material. Borings drilled on the asphalt paved surfaces generally encountered Asphaltic 
Concrete (AC) or Asphaltic Treated Base (ATB) with thicknesses ranging between 4 and 
8 inches underlain by about 8 to 10.5 inches of the aggregate base material. It should be 
noted that Boring No. 207 was the only boring to encounter PCC underlain by AC/ATB 
and aggregate base material. 
The pavement structure generally was underlain by fills, alluvium, and coralline 
deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of about 12 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. The fill soils generally consisted of medium dense to very dense sands 
and gravel with varying silt content and very stiff clayey silts. The alluvial soils generally 
consisted of stiff to very stiff silts and clays. The coralline deposits generally consisted of 
medium dense silty sands and medium hard to hard coral formation. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration except for Boring Nos. 210 
and 211, where groundwater was encountered at depths of about 11 and 9.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface, respectively. It should be noted that groundwater levels are 
subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, storm surge conditions, seasonal rainfall, time 
of year and other factors. 
The anticipated aircraft traffic forecast was not available at the time this report was 
prepared; therefore, a mix of aircrafts, including Boeing B717, Airbus A321neo and 
Airbus A330, were assumed in our analysis. Based on our pavement design analysis, the 
aircraft controlling the new apron pavement thickness was the Airbus A330 and was, 
therefore, selected as the primary design aircraft in our analysis. 
Based on the estimated design aircraft traffic mix and the subgrade conditions 
encountered at the site, the new rigid pavement section should consist of 16.0 inches of 
PCC underlain by 6 inches of CTB placed over a recompacted subgrade. The new flexible 
pavement section should consist of 6 inches of AC underlain by 5 inches of ATB placed 
over a recompacted subgrade. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the 
majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became extinct while 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lava. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its geomorphology 
and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea 
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles 
of continental glaciation. As the continental glaciers advanced, less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea level fell below its present stand. When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea level 
rose to above its present level. 
Glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and 
deposition. The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal 
deposits, and induration of the calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial 
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under 
relatively high-energy conditions.

During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial delta and fans seaward by deposition of alluvium over the previously 
deposited lagoonal sediments. 
The geology of the project area has been further complicated by the deposition of 
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of vents of the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series, which included the nearby Aliamanu-Salt Lake-Makalapa vent. These 
eruptions were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations. 
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy 
estuarine or lagoonal environments, resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments 
along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways. Urban 
development and reclamation projects have since altered the natural formation of the 
area. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located within the Daniel K. Inouye International Airport in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Gates A13 through B5 are generally located at 
the west end of the airport in Terminal 1, as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the existing concrete apron pavement at Gates A13 
through A20 consisted of about 15 to 16 inches of Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) 
pavement underlain by about 10 to 18 inches of the aggregate base material. The 
remaining borings generally encountered AC or ATB with thicknesses ranging between 
4 and 8 inches underlain by about 8 to 10.5 inches of the aggregate base material. It 
should be noted that Boring No. 207 was the only boring to encounter PCC underlain by 
AC/ATB and aggregate base material. The pavement structure generally was underlain 
by fills, alluvium, and coralline deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 12 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
Based on the design aircraft traffic mix and the subgrade conditions encountered 
at the site, the new rigid and flexible pavement sections should consist of the following: 
Sixteen inches of PCC underlain by 6 inches of cement-treated base (CTB) 
placed over a recompacted subgrade. 
Six inches of AC underlain by 5 inches of ATB placed over a recompacted 
subgrade. 
Several jet fuel system pipelines are present at each gate to provide fuel to 
incoming aircraft. To reduce the potential for distress and damage to the fuel lines during 
placement and compaction of the cement-treated base, consideration may be given to 
replacing the stabilized cement-treated base layer (P-304 – Cement-Treated Base) with 
lean concrete subbase (P-306 – Lean Concrete Subbase).

3.2 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural sections presented herein were determined based on the 
methodology described in Chapter 3 – Pavement Design of the Advisory Circular 
No. 150/5320-6G. The design procedure is based on a layered elastic design and 
three-dimensional finite element-based structural analysis. This design method is 
computationally intense and is thus in the form of a computer program called 
FAARFIELD. The results of the Federal Aviation Administration FAARFIELD 2.0 Section 
Report are presented in Appendix C. 
In accordance with the design guidelines provided in the Advisory Circular, 
Section 3.5.1, if the aircraft in the design traffic mixtures have gross loads of 
100,000 pounds or more, use of a stabilized base is required. Therefore, our pavement 
sections included a layer of stabilized base (Item P-304 – Cement-Treated Base). 
It should be noted that several jet fuel system pipelines are present at each gate 
to provide fuel to incoming aircraft. Based on available information, the invert and existing 
quality of these lines are unknown and may be within close proximity to the pavement 
section subgrade elevation. To reduce the potential for distress and damage to the fuel 
lines during placement and compaction of the cement-treated base, consideration may 
be given to replacing the stabilized cement-treated base layer (P-304 – Cement-Treated 
Base) with a lean concrete base (P-306 – Lean Concrete Base).

particles greater than 3 inches in the largest dimension. Reuse of the excavated on-site 
materials as a source of Select Fill may require segregation of the materials during 
earthwork construction. If the excavated on-site soils are very moist to wet, the soils likely 
will require aeration to reduce the moisture content prior to being reused as a source of 
fill or backfill materials to achieve the specified compaction. 
The Select Granular Fill used should consist of soil materials with a maximum 
particle size of 3 inches or less with sufficient fines (between 10 and 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve) to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In 
addition, the Select Granular Fill materials should have a CBR value of 30 or greater and 
a swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883 compacted at 
95 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
11 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 201 through 211, extending to depths 
approximately between 8.5 and 12 feet below the existing ground surface. Five bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, were collected 
as composite samples generated from similar soil cuttings from no more than two borings 
per each bulk sample. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock Samples were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate 
A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs 
of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits to aid in soil classifications. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-1. 
Eleven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the near-surface soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 through B-4. 
Three One-Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under 
different surcharge loads.  The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B-5. 
Six laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on selected bulk soil samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 through B-11. 
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 B) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship.  The test results are presented on Plates B-12 through B-14.
