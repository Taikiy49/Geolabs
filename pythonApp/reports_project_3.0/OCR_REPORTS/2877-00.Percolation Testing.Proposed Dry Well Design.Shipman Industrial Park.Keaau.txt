PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the Shipman Industrial Park of Keaau District of Puna
on the island of Hawaii It is proposed to develop the site for building material
warehousing and sales
It is proposed to construct dry wells to dispose of storm run off generated by
paved areas and surrounding buildings at the project site
Our current scope of work specifically excludes geotechnical engineering for design
of site preparation or building foundations Our scope also excludes preparation of permit
applications for Underground Injection Control permits for the dry wells

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in Keaau at the Shipman Industrial Park District of
Puna on the island of Hawaii
At the time of percolation testing the site was undeveloped cleared and nearly
level Site grading appeared to have been performed at a previous date Temporary
drainage pits had been excavated along the perimeter of the property to aid in the
disposal of storm water run off The existing surface material exposed on the site
consisted of basalt rock with no soil cover
SUBSURFACE SOIL CONDITIONS
Test Pits Nos 1 and 2 are drainage pits which had been previously excavated for
the disposal of storm run off They were deepened by an additional 2 to 3 feet as a part
of this effort for the performance of the percolation tests Test Pit No 3 was excavated
solely for the performance of percolation testing under this effort
All three test pits penetrated volcanic clinker formation A a Generally the
material consisted of loose to medium dense sandy gravel with blocks of dense to very
dense basalt rock There appeared to be a greater amount of fines and sandy material
in Test Pits Nos 1 and 2 This may have resulted from the fact that these pits were
excavated at an earlier date therefore being exposed to siltation from the introduction of
storm run off The logs of the test pits are presented on Plate A 1
The ground surface elevation at the site at the time of percolation testing was
approximately 330 feet above Mean Sea Level Groundwater was not encountered in any
of the test pits at the time of testing

PERCOLATION TESTING
Field percolation tests were performed in the test pits by the falling head method
In addition a short term constant head test was conducted in Test Pit No 3 The results
are presented in Plates B 1 thru B 3
The procedure involved pre wetting the lower one to two feet of each pit prior to
the introduction of clear water for the performance of the test Water was introduced into
the pits via both gravity flow and the use of a pump from an 8000 gallon capacity water
wagon supplied by the client
FINDINGS AND CONCLUSIONS
General
Based on our observation of the test pits and the percolation test results it
appears that it is feasible to utilize dry wells to dispose of storm water run off
Based on our field testing and calculations the materials encountered in Test Pit
Nos 1 and 2 can absorb about 0 24 gallons per minute and 0 35 gallons per minute per
square foot of wetted surface area respectively Our calculations indicate that Test Pit
No 3 is capable of absorbing about 3 0 gallons per minute per square foot of wetted
surface area
Dry Well Design
It is our opinion that since they had previously been used for drainage the values
obtained from Test Pit Nos 1 and 2 represent a worst case design scenario i e the
formation has been partially clogged by material introduced by the storm water run off

flows The value obtained from Test Pit No 3 is believed to be more representative of the
clinker formation
Therefore it appears that a new dry well having a diameter of eight 8 feet and
a depth of twenty 20 feet could handle a run off flow of about 3 4 cubic feet per second
This dry well would also provide a temporary storage capacity of about 1 000 cubic feet
As noted from the tests conducted in Test Pit Nos 1 and 2 the infiltration capacity
of the dry wells will decrease with time The rate of decrease in capacity is generally a
function of how clean the run off water is when it enters the well
The materials encountered in the test pits generally consisted of loose to medium
dense clinker material There may be a tendency for this material to unravel and slough
in an open dry well Therefore we recommend that a lining be installed in the portions
of the proposed dry wells that penetrate clinker material
Regulatory Considerations
Dry wells are regulated as injection wells under the State of Hawaii Department of
Health Administrative rules Chapter 11 23 Underground Injection Control UIC Under
the UIC program separate permits are needed to construct and to operate injection wells
The permitting procedure is a two stage process requiring an application for an approval
to construct followed by testing of the wells and submittal of an application in the form
of an engineering report for a permit to operate the wells

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as test pits Variations of conditions
between the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of these variations
may not become evident until operation of the dry well If variations then appear evident
it will be necessary to re evaluate the findings presented in this report
The location and elevation of the field data points were determined by others We
can not assume responsibility for the accuracy of the location and or elevation of the test
pits
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field data points
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a
gradual transition
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the time given on the
graphic representations and or in the text and appendices of this report This data has
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it
must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature
and other factors
