SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by man-made 
fill, consisting of hard clayey silts and silty clays overlying alluvial and residual soils 
consisting of very stiff to hard clayey silts extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend 
utilizing shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new building 
structures planned at the project site. The shallow foundations should bear on the in place 
fill soils after recompaction or new compacted fills if required to achieve the finished 
grades. A net allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,500 psf may be used in the design of 
the shallow foundations. 
Laboratory tests indicate the on-site clayey soils exhibit low to moderate swelling 
and shrinkage potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential 
for distress to the future slabs-on-grade resulting from the swelling of the on-site clayey 
soils, we recommend providing at least 6 inches of non-expansive select granular material 
below the slab cushion fill. The non-expansive select granular material does not need to 
extend below the building foundations. 
A flexible pavement section consisting of 3 inches of asphaltic concrete (AC) placed 
on 6 inches of aggregate base course over 6 inches of subbase course may be used for 
design of the new POV parking lot area.  The AC thickness for roadway, driveways and 
trash pickup areas should be increased to 4 inches. 
Sidewalks supporting vehicular traffic, including driveway aprons, should have a 
minimum slab thickness of 7 inches over 6 inches of subbase course. 
The excavated subgrades underneath pavement areas should be scarified to a 
depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base course 
material should be moisture-conditioned to near the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 100 percent 
relative compaction.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Combat Aviation Brigade Complex, Phase 2 project within Wheeler 
Army Air Field in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations, 
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, pavements, and earthwork only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle 
to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  
The project site is located on the Schofield Plateau which is a broad plain 
situated between the two volcanic massifs.  The Schofield Plateau was formed when 
lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes of the 
Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips of the lava beds generally range 
from 3 to 5 degrees. The lava flows on the plateau have weathered to depths of about 
50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the residual soils. In some areas, 
alluvial deposits have buried the volcanic materials and deeply weathered into residual 
type soils.  The alluvium derived soils are characterized by their brown and reddish brown 
colors and somewhat higher clay content.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the building area of the project site were explored 
by drilling and sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending 
to depths of about 15.0 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Two bulk 
samples, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained from the near-surface 
materials to evaluate their swelling potential and pavement support characteristics. The 
approximate locations of the borings drilled and near-surface bulk samples collected are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In addition, a total of 15 borehole infiltration tests and two Double Ring 
Infiltrometer tests were conducted to aid in evaluating the subsurface infiltration 
characteristics of the soils at the project site.  The borehole infiltration tests were 
conducted in boreholes drilled and sampled to the depth of about 10 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  Solid PVC casing was installed in the boreholes flush with the 
bottom of the borehole; and, the bottom of the borehole was cleaned using a clean-out 
auger.  The cased boreholes were then pre-soaked overnight before a falling head 
infiltration test was conducted for an 8-hour duration.  The locations of the borehole 
infiltration tests, designated as I-1 through I-15 and I-21, are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The Double Ring Infiltrometer tests, designated as DRI-1 and DRI-2, were 
conducted in the bottom of the detention basin at the location of the future parking lot.  
The tests were performed on the floor of the basin at about 13 feet below the proposed 
finished grade; and were performed in general accordance with ASTM Test Method 
D3385 except that the duration of the test was extended to 8 hours as required in the 
project specifications.  These test locations are also shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2012 Edition (IBC 2012), the project 
site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to 
be addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and soil 
profile type for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and

To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes

2.4.2 Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design 
Our field exploration generally encountered very stiff to hard clayey silts extending 
to the maximum depth explored of approximately 16.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Borings at other sites in the Wheeler-Schofield-Wahiawa area 
indicate that these soils extend to depths of over 50 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered below the foundation subgrade 
level and the geologic setting of the area, we anticipate the project site may be 
classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as a “Stiff Soil Profile” corresponding 
to a Site Class D soil profile based on the International Building Code 
(Table No. 1613.3.5), 2012 Edition.

3.1 
Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend utilizing a shallow foundation system to support the new six-story buildings 
planned for the project. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,500 psf may be used 
to design the shallow foundations bearing on the stiff to hard on-site silty and clayey 
soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing value 
is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of the perimeter footings should be embedded a minimum 
of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade to reduce the potential for 
excessive water infiltration into the footing subgrade soils from adjacent landscape 
areas. For the interior footings, the footing embedment may be reduced to a minimum of 
18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footing embedment for the 
one-story miscellaneous structures may be reduced to a minimum of 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade. Foundations located next to utility trenches and/or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footings should be embedded to a 
depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid 
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to 
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be recompacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. As indicated previously, the non-expansive 
select granular material does not need to extend below the building foundations. Soft 
and/or loose materials that are encountered at the footing subgrade level should be

water should be diverted away from the foundations.

to confirm the foundation embedment depths. If unsuitable materials are encountered in 
the foundation excavations, these materials should be over-excavated to expose the 
underlying firm materials and replaced with compacted fill.

3.3 
Retaining Structures 
We anticipate retaining structures, such as elevator pit walls, will be required for 
the project construction. In general, below-grade retaining structures should be 
designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent soil and surcharge 
effects. The following guidelines may be used for design of the below-grade structures. 
3.3.1 Wall Foundations 
In general, we believe the retaining structure foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the 
“Foundations” section herein. In addition, retaining wall footings should have a 
minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent grade. 
3.3.2 Static Lateral Earth Pressures

Earthwork 
We anticipate that the majority of the site grading work will involve cuts and fills 
on the order of less than 5 feet thick, with the exception of the existing detention basin 
that will require about 13 to 15 feet of fill to raise it to the proposed parking lot grade. In 
general, a Geolabs representative should monitor site grading and earthwork operations 
to observe the grading operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are 
encountered during the excavation process, and to confirm whether the exposed soil 
conditions at the invert elevations are similar to those encountered in our exploration. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, and other deleterious and organic 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and/or yielding 
areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill 
should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or 
organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, 
where appropriate.  
After clearing and grubbing, existing structures and pavements that are to be 
demolished should be removed completely. Over-excavations resulting from demolition 
should be backfilled with compacted on-site soils or select granular fill materials. 
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation 
should be properly backfilled with excavated on-site soils or select granular fill materials 
placed in 8-inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Utilities to be abandoned in-place under the proposed structures should be 
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure. 
After clearing, grubbing, and demolition, areas at grade or areas designated to 
receive fill should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Subgrades for areas subjected to vehicular traffic 
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative

Underground Utilities 
We understand that new underground utilities and utility connections will be 
required for the new housing project. In general, good construction practices should be 
utilized in the installation and backfilling of the trenches. The contractor should 
determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to 
practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply 
with the applicable local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for 
trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the pipes for support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on-site clayey soils. The 
backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically 
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the 
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

Corrosion Protection 
Laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride, sulfate 
and salinity contents, were performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying soils 
at the project site. The corrosion test results are provided on Plate B-14 of Appendix B. 
Based on the minimum resistivity test results, the on-site soils appear to be “Very 
Corrosive” to metallic substructures (such as piping) in contact with the soil. Therefore, 
we recommend properly designing metallic substructures for protection against the 
potential for corrosion. A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed 
recommendations on corrosion protection of metallic substructures, if appropriate. 
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, 
the tested values are relatively low. The typical Type I/II cement used in Hawaii and the 
minimum concrete cover of 3 inches over reinforcing steel should be adequate for 
concrete substructures in contact with the on-site soils.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths ranging between about 
15.0 and 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-5.  Logs were also maintained for the 15 boreholes drilled and 
sampled for the field infiltration tests, I-1 through I-14 and I-21.  These logs are presented 
on Plates A-6 through A-20. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer 
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface soils.  
The test results are summarized on Plate B-1. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils, 
five unconfined compression tests were performed on selected soil samples in accordance 
with ASTM D2166. Individual stress-strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are 
presented on Plates B-3 thru B-7. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-8 and B-9. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-10 and B-11. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-12 and B-13. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), and salinity 
were performed by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-14.

Field Infiltration Testing 
The infiltration capacities of the near-surface materials at the project site were 
evaluated by performing 15 falling head infiltration tests at selected locations on the project 
site. The tests were performed at depths ranging from about 9.5 to 10.0 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering a boring to the selected test 
depth of about 10 feet below ground surface.  Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch 
diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only 
through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring.  Each test consisted of four trials of 
filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a one hour trial 
period.  The infiltration rates are then calculated based on the result of the fourth and last 
trial for each test location.   
The infiltration test data are presented on Plates C-1 through C-15. 
In addition to these field infiltration tests, two Double Ring Infiltrometer Tests were 
conducted in the bottom of the existing detention basin in the eastern portion of the site.  
These tests were conducted in general accordance with ASTM D3385.  The results are 
presented on Plates C-16 and C-17.
