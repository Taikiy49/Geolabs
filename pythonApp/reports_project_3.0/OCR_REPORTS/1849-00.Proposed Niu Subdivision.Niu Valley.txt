INTRODUCTION
The purpose of our exploration was to explore the subsurface soil
conditions at the site in order to provide pertinent soil data to aid in
the site grading roadway and house foundation designs
The scope of our investigation included drilling and sampling seven
7 borings to depths of 3 0 to 10 7 feet below the existing ground
surface Also included were laboratory testing of selected samples
obtained engineering analysis and evaluation of the field and laboratory
data development of the preliminary design recommendations and report
preparation

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed development includes 31 single family residential lots
and approximately 800 L F of access road Minimal cuts and fills are
anticipated for the majority of the proposed site grading while
relatively small cuts about 2 to 6 feet are planned along the rear
portions of mauka lots to create more level area
At the present time the type and exact locations of structure are
unavailable
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located north of Halemaumau Street and west of
Halaki Street in Niu Valley on the island of Oahu The site is located
near the toe of the Kulepiamoa Ridge
The ground elevations at this site vary from about 16 to 50 feet
above Mean Sea level The existing ground surface generally slopes
upwards in the northerly direction
Two existing houses with garages and fruit trees are located in the
southeastern quarter of the site The rest of the site consists of a
pasture land covered with grass Some surface boulders were observed in
the northwestern corner of the site

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The soil conditions at the site were explored by drilling seven
to 10 7 feet deep below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings generally encountered about 1 to 5 5 feet of brown to
dark brown stiff to very stiff clay adobe over basalt cobbles and
boulders that extended to the bottom of most of the borings up to about
11 feet deep
At discrete areas close to the existing houses and concrete drainage
ditch sandy silty surficial soils were encountered instead of adobe
clay We believe this consists of localized fill and or alluvial
deposits by the stream
DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration disclosed a surface layer of highly expansive
adobe clay underlain by dense bouldery alluvium colluvium at the
proposed residential development site Since only minimal cuts and fills
are planned and because light structure loads are anticipated the main
concern in designing the on grade slabs and building footing foundations
would be to adopt special procedures in order to reduce the shrink swell
effect of the subsoils
We suggest that post and beam support systems should be considered
These would reduce the need for on grade slabs and therefore the amount
of over excavation and replacement with non expansive borrow that are
recommended to reduce the shrink swell effect of the subsoil

Foundation Support
Spread footing foundation may be used for the proposed houses
provided special procedures are utilized to reduce the shrinkage swelling
effects of the clay soils
The adobe clay soils below the foundations have high volume change
potential and can shrink when dried or heave considerably if saturated in
the future Therefore we recommend that the expansive clays below the
foundations should either be removed or over excavated down to a minimum
depth of 3 feet below the bottom of the footings The over excavation
should also extend a minimm of 6 inches beyond the edge of the footings
The bottom 2 feet of the over excavation should be backfilled with
non expansive borrow materials such as select borrow crusher waste or
coral placed in horizontal lifts not exceeding 6 inches thick and
compacted to a minimm of 90 of its maximum dry density per ASIM
D 1557 78 The top 1 foot should be backfilled with impervious
non expansive soil compacted to the above density Alternatively the
footings may be embedded a minimum of 3 feet below the lowest adjacent
grade for details refer to Plate 3 Shallow belled piers were also
considered for the house foundation However due to excessive amounts
of boulders encountered this may not be an economical alternative
Based on the anticipated soil conditions site grading and design
loading we believe that an allowable bearing capacity of 2500 p s f may
be used for spread footings bearing on compacted structural fill or stiff
in situ soil
If a footing is situated next to a utility line it should be
extended to the bottom of the utility trench or the trench backfill must
be well compacted to prevent footing settlement due to settlement of the
trench backfill

Surface water should be diverted away from all foundations This is
to avoid ponding of surface run off water that may affect the expansive
soils around the foundation
Slab on Grade
The expansive on site clay soil under on grade slab areas should be
over excavated to a depth of at least 3 feet below the slab or to less
expansive soil and replaced with non expansive borrow soils in order to
reduce the effects of soil heaving on the slabs see Plate 4
Similar over excavation and replacement of the expansive clay soils
are recommended for driveways walkways and other slabs if required
To reduce future post construction swelling of the underlying
expansive soils we recommend that the on site soils below the
over excavation level be kept continuously moist prior to concrete
placement
To prevent ponding condition the foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled tight against the footing immediately after setting
of the concrete
Drainage swales must be provided as soon as possible and maintained
to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundation
A 4 inch thick cushion layer of 3B Fine gravel should be provided
below the slab In order to protect against moisture infiltration an
impervious moisture barrier may be incorporated below the slab
Driveways
For concrete driveways over excavation and replacement of the
expansive clay is recommended However since the driveway can tolerate
some movement the depth of over excavation may be reduced to 2 feet
provided some small slab cracking is tolerable

Site Grading
Minimal site grading is anticipated for the majority of the project

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading
limit should be thoroughly cleared Any unsuitable materials such
as rock walls cesspools previous fill rubbish soft material and
vegetation should be removed and disposed of properly off site
loose spots encountered at subgrade level should be removed and
replaced with imported fill material compacted to a minimm of 90
of its ASTM D 1557 78 maximum dry density

Retaining Walls
It is our understanding that low CRM retaining walls generally less
than 5 feet high may be required along the mauka sides of the site The
guidelines for retaining wall design are listed below
1 In order to reduce the expansive effects of the existing clay
soils on the wall we recommend that the expansive adobe
clays if encountered be over excavated down for a depth of 2
feet below the bottom of the footing or to dense boulderly
formation and then replaced with non expansive borrow soils
such as crusher waste select borrow or coral compacted to a
minimum of 90 of its maximm dry density per ASTM D 1557 78
The over excavation and replacement should extend a minimm
distance of 12 inches beyond the toe of the footing if
possible

The retaining wall footings should be embedded a minimum depth
of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
Therefore the total depth of the above over excavation and
replacement would be 3 feet below the lowest adjacent finished
grade where adobe clay is encountered refer to Plate 5 for
details
An allowable bearing value of up to 2500 p s f is recommended
for foundations bearing on the imported non expansive fill
A base friction factor of 0 4 for soil can be utilized to
determine the sliding resistance of the wall In addition a
passive soil resistance of 250 p c f can be utilized if there
is sufficient soil in front of the wall
To minimize excessive swelling pressure that may be exerted
laterally on the retaining wall the expansive adobe clay
should be over excavated at least 4 feet behind the wall or
equal to the height of the wall whichever is smaller
Retaining wall backfill should consist of granular materials
such as select borrow or crusher waste This type of backfill
would improve drainage reduce the compactive effort required
for the wall backfill and reduce lateral pressure on the walls
Impervious soil such as on site clay soils should be used only
for the top one foot of backfill to reduce seepage of surface
run off behind the walls
A subsurface drainage system subdrains or weepholes should be
utilized behind the walls Subdrains should be properly
constructed to drain water away from the wall

Access Road Pavement
Based on our field exploration and previous experience the
following preliminary access road pavement section is recommended for
adobe clay subgrade condition
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Compaction
18 0 Inch Select Borrow
26 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
The select borrow may be eliminated if the top 2 feet below the
subgrade is fairly non expansive bouldery material
CBR tests and or visual cbservations should be performed on the
actual subgrade soil samples during construction and the above design
section can be revised if necessary The recommended section considers
light traffic and that good drainage will be provided adjacent to paved
areas

Design Review
Plans and specifications for site grading foundation and pavement
design should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written

Construction Monitoring
It is recommend that the site grading and footing excavation be
observed by the soils engineer during construction to see that adequate
compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface conditions are
consistent with the findings of our study
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site for use
If conditions encountered are different from those presented in this
report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made
