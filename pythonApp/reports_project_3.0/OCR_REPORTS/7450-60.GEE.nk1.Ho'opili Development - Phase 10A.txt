SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surficial fills about 1 to 
7.5 feet thick overlying stiff to hard silty and clayey alluvial soils extending down to the 
maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Seepage 
water was encountered in Boring No. 104 at a depth of about 19 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The other borings and test pits did not encounter groundwater at the time 
of our field exploration. 
Based on our field observations, the surface soils at the project site are dry and 
friable. Although minimal shrinkage cracks were observed on the existing ground surface at 
the time of our field exploration, our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey 
soils exhibit moderate to high shrink-swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations 
in the soil moisture contents. When considering the lightly loaded structures planned for 
the Phase 10A development, it is anticipated that the moderate to high shrink-swell 
potential of the on-site soils could result in structural distresses to the new buildings and 
to pavements unless special subgrade preparations are implemented during construction. 
We recommend the site grading preparation consist of the following procedures to 
create a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near-surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These 
soils should be disposed of properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, 
if appropriate. 
2.

for use as general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, 
deleterious material and other unsuitable materials.

Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as fill, the bottom of the 
over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. 
The intent of the above site preparation is to create a 2-foot thick reworked soil 
layer within the project grading limits. The reworked soil serves to close-up existing 
shrinkage cracks that may be present at the project site. It would also reduce the future 
soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing the clayey soils with elevated moisture 
contents and reduced compaction requirements. In addition, it decreases the affinity of 
the clayey soil for water and creates a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the

Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of

relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished subgrades subjected to 
vehicular traffic). 
The on-site clayey soils should not be allowed to dry out and should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site 
clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks develop, the soils should be re-scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and recompacted again. 
Based on our field exploration and analyses, we believe the planned building 
structures may be supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 
2-foot reworked soil layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The 
Wafflemat is a post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. We recommend using 
12-inch high Waffleboxes to provide a stiffer foundation system in order to reduce 
differential movement and resulting angular distortion. We also recommend installing a 
vapor barrier underneath the Waffleboxes to a minimum of 2 feet beyond the slab 
perimeter to reduce water infiltration beneath the slab foundations. 
If conventional concrete slabs-on-grade with thickened edge footings are used 
in lieu of the Wafflemat foundation system, we recommend providing 3 feet of 
non-expansive, select granular fill material beneath the concrete slabs-on-grade and 
extending the material beyond the perimeter of the slab to a minimum of 3 feet. 
The individual homeowners and Homeowner’s Associations should be strongly 
cautioned against over-irrigating or landscaping within 3 feet around the house structures, 
which could adversely affect the proper performance of the foundation system. In addition, 
a qualified geotechnical engineer should be retained to provide recommendations to the 
individual homeowner or Homeowner’s Association if improvements or expansions are 
planned beyond the original floor area.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through 
the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is situated at the southeastern portion of Ho`opili Development, 
east of Phases 1 and 3, in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is 
bordered by cultivated agricultural land to the north and east, Ewa Villages to the south, 
and the Phase 1 and 3 developments to the west.  
At the time of our field exploration, the site was generally being used for commercial 
farming; the observed farming areas appeared to have been recently tilled possibly in 
preparation for future planting. Bordering the southwest portion of the project site, an 
existing, relatively shallow, heavily vegetated earthen drainage swale was observed. In 
addition, several unpaved access roads traverse across the Phase 10A site. At the time of 
our field exploration, minimal shrinkage cracks were observed on the existing ground 
surface.  
Based on our site observations and topographic survey map provided, the project 
site generally slopes gently down from north to south at an average slope gradient of about 
1 percent with the exception of the approximately 12-foot deep by 100-foot wide earthen 
drainage channel on the southwest border of the project site. Based on the information 
provided, ground surface elevations range from about +98 to +70 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) at the northern and southwestern portions of the project site, respectively.

3.1.2 Site Perimeter Cut-Off Trench 
Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa/Kapolei 
area during prolonged dry weather conditions and were observed at the existing 
ground at the time of our field exploration. Since shrinkage cracks tend to be a conduit 
for water infiltration into the subsurface soils, we recommend implementing special 
site preparation procedures consisting of installing a cut-off trench along the 
perimeter of the grading limits at the project site. We recommend a cut-off trench at 
least 3 feet deep and 5 feet wide consisting of the on-site clayey soils placed and 
compacted with elevated moisture contents, as illustrated on Plates 5.1 and 5.2. As 
an alternative, the cut-off trench may be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength 
Material (CLSM). 
The on-site soils used for this cut-off trench should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The intent of this cut-off trench is to create a barrier to reduce water 
infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil from the shrinkage cracks on the 
adjacent properties.

3.2 
Building Foundations 
Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked soil 
layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a 
post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. This proprietary system utilizes a series 
of recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side down on 
the soil. The Waffleboxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings throughout the floor 
system, creating a very stiff foundation. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically 
poured over and between the boxes. The empty space beneath the boxes allows for soil 
expansion, reducing uplift of the slab. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
may be used for design of the Wafflemat foundations bearing on the compacted reworked 
soil layer or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations presented 
herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.

(based on the soluble soil sample prepared in accordance with Standard Methods for the 
Examination of Water and Wastewater SM 2510B) and pH values of between 6 and 7 
within the upper 5 feet of soils, corresponding to a corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely 
Corrosive) based on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of 
Water Supply.  
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a corrosion 
rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of 
metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider 
the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Levels of the chloride content of the on-site soils encountered near the existing 
ground surface are relatively low, on the order of 110 parts per million (ppm) or less, while 
the sulfate content is relatively low, on the order of about 140 ppm or less.  
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the soil. 
In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the relatively 
low values of pH, chloride content, and sulfate content tested on the in-situ materials, we 
believe the near-surface soils at the project site generally may be considered 
“non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the 
concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional 
corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for 
corrosion protection.

3.7.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
As mentioned previously, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry 
and friable and exhibit moderate to high shrink/swell characteristics when 
subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 18-inch 
the non-expansive, select granular fill, we recommend that the subgrade  be 
properly scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to between 
2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted between 
85 and 90 percent relative compaction, as specified in Section 3.1.1 – Site 
Preparation herein. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to 
re-compacting. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due 
to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft 
areas and replacement with well-compacted fill.

3.7.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
The 18-inch capping layer below the pavement sections should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent 
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material 
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in 
Sections 31 and 30, respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
3.7.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirement for the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least 
95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course and 
should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional effort for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the 
aggregate base course (for flexible pavement section) or the aggregate subbase 
course (for rigid pavement section) materials using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with

The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to 
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend 
extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base 
or subbase layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water 
into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be constructed 
to collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term performance, 
we recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the paved/landscaped

3.9 
Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
Shrinkage cracks are regularly formed at the existing ground surface. In general, 
shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil 
moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions. 
The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground is apparently intensified by the 
prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Ewa area resulting in large fluctuations in the soil 
moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to 
reduce the potential for significant soil erosion soon after completion of the site grading. 
The relatively dry climatic conditions of the Kapolei/Ewa area would likely require 
controlled irrigation of the graded surfaces to maintain the moisture contents and to keep 
graded surfaces free of large shrinkage cracks. However, over-watering of the graded 
surfaces should be avoided to reduce the potential for saturation and softening of the 
surface soils. 
3.10 Utility Trenches 
It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed development project will consist 
of primarily electrical, water, sewer, and drain lines. In general, good construction 
practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the 
proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The excavation 
should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements. The 
contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation. Trench 
shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining 
backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from 
compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce 
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate

3.11 Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed water 
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. Surface 
water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It is highly 
recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert discharge away 
from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations 
and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. Planters next to 
foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the 
potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface. 
In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be 
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 106, extending to depths of about 
20.3 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, three test pits, designated 
as Test Pit Nos. 101 through 103, were each excavated to a depth of about 10 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Four bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as 
Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. The test pits were excavated using a Komatsu PC160 Excavator 
and CAT Hoptoe. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits, Plates A-1 through A-9. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

A P P E N D I X   B 
Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) including two hydrometer tests 
(ASTM D422) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentation of the 
grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
Eleven one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and/or 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Eight one-dimensional swell or settlement potential tests (ASTM D4546 Method A) 
were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of the 
remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is remolded to 
approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to simulate the 
anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle until it has 
stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil sample to evaluate 
its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-4 and B-5. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, three Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil 
samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-6 through B-8. 
Two sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-9. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method A) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-10 through B-13.
