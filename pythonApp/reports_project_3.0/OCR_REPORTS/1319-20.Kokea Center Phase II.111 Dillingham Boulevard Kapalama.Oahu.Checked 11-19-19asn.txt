INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a foundation investigation
prepared for the proposed Kokea Center Phase II to be located on the
southeastern side of the junction of Kokea Street and Dillingham Boulevard
in Kapalama on the island of Oahu The general location of the project
site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 2
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this investigation was to explore the subsurface
conditions and provide soils and foundation design recommendations for the
proposed development
The scope of investigation included drilling and sampling seven 7
borings one 1 cone penetration test laboratory testing of samples
obtained engineering evaluation of the field and laboratory data
development of design recommendations and report preparation
Detailed description of our field exploration logs of borings and
laboratory test results are presented in the appendices of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
It is our understanding that the proposed Kokea Center Phase II
will cover an area of about 3 1 acres and consist of six 6 metal framed
structures with timber roofs and tilt up concrete panel walls The
proposed layout of these structures namely Building Nos D to I are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The design foundation loading will vary up to a maximum of 34 kips
per column for the proposed buildings A tilt up concrete panel wall is
planned for the boundary between the units The wall loading would be
about 4 4 kips per foot
It is our understanding that the finished floor grade for the
proposed buildings would be at elevation 6 0 feet and therefore 1 to 2 5
feet of new fill will be required over most of the areas where these
structures will be located
In addition the existing Kokea Street will be improved with curb
and gutter on the project side
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 3
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is relatively level It is an irregular polygon
bounded by Dillingham Boulevard on the east Kokea Street on the north and
the existing Kokea Center Phase 1 on the west
The site was previously occupied by a one and two story concrete
masonry building at the intersection of Dillingham Boulevard and Kokea
Street and metal framed warehouse type structures along Kokea Street In
addition there were some underground structures such as cesspools drains
and other utility lines At the time of our field exploration the
superstructures on the site had been cleared but some concrete slabs and
underground structures might still possibly be there
Regional Geology
The project site is located on the Honolulu Coastal Plain which is
composed primarily of interbedded terrestrial and marine sediments
During geologic history this plain had undergone several sequences of
erosion and deposition as a result of glacio eustatic sea level changes
during the Pleistocene
Due to its proximity to Kapalama Stream these sequences have had a
significant effect upon the formation of the project site During high
stands of the sea the area was a lagoon marsh or estuary area subjected
to low energy deposition of marine and terrestrial sediments During low
stands of the sea portions of these sediments were eroded away
Following the last low stand of the sea Koko at minus 15 feet
elevation the area was gradually filled with soft and loose sediments in
a lagoonal marsh environment The area was filled during land reclamation
projects during the early part of the century

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and
sampling seven 7 borings and one 1 cone penetration test at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of the
borings were 30 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface while the
cone penetration test was performed to 25 feet depth
In general the subsurface conditions across the site are relatively
Similar and consist of the following units
1 A 2 to 4 foot thick surface fill layer mostly of dense coral
sand and gravel with occasional pockets of stiff clayey silt
2 One to 6 feet of soft to medium stiff organic silt and clay
marsh deposits
3 Six to 12 feet of loose gray sand and gravel lagoonal deposits
which were clayey and silty in some places
4 Six to 12 feet of stiff to very stiff dark brown clayey silt
alluvial deposit
5 Four to 8 feet of stiff to very stiff brown to gray calcareous
silty clay with sand seams probably of marine origin
Underlying the above units was a layer of medium dense tan to white
coral sand and gravel with some cementation to 40 feet
Groundwater Level
Groundwater was generally encountered in the borings at about sea
level The depths to the groundwater level were measured in the borings
and are listed on the log of borings

W O 1319 20 Page 5
The groundwater levels fluctuate somewhat with tide changes and are
influenced by the adjacent Kapalama Stream water levels
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The project site is underlain by two different compressible layers
a thin layer of soft silts and clays and a thicker layer of sandy lagoonal
deposits Settlements should be anticipated due to the new fill and
building loads However we believe that the majority of the settlements
would occur rapidly because of the predominantly granular nature of the
compressible deposit To induce ground settlements to occur prior to
foundation construction we recommend that the required new fill be placed
as soon as possible
The building construction should be delayed until most of the
settlements due to the site filling have occured which is estimated to
be about 1 to 2 months Then spread footing foundations may be used for
the building support with allowance for some future settlements due to the
structural loads If the anticipated settlements are not tolerable a
surcharge program or pile foundations would have to be considered
Our recommendations are discussed in detail and presented in the
following sections
Settlements
Based on the findings we believe that ground settlements will occur
due to 1 the weight of the new fill 2 the building load and 3
additional settlements that may develop if a heavy warehouse floor loading
is applied
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 6
The compressible strata consist mainly of loose gravelly and silty
sand Therefore we anticipate that the majority of the settlements in
the project site would develop quickly probably within one to two months
after loading is applied The portion of the settlements from the layer
of soft silts and clays would occur more slowly after the majority of the
settlements in the loose sand and gravel have occurred
The anticipated areal settlements due to the 1 to 2 5 feet of new
fill are estimated to range from 2 5 to 6 inches Additional areal
settlements under warehouse floor loading are about 1 to 2 inches under
without surcharge It is our estimate that the slower occurring
settlements from the layer of silts and clays may range from one half
the warehouse loading
Settlements under foundations are discussed under the FOUNDATION
section
We recommend that the settlement of the fill be monitored to
determine when the majority of the settlement has occurred and when
building construction can begin
If it is desired to reduce future settlements from the building and
floor loads a surcharge fill program may be utilized We would be glad
to consult with you on such a program
Foundation Support
Based on our past experience we believe that shallow spread footing
may be considered for the foundation support of the proposed warehouse
structures Although some settlements will result these structures are
generally flexible and can tolerate moderate amount of settlements Where
the settlement of foundation cannot be tolerated pile foundation should
be considered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 7
Spread Footings
In general spread footings should be placed at a level as high as
possible to reduce the footing load applied over the underlying
compressible deposits It is recommended however that the footing
bottom be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent
grade A maximum net allowable soil bearing pressure of 2000 p s f
may be used for the design Foundations should not be placed below
about elevation 3 feet othewise special design may be required due
to the close proximity of the soft subsoils and water level
The anticipated footing settlement under 34 kips of column loading
inches Since some footings will be underlain by a
relatively thin layer of soft silts or clays beneath the fill a
portion of the abovementioned settlements would occur slowly
It is recommended that the actual settlement at each major column be
monitored prior to roof construction so that appropriate adjustments
could be made at a later date Provisions should be made so that
these columns could be adjusted as deemed necessary
The wall foundation for the tilt up concrete panel wall should be at
least 2 2 feet wide and embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade
The settlements under the wall may range from 1 to 2 inches with
differential settlements of about three fourths 3 4 of an inch
over a 15 foot span
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 8
Pile Foundation
For pile foundation support a 12 inch square concrete pile
extending 15 feet into the lower very stiff clayey or coral layer
encountered at about 15 foot depth can develop an allowable
bearing capacity of 40 tons per pile This pile should be driven to
a final driving resistance of 65 blows per foot for the last two
feet or 8 blows per inch with a hammer delivering a rated energy of
15 000 foot pounds For cost estimate and comparison purposes an
estimated average pile length of about 35 feet may be used
Prior to production pile driving 6 test piles of 40 foot length
should be driven to establish more accurately the order lengths and
final driving resistance criteria
The piles should be spaced no closer than three pile diameters
otherwise group action may significantly reduce the pile capacity
The driving criteria may be modified during construction if
excessive resistance is encountered
Site Preparation
The overall site preparation will include removal of any vegetation
debris cesspools and their organic matter and other structures The
existing concrete slabs if situated within 12 inches of finished grades
should be removed to provide a more uniform subgrade support of pavements
and slabs Further removal of concrete slabs below this depth will be
dictated by other considerations such as footing and utility
constructions
The resulting excavations should be backfilled with the on site
granular material placed in 9 inch loose lifts and compacted to at least
90 of the ASTM D 1557 78 maximum density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 9
The existing ground in the Kokea Street improvement area should be
scarified to a depth of 6 inches after clearing and grubbing moisture
conditioned and compacted to a density of at least 95 of the ASTM
D 1557 78 maximum density
Fill Materials
Imported i11 will be required to raise the grades of the site For
the anticipated project use the imported materials may consist of the
following
The material below the slab or pavement base course and within 16
inches of the finished grade should be granular material with a CBR value
greater than 25 and the maximum size of the particle should be less than
6 inches
The material below the top 16 inches may consist of the general
borrow type material as required in Section 16 of the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction City County of Honolulu
May 1975
For Kokea Street improvement to minimize the thickness of the
pavement section the fill under the pavement section should consist of
select borrow material
Fill Placement
All fills and backfills should be placed in horizontal lifts not
exceeding 9 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a dry density of
at least 95 of the maximum ASTM D 1557 78 maximum density Cohesive
materials if used below the top 16 inches plane may be compacted to 90
minimum
For Kokea Street construction the required compaction should be 95
minimum for all materials as the required fill thickness will probably be
less than 1 5 feet

Warehouse Slab
Based on the projected settlements flexible A C pavement would be
appropriate for the proposed warehouse in lieu of concrete slabs The
following pavement section may be used for designing the new warehouse
slab pavement
2 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 Inch Base Course
8 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
The base course should be crushed rock or other granular material
with a CBR of 85 and conforming with Section 31 2 of the abovementioned
Standard Specifications or equivalent The subgrade soil which is
assumed to be granular material with a CBR value greater than 25 for at
least the top 6 inches should be compacted to a minimum of 95 compaction
If concrete slab is utilized it should be designed and constructed
to accommodate future ground settlements and to control crack pattern
Pavements
The project will also include improving the property side of Kokea
Street The existing roadway pavements apparently were constructed at
different times and are showing various degree of deterioration However
no deep seated pavement failure was noted in most areas It is recommended
that deteriorated section of the pavements be broken down before new
pavement is constructed
In general the subgrade materials at Kokea Street appear to consist
of mixtures of dense silty coral sand and gravel
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 11
The following pavement section may be used for design of the project
driveways and Kokea Street
2 5 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 compaction
8 5 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
The subgrade soil should be scarified to a depth of 6 inches
moisture conditioned and compacted to 95 compaction
The above pavement section should be used in the main traffic lanes
of the Kokea project site For lighter passenger automobile pavements
the asphalt concrete thickness may be reduced to 2 inches
Utility Lines
The underground utility lines should be placed as late as possible
after the completion of site grading to minimize post installation
settlements In addition flexible joints should be considered
particularly the connections at building should have a higher degree of
flexibility to allow differential settlements
Trench excavations would require proper shoring particularly if
patches of soft organic clays or silts are encountered Since dewatering
may affect adjacent structures a soils engineer should be consulted if
the water level is to be lowered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 12
The materials and compaction requirements for the backfilling of
trench should be consistent with those recommended under the FILL
PLACEMENT section
Generally for backfilling the utility trenches the compacted
density of the backfill from the pipe bed up to 12 inches above the pipe
should be a minimum of 85 of ASTM D 1557 78 The remaining portion
should be 90 If trenches are located under roadways the top 16 inches
of the backfill from the finished grade should be compacted to 95 minimum
Design Review
Plans and specifications for foundation and pavement should be
forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written comments prior to
construction to see that the intent of our recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
The earthwork operation including proofrolling and fill placement
should be monitored by a soils engineer during the early stage of site
grading in order to detect soft pockets and determine their removal
We recommend that the footing excavations be inspected by the soils
engineer prior to placement of concrete or reinforcing steel to check that
adequate bearing has been obtained and that the subsurface conditions are
consistent with the findings of our subsurface investigation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1319 20 Page 13
If the structural columns are to be supported on a pile foundation
the pile driving operations should be monitored by a qualified soils
engineer to verify that the piles have been driven adequately and in
accordance with our recommendations
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon the
above inspections

seven 7 borings and one 1 cone penetration test at the approximate

monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Log of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 7 1

sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the
appropriate sample depths
One cone penetration test was performed by mechanically pushing an
electric cone into the ground The tip resistance and the shaft friction
were measured at regular depth of penetration Interpretation of soil
profile was carried out based on the magnitude of the recorded forces and
the friction ratio A graphic presentation of the result is shown on
Plate A 8 1
