GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING STUDY 
NANAKULI DRAINAGE CHANNEL WALL REPAIRS – CONCEPTUAL DESIGN 
NANAKULI, OAHU, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Char: 
This letter report provides geotechnical considerations for the conceptual design of 
the channel wall repair project, located in the Nanakuli area in the District of Waianae on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
attached Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes the findings from our desk-top study and presents 
geotechnical considerations for the conceptual channel wall repair alternatives for the 
proposed project.  The findings and considerations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located within the west side of Nanakuli Valley on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  The project site extends from Farrington Highway up to Nanakuli High 
School.  We understand that channel wall distresses were observed for the existing 
U-shaped concrete drainage channel.  We understand that it is desired to repair the 
distressed channel walls.  It appears that the channel walls are about 7-foot high and 
about 20 to 25 feet wide. We understand that the project consists of developing the 
conceptual design for repair alternatives of the distressed channel walls. 
Borings were not drilled for this study.  A desk-top study was performed using the 
available subsurface information only. 
REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to

late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was 
partially buried below the Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked 
or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials, which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series. 
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower, middle, and upper volcanic 
members. The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks 
that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano. The middle member consists of 
rock that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera. The upper member 
is a relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in 
its history of evolution. 
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed, a long period of deep erosion, 
sedimentation, and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred, which produced the 
large valleys of the western side of the shield volcano. These erosional valleys were 
gradually filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a 
combined result of stream erosion, base level (sea level) rise, and subsidence of the 
island mass. Over time, and as a result of sea level fluctuation, some of the older 
alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation 
formed relatively dense deposits. However, the current surface soil deposits above 
these older deposits are typically found to be unconsolidated. 
Nanakuli Valley, like all the valleys of the Waianae Range, was formed by stream 
erosion and is choked with massive amounts of alluvium and colluvium. Alluvium is 
basically defined as soil deposits transported by water and ranges from very soft silts 
and clays of a low energy estuarine or flood plain to large boulders transported by high 
energy torrential rains and flash floods. Colluvium is characterized by gravity 
transported deposits and range from slow creeping adobe soils to talus slopes and 
catastrophic landslide breccia deposits.  
ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site were evaluated based on 
available in-house subsurface information near the project area. 
Based on our review of available subsurface information in the general area, we 
anticipate that majority of the project site will be underlain by recent alluvial deposits 
consisting of clays with varying amounts of gravel, cobbles, and boulders.  In the mauka 
end of the project site, basalt rock formation of the Waianae Volcanic Series may be 
encountered at relatively shallow depths.  Consolidated and unconsolidated coralline 
deposits are anticipated to be encountered at the makai end of the project site. We

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our review of available subsurface information near the project site, we 
anticipate that majority of the site is generally underlain by recent alluvial deposits 
consisting of clay with gravel, cobbles, and boulders.  Coralline deposits and basalt rock 
formation may be encountered at the makai and mauka ends of the project, respectively.  
Shallow groundwater conditions may be encountered towards the makai end of the 
project. 
Our understanding of the existing wall condition is briefly described, and 
construction considerations in developing the channel wall remediation alternatives are 
provided below.  In addition, discussions on the various channel wall remediation 
alternatives are provided below. 
Existing Channel Walls 
We understand that some areas of the existing channel walls have experienced 
distress consisting of leaning of the channel walls towards the middle of the channel. In 
one area of the channel adjacent to 89-198 Mano Avenue, approximately 55 linear feet of 
the eastern channel wall collapsed on January 12, 2017 requiring emergency repair of the 
channel structure.  The emergency repair consisted of removal of the channel wall and 
portion of the invert slab and replacement with a new channel wall and invert slab section.  
Construction Considerations 
Based on our desk-top study, there are many residential homes and structures 
located adjacent to the existing drainage channel.  Construction access through the 
residential parcels will be difficult especially for the larger construction equipment.  
Therefore, we anticipate that the remediation work will be performed from within the 
channel.  Because of the limited construction work area within the existing channel, 
utilizing smaller construction equipment will be required.  
Since construction equipment will be situated within the drainage channel, an 
evacuation plan for the removal of equipment and construction material prior to an 
impending storm event needs to be in-place.  In addition, diversion of stormwater during 
construction may be needed.  
Channel Wall Remediation Alternatives 
We understand that several channel wall remediation alternatives are being 
considered for the project.  The remediation alternatives consist of constructing a new 
channel wall section, constructing a new “L” or “U” shaped channel section inside of the 
existing channel, installation of struts across the top of the channel walls, and supporting 
the channel walls with soil nail anchors.  Discussion of each alternative is provided below.

Demolish and Reconstruct New Channel Section 
One of the alternatives is to demolish and reconstruct a new channel section.  The 
distressed section of the channel will be demolished and removed, and replaced 
with a new channel section. Excavation will be required for the installation of forms 
for the new wall.  Temporary shoring of existing structures adjacent to the repair 
area may be required. 
The advantage of this alternative is that the hydraulic section of the channel can be 
maintained.  The disadvantage is that it may be more costly than the other 
alternatives.  In addition, further excavation into the adjacent property will be 
required for construction and may require temporary shoring during construction.  
Construct New “L” or “U” Shaped Channel Section Inside of Existing Channel 
This alternative consists of constructing a new “L” or “U” shaped channel section 
inside the existing channel.  The new channel section would provide support for the 
distressed section of the existing channel.  A transition section would be needed at 
the ends of the new channel section. 
The advantage of this alternative is that it is less costly compared to the “Demolish 
and Reconstruct New Channel Section” alternative.  Removal of the distressed 
walls may not be required.  In addition, temporary shoring may not be required.  
The disadvantage is the hydraulic section of the channel will be reduced and, 
therefore, will reduce flow capacity. 
Struts Across Top of Wall 
One of the alternatives is to install struts across the top of the channel wall.  The 
struts will provide lateral support at the top of the distressed channel walls that are 
leaning.  The connection at the bottom of the channel wall needs to be checked to 
ensure the bottom of the wall is laterally supported adequately.  The struts should 
be protected against corrosion.  
The advantage of this alternative is that it is the least costly.  The disadvantage of 
this alternative is that the new struts may catch stormwater debris and may 
potentially clog the channel during heavy rainstorms.    
Channel Wall Supported with Soil Nail Anchors 
This alternative consists of the removal of the distressed channel wall and replacing 
with a shotcrete wall supported by soil nail anchors.  The soil nails are typically 
installed at an inclination of about 15 degrees from horizontal and will provide 
lateral support for the new channel wall. 
The advantage of this alternative is that further excavation into the adjacent 
properties for installation of the wall forms is not required.  A shorter construction
