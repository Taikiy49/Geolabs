exploration performed for the proposed East End Relief Sewer Phase 2 project in the
District of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the preliminary design
of pipe support trench excavation and shoring and trench backfilling only The
findings and preliminary recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk
of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 6
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau shield Its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana area are
dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits
which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments laid during high stands of the sea
The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly
bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has
subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbott 1970 During this period of subsidence coral algal reefs
began to grow on the southern coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs
across its mouth The growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of
subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous
and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 7
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under
relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways Land development and
reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the project area to its
present form

DISCUSSION AND PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions along the two alternate
alignments generally consist of medium dense sands underlain by very loose to
medium dense sands and gravels over coral detritus formation Shallow groundwater
levels were generally encountered in our borings during the field exploration Based
on the information provided we understand that Alternate No 1 is the preferred
alignment for replacement of the existing sewer line Based on the subsoil conditions
encountered during our field exploration the subsurface conditions are quite similar
along the two alternate alignments Therefore it is our opinion that the subsurface
conditions along the alignment of Alternate No 1 are not more or less favorable for the
proposed construction when compared to the subsurface conditions along Alternate
No 2 However we understand that there are numerous existing utility lines along
Kamakee Street which would make Alternate No 2 less favorable Based on our
evaluation of the subsurface conditions encountered with respect to the proposed new
sewer line construction the principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the
proposed project include the following
1 foundation support of the proposed 48 inch sewer pipe
2 excavation conditions and the need for shoring support of the excavations
along the proposed alternate sewer alignment
3 the relatively permeable subsoil conditions for general dewatering of the
sewer trench excavation and the potential effects on the surrounding
structures and
4 potential settlements of the pipes due to the placement of the heavier
backfill material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 12
We understand that the proposed sewer pipe inverts will be about 15 to 16 feet
beiow the existing ground surface therefore we envision that the trenches for the
sewer line will be about 15 to 18 feet deep Due to the relatively deep trench
excavation planned and loose soils anticipated shoring of the trench excavations will
be necessary to support the sides of the trenches In addition due to the shallow
groundwater table encountered dewatering will be required during construction to
install the proposed sewer pipes and to backfill the trenches To facilitate dewatering
of the trenches we believe that an adequately designed cut off wall such as
interlocking sheet pile shoring should be considered to reduce the volume of water to
be removed within the trench excavations In addition the sheet piles should be
extended to a depth sufficient to reduce the influence of the dewatering on adjacent
existing structures and utilities due to the relatively permeable subsoils
Because of the need for shoring and dewatering of the excavations for the
proposed sewer line other methods of installing the sewer pipe may be considered
One possible method would be by microtunnelling The use of microtunnelling to install
the pipe may reduce the disturbance to traffic and commercial activities on the ground
surface above and in the vicinity of the project site Based on the borings drilled along
the proposed alternate alignments microtunnelling appears to be a possible method
of installing the sewer line The microtunnelling machine will traverse several types of
subsoils with differing physical properties ranging from loose sands to dense coral
formation If microtunnelling is opted for use as the preferred method for the pipe
installation additional borings should be drilled to obtain subsurface information to
better characterize the lateral extent and hardness of the coral detritus formation

Trench Excavation
Based on the information provided for the proposed sewer line alignments the
new sewer line inverts will be about 15 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface
Our field exploration disclosed that the site generally is underlain by medium dense
sandy soils to depths of about 4 to 6 feet underlain by very loose to loose coral sands
and gravels to depths of 13 to 22 feet below the surface in the borings Shallow

encountered
Excavation Method
it is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to
excavate the trenches based on the subsurface conditions encountered
However the portion of the Alternate No 1 alignment near Boring No 1 and
along Queen Street would likely encounter the coral detritus formation near the
proposed invert elevation Therefore presence of the coral detritus formation
may require the use of heavier equipment to excavate the trenches In addition
it should be noted that coral ledges localized hard horizons and other below
grade structures may exist in some areas of the proposed alignments and would
require heavy equipment for excavation
In general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and _ safety
considerations The excavated soils should be stockpiled no closer than a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the
excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement It should be noted that
the excavated soils will be wet since a large portion of the excavated material
will be from below the groundwater level

Excavation Support
We envision that the bottom of the trenches will extend to depths of about 18
feet below the existing ground surface Because the proposed new sewer line
will be along the roadway sloping of the sides of the trenches will not be
possible due to the limited space Based on the loose soil conditions and
shallow groundwater table encountered in our borings shoring of the sides of the
trenches will be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench excavation and to
protect personnel working in the trench All trench excavation dewatering and
construction works should be conducted in conformance with Federal State and
other safety requirements
It should be noted that the proposed sewer line will likely traverse other utility
lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility
lines if sheet piles are to be used as shoring If sheet pile shoring is used it
would also serve as a cut off wall for the trench dewatering operations To use
the sheet piles as a cut off wall the sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient
depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the
amount of water to be dewatered within the trench due to the relatively high
permeability of the subsoils For preliminary design of a braced sheet pile
shoring system the apparent pressure diagram provided on Plate 3 may be
used as a guide
Based on the conditions encountered in Boring No 1 medium dense to dense
coral detritus formation was encountered at a depth of about 13 feet below the
ground surface Therefore some difficulties may be expected during the
installation of the sheet piles where the medium dense to dense coral detritus
formation will be encountered In these areas if refusal conditions are
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 15
encountered during sheet pile installation pre drilling of the sheet pile locations
may be considered As a result we recommend that additional exploration be
conducted in the detail design phase to obtain more subsurface information to

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer
be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor Even if good construction procedures are
followed some movement of the shoring system and the adjacent ground may
still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation Due to the
complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to estimate accurately the
magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground movements also
depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring
supports are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the
rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be
installed properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent
ground should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other
indications of movements with instruments such as inclinometers
Dewatering
We anticipate that the trenches will be about 18 feet deep and groundwater was
encountered at about 4 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface Due to the
relatively high groundwater levels dewatering of the proposed trenches will be
necessary for the proposed construction Because of the granular nature of the subsoil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 16
conditions and the coral detritus formation encountered it is anticipated that dewatering
of these excavations would involve large volumes of water
In addition because the proposed new sewer line alignment is located in a
developed area the dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that
the dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause potential
damage to the existing structures Therefore consideration should be given to a
dewatering system that includes a deep cut off wall to reduce the volume of water to
be removed within the excavation and to reduce the areal extent of groundwater draw
down outside of the trench excavation We recommend that some field pump testing
be conducted during the detail design phase to provide reliable field data for the
contractor to base the construction dewatering
Since the excavation dewatering will involve discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit will be necessary The contractor should consult
their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the NPDES permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method
selected should have minimal impact on the existing groundwater levels surrounding
the proposed excavation Dewatering operations should be performed such that only
clear water is pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a
blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 17
In addition special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility
trenches connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill
in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater
drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other
existing adjacent structures
We believe that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and or to exclude the water from the excavation to allow construction of the
proposed sewer line replacement structures under safe and dry conditions These
works or systems may include but are not limited to well pumping grouting cut off
walls tremie plugs or any combination of the above and or other possible methods
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage
to existing buildings roads and the environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be _ sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For the pipes bearing on the medium dense to dense sands and
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3215 00 Page 18
gravels and coral detritus formation a minimum of 6 inches of granular bedding
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be
provided below the pipe Prior to the placement of the granular bedding material the
bottom of the trench should be compacted For pipes with inverts in the soft loose
soils an additional 18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped in a filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be provided below
the bedding layer for more uniform support A typical section of the trench is presented
on Plate 4 for reference
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend at least half way up the sides
of the pipe The bedding material should consist of clean granular material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation Before the placement of bedding
material the excavated trench bottom should be compacted
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 The first zone of
backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the
pipes should consist of free draining granular materials to reduce the compaction effort
required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material should be less than
1 inch in maximum size and should have less than 10 percent passing the No 200
sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed provided the granular
material is free draining such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet
below the finished subgrade may consist of the excavated on site granular soils It
should be noted that the excavated on site soils are likely to be wet and saturated since
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 19
a large portion of the excavated material will be below the groundwater level If the on
site granular soils are to be used as backfili material it may be necessary to aerate the
excavated soils to permit compaction of the backfill material to the specified
requirements The backfill should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift
thickness with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 The trench backfill from 2 feet below the
finished subgrade elevation up to the subgrade elevation should consist of select
borrow compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction A typical section
of the trench is presented on Plate 4
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline floatation before
and during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of groundwater
into the trenches It should be noted that the uplift buoyant forces due to groundwater
influx may lift empty or even partially filled pipes

backfill material and the trench walls For a preliminary estimate of the vertical loads
on the buried sewer pipes an average unit weight of 125 pounds per cubic foot p c f
for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 35 between the backfill and
trench sidewalls may be used Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly
with increased width of the trench Therefore the width of the sewer line trench should
be kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered
where the proposed sewer line alignment is located within travelled ways

Settlements
Primary settlement of the new sewer line construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the sewer pipes In addition dewatering will
also cause a net increase in earth stresses on the underlying soils below the pipe
invert The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying loose
subsoils below the pipe invert Based on the results of our preliminary exploration and
engineering analyses primary settlement on the order of 1 to 2 inches is anticipated
for this project based on a pipe invert of about 15 feet deep with the loose subsoils
extending to a depth of about 25 feet below the surface
The above settlement estimate is based on the results of our preliminary
exploration and engineering analyses We recommend that additional field exploration
laboratory testing and engineering analysis be performed to accurately evaluate the
settlement characteristics of the subsoils In addition proper construction procedures
and good workmanship should be engaged during construction Additional settlement
could occur if improper trench support or dewatering procedures are used
Manhole Structures
We understand that the bottom of the manhole structures will be about 16 feet
below the ground surface Based on our field exploration we believe that some of the
manhole structures will be bearing on the very loose to loose sands and gravels and
some manholes will be bearing on the medium dense to dense coral detritus formation
For the manhole structures bearing on the medium dense to dense coral
detritus formation near Boring No 1 we recommend that the manhole structures be
supported on a cushion layer consisting of about 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation For the manhole structures bearing on the very loose to loose
sands and gravels we recommend that these manhole structures be supported on a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3215 00 Page 21
stabilization layer to provide more uniform support for the structures and to reduce the
potential for significant settlements due to new loads imposed by the manhole
structures The stabilization layer should consist of 24 inches of open graded granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric The filter fabric should
consist of a non woven type geotextile such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent To estimate
the foundation support of the proposed manhole structures allowable bearing pressures
of 2 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the manholes in the loose soil
conditions and 3 000 p s f may be used for the firm or dense subgrade conditions
Settlements on the order of 1 inches are anticipated for these structures under the
anticipated loading conditions
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground manhole
structures will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the
compactive effort on the backfill material around the structures Therefore granular
material with less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve is recommended for use
as backfill around the structures to reduce the compactive effort required which may
cause additional lateral earth pressures We recommend that the new manhole
structures be designed for an equivalent fluid pressure of 50 pounds per cubic foot
above values assume that the backfill around the manhole structures is properly placed
and compacted
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges traffic loads line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structure should be
considered in the design Additional analyses during design may be needed to
evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads

Microtunnelling
We envision that excavations up to about 18 feet and shoring and dewatering
will be required for the cut cover method to install the proposed sewer line To reduce
the potential difficulties associated with sewer trench construction the microtunnelling
method of pipe installation may be considered Microtunnelling method of pipe
installation may also reduce the disturbance to traffic and commercial activities on the
ground surface above and in the vicinity of the project site
Microtunnelling is a tunnelling and pipe jacking technique which uses a closed
face remotely controlled tunnel boring machine to excavate the pipe alignment while
the pipeline is installed using pipe jacking techniques Based on the borings drilled
along the proposed alternate alignments the microtunnelling machine will excavate
through the loose to medium dense sands The microtunnelling machine will also be
required to excavate through the coral detritus formation which has higher strength
characteristics These variable subsurface conditions would have an impact on the
contractor s cost of microtunnelling Therefore the microtunnelling contractor should
be made aware of these variable subsurface soil conditions during the bidding stage
If microtunnelling method of pipe installation is opted we recommend that additional
exploration be conducted along the proposed alignment to characterize the subsoil
conditions for preparation of the construction documents using microtunnelling
