INTRODUCTION
This preliminary report presents the results of our field percolation testing
performed to provide preliminary design input to the proposed storm water drainage
system at Kapolei Golf Course in the district of Ewa on the island of Hawaii Hawaii The
general project area is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field percolation testing and presents
our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the
preliminary data obtained Since the subsurface soil conditions and their percolation
characteristics could vary a great deal the recommendations presented herein are
intended for preliminary design input and are not intended to form the basis of
preparation of final construction cost estimates or for bidding purposes
This report supplements our progress report dated which
presented our preliminary findings and design recommendations for the project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
A new golf course Kapolei Golf Course is currently under construction as a part
of the development for Villages of Kapolei in Ewa
Surface and subsurface drainage through the golf course is a critical design
consideration for the entire development Storm water run off will discharge from Village
3 of the development Makakilo Gulch Makalapa Gulch and also a tributary of Makalapa
Gulch into the golf course through large concrete pipes and culverts After passing
through the golf course the remaining flow will discharge into a new channel Kapolei
Lower Channel which currently under construction is located northerly adjacent to
Franklin D Roosevelt Road in Barbers Point Naval Air Station
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 10 Page 3
It is proposed to evaluate the feasibility of groundwater percolation into the near
surface subsoils during the passage of storm water run off through the golf course
landscape areas
If the surface soil percolation rate is verified to be too slow the alternative is to
evaluate the feasibility of utilizing deep dry wells to dispose of the surface run off
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
Dry wells are considered injection wells under the State of Hawaii Department of
Health administrative rules and therefore require permits for their construction and
operation The purpose of our work on the project was to prepare the UIC permit
application for submittal to the Department of Health and to provide preliminary
information on the percolation rates of the subsoils in the Kapolei Golf Course under
construction The boring and percolation testing information obtained was used as a
basis to formulate our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining
to the selection and design of the golf course storm water drainage disposal system
which may include shallow leaching fields and or deeper dry wells
The work for this project was performed in general accordance with our fee
proposal to Park Engineering dated The scope of work
included the following items

locations within the golf course site to provide data for preliminary sizing of
dry wells and to evaluate the potential groundwater percolation rates into
the underlying subsoils through the installed dry wells

Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of
three 3 borings to depths ranging between about 31 5 to 50 0 feet below the existing
ground surface The current borings were drilled near the tentative locations for Dry Well
IW 3 and IW 14 proposed after a previous initial study Therefore these
borings are also referred to as Boring IW 3 and IW 14 for easy reference
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In Boring No IW 1 a thin layer of hard alluvial clay was encountered above
medium dense to very dense weathered cinder layers interbedded with a thin coral

In Boring No IW 1 a thin layer of hard alluvial clay was encountered above
medium dense to very dense weathered cinder layers interbedded with a thin coral
formation The weathered cinder appears to be less favorable to water percolation due
to its generally high density and silt content Dense basalt was encountered at the end
of the boring at approximately 45 feet below the existing ground surface
In Boring No IW 3 a thick layer of very stiff to hard alluvial clay was encountered
at the ground surface overlying dense weathered cinder layers and a layer of stiff residual
clay The upper clayey subsoils are generally impermeable However it is our
understanding that the upper hard alluvial clay will be removed by approximately 17 feet
to attain the required finish grade for the golf course construction In addition a generally
permeable coral formation with loose consistency was encountered at the bottom of the
boring
In Boring No IW 14 medium dense to very dense coral detritus consisting of sand
and gravel materials were encountered to the maximum depth of 31 5 feet It is also our
understanding that the upper 8 feet of surface soils below the current boring ground
surface elevation of the boring will be removed by site grading It appears that the
underlying more gravelly coral detritus are generally permeable and it will remain
Groundwater level was not observed in the drilled borings during our field
exploration

DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The test wells were drilled at three 3 different areas within the golf course to
evaluate the subsoil percolation characteristics Based on the soil conditions
encountered it appears that the near surface subsoils are relatively impermeable
However the percolation test results obtained seem to indicate that dry wells may be
feasible to dispose of storm water run off into the underlying permeable detritus coral
formations Based on the obtained data it also appears that the underlying subsoils in
the central and southern portions of the golf course are much more permeable than those
in the western portion of the golf course site

Preliminary Dry Well Design
Based on extrapolation of the current field percolation test data we recommend
that 18 inch diameter dry wells be drilled to a minimum depth of 30 feet below the finished
grade for the preliminary design of a storm water disposal system in the golf course
Based on our calculations dry wells located in the central portion of the golf
course could theoretically dispose of up to 172 000 cubic feet of storm water run off per
day and dry wells located at the southern portion of the golf course could dispose of up
to 170 000 cubic feet of storm water run off per day However the calculations indicate
that dry wells of the same size and depth could dispose of only up to 61 000 cubic feet
of storm water run off per day when located at the western portion of the golf course
A safety factor of one 1 was included in the above preliminary design
An illustration of the typical dry well detail is presented on Plate 3
Design Limitations
Due to the limited number of test wells drilled and the inherent heterogeneity of any
subsoil profile in a large area such as the golf course it should be understood that
the calculated in situ permeability values only represent the percolation
characteristics of the underlying subsoils at the test locations and depths under the
prevailing conditions during our field percolation testing The above calculated
percolation capacities of the proposed 18 inch diameter dry wells are based on
theoretical extrapolation of the smaller diameter borehole test data and therefore
may vary significantly from the actual percolation capacities of the proposed dry
wells
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 10 Page 9
If the actual percolation capacities of the proposed dry wells are found to be much
different from the predicted percolation capacities during the operational injection
testing to be performed later design modifications should then be made A
sufficient contingency fund is recommended to accommodate the possible extra
costs for the resulting design and construction modifications
In addition water injected into dry wells typically contains silt or other small size
particals suspended solids micro organisms and other matter which may reduce
the efficiency of the wells with times In Hawaii there had been reports of
numerous failures of dry wells These failures have been attributed to numerous
causes e g lack of maintenance accumulation of silt and well cave in
In view of the past history of failures we recommend that the dry wells be over
designed to allow for reduction in capacity or failure by using a large factor of
safety deemed appropriate
Permit Application and Operational Testing
The initial application for an Underground Injection Control Permit is in the process
of preparation and will be submitted when available
The mass grading for the proposed golf course is currently underway It appears
that there is no advantage of drilling the proposed two 2 dry wells for operational
injection testing before the completion of the golf course mass grading The two 2 test
wells may be incorporated into the actual dry well drainage system if the tested well
capacities are acceptable

Design Review
Final construction drawings and specifications for the proposed development
should be forwarded to the project geotechnical engineer for review and written
comments prior to construction This review is needed to determine adherence to the
recommendations given herein If this review is not made the geotechnical engineer
cannot assume responsibility for misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the project geotechnical engineer be retained to observe
the drilling of the proposed dry wells This is to observe compliance with the design
concepts specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event
that subsurface conditions differ from those anticipated at the time that this report is
written The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such
observations
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
LIMITATIONS

in part upon information obtained from field borings and field percolation testing

wells may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident
until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
