REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes named Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4011 00 Page 4
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned
with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The processes of erosion and
deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea
level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were
carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level was high the erosional
base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
the detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transportation
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit
large volumes of water for very short duration Therefore transport of sediments is
intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture
of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change
these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4011 00 Page 5
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time
In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older
partially consolidated deposits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Pavement distresses and failures including alligator cracks and raveling were
observed on the existing asphalt concrete pavements Based on our field exploration the
existing pavements are generally underlain by clayey gravel and gravelly clay overlain by
6 to 8 inches of dense base course layer We believe that the pavement distresses and
failures observed at the project site were caused by traffic loads over an inadequate
pavement section underlain by subgrade soils with poor pavement support
characteristics Therefore it is our opinion that the existing deteriorated pavements
should be removed and replaced with a new pavement structural section constructed on
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4011 00 Page 7
a properly prepared subgrade Special attention should be given to the drainage
requirements since water infiltration into the underlying subsoils may result in subsoil
softening and loss of bearing support Detailed discussion of these items and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections of this
report
Pavement Design
Based on our field exploration performed for the subject project the existing
pavement structure is generally underlain by subsoils with poor pavement support
characteristics at shallow depths Therefore complete removal of the existing pavement
sections to expose the subgrade areas and replacement with compacted base
and subbase materials are recommended for reconstruction of the deteriorated
pavement In our pavement analyses we assume 64 buses per day and a design life
of 20 years It is our opinion that reconstruction with the following new pavement
sections may be used for the subject project
Flexible Pavement Section
3 5 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Basaltic Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Select Borrow 95 Percent Relative Compaction
15 5 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
Rigid Pavement Section
8 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
If it is desired to resurface the existing asphaltic concrete pavement to reduce
costs it is recommended to cold plane one inch of the existing asphaltic concrete and
overlay with three inches of new asphaltic concrete However it should be noted that the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4011 00 Page 8
resurfaced pavement does not have the same strength characteristics of a new pavement
and will have a design life of less than 20 years
The pavement subgrades should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The base
course and select borrow subbase for the flexible pavement section should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction as
determined by ASTM D 2922 91 Test Methods for Density of Soil and Soil Aggregate in
Place by Nuclear Methods Shallow Depth or ASTM D 1556 90 Test Method for Density
and Unit Weight of Soil in Place by the Sand Cone Method Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 Test Method for
Laboratory Compaction Characteristics of Soil Using Modified Effort
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and current scope of work we anticipate that
minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed pavement reconstruction
project At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Asphalt concrete paving vegetation debris rubbish
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft
and yielding areas encountered below pavement areas should be over excavated to
expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
After clearing grubbing and demolition the future pavement areas should be
excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the select
borrow layer The pavement subgrades should be moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4011 00 Page 9
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density If the
subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping subgrade may be
stabilized by cement treatment As a guide one sack of cement may be used for
approximately 25 square feet of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may be
proof rolled and a woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be
placed on the subgrade prior to placement of the select borrow layer
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be re scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due
to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with well compacted engineered fill Contract documents should include
additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to
account for variations in the over excavation quantities
Select borrow and base course materials required for the new pavements should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in 8 inch level loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Site grading operations
should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a
representative from our office observe the site grading operation to evaluate whether
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and whether the
exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered during our field exploration
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to the appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4011 00 Page 10
be allowed anywhere on site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used
to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the
curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the base and
subbase course layers to reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the
pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect
excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend
that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to paved landscaped areas
Design Review
Drawings and specifications for the proposed pavement reconstruction should be
forwarded to Geolabs Hawaii for review and written comments prior to advertisement for
bids This review is necessary to evaluate adherence of the plans and specifications to
the intent of the pavement design and earthwork recommendations provided herein If
this review is not made Geolabs Hawaii cannot assume responsibility for
misinterpretation of the recommendations presented herein
Construction Observation
It is recommended that Geolabs Hawaii be retained to provide geotechnical
engineering services during reconstruction of the existing pavements The items of
construction which are critical and require Special Inspection include observation of
subgrade preparation and fill placement and compaction This is to observe compliance
with the design concepts specifications or recommendations and to expedite
suggestions for design changes that may be required in the event that subsurface
conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared The
recommendations provided in this report are contingent upon such observations If actual
exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are different from those
assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the design should
be made

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from the field borings and laboratory test data Variations of
conditions between and beyond the field borings may occur and the nature and extent of
these variations may not become evident until pavement reconstruction is underway If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations
presented in this report
The locations of the field borings are approximate having been estimated by
taping from reference points and or visible features shown on the Site Location Plan
transmitted to our office by M E Pacific Inc on Elevations of the
borings were interpolated between the elevation points shown on the same plan The
locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered accurate only to the
degree implied by the methods used
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the borings depict
the approximate boundaries between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual
transition No free ground water level was observed from the borings at the time of our
field exploration However it must be noted that fluctuation on the water table may occur
due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

sampling six shallow borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 to a depth of up to
about 5 5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped

Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered in the borings is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6

ASTM D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One sieve size analysis test was performed on the sandy gravel materials to
evaluate the grain size distribution of the in place base course materials The test results
are presented on Plate B 1
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
