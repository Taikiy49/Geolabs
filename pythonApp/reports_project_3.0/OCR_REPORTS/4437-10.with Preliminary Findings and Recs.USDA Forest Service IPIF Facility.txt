shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to

rock formation respectively These bearing values may be increased by 50 percent for

suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer
spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The
probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected
at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about
6 to 9 inches
Due to the low structural loads we believe that the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from collapse of the cavities below the foundations is relatively small in
areas where the fill thickness is greater than 5 feet Therefore probing and grouting at
footing locations where fills are greater than 5 feet in thickness may be omitted
Rock Anchors
Due to the open nature of the covered pedestrian breezeways these structures will
be subjected to high uplift forces and overturning moments caused by wind The covered
pedestrian breezeways may resist the uplift forces by rock anchors embedded in the
medium hard to hard basalt rock formation
A rock anchor is constructed by drilling a borehole installing a steel tendon or
reinforcing bar into the borehole and grouting the borehole The basic components of a
grouted rock anchor consist of the anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of
the steel tendon or reinforcing bar In general the unbonded length of the rock anchor is
the portion of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with
the grout allowing the tendon or reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transferring
the resisting force from the structure to the bonded length The bonded length of the rock

2 1 Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano
overlying the Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed
to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

3 4 Uplift Resistance
Due to the open nature of the covered pedestrian walkways these structures will
be subjected to high uplift forces and overturning moments caused by transient loads
Uplift loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by the dead weight of the footings
and the side shear along the vertical faces of the footings A side shear value of 250 psf
may be used for estimating resistance to uplift forces Additional uplift resistance may be
obtained from the gravity loads of grade beams and or floor slabs provided that they are
structurally connected to the foundations
Uplift forces and overturning moments may also be resisted by rock anchors
embedded in the hard to very hard basalt formation A rock anchor is constructed by
drilling a borehole installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole and

anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar
In general the unbonded length of the rock anchor is the portion of the steel tendon or
reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact with the grout allowing the tendon or
reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transferring the resistance force from the
structure to the bonded length The bonded length of the rock anchor is the portion of the
steel tendon or reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer
the applied tensile load into the ground
In general the rock anchors would derive support principally from the bond
between the grout and the hard to very hard basalt rock formation encountered at the site

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using

method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality

APPENDIX B
Field Percolation Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was estimated by performing one constant head
percolation test in a selected boring location at a depth of about 25 feet below the existing
ground surface The test results are presented on Plate B 1
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until the
water surface had stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to
base calculation of the subsoil permeability
