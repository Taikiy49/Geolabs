REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
banking against its eastern flank
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised deep valleys
into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by
cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic
Series Due to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma many of the
eruptions resulted in steam explosions that expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material
consisting predominantly of ash and cinder Some of these eruptions produced ash that
cemented together rapidly to form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as mudrock
This type of cemented material underlies the project site Man made fills cover the
mudrock to the existing ground elevations
SOIL SURVEY
Soil survey maps were also used to evaluate the existing subsurface conditions at
the project site Based on our research the anticipated surface soil underlying the project
site are described by the U S Soil Conservation Service in their publication Soil Survey of
Islands of Molokai and Lanai State of Hawaii 1972 A soil survey
map showing the distribution of the major soil at the site is presented on Plate 2 The
major soil at the site and some of its general properties are as follows
Makalapa Clay MdB The soil is gently sloping with slopes ranging from about
2 to 6 percent The permeability is low and the erosion hazard is slight The soils
workability is difficult because the clay is sticky and very plastic The shrink swell
potential of these soils is low to moderate Corrosion potential to uncoated steel is
moderate and concrete is low

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to a depth of about 11 5 feet below the existing
ground surface In addition bulk samples were obtained of the soil materials below the
pavements to evaluate their pavement support characteristics The approximate locations
of the borings drilled and bulk samples collected are shown on the Site Plan 3
In general Boring No 1 encountered 2 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by
4 inches of Portland cement concrete Based on aerial photo circa 2007 a building was
located at Boring No 1 We believe that the Portland cement concrete encountered in the
boring could be the concrete floor slab of the building After demolition of the building we
believe that the concrete floor slab was left in place and an asphaltic concrete overlay was
placed above the concrete floor slab Boring Nos 2 through 4 encountered 6 inches of
asphaltic concrete It appears that several overlays have been placed over the original
asphaltic concrete surface
The asphaltic concrete and Portland cement concrete were underlain by medium
dense silty sand and gravel extending to a depth of about 1 foot below the existing ground
surface We encountered loose to medium dense sandy gravel weathered volcanic tuff
in Boring No 1 below the silty gravel and extending to the maximum depth explored
Boring Nos 2 through 4 encountered fill material below the silty sand and gravel which
extended to the maximum depth explored The fill material consisted of very stiff clayey
soils and loose silty sand
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall
time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors

preparation and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following

Pavements

raised an average of about 3 inches above the existing grades In general we anticipate
the vehicle loading for the parking lot will primarily consist of passenger vehicles light
pick up trucks and occasional large trucks Based on the Request For Proposal
two types of pavement reconstruction are planned for the existing parking lot Type I
which consists of removing the existing asphaltic concrete recompacting the existing base

We conducted pavement analyses to determine the minimum pavement thickness

Based on our field exploration the existing pavement at the Type I reconstruction
area may consist of either 2 inches of asphaltic concrete over 4 inches of Portland cement
concrete area where buildings were demolished or 6 inches of asphaltic concrete The
asphaltic concrete and Portland cement concrete are underlain by medium dense silty
sand and gravel extending to a depth of about 1 foot below the existing ground surface
Due to the relatively thick asphaltic and Portland cement concrete layers we envision that
new base course material will be required after removal of the asphaltic and Portland
cement concrete layers at the Type I reconstruction area We anticipate that a minimum
of about 4 inches of new base course material will be required to bring the base course
layer up to bottom of new asphaltic concrete elevation at the Type I reconstruction area
Based on our field exploration the existing pavement at the Type II reconstruction
area may consist of 6 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by medium dense silty sand
extending to a depth of about 1 foot below the existing ground surface After removal of
the asphaltic concrete a thin layer of the existing granular fill material below the asphaltic
concrete would need to be removed to accommodate the new pavement section
recommended above
After removal of the asphaltic and Portland cement concrete layers and portion of
the in situ granular fill material we recommend moisture conditioning and recompacting
the pavement subgrade to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The new
aggregate base course placed over the recompacted subgrade should consist of crushed
basaltic aggregates compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry the surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material pavements
slabs and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated
to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be disposed properly off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
After clearing and grubbing areas receiving fill and finished subgrades should be
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and recompacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately
25 square feet of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a
woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of subsequent lifts of fill
The excavated on site soils and recycled asphaltic concrete may be re used as a
source of general fill materials provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious
materials and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum
dimension It should be noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as
guidelines only and may be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and
field observations on the available fill materials
Imported fill materials needed to fill the site should consist of select granular fill such
as crushed coral basalt or recycled asphaltic concrete The material should be well
graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The
material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher
and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Fill and backfill materials required for the project construction should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Aggregate base course required should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and
should conform to Subsection 703 06 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Roads and
Bridge Construction 2005 Aggregate base course should be moisture conditioned to

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to a depth of about 11 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 We used a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by laboratory testing Soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are provided on
the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer tests results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results
are provided on Plate B 1
Six Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates B 2 and B 3
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils and potential borrow material to evaluate the dry density
and moisture content relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 6
