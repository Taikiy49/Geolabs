REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) and forms 
the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while Koolau 
was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking 
against its eastern flank. Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their original 
shield outlines and are now long, narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion. 
Following the cessation of the Koolau Volcano and about a 2-million-year period 
of volcanic inactivity dominated by erosional processes, volcanic activity on the 
southeastern end of the Koolau Range resumed with the eruption of some 30 or so vents 
which represent the Honolulu Volcanic Series. These secondary eruptions are believed 
to have occurred scattered throughout a period of hundreds of thousands of years 
extending from the late Pleistocene Age to the Recent Age. 
The project site abuts the north-facing slope of the Diamond Head Crater. Diamond 
Head is a tuff cone formed during the Honolulu Volcanic Series by hydromagmatic 
eruption and consists of volcanic ash that has been altered by weathering to palagonite. 
Furthermore, there are scattered blocks of Koolau basalt material and fragments of coral 
limestone embedded in the tuff deposits. These materials were entrained by the 
hydromagmatic explosions as the vent blasted through the underlying and older age 
Koolau basalt and coral reef deposits.

The subject slopes are near the south side of the property boundary near the future 
vehicle turnaround and restaurant building being constructed for the KCC Culinary 
Institute of the Pacific Phase 2 improvements. Based on the information provided, we 
understand that outdoor planter boxes and grilling stations are planned near the base of

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In addition to our field reconnaissance, our evaluation of the potential rockfall 
hazards at the project site included the performance of a statistical analysis of potential 
rockfall activity using the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program (CRSP) Version 4. 
Based on our evaluation, we believe the existing slope within the project limits may 
contain multiple rockfall hazards. Therefore, the following discussions are presented to 
aid the owner (the University of Hawaii) in selecting a rockfall mitigation scheme. The key 
design elements to be considered are vegetation clearing, rock slope scaling, rockfall 
catchment areas, and draped wire mesh installation. Discussions of each of these 
elements are presented in the following sections. 
Due to the area being an active construction site with nearby buildings under 
construction, adequate temporary rockfall barriers should be installed at the base of the 
slopes prior to the start of rockfall construction activities to protect workers, vehicles, and 
nearby structures. The temporary barriers should be able to resist the anticipated kinetic 
energy of the falling boulders and be constructed to an adequate height to catch boulders 
that may bounce an appreciable height. 
Rockfall Mechanisms 
Based on the information provided, we understand that there is a drainage swale 
behind the existing chain-link face above the top-of-slopes in the project area. Therefore, 
we anticipate that excessive stormwater does not overtop and run over the face of the 
slopes within the project limits. Therefore, based on our site reconnaissance, it appears 
that the main mechanism generating rockfall hazards at the site is raveling failure. 
Raveling of one or several rocks from the slope may occur due to several causes. 
The hazard develops when the raveling rock falls and has sufficient velocity and volume 
to impact and injure/damage people near the base of the slope or adjacent structures. 
Raveling may be caused by water pressure behind the rock, differential weathering or 
erosion along faults or shear zones, weakening of rock due to temperature and moisture 
cyclic fluctuations, loosening by animals, and/or loosening by trees and wind. Due to the 
weathered condition of the volcanic tuff slope surfaces in the project area, raveling is an 
important process that continually works to shed weathered and broken rock materials 
from the slope. 
One of the most frequent causes of raveling is by invasive tree roots prying on the 
existing cracks located on the rock slope surface or at the top-of-slope. Tree roots 
(especially of the larger trees) frequently develop and grow larger in the discontinuities of 
rock materials. The added force of wind action on tree limbs and branches may create 
leveraged forces that are transferred to the roots lodged within the discontinuity. Over

Rockfall Simulation Analysis 
As previously discussed, site reconnaissance was performed to visually identify 
and locate potential rockfall hazards, such as loose surface boulders and unstable rock 
outcroppings, within the project study area. The site reconnaissance also permitted us to 
evaluate probable rolling trajectories in relation to the existing topographic features 
encountered in the field. Following the site reconnaissance, selected information recorded 
in the field was input to the CRSP computer program to validate the field evaluation.  
The CRSP computer program is a widely accepted engineering tool used by 
experienced personnel to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall 
activity based on input parameters that are assigned on a site-specific basis. The input 
parameters for this project were based on the observations and measurements collected 
in the field. The program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior 
based on the typical hillslope topographic profile and other input information such as rock 
size, shape, and parameters used to describe ground surface irregularities. Information 
obtained from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity, bouncing 
height, kinetic energy, and roll-out distance. The output information is useful to assist in 
the site-specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes, such as rockfall impact 
barriers or other rockfall containment systems.  
Based on our site reconnaissance, the available topographic survey information 
does not match the existing slope topography at the project site. Therefore, the slope 
profiles used in our CRSP analyses were estimated from our field measurements.  
Based on our preliminary CRSP analyses, we estimate that a significant amount 
of boulders will travel at least 10 feet beyond the toe of the slope. Therefore, because 
outdoor activity areas are planned at the base of the slopes, we believe that a rockfall 
mitigation scheme is necessary for the project site.  
Rockfall Mitigation Design Alternatives 
Based on our site reconnaissance and our engineering analyses, we believe there 
are potential rockfall hazards at the project site. Therefore, priority should be assigned to 
implementing a rockfall mitigation system at the project site.

As previously mentioned, based on our CRSP analyses, the potential rockfall 
hazards at the project site may reach up to 10 feet beyond the toe of the slope under the 
existing site conditions. We understand that it is desired to maximize the space at the 
base of the slope for outdoor activity areas. Therefore, we do not believe that a rockfall 
setback area in excess of 10 feet wide would be desirable for the project. Based on these 
considerations, we analyzed the effectiveness of rockfall catchment ditches of differing 
widths and geometries constructed at the base of the slopes. Our analyses showed that 
approximately 90 percent or more of simulated spherical boulders about 1 to 2 feet in 
diameter would be retained by the 10-foot-wide catchment ditch. To further decrease the 
amount of potential rockfall hazards and provide additional space at the base of the slope, 
a draped wire mesh system may be considered. The installation of a draped wire mesh 
system on the slope face serves to control the release of loose rock that may develop on 
the slope face in the years following the scaling work. A setback zone (about 5 feet wide) 
should be provided at the base of the slope for the fallen debris. 
Based on the above considerations, we believe that there are two potentially viable 
long-term rockfall protection systems for the project site and are listed below.  
1. 
The first option consists of slope vegetation removal, major scaling of the 
loose rocks from the slope face, and installation of a rockfall catchment 
ditch.  
2. 
The second option consists of slope vegetation removal, major scaling of 
the loose rocks from the slope face, and installation of draped wire mesh 
system on the prepared slope surface.  
For the first option, the useful life of the rockfall catchment ditch may be up to about 
50 years or more, provided that maintenance is performed to remove accumulated 
rockfall debris in the catchment ditch on a regular basis or after significant rockfall events. 
For the second option, the useful life of the draped wire mesh system may be about 
10 to 15 years (or the life of the draped wire mesh based on the corrosive environment at 
the project site). In addition, regular maintenance should also be performed to remove 
fallen debris at the base of the slope within the small setback zone.

aid the client in selecting a system for the KCC Culinary Institute of the Pacific Phase 2 –

Rock Slope Scaling 
Rock slope scaling consists of experienced crews employing manual labor and 
hand tools to remove loose rocks and precariously positioned rock fragments from 
the slope face. The scaling crews performing the rock slope scaling should be 
experienced in the performance of rock slope scaling by rappelling and/or working 
from crane baskets. Rock slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting from 
the top of the slope and working downward in a controlled and orderly manner in 
conjunction with the combined progress of all the scaling crews working on the 
slope face. Rock slope scaling should be typically performed on slopes with a slope 
inclination steeper than about 2H:1V. 
As previously mentioned, adequate temporary rockfall barriers should be installed 
near the base of the slopes prior to commencing the rock slope scaling work. The 
temporary barriers should be able to resist the anticipated kinetic energy of the 
falling boulders and be constructed to an adequate height to catch boulders that 
may bounce an appreciable height. 
Rockfall Catchment Ditch 
Rockfall catchment ditches are designed to capture and retain cobbles and 
boulders at the base of the slope. The side slopes of the ditch are designed to 
absorb the impact forces of the fallen material and redirect the rocks back towards 
the slope. Based on our analyses, we recommend a minimum 10-foot-wide, 
2.5-foot-deep catchment ditch with a front slope inclination of 4H:1V and a 
backslope inclination of 0.5H:1V. The catchment ditch should be surfaced with 
concrete to match the adjacent concrete slabs-on-grade or surfaced with topsoil 
and grass. A low barrier, such as a chain link fence, should be constructed in front 
of the catchment ditch to deter pedestrian access. The rockfall catchment ditch 
should have adequate drainage to prevent the accumulation of standing water 
within the ditch. A typical rockfall catchment ditch detail is presented on Plate 4.

Draped Wire Mesh System 
Once the rock slope scaling work has been completed, installation of the draped 
wire mesh may proceed. The draped wire mesh should assist in the controlled 
release of loose rock that is shed from the slope face during weathering and 
erosion. Therefore, it is not the primary intent to closely pin the wire mesh to the 
slope face as a retention system. A typical detail of the draped wire mesh system 
is provided on Plate 5.  
The draped wire mesh should provide positive rockfall control for the useful life of 
the wire mesh material. The draped wire mesh cover over the slope essentially 
contains broken rock material against the slope face as the fallen rock materials 
move downward by gravity. In this way, the rock fragments are deposited along 
the mesh outlet located at the base of the slope. A low barrier, such as a chain link 
fence, should be constructed a minimum distance of 2 feet away from the base of 
the slope to deter pedestrian access and help to contain debris. 
Because of Hawaii’s tropical climate, the draped wire mesh and hardware should 
be corrosion resistant. Therefore, we recommend that the draped wire mesh and 
hardware be galvanized with coatings such as zinc-aluminum and fused PVC, as 
a minimum. The useful life of the draped wire mesh is estimated to be about 10 to 
15 years, depending on the quality of the draped wire mesh, hardware, and 
workmanship employed during the draped wire mesh installation. Alternatively, 
stainless steel wire mesh may be considered for longer life.

Close Off Area and Install Fencing 
We understand that the rockfall mitigation design alternatives presented previously 
may be cost prohibitive. Therefore, we understand that the University of Hawaii has chosen 
to forego constructing the outdoor barbeque and planter box areas that were planned at the 
base of the subject slopes. Based on the information provided, we understand that this area 
will be fenced off to prevent pedestrians from entering near the base of the slopes that were 
identified to have potential rockfall hazards. 
Based on our additional rockfall simulation analyses, we recommend constructing 
the chain link fence a minimum distance of 5 feet from the toe of the slope. We understand 
that the fence alignment is closest to the toe of the existing slope near the western end of 
the project limits. Therefore, an additional site visit was conducted by two of our engineers 
(Arshia Taeb and Nick Kam) on July 12, 2023, to observe the conditions of this section of 
slope relative to the planned fence alignment. During our site visit, we observed a significant 
amount of manmade debris (concrete blocks, barbed wire, scrap metal) on the top of the 
slope. We also observed some loose volcanic tuff material. Therefore, for the western 
section of slope that is within a 5-foot lateral offset from the new fence alignment, we 
recommend clearing all loose material from the top of the slope and the slope face. The 
loose debris may be cleared by manual methods by an on-site laborer (i.e. does not need 
to conform to normal slope scaling requirements performed by a specialty rockfall 
contractor).  
The section of chain link fence that is offset 5 feet from the base of the slope should 
consist of thicker support posts and heavier gauge fence mesh. The support posts should 
be a minimum of 3 inches in diameter and spaced at 7 feet on-center. In addition, we 
recommend 6-gauge fence mesh. Standard fencing posts and mesh may be utilized for the 
remaining fence alignment where offset distances are greater than 5 feet from the toe of the 
slope.
