2 1 Regional Geology
The island of Hawaii is composed of five shield volcanoes Kohala Mauna Kea
and Kilauea Three of these volcanoes and
Kilauea have been active during recent history
The site is situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa on rocks of the
Prehistoric Member of the Kau Volcanic Series In the area of the site these rocks are
typified by thick a a basalt lava flows with a surface of coarse spiny fragmental clinker
generally overlain by a thin mantle of mucky organic soil
The surface soils in the area of the property have been classified by the
U S Department of Agriculture as Keaukaha extremely stony muck rKFD These soils
are extremely thin organic soils developed over basic igneous lavas They are very well
drained Runoff is slow to medium and the erosion hazard is slight to moderate At the
site previous grading work appears to have removed most of the topsoil layer

The site for the proposed Oceanic Time Warner Cable Hilo Office is in a

District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The site is bounded by A ala Lane to the
north and existing mixed commercial and residential developments to the north east
and west as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Portions of the site were previously graded to create a relatively level lot area for
an existing building other portions may have been cleared and grubbed in the past
however a relatively thick ground cover of subtropical vegetation has become
re established Existing ground surface elevations range from 30 to 45 feet above Mean
Sea Level Site grading consisting of relatively shallow cuts and fills is anticipated for
the project

Site Preparation

unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site Soft and or
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to

prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in
the over excavation quantities

that cut slopes for this project will expose competent granular materials In general

Voids and cavities are commonly encountered in basalt rock formations
Although no voids or cavities were encountered by our exploration it is our opinion that
due to the nature of the site development a program of cavity probing and grouting in
subgrade areas and in major foundation systems should be implemented Probing and
grouting essentially consists of drilling an array of probe holes to depths of about 10 feet
below the finish grade In general we recommend that the probes be spaced at about
8 foot centers across the containment area and at about 5 foot centers along major
foundations Cement grout should be pumped at low pressure in the drilled probe holes
in an effort to stabilize the buried voids encountered by the probe holes against potential
collapse Due to the granular nature of the upper clinker formation at the site and the
associated difficulties in maintaining clean open holes for injection of the grout we
recommend that grout pipes be placed to the bottom of each hole and that the grout be
placed as the pipes are withdrawn
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by our representative prior
to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Because the project site is on the seismically active Island of Hawaii
Seismic Zone 4 in accordance with the 1997 Uniform Building Code forces due to
dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining
structures The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be
estimated using 16H pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions
where H is the height of the wall in feet For a maximum 3H 1V sloping backfill
condition dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated
using 28H pounds per lineal foot of wall
It should be noted that the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures provided
assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally in the event of an earthquake
by up to about 2 to 4 inches If this lateral movement amount is not acceptable the
retaining wall should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a
semi restrained wall condition For a semi restrained wall condition the forces due
to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be estimated using 28H pounds per lineal
foot of wall for level backfill conditions
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the
mid height of the wall The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures An appropriately reduced
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted
for in the design of the retaining structure
It should be noted that due to relatively high dynamic lateral forces sloping backfill
behind the retaining structures should be limited to an inclination of 3H 1V or less
The sloping backfill inclination of 3H 1V should extend beyond a 45 degree
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the retaining wall
footing The sloping backfill may be steepened to a maximum slope inclination of
2H 1V behind this horizontal setback distance

Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up
of hydrostatic pressures
For site retaining walls the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic foot of
permeable material such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped with non woven filter fabric placed at each of the weephole locations The
weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the permeable drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining wall
Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist
of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration behind the retaining structure

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings ranged from about 5 0 to 16 0 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with rotary coring tools
The subsurface conditions were also explored by excavating and sampling
four test pits designated as Test Pits Nos 1 through 4 at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our test pits ranged from about 2 7 to
4 0 feet below the existing ground surface The test pits were excavated using an
excavator and were terminated upon excavator refusal
The materials encountered in the borings and test pits were classified by visual
and textural examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
