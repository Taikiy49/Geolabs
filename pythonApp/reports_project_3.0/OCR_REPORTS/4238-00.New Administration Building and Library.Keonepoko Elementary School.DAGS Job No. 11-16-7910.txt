INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Administration Building and Library project at Keonepoko
Elementary School in Pahoa on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report

The project site is located within the existing Keonepoko Elementary School in the

east of the existing cafetorium and north of the existing parking lot The new building will
be a one story concrete structure covering approximately 4 600 square feet in area The

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These flows are of the Pleistocene Epoch
and Recent Age and belong to the Ka u volcanic series The lava flows were
subsequently covered by volcanic ash deposits
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which
is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a very thin surficial layer of sandy soils
overlying hard basalt rock formation at shallow depths Based on the relatively competent
subsoil conditions encountered in our borings and the anticipated structural loads it is our
opinion that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed administration building and library Based on the existing topography and
proposed finished floor elevations for the project we anticipate that the new
administration building and library may straddle across minor cuts of about 1 foot and fills
of up to about 5 feet thick In order to provide more uniform support for the foundation
and to reduce the potential for foundation distress resulting from differing subgrade
conditions we recommend that the footings be extended through the surficial fill materials
and bear on the underlying basalt rock formation for support of the proposed buildings
For more uniform support of slabs on grade we recommend that concrete slabs be
supported on at least 12 inches of granular fill material
Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic
lava flows we recommend that the slab subgrades be proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton
vibratory drum roller a minimum of 6 passes to aid in detecting and collapsing
near surface cavities in addition a program of probing and grouting the footing locations
should be implemented during the project construction A representative of
Geolabs Hawaii should observe the probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent
of cavities if found Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
design recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

Building Foundations
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed buildings
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevations we anticipate
that the new buildings may straddle across cut and fill areas In order to provide more
uniform support for the foundation and to reduce the potential for foundation distress
resulting from differing subgrade conditions we recommend that the footings be extended
through the surficial fill materials and bear on the underlying basalt rock formation for
support of the proposed buildings
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 5 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation
encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Depending on the depths of the basalt rock formation deeper
excavations may be required at localized areas to provide a competent bearing surface
for the footing foundations If foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep
as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of

Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be
present in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a
footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented
during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled at each
spread footing location and at 10 feet on center along wall foundations The probe holes
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probeholes should be drilled at closer
spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The
probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected
at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout
mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations

Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a

Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the proposed buildings may straddle cut and fill
transitions To reduce the potential for differential settlements at the cut and fill transition
due to differing subgrade materials we recommend that concrete slabs be supported on
at least 12 inches of granular fill material The intent of the 12 inch granular fill layer is to
reduce the potential for slab cracking due to differential settlements at the cut and fill
transition Prior to placement of the 12 inch granular fill the subgrade soils should be
prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation
section of this report
In addition to the 12 inches of granular fill a basaltic termite barrier BTB should
be incorporated into the slab on grade design of the proposed buildings in accordance
with the requirements of D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61 dated
Therefore for the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of BTB be placed below the
slabs to serve as a termite barrier and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To
reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the BTB cushion layer Two inches of BTB may be considered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4238 00 Page 10
above the plastic membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing
of the slab concrete
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of BTB be provided below the
concrete walkway slab Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of
the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
relatively impervious soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the foundation
Retaining Walls
Retaining walls will be required for grade separation at the project site The
following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of retaining structures
Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations
section of this report Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches
Wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground
conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot set back
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of
the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
in lieu of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of select granular fill
material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of low expansion relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for
water infiltration behind the walls
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated finished floor elevations of
the proposed buildings we anticipate that site grading work consisting of minor cuts of
about 1 foot and fills of up to about 5 feet thick will be required for the project
construction At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should
be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The surficial sandy soils
should be removed and may be stockpiled for use in landscaped areas if appropriate
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted engineered fill
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should

Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed building sites are underlain at
shallow depths by volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard It is anticipated
that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such
as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the existing ground surface
However excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams
chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing rock formations and field data from the borings
drilled at the site We recommend that all contractors proposing to bid on this project be
encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own
interpretation

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the sites were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to a depth of about 20 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous
flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representation of the materials encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 4
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling
is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks
If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation
