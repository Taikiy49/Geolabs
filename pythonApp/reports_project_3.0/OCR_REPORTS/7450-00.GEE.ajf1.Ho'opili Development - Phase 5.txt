SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered clayey surficial fills and 
cultivated soils approximately 1 to 3 feet thick overlying alluvial soils extending down to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
alluvial soils generally consisted of medium stiff to hard silty clay, dense to very dense silty 
sand and gravel with varying amounts of cobbles and boulders, and hard sandy silt.  
We encountered groundwater in Boring No. 2 at a depth of about 14 feet below the 
existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the remaining borings and 
test pits at the time of our field exploration. We believe the groundwater level observed in 
the boring may be attributed to a leaking irrigation line. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on our laboratory test results, the near-surface clayey soils generally exhibit 
low to high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture 
contents. Based on the cut and fill thicknesses shown on the preliminary mass grading plan 
provided, we anticipate the near-surface soils underlying the planned buildings and 
pavements will consist of the clayey soil with low to high shrink/swell potential. Considering 
the lightly loaded structures planned for the Phase 5 development, it is anticipated that the 
low to high shrink-swell potential of the on-site soils could result in structural distresses to 
the new buildings and pavements. To reduce the risk of structural distress, we recommend 
implementing special subgrade preparations during construction.  
After the clearing and grubbing operations to remove the existing vegetation, 
debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials, the upper 12 inches of the 
cultivated soils below the existing ground surface should be removed and disposed 
properly off-site (or used in landscaped areas, if appropriate) because they contain an 
appreciable amount of organic materials.  
Upon removal of the upper 12 inches of cultivated soils, we recommend 
over-excavating an additional 12 inches of soil in areas designated to receive fill and 
moisture-conditioning the soil to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content 
for use as general fill. Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as general fill, the 
bottom of the over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. It should be noted that the 
2-foot thick reworked soil layer should also be constructed below the finished subgrade 
and pad grade in cut areas.

The intent of the above site preparation recommendation is to create a 2-foot thick 
reworked clayey soil layer to close-up the existing shrinkage cracks observed throughout 
the site and reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by preparing the clayey soils 
with elevated moisture contents and compaction reduction. In addition, the reworked 
clayey soil with elevated moisture contents would decrease the affinity of the dry clayey 
soil for water and create a barrier to reduce water infiltration into the underlying dry clayey 
soil.  
Due to the hard, dry and friable in-situ clayey soils, the materials generated from the 
over-excavation and scarification process and/or from cut areas would likely contain large 
clumps. To moisture-condition the soils effectively, the clayey clumps should be thoroughly 
broken down and mixed by the compaction equipment, such as large sheepsfoot 
compactors and/or large dozers, until achieving a relatively uniform moisture condition. 
Contractors bidding on this project should anticipate processing the on-site soils to break 
down the hard clayey clumps and achieve a relatively uniform moisture content. 
The on-site clayey soils should not be allowed to dry out and should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site 
clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks develop, the soils should be scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and compacted again. 
Based on our field exploration and analyses, we believe the lightly loaded residential 
building structures may be supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on 
the 2-foot reworked soil layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The 
Wafflemat is a post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. We recommend using 
12-inch high Waffleboxes to provide a stiffer foundation system in order to reduce differential 
movement and resulting angular distortion. We also recommend installing a vapor barrier 
underneath the Waffleboxes to a minimum of 2 feet beyond the slab perimeter to reduce 
water infiltration beneath the slab foundations. 
It is critical to keep the subgrade soil moist during the entire mass grading and site 
work phases. The individual homeowners and Homeowner’s Associations should be made 
aware of the potential for recurrence of shrinkage cracks, and appropriate maintenance 
measures should be implemented to reduce the potential for recurrence of shrinkage cracks 
around the building foundation.

geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through 
the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is generally situated at the west-central portion of the Ho`opili 
Development, east and north of the future Phases 2 and 4 development sites, respectively, 
in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bordered on all sides by existing 
agricultural land.  
At the time of our field exploration, the site was generally being used for 
commercial farming of various vegetable and herb crops; however, portions of the farming 
areas appeared to have been recently tilled possibly in preparation for future planting. In 
addition, several buildings surrounded by rows of palm trees were observed in the central 
portion of the site. We understand these buildings were used for Aloun Farms’ annual 
Pumpkin Patch festival.  
Several undeveloped pockets of vacant land were observed throughout the project 
site with light vegetation ground cover consisting of weed grasses. Several unpaved 
access roads were observed traversing the site and were covered with coralline sand and 
gravel. Field observations during our exploration indicated the surface soils at the project 
site were dry and friable. In addition, we observed shrinkage cracks about 6 inches deep 
at the existing ground surface at various locations throughout the project site at the time 
of our field exploration. 
Based on our site observations and topographic survey map provided, the project 
site generally slopes gently down from north to south at an average slope gradient of 
about 2 percent. Based on the information provided, ground surface elevations range 
from about +143 to +114 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the north and southeastern 
portions of the project site, respectively.

3.1.2 Site Perimeter Cut-Off Trench 
Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa/Kapolei

site preparation procedures consisting of installing a cut-off trench along the

above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The intent of this cut-off trench is to create a barrier to reduce water 
infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil from the shrinkage cracks on the 
adjacent properties.

hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the 
base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, 
a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or 
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of 
the retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to 
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure 
unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface.

Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0),

were performed (by our office and Test America Laboratories, Inc.) on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils. 
Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from approximately 540 to 950 ohm-cm and 
pH values ranging from approximately 5.8 to 7.1 within the upper 8 feet of soils, 
corresponding to a corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) based on the guidelines 
provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply.  
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a corrosion 
rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of 
metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider 
the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Levels of the chloride and sulfate content of the on-site soils encountered near the 
existing ground surface are relatively low on the order of about 150 parts per million (ppm) 
and 160 ppm or less, respectively. Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests 
performed on the on-site soils, we believe that either Type I or Type II cement may be 
used for concrete in contact with the ground planned for the project. It may be appropriate 
to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide 
detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

3.7.3 Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on our field exploration, the pavement subgrades along the proposed new 
roadway alignments are generally underlain by stiff to hard clay. Laboratory testing 
performed on the surface clayey soils at the project site generally indicated the 
on-site clayey soils exhibit poor to fair pavement support characteristics. Therefore, 
we recommend placing the pavement sections on a minimum 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive, select granular fill material.  
The select granular fill material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more 
and a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. Therefore, a design CBR value of 20 and a maximum swell of less 
than 1 percent with a subgrade of resilient modulus (MR) of 17,380 pounds per 
square inch (psi) were adopted in our pavement analyses for the select granular fill 
subgrade conditions.

3.7.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
As mentioned previously, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry and 
friable and exhibit low to high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive select granular fill, we recommend that the subgrade be properly 
scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction, as specified in Section 3.1.1 – Site Preparation herein. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to re-compacting.

3.7.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell 
of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material requirements 
for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 31 and 30, 
respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, City & 
County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use. 
3.7.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches 
in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement 
for the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course and should be 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional check for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the

3.7.8 Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to isolate 
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the 
curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base or subbase 
layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water into the 
pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be constructed to collect 
the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term performance, we 
recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the paved/landscaped 
areas.

appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench backfill below 
the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative 
compaction.

Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed water 
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. Surface 
water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It is highly 
recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert discharge away 
from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations 
and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. Planters next to 
foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the 
potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, four test pits, designated as Test 
Pit Nos. 1 through 4, were excavated to depths ranging from about 8 to 10.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Four bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 
through Bulk-4, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. The approximate boring, test pit, and bulk sample locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers. The test pits were excavated using a Hitachi 200 Excavator 
with a 39-inch wide digging bucket. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings and test pits by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling and 
excavation operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were 
further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering 
Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits, Plates A-1 
through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths.

A P P E N D I X   B 
Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings and Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of 
the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) including two hydrometer tests 
(ASTM D422) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentation of the 
grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-2. 
Ten one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Three Expansion Index of Soil tests were performed on selected samples in 
general accordance with ASTM D4829. The test is a standardized test used to determine 
the expansive characteristics of the soil. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4. 
Twelve one-dimensional swell or settlement potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is remolded 
to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to simulate the 
anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle until it has 
stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil sample to evaluate 
its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-5 through B-7. 
One Consolidation test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the 
compressibility characteristic of the on-site soils. The test was performed in accordance 
with ASTM D2435, and the test results are plotted on Plate B-8. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-9 and B-10.

Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples in accordance with ASTM D2850 to evaluate 
the undrained shear strength of the soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” samples. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-11 and B-12. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method A) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-13 through B-16. 
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-17 through B-20. 
Three sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-21.
