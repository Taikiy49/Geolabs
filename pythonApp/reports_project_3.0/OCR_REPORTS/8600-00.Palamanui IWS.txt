Geolabs, Inc. is pleased to submit the results of our percolation testing performed 
at the proposed project. This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical 
recommendations derived from our field percolation testing only. These geotechnical 
recommendations are intended to assist the client in the design of individual wastewater 
systems (IWS) at the project site. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site (TMK: 7-2-5: 001) is in the Palamanui area in the District of 
North Kona on the Island of Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
New IWS with a septic tank and leach field will be constructed at three locations. 
To aid in the design of the IWS, three percolation tests were performed near the proposed 
leach field locations in accordance with the State of Hawaii, Department of Health's 
Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, "Wastewater Systems" and Chapter 10 of the 
Ten State’s Standards to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface soils 
for disposal of wastewater effluent.  
Percolation Testing 
Three percolation tests, designated as P-1 through P-3, were performed near the 
proposed leach field locations in general accordance with the procedures outlined by the 
Department of Health for the design of IWS.  The locations of the percolation tests are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

The percolation tests were performed in borings drilled down to about 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Approximately 1 inch of gravel was placed at the bottom of 
the boring to protect the bottom from scouring and sediments. 
During testing, the test holes were filled with water and refilled as necessary. The 
time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the 
calculation of the percolation rates were based.  It should be noted that due to the 
relatively high permeability of the subsurface materials, water build-up was not measured 
in the test holes. 
Based on the percolation tests conducted, the percolation rates are less than one 
minute per inch at the test elevations for P-1 through P-3. In accordance with the "Manual 
of Septic-Tank Practice" prepared by the U.S. Department of Health, Education, and 
Welfare, the leach field location with the percolation rate obtained at the site would be 
suitable for siting of the absorption system at these locations. The percolation test results 
are presented on Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
Limitations 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from our percolation testing. Variations of subsoil conditions beyond 
the testing locations may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The percolation test locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by obtaining coordinates using a hand-held GPS device.  The coordinates 
obtained were overlaid on a Google Earth map.  Ground surface elevations at the test 
locations were provided by Nan, Inc. The physical locations and ground elevations of the 
percolation test locations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the 
methods used. 
This letter report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Nan, Inc. for the 
specific application for the design of the new IWS at the project site as described herein 
in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices. 
No warranty is expressed or implied. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered. Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft deposits, 
hard layers or cavities, may occur in localized areas and may require additional probing 
or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to attain a properly 
constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is recommended to 
accommodate these possible extra costs.
