SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the four work packages for Task Order
No 0009 Base Camp Improvements the base camp site is
generally underlain by stiff residual and alluvial soils consisting
of silts and clays with varying amounts of sands gravel
cobbles and boulders The predominant soil type encountered
along the Base Camp Road B alignment is the clayey residual
and alluvial soil type The clayey residual and alluvial soils
consist of clays with fine sands and gravel that are slightly to
moderately expansive
The Base Camp Road A Helipads and DRMO Fuel Storage
Facility project sites have generally more uniform soil materials
Overall the surface soil deposits were observed as generally a
thin soil cover overlying an irregular bedrock surface consisting
of dense basalt rock formation The near surface soils exist in
a relatively dry state and are susceptible to erosion due to wind
and or surface water runoff It should be noted that the
near surface soils at the Helipads appear to be moderately to
highly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations
In general the excavated on site soils may not be suitable for
use as a source of controlled fill material considering the
moderately expansive nature of the soils and the low CBR
values of the soils generally less than 12 However we
believe that the excavated on site soils may be re used as a
source of general fill provided that the materials are free of
vegetation and deleterious materials and the particle size is less
than 150 mm in maximum dimension In addition the
excavated on site materials may also be used as a source of
materials for the soil cement pavement structure provided that
GEOLABS INC
W O 4161 00 Page 2
an adequate amount of cement is added to the soil to produce
a soil cement product with a minimum unconfined compressive
strength of at least 5 MPa Based on our soil cement tests we
believe that a cement content of at least 26 percent by weight
would be needed to produce the specified unconfined
compressive strength for the soil cement pavement The
soil cement pavement structure for the access roads should be
at least 150 mm in thickness
The near surface on site soils exist in a relatively dry state and
will require a substantial amount of water and water trucks for
transport during construction to achieve the specified moisture
content of the soils for subgrade preparation controlled fill
placement and soil cement mixing For preliminary estimating
purposes we believe that approximately 160 to 240 liters with
an average of about 200 liters of water would be needed for one
cubic meter of subgrade preparation controlled fill placement
and soil cement mixing It should be emphasized that
moisture conditioning of the subgrade soils is a critical element
of earthwork considering the slightly to moderately expansive
nature of the on site clayey soils
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific design recommendations

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of Maui and
about 27 kilometers to the southeast of Lana i as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 1
Kaho olawe is approximately 117 square kilometers in land area with a maximum elevation
of about 450 meters above sea level at the Lua Makika summit on the eastern portion of the
island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layered thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the rift zones and caldera at Lua Makika The primary eruptive rift zones of
the former volcano extend westerly from the summit and to a lesser degree to the north and
east from the summit Two cinder cone remnants can be seen located at Puu Moiwi and Puu
Kamama The area at Lua Kealialalo is believed to represent the weathered remains of a
collapsed crater at the crest of the broad lava shield Some of the more recent aged rocks
composing the upper caldera filling layers of the island have been dated at about 1 million
years old
Intermittent stream flow has incised gullies and some deep gulches into the residual
soil and weathered rock surface of the island The climate is generally arid due to the
island s leeward location with respect to the Island of Maui Most rainfall occurs during large
Kona storm events and as light misting showers carried by the tradewinds The
geomorphology of the island is most significantly affected by wind erosion which has created
a generally barren hard pan soil surface The lower slopes of the island show exposure of
bare rock with thin soil covering suggesting that some ocean wave erosion had occurred
GEOLABS INC
W O 4161 00 Page 9
during higher stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch It is believed that much of the
soil cover existing below about Elevation 240 meters Mean Sea Level MSL was
historically swept into the ocean by a large tsunami Similar large scale soil erosion and coral
fragment deposition have been found on the Islands of Maui and Lana i

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field reconnaissance and laboratory test results we believe that the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for design and construction of the
proposed Task Order No 0009 Base Camp Improvements Work Packages 9
include the following
Generally thin soil cover over shallow rock underlie the majority of the Base
Camp project area Therefore the proposed site grading should consider fill
placement as opposed to excavation into the shallow rock materials
e Excavation of the planned cut slopes is anticipated to encounter some hard
basalt rock which may produce boulders or large blocks of rock material that
may require disposal off site or processing for use as general fill
GEOLABS INC
W O 4161 00 Page 15
The near surface soils are relatively dry and will require a significant amount
of water to moisture condition the soils for site preparation grading and
soil cement mixing
The fine grained soils at the Base Camp project sites are generally slightly to
moderately expansive In addition the soils at the helicopter landing pads are
moderately to highly expansive when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture
content Therefore special attention should be given to the recommendations
for site preparation considering the expansive soil conditions and the generally
dry weather conditions of the site
e Excavation of the near surface soils may encounter soil clumps cobbles and
boulders which would affect the mixing of the on site soils to form a
soil cement pavement structure especially in cut subgrades
e Proper backfilling around the excavated areas and grading to shed water away
from and around the concrete pads pavements and helipads
e Based on the laboratory test data the Base Camp project area may be
underlain by some soils that exhibit lower California Bearing Ratio CBR
values than usually desired However it appears that the desired soil cement
strengths of 5 MPa may be attained despite the low CBR values
As previously mentioned the near surface on site soils exist in a relatively dry
condition of about 10 to 15 percent below the optimum moisture content Therefore in order
to prepare the on site soils from their existing moisture content to near the optimum moisture
content for site preparation grading and soil cement mixing a large amount of water will be
needed at the site to facilitate the site grading operation Based on our estimates we believe
that approximately 160 to 240 liters of water with an average of 200 liters would be needed
for each cubic meter of earthwork for the grading operations Therefore an adequate water
supply and number of water trucks should be available at the site to moisture condition the
on site soils to the specified moisture contents to prepare the subgrades and work the on site
soils as general fills and controlled fills
It should be noted that the soil cement pavement mixture is quite sensitive to the
moisture content of the on site soils and during the mixing procedures The contractor

Soil Cement Pavements
We understand that the Base Camp Roads A and B and the access roads leading
to the three helipads from the Base Camp Road B are planned to be surfaced with
soil cement pavements using the on site fine grained soils We also understand that the
access roads will generally be subjected to relatively light traffic such as pedestrian traffic
pick up trucks light duty vehicles and occasional moderately loaded trucks
Based on the relatively light traffic loading conditions anticipated for these road
pavements we believe that a 150 mm thick soil cement pavement may be used for the road
pavement section Our laboratory tests conducted for the overall Base Camp project area
indicate that an unconfined compressive strength of day curing period may be
generally achieved based on a cement content of 25 percent by weight in the laboratory
To account for the field environment which is not as ideal as in the laboratory the cement
content is normally increased by 1 to 2 percent for field conditions For this project
considering the relatively high cement content needed to achieve the desired unconfined
compressive strength of 5 MPa we believe that a 1 percent increase in cement content
would be reasonable Therefore we recommend that a cement content of 26 percent by
GEOLABS INC
W O 4161 00 Page 17
weight be used for the Base Camp Roads A and B and access roads for cost estimating
purposes
It should be noted that two of the samples tested Samples S 04 and S 08 nearly
achieved the desired unconfined compressive strength of 5 MPa at the recommended
cement content of 26 percent Samples S 04 and S 08 are located in the vicinity of Base
Camp Road A and the Helicopter Landing Pads as shown on the General Site Plan
Plate 2 Therefore we recommend that additional soil cement testing be conducted after the
site grading operations to near the finished grades to confirm and or modify the cement
content for the soil cement pavement structure The relationship between unconfined
compressive strength and cement content for the six soil samples conducted for the overall
Base Camp project is presented on Plate 5 for reference
The soil cement pavement mixture should be moisture conditioned to between
5 percent below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content based on ASTM
Test Designation D 698 and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 558
Prior to placement of the soil cement pavement structure the subgrade soils should
be scarified to a depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioned to between 5 percent below
the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The subgrades should be finished to provide a relatively
smooth and unyielding surface prior to placement of the soil cement pavement structure
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 698 test procedures Standard Proctor Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

As previously mentioned the three Base Camp helicopter landing pads are to be

Concrete Pads and Portland Cement Concrete Pavements
Based on the plans provided we envision that concrete pads and Portland Cement
Concrete PCC pavements will be constructed at the proposed DRMO and Fuel Storage

The concrete pad foundations may be designed using an allowable bearing pressure
of up to about This bearing value may be increased by one third 1 3
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces The thickened edges
of concrete pads adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 300 mm below the
lowest adjacent grade Lateral loads acting on the foundation may be resisted by frictional
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive
earth pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of the
foundation A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for foundations bearing on the
aggregate subbase placed on the clayey soils Resistance to lateral loads due to passive
pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 50 kilonewtons per square
meter per meter of depth kN m These values assume that the concrete for footings is cast
neat against the bearing materials Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 300 mm below the design finished grade should be neglected

Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of

The underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until placement of the

We anticipate that some minor cut and fill slopes on the order of 3 meters or less will
be constructed at the site to provide the level pads for the proposed construction We
also understand that cut slopes up to about 8 meter high will be required at the
helicopter landing pad site We envision that the cut slopes would generally expose
the alluvial soils and basalt rock described in the Surface Soil Conditions section of
this report In general we believe that cut slopes exposing the very stiff alluvial soils
may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter As previously indicated graded surfaces exposing the on site materials are
GEOLABS INC

highly susceptible to erosion by wind and surface water run off Therefore freshly
graded cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope planting or other means
to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed materials as soon as possible after

Permanent fill slopes constructed using compacted general fill or controlled fill
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on
slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to

be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well compacted slope face In addition slope planting should be provided as soon
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the concrete pads soil cement
pavements and helipads should be sloped to shed water away from the pavements and
pads to reduce the potential for ponding Surface water ponding should not be allowed on

proper performance of the concrete pads soil cement pavements and helipads since
ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of
strength The edges of the concrete pad pavement and helipad excavations should be
properly backfilled to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water
runoff away from the pads soil cement pavements and helipads

presented on the Summary of Index Property Tests Table A 1
Seven Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on the bulk soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results of the tests
are presented on the Summary of Index Property Tests Table A 1 Graphic presentation of
the test results is provided on Plate A 1
Eight sieve analysis tests with hydrometer ASTM D 422 were performed on the bulk
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soll
classification The results of the tests are presented on the Summary of Index Property
Tests Table A 1 Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plates
A 2 and A 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The tests were performed
on remolded samples from the bulk samples Results of the direct shear tests are presented
on Plates A 4 and A 5
Eight Standard Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 698 were performed on the bulk
soil samples to evaluate the moisture dry density relationship Results of the tests are
presented on the Summary of Index Property Tests Table A 1 and graphic presentations
of the test results are provided on Plates A 6 and A 13
Eight California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on the bulk
soil samples retrieved to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade
support The results of the CBR tests are presented on the Summary of Index Property
Tests Table A 1 Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates A 14
through A 21
