SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicates that the near-surface soils at 
the site are very stiff to hard in consistency; and, have low to moderate expansion potential 
when subjected to cyclical drying and wetting.  It is recommended to implement moisture 
control measures during site preparation and grading. 
The primary structural elements for the future site improvements include the 
construction of a slab to support the aboveground fuel storage tank and installation of a 
new flagpole and a new light standard.  It is assumed that the slab for the fuel tank will be 
a reinforced structural slab.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the 
project site, we recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf to 
design the slab and other shallow foundations bearing on the hard to very hard on-site 
clayey soils. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
It is anticipated that the axial loadings for the new flagpole and light standard will be 
minimal.  However, there will be relatively significant lateral loads from wind forces. Lateral 
loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed between the bottom of 
the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure against the near-vertical 
faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.40 may be used for footings 
bearing on the in-situ clayey soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished 
grades. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent 
fluid pressure of 350 pcf. This assumes that the soils around the footing is well compacted. 
Based on the results of the field infiltration testing, the average infiltration rates at 
the site indicate that the subsurface conditions are restrictive for the use of infiltration 
retention systems, such as infiltration basins, infiltration trenches and similar methods of 
storm water management. Other systems, or combinations of systems, of storm water 
management should be considered for this project. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is at the existing Aiea Fire Station at 98-1239 Ulune Street in 
Aiea on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project involves replacement of the existing 
asphaltic concrete pavements at the site with rigid concrete pavement, along with 
drainage improvements, including erosion control, infrastructure improvements, 
installation of a new aboveground fuel storage tank, new flagpole, new light standard 
and paving of a new American With Disability Act-compliant parking area and walkway. 
The project site is situated to the southwest of Ulune Street across from Aiea High 
School. 
Based on our field observations and elevation data from Google Earth™, the site 
slopes down slightly towards the southwest with existing ground surface elevations 
ranging from about +120 to +112 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  Since the major site 
improvement is replacement of the existing pavements, we envision that the site 
grading work will consist of minimal cuts and new fills of up to about 1 foot thick.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Site Description 
The project site is at the existing Aiea Fire Station located at 98-1239 Ulune 
Street in Aiea on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The site is generally rectangular in plan 
view and is occupied by the existing fire station and paved parking and access drives.  
The site is bounded by Ulune Street and Aiea High School to the northeast, Aiea District 
Park Annex to the southeast, Interstate Route H-1 to the southwest, and single family 
residences to the northwest. 
Based on our field observations and elevation data from Google Earth™, the site 
slopes down slightly towards the southwest with existing ground surface elevations 
ranging from about +120 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) along Ulune Street to about 
+112 feet MSL at the boundary with the H-1 freeway.  
2.2 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Koolau 
Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The site is located on a planeze, or a triangular relatively uneroded intravalley 
ridge, on the southwest facing flank of the Koolau dome.  The soils on the ridge are 
generally residual soils, derived from the in-situ weathering of basaltic rock. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling four borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of ranging from about 6.5 to 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface (BGS). We explored the shallow 
near-surface infiltration conditions by drilling and testing two infiltration test bores to 
depths of about 5 feet BGS. The approximate boring and infiltration test locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

ground surface. Based on the subsurface materials anticipated below the foundation 
subgrade level and the geologic setting of the area, we anticipate the project site may 
be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as a “Stiff Soil Profile” corresponding to

3.1 
Site Grading 
Grading plans were not available at the time this report was prepared.  Based on 
the existing topography of the site, we anticipate that the majority of the site grading 
work will involve shallow cuts and fills on the order of less than 1 foot deep or thick.  
Since the near-surface soils have a low to moderate potential to shrink when 
allowed to dry and to expand when re-wetted, moisture control measures are 
recommended during the earthwork portions of the project.  Generally, the soils should 
be maintained at a field moisture content that is about 2 percent above optimum 
moisture content as established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is 
the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Generally, a Geolabs representative should monitor the site grading operations 
to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification 
process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those 
anticipated. Items of grading addressed in the subsequent subsections include the 
following:

to evaluate whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in

3.2 
Foundations 
The primary structural elements for the future site improvements are a slab to 
support the aboveground fuel storage tank, a new flagpole and a new light standard.  It 
has been assumed that the slab for the fuel tank will be a reinforced structural slab.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend 
using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf to design the structural slab and 
shallow foundations bearing on recompacted on-site silty soils. This bearing value is for 
supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
It is anticipated that the axial loadings for the flagpole will be minimal.  However, 
there will be relatively significant lateral load from wind forces.  Lateral loads acting on 
the structure may be resisted by friction developed between the bottom of the 
foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure against the near-vertical 
faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.40 may be used for footings 
bearing on the in-situ clayey soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished 
grades. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf. This assumes that the soils around the footing is 
well compacted.

We recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support new

pavement due to the difference in distribution of forces within the slab and the 
pavement.  
For footings that will bear on recompacted on-site soil or new compacted fill, the 
bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at 
the bottom of footing excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying 
firm materials. The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular material 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the footing bottom may 
be deepened to the underlying firm materials. 
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade. Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face. Footings located 
adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to 
avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations.

Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from the 
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement.

earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf. This 
assumes that the soil around the footings is relatively undisturbed or well-compacted. 
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of

material below the foundations, observation of the foundation excavations and 
preparation operations should be designated a “Special Inspection” item in accordance 
with Section 1701 of the International Building Code (2009).

Pavement Design 
Based on the information provided for the proposed project, we envision that rigid 
pavements will be used to reconstruct the existing parking areas and access driveways. 
In general, we anticipate the typical vehicle load for the parking lot and southern 
driveway will consist primarily of passenger vehicles, light trucks and similar vehicles. 
However, we understand that the fire trucks at the site are driven through the parking 
area and on all driveways.  Therefore, we are designing for the heavier vehicles. 
The information provided indicates that the current fire trucks have a maximum 
axle loading of about 27 kips; and, it has been requested that the pavement design 
include an increase of 25 percent for future fire fighting vehicles and traffic.  Therefore, 
the assumed axle load is 34 kips and the assumed traffic load is 1,900 annual passes.  
Based on the above assumptions, we recommend using following pavement section for 
preliminary design purposes: 
Rigid Pavement 
7.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete 
   6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
 13.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness  
Base course material should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to 
no less than 95 percent relative compaction. CBR and density tests should be 
performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the 
adequacy of the above sections.

In general, paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be

on-site during or after construction. If concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in 
or adjacent to the pavement area, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 
2 inches into the soil below the aggregate base course to reduce the migration of 
landscaping water into the pavement section. In addition, a subdrain system is 
recommended to collect the excess water from the landscaping for long-term usage. 
3.5 
Utility Trenches 
We anticipate that utility work for the fire station site improvement will consist 
primarily of replacement of an existing sewer line. Based on our field exploration, the 
excavation will encounter stiff to hard clayey material. We envision that conventional 
excavation methods may generally be utilized for the trench excavation. However, 
cobbles and boulders may be encountered at shallow depths within the residual soil and 
saprolite, which may pose some difficulties during the excavation. The contractor is 
responsible for determining the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation 
and shall comply with the applicable federal, state, and local requirements. 
For support of the utility line, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 
6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes. The 
initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free-draining material, 
such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for pipe damage from compaction of 
the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce the potential 
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for 
the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site soils as backfill directly around utility pipes is 
not recommended. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and utility 
pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 1 foot above the pipes to 
the finish subgrade should consist of the approved on-site silty clay soils (with a 
maximum particle size of 6 inches) or select granular material. The backfill material 
should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted

3.6 
Drainage 
The surface and subsurface drainage at the site are especially critical 
considering the expansive nature of the soils at the site. We believe that the finished 
grades adjacent to structures should be sloped to shed water away from the 
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also advised to install gutter 
systems around the structures and to divert the discharge away from the foundation and 
pavement areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should 
also be avoided. Gravel strips adjacent to slabs and foundations should be avoided. 
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to 
reduce water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near-surface soils. The foundation 
excavations should be backfilled properly against the footings immediately after setting 
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration. Drainage swales should be provided as soon 
as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run-off away from walls and 
foundations.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, to depths ranging between 6.5 and 
16.5 feet below the existing ground surface; and, by the drilling and testing of two 
infiltration test holes, designated as P-1 and P-2, to depths of approximately 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The borings and test holes were drilled using a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  The approximate boring and test hole 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings and 
the Logs of Infiltration Tests, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Infiltration Tests 
In order to provide information on the surface infiltration rates of the on-site soils, 
we conducted two falling head infiltration tests at the locations shown as P-1 and P-2 on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2.  These tests were performed in general accordance with the 
procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The procedure used for the infiltration consisted of augering a borehole to a 
depth of about 4.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  After reaching the test depth, 
a solid PVC pipe, or well casing, was installed in the borehole flush with the bottom so 
that water could only infiltrate into the soil through the exposed bottom soil. 
Water was placed into the open pipe and allowed to pre-saturate the soil prior to 
conducting the infiltration test.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with approximately 24 inches of water and 
taking periodic readings over a one hour trial period.  The infiltration rate is then 
calculated based on the result of the fourth and last trial for each test location.
