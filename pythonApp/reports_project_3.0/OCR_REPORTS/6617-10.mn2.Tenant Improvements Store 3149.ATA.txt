limited geotechnical exploration for the Tenant Improvement – Store No. 3149 project at 
the former Macy’s Downtown Store building at the Bethel and South King Street 
intersection in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This report summarizes the

this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project includes improvements to the existing building, which was the former 
Macy’s Downtown store. The improvements include a new elevator (between grids 5 and 
6 and D and E) and escalator (between grids 5 and 6 along grid G) within the existing 
building, which will require new foundations and/or placement of additional load on 
existing foundations. Also, included is a new passageway (grids F and 2) from the exterior 
elevator into the store. We understand that the structural loading on the remainder of the 
building will remain the same. 
We conducted a limited study for due diligence purposes and presented our 
findings in our report dated April 27, 2012. Due to the on-going retail business in the 
building at the time, subsurface exploration was limited to hand auger excavation in 
two areas away from the retail operations. The depths of the hand auger exploration were 
limited due to the hard and gravelly conditions encountered.  
We understand that geotechnical information from the original building construction 
is not available though it is believed that the footings bear on hard coral material. After the

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The building is situated over the Honolulu coastal plain which consists of a series of 
thick marine and non-marine sediments capping the Koolau Basalt. The near subsurface 
soil structure at the site consists of a broad elevated coral reef partially covered by 
non-marine alluvium and fill. These conditions are perceived to be the result of a complex 
series of Pleistocene sea level changes. The present reef platform is probably a wave cut 
terrace formed by wave erosion of an older reef which originally stood about 25 feet above 
sea level. This widespread reef structure is known locally as the Waimanalo Reef. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, to a depth of about 25 feet below the existing floor 
slab. The approximate boring locations drilled are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The three borings encountered similar conditions consisting of about 4 inches of 
concrete over dense silty sand and gravel fill to about 5 to 7 feet deep underlain by coral 
formation to 25 feet, the depth drilled. 
We encountered groundwater in the borings at about 16.9 to 17.3 feet below the 
existing floor slab level at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water run-off, and other factors. 
SEISMIC SITE CLASS 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site and vicinity, the 
project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a “Stiff Soil” site 
corresponding to a Site Class D soil profile type based on the International Building Code 
(Table No. 1615.1.1) 2006 Edition. 
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The tenant improvements to the existing building consist of a new elevator and 
escalator in the central portion of the building and a new passageway from the outside 
elevators into the store. The new escalator and elevator footings are adjacent to existing 
building footings. The as-built drawings of the existing building indicate that the existing 
footings may be about 6 to 7 feet below the building floor level. The bottoms of the new 
footings will be placed at the same elevation as the existing footings.  
New Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we believe shallow spread 
and/or continuous footings may be used to support the new elevator, escalator, and 
passageway into the building.  Based on the boring data, the footings may bear on hard

coral formation in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 and dense cobbles and boulders in Boring No. 3 
encountered at about 5 to 7 feet below the floor slab elevation. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of 
foundations for the building structures bearing on hard coral, dense cobbles and boulders 
or compacted select granular fill material. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces. 
The footings should be placed at the same elevation as the existing adjacent 
building footings to avoid undermining the footings. Foundations next to utility trenches or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
Soft and/or loose soils encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should 
be removed to expose the underlying hard coral or dense cobbles and boulders. The 
resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fills compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing may extend down to bear on the 
underlying firm material. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundation to be on the order of 
about 0.75 inch or less. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on 
similar materials may be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the building structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 
0.40 may be used for footings bearing on the hard coral or compacted select fill. 
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid 
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soils 
around the footings are well compacted.  
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and 
the required embedment depths.

Utility Trench and Backfill 
We anticipate the underground utilities for the building renovations will primarily 
consist of water, electrical, and sewer line connections. In general, good construction 
practices should be implemented in the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the 
new utilities. The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for 
trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the 
excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety 
requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation where deemed necessary.  
In general, we recommend  providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes or groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is 
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for the formation of voids 
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or 
groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils 
(with a maximum particle size of 3 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill 
should be placed in 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench 
backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information
