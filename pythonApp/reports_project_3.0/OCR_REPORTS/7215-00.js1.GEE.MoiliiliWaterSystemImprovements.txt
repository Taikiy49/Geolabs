SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 
3 to 8 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying medium dense sandy gravel and silty sand 
fill material. The fill was generally underlain by marsh and lagoonal deposits, consisting 
of very loose to loose clayey sand and sandy gravel, extending to depths of about 
11.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The marsh and lagoonal deposits 
were underlain by slightly weathered, medium hard to hard coral formation extending to 
the maximum depth explored of about 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered at depths of approximately 2.8 to 4.7 feet below 
existing grades at the time of our field exploration. These groundwater depths 
correspond to about elevation -0.3 to +1.2 feet MSL. It is anticipated the groundwater 
depth will vary based upon tides, rainfall, surface water runoff, seasonal precipitation 
and other factors. 
We anticipate that the majority of the water main inverts will be on the order of 
about 4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. However, the pipe invert will be 
extended to depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the pavement surface where the new 
water main traverses under an existing box culvert at the corner of University Avenue 
and Hihiwai Street.  Installation of the proposed water mains will involve excavation into 
the underlying fills, marsh, and lagoonal deposits. In general, we anticipate that the 
in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignments may be excavated with normal 
heavy excavation equipment. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut 
excavations are not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically 
necessitating the use of shoring during construction. One possible method of shoring 
would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring. Use of a sheet pile shoring system may 
also serve as a cut-off wall to aid in the dewatering operations.  
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater 
level is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Dewatering of the excavation may 
involve moderate to large volumes of water due to the granular nature of the on-site 
soils. Based on the results of our field permeability tests, we estimate that the 
subsurface permeability of the on-site soils may be on the order of about 0.001 to 
0.005 centimeters per second.

performed for the Moiliili Water System Improvements, Part IV project in the Moiliili area

recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The bulk of the Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of 
two extinct shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau.  Koolau is the younger of the two 
and forms the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Waikiki/Moiliili area resides on the 
southern flank of the Koolau Volcano where relatively young coastal and marine 
deposits comprise the low gradient Honolulu Coastal Plain of Oahu.  These deposits 
overlie buried (older) seaward sloping Koolau Volcano deposits.  Because of the coastal 
plain location, the geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the region were directly 
affected by past global glacio-eustatic fluctuations (rise and fall) of the sea level during 
the Pleistocene age (~2 million years ago). These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in 
stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on 
Oahu resulting in the repeated deposition and erosion of a variety of coastal and marine 
deposits over a long period of time.  Some of these intercalated deposits include soft 
lagoonal and marsh sediments, broken granular coralline detritus, and hard coralline 
reef ledge materials related to varying stands of the sea during deposition. 
In addition, with the passage of time the Honolulu Coastal Plain was incised by 
multiple seaward trending drainage channels (streams) emanating from higher 
elevations of the Koolau Volcano.  These prominent drainage channels cut deeply into 
the layered terrestrial, coastal, and marine deposits existing at the time and locally 
removed (scoured) the native deposits and replaced them by deposition of various 
younger age alluvial soil products derived from inland terrain.  It should be noted that at 
depth, the base of the Honolulu Coastal Plain stratigraphic section is the basaltic lavas 
of the Koolau Volcano, identified as Tertiary Koolau Basalt. 
During the Pleistocene (a.k.a. last Ice Age), many glacio-eustatic sea level 
changes occurred as a result of worldwide continental glaciation.  The higher sea level 
stands caused the accumulation of sediments in the heads of the old bays, 
accumulation of reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and deposition of 
lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing coralline reefs.  The

Rapid rises in the base level erosion-deposition equilibrium caused the hasty

coralline materials, and some volcanic deposits in the valleys forming the alluvial 
channels.  Today, some of these buried channels are 100 to 200 feet or more in depth

The channel widths can vary from a few tens to several hundred feet.  Natural 
depositional processes and placement of fills during the urbanization of Honolulu has 
covered most of these channels so that they are not evident from the existing ground 
surface.  Buried channels may be found traversing all, or part, of a potential 
development site.  Major channels that have been identified in the project site vicinity 
include: 
Makiki – Old Ala Wai – The Makiki Channel generally runs on the eastern 
side of Kalakaua Avenue to the Ala Wai Canal.  Available information 
indicates that the channel system then runs along the canal alignment to 
the mouth of the canal.  This channel is believed to be on the order of 
about 100 feet deep below the existing ground surface. 
Manoa – Launiu – This system of channels generally runs from the 
University Avenue and King Street intersection to Waikiki along 
Beachwalk.  The system appears to consist meandering channels 
following the old drainage trend of Manoa Stream.  In the Waikiki area, the 
system appears to extend to depths of about 150 to 200 feet below the 
ground surface.  
In the early part of this century, the Waikiki/Moiliili area consisted of low, marshy 
areas. As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki/Moiliili area urbanized, man-made 
fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons.  The fill was placed upon 
lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments.  The lower clayey 
materials were deposited during periodic influxes of non-marine alluvial debris, which 
apparently smothered the coral reef development.  Land development and reclamation 
projects within the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki/Moiliili area to its present 
form.  Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy 
structural loads.

3.1 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the majority of the pipe inverts will be on the order of about 
4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. However, the pipe invert will be 
extended to depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the pavement surface where the new 
water main traverses under an existing box culvert at the corner of University Avenue 
and Hihiwai Street.  Installation of the proposed water mains will involve excavation into 
the underlying fills, marsh, and lagoonal deposits. In general, we anticipate that the in-
situ soils along the proposed water main alignments may be excavated with normal 
heavy excavation equipment. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential 
for excessive ground movement. In addition, the excavated soils should be stockpiled at 
least 10 feet away from underground utilities to reduce the potential for ground 
movement or subsidence, which may damage the existing underground utility lines. 
The above discussion regarding the excavation of the subsurface materials is 
based on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site. We recommend 
encouraging contractors bidding on this project to examine the site conditions and soil 
data to make their own interpretation.

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Deeper excavations 
up to 15 feet below the pavement surface may be required where the water main 
traverses under an existing box culvert at the corner of University Avenue and Hihiwai 
Street. Where deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut 
excavations are not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically 
necessitating the use of shoring during construction. 
One possible method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring. 
Use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut-off wall to aid in the 
dewatering operations, which is further discussed in the “Dewatering” section herein. 
The sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient depth to reduce 
the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within 
the excavations due to the relatively permeable nature of the subsoils. It should be 
noted that the excavations will likely encounter very soft/loose soil deposits at the 
bottom of the excavation. Therefore, there is potential for bottom heave in these very 
soft/loose soil conditions. The contractor should carefully evaluate the potential for 
bottom heave and design the shoring system accordingly. 
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain it 
tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as roads or utilities. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for adequacy and safety 
of the shoring installation. His/her representative, who should be required to be 
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work, will have the best 
opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such as

However, it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and

3.3 
Dewatering 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater 
level is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Groundwater was encountered at 
depths ranging from about 2.8 to 4.7 feet below the existing ground surface in the 
borings drilled during our field exploration. Based on the groundwater levels anticipated 
and the permeable nature of the sandy soils to be encountered during the excavations, 
dewatering will be required at the planned excavations of the project construction. 
Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for 
the proposed construction. Since the excavation will involve discharge of groundwater, 
a NPDES permit will be required for this discharge. Groundwater sampling and testing 
was performed for the project in support of the NPDES permit application and the 
results are presented in the “Groundwater Quality Testing” section herein.

settlements, which could affect the existing structures and utility lines. The potential 
impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table 
must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering. 
It is our opinion that a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking sheet piles, should 
be considered to aid in dewatering the excavation. However, sumps will be needed to 
collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates through the 
sheet piles. The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential 
for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the 
excavations in areas underlain by granular subsoils with generally high permeability. 
Use of an interlocking sheet pile shoring support system with tremie concrete plug is 
relatively watertight, which should allow the groundwater levels outside the excavations 
to be maintained close to the original pre-construction levels. Therefore, some type of 
groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents. 
The contractor is responsible for dewatering for construction. The contractor 
should select equipment and methods of dewatering, and he/she should be aware that 
modifications to the dewatering system may be required during construction depending 
on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method selected should have minimal 
impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed excavation. The dewatering 
operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring support such that the stability 
of the excavations is not jeopardized. The operations should be carried-out without 
softening the bottom of the excavations.

should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural

the excavation bottom or sides. 
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement 
resulting from consolidation of the soft/loose compressible soils. Therefore, the use 
of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level 
should not be allowed. 
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected 
to the excavations. If this occurs, the granular bedding and/or backfill in the utility 
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater 
drawdown. Significant areal groundwater drawdown would result in ground 
settlements and potential damage to utility lines and/or other existing adjacent 
structures.

3.6 
Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important design 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
W.O. 7215-00 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Page 19 
Hawaii • California 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the medium dense silty sand and sandy gravel 
surface fill soils, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material, such 
as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) should be provided below the 
pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, 
such as in the marsh/lagoonal deposits, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 
18 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a non-woven 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer for 
uniform support. 
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4)  the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of 
the pipe. Before the placement of bedding material, a Geolabs representative should 
observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials are exposed at the 
bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should be provided below the 
bedding layer. 
3.7 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select 
free draining granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction 
effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site surface fill soils or 
imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, 
and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It 
should be noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines 
only and may be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field 
observations on the available fill materials.

Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well graded from coarse

would also reduce the potential for future settlement of the trench backfill under the 
repeated wheel loading from traffic on the roadway. 
3.8 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site surface fill soils should be 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level 
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.12 Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
3.13 Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Four sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including pH and minimum resistivity, were 
performed by TestAmerica Laboratories Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site 
soils encountered. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4 of Appendix B. The 
soil resistivity was determined in general accordance with SM 2510B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity is, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure will have. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil 
will contribute to corrosion to metallic objects. Results of the resistivity testing indicate

varying from 8.1 to 8.3. Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered 
very to extremely corrosive. 
In addition, four sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including chloride content and 
sulfate content, were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the 
corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. The test results are also summarized on

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling seven borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, to depths of about 11.5 to 31.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the Overall Site Plan, 
Plate 2, and Boring Location Plans and Generalized Geologic Profiles, Plates 3.1 through 
3.10. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers to advance 
the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock was described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Four sets of pH (Method 9045C), Minimum Resistivity (SM 2510B), Chloride 
(EPA 300) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300) tests were performed by TestAmerica 
Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration.  The test 
results are presented on Plate B-4. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.

Field Permeability Tests 
The permeability of the near-surface materials at the project site was evaluated by 
performing constant and falling head permeability tests in Boring No. 1, at the approximate 
locations shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2, and the Boring Location Plan and 
Generalized Geologic Profiles, Plates 3.2 and 3.3. The tests were performed to a depth of 
about 23.7 feet below the existing ground surface. 
During the constant head test, a water pump was used to pump clear water into 
the boring. The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the volume as a 
relatively constant flow rate was achieved. The results of the constant head permeability 
test are presented on Plate C-1. 
During the falling head test, the test hole was initially saturated. After saturation, 
the falling head percolation test was performed by filling up the test boring with clear 
water. Time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which 
calculation of the permeability values were based. The falling head permeability test 
data is presented on Plate C-2.
