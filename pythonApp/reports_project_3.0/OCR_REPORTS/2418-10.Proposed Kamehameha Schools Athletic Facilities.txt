INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kamehameha Schools Athletic Facilities located at the
Kamehameha Heights Area in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for input for foundation and wall
design and may not be appropriate to form a basis for the preparation of construction
cost estimates or for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by extrusion of
basaltic lavas from northwest to southeast trending rift zones These lavas are
interbedded Pahoehoe and Aa type flows with the Pahoehoe being the dominant form
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular rock Cavities are commonly encountered in Pahoehoe lavas
These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Aa
is characterized by a rough spiny surface of clinker and has a core of dense rock
Generally there is another layer of clinker at the base of the flow Cavities are
occasionally found in Aa flows
Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the
cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalts are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2418 10 Page 7
porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve
as the major water transmission features
In situ weathering of the basaltic lava flows at the project site has generated a
mantle of residual soils and saprolites overlying basaltic lava rock Saprolite is a form of
residual soil that retains the relict structure of the original rock mass from which it was
derived
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The borings drilled for this exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff clayey
silts and silty clays overlying weathered in situ basalt formation The stiff soils range in
thickness from about 5 to 60 feet and generally consist of residual saprolite and
weathered clinker soils derived from in place weathering of the parent basalt formation
Some of the near surface stiff soils consist of man made fills which were placed during
the development of the Kamehameha Schools
The underlying basalt formation was generally in a moderately to highly weathered
State Although weathered the basalt formation encountered by our exploration should
be able to provide relatively good foundation support characteristics and should be
generally easier to excavate than unweathered rock
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that potential seepage water exists within the
basalt formation due to the permeable nature of this type of rock During periods of high
precipitation cavities intra flow contacts and clinker layers can serve as conduits for
seepage or transient groundwater flow

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing the significant geotechnical
engineering considerations for the proposed project is the depth of the basalt strata from
the finished grades uniformity and quality of the basalt formation and strength
characteristics of the in situ soils
Because of the deep cuts planned for the up slope portion of the proposed athletic
facilities the basalt formation will be close to the foundation level for many of the
proposed structures located along the upper northern section Where it is estimated that
the basalt formation may be encountered at about five 5 to six feet 6 below the
finished floor elevation we recommend that a spread footing foundation system bearing
on the basalt rock be used to support the structure
Depths to the top of the basalt formation from the finished floor elevation are
anticipated to be variable and deeper in the lower western areas therefore we
recommend that a composite foundation system of spread footings and drilled piers be
used to support the proposed football field structure
Where the basalt formation may be present at shallow depths below retaining
structures we recommend that spread footing foundations bearing on basalt be used to
support these structures In some wall sections where basalt formation is deeper the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2418 10 Page 9
retaining wall footing foundations may also bear on either the stiff in situ soils or the
engineered fills planned for raising the grades at the southeastern portion of the site
Because of the relatively large heights planned for several retaining walls we
recommend that alternate earth retention systems such as a tied back retaining wall
system and or a soil nail retaining wall system be considered We believe these
alternative earth retention systems could provide the lateral support needed for the high
retaining wall excavations The selected retention system can serve as temporary shoring
and reduce the structural requirements of the permanent retaining walls Because of this
dual function we believe that these earth retention systems would result in appreciable
cost savings over conventional concrete retaining walls

Spread Footing Foundations
For a significant portion of the proposed football field structure spread footings
bearing on in situ basalt formation may be used to support the large structural
loads In order for footings to bear on the weathered basalt formation footing

variable nature of the top of basalt formation footing excavations deeper than the

bearing materials and the embedment depths If unsuitable materials are

Tied Back Retaining Walls
We believe that a tied back retaining wall system with permanent tie backs could
be adopted for Retaining Walls E and F Construction of the permanent tied
back wall would involve installing soldier piles consisting of wide flange steel
beams in drilled holes near the face of the retaining wall As the excavation for
the cut is made approximately 5 to 6 feet the first level of tie backs would then
be installed Welded wire fabric could be placed on the excavated face and
shotcrete is applied to the face for temporary shoring of the excavation As the
permanent tie backs are installed they are stressed and_proof loaded
Subsequent levels of tie backs are installed and the temporary facing of shotcrete
with welded wire fabric is applied and extended downward as the excavation
progresses
Upon completion of the permanent tie backs and temporary facing a permanent
retaining wall may be constructed Either a cast in place wall or reinforced
shotcrete wall may be used as the permanent wall face The lateral earth
pressures would be resisted by the permanent tie backs and the permanent
retaining wall generally would only need to be designed to span between the tie
backs and the lower portion of the wall
The tied back retaining wall should be designed to resist lateral earth pressures
due to the adjacent soil and any surcharge effects resulting from loads adjacent
to the wall We recommend that the permanent tied back retaining wall be
designed for a uniform pressure of 30 pounds per square foot per foot of height

Soil Nail Retaining Wall
As an alternative to the tied back retaining wall system a soil nail retaining wall
system may be used for the proposed Retaining Walls E and F at the project
site Similar to the tied back retaining wall system the soil nail wall system would
serve both as temporary shoring of the excavation and the permanent wall
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope A shotcrete facing
is generally applied to the face of the reinforced excavation to provide the
appearance of a conventional concrete retaining wall The reinforcing bars
provide both additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the

Cavity Probing
Cavities and other voids are commonly encountered in basalt formations To
reduce the possibility of foundation settlements resulting from the collapse of such voids
we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the spread footing locations
be considered when preparing specifications and engineering estimates for the project
For estimating purposes we recommend that the probes be drilled at each
column location and on ten foot 10 centers for continuous footings The probe holes
should be at least three inches 3 in diameter and should extend at least ten feet 10
below the footing subgrade The probe holes should be backfilled with sand cement
grout injected at low pressures

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of

ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted and portable drill rigs advancing continuous flight auges

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural

Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM

with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D
2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD
50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based
on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 90 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to evaluate the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs and are summarized on
Plates C 1 through C 3
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Unconfined Compression tests ASTM Test Method D 2938 79 were performed
on selected rock core samples The ends of the rock core samples were cut with a
diamond saw parallel to each other and at right angles to the longitudinal axis of the core
The axial lengths of the samples were 2 0 to 2 5 times its diameter The load was applied
continuously at a rate of 0 05 inches minute until failure occurred The stress at failure
are presented on Plates C 1 through C 3
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate C 4

Preliminary Geotechnical Engineering Recommendations
Kamehameha Schools Athletic Facilities
Honolulu Oahu Hawaii
We have completed the field exploration consisting of twenty one 21 borings
drilled for the second phase of our exploration for the above referenced project Based
on the boring data the following foundation recommendations are presented
Subsurface Conditions
The borings drilled generally encountered approximately five 5 to sixty feet 60
of very stiff clayey silts and silty clays overlying weathered in situ basalt formation The
stiff soils are generally residual saprolite and weathered clinker soils derived from in
place chemical weathering of the parent basalt rock formation Some of the stiff soils
represent man made fills placed near the ground surface during the development of the
campus area
The underlying basalt formation was generally in a moderately to highly weathered
state Basalt in this state usually has relatively good foundation support characteristics
and is generally easier to excavate than unweathered rock
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration

Foundation Support
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration we believe that either
spread footing or drilled pier foundations may be used to support the structural loads
generated by the proposed athletic facility
Spread Footing Foundation
Based on our exploration and laboratory testing we believe that an allowable
bearing capacity of 20 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for spread
footings bearing on the in situ weathered basalt formation The footings should be
embedded a minimum of four feet 4 below the finished grade For the anticipated
maximum column load up to 1800 kips estimated total settlements should be less than
The estimated top of rock elevation at the proposed footing locations of the
proposed athletic facility are shown on the Rock Elevation Plan Plate 3
An allowable bearing capacity of 4 000 p s f may be utilized for the design of
spread footings bearing on residual and fill soils These allowable bearing values may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as wind and seismic
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded foundation system and by
frictional resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth for soil and an equivalent fluid pressure of 10 000
p c f for basalt rock This assumes that the soil around the foundation is properly
compacted for the soil condition and the concrete footing is poured directly against the
basalt rock for the rock condition Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper eighteen inches 18 of soil should be neglected A coefficient
of friction of 0 4 and 0 6 may be used between the bottom of the footings and the
properly prepared soil subgrade and basalt rock respectively
Drilled Pier Foundation
Where the estimated depth from the finish floor to the weathered basalt bearing
formation is greater than about five feet 5 the use of drilled piers caisson may be
considered

The drilled pier construction should be closely monitored by a representative of

shrinkage in order to maintain frictional contact between the in situ soils and pier
concrete The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of
the aggregates

Tied Back Retaining Wall
It is our opinion that a tied back retaining wall system with permanent tiebacks
could be adopted for retaining walls E and F Construction of the permanent tied back
wall would consist of installing soldier piles consisting of wide flanged steel beams in
drilled holes for the tie backs The tie backs would be installed at the location of the
soldier piles as the excavation for the cut is made The face of the cut between soldier
piles would be shotcrete with welded wire fabric for temporary shoring of the excavation
As the permanent tie backs are installed they are stressed and proof loaded
Subsequent tie backs are installed and the temporary facing of shotcrete with welded wire
fabric is applied as the lifts of excavation are made Upon completion of the permanent

The tied back retaining wall should be designed to resist lateral earth pressures

We recommend that the permanent tied back retaining wall be designed for a uniform

evaluate the effects of point loads and line loads
Drainage
The lateral earth pressures provided above do not include hydrostatic pressures
that might be caused by groundwater or water trapped behind the wall Retaining walls
should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage
system would consist of a one foot 1 to two foot 2 wide zone of permeable material
immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations down at the base
of the wall discharging to a discharge facility As an alternative a prefabricated drainage
board may be used instead of permeable material The prefabricated drainage board
should also contain a perforated pipe at the base For the tied back wall the drainage
system may consist of strips of prefabricated drainage boards connected to weep holes
near the base of the wall
