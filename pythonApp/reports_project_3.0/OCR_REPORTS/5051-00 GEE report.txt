REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area
may be divided into 4 basic geographic provinces The provinces include the Waianae
Mountains Ko olau Mountains Leilehua Plateau and Coastal Plain The Island of Oahu
was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the two prominent volcanoes the
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
Turtle Bay Resort Hawaii Page 3
W O 5051 00
Wai anae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two volcanoes the Wai anae Volcano is the
older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The Ko olau Volcano
rose above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the younger of the
two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly bedded
a a and pahoehoe lava flows which were erupted from elongate and linear trending
volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes The
Ko olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai anae Volcano to form the Leilehua
Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains which form the
western third of the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt rock formations on the Island
of Oahu The Ko olau Mountains form the eastern two thirds of the island and include the
rock layers exposed along the extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of
Wai anae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet
under the tremendous weight of the rock mass In addition to the subsidence of the island
the force of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and
slumping of portions of the land mass to form the topography of today Between 850 000
and 70 000 years ago volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the eruption of
ash cinder and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and southeastern
portions of the island These most recent eruptions are collectively known as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series Subsequently erosion by streams and ocean waves have continued to
work on the exposed land surfaces to form the topography of the present day
The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of
the early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene
age sea level fluctuations worked to create drowned river valley systems and the
development of coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be found
exposed subaerially on the Island of Oahu and also buried in submarine deposits well
offshore of the island The coral reefs and sand dune deposits were formed in shallow
offshore seas during periods of higher and lower sea levels associated with the effects of
the advance and retreat of glaciers which controlled the volume of water contained in the
seas Today some of the shallow sea coralline deposits are visible on the flanks of
mountains or buried beneath terrestrial sediments evidence of the higher and lower
stands of the sea in prehistoric time

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the New Ballroom site were explored by drilling and
sampling three test borings extending to depths of about 17 to 19 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the test borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
In general the site is underlain by 2 to 8 feet of surface fill material consisting of
very stiff and dense silty and sandy soil The surface fill layer is underlain by coral
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 19 feet below the
existing ground surface The top elevation of the coral formation was encountered at
depths of about 2 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface equivalent to approximately
10 feet MSL
It should be noted that a void about 3 inches thick was encountered in one of the
borings drilled As mentioned previously numerous cavities were encountered during
subsurface investigation for the original Turtle Bay Resort construction Therefore special
considerations should be given to the performance of a foundation probing and grouting
program in order to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from
collapse of the cavities below foundations
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths of about 10 to 16 feet below
the existing ground surface equivalent to the elevation of about 6 to 0 feet MSL at the
time of our field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are
subject to change due to seasonal precipitation tidal fluctuations storm surge conditions
and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on
the Logs of Borings Plates 4 thru 6 Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected
soil samples are presented in Appendix B
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously indicated our field exploration disclosed that the project site is
generally underlain by the coral formation overlain by a thin layer of surface fill material
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we
believe that the new ballroom structure may be supported by a shallow foundation system
bearing on the dense sandy or very stiff silty on site materials for the subject project
Based on our borings and previous borings drilled for the original resort construction we
recommend that a program of foundation probing and grouting for the new ballroom
structure be implemented during construction Detailed discussion and our geotechnical

Building Foundations
In general we believe that an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the dense
sandy or very stiff silty on site soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
It should be noted that the near surface sandy fill materials are poorly graded with a
relatively uniform particle size As such the sandy materials may be difficult to compact
particularly when drier than the optimum moisture Therefore the bottom of the footing
excavations should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing
steel and concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations the soft and or loose materials should be removed to expose the underlying
firm and or dense materials The over excavation may be backfilled with the excavated
on site sandy materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of footings to be less than 1 inch
Differential settlements between adjacent footings should be on the order of about
0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted sandy materials
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the

Foundation Probing and Grouting
As mentioned previously a 3 inch void was encountered within the coral formation
during our field exploration In addition numerous cavities were encountered during
subsurface investigation for the original Turtle Bay Resort construction Our experience
suggests that cavities and or voids are commonly encountered in the coral formations
underlying the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting
from collapse of the cavities below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity
probing and grouting of the foundation locations for the new ballroom structure be
implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each
spread footing location one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area and at
10 feet on center along wall foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in
diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation
elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to aid in delineating
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout with a slump range of
6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations In lieu of the sand cement grout fluid
lean concrete such as controlled low strength material CLSM flowable fill may also be
used for the grouting operation
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be provided for the new structure and
surrounding sidewalks In general we recommend that the subgrade soils below the
concrete slabs on grade be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Site Preparation section of this report
For concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that
a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be provided below the slabs to provide for
uniform support and to serve as a capillary break The cushion fill layer should consist of
open graded materials such as No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation If
desired an impervious moisture barrier may be provided above the cushion fill layer to
reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils and graded to
divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations and slabs
Site Preparation
We anticipate that minimal site grading work on the order of about 1 to 3 feet of
cuts and fills will be required for construction of the new ballroom structure at the subject
site In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor site
grading anticipated at the site At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious
material asphaltic concrete pavements and other unsuitable materials within the grading
limits should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should
include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill
placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the
resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density

The on site sandy materials are uniformly graded and therefore may be difficult to
compact particularly when drier than optimum moisture Where the soil is unconfined
such as at the ground surface the material may be easily disturbed due to lack of
cohesion and interlocking Therefore the contractor should properly moisten the soils
prior to compaction and be careful not to disturb the final grades prior to placement of
aggregate subbase base course and or concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and
prepared again as recommend above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the
soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Site preparation should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It is
important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation operations to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
Utility Trenches
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for the proposed
project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The
backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new ballroom should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundations and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and pavement areas should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration In addition drainage swales should
be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff
away from the foundations and pavement areas

the actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
