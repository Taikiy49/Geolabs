Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the project site
generally consist of loose clinker materials at the ground surface extending down to about
4 to 9 feet below the existing ground surface. The clinker materials generally were
underlain by interbedded layers of dense basalt formation and clinker materials extending
down to the maximum depth explored of about 36.5 feet below the existing ground
surface. Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled during our field
exploration.
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision
that shallow continuous footings, such as a perimeter ring footing, should be used to
support the new reservoir tank planned at the site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to
6,000 psf may be used for design of shallow foundations bearing on the structural fill. The
bearing value provided is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
We understand that the new reservoir will have a concrete floor in the interior of the
reservoir. In order to provide a uniform bearing surface for the concrete floor, we
recommend that a 12-inch thick layer of compacted subbase be provided below the
concrete floor of the reservoir. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the
borings, we anticipate loose a’a basalt to be present at the reservoir subgrade elevation.
Where loose a’a basalt are encountered at the reservoir subgrade elevation, we
recommend that the loose a’a basalt below the reservoir footprint be over-excavated to
expose the underlying dense basalt formation or over-excavated to a maximum depth of
36 inches below the subbase layer. The over-excavation should be backfilled with
compacted structural fill materials.
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic
lava flows, we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the foundation
locations for the reservoir tank structure be implemented during construction. The text of
this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific recommendations for
design of the new reservoir tank project.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Waikoloa Tank 300-2 and 3 project for the Waikoloa Beach
Resort located in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed reservoir
project only. These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations, site
grading, pavements, and thrust blocks only. The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project site is located adjacent to the existing water reservoir along Waikoloa
Road within the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The project consists of
constructing two 2.5-Million Gallon (MG) reservoirs as part of the Waikoloa Beach Resort
Development Plan and Potable Water System. We understand that a paved access road
will be constructed around the perimeter of the new reservoirs. In addition, new water
lines and other appurtenances are anticipated for the proposed project.
Based on a topographic survey map provided, the existing grades at the project
site vary from about +280 to +290 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). In general, we envision
that site grading consisting of cuts and fills of up to about 5 feet in height may be
required to provide a relatively level ground surface at the reservoir project site. In
addition, we understand that an earth berm of about 20 feet in height will be constructed
on the western portion of the project site to provide a visual barrier for the new
reservoirs.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes, which include the following: Kohala, which is long extinct; Mauna Kea,
which has had activity during recent geologic time; Hualalai, which last erupted in 1801;
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea, both of which are still active.
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano, which has formed the west-central portion of the island. The project site
appears to be underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of
Mauna Kea Shield Volcano, which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch
(Stearns and Macdonald, 1946).
The Hamakua Volcanic Series typically is composed of basaltic lava flows that
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash, locally referred to as
Pahala Ash. The volcanic ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in
other portions of the Island of Hawaii in that the in-situ moisture content is generally
lower, and the soil exists in a dry, friable state. The volcanic ash soils at the site have
been subjected to wind erosion, which has resulted in the formation of a desert
pavement on the surface of the site. This desert pavement is a surface layer of cobbles
and gravel, which were left behind as the finer volcanic ash soils were blown away.
The lava formation at the project site appears to be of a’a and pahoehoe basalt
type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean. A’a lava are
typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a
more dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core.
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas. These cavities were formed when the lavas were still in a molten state and

2.2 Site Description
The project site is located to the east (mauka) of Queen Ka’ahumanu Highway
near the Waikoloa Beach Resort in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii.
The Waikoloa Tank 300-2 and 3 site is located adjacent to the existing water reservoir
along Waikoloa Road. A site plan showing the project location is provided as Plate 2 of
this report.
The project site generally slopes downward from east to west with localized
undulating topography. Based on the Topographic Survey Map, the existing ground
surface elevations vary from about +280 to +290 feet MSL. The existing ground surface
is nearly level at the proposed reservoir locations.
The project site is located on vacant, undeveloped land situated in a relatively
arid climate. At the time of our field exploration, the site generally was barren with very
sparse vegetation.

2.4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most of the earthquakes are
so small that only instruments can detect the minor earthquakes. However, some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage.
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of
Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent of
earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the Island
of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 6 (M6+). Based on information obtained from the United States Geological

2.5 Seismic Design Considerations
We understand that this project will be designed in general accordance with the
requirements of the 1997 Uniform Building Code (UBC). It should be noted that the
Island of Hawaii is located within Seismic Zone 4 with an effective peak ground
acceleration of 0.40g. Based on the subsurface materials anticipated at the site (basalt
rock formation intermixed with volcanic ash) and the geologic setting of the area, the
project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as a Very Dense Soil
and Soft Rock Profile corresponding to a Soil Profile Type Sc. Based on our review of
available geologic information, the project site does not appear to be located in the
immediate proximity of mapped geologic fault structures. Therefore, the near-source
factors (Nz and N,) may be assumed to be 1.0 for this project site.

3.2 Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area, voids and/or cavities
appear to be present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site. To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below
foundations, we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting of the
reservoir tank structure foundation be implemented during construction. Foundation
probing and grouting may be omitted for other minor structures planned at the site.
For estimating purposes, we recommend that probe holes be drilled at 10-foot on
centers for the perimeter ring foundation. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at
each isolated spread footing location. Probe holes also should be drilled within the tank
floor area at about 10-foot on centers starting at the center of the tank and radiating out
at 10-foot radial increments. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter
and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation.
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation, additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a
slump of about 6 to 9inches. The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate
pressures. As an alternate to CLSM, sand-cement grout with a slump of about 6 to
9 inches also may be used for the grouting operations.
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the site, we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding. In addition, the probe drill should be available on-site until the probing and
grouting operations are completed. The contractor also should be made aware that a
longer lag time between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might
be required in the construction schedule.
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of
a representative from Geolabs. This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of

3.3.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as
embankment fill materials.
Following clearing and grubbing, the ground surface should be proof-rolled with a
minimum 15-ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to assist in
detecting and possibly collapsing near-surface cavities. We wish to point out that
caution should be exercised by the contractor while clearing and grading near the
existing collapsed features, which may represent potential lava tubes or cavities
that may further collapse under the load of heavy equipment. The vibratory drum
roller used for the proof-rolling operations should be operated at a speed of about
300 feet per minute.

general fill materials capped with a minimum of 2 feet of structural fills may be used
in these areas. General fills should consist of well-graded granular materials, the
majority of which are less than 12 inches in size with an absolute maximum
dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 12 and 18 inches in particle size should
be limited to less than 15 percent of the total general fill materials. The excavated
on-site materials, if less than 18 inches in maximum dimension, may be used as a
source of general fill or backfill materials (except for trench backfill), provided that
they are processed to meet the above gradation requirements for general fill.
If the excavated on-site materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a
well-graded material, off-site borrow or on-site rock crushing of large-sized rock
fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and
particle sizes to develop a well-graded material. Imported material, if necessary,

3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557. Optimum
moisture content is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the
maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers,
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment.
Conventional compaction testing generally is not practical in fills composed of rocks
and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the number of passes by a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted
at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs. Based on this testing program, the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction.
For preliminary estimating purposes, compaction may be accomplished by using a
large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute. The roller should pass over the fill surface at
least eight times for structural fill and six times for general fill. For general fill, a
Caterpillar D-9 bulldozer or similar size equipment may be used in-lieu of the
vibratory drum roller.

3.3.4 Excavation
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by clinker
(a'a lava flow) materials and fractured pahoehoe lavas ranging from medium hard
to very hard. It is anticipated that the near-surface materials may be excavated
quite readily with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators and
ripping with large bulldozers. However, some of the excavations in the pahoehoe
formations and/or the core of the a’a lava formation may require the use of
hoe-rams, chipping, or blasting.
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and experience with
similar type subsurface conditions at the project site. We recommend that
contractors be encouraged to examine the site conditions and boring data to make
their own interpretation.
3.3.5 Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on our observations and field exploration, it appears that the cut slopes at
the project site generally would expose clinker materials. In general, we believe
that the cut slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one
vertical (2.H:1V) or flatter. In areas where dense basalt formation is encountered at
the cut slope face, the cut slopes may be steepened to a slope inclination of 1H:1V
or flatter. Large cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face should be
backfilled and/or grouted, if necessary. A representative from Geolabs should
observe the cut slopes during construction to evaluate whether the exposed cut
slope materials are consistent with our assumptions.
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general and structural fill materials
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.
Fills that are to be placed on existing ground steeper than 5H:1V should be
benched. The fill slope face should be free of boulders (rock fragments greater
than 12 inches in largest dimension) and should be free of voids, which would allow
erosion and migration of fines to occur. In addition, slope planting or other means

of slope protection should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential
for significant erosion of the finished slopes.
3.4 Concrete Pads
We envision that concrete pads, such as pads for the altitude valve station,
would be constructed at the project site. In general, we recommend that a minimum
4-inch thick cushion fill layer consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67
gradation) be provided below the concrete pad for uniform support. As an alternative,
the concrete pad may be constructed directly on the compacted structural fill subgrade
(in fill areas), provided that the subgrade is properly prepared and compacted. To
reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, crack control
joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the concrete pads with
expansion joints at right-angle intersections.
3.5 Pipelines
We envision that a water pipeline will be installed at the project site. It is
anticipated that most of the trenches for the pipelines will be excavated in the clinker
materials and the medium hard to very hard basalt rock encountered in the borings
drilled for the project. In general, we recommend that a granular bedding layer
consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be
provided below the pipes for uniform support of the pipes.
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement
of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes.
As an alternative, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill (up to about
12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch minus, well-graded granular
materials (aggregate base course). Where used, the 1-inch minus, well-graded

3.6 Thrust Blocks
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at the bends of the new pipeline.
The following recommendations may be used for the preliminary design and sizing of
the thrust blocks. Lateral loads acting on the thrust blocks may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the thrust blocks and the bearing materials
and by passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the thrust block.
For frictional resistance, a coefficient of friction of 0.45 may be used for the concrete
thrust blocks bearing on the compacted structural fill or basalt formation.
In general, we envision that the bottom of the thrust blocks may be embedded at
least 4 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our field exploration, clinker
materials and dense basalt formation generally were encountered at relatively shallow
depths. Therefore, we believe that a lateral bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per
square foot (psf) may be used for the design of thrust blocks. This assumes that the
soils around the thrust blocks are well compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction).

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling seven borings designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7. The borings were
advanced to a maximum depth of about 36.5 feet below the existing ground surface
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools. The approximate locations of
the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
