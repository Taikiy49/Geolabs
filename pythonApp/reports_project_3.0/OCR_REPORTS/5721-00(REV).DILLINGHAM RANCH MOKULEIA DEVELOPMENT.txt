2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the
island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island
The project site is situated on a narrow coastal plain and pediment which was
formed by the accumulation of a relatively thick wedge of colluvium alluvium and
marine sediments along the base of an extensive wave cut cliff on the northern flank of
the Waianae Volcano Geological conditions at the site and in its immediate vicinity can
generally be delineated by the location and slope of the various portions of the site In
the area close to Farrington Highway where the slope is relatively level the shallow
subsurface conditions generally consist of marine sediments dune sands and recent
alluvium The soils in this area generally have poor engineering characteristics
e g soft soil conditions and high ground water table
As the slope steepens to the inland side of the site the subsurface conditions
become dominated by older alluvial materials that have accumulated in alluvial fans
originating from major drainageways draining to the north from the Waianae Range
These alluvial materials consist of interbedded clays silts gravels and some areas of
boulder conglomerate In some areas the alluvium has weathered to a residual type
soil and the underlying conglomerates have weathered to a saprolitic state The soils in
these areas are generally more competent however they exhibit moderate to high
shrink swell potentials
Further inland as the property approaches the base of the Waianae Range the
geology grades to an accumulation of talus and colluvium at the base of the wave cut
cliff This interbedded talus and colluvium generally consists of angular boulders and

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contaminating the
excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill material
Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Boulders or cobbles
greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension produced during scarification should
be removed and disposed properly at a location outside of the grading limits
The compacted subgrade should be kept moist until new fill is placed over it If the
subgrade is allowed to dry and shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of
the subgrade we recommend scarifying the soils again and preparing the subgrade
as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill The

Fills and Backfills
The upper expansive clay soils are not suitable for use as fill material and should

General fills below 3 feet from finished grade level may consist of soil and rock

Structural fill material should be used for the top 3 feet of the fill and consist of

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density We emphasize that moisture
conditioning of the fill materials is an integral part of the fill placement
recommendations provided herein Sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment should be used to achieve the proper
degree of compaction
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests
during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of
compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that
the moisture requirement about 2 percent above the optimum moisture of the fills
and subgrades is an important requirement for the use of the on site clayey soils

adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 1 foot above the pipes to
the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or general fill
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical
compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials at the project
site Compaction efforts by water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed
considering the expansive soil conditions at the site
In general we recommend providing a minimum cover of 3 feet between the top
of the utility pipes and the finished grades to facilitate construction of the pavement
structure and compaction of the trench backfill If the select granular fill is placed before
the installation of underground utilities the contractor should exercise care during
backfilling operations to replace the required thickness of select granular fill in the
trench backfill
In the western part of the site since basalt formation or boulder conglomerate
was encountered at shallow depths during our field exploration our opinion is that
trench work for utility installation may involve excavation in the coral formation and may
require hard ripping or hoerams The contractor must exercise care to avoid
over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the formation resulting in a potential
loss of bearing strength
