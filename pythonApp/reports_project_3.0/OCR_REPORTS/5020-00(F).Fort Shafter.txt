FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of about 6 to 11 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers The approximate locations of the
borings drilled at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 4 through 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

encountered we recommend that the light poles be supported by shaft foundations
Based on the preliminary design of the light pole foundations 24 inch diameter
drilled shafts were considered in our analyses The major considerations in our
foundation analyses included the lateral loads and ground line overturning
moments induced in the shaft foundations
In general lateral load resistance for shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the

Light Pole Construction Considerations
The performance of the shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend
that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe the installation of the
drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may have a
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the shaft foundations
The load carrying capacity of shafts depends to a large extent on the contact
between the shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling or
excavating the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of
shafts should be cleaned of loose or disturbed material prior to constructing the
foundation The bottom should also be fairly level The concrete should be poured
neat against the excavation sidewalls Backfill against the shaft foundations should
not be allowed
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used
to provide close contact between the shafts and the surrounding soils The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from the
bottom of the drilled shaft

The procedures described in Chapter 8 of the Pavement Design for Roads
Streets Walks and Open Storage Areas prepared by the Departments of the Army and
the Air Force dated June 1992 were referred to in determining the new pavement sections
We have made our preliminary pavement designs assuming the pavement subgrade soil
will consist of the silty near surface soils encountered during our field exploration with CBR
values ranging between 31 and 36 2 and expansion values ranging between 0 5 to
0 6 percent In addition we have assumed that the loading on the pavements will consist
of light vehicular traffic If site grading exposes soil other than those assumed additional
tests should be performed to confirm or revise the recommended pavement sections for
actual field conditions Based on the above assumptions we recommend the following
pavement section be used for preliminary design purposes
Flexible Pavements
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density The select borrow subbase and aggregate base course should
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The recommended pavement section assumes that good drainage will be
provided for areas adjacent to the pavements To reduce the potential for infiltration of
water into the pavement structural section the pavement surface and the subgrade should
be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to
appropriate drainage structures This is critical to the long term performance of the
pavement structure considering the nature of the on site soils Surface water ponding
should not be allowed on the site during or after construction Ponding of water adjacent
to the edge of the pavement structural section will allow infiltration and accumulation of
subsurface water which will likely soften the subgrade soils and cause premature failure of
the pavement structure Extensive landscaping should be avoided adjacent to the
pavement areas otherwise we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed to
collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation and to reduce the potential for
migration of landscape water into the pavement section

Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and

Soft loose and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated
to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft loose and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed
and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with fill material placed in 8 inch
loose lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete or Controlled Low
Strength Material CLSM under low pressure
The areas to receive fill and finished subgrades should be scarified to about
8 inches moisture conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and
prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the

over excavation of the soft and or loose areas and replacement with well compacted fill

Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the duct lines Free draining granular materials such as
clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of
the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density
