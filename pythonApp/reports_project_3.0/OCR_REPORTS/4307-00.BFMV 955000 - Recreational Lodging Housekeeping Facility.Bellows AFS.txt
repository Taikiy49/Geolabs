INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Recreational Lodging Housekeeping Facility project within the Bellows Air
Force Station in the District of Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of
foundations cesspool and leaching field site preparation and pavements only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two separate
submarine shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and Koolau is estimated to
be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 5 million years ago After a long period
of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield
volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone
formations These more recent series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded a larger volume of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The larger glacioeustatic sea level
GEOLABS INC
W O 4307 00 Page 5
fluctuations ranged between about 250 feet above the present sea level to about 400 feet
below the present sea level Thus the island shorelines have transgressed and regressed in
response to the fluctuations
The processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments were
affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the
erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the
present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were carried further seaward and
deposited in what had been marine environments When the sea level was high the erosional
base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations In addition it
allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as coral and limestone in environments that
had been covered with terrestrial soils and rock
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material downslope
toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the colluvium reaches
streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported
and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be found in the lowland areas and
bordering streams
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place or buried for long periods of time
chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or
weathering of the material The chemical processes also cause induration or cementation
of the coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary
rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in areas above the valley floors and
upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported
downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and density are
generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

The project site is situated on the Waimanalo coastal plain located northeasterly of the

Foundations
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for support
of the new recreational lodging housekeeping facility planned at the site An allowable bearing
pressure of up to may be used for the design of foundations bearing on compacted
sand This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
It should be noted that the near surface beach sand is poorly graded with uniform
particle size As such the sand is difficult to compact particularly when dry Therefore the
bottoms of the footing excavations should be carefully compacted at above the optimum
moisture content prior to steel and concrete placement
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should
be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility tench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
GEOLABS INC
W O 4307 00 Page 9
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the over excavated
footing excavations the soft and or loose materials should be removed to expose the
underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation may be backfilled with the excavated
on site materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our recommendations
we estimate the total settlements of footings to be less than 1 inch Differential settlements
between adjacent footings should be on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used for footings bearing on compacted sand Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot
per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive pressure resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected
Observation and testing of the over excavation below the footings should be designated
as a Special Inspection item Therefore we recommend that footing excavations be
observed by a representative of Geolabs Inc prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete to confirm the competency of the foundation bearing materials and the required
embedment depths

For interior slabs we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting

Cesspool and Leaching Field
We understand that on site sewage treatment and disposal by cesspool and leaching
field method are planned within the new lodging housekeeping facility site Therefore field
percolation tests were conducted to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of the
sewage disposal system A total of three field percolation tests were performed at selected
locations across the project site including two tests at the location of Boring No 1 and one test
at the location of Boring No 4 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
One field percolation test was performed in Boring No 1 in general accordance with
GEOLABS INC

Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State s Standards to evaluate the
permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The test was performed by
drilling a 4 5 inch diameter borehole to a depth of 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Approximately 6 inches of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole to protect the
bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments During testing the hole was carefully filled
with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the gravel The time intervals and water
drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation test
was based Based on the percolation tests conducted the percolation rate is on the order of
approximately 0 02 minutes per inch at the test location of Boring No 1
Two field percolation tests were performed in Boring Nos 1 and 4 to evaluate the
permeability of the subsurface soils at the site These two tests were conducted using the
constant head and falling head methods The tests were performed by drilling with 4 inch D
hollow stem augers to 7 feet below the existing ground surface which remained in the
boreholes to act as casings After the borings were drilled water was injected into the borings
The water was maintained at a certain level and the water pumping rate was measured for the
constant head test For the falling head test the water source was shut off after water
overflowed and the drop in the water level within the boring was measured
The tests indicated that the subsurface soils have a coefficient of permeability k in the
range of 0 04 centimeters per second cm s for the constant head test in Boring No 1 and
about s for the falling head test in Boring No 4 Results of the individual percolation
tests are presented on Plates 3 through 5 of this report
According to the Manual of Septic Tank Practice prepared by the U S Department
of Health Education and Welfare proposed leach field locations with the percolation rates
obtained at the site would be suitable for the siting of the absorption system at the site

Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated at the site At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish asphalt concrete
pavements and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and
disposed of properly off site As indicated previously based on our field observation due to
the large trees near and at the project site we envision that extensive root systems may be
presented in the area of the proposed new facility building To reduce the potential for
settlement from deterioration of the roots we recommend that roots larger than 1 inch in
diameter should be completely removed from the proposed project site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive
fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft soils
should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation
in the over excavation quantities
Existing underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
After clearing and demolition areas beneath the future pavement areas should be
scarified to a depth of 12 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In addition the subgrade
soils should be wetted and kept moist until placement of the pavement structural section or
concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
GEOLABS INC
W O 4307 00 Page 13
D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The beach sand is uniformly graded and therefore is difficult to compact particularly
when dry Where the soil is unconfined such as at the ground surface the material is easily
disturbed due to lack of cohesion and interlocking Therefore the contractor should properly
moisten the soils prior to compaction and be careful not to disturb the final grades prior to
concrete placement
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommend above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent
or less Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Site preparation should be observed by a representative of Geolabs It is important that
a representative from our office observe the site preparation operations to evaluate whether
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification process and
whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field
exploration

Pavement Design
We understand that asphalt concrete pavements will be required for the parking areas
on the western side of the new building In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for
this project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks Our laboratory
tests performed on the near surface sands encountered in the area of the proposed parking
Stalls appears to have a CBR value of 9 with a swell value of less than 1 percent Considering
the generally light to medium traffic anticipated for the proposed project we recommend that
the following pavement design be used for preliminary budgeting purposes
Flexible Asphaltic Concrete Pavements
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 percent relative compaction
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase 95 percent relative compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The subgrade soils below the new pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction The select borrow subbase and aggregate base course
should consist of crushed basalt aggregates compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or
after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential for migration of
landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system is recommended
to collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we believe
that a subdrain system should be considered adjacent to the paved landscaped areas

Utility Trench
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for the proposed project
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill
up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench
backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches
are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundations
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and pavement
areas should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 10 5 to
31 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
