INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed cold storage facility within the U S Naval Supply Depot at
Orote Point on the island of Guam in the Mariana Islands The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
of the data obtained from the field exploration and laboratory testing of selected samples
The recommendations contained herein are intended for design input and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding
purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new cold storage warehouse building at the U S
Naval Supply Depot within the U S Naval Station at Orote Point on the island of Guam
in the Mariana Islands
The new storage facility would consist of a single story concrete structure providing
approximately 55 000 square feet of floor space We understand that the proposed
concrete floor slab will be subjected to forklift traffic due to materials handling Supporting
facilities will include paved parking areas walkways and site utilities

The foundation of the island is composed of volcanic rocks including pyroclastic

Seismic Considerations
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes When
the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibrations Such as those induced during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build
up of pore pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soils is reduced Under
long durations of vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as
a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations Soils
most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose
silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction would not occur
if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground surface In deeper deposits the
greater overburden pressure is sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3010 00 Page 8
Generally the major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil include the
following
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniformly graded sands and silts with little
cohesion are more susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded
sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands are most susceptible to liquefaction
Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative density
3 Saturation Saturated sands and silts with little cohesion are more
susceptible to liquefaction
4 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
Our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by loose saturated
sandy materials up to approximately 15 feet thick below the surficial fill crust Based on
the seismic zonation Guam is classified as Seismic Zone 3 with an effective peak ground
acceleration of 0 3g Based on a design peak ground acceleration of 0 3g liquefaction
may occur resulting in appreciable settlements to the ground surface Therefore the
effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into account in the design
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration of the site thick compressible deposits consisting
of very loose clayey gravel materials were encountered to a depth of about 25 to 35 feet
below the surficial fill crust Because of the moderate to high structural loads anticipated
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3010 00 Page 9
for this project a shallow foundation system used to support the proposed storage facility
would be subjected to appreciable total and differential settlements As a result we
recommend that a foundation system consisting of friction and or end bearing piles be
used to support the proposed cold storage facility structure
We understand that new fills on the order of 1 to 4 feet may be needed within the
limits of the proposed structure to accommodate the docking facility We also understand
that significant structural loads will be imposed on the floor slab of the structure Because
of these additional loads which would be imposed on the significant thickness of the
compressible deposits future settlements of the ground surface are anticipated As a
result we recommend that the floor slab for the proposed storage facility be structurally
supported on pile foundations to reduce the potential for differential settlements between
a floor slab supported on grade and the columns supported on piles
In addition we anticipate that liquefaction of the loose sandy soils would occur
when subjected to earthquake induced vibrations Because of the anticipated ground
settlements pile foundations founded through the loose sandy layer would be subjected
to downdrag forces Therefore the allowable load capacity of the piles will need to be
reduced by the estimated downdrag loads
Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to foundation
design site grading and pavement design are further discussed in the following sections
of this report
Pile Foundations
Because of the anticipated moderate to high column loads for the proposed
storage facility we recommend that pile foundations deriving support from skin friction
and or end bearing in the sandy limestone gravel deposits be used to support the

proposed structure Based on the data obtained from our field exploration and laboratory
testing piles driven through the upper fill crust and compressible deposits to a depth of

recommended capacity to support the proposed structure
Vertical Pile Capacities
We recommend that 16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles with
an allowable compressive load capacity of 70 tons per pile be used to support the
proposed storage facility structure The allowable compressible load capacity will
need to be reduced by an estimated downdrag load of 15 tons per pile to support
building loads This is further discussed in the following subsection The piles will
derive load supporting capacity from frictional resistance and or end bearing in the
sandy limestone gravel deposits Due to the potentially compressible nature of the
very loose clayey sand gravel deposits the frictional supporting capacity of the
piles within these loose layers have been discounted To develop the
recommended pile capacity of 70 tons a pile embedment of approximately 50 to
60 feet below the existing surface will be required A minimum pile spacing of
4 feet center to center is recommended to avoid reduction in pile capacity due to
group action
An allowable uplift capacity of 30 kips per pile may be used in design The
recommended pile capacities are for sustained loading conditions For transient
loads such as wind or seismic forces the compressive and uplift load capacities
may be increased by one third

The above pile lengths are developed for preliminary pile foundation cost
estimation purposes The production pile casting lengths will be estimated after

Downdrag
Where regional artesian head is lowered or fill is placed over a compressible soil
the latter consolidates and moves downward relative to the pile The downward
movement results in negative skin friction forces generally known as DOWNDRAG
forces on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the
thickness of the compressible layer adhesion between the soil and pile and the
pile perimeter area
With a structurally supported floor slab the amount of potential ground settlements
would be induced only by the 1 to 2 feet of new fills needed to raise the existing
ground surface to the bottom elevation of the structural slab We estimate these
potential fill ground settlements would be on the order of to inch As a result
we do not believe that significant downdrag forces would be imposed on the piles
as a result of this small settlement
However as previously discussed in the Seismic Considerations section of this
report liquefaction of the loose clayey sand gravel deposits would occur in the
event of an earthquake The earthquake induced liquefaction would result in
significant settlements and would impose downdrag loads on the pile foundations
driven through these deposits We estimate that downdrag loads on the order of
15 tons per pile may be anticipated during a moderate to large earthquake
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3010 00 Page 12
The allowable pile capacity of 70 tons will need to be reduced by the estimated
downdrag load of 15 tons resulting in a net allowable pile capacity of 55 tons for
support of building loads However the pile should be designed to support a
structural load of 70 tons
Foundation Settlements
Pile settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than one inch with
differential settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that amount
Settlements should be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Predrilling
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered during our field exploration it is our
opinion that the piles should be able to penetrate through the surficial fill crust
without the need for predrilling However this upper fill crust will contribute
downdrag load on the pile foundation system during seismic excitations
Therefore predrilling through the upper fill crust is recommended to reduce the
contribution of downdrag loads from this upper fill layer and unit prices for
predrilling should be obtained during the bidding process
Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
at least 40 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the loose and soft deposits may be performed
at a lower energy level to reduce the development of tensile stresses within the
piles which could cause damage cracking to the piles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3010 00 Page 13
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
during driving This should be accomplished by using a driving resistance
procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the more
sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity The
driving criteria will be developed after the selection of the pile driving equipment for
production pile driving Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose sandy soil the
hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with reduced energy levels so
that excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven
This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of
the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs Hawaii should review the pile
driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of installing the piles
to the recommended capacity Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit
prices should be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles additional welds if
used at the connection etc

Floor Slabs
We understand that significant live loading of the floor slabs are planned for the
concrete floors of the storage facility Because of the presence of the loose and soft
compressible soils at the site we anticipate that future settlement of the ground surface
would occur due to the loading imposed on the compressible soils by the new fills floor
slab and the anticipated loading on the slab As a result to reduce the potential
differential settlements between the structure supported on piles and the floor slab
supported on grade we recommend that the floor slabs be structurally supported on pile
foundations Floor slabs should be designed to span between pile foundations
A base course layer beneath the structurally supported floor slab will not be
necessary However the floor slab should be supported on a prepared subgrade The
subgrade soils beneath slabs should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches and compacted
to at least 90 percent of the maximum dry density determined in accordance with ASTM

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
resistance of the pile soil system to deflect and passive resistance on the front side of pile
Caps and grade beams Due to the possible future settlement of the ground surface
sliding friction between the bottom of the slab and the supporting subgrade should be
neglected due to uncertainty regarding long term soil contact
The lateral load resisting capacity of the 16 2 inch octagonal pile is estimated to be
on the order of 15 kips per pile This assumes a lateral deflection of inch at the top of
the pile for a free head condition For passive soil resistance an equivalent fluid pressure
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth may be used

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings to
depths ranging between 103 and 150 feet below the existing ground surface Boring
No 7 was drilled to a depth of 128 5 feet and was further advanced by continuous
penetration without sampling to a depth of 136 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The borings were drilled using truck mounted equipment The approximate locations of
the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System Graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 2 inch O D standard
penetration split spoon sampler or a 3 inch O D split barrel ring sampler with a 140
pound hammer falling 30 inches Soft soil samples were obtained by hydraulically
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive or the hydraulic
pressures needed to push the sampler each 6 inches of the first 24 inches where
available are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74 percent
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
GEOLABS HAWAII

rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its

would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
