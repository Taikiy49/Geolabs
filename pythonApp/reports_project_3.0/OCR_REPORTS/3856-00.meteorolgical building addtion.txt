INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Meteorological Building Addition project located within the
Pearl Harbor Naval Base on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation retaining
walls and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a single story building addition to the existing
Meteorological Building at the corner of Makalapa Drive and Luapele Drive in Pearl
Harbor The proposed addition will cover approximately 216 square meters and includes
relocation of the loading dock We understand that a parking area site work and
supporting utilities are also planned
Detailed grading plans for the project were not available at the time this report was
prepared However we anticipate that only minor cuts and fill on the order of less than
0 3 meters relative to the existing ground surface will be required to provide a level
building pad and parking areas Details regarding structural loads were not available at
the time this report was prepared

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3856 00 Page 4
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised
deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series and includes Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa Kamanaiki Punchbowl
and other minor vents Many of the eruptions were rather
explosive in nature due to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma resulting
in steam explosions which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material
predominantly ash and cinder Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented
together rapidly to form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as mudrock
The proposed project site is located at the edge of Makalapa Crater in southern
Oahu With the urbanization of the area materials dredged from Pearl Harbor were
hydraulically filled in the lower areas of the crater Subsequently man made fill was
placed over the hydraulic fill to level out the ground surface Various types of fill ranging
from good quality material to construction debris may have been used Land
development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the area to
its present form

2 borings to depths of about 12 2 and 12 7 meters below the existing ground surface

Based on our field exploration the proposed project site is generally underlain by
fill consisting of very stiff gravelly clay and medium dense clayey gravel This layer is

Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing we believe that a shallow
foundation system bearing on the recompacted near surface fill may be used to support
the proposed structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to 145 kPa may be used

Building Foundations
The proposed facility will consist of a single story building addition to the existing
structure Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used to support the proposed structure
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 145 kPa may be used for the design of
footings bearing on the recompacted fill This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as temporary
wind and or seismic forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of 450 millimeters
below the lowest adjacent finished grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3856 00 Page 7
For the light structure planned the zone of influence due to the foundation loads
would essentially be confined to the upper fill layer Because the existing fill over the soft
to medium stiff silty clay have been in place for many years the ground settlements due
to the weight of the existing fill can be considered complete Therefore the condition of
the upper fill layer would govern the foundation design We estimate that the total footing
settlements under the anticipated design loads to be on the order of less than
25 millimeters with differential settlement of approximately half of that amount
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the spread footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings and the
supporting materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 50 kilonewtons per square meter per meter of depth
kN cu m for footings embedded in the recompacted fill These values assume that the
concrete for footings is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless covered
by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 300 millimeters below the
finished grade should be neglected
Where footings are located adjacent to or on slopes the footings should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of at least
2 5 meters measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the slope Where
footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the footings
should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the
bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3856 00 Page 8
Footing excavations should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations the soil should be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete
placement
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the competency of the
bearing materials and embedment depths If unsuitable materials are encountered in the
footing excavations these materials should be over excavated to expose firm soils and
replaced with compacted engineered fill
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
addition The subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of at least
200 millimeters moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
To provide a more uniform support under slabs we recommend that the concrete
slab on grade be supported on a minimum 100 millimeter cushion fill The cushion fill
layer should consist of open graded gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation which may also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion
fill should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification and recompaction of the
existing subgrade soils as recommended above For slabs on grade_ with
moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an impermeable membrane be placed
over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration of moisture vapor through the concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3856 00 Page 9
slab In order to promote more uniform curing of the slab concrete and to provide
protection of the vapor membrane we recommend that a 50 millimeter layer of moist fine
sand be placed above the membrane and moistened slightly prior to placing the slab
concrete
Site Grading
Final grading plans were not available to us at the time this report was prepared
However we anticipate minor grading with cuts and fill on the order of less than
0 3 meters relative to the existing ground surface for the proposed project
Site grading operations should be observed by a _ representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe whether the
exposed soil conditions during excavation are similar to those encountered in our
exploration The following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the
design of site grading work
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site After clearing and grubbing the
exposed subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of 200 millimeters properly
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For subgrades of
improvements subjected to vehicular traffic such as the parking areas the compaction
requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3856 00 Page 10
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Prior to
placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of
at least 200 millimeters moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
The on site soils generated from the excavation may be reused as a source of fill
and backfill materials The on site soils should be properly moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding
200 millimeters in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
Permanent cut and fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two
horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fill placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched
If imported fill materials are required it should consist of well graded granular
materials less than 75 millimeters in maximum dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing
the No 200 sieve Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended
use Imported materials should be brought to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction

Retaining Walls
Short retaining walls of up to about 1 5 meters in height may be required for the
relocated loading dock wall Foundations for the loading dock walls should be designed
in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations section
of this report
Wall footings should have a minimum width of 450 millimeters In general wall
foundations on relatively flat areas 6H 1V or flatter should be embedded a minimum
depth of 450 millimeters below the lowest adjacent finished grade

In general lateral load resistance recommendations presented in the Building
Foundations section of this report should be used for the design of retaining walls
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 0 3 to 0 7 meter wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated
drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage
material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the design For
uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a rectangular
distribution with uniform pressure equal to 30 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure
acting on the entire height of the wall which is free to deflect cantilever may be used in
design For walls that are restrained a rectangular distribution equal to 45 percent of the
vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may be used for
design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge
effects of point loads and line loads
Pavements
New flexible pavements are anticipated for the parking and loading dock areas for
the proposed project We anticipate that vehicle loading for this project will consist
primarily of passenger vehicles light pick up trucks and occasional heavy trucks We
have made our preliminary pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will
be similar to the clayey gravel and gravelly clay soils encountered in the borings with a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3856 00 Page 13
minimum CBR of 36 and swell of less than 1 percent Based on the above assumptions
we recommend the following pavement section be used for preliminary design purposes
Asphaltic Concrete Pavement
50 mm Asphalt Concrete
150 mm Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
200 mm Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of
200 millimeters moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction If pumping of
the subgrade occurs during construction the subgrade should be proof rolled to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction After proof rolling a layer of filter fabric such
as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the subgrade prior to placement of the
subbase and base course The subbase and base course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Laboratory CBR tests should be performed on samples of the actual subgrade
soils encountered during construction to confirm the above recommended design section
The recommended pavement section is based on the assumption that good drainage will
be provided for areas adjacent to the pavements
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of
50 millimeters into the subgrade below the base and or subbase course to reduce
migration of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system
could be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we recommend a_ subdrain system be constructed adjacent to
paved landscape areas

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines would be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting
of 150 millimeters of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes The backfill up to about 0 3 meters above the pipes should consist of
free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 0 3 meters above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or
the on site soils The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 200 millimeter level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper
0 7 meters of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface soils The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3856 00 Page 15
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately
after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slabs and footing foundations

If the actual soil conditions encountered during construction are different from
those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the design
should be made

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations presented in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field borings Variations of conditions between the field
borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident
until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
reevaluate the recommendations presented in this report
The locations of the field borings are approximate having been estimated by
taping from visible features shown on the Proposed Site Plan provided by Kajioka Okada
Yamachi Architects dated February 1997 The locations of the field borings should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used

the approximate boundaries between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual
transition Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or presented in the text of this report These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall time of year temperature and
other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings to depths of about 12 2 and 12 7 meters below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a hammer falling 760 millimeters The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 300 millimeters of a 460 millimeter drive are shown
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
