SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration of the new athletic field (Site 1) generally encountered surface 
fills and alluvium/colluvium overlying saprolites and basalt formation at greater depths. We 
anticipate basalt formation may be encountered at shallower depths along the northern 
boundary of Site 1. Based on our field exploration at the new mulching site (Site 2), the lot 
is generally underlain by surface fills over relatively shallow saprolite and basalt formation. 
At the time of our field exploration, groundwater encountered at Site 1 generally 
corresponds to Elevations +5.5 to +14.5 feet MSL, whereas the groundwater encountered 
at Site 2 generally corresponds to an elevation of about +0.3 feet MSL. It should be noted 
that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated structural 
loads, we envision that the proposed site improvement structures may be supported on 
shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design finish 
grades. This bearing value is for supporting dead plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
We anticipate earthwork for this project constitutes a major portion of the construction 
costs, including cuts as deep as 20 feet and fills of up to about 8 feet thick to construct a 
level athletic field. We believe that slope inclinations of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) 
or flatter may be used for the design of planned cut and fill slopes. We believe that a cut 
slope inclination as steep as 1H:1V may be feasible in areas where very dense basalt 
formation is encountered, especially along the northern boundary of Site 1. Geolabs should 
be consulted to evaluate the quality of the exposed basalt formation and provide site-specific 
recommendations pertaining to cut slopes steeper than the 2H:1V slope inclination. Areas 
with limited horizontal setback will require retaining structures of up to about 30 feet in height 
for grade separation purposes.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the design of the Athletic Field Relocation and Upgrades at Lower 
Campus project at the University of Hawaii at Manoa campus in Honolulu on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These 
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining 
structures, site grading, pavements, underground utility lines, and an underground 
detention/infiltration system only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The design-build project is located at two sites within the Lower Campus of the 
University of Hawaii at Manoa. The first site (designated in this report as “Site 1”) is 
located at the northeast corner of Lower Campus, where it is proposed to construct a new 
athletic track and soccer field. Site 1 is currently occupied by two practice fields covered 
by lawn grass with a grade separation slope between the two fields. 
Based on the Grading and Drainage Plan prepared by Hida Okamoto & 
Associates, Inc., dated July 7, 2023, we envision cuts as deep as 20 feet and fills of up 
to about 8 feet thick would likely be required. We understand retaining wall systems of up 
to about 30 feet high would be constructed for grade separation.  
The second site (designated in this report as “Site 2”) is proposed as a mulching 
site to process and hold the green waste to be removed from Site 1. Site 2 is an unused 
field of about 0.5 acres in land area, located at the southeast corner of the Lower Campus. 
In addition to the green waste holding, some site improvements are desired, including a 
storage shelter supported on a new concrete pad, a new gravel paved access driveway, 
and surrounding hedges and fences.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano reached 
the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the northwest 
to southeast trending rift zones. These lavas consist of interbedded pahoehoe and a’a 
type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form. “A’a” is the Hawaiian term for 
basaltic lava flow typified by a rough, jagged, spinose, clinkery surface. “Pahoehoe” is the 
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth, billowy or ropy surfaces. After 
the cessation of the main shield-building phase of Koolau Volcano, there was a long 
period of erosion. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Along the crest of the ridge along the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley, there are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. 
When these vents were active, one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down the 
westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor. The lava flows ponded and 
filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley. The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it 
cooled, forming dense massive rock. The new lava flows from these vents blocked Manoa 
Stream and forced the stream to the east between the East-West Center and Waahila 
Rise. These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili District, where the basalt rocks were 
quarried in the past. Reef limestone related to the Waimanalo stand of the sea underlies 
the basalt at the former Moiliili Quarry. Man-made fills were placed in the area after the 
termination of operations at the Moiliili Quarry and brought the project site to its current 
conditions.

Chapter 20, Site Classification Procedure for Seismic Design, contained in ASCE 
Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures, 2016 Edition (ASCE 7-16).

3.1 
Shallow Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated loading of 
on-site improvements, we believe that the new structures may be supported on a shallow 
foundation system, such as spread and/or continuous strip footings. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design finish 
grades. The bearing value recommended is for dead plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
For footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills, 
the bottom of the footing should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction 
to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of 
reinforcing steel and/or concrete. If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the 
bottom of the footing subgrade, they should be over-excavated to expose the underlying 
firm materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with select granular materials 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the bottom of the footing 
may be extended down to bear directly on the underlying competent materials. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finish grade. The bottom of footings located adjacent to slopes 
or on slopes should be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of at least 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footing to the 
face of the slope. Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge 
of the utility trench, or the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts 
of the utility lines.

3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects.  
Two sets of corrosivity testing, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and Eurofins Sacramento on selected soil samples obtained from 
our field exploration. A summary of the corrosivity tests is presented in Appendix B, 
Plate B-12.

Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory testing, the subsurface soils at Sites 1 
and 2, respectively, exhibit minimum resistivity values of approximately 2,300 and 
2,200 ohm-cm, both corresponding to a corrosion rating of 2 (very corrosive) to buried 
metallic structures.  
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH value, chloride content, and sulfate content found in 
the soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. 
Based on the relatively high value of pH and relatively low contents of chloride

project site may be considered “non-corrosive,” therefore, either Type I or Type II (Type 
I/II) cement may be used for the concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate 
to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide 
detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling eight exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5 and I-1 
through I-3, extending to depths of about 3 to 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem 
augers and coring tools. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on 
the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (April 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-8. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the harder and/or rock materials encountered at the project site 
were obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling

method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation or harder materials. The rock

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. For example, if 2.5 feet of 
sound material is recovered from a 5.0 foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent 
and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is 
used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of 
Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture content (ASTM D2216) and unit weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
in-situ soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results 
and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-7. 
Two Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected intact core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-8. 
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D2435) was performed on a sample 
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered. Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-9. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-10 and B-11.

Field Infiltration Tests 
Three field infiltration tests were conducted within three borehole locations, 
designated as Boring Nos. I-1 through I-3, at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. Infiltration tests in Boring 
Nos. I-1 and I-2 are approximately located at the east corner and center of the proposed 
athletic field, respectively, whereas Boring No. I-3 is approximately located along the 
southeast boundary of the proposed mulching site. The approximate field infiltration test 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. These tests were performed in 
general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, 
Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering boreholes to a selected test 
depths of about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test 
depth, a 4-inch diameter solid steel casing was set to the bottom of the drilled borehole 
to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the borehole. Falling 
head infiltration tests were performed to determine the infiltration rate of the underlying 
subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches 
of water and taking periodic readings until the water drained out or up to one hour. The 
test results are presented on Plates C-1 through C-3.
