Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with
a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series sti i WO WWWOWOWOWWOWOUOUOUOUOUUUUUU

During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the ocean basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea level When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines
When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3158 00 B Page 6
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geological time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits
Geologic maps of the vicinity of the project site indicate that the project site is
likely to be underlain by alluvium Most of the project site is mapped as older alluvium
except for the area near the existing pump station which is mapped as young alluvium
Alluvium at the project site has been deposited in the Heeia Stream drainage basin
The content of the alluvium can be highly variable although alluvial deposits in sloping
terrain such as the western portion of the project site typically include coarser grained
materials such as sands and gravels Alluvium in flatter lowlying terrain such as the
eastern portion of the project site typically includes finer grained sands silts and clays

Excavation Method
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered it is anticipated that conven

it should be noted that boulders were encountered in Boring No 6 at a shallow

excavated soils should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For pipes bearing on the medium stiff to hard silty and clayey
soils a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material such as select
borrow or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided
below the pipe Although soft loose soils were not encountered in the borings drilled
if soft loose soils are encountered at the proposed pipe inverts an additional 18 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be
provided below the bedding layer for more uniform support A typical section of the
trench is presented on Plate 3
Before the placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom should
be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to confirm that firm materials are
exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper as
recommended above
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard

backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the
pipes should consist of self compacting clean granular material such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to reduce the compaction effort required The
granular material should be less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less
than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve

The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet

in achieving the compaction requirement in the space between the pipes Therefore
we recommend that lean concrete be considered as backfill material

Settlements
Primary settlement of the force main replacement construction is normally
caused by the difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and
the heavier compacted backfill material placed over the force main pipes The net
increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench
invert Based on our calculations primary settlement on the order of less than 1 inch
is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur
if improper trench support dewatering procedures and backfilling methods are used
Therefore we believe that the force main replacement should be designed with
sufficient flexibility to accommodate possible settlements

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of
a suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing buildings roads utilities and the environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also
provide safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 10
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9A at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 An eleventh boring Boring No MW 1 was drilled near the
pump station to install a monitoring well The depths of the borings range from about
7 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck
mounted drill rig
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1
through A 11 1
After drilling and sampling Boring No MW 1 was converted into a monitoring
well during the field exploration The monitoring well was used for groundwater
sampling purposes to provide baseline groundwater quality data as required by the

Percolation Testing
Three percolation tests were performed in Boring No 9 and No 9A during our
field exploration In Boring No 9 both constant head and falling head test methods
were utilized In Boring No 9A falling head test method was used
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until
water surface reached equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The depth to the water
level and the rate of pumping were then measured
The falling head test was performed by measuring the rate of water level drop
after filling up the borehole with water
The test data and the corresponding calculated permeabilities of the subsoils are
presented on Plates B 1 through B 3

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained during our
field exploration A laboratory testing prograrn was developed to schedule appropriate
tests to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock
materials sampled
Classification Tests
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determina
tions are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties
such as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the
dry weight of the tested sample and dry unit weight are shown at the appropriate
sample depths on the Logs of Borings
Three Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 were performed
to evaluate the plasticity of the fine grained portion of the soils The Liquid Limit and
Plasticity Index of the material are used to classify the soil in accordance with the
Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests are shown at the
appropriate sample depths on the I ogs of Boring Graphic presentation of the test
results is shown on Plate C 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of the soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining
to the performance or suitability of a soil
Seven Unconfined Compression tests ASTM Test Method D 2166 85 were
performed on selected cohesive soil samples Results of these tests are shown at the
appropriate sample depths on the Logs of Borings
Corrosive Property Tests
Six sets of corrosion tests were performed by our firm and Brewer Environmental
Industries Inc on selected samples obtained during our field exploration Each set of
corrosion test includes the determination of pH value minimum resistivity chloride and
sulfate contents The test results are presented on Plate C 2
