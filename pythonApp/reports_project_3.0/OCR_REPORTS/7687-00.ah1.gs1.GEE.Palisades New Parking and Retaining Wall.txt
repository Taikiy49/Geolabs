SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration in 
support of the design of the new parking lot expansion at the Palisades Elementary 
School in the Pearl City area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses of the 
parking lot expansion project. These recommendations are intended for the design of 
pavements, site grading, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures and infiltration test results 
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this report.

City area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on the information provided, it is proposed 
to construct a new parking lot at the northern end of the existing classroom buildings to 
provide ten additional parking stalls for the campus.  In addition, a new driveway to the new 
parking lot will be constructed.

understand a new retaining wall will be required to provide the necessary grade separation

system is planned west of the new parking stalls.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas 
banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau 
referred to as the Schofield Plateau.  Both the Waianae and Koolau ranges have lost 
most of their original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by 
erosion. 
The site is located on a planèze, or a triangular relatively uneroded intravalley 
ridge, on the southwest facing flank of the Koolau dome.  The soils on the ridge are 
generally residual and saprolite soils, derived from the in-situ weathering of basaltic rock. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located at the existing Palisades Elementary School in the Pearl 
City area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The new parking lot expansion is planned at the 
northern end of the existing classroom buildings and south of the existing Pacific 
Palisades Park, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The project site spans approximately 
260 linear feet of mostly vacant land, which is currently being used by the school for 
recreational purposes.  The project site is in an area characteristic of relatively high rainfall 
conditions. Therefore, the underlying soils at the project site have relatively high in-situ 
soil moisture contents.  
Existing ground elevations at the project site generally range between about 
+589 and +601 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). In general, the existing ground is sloping 
from southeast to northwest and is mostly covered with grass and trees.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 6.5 
to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. As part of the field exploration 
program, one borehole infiltration test was performed at a depth of about 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface at the location of Boring No. 2 to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials for the design of the storm water runoff 
disposal system in order to meet the drainage requirements for Low Impact Development 
(LID). The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin top soil layer underlain by 
residual soils extending to depths of about 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Below the residual soil layer, saprolite was encountered extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The top soil generally 
consisted of sandy silts with vegetation. The residual soils consisted of soft to stiff silty 
clays and clayey silts.  The saprolite was composed of medium stiff to stiff sandy and 
clayey silts with seams of gravel. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it should be noted that groundwater level and seepage are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

3.1 
Pavement Design 
Based on the information provided, the pavement planned for the project will 
consist of flexible pavement, concrete pavement, and pavement utilizing permeable 
pavers. In general, we anticipate vehicle loading will consist primarily of passenger 
vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and occasional heavy vehicular traffic, such as handi-vans 
and school buses, which will result in less than 200,000 equivalent single axle loads 
(ESALs) during the pavement’s design life of 30 years. 
3.1.1 Design Subgrade Conditions 
We envision that the majority of the driveway and parking lot will generally be 
constructed at or near the existing grades or in a cut condition. Based on our field 
exploration, we anticipate that the majority of the pavement subgrade soils will 
generally consist of medium stiff to stiff clayey silts and silty clays. 
We have performed our pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soils 
will generally consist of the near-surface clayey silt and silty clay soils.  Laboratory 
testing of two bulk samples of the near-surface soils (Bulk-1 and Bulk-2) resulted 
in California Bearing Ratio (CBR) values of 5.3 and 26.9 with swell values of 4 and 
0.5 percent, respectively. Based on the above, a design CBR value of 5.3 has been 
adopted for the subgrade soils in our pavement design analyses for this project.

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and/or yielding 
areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive 
fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted fills.  
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills 
should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirements for finished subgrades

as crushed coralline or basaltic materials. The materials should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less.  
Aggregate base course and select borrow subbase should meet the material 
requirements for Aggregate Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in 
Sections 31 and 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, 
City and County of Honolulu, September 1986, respectively. Pavement materials 
such as No. 3A Fine aggregate and No. 2A Coarse aggregate should meet the 
material requirements for ASTM C33 No. 67 gradation and ASTM C33 No. 4 
gradation, respectively. Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with 
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should be

3.2.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision that the cut slopes planned at the project site will generally expose the 
medium stiff clayey silts and silty clays encountered in the borings. In general, cut 
slopes and fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may be designed with a slope 
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes 
steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest 
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the 
above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that over-cutting of a slope 
occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted

3.2.5 Excavation 
We understand that some relatively deep excavations for the retaining wall structure

medium stiff to stiff clayey silts and silty clays with localized remnants of weathered

Exterior concrete slabs, such as walkways, also may be required for the project.

3.4 
Retaining Structures 
Based on the information provided, we understand a new retaining wall is planned 
to be constructed in the eastern portion of the parking lot expansion. In general, retaining 
structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent 
soils and surcharge effects. Based on the medium stiff to stiff residual soil and saprolite 
materials encountered, the foundation designs for the retaining walls should be based on 
the parameters presented in the following subsections. 
3.4.1 Shallow Foundations 
Foundations for retaining walls should have a minimum width of 24 inches and the 
bottom should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade on relatively flat areas. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of the footings 
bearing on the re-compacted subgrade (saprolite) materials. This bearing value is 
for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.  
The subgrade beneath the retaining wall structure should be re-compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at 
the bottom of the foundation excavations should be over-excavated to expose the 
underlying firm materials.  The foundation over-excavation may be backfilled with 
select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. 
For sloping ground conditions, the foundation should extend deeper to obtain a 
minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of 
the footing to the face of the slope.  
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structure may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A 
coefficient of friction of 0.3 may be used for footings bearing on the saprolitic 
subgrade. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an

3.4.4 Drainage 
The backfill behind the walls of the retaining wall structure should be well-drained to 
reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist 
of a 12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations down) at the base 
of the retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an 
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may 
be used in lieu of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also 
should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall.  
Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements, the upper 12 inches of backfill 
should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for water 
infiltration behind the walls. In addition, the backfill below the drainage outlet (or 
weepholes) should consist of the relatively impervious material to reduce the 
potential for water infiltration into the footing subgrade. The relatively impervious 
material should be compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction.

3.5 
Infiltration Tests 
We envision that the project may require on-site storm water runoff management, 
such as detention basins or bioswales. In order to obtain subsurface infiltration 
information in support of the planning of such system, we conducted a falling head 
infiltration test, designated as I-1, at the location of Boring No. 2 at the site to evaluate 
the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The test was 
performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of 
Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration test was performed by augering a borehole to a selected test 
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test depth, a 
4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled borehole to allow 
infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the borehole. The falling head 
infiltration test was performed to determine the average infiltration rate of the underlying 
subsurface materials. The test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches 
of water and taking periodic readings over a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is 
then calculated based on the result of the fourth and last trial for the test location. The 
calculated infiltration rate at Infiltration Test I-1 was 0.1 inch per hour at a testing depth of 
5 feet below the existing ground surface. Details of our field infiltration testing results are 
presented in Appendix C. 
It should be noted that the infiltration value presented above is the rate of infiltration 
through the soil exposed at the bottom of a 4-inch diameter borehole, which may not 
represent the actual infiltration condition within a typical infiltration chamber footprint or 
an open basin.  Due to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the absorption capacity 
of the selected storm water disposal system should be confirmed by conducting additional 
infiltration tests during construction. Based on the infiltration testing, an infiltration rate of 
about 0.1 inch per hour was measured. Based on the measured infiltration rate, it appears 
that retention/infiltration systems for storm water management are not feasible at the 
project site.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
three exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of 
about 6.5 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. The borings were drilled 
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil and samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-2. 
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D2166) was performed to evaluate the 
unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils on a selected in-situ sample. 
The test results are shown on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth. 
Individual stress-strain curve of the unconfined compression test is presented on 
Plate B-3. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
