INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Anti Crime Streetlighting Improvements project in the
Waikiki area in the District of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations for the
proposed street lights and traffic signal lights only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project consists of the construction of new street lights and traffic
signal lights along Kalakaua Avenue The proposed Anti Crime Streetlighting
Improvements project site is approximately 6 000 feet in length stretching along
Kalakaua Avenue from the intersection of Kuhio Avenue to Monsarrat Avenue as shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project site includes both sides of Kalakaua
Avenue except between Kaiulani and Monsarrat Avenue where the project site includes
only the northeast side

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene
Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which
are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this
area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3936 00 Page 5
relative to present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in
sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the
last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy
wetlands As the city of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made
fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon
lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey
material was deposited during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which
apparently smothered the coral reef development Land development and reclamation
projects within the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki area to its present form
Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads
Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping the proposed street light and
traffic signal light pole locations are generally underlain by two major stratigraphic units
These include the Recent Unconsolidated Marine Calcareous Sediments and Recent Fill

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 10 borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to depths ranging from about 37 5 to 64 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
Below the pavement section our field exploration generally encountered a loose
to dense surface fill crust of about 2 to 4 feet in thickness The surface fill crust
generally consisted of tan to gray loose to dense silty calcareous and basalt sand
The near surface fill crust is underlain by very loose to medium dense lagoonal sand
deposits The lagoonal deposits encountered consist of tan to gray very loose to
medium dense calcareous and coral sand extending to depths of about 34 to greater
than 61 feet below the existing grade Based on our previous geotechnical engineering
explorations interbeds of clayey alluvial material and weathered coralline materials are
generally present below the lagoonal deposits
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths of about 4 3 to 7 7 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration However the
water levels at the project site may be influenced by tidal influences seasonal
precipitation and storm surge conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed light and traffic signal light pole
locations are generally underlain by loose to dense silty sand and gravel deposits Based
GEOLABS HAWAII

on the materials encountered in our field exploration we believe that the proposed lights
and traffic signal poles may be supported on drilled shaft type foundations to resist the
generally high ground line and torsional moments induced by loading of the poles
We understand that three types of drilled shaft foundation systems are planned for
supporting the poles These include
1 For centerline loading conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a
single concrete drilled shaft at grade
2 For eccentric loading anchor bolt onto a structural member that is
connected to a single concrete drilled shaft at grade
3 For a two shaft design conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a pile cap
Except for the base shear the loading and boundary conditions on the drilled shaft
for eccentric loading were adjusted based on a maximum 3 foot off center loading
The loading and boundary conditions on a two shaft design were adjusted assuming that
the loads would be equally distributed
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our analyses of the loading
on the foundations we recommend that 30 inch diameter drilled shafts be used to
support the poles
Foundation analyses were conducted based on a combination of axial and lateral
loads in addition to ground line moments using the computer software program
LPILE Plus In addition the drilled shaft foundations were also checked for the torsional
moments induced by the poles The analyses were based on a free head condition and a
maximum lateral deflection of not more than inch at the top of the shaft

support the proposed poles Axial loads of up to 12 5 kips and torsional moments of up to

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the loading conditions provided and the subsurface conditions
encountered we recommend that the poles for the street lights and traffic signal lights
be supported by drilled shaft foundations As previously indicated three types of drilled
shaft foundation systems are planned for supporting the poles These include
1 For centerline loading conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a single
concrete drilled shaft at grade
2 For eccentric loading anchor bolt onto structural member that is connected
to a single concrete drilled shaft at grade
3 For a two shaft design conventional L shape anchor bolt onto a pile cap
Based on the bottom diameter of the steel poles 24 inches at the base the
available auger core barrel sizes from the local drilling companies and the shaft lengths
anticipated drilled shaft diameters of 30 inches were considered in our foundation
analyses The major considerations in our foundation analyses included the lateral loads
and ground line moments induced in the drilled shaft foundations
In general lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the shaft
and induced moment in the shaft Based on a maximum horizontal deflection of inch at
the top of the drilled shaft subjected to ground line overturning moments and lateral
forces we have evaluated the required drilled shaft lengths using the computer software
program LPILE Plus for Windows This program is a microcomputer adaptation of a
finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the University of
Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a drilled
shaft under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3936 00 Page 12
of internally generated non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral
deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths
The analyses were performed for a laterally loaded 30 inch diameter cast in place
concrete drilled shaft The results of our engineering analyses are summarized below

Torsion Resistance
In general pole foundations will be subjected to torsional moments resulting in
torsion on the drilled shaft foundations Torsion may be resisted by the side shear along
the drilled shaft surface and adjacent soils In our foundation analyses we have
evaluated the drilled shafts subjected to the torsional moments provided by the Project
Structural Engineer Based on our analyses we believe that the 30 inch diameter drilled
shafts extending to the lengths recommended should be able to resist the torsional
moments without significant movements We wish to emphasize that the sides of the
drilled holes should be relatively free of loose materials and the concrete for the drilled
shaft should have close contact to the in situ soils after the drilled shaft has been
constructed to provide the torsional resistance required
Uplift Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the pole
structure drilled shaft foundation and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent
soils The combined dead load of the pole structure and drilled shaft foundation were
determined to range from 7 0 to 12 5 kips The combined dead load of the pole structure
and drilled shaft foundation alone should be able to resist the uplift loads without
significant movements
Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the Contractor s
method of construction construction procedures and workmanship As a result we
recommend that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the
installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may have a
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we anticipate that drilling
through loose soils will be required during construction of the majority of the drilled shaft
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3936 00 Page 14
foundations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of
the drilled holes will be needed during construction Installation of the casing may be by
vibration driving or twisting to advance the casing The contractor should be made aware
that temporary casing of the drilled holes will be needed at no additional cost to the City
and County of Honolulu In addition care should be exercised during removal of the
temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a
minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained above the bottom of the casing
and or groundwater level during removal of the casing
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all drilled shafts should be
cleaned of disturbed material prior to constructing the foundation
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids slurry is not recommended
Groundwater conditions are anticipated therefore placement of concrete by wet
construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required A low shrink concrete mix with
high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide close contact between
the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace
disturbed material from the bottom of the drilled shaft
Placement of concrete using a tremie pipe will likely cause some groundwater and
soil to discharge out of the top of the drilled shaft The Contractor should be made aware
that this fluid should be properly disposed of Since the water will be turbid from soil
particles disposal into storm drains will likely not be acceptable The Contractor
should also be made aware that a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination
System NPDES permit may be necessary depending on the _ proposed
method of water disposal The Contractor should consult their independent consultant or
GEOLABS HAWAII

the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest regulations and information
pertaining to the permit application
Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should
comply with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring
may be necessary due to the presence of near surface loose soils Trench shoring
design and installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and
bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfill material such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining
granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Soft or loose soils encountered at the bottom of trenches should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material or to a maximum 12 inches and backfilled
with No 3B Fine gravel This material should be considered as additional to the
recommended 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel granular bedding
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade below the existing pavement section or sidewalk section should consist of
the on site soils or select borrow subbase material The material used for pavement
section or sidewalk section should consist of base course or select borrow subbase
material The base course and select borrow thickness should match the existing
thickness
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3936 00 Page 16
The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement or sidewalk finish
subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
The pavement section material and sidewalk section material should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be
used to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be used to compact the backfill material
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of
backfilling observations and soil density tests should be performed by Geolabs Hawaii
during excavation and backfilling operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the
required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift
Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be applied
with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 10 borings to depths ranging from about 37 5 to 64 5 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
