INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soils engineering
investigation conducted for the proposed construction of an addition to
the Intelligence Center Pacific Facility Building 352 a utility building
and an adjoining parking lot at FICPAC Pearl Harbor Oahu Hawaii The
general location of the site is shown on Plate 1 Project Location Map
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the subsurface investigation was to explore the soil
conditions at the site to provide data for use in the design of the
foundations of the proposed structures
The scope of our investigation included field reconnaissance the
drilling of nine exploratory borings and laboratory testing of selected
soil samples The locations of the nine exploratory borings are indicated
on Plate 2 Boring Location Plan
The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which were
used in the engineering analysis that formulated our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
It is our understanding that the project will consist of a
five story rigid steel framed structure which is 68 x 124 feet in plan
dimensions and is designed to adjoin the existing Fleet Intelligence
Center Pacific Facility Building 352 The proposed structure will house a
computer facility on the first floor and is designed to have an
accessible raised floor

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the southeast intersection of
Kamehameha Highway and Halawa Drive adjacent to the Fleet Intelligence
Facility at Makalapa Pearl Harbor The site which is rectangular in
shape has a plan dimension of roughly 400 x 100 feet It is bordered to
the northeast by Halawa Drive and the southeast by a 2 1 H V slope

352
The ground surface elevations range from seven to ten feet utilizing
the USC G Mean Sea Level datum of 0 00 feet The area is presently
covered with a paved parking area and a grass field
There are several utilities below the site including drain
telephone and water There is also an 8 feet wide Navy tunnel near the
site

Geology
The geologic condition underlying the site is the result of a
complex history of deposition and erosion during the Pleistocene and
Recent epochs Most of this complex history is the result of
glacioeustatic sea level changes during the Pliestocene
During the early Pleistocene following the cessation of activity of
the Koolau Volcano material eroded from the main body of the volcano was
transported by streams to the area of what is now Pearl Harbor Alluvial
material was deposited when those streams reached the ocean in a deltaic
or estuarine environment
Following that sequence of deposition the sea level fell to a lower
level and the streams eroded a channel through their older deposits The
sea level then rose again to the Makai Range stand at 40 to 60 feet below
the existing sea level
Concurrent with that rise in sea level eruption of the Makalapa
member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium and
stream channels with volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff
locally known as mudrock Subsequent stream and wave erosion
dissected that mantle of tuff and formed shallow fingers of embayment into
the rock
During the Recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand
these embayments were filled with soft lagoonal deposits Those deposits
were subsequently covered by man made fill placed during the middle
portion of this century for land reclamation projects during the
construction of the new existing Naval Submarine Base

Subsurface Soil Conditions
The site is underlain by a layer of man made surficial fills that
consist of brown medium stiff sandy and clayey silts and or medium dense
silty sand with coral gravel The fills extend to about 7 feet below the
ground surface
Underlying the fills are geologically recent lagoonal deposits
consisting of soft slightly organic clayey silts with some sands and
gravels The soft lagoonal deposits extend to depths ranging from 18 to
88 feet with an approximate deposit thickness as depicted on Plate 4
Contour Map Approximate thickness of the Soft Lagoonal Deposit
3 and 4 which penetrated the base of the lagoonal deposits
encountered the underlying tuff formation
The volcanic tuff formation consists of an upper layer of dense
silty tuff gravel and a lower layer of very dense tuff rock mudrock
Unconfined compressive strength test performed on cores of the tuff from
respectively see Summary of Shear Strength Tests Plate B 9 The
approximate surface of the tuff formation is depicted on Plate 5 Contour
Map Approximate Depth to the Top of Tuff Formation
The tuff formation is underlain by older alluvial soils consisting
of very stiff to very hard brown clayey silt and silty clays with some
sand and gravel The older alluvial deposit was encountered in Borings 1
3 and 4 at depths 107 85 and 101 feet respectively

DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Because of the soft compressible lagoonal deposits underlying the
site a pile foundation is recommended for support of the proposed
five story FICPAC addition In order to develop the high end bearing
capacity required to support the structural loads the piles should bear
on the competent tuff formation As suggested in the Idealized Subsurface
Cross Section A A Plate 3 and the Contour Map Approximate Depth to Top
of Tuff Formation Plate 5 a variety of pile lengths will be required to
reach the tuff formation In general pile lengths may vary from 30 feet
in the south corner of the building to greater than 95 feet in the north
corner Test piles will be needed to provide a better estimate of the
production pile lengths
Due to the heavy second floor loads pile foundations are
recommended for the utility building Additional field exploration is
required to determine the pile lengths for this building More detailed
discussions and recommendations of the foundations and the pavement of the
parking area are presented in the following sections
Pile Foundations
We recommend the use of 16 5 inch octagonal prestressed precast
concrete piles The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of
delivering at least lbs of energy per blow The piles should
be driven into the dense tuff and to a resistance of 100 blows per foot
for the last foot but not to exceed 15 blows for the last inch

For the aforementioned driving criteria an allowable pile capacity
of 100 tons per pile is recommended Pile settlements on the order of
less than one half of an inch are anticipated
Where lower pile capacities may be used a smaller 12 inch square
pile with an allowable pile capacity of 60 tons may be considered
Piles should be spaced as far apart as possible and maintain a
minimum center to center distance of three pile diameters
The pile lengths and driving criteria recommended above may need to
be re evaluated according to the results of the test pile program so that
more accurate pile lengths may be selected for production driving
The actual lateral pile capacities will vary according to the actual
installation procedure loading conditions locations and the relationship
with other structural elements Therefore no specific recommendation is
presented at this time
It is anticipated that there will be difficulty penetrating the end
bearing tuff formation without damaging the pile tip This is due to a
number of features including the short lengths required of some of the
piles and the relatively low lateral resistance which can be mobilized in
the thick soft lagoonal deposit These features suggested on the
Idealized Subsurface Cross Section A A Plate 3 in combination with the
high compressive strength of the tuff and the steep slope of the tuff
formation suggest a situation where a driven pile may deflect when the tip
encounters the dense tuff layer This may prevent a satisfactory vertical
penetration into the tuff formation which is required to develop the
necessary end bearing to support the structural loads To assist the
proper penetration of the pile tip into the end bearing tuff formation we
recommend the use of predrilling into the tuff formation prior to pile
driving or the use of steel rock shoes fitted to the tip of the piles

A hardened steel point will further facilitate the driving of the pile
successfully into the very dense tuff When the pile is judged to he
nearing the tuff formation surface the hammer drop may be reduced so that
the pile point is seated on to the rock by a sufficient number of blows
with a small hammer drop As soon as there is an indication that a
seating on to the tuff formation has been obtained the hammer drop can be
increased and the pile then driven to refusal
Test Pile Driving and Load Test Program A test pile driving and
pile load test program is recommended to evaluate the in situ pile
settlement behavior under loading the actual pile driving behavior
to confirm the supporting capacity to develop the production pile
driving criteria to determine the need for predrilling through the
surface crust to minimize damage to the adjacent building and to
determine the length of production piles to be ordered
The test pile driving should consist of driving 10 to 15 percent of
the number of piles required These test piles can be incorporated
later in the actual building foundation The test piles should be
installed with the same hammer and procedure that are to be used for
the production pile installation
We recommend that one quarter of the total test piles be casted to
50 foot length one quarter to 75 foot length and the remaining half
to be 100 feet long We will be happy to assist in locating the
test pile locations after the production pile locations are
finalized
Following the installation of the test piles one of the piles
should be selected for the load testing We recommend that the test
load be carried to two 2 times the design load to examine the
load settlement relationship of the pile The pile test should be
performed in accordance with the ASTM test procedure D 1143 74

Shallow Foundations
Initially light colum loads generally less than 75 kips were
anticipated for the utility building Therefore spread footing
foundations were planned and the field exploration concentrated on the
shallow soil conditions which would be influenced by the footings
However due to the heavy second floor loads column loads up to 230 kips
are now anticipated The anticipated settlements for these column loads
are on the order of 2 to 4 inches In order to reduce the building
settlements pile foundations are recommended for the building
Additional deep borings are required to verify the presence of the tuff
layer for end bearing
Utility Tunnel
A cast in place concrete utility tunnel is planned between the new
building addition and the utility building The tunnel will be about 6
feet wide and extend to about 8 to 10 feet below the existing ground
surface
Based on Boring Nos 5 and 6 it appears that the bottom of the
tunnel will be into the loose lagoonal deposits Additionally the bottom
of the tunnel will be several feet below the groundwater level
The tunnel will not materially increase the loading on the loose
lagoonal deposits therefore the tunnel settlements should be small if
proper construction methods are followed A mat type foundation may be
used for the tunnel with an allowable bearing value of 1500 psf A
30 inch thick granular blanket is recommended below the tunnel to act as a
stabilization layer and working platform
Due to the soft ground condition lateral wall supports will be
required for the utility tunnel construction Also dewatering of the
excavation will be required prior to the tunnel construction

Site Grading
Because the site is underlain by soft compressible materials new
fills would induce ground settlements Therefore any additional fill
placement over the building sites should be avoided or minimized to less
than 1 foot It is our understanding that minimal fill is planned
Should any fill be contemplated subsequent to the issuance of this report
the soils engineer should first be consulted so that the potential ground
settlement can be re evaluated and the above recommended pile carrying
capacity can be modified due to the subsequent downdrag effect
Subgrade Preparation

After clearing and stripping the on site surface material should be
scarified to at least 6 inches deep and proof rolled with at least
4 passes using a roller weighing at least 10 tons Any soft spots
disclosed by the proof rolling should be removed and replaced with
compacted select backfill The proof rolling should be observed by
a soils engineer
Fill and Backfill
Large new fills over the proposed building sites are not
recommended If minor fill is required outside the building
location it should be done as soon as possible to induce early
settlement and minimize pile downdrag forces to the adjacent new
structures
Generally it is anticipated that the majority of the on site
material is suitable for fill except that all clayey soils should
not be reused If imported materials are required they should be
Classified as SW SP or SM soils according to the
Unified Soil Classification System with liquid limit not exceeding

Slab On Grade
A structural slab system is recommended for the five story
building However for the two story utility building the floor slabs
may be supported on grade Generally the floor slabs should be placed
over a capillary break layer consisting of at least 4 inches of crushed
rock smaller than 3 4 of an inch and retained on a No 4 sieve The local
quarry product No 3 Fine is recommended for this purpose The crushed
rock base should be placed over a compacted subgrade that has appropriate
slopes to drain off any accumulated moisture
Pavements
The pavement section should consist of 2 0 inches of asphaltic
concrete AC for driveway and parking areas over 6 inches of aggregate
base course over a subgrade that has been scarified and recompacted to a
depth of at least 6 inches All compaction should be to a minimm of 95
percent

Moisture content determinations were performed on the soil samples
to aid in the classification and evaluation of the soil properties Unit
weight determinations were performed on selected samples These test
results are presented on the boring logs at the appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limit tests were performed on selected samples to
Classify the soils and evaluate plasticity potential The test results
are presented on Plates B 1 through B 5 and summarized on the Plasticity
Chart Plate B 8
Ten sieve analysis were performed on selected samples to evaluate
grain size distributions The test results are summarized on Plates B 1
through B 5
One consolidation test was performed on the deep stiff old alluvium
deposit to evaluate its settlement characteristics The consolidation
curve is presented on Plate B 6
One unconsolidated undrained triaxial test was performed on a sample
of the deep stiff old alluvium to evaluate its undrained strength The
test result is summarized on Plate B 7
Torvane shear and unconfined compression tests were performed on
various samples of the subsoils to evaluate shear strengths The test
results are summarized on Plate B 9
