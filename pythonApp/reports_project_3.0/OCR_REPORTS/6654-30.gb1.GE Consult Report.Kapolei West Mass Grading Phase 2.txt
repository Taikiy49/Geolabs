SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site consist of a thin surficial 
layer of silt over medium dense (with occasional loose zones) calcareous sediments and 
medium hard to hard coralline limestone and coral formations.  Alluvial deposits consisting 
of brown clayey soil may be interbedded in the calcareous sediments and coralline 
limestone and coral formations. Groundwater is generally not anticipated to be a 
consideration for this project. 
We understand that cuts on the order of 6 feet deep or less are planned at the 
western half and fills on the order of about 5 feet high or less are planned at the eastern 
half of the site.  We believe that the excavated calcareous sediments and reef formation 
may be used as a source of general fill materials provided the materials are free of 
vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum 
dimension.  Large sized cobbles and boulders generated from the excavations should be 
processed into smaller particles that are no greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension. 
If it is desired to import general fill material, the material should be less than 
6 inches in maximum dimension and be free of vegetation and deleterious materials. The 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 5 or more and should have a maximum 
swell of less than 3 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Imported 
general fill material meeting the above requirements should be placed at least 2 feet below 
the proposed finished mass grades.   
Areas filled with the imported general fill materials should be capped with at least 
2 feet of structural fill material.  Structural fill material may consist of the excavated on-site 
calcareous sediments and reef formation materials provided that the material is well 
graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  Imported 
structural fill used for the capping layer should meet the above requirements. In addition, 
the imported structural fill should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent.  The text of this report should be referred to 
for detailed discussions and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was mainly built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western portion of the island.  
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the northern and eastern portions of the island.  
As the older Waianae volcanic activity ceased, younger lava flows from Koolau 
continued to bank against Waianae’s east facing slopes forming a broad upland, known 
as the Schofield Plateau. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main 
shield-building phase approximately 2 million years ago.   
Chemical weathering and physical erosion of the volcanic uplands generated 
copious terrestrial sediments, which were transported down slope toward the coastline. 
The terrestrial and embedded marine sediments accumulated to comprise the low 
topographic gradient coastal plain known as the Ewa Plain.  In the project vicinity 
(Ko Olina) and further towards the east and northeast (East Kapolei), the terrestrial 
sediments accumulated and became interbedded with other marine sediments and 
hardened coral-algal and coral limestone reef formations to form a thick sedimentary 
wedge of material fronting the mountain foothills.  The thickness of the collective 
sedimentary wedge ranges from nearly absent in the area underlain by the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at the shoreline near Ewa Beach.  This wedge of 
sedimentary and calcareous reef materials comprising the Ewa Plain serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu.  
Deposition of recent or Holocene age sediments has continued from earlier geologic 
time through the present.   
During the Pleistocene Epoch, the global sea level fluctuated in response to 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.

The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.  These changes in sea level, over periods of geologic 
time, was a major driving force in both the deposition and erosion of the terrestrial 
alluvial/colluvial deposits and coralline reef materials underlying the project site.  In 
addition, previous agricultural development conducted within the last 100 years or so 
have aided in the transformation of the relatively undeveloped project site to its present 
landform. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is at the southwest area of the Kapolei West Development in the 
Kapolei area in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The site is located 
north of the Oahu Railway and Land (OR&L) railroad tracks and south and east of 
Ko Olina Golf Course.  The location of the project site is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The site is undeveloped and appears to have been used previously for 
agricultural purposes.   In general, the site is vegetated with dryland grasses, Haole Koa 
brush and scattered Kiawe trees.   
The project site topography gently slopes from the northeast corner of the site to 
the southwest corner of the site. Ground surface elevations generally range between 
about +36 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the southwestern corner of the site to about 
+62 feet MSL at the northeastern corner of the site.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site.  Soft 
and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills.  The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site and/or used 
in landscape areas, where appropriate.  Contract documents should include 
additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement 
to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
Cavities and/or voids are encountered commonly in coralline limestone and coral 
formations. In areas where coralline limestone and coral formations are exposed at 
the finished subgrade in cut areas or areas to receive fill, special attention should 
be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for distress to the 
future improvements. 
The finished subgrade in cut areas or areas to receive fill that expose coralline 
limestone or coral formations should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton 
vibratory drum roller to detect the potential presence of near-surface cavities, 
sinkholes, or loose zones. The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed 
of about 300 feet per minute. A Geolabs representative should monitor the 
proof-rolling operations. If the proof-rolling operations detect a near-surface cavity

Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, subgrades should then be scarified,

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the majority of the excavated on-site soils will consist of calcareous 
sediments and reef formation.  Some large sized cobbles and boulders may be 
generated when excavating into the medium hard to hard reef formation.  
We believe that the excavated calcareous sediments and reef formation may be 
used as a source of general fill materials provided that the materials are properly 
processed and placed in accordance with the “Fill Placement and Compaction 
Requirements” subsection herein.  The excavated calcareous sediments and reef 
formation to be used as fills should be free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and 
rock fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension.  If large sized cobbles 
and boulders are generated during the excavation into the reef formation, the large 
sized cobbles and boulders should not be used as fill material unless these cobbles 
and boulders are processed into particles that are no greater than 6 inches in 
maximum dimension. 
Imported general fill material should consist of materials less than 6 inches in 
maximum dimension and be free of vegetation and deleterious materials. The 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 5 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of less than 3 percent when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. Imported general fill material should be placed at least 2 feet below 
the proposed finished mass grades.

Where imported general fill materials are used within the fill areas, these areas 
should be capped with at least 2 feet of structural fill material.  The excavated on-
site calcareous sediments and reef formation materials may be used as a source of 
structural fill provided that the material is well graded from coarse to fine with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  It should be noted that 
natural stratification (horizontal layering) and lateral transitions within the reef 
formation materials may produce coralline aggregate materials that are high in silt 
content with limited granular material components.  The calcareous sediments and 
reef formation should be properly blended to provide a well-graded granular fill. 
Imported structural fill used for the capping layer should consist of crushed coralline 
or basaltic materials. The imported materials should be well graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. Structural fill 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum 
swell of less than 1 percent.  Imported fill materials should be tested for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use.  
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Structural fill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.1.4 Excavation 
The project site should consist of medium hard to hard coralline limestone and coral 
formations and medium dense calcareous sedimentary reef deposits, collectively 
referred to as reef formation deposits. Based on our experience, the reef formation 
in the project area would typically be slightly to moderately weathered and range 
from soft to hard in nature. It is anticipated that the majority of the reef formation 
may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as a large backhoe 
excavator. However, reef formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized 
zones. Therefore, we anticipate that some difficult excavation conditions may arise 
in localized areas during construction. These hard zones will require hoe-ram or 
chipping.  The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would 
disrupt the structure of the reef formation, resulting in a potential loss of bearing 
strength for improvements in the vicinity. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
experience of the existing coralline reef formation in the area. We recommend 
encouraging the contractor to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data 
to make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation.

and coral formations.  In general, we believe that cut slopes may be made at an 
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter.   
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  If the 
existing ground surface is steeper that 5H:1V, fills should be keyed and benched 
into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling 
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal 
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be 
allowed.  In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching 
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills.  Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and 
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
erosion of the finished slopes.
