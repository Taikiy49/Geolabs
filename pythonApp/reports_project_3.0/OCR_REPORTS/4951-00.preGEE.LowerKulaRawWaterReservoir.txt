2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is the second largest of the Hawaiian Islands and
encompasses approximately 728 square miles of land area Two shield volcano
masses East Maui Volcano Haleakala of the eastern side of Maui and the West Maui
Mountains comprise the principal volcanic mountains that formed the Island of Maui A
broad low elevation plain Maui Isthmus between the two mountain masses was
formed by the coalescence of the lava flows emitted from Haleakala Volcano and
West Maui Mountain Volcano
Shield building volcanic activity commenced in the Pleistocene Epoch
about 1 6 to 2 million years ago and generally ended by about 900 000 years ago
Most of the post shield building volcanic activity generally ended about 400 000 years
ago During the evolution of the Island of Maui in Pleistocene time the island
experienced large scale subsidence due to the weight of the growing island mass in
addition to episodes of partial submergence and emergence related to the last
significant Ice Age sea level fluctuations During the Pleistocene time of significantly
lower worldwide sea levels the Island of Maui was once connected as a single large
volcanic landmass encompassed by the Islands of Molokai Lanai and Kahoolawe
The West Maui Mountains generally consist of inclined layers of basaltic lava
flows that consist of the Wailuku Honolua and Lahaina Volcanic Series progressing
from oldest to youngest in age A relatively long period of erosion and sediment
deposition occurred following the Honolua Volcanic Series during which time thick
sequences of sedimentary conglomerate were deposited along the base of the
mountains The lavas of the younger Lahaina Volcanic Series were erupted on top of
and are interstratified with the sedimentary deposits The volcanic center of the
West Maui Mountains is believed to be extinct
Haleakala Volcano composes the eastern half of the Island of Maui and generally
consists of layered basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Kula and Hana Volcanic

2 2 Local Site Geology
The project site is located on the northerly facing slope of Haleakala at about the
2 900 foot elevation within the Koolau Forest Reserve in the District of Makawao on the
Island of Maui The project site is situated between Kailua Stream located toward the

volcanic rock materials which compose the northerly facing slopes of
Haleakala Volcano The typical slope of the ground surfaces at the project site
averages about 5 to 10 degrees in declination down toward the northern direction
Based on a review of available geologic information pertaining to the project site
and vicinity the project site is underlain by a thick sequence of lava flows belonging to
the post shield building Kula Volcanic Series It is believed that the Kula Volcanic

large cinder cone vents located along the primary rift zones of the volcano The north
rift zone of Haleakala Volcano is located in close proximity to the project site as
evidenced by the multitude of nearby surface cinder and spatter lava cone features

and ankaramite The individual lava flows in the vicinity of the project site are estimated

2 4 Climatic Conditions
The project site is located at about the 2 900 foot elevation of Haleakala Volcano
summit elevation of 10 023 feet MSL on the north facing slopes in the vicinity of the
northern rift zone of Haleakala Volcano The north rift zone comprises a broad region of
directional shift in the orientation of the slopes from predominantly northwesterly facing
slopes located toward the west to predominantly northeasterly facing slopes located

this broad region of shifting slope directional orientation

distances due to the effects of the mountain topography with respect to the orientation

rainfall on the order of about 200 to 275 inches per year and locations located westerly
of the project site receive substantially lower rainfall on the order of about less than
40 inches per year In addition the distribution of precipitation on the mountain slopes
may typically be greater between elevations of about 1 000 feet MSL and 3 000 feet

2 5 Geophysical Field Survey
Surface geophysical seismic refraction surveys were performed at six selected
locations within the proposed reservoir project site to evaluate the general subsurface
conditions The Seismic Refraction SR surveys are identified as SR 1 through SR 6
and the approximate location and orientation of the field surveys are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 3
The seismic refraction exploration method is a non intrusive geophysical means
of evaluating the relative density thickness and depth of subsurface earth materials
such as soil and rock layers Seismic refraction is used to record compression wave
primary or P wave response as the wave passes through or is refracted at different
velocities in various types of soil and rock media The seismic refraction method is a
useful subsurface exploration tool where geologic layers possess increasing material
density with increasing depth a condition that normally holds true for about 80 percent
of surface naturally occurring geologic deposits However the seismic refraction
survey method cannot be used to profile the subsurface conditions where the P wave
velocity of the earth material decreases with depth P wave velocity inversion In

addition the wave velocities of mixed thin bedded earth layers having variable wave
velocities such as loose basalt clinker and massive hard rock lava flow sequences are
generally averaged to obtain approximate layer velocities Therefore the seismic
refraction exploration method should be used to supplement other sources of
subsurface information such as drilled exploratory test borings
The seismic refraction methods also require experienced interpretive judgment
by the analyst to identify various trends and inconsistencies that may be present in the
recorded field data As a result the profile and wave velocity information obtained from

2 6 Subsurface Conditions

bulk soil samples of the various surface clayey silt soils were collected from the project

Based on the results of our site reconnaissance and the seismic refraction
surveys we believe that the project site may be underlain by a surficial cover of clayey
silt soil and highly weathered basalt rock which may extend to depths generally of
about 3 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface depending on the location Some
surface soil horizons may extend to depths of about 10 feet below the ground surface at

3 2 Fill Embankment Construction
We understand that reservoir embankments ranging up to about 75 feet in height
are considered for this project Considering that the project site is located in a wet
climatic environment and that the on site clayey soils encountered in the excavation
could be used as embankment fill materials we anticipate that compaction of the fills
containing clayey soil materials likely will be problematic due to the inherent high soil
moisture contents and limited material drying capability Therefore we recommend that
the size of the embankments should be minimized and not fattened considerably to
accommodate balancing the earthwork quantities for the project
Due to the wet climatic environment and the high moisture clayey soils we
recommend that the excavated surface soils be wasted and a suitable disposal site be
provided for disposal of the excess materials generated during the earthwork
operations Furthermore the surface soils at the project site contain an appreciable
amount of organic matter Therefore the organic rich surface soils are not suitable for
use as embankment fills and should be wasted In addition we recommend that
processed rock materials generated from the reservoir rock excavation be used as the
primary materials to construct the fill embankments

We anticipate that the excavation of basalt rock material at the project site may
encounter some possible discontinuities in the subsurface such as buried volcanic ash

may require implementation of some additional engineering measures to improve the

discontinuities within the basalt rock formation underlying the project site
3 5 Groundwater Conditions
Regional groundwater studies conducted by others have documented the
occurrence of groundwater impounded within the basalt rock comprising the
Kula Volcanic Series The groundwater is referred to as high level groundwater that
occurs above the regional deep aquifer within the generally low permeability basalt rock
of the Kula Volcanic Series As mentioned previously the basalt rock underlying the
project site belongs to the lava flows associated with the Kula Volcanic Series The
depth of the potential groundwater surface has not been identified at the project site
therefore we recommend that additional subsurface exploration such as test borings be
performed to evaluate the potential for the presence of shallow groundwater at the
project site
