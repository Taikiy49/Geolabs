Dear Mr. Waters: 
As requested, we submit this letter report presenting the findings of our consultation 
services with respect to the use of rock materials from the Our Lady of Keeau site in the 
Keeau area of Waianae as armor stone for improvements at Iroquois Point. 
Project Considerations 
Armor stones are required for a beach restoration project at Iroquois Point.  A 
potential source of armor stone material has been located at the Our Lady of Keeau site 
in the Keeau area of Waianae on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
It was desired to conduct a preliminary evaluation of the material to determine 
whether it might be suitable for the intended use. 
Purpose and Scope 
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services is to provide consultation on 
the potential use of the rock material as armor stones.  Our scope of work generally 
consisted of the following tasks and work efforts: 
1. 
Visual and manual examination of stockpiled rock material by our project 
engineer/geologist. 
2. 
Collection of hand samples from randomly selected stones at the site for 
laboratory testing consisting of specific gravity and absorption.

Field Exploration 
On October 26, 2012, a site visit was made by our project engineer/geologist to 
make observations of rock materials at the Our Lady of Keeau site. 
Field testing was conducted on a stockpile of stones ranging from about 3 to 
6 feet in diameter that had been collected by on-site personnel.  The field testing 
consisted of striking selected stones with a 3-pound sledge hammer, and drop testing of 
selected stones at the site.  Hand samples were collected for laboratory testing. 
Findings and Conclusions 
The stone material is generally sub-angular to sub-rounded in shape.  The material 
appears to be sub-aerially weathered colluvial material that had accumulated at the base 
of the steep slopes in the rear of the property. 
Subjective testing was conducted by striking selected stones with a 3-pound 
sledge hammer.  This test indicated that most of the stones tested had little rind of 
weathered material on their exteriors.  Most of the stones produced a pinging sound 
indicating sound rock on their interiors. 
Drop testing was conducted on selected stones in general accordance with the 
specifications by placing one large stone on the ground surface as an anvil stone and 
dropping another stone on to the anvil from a height of roughly 10 feet.  None of the 
five stones tested cracked when dropped, although, there was minor chipping and 
spalling.

Our laboratory testing indicates that the material generally meets the project
specifications for armor stone. However, there is a possibility of variability in the material
as different areas are exploited for material. Therefore, we recommend implementing a
program of periodic testing as a quality control measure to assure that the material
continues to meet specifications.
