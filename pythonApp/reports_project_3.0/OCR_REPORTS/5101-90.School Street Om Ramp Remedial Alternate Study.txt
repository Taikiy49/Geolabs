Dear Mr Chu
Prior to the pursuant of a detailed engineering analyses and design we have
conducted a geotechnical study of various remedial alternatives to address the retaining
wall distress observed along the School Street on ramp to the Interstate Route H 1
Highway This letter report summarizes the findings based on our evaluation and analysis
of several remedial alternatives that may be considered for the project The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
letter report
INTRODUCTION
The existing retaining wall project site is located along the Ewa bound lanes of the
Interstate Route H 1 Highway at the School Street on ramp in Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1 Based on the information provided we understand that the existing
retaining wall along the School Street on ramp to the highway has exhibited various signs
of distresses such as cracking and appreciable tilting especially after the heavy rains in
early 2006 The cracking and appreciable tilting of the concrete retaining wall has
apparently resulted in some of the distresses reported by the City and County of Honolulu
for School Street which is located at the top of the back slope above the distressed
retaining wall Therefore it is desired to conduct a geotechnical engineering exploration in
an attempt to evaluate the probable cause s for the distresses observed and to formulate
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the remediation of the existing retaining
wall distresses at the project site The approximate limits of the project site are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
Based on our review of the as built construction plans entitled Lunalilo Freeway
Houghtailing Street to Nuuanu Stream Federal Aid Project No U District of
Honolulu Island of Oahu dated we believe that the existing distressed
retaining wall designated as Retaining Wall 8 on the drawings was constructed sometime
between 1963 and 1968 The retaining wall measuring approximately 675 feet in total
length has a retaining height varying from about 1 5 to 3 feet at the ends and up toa
maximum of about 11 feet at the highest point According to the as built construction
plans the retaining wall is supported on shallow footing foundations with a 12 inch by
12 inch footing key along the majority of the wall length The footing width varied from a
minimum of 3 feet at both ends to a maximum of 8 25 feet at the highest retaining height
Based on our field exploration program as described below we believe that the retaining
wall footings likely bear on the in situ hard basalt rock formation The as built plan and
profile views of the retaining wall have been reproduced and are shown on Plate 3 for
reference
Based on the as built construction plans mentioned above the existing retaining
wall is designed with a back slope of one and one half horizontal to one vertical
rising up to the School Street level Our field observations suggested that the existing
back slope gradient may be on the order of about 1H 1V However the topographic
survey map prepared for the project indicated that the existing back slope may have a
slope ratio of closer to 2H 1V Large Monkeypod trees with a spread of up to 80 feet were
observed on the back slope above the distressed areas of the retaining wall
Based on our field observations the majority of the distresses occurred towards the
Ewa portion of the retaining wall where the retaining height is on the order of about 6 to
7 feet The observed distresses included but are not limited to the following
e Cracking on the retaining wall face
e Spalling of concrete and exposure of steel reinforcement
Tilting leaning of wall face towards the highway
e Separation between adjacent wall sections at the top
e Cracking and upheaval of existing curb gutter and pavement along
School Street at the top of the back slope

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We conducted a field exploration program in July 2007 to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions along the distressed retaining wall Our field exploration program
consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8
at selected locations along the project limits extending to depths ranging from about
47 to 65 5 feet below the existing ground pavement surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our borings encountered a surface layer approximately 3 to 11 feet
thick consisting of stiff brownish to light gray adobe clays Below the adobe clay layer
hard basalt rock formation interbedded with clinkers alluvial soils and volcanic tuff were
generally encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately
65 5 feet below the existing ground pavement surface
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater level at the project site may be
influenced by seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors We
understand that the existing retaining wall has shown signs of substantial water flow during
heavy rains in the past
For illustration purposes only two generalized subsurface profiles depicting the
interpreted subsurface conditions along selected stations of the retaining wall are provided
on the Generalized Subsurface Profile Plate 4 The approximate surface locations
projections of the subsurface profiles prepared for this report are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented
on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8 of Appendix A Results of the laboratory
tests performed on selected soil samples and rock cores are presented in Appendix B

PROBABLE CAUSE S OF OBSERVED DISTRESSES
Based on our review of the available construction documents and the results of our
field exploration and laboratory testing programs we believe that the following conditions
were present or evident at the project site
e Presence of adobe clays in the shallow subsurface as encountered in our
field exploration
e Presence of subterranean seepage behind the retaining wall as evident after
heavy rainfall

e Presence of large Monkeypod trees on the back slope above the distressed

We believe that the existence of one or more of these conditions may help to
explain most of the distresses observed at the site Detailed discussions and evaluation of
these findings and observations are presented below
Presence of Adobe Clays
Based on our experience in the area and the field exploration results the project
site is generally underlain by adobe clays in the shallow subsurface The near surface
adobe clays encountered within the project limits generally consists of brownish to light
gray clay with sand and gravel These clayey soils which are typically very sticky and
plastic are classified as CH according to the Unified Soil Classification System These
soils generally exhibit high shrink swell potential low permeability and will lose
considerable shear strength when saturated The soils are known to be susceptible to
sliding or creeping on slopes generally greater than about 4H 1V
Laboratory test results and past experience with adobe clays indicates that the
material generally exhibit very low friction angle on the order of about 15 to 20 degrees
Based on the steep back slope above the retaining wall and the low friction angle of the
adobe clays we anticipate the lateral earth pressure acting on the retaining wall could be
on the order of about 80 to 100 pounds per square foot per foot of depth Therefore we
believe that there is a possibility that the existing retaining wall constructed in the 1960 s
may not have been adequately designed to take into account the high lateral earth
pressure exerted by the sloping adobe clay soils
It should be noted that adobe clays are present in the back slope within the entire
project limits therefore one would expect that the retaining wall would exhibit similar signs
of distresses along the entire wall length especially in the area with the higher wall height
However our field observations indicated that the majority of the distresses are
concentrated toward the Ewa end of the retaining wall where the retaining height is
relatively low Therefore we believe that the presence of the sloping adobe clays may
be a significant but not the sole contributing factor to the observed distresses at the
retaining wall
Presence of Trapped Subterranean Seepage
The project site is situated at the lower Liliha area which is gently sloping uphill
towards the mauka direction until it reaches the base of the mountain ridge The retaining
wall provides the necessary grade adjustment between the upper School Street and the
lower Interstate Route H 1 Highway which was constructed by excavating into the original
ground Therefore the project site is susceptible to high water infiltration and

accumulation from the upslope runoff and likely subsurface seepage during heavy
rainfalls It was reported that a significant amount of runoff or seepage could be observed
flowing over and through the cracked walls during periods of heavy rains suggesting that
the existing drainage system weepholes may not be functioning properly to relieve the
subterranean water trapped behind the retaining wall This was supported by the fact that
relatively high moisture contents on the order of about 40 to 50 percent were observed in
the soil samples retrieved during our field exploration The trapped subterranean seepage
resulted in additional hydrostatic pressure that may not have been fully accounted for
during the design of the existing retaining wall resulting in the distresses observed at the
site However we believe that the trapped subterranean seepage is generally a transient
condition and would be a significant but not the primary contributing factor to the observed
distresses at the retaining wall
Presence of Large Monkeypod Trees
Based on our field observations several large Monkeypod trees are present on the
back slope above the retaining wall where the majority of the distresses were observed In
addition to the wall distresses immediately below these Monkeypod trees significant
cracking and upheaval of the curb gutter and pavement could be observed along School
Street directly above these large trees Our field exploration indicated that the back slope
is generally underlain by approximately 6 feet of adobe clays overlying in situ hard basalt
rock formation
Based on our evaluation we speculate that the root system of these large
Monkeypod trees have extended through the upper adobe clays and encountered
resistance when it reached the underlying in situ hard basalt rock formation which prevent
the root system from growing further downward into the rock formation As a result the
root system seeks the least resistance by growing sideway toward the retaining wall Over
the years the lateral growth of the root system may have caused the gradual tilting of the
retaining wall Since the retaining wall footings are likely bearing on the in situ hard basalt
rock formation with a footing key we believe that the wall footings will not yield or move
under any conditions Therefore we speculate that the wall tilting resulting from the
lateral growth of the root system has reached a point that the wall stem started to shear
off from the base footing Once this happened the tilting became more pronounced ina
short period of time as evident at the site Therefore we believe that the presence of the
large Monkeypod trees on the back slope could be a primary contributing factor to the
observed distresses at the retaining wall

Based on the discussions presented above we believe that the remedial alternative
proposed for the project should achieve the following objectives

1 It should take into account or eliminate the high lateral earth pressures

Based on our preliminary evaluation and analysis of the existing retaining wall
distress conditions we have reviewed various options for remedying the distressed
retaining wall along the School Street on ramp to the Interstate Route H 1 Highway One
of the remedial alternatives considered for the project includes jet grouting which is
deemed to be not viable due to the difficult access conditions and high costs Conceptual
drawings depicting the three mitigation options that are considered viable for the project
are presented on Plates 5 through 7 In addition a summary of the advantages and
disadvantages of the various options are discussed below The three options reviewed
and considered in our study include the following

Option 1 Drilled Shaft Retaining Wall
This option consists of the construction of a new drilled shaft retaining wall at the
top of the back slope alongside School Street The closely spaced drilled shafts will
extend up to about the roadway level of School Street to provide a level backfill condition
This will eliminate the sloping adobe clays with high lateral earth pressure and provide
the necessary lateral resistance to support the existing School Street from shifting towards
the highway The drilled shaft would extend into the underlying in situ hard basalt rock
formation and derive vertical and lateral support from the stable rock formation below the
upper adobe clays The portion of the drilled shaft above the bedrock surface would not
be counted on for support because the soil materials actually impose a driving force on the
drilled shafts The drilled shafts will need to be heavily reinforced for the lateral forces and
moments induced in the shafts
Conceptual drawings consisting of plan and typical section renderings of the drilled
shaft retaining wall option are presented on Plates 5 1 and 5 2 The following is a
summary of some advantages and disadvantages for the drilled shaft retaining wall option

Option 2 Tieback Anchor
The tieback anchor option consists of the installation of post tensioned grouted
anchors oriented roughly perpendicular to School Street to stabilize the existing retaining
wall from further tilting and movement The existing concrete retaining wall may be used
as anchor blocks for the installation of the new tieback anchors A new wall facing will be
constructed in front of the existing retaining wall after the anchor installation It should be
noted that this option does not eliminate the sloping adobe clays in the back slope and
the potential shifting of School Street and underground utilities toward the highway could
still exist

behind the new retaining wall Extensive shoring will be required along the upslope area

a technical perspective we believe that Option 1 will provide the best solution to the
existing retaining wall distresses and address the potential shifting of School Street and
