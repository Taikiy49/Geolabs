Dispersive Clay Testing
As requested by 0 1 C C Manila additional laboratory testing was
performed on selected samples to determine whether the in situ soils
can be classified as dispersive clays Dispersive clays are clay
soils which disperse or deflocculate in the presence of water and
therefore are highly susceptible to erosion and piping
There are various types of soil tests which have been used to
attempt to identify dispersive clays However in the present
State of the art all of the tests have some limitations and variable
degree of success have been reported The generally recommended
procedure is to perform a minimum of two 2 different types of tests
on each sample in which the results can be compared

The following three types of tests were performed in our
laboratory
1 Double Hydrometers This test consists of first performing a
standard hydrometer tests using a dispersing agent A second
test is performed without mechanical agitation and without a
dispersing agent The percent of dispersion is expressed as
the amount of particles smaller than determined in
the second test divided by the amount of particles smaller
than determined with the standard hydrometer test
Pinhole Erosion Tests This test is performed by causing
water to flow through a small 1 16 inch diameter hole

of the sample clear cloudy dark is used to determine if
the soil is dispersive intermediate or nhon dispersive

SITE CONDITIONS
Site Description
The hangar and apron project site is located in the northeast
corner of Naval Air Station Cubi Point Bataan Republic of the
Philippines The proposed layout of the improvements is shown on an
aerial photo of the area on Plate 5 The principal study area is
roughly square in plan view with dimensions of 1000 feet per side and
is bounded by the aircraft operations area southeast Taxiway to the
north Argonaut Highway to the east Tarlac Road to the south and
unimproved land natural hillside to the west
The terrain slopes down moderately towards the north at an
average slope of approximately 4 horizontal to 1 vertical There is a
broad drainageway in the western portion of the site which generally
traverses in a north south direction The site is crossed by Argonaut
Highway and an unlined interceptor ditch which traverse the site
roughly in an east west direction The northern two thirds of the
site below the interceptor ditch has been previously used as a
borrow site and is sparsely vegetated with cogon grasses The
southern one third of the site is densely vegetated with native and
exotic tropical and sub tropical vegetation including members of the
lauan family guava vines and bamboo This forested area provides a
habitat for a variety of native and feral animals including monkeys
pigs chickens monitor lizards and snakes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 11
The ground elevations at the site generally vary from about 50 to
200 feet above mean sea level
Geology
From available geologic literature the site is located within
the Western Volcanic Intrusive Province and comprises Pliocene
Quaternary volcanic deposits The E W trending San Antonio fracture
zone is the closest identified major fault zone about 10 miles
northeast of the site
The site investigation carried out revealed that the surface
materials in the study area are mainly of pyroclastic origin and
subsequent chemical weathering has resulted in a mantle of chemically
decomposed rock saprolite being formed overlying the relatively
less weathered tuffaceous rock

Grading Control
OS eS sete am os
Since residual soils saprolites and weathered rock materials
such as those encountered on the project site rely upon their
in situ structure for a great deal of their strength certain
procedures and controls should be utilized to help preserve the
relict structure of the materials
Firstly the earthwork should be scheduled to be performed as
much as possible during the dry season The infiltration of
surface water into the on site geological materials may cause
disruption of the relict structure and result in a loss of
strength
Secondly close attention should be paid to obtain the finish
Slope grades Over ripping or over excavation should be
avoided Over cutting will also destroy the relict structure and
result in the loss of soil strength It is recommended that
smaller equipment be utilized to carefully provide the final
finished grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1230 00 Page 31

geological materials the materials may be subject to pumping

his haul routes frequently as finished grade is approached

APPENDIX A 2
Most of the borings were advanced by the use of rotary wash
drilling and coring techniques However 4 5 inch diameter cont inuous
flight augers were used in some of the borings drilled by the Mobile
B 34 drill rig
A variety of drilling fluids were used throughout the course of the
project The most common was fresh water which was recirculated The
water was supplied by U S Naval Public Works Center and stored in 400
to 500 gallon tanks water buffaloes which were placed in various
locations of the site The water was then piped or even hand carried to
the boring locations as necessary
Stiff gel foam was used as a drilling fluid in one of the borings
The foam consisted of a foaming agent and a stabilizer mixed with fresh
water This mixture was injected into the drill string through a
venturi system Compressed air was supplied by a 600 cfm compressor
The use of stiff gel foam as a drilling fluid seemed to be successful
i e good core recovery with minimal core erosion in uniform residual
Soil or saprolite However much of the subsoils contained gravel or
cobbles which caused significant reduction in core recovery The foam
drilling with a triple tube core barrel was unsuccessful in obtaining
quality samples in these soils This plus other mechanical difficulties
with the compressor resulted in the suspension of the use of foam

APPENDIX A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by the use of drive
samplers and triple tube core barrels The drive samples were taken by
driving a standard penetration sampler or a 3 0 inch 0 D split barrel
Sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the log of the borings
at the appropriate sample depths Hydraulically pushed thin wall tube
samples were attempted in the saprolite but could not be pushed into the
stiff soil
Two varieties of triple tube core barrels were used to obtain
undisturbed soil samples The first variety the Mazier triple tube
core barrel met with moderate success in the residual soils However
the design of this barrel precluded the drilling through the hard ejecta

therefore the use of this barrel was severely limited

core barrel met with much greater success and was able to cut through

samples

barrels and an NWD4 barrel

APPENDIX B
SEISMIC REFRACTION AND RESISTIVITY SURVEYS
In conjunction with the sampling operations geophysical
exploration techniques were used to obtain further subsurface data
A series of five 5 shallow seismic refraction surveys were
performed in selected areas of the project site These surveys were
performed using a BISON Model 1570 C Refraction Seismograph receiving
seismic signals generated by the impacts of a 10 pound sledge hammer
The signal enhancement feature of this seismograph provided a
relatively noise free signal as the result of repeated blows of the
Sledge hammer The results of the surveys are presented on Plates B 1
through B 5
Additionally a series of three 3 earth resistivity surveys
were made on the site to evaluate the corrosion potential of the
on site soils These surveys were performed using a SOILTEST R 40
Strata Scout Resistivity meter with a Wenner Four Pin electrode
array This method is in general accordance with the recommended
procedure presented in Corrosion Prevention and Control t NAVDOCKS
1964 The results of the resistivity surveys are
presented on Plates B 6 through B 8 The locations of the seismic and
resistivity surveys are presented on Plate B 9

APPENDIX D
LABORATORY TESTING
ae ee
Most of the moisture content unit weight and classification
tests were performed by a local testing laboratory in the Philippines
The strength tests direct shear and triaxial shear tests and
consolidation tests were performed in our laboratory in Honolulu
The moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit determina
tions were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classifica
tion and evaluation of soil properties The results of the moisture
content and unit weight tests are presented on the log of borings at
the appropriate sample depths The Atterberg Limits are summarized on
Plates D 1 through D 7
Five laboratory CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of the
hear surface soils to evaluate the suitability of the saprolite soil
for pavement support The test results are summarized on Plates D 1
through D 7
Five moisture density tests modified Proctor were performed on
bulk samples of the soils to determine their compaction characteris
tics The test results are summarized on Plates D 1 through D 7
Six specific gravity tests were performed on typical samples of
the underlying soil The test results are presented on Plates D 1
through D 7
Ten sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of the under
lying highly weathered tuff to evaluate its grain size distribution
The test results are summarized on Plates D 1 through D 7
PH tests were performed on selected in situ samples to evaluate
its corrosion potential for utility pipe design The test results are
summarized on Plates D 1 through D 7
Seven unconfined compression tests were performed on core samples
of the deeper weathered tuff to evaluate its undrained strength The
test results are presented on Plates D 1 through D 7
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on natural and
remolded samples of the subsoils to evaluate the swelling potential of
the soils The test results are summarized on Plate D 8

Six consolidation tests were performed on the natural soils to
evaluate its settlement characteristics The curves are presented on
Plates D 9 through D 14
Seven direct shear tests and 20 triaxial shear including
unconsolidated undrained and consolidated undrained tests with pore
pressure measurements were performed on saturated samples of the
various soil types to evaluate the shear strength parameters of the
soils The test results are presented in Plates D 15 through D 41

Slope stability analyses for this investigation were performed
utilizing Slope II computer program which utilizes the limit
equilibrium theory to analyze the stability of an earth slope This
program is a resident library file in Control Data Corporation s
CYBERNET Services with data imput and output handled by an in house
IBM PC micro computer
This comprehensive and flexible program analyzes slope stability
by the method of slices and factors of safety can be obtained for
circular and non circular failure surfaces considering earthquakes and
live loads Cross sections for analyses may consist of a maximum of
15 soil layers and 1000 slices Various pore pressure conditions may
be incorporated in each analysis with any of the following six
analytical methods
1 The Fellenius or Ordinary method of Slices
2 the Simplified Bishop method
3 the Spencer method
4 the Janbu s Simplified method
5 the Janbu s Generalized method
6 the Morgenstern Price method and
7 the Nonvellier method Lowe Karafiath method Corps of
Engineers method and modified Swedish method can be solved as
special cases of the Morgenstern Price method
For our analyses Janbu s Simplified method was utilized with
occasional cross check runs utilizing the Ordinary Method of slices
and the simplified Bishop method
