INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Ultra Violet UV Disinfection Facility located within the
existing Kailua Regional Wastewater Treatment Plant in the Kailua area on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site grading
shoring and dewatering and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Kailua Regional Wastewater
Treatment Plant WWTP in the Kailua area on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the
information provided it is proposed to expand the existing Kailua WWTP with
construction of the UV Disinfection Facility The approximate location of the new facility is
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The proposed UV Disinfection Facility will be a U shaped concrete channel
measuring approximately 45 feet long and 8 feet wide in plan dimensions The top of the
concrete channel will be set at an elevation of about 7 82 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The channel will be deepened at both ends with a slab elevation of about 5 86 feet MSL
The remaining channel area will be higher with a slab elevation of about 0 43 feet MSL
The new UV module weighing approximately 14 000 pounds will be located at the center
of the concrete channel In addition we understand that new 54 inch diameter reinforced
concrete pipes and a junction box will be installed to connect the new UV Disinfection
Facility to the existing Solids Contactor and 54 inch reinforced concrete pipes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3918 00 Page 5
The project site is located at the base of the northeastern slope of Puu Papaa
which is essentially an erosional remnant of caldera filling lava flows of the Koolau
Volcano After the Koolau Volcano ceased its activity it was followed by a period of
erosion and subsequent submergence during which time most of the eastern flank of the
volcano was removed Later glacio eustatic sea level changes during the Pleistocene
Epoch resulted in the accumulation of several hundred feet of marine and non marine
sediments
Previous field explorations at the wastewater treatment plant indicate that the
southwestern upper portion of the site is underlain by highly weathered rock and
saprolite soils derived from the Koolau Volcano flows while the central and northeastern
lower portions of the site are underlain by interbedded sediments of both detrital marine
and alluvial origins During the last century development of the treatment plant has
shaped the land to the present form by grading and fill placement

Based on our field exploration the proposed UV Disinfection Facility site is

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site appears to be underlain primarily by
fill material extending to depths of about 15 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface
Soft silty clays and loose clayey sands were generally encountered near the design invert
elevations of the UV module concrete channel the new 54 inch concrete pipes and the
junction box Our previous exploration in the vicinity of the project site suggest that highly
compressible soils exist and extend to depths of over 60 feet below the existing ground
surface
Based on the highly compressible soil conditions anticipated at the invert elevation
and the stringent settlement tolerance allowed by the new UV module equipment we
believe that driven pile foundations would be required for support of the channel slab for
the new UV module equipment The new building enclosure over the UV module
equipment may also be supported on driven pile foundations For preliminary foundation
design and cost estimation purposes we recommend that 12 inch square precast
prestressed concrete piles driven to a depth of about 100 to 110 feet below the existing
ground surface be used for the proposed facility An estimated compressive load
capacity of up to 70 tons per pile may be developed by end bearing on the basalt rock
formation underlying the site at greater depths
Due to the presence of soft loose soils near the invert elevations of the new
54 inch concrete pipes and junction box we recommend that the invert elevations be
over excavated a minimum of 3 feet and be replaced with 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel

Pile Foundations
Based on the compressible soil deposits encountered near the invert elevation of
the concrete channel and the stringent settlement tolerance required by the new
UV module we believe that driven pile foundations would be required for support of the
proposed new UV Disinfection Facility planned at the subject site Based on our field
exploration and engineering analyses we recommend that 12 inch square precast
prestressed concrete piles be used to support the proposed concrete channel and the
building enclosure The piles would derive support primarily from end bearing on the
basalt rock formation underlying the site at greater depths as encountered in our
previous borings drilled in the vicinity of the project site An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 70 tons per pile may be used for the recommended pile foundation
design The allowable compressive load capacity for the piles is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces In
general piles should be spaced a minimum of 3 feet center to center to avoid reduction in
vertical capacity due to group action
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3918 00 Page 9
For preliminary design and cost estimation purposes we believe that the piles
should be driven to a depth of about 100 to 110 feet below the existing ground surface to
develop the recommended pile capacity The estimated pile lengths are based on the
subsurface conditions encountered in our previous boring drilled in the vicinity of the
project site see Appendix C and the actual pile embedment depths for the adjacent
Clarifier No 3 It is anticipated that pile splicing using simple non mechanical type splice
cans will be needed for driving the long piles Therefore we recommend that the upper
pile section be a minimum of 50 feet long since the channel excavation and pile cut off
will remove the upper 10 to 20 feet of soil and pile assuming that the piles will be driven
from the surface
Predrilling
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the piles may be installed without the need for predrilling However we
understand that the potential impacts on the operations of the existing structures
due to vibrations generated from the pile driving operations would be of great
concern Therefore predrilling at the proposed pile locations should be performed
prior to pile installation to reduce the potential impacts on the operations of the
adjacent structures during the pile driving operations
Our field exploration indicated that soft and or loose soil deposits were
encountered below the surficial fill crust at a depth of about 10 feet below the
existing ground surface Therefore we recommend that the proposed pile
locations be predrilled a minimum depth of 10 feet below the existing ground
surface to reduce the potential for vibration impacts on the existing structures
The predrilled holes should have a diameter of about 12 inches for the 12 inch
square piles The required predrilling size and depth should be verified and or
modified by Geolabs Hawaii during the pile driving program depending on the
conditions encountered and pile driving equipment used

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the driven pile
and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils Based on our estimate
the remaining 30 foot pile length of the upper pile sections of the spliced pile may
provide an uplift capacity of up to about 12 kips per pile to help resist the buoyant
uplift force on the concrete channel The uplift load capacity already includes the
dead weight of the pile
Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the
pile supported foundation to be less than 0 5 inches with differential settlements
between piles not exceeding about one half that amount We believe that these
settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of about 30 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level contro such that driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled

Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design purposes pile driving may be terminated when aggregate
blow counts are at least 60 blows per foot for the last foot or 5 blows per inch for
the last 6 inches This preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed after the
selection of the pile driving equipment by performance of a wave equation
analysis
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be
rejected or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is
interrupted for more than four hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required
depth During soft driving such as when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the
hammer stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant
tensile stresses do not develop within the pile

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important
design consideration In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the
pipes and junction box located over stiff dense soil conditions However our field
exploration indicated that soft loose soil conditions were encountered near the
design invert elevations of the concrete pipes and junction box
Therefore we recommend that an additional of 30 inches of No 2 Rock
ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent
be provided below the new piping and junction box for more uniform support The
granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth the outside pipe diameter A typical section of the trench details is
presented on Plate 3
Backfill
In general trench backfill should be performed in general accordance with the
Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works
Construction 1994 Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches above the groundwater level to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3918 00 Page 15
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes Compaction by water ponding or
jetting should not be allowed for this project
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or select
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch horizontal loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade
should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline flotation before
and during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of
groundwater into the trenches The buoyant uplift forces due to groundwater influx
may lift empty or even partially water filled pipes
Pipe Settlement
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the
heavier compacted backfill material placed over the pipe structure The net
increase in loading may cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the
trench invert Based on our exploration and analyses we anticipate small
primary settlements for the project Additional settlement could occur if improper
trench support or dewatering procedures are allowed

Construction Considerations
The above settlement estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and
good workmanship will be consistently practiced by the contractor Based on our
knowledge and past experience at the Kailua WWTP we understand that the
existing 54 inch concrete pipe located between the Secondary Clarifier Effluent
Collection Box and the Effluent Pump Station has experienced substantial
amounts of settlement Our findings indicate that the significant settlement may be
attributed to the construction methodology and equipment used by the contractor
Therefore special attention should be given to the installation procedure for the
proposed 54 inch concrete pipe to reduce the potential for similar settlement
distress in the future
As previously recommended the concrete pipe and junction box should be
supported by approximately 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel bedding layer over
30 inches of free draining No 2 Rock wrapped in a non woven filter fabric The
filter fabric is used to provide separation between the coarse aggregate
No 2 Rock and the fine grained soil soft clays In general a minimum overlap
of 2 feet should be provided between the ends of each roll of the fabric placed
along the pipe alignment under dry conditions If the filter fabric is not properly
overlapped it is possible that the gravel placed may migrate through the openings
in the filter fabric and mixed with the underlying soft soils This may result in
settlement of the concrete pipe due to loss of cushion materials into the soft
subsoils
Since the construction below the pipe invert may be performed in wet conditions
we envision that it would be difficult to observe the fabric installation process below
the groundwater level In addition we believe that it would be difficult for the

contractor to assure that the proper minimum overlap is maintained Therefore it
is our opinion that if placement of the filter fabric in wet conditions is necessary the
seams of the fabric should be sewn to provide higher assurance that gaps will not
occur in the filter fabric As a result we recommend that sewing the seams of the
filter fabric be incorporated into the construction process to reduce the potential for
large scale settlements
Due to the soft soil conditions anticipated at the pipe invert elevation care should

resulting in potential settlement of the soft clays and pipe during construction
i Therefore the size of the equipment used to compact the backfill around and

We speculate that this type of settlement to be on the order of 1 to 2 inches
assuming that small equipment is used to compact the backfill around and above
the pipe and that extra care is exercised during construction

Site Gradin
We anticipate that the majority of the site grading work will involve relatively deep
excavations for construction of the UV channel structure and the installation of the 54 inch
reinforced concrete pipe and associated junction box Site grading and earthwork
operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is important that
a representative from our office be present to observe the grading operations to evaluate
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and
whether the exposed soil rock conditions at the invert elevations are similar to those
encountered in our exploration
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris and other deleterious and organic
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Existing pavements to be
demolished should be completely removed Over excavation resulting from demolition
should be backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be
abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly
backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition areas at grade or are designated to
receive fill should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

Excavation
Based on the design invert elevations of the below grade structures excavation
depths on the order of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface will be required for
the project construction Based on our field exploration results the relatively deep
excavations will likely encounter groundwater and soft loose soil conditions
Excavation Method
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated
soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from any underground utilities to
reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence which may damage the
underlying utility lines The excavated materials should not be stockpiled adjacent
to the planned excavation within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the
excavation
Excavation Support
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing
may be used for excavation support and to reduce the potential for adjacent
ground movement It should be noted that during and after installation of the sheet
piles minor settlements may be possible Therefore it is recommended that a
qualified Geotechnical Engineer be retained by the Contractor to design and
evaluate the shoring system used
It is important that adequate sheeting be installed prior to excavation and
maintained tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during
excavation The properly braced sheeting is essential to reduce lateral
movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in
potential settlements or distress to adjacent structures or other improvements
such as roads or utilities
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3918 00 Page 21
The steel sheet piling should be driven with a suitable hammer to a depth sufficient
to provide stability of the excavation bottom and to reduce the possibility of bottom
heaving or quick conditions which may be caused by upward seepage pressure
during dewatering Based on our estimate the sheeting should be driven to a
minimum penetration depth of 12 feet below the trench bottom along the proposed
alignment The above recommended sheet pile penetration depth is based on the
assumption that sump pumping inside the excavations is used to dewater the
excavations The use of a deep well system outside the excavation to draw down
the natural groundwater level to reduce the required sheet pile embedment should
not be allowed considering the soft soil conditions at the site
For estimating earth loads in the struts of the trench bracing the apparent
pressure diagram presented on Plate 4 may be used as a guide An appropriate
safety factor should be included by the shoring designer Appropriate loads due
to surcharge equipment and traffic adjacent to the excavations should be added
to the recommended earth pressures for design purposes
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the Contractor His representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during
construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring
stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3918 00 Page 22
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements with instruments
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation Therefore we recommend that
dewatering provisions be included in the contract documents for construction Since the
excavation will involve discharge of groundwater a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary The Contractor should consult
their independent consultant for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the
permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the Contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the Contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring
support such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized In addition the
operations should be carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction
of the proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may
include but are not limited to pumping grouting cut off walls tremie concrete plugs or
any combination of the above and or other possible methods

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing structures roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified Geotechnical Engineer be
retained by the Contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the Contractor His Her qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done
without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3918 00 Page 24
coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in
blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the
use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater
level should not be allowed
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches
connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in
the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater
drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or
other existing adjacent structures

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate B 2
Two consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on site soils The test results are
presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
