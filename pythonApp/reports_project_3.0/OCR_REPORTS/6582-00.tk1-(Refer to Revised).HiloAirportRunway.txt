FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS 
As previously mentioned, a field exploration and laboratory testing program was 
undertaken to evaluate the character and thickness of the existing pavement structure 
as well as the underlying subgrade condition at selected locations along Runway 3 and 
Runway 21.  Detailed discussions of our findings and discussions are presented below. 
Field Exploration and Laboratory Testing Program 
Our field exploration program consisted of coring and sampling 10 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4 at Runway 21 and Boring Nos. 5 through 10 at 
Runway 3, extending to depths of about 12 to 20 inches below the pavement finished 
grades.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 
2.2. 
In general, our field exploration indicated that the existing pavement structure 
consisted of 4 to 5.5 inches of Hot Mix Asphalt (HMA) with the majority of the borings 
showing an HMA thickness of about 4 to 4.5 inches.  Visual examination of the AC cores 
retrieved from the field exploration indicated a relatively poor bonding break at about 
2 inches below the pavement finished grade at the majority of the boring locations. 
The materials below the HMA layer were sampled using a standard penetration 
sampler to a maximum depth of approximately 20 inches below the pavement finished 
grade.  Sieve analysis tests were performed on the materials obtained to evaluate the 
gradation characteristics of the materials and to aid in soil classification. 
Based on our field observations and laboratory test results, it appears that the HMA 
layer is generally underlain by well-graded basaltic sands and gravel with some silts 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 20 inches below the pavement 
finished grades.  Our visual examination and laboratory test results suggested that the 
materials encountered below the HMA layer are likely basaltic base course material.  
Based on the penetration resistance of approximately 4 to 9 blows per foot obtained during 
the sampling operation, it is our opinion that the basaltic sands and gravel (base course 
material) below the HMA layer generally exist in a loose condition. It should be noted that 
some of the samples obtained below the HMA layer exhibited odor of hydrocarbon 
byproducts. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology and graphic 
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of 
Borings in Appendix A.  Results of the laboratory gradation tests performed on the 
materials encountered below the HMA layer are presented in Appendix B.  Photographs of 
the AC cores and underlying materials are presented in Appendix C.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at Runway 3 and Runway 21 by coring 
and sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, extending to depths 
of about 12 to 20 inches below the pavement finished grade.  The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2.  The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-10. 
In addition, soil samples below the HMA layer were obtained from the borings 
drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler for the sampling 
distance are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths.
