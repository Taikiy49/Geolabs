one boring that generally encountered fill materials consisting of medium stiff sandy clays

fluctuate depending on rainfall, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors.

exploration performed in support of the feasibility study and Rough Order of Magnitude 
(ROM) cost estimate for the transit-oriented development at the Moanalua Kai site in the

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau 
Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
When the building of the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes came to an end, only a 
slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu. 
The project site is generally located on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The 
coastal plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the 
eastern two-thirds of the Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive 
accumulation of alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral 
reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess 
of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava flows 
to be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils. During the Kaena stand of the sea 
(at approximately +90 feet Mean Sea Level [MSL]) of the Pleistocene Epoch, a delta of 
silt, sand, and gravel built out into the embayment near the project site. Therefore, the 
project site is generally underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments and 
lagoonal deposits.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the project 
site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to 
be addressed. The following sections provide discussions on the soil profile type for 
seismic design and liquefaction design consideration at the project site. 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling using seismic cone penetration testing 
(SCPT) equipment was performed in Boring No. 1 in an effort to closely analyze the 
seismic design considerations at the site. To analyze the subsurface soil parameters for 
seismic design considerations, the shear wave velocity profiling was performed at 
discrete intervals extending to approximately 153 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. Based on the shear wave velocity profiling, the weighted average shear wave 
velocity for the subsurface materials within the upper 100 feet of the profile is on the 
order of about 378 feet per second. Results of the shear wave velocity test are provided 
in Appendix B. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered, the project site is underlain by 
thick soft soil layers meeting one of the following conditions.

2.3.1 Site Specific Response Spectrum 
A site-specific elastic response spectrum (site-specific design ground motion) 
was developed using the Shake 2000 computer program for the one-dimensional 
wave propagation analysis. In general, the site-specific elastic response 
spectrum (site-specific design ground motion) was generated in general 
accordance with Chapter 21, Site-Specific Ground Motion Procedures for 
Seismic Design contained in ASCE 7-05 (Minimum Design Loads for Buildings 
and Other Structures). 
An idealized site response model was established based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered in our boring at the project site and the seismic shear 
wave velocity profiling performed in the boring. Non-linear shear stress-strain 
relationships were selected for each soil material as appropriate. 
Seven pairs of horizontal ground motion acceleration time histories were selected 
from seven earthquake events with earthquake Magnitudes of 5.99, 6.0, 6.06, 
6.19, 6.6, 6.61 and 6.7. The seven pairs of horizontal ground motion acceleration 
time histories were from earthquake events recorded on the Island of Hawaii and 
California. Each selected time history was scaled to approximate the maximum 
considered earthquake bedrock acceleration of 0.26g expected at the project 
site. The ground motion time histories were input at the base of the soil profile 
and propagated through the soil profile to generate the site-specific ground 
motion time history and elastic response spectrum at the ground surface.

2.3.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near 
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of 
excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected 
to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and 
decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is 
unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, 
the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained 
vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden 
pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to 
behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient mobility 
to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, will result 
in significant deformations. 
In general, the project area is underlain by medium stiff and medium dense fill 
materials placed over soft to very soft recent alluvial deposits overlaying stiff to very 
stiff older alluvial deposits. Based on the medium dense fill and cohesive material 
encountered in the boring, we do not consider the phenomenon of soil liquefaction 
is a design consideration.  
It should be noted that this preliminary assessment is based on a single boring in 
the project area. Therefore, additional engineering analyses should be conducted during 
the project design phase when additional subsurface information becomes available to 
confirm and/or modify the above assessment.

3.1 
Deep Foundation Design Alternatives 
Based on the information provided, we understand the proposed project will 
consist of developing 6-story and single-story industrial buildings and a 5-story parking 
structure. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions described above and the 
structural loads anticipated, we recommend the following deep foundation alternatives 
be considered for the foundation support of the planned development. 
1. Precast, Prestressed Concrete Driven Piles 
2. Cast-in-Place Concrete Drilled Shafts 
Lateral load analyses for the deep foundation systems should be performed 
during the project design phase when detailed foundation design layout information is 
available.  
It should be noted that all deep foundation systems considered for the proposed 
development may be subjected to downdrag loading resulting from the consolidation of 
the compressible lagoonal deposits under the weight of the previously placed fills and/or 
the new fills planned for the project. As mentioned above, the project site is underlain by 
relatively thick deposits of very soft to soft compressible recent alluvial soils. When the 
very soft to soft soil deposits move downward relative to the stationary pile/shaft, the 
downward movement will result in negative skin friction forces, generally known as 
downdrag forces, acting on the pile/shaft. The magnitude of the downdrag force 
depends on the pile/shaft perimeter, the thickness of the compressible layer, and 
compressibility/relative density of the soft/loose soil deposits. Therefore, the allowable 
compressive load capacities of the deep foundations recommended have been reduced 
by the estimated downdrag loads in the foundation analyses.

3.1.1 Precast, Prestressed Concrete Driven Piles 
As a deep foundation design alternative, we believe precast, prestressed concrete 
driven piles may be considered to support the new building structures. It should be 
noted that the use of driven pile foundation may pose some significant design and 
construction issues for the project development due to the presence of cobbles and 
boulders anticipated at depths between 123 and 138 feet below the existing 
pavement and the moderately high structural load demand. The presence of 
cobbles and boulders anticipated at various depths across the project site would 
result in the potential for driven piles to “hang up” on the cobbles and boulders and 
the potential need to increase the size of the pile caps substantially to 
accommodate the additional replacement piles. 
The concrete driven pile system can also be installed with relatively higher 
production rate and minimal spoil disposal as they are considered displacement 
piles. However, the pile driving operation may generate excessive noise and 
vibration that may have an impact to the adjacent existing structures supported on 
a shallow foundation system. 
The driven piles would derive its bearing support capacity from the dense gravelly 
and stiff to very stiff clayey alluvium encountered at greater depths through a 
combination of skin friction contribution between the pile and the surrounding soils 
and the end bearing component at the tip of the pile. 
Based on our engineering analyses, the new building structures may be supported 
on 16.5-inch or 20-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete piles. In general, 
the driven piles installed in groups should have a minimum center-to-center spacing 
of 2.5 times the pile diameter to avoid reduction in the vertical compressive load 
capacity and to facilitate pile driving in groups. Our preliminary recommendations 
pertaining to the driven pile capacities are presented in the following table. It should 
be noted that the pile lengths are based on an assumed bottom of cap elevation at 
about 5 feet below the finished ground surface.

3.2 
Ground Floor Slabs 
For the new building construction, we anticipate the ground floor slabs likely will 
consist of reinforced concrete slabs‐on‐grade. We envision the slabs‐on‐grade for the 
new building structures will be supported on new compacted fills placed to raise the 
existing ground surface. Generally, we recommend the building slab thickness be 
5 inches (minimum) with some light steel reinforcement (No. 3 bars at 12 inches 
on‐center in each direction). 
New fills needed to raise the project site to the finished subgrades could induce 
some ground settlements due to the consolidation of compressible recent alluvial 
deposits that are present at the site. Therefore, we recommend placing the new fills as 
soon as practical to allow the anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to 
slab‐on‐grade construction.  
The subgrades for concrete slabs-on-grades (not subjected to vehicular traffic) 
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture, and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. The 
compaction requirement for subgrades of slabs on grades subjected to vehicular traffic, 
such as the parking structure ground floor level, should be increased to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percent by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Fill materials required to raise the existing ground to the finished grades should 
consist of select granular fills.  Select granular fills should consist of imported granular 
materials such as crushed coral and basalt. The materials should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The materials 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a 
swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The 
material also should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve. The select granular fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the

3.4.1 Jet-Grouted Columns 
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with 
radial horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil-cement columns. The 
jet-grouting technique is a process that produces soil-cement columns by 
pumping neat cement grout slurry through horizontal jets injected at high 
pressures. The horizontal jets of cement grout slurry cuts and mixes the 
surrounding in-situ materials with the neat cement slurry grout as the drill bit is 
slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil-cement column. 
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site and the anticipated size 
of the new sewer lines, we recommend that the jet-grouted columns have a 
minimum diameter of 30 inches. In addition, the grout mix should have a specific 
gravity of at least 1.5 and should be able to produce jet-grouted columns with a 
7-day unconfined compressive strength of at least 150 pounds per square inch 
(psi) and a 28-day unconfined compressive strength of at least 300 psi. The 
jet-grouted columns should be installed from the bottom of the jet-grouted cradle 
and extend down to about 110 feet below the existing ground surface.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
one exploratory boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 153 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. The boring was drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers and PQ coring tools. The boring was drilled at 
the approximate location shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-1.1 through A-1.5. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the logs of boring indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. Relatively “undisturbed” samples of the 
soft soil formation were obtained by pushing a 3-inch OD thin-walled stainless steel 
Shelby tube into the soil formation in general accordance with ASTM D1587, Standard 
Practice for Thin-Walled Tube Sampling of Soils for Geotechnical Purposes. The range 
of hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler through a 24-inch push is shown 
as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
It should be noted that the efficiency of the safety hammer used for this project was 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratio is shown on the Logs of 
Boring. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.

the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Core Recovery may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Core Recovery is defined as the 
actual length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core 
attempt.  For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the 
recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Boring as Core 
Recovery = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the 
Logs of Boring as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative 
quality of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks 
and Minerals."

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials at the project site 
was performed using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. The purpose 
of the seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was to analyze the 
seismic design considerations more closely for the project. Shear wave velocity testing 
using seismic cone penetration test equipment was performed at the boring location, 
designated as Boring No. 1, on the Site Plan (Plate 2). The seismic shear wave velocity 
profiling was performed at various depths extending to a maximum depth of about 153 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
To conduct the seismic shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test boring was 
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. Log of the 
materials encountered in the boring are presented in the Logs of Boring in Appendix A. 
After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the bored hole was 
backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter coated bentonite pellets. The temporary casing from the 
coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the bentonite pellets starting from the 
bottom and advancing upward. When the bentonite pellets are in contact with the water in 
the borehole, the pellets start to hydrate slowly. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell 
and soften. The probe was then pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a 
depth of about 153 feet below the existing ground surface using seismic cone testing 
equipment (SCPT). 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected surface 
area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the bored hole. The cone carries a 
uniaxial horizontal accelerator geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave generated 
and propagated from the ground surface. The seismic measurements were made when 
the SCPT had stopped and a shear wave was sent into the subsurface. A shear wave was 
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer. The 
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each 
discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference 
for the shear wave to arrive at various depths given provided the average shear wave 
velocity over the depth interval. 
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average seismic shear 
wave velocity calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the boring location are 
presented on Plates B-1.1 through B-1.5 in Appendix B.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate C-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) including one hydrometer test 
(ASTM D422) was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentation of the 
grain size distribution is provided on Plate C-2. 
Five Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
in-situ soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results 
and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates C-3 through C-7. 
Three Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D2435) were performed on 
samples of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics 
of the materials encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates 
C-8 through C-10. 
One set of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), was performed on a selected soil sample obtained from our field exploration. 
The test results are summarized on Plate C-11.
