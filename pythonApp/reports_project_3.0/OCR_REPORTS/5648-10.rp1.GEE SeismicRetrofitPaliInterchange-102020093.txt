Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Seismic Retrofit of Interstate Route H 1 Pali Interchange
and Nuuanu Grade Separation Honolulu Oahu Hawaii The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
above project These recommendations are intended for the design of the micropile
foundation for this project only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The volcanic
materials erupted from the Waianae Volcano is referred to as the Waianae Volcanic
Series
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island Volcanic materials erupted from the Koolau Volcano are referred to as the
Koolau Volcanic Series Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano
remained active therefore the Waianae Volcano s eastern flank was partially buried
below the younger Koolau lavas that banked against its eastern flank These banked or
ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central
Oahu The present day form of the Island of Oahu is a result of stream dissection and
mass erosion of the steep valley walls
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu multiple global
fluctuations in sea level occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of the great
continental glaciers These sea level changes occurred more substantially in the
Pleistocene Epoch and had some significant effect on the geologic evolution of the
Island of Oahu The changes in worldwide sea level affected the erosional baseline of
streams and caused local submergence and emergence of the coastal landforms with
respect to the level of the sea
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by
a long period of erosion whereby deep valleys were carved into the Koolau Mountain
Range The valleys were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediments as the
island slowly sank under the weight of the new landmass

The project site is located along the southern flank of the Koolau Mountain
Range The geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are related directly to
the glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the
genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Honolulu Coastal Plain These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu The subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated
by fossil Pleistocene age alluvial soil deposits and related deposits laid during high
stands of the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within
the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of
the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions
During periods of insignificant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During
that rise the deep valleys in the project area were submerged Within the last
10 000 years the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in
areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered medium dense and or stiff alluvium
overlying hard basalt formation Cobbles and boulders were encountered at various
depths within the alluvium layer Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our
borings and the structural loading provided by the project structural engineer we
recommend using micropile foundations to provide the additional lateral and vertical
support for the seismic retrofit of the High Level Ramp over School Street structure We
understand additional foundation support will be required below the abutment wall
extensions and at the new pier location
Based on the generally limited space and limited headroom constraints at the
project site we recommend utilizing new micropiles to provide the lateral and vertical
support for the seismic retrofit of the High Level Ramp over School Street structure Due to
the relatively small cross sectional area of the micropile point bearing area we
recommend the micropiles derive the axial load support from skin friction between the
micropile shaft and the surrounding subsurface materials Detailed discussions and
geotechnical recommendations for the design of the micropile foundations are
presented in the following sections herein
3 1
Micropile Foundation
Based on the current design concept both abutments will be retrofitted along
with an additional new pier for the High Level Ramp Due to the limited space for
construction and the load demands for the new structural elements we recommend
utilizing a deep foundation system consisting of micropiles to provide the lateral and
vertical foundation support for the proposed retrofit project
In general a micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter
usually less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with reinforcing steel The
micropile foundation typically is constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing
steel in the hole and grouting the borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can
be installed readily in access restrictive environments with generally smaller equipment
and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In addition installation of the

Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the load carrying capacity of the micropiles is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor to install the micropile Therefore the load capacity of the micropile may
vary considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation systems
In order to determine whether the contractor s methods of micropile installation are
adequate and to determine the ultimate load capacity we recommend performing
at least one pre production compressive load test on a sacrificial micropile at or
near the new pier location In addition we recommend conducting at least one
pre production tensile load test at each of the two abutment locations In general
the purpose of the pre production load test on a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the
following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
The pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1143 Compressive Load Test and D 3689 Tensile Load Test Based on
experience with similar projects we recommend conducting load tests no earlier than
7 days after completion of the micropile installation Additional micropiles may be used
for reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre production load test
micropile The reaction micropiles should be installed to adequate depths or as deep as

Lateral Load Resistance
In general lateral load resistance of micropile is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the micropile allowable deflection at the top of
micropile and induced moment in the micropile Our lateral load analyses utilized
the computer program GROUP 3D to evaluate the lateral deflection and reactions
of the micropile The program was developed to compute the distribution of loads
vertical lateral and overturning moment in up to three orthogonal axes from the
cap to micropiles arranged in a group The program solves the non linear response
of each micropile under combined loadings and assures compatibility of geometry
and equilibrium of forces between the applied external loads and the reactions of
each micropile head

Corrosion Potential
Two sets of corrosion tests including pH minimum Resistivity chloride content
and sulfate content tests were performed by Test America Analytical Testing
Corporation on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test
results are summarized on Plate B 8
Based on the test results presented the subsurface soils at the project site
generally exhibit minimum resistivity values of about 1 860 to 5 580 ohm cm
indicating that the subsurface soils are moderately to very corrosive to buried
metallic substructures Therefore we recommend properly designing the metallic
substructures to be protected against potential corrosion A corrosion engineer
should be consulted for detailed recommendations on corrosion protection
As a minimum we recommend providing a permanent steel casing in the upper
20 feet of the micropile to protect the micropile reinforcing steel bar from corrosion
and also to provide adequate confinement to the upper portion of the micropile
where the moment demand on the micropile is the greatest

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings extending to depths of about 71 to 111 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
advanced using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and
coring tools The three borings were drilled at the three intermediate pier locations
where substructure retrofit was originally planned Following completion of the field
exploration the seismic retrofit scheme was changed with substructure retrofit work at
the two abutment locations and at a new pier location
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered and other observations are presented on
the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Standard Practice for Thick Wall Ring Lined
Spilt Barrel Drive Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD modified California sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained
from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Standard Test
Method for Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques consisting of a PQ wireline coring system
with double tube core barrel core size of 3 35 inches in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
