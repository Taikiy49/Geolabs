Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years
ago
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present
stand When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas
and fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the
sediments and calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and
lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed
subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial
soils under relatively high energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered
the basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes
consisting of detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls transport
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also
altered the natural condition of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that the proposed
new pier may be supported on shallow foundations consisting of spread and or
continuous footings provided that the footings are embedded approximately 12 inches
below the surrounding mudline An allowable bearing pressure up to 500 pounds per
square foot psf may be utilized for the design of pier foundations bearing on the
surface layer of cobbles and gravels This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind
wave or seismic forces
Due to the soft on site materials at the project site significant settlement up to
6 inches might occur if a shallow foundation scheme is utilized As an alternative a
deep foundation system consisting of driven or drilled pipe piles might also be used to
support the new pier
Detailed discussions and specific recommendations for design of pier
foundations and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following
sections
3 1
Pier Foundations
Based on the anticipated column loads and the subsurface conditions
encountered the new pier may be supported on shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings Since soft alluvial soils may be encountered at the
bottom of footing elevations we recommend using an allowable bearing pressure of up
to 500 psf for the design of the foundations This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces
The footings should be embedded to a minimum depth of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent mudline If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance
with our recommendations we estimate total settlements to be approximately 6 inches
with differential settlements on the order of 3 inches

Alternate Foundation Design
Our field exploration indicated that soft alluvial soil are present to approximately
36 feet below the existing mudline Based on the current shallow foundation design
scheme significant settlement of up to 6 inches might occur due to the consolidation of
the soft soil If the amount of settlement estimated above is not acceptable for the new
pier structure a deep foundation system could be utilized to support the new pier
In general the deep foundation should consist driven or drilled pipe piles The
piles would derive support mainly from adhesion between the pile and the recent alluvial
materials encountered in the borings The actual pile length could not be determined at
this time this report was prepared since the foundation load demand was not yet clear
However based on our field exploration medium stiff materials were encountered at
about 30 feet below the existing mudline and it grades to stiff at 36 feet We expect that
the pile foundation would extend at least 36 feet below the mudline in order to provide
some bearing capacity Once the deep foundation option is selected a detailed analysis
will be performed to determine the design of the piles
It should be noted that a deep foundation system has significant advantages over
a shallow foundation system for this project It will provide not only a higher bearing
capacity and less settlement but also a smaller footprint over the ocean floor In
addition it can be integrated into the bridge foundation structure which eliminates the
requirement of additional footings However due to the project location a deep
foundation system would be costly Furthermore the upper sections of the foundation

Alternate Design Scheme
As discussed above either a shallow foundation or a deep foundation option
carries advantages and disadvantages Therefore the options shall be properly
weighted prior to finalizing the design
As an alternative design scheme a floating pier design may be considered In
general the pier will be restrained laterally using anchors and will be allowed to move
vertically during tide changes When the tide drops below the mudline at the pier
location the pier will be directly supported by the mudline soils
We anticipate that this scheme will carry similar rigorous environmental permit
applications as other types of foundation system however the cost of construction
could be the least of all three approaches We recommend consulting a specialized
water structure vendor designer for the final design if this option is selected

Field Exploration

two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 The borings extended to depths of about
23 and 43 feet below the existing mudline The approximate boring locations are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a Con Core drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools

textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2
