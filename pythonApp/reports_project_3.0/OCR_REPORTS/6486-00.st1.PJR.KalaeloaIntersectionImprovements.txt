SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Kalaeloa FBI Frontage
Improvements project Based on the traffic data provided the R value of the subgrade
soils and the economic analyses performed a rigid pavement structural section was most
economical However since the project consists of widening the roadway near the
intersection area and the existing pavement consists of a flexible pavement section we
recommend using a flexible pavement structural section for the new pavements although
a rigid pavement section could be more economical
The pavement design manual generally requires incorporating a permeable
drainage layer in the pavement design However due to its granular and porous nature
the anticipated pavement subgrade materials generally have relatively high permeability
Therefore we believe subgrade drainage would generally be good and a permeable
drainage layer may be omitted for the design of pavement sections
The recommended pavement structural section for new pavement along Franklin D
Roosevelt Avenue is 4 0 inches of AC over 7 5 inches of ACB A pavement section
consisting of 4 0 inches of AC over 9 5 inches of ACB should be used for new pavements
along Enterprise Avenue
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore special attention should
be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements As a minimum the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures To collect infiltrated
water in the pavement structural section we recommend providing a pavement edge drain
system along the edges of the new pavements
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific recommendations for the pavement designs

Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed
for the new pavement designs for the Kalaeloa FBI Frontage Improvements project in
the Kalaeloa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for pavement design only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted
at the end of this report

Project Considerations
We understand that a new FBI facility is planned at the intersection of
Franklin D Roosevelt Avenue and Enterprise Avenue in the District of Ewa on the Island
of Oahu As part of the development of the new FBI facility we understand that off site
improvements to the existing Franklin D Roosevelt Avenue and Enterprise Avenue
intersection will be required This report covers the off site improvements of the existing
Franklin D Roosevelt Avenue and Enterprise Avenue intersection only

underground utility lines and medians Drywells and seepage pits will also be required for

pavement designs

other elements of the project Detailed discussion and geotechnical recommendations
for design are presented in the geotechnical engineering report

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain which extends seaward off
the southeastern flank of Waianae and the southwestern flank of Koolau The Ewa
Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the deposition of alluvial clays and silts
derived from the weathering of basalt rock further up slope The alluvial deposits
accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations
to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from
zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach
This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock
over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Basalt rock formation resides below
the marine deposits at a substantial depth

Methodology of Pavement Design
In general we considered two types of pavement structural sections flexible and
rigid pavements in the pavement analyses for this project The flexible pavement
sections presented herein generally were determined based on the methodology
described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 The
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and
Research Branch prepared the Pavement Design Manual The pavement design
methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the
California Department of Transportation Caltrans
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections were generally based on
the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association PCA Thickness
Design for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements

Design Pavement Section
As previously mentioned the new pavements for the project will generally be
constructed on compacted select granular fill or re compacted on site granular soils
Due to its granular and porous nature select granular fill materials and on site granular
soils generally have relatively high permeability Therefore we believe subgrade
drainage would generally be good and a permeable drainage layer may be omitted for
the design of pavement sections
Based on the relatively high traffic volumes greater than 10 000 vehicles per
day anticipated along Franklin D Roosevelt Avenue and Enterprise Avenue we
believe asphalt concrete base ACB should be used as the base material for flexible
pavements Therefore we did not consider pavement sections utilizing untreated
aggregate base courses in the flexible pavements in our pavement design analyses
For the purposes of rigid pavement design concrete MR of 650 psi was used for
the project In addition we have assumed that dowel baskets will be required at the
concrete pavement joints

Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill
materials provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and clay lumps
and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension
Imported non expansive select granular fill should consist of crushed coral or
basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 It should be noted that the fill requirements
presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified by Geolabs based
on additional laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials required should
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Sections 30 and 31 of the

Compaction Requirements
Compaction should be accomplished with sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be allowed for compaction General and select granular fill materials should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The compaction requirement for fill materials placed within 3 feet
vertically from finish grades under pavements should be increased to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Aggregate subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to
the requirements stipulated in Subsection 703 16 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications
for Road and Bridge Construction 2005 HSS The aggregate subbase should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to not less than 95 percent of the maximum
density according to Section 106 09 Special Test Methods of the HSS
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basaltic
aggregates placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined
in accordance with AASHTO T 209 ASTM D 2041 test procedures Asphaltic concrete
AC materials should be constructed in general accordance with Section 401 Asphalt
Concrete Pavement of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge
Construction 2005 and subsequent amendments

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore special attention
should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements As a
minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during or after construction
In general we recommend providing a pavement edge drain system along the
sides of the new roadway to collect infiltrated water in the pavement structural section
In general the edge drain should consist of a 6 inch PVC perforated pipe
with perforations facing down surrounded by untreated permeable base A permeable
separator non woven filter fabric conforming to Section 648 of the HSS should be
placed around the edge drain as shown on Plate 3 Care should be exercised when
placing the subbase base and or constructing the surface courses to avoid damaging
the edge drain collector pipe A typical edge drain detail is provided on Plate 3 for
reference The edge drains should be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for
proper discharge of infiltrated water
