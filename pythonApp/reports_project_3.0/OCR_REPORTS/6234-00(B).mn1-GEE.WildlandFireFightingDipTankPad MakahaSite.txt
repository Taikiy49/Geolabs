performed for the Wildland Fire Fighting HFD Dip Tank Service Area project along
Kili Drive at Makaha Valley in the District of Waianae on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The

Fire fighting helicopters use dip tanks when other water sources are not an
option Dip tanks cut the distances that helicopters must travel to the nearest body of
water help when the local water source is not deep enough or alleviate the
environmental concerns with using local water

Makaha Valley project site

have a diameter of 15 feet a wall height of 5 feet and an operational volume of

the dip tank will be supported on a 20 foot by 20 foot concrete pad foundation

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is situated in Makaha Valley on the southeastern base of
Puu Keaau which forms the northwestern ridge of Makaha Valley in the district of
Waianae on the Island of Keaau is an eroded ridge expression of a
portion of the Waianae Shield Volcano which forms the western part of the Island of
Oahu
The Waianae range is made up of layered basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
material which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series The
Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into lower middle and upper volcanic members
The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that built the
main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock that
accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a
relatively thin capping which covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its
evolutional history The northern side of Makaha Valley comprises the northern wall of
the vast caldera which extends southeastward to Lualualei Valley The lava rock layers
forming the caldera walls are nearly horizontal in deposition
After the formation of the Waianae shield volcano a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of Oahu occurred forming the large valleys on the
western side of the shield volcano These erosion valleys were gradually filled with
enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined result of
stream erosion base level sea level rise and subsidence of the island mass Over
time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and colluvial

deposits consisting of stiff gravelly clay over basaltic gravel and cobbles in a sandy silt

Utility Lines
The following guidelines and recommendations may be used for the installation
of the new water line planned for the project In general good construction practices
should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional excavation
methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation The contractor should
determine the method and equipment for trench excavation subject to practical limits
and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the
applicable federal state and local safety requirements The contractor should be
responsible for trench shoring design and installation
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground
subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the dip tank pad and retaining structures should be

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
one boring extending to a depth of about 21 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The boring was drilled
using a track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and core barrel
system
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Log of Boring
Plate A 1
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Core Samples of Rock Materials encountered were obtained using diamond core
drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method
which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material contained in the
hollow core of the bit is recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide in the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 4 0 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the boring log as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
