SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed East Kauai Irrigation System Improvements,
Wailua Reservoir project on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testings, and
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of the pile
foundation for this project only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report.
1.2. Project Considerations
The project site is north of Kuamoo Road in the Kapaa area on the Island of
Kauai, Hawaii. Currently, there are two existing wooden catwalks, 100 feet and 40 feet
long. The existing 100-foot catwalk is at the southern side of the reservoir, while the
40-foot catwalk is at the southwestern corner of the reservoir. Intake control gates for
the reservoir can be accessed at the end of the catwalks. Both catwalks are supported
on timber piles, which are severely deteriorated. Based on the information provided, it is
proposed to construct new replacement catwalks to access the intake control gates.
The approximate location of the proposed new catwalks is shown on the General Site
Plan, Plate 2 and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2.
Based on the structural load provided, the depth of the mudline and the
subsurface conditions, we anticipate that pile foundations will be used to support the
new replacement catwalks. The pile will be supported by both skin friction and end
bearing from stiff saprolite soils.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series that began about
5 to 6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2% million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the
cessation of this main volcano shield-building phase, about 1% million years ago
renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the
post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation.
The basalt rock of the Koloa Volcanic Series is generally characterized by thick
lava flows of dense basaltic rock extruded from groups of vents aligned in north-south
trends in various locales on the eastern half of the Island of Kauai. Associated with the
Koloa Volcanic Series lava flows are some localized volcanic deposits consisting of
pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which are usually encountered as
surficial mantling deposits and accumulations surrounding the cinder cone vents. Rocks
of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai,
including the regional area surrounding the project site.
Based on our review of available geological mapping information, the project site
appears to be on a gently sloping plateau region along the foot of the
Makaleha Mountains as indicated on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. We anticipate
that the project site is underlain by weathered soils and basaltic rock belonging to the
Koloa Volcanic Series.
2.2 Site Description
The project site is located north of Kuamoo Road in the Kapaa area on the
eastern side of the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The Wailua Reservoir is part of the East
Kauai Irrigation System. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map, Plate 1.

3.1.1 Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the driven pile
and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils. The recommended
allowable uplift load capacity of 40 kips per pile may be used in the design of the
catwalks. The uplift load capacities provided already include the weight of the pile
and assume that the piles will not be spliced. The structural engineer should check
the structural capacity of the pile member in tension.
3.1.2 Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral load resistance for the driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, allowable deflection at the top of the pile,
and the induced moment in the pile.
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for
Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally
loaded deep foundation program originally developed at the University of Texas at
Austin. The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep

3.1.3 Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of
the pile member. We estimate that the total settlement of the pile-supported
foundation to be less than 0.5 inches with differential settlements between piles not
exceeding about one-half of that amount. We believe that these settlements are
essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.
3.1.4 Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
up to about 40,000 foot-pounds per blow. The hammer should be equipped with an
energy control level such that driving within the loose/soft soils may be controlled
at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development within the concrete piles,
which could damage or cause cracking of the piles.

Driving equipment used for this project should have a pile hammer suitable for the
weight of the piles to be driven. This will result in the pile hammer efficiently
delivering the required energy to the top of the pile and also reducing the potential
for damage to the piles. Geolabs should review the proposed pile driving
equipment to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of installing the piles to
the recommended capacity.
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms for review
and evaluation prior to construction. Geolabs should review the submittal and
assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer. A wave
equation analysis should be performed after selection of the pile driving equipment
to assess the driveability of the piles and to derive the pile driving criteria.
3.1.5 Pile Driving Criteria
As mentioned above, due to the uncertainties regarding the selection of the pile
driving equipment, the driving criteria for the precast concrete piles should be
determined after a wave equation analysis is performed.
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption. A pile may be
rejected or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is
interrupted for more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required
depth. During soft driving, such as when the pile tip is within soft/loose soils, the
hammer stroke (or energy) should be carefully controlled such that significant
tensile stresses do not develop within the concrete pile.
Due to the limited number of driven piles anticipated for the project construction,
we believe that a test pile program would not be conducted for the project.
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils, our representative should confirm the supporting capacity of each pile in the
field during driving. In addition, the driving criteria may need to be revised based
on the field observation.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths of about 24.3 to 40.65 feet below the mudline (about 32.5 to 50 feet
below the water surface) at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans,
Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The borings were drilled using a skid-mounted drill rig equipped with
casing and coring tools secured on a floating platform (raft).
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a near-continuous
basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing in the
laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representation of the materials encountered is
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-4.2.
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2.0-Inch
OD standard penetration sampler or 3.0-inch OD Split-Barrel sampler with a 140-pound
hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Core samples of the rock or consolidated materials encountered at the site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation or consolidated .
materials. The rock (or consolidated) material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D 2937)
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic
representations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Direct shear test
results are presented on Plate B-2.
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D 2166) was performed on a selected
core run to evaluate their Unconfined Compressive Strength of the cohesive soil
encountered. Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plate B-3.
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests
(ASTM D 850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are
presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D 2435) was performed on a sample
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered. Consolidation test results are presented on Plate B-6.
