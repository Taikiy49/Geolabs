INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical
engineering exploration performed for the proposed Undersea World Hawaii
in the Kakaako area of metropolitan Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location of the project is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our preliminary exploration and
our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses of
the data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and not intended for construction estimates or
bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is our understanding that the proposed facility will consist of a
four story aquarium building designed with two 2 basement levels housing
large filter tanks and fish tanks a three story quarantine building also
with two 2 basement levels and a one level basement parking building
adjacent to the aquarium building The three 3 proposed buildings will
be interconnected at the basement levels
Open on grade parking is planned over the underground parking
building
The proposed aquarium building is about 100 by 280 feet in plan
dimensions and the proposed quarantine building is about 70 by 80 feet in
plan dimensions
Shoreline protection structures are contemplated along the existing
Kewalo Basin boundary of the project site However ocean engineering
input to provide shore protection design and construction details for
these shoreline structures was not included in our scope of work
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2237 00 Page 3
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration
was to obtain an overview of the subsurface conditions at the project site
to develop a generalized soil rock data set for the formation of
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the site grading
foundation and pavement design of the proposed construction at the project
site
The work was done in general accordance with our proposal dated
to Belt Collins Associates The scope of work
included
1 Drilling and sampling four 4 borings to depths ranging from
46 5 to 105 0 feet below the existing ground surface
2 Reconnaissance of the project site and its vicinity to make
visual observations of the surficial soil conditions
3 Review of available soil and geologic information pertinent to
the project site and its vicinity
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained from the field
exploration to determine the engineering properties and
Classification of the subsoils encountered
5 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work and presenting
our findings and recommendations

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located about 800 feet south of Ala Moana
Boulevard between Ahui Street and Kewalo Basin in the existing Keehi
Marine Inc Kewalo Shipyard The site covers an area of about 2 4 acres
The shipyard is presently occupied by a corrugated iron building
approximately 80 by 150 feet in plan dimensions housing repair shops and
the shipyard office The other open areas are dry dock areas currently
occupied by ships or boats under repair a partially submerged dock area
a concrete pier a depressed movable track area and open parking
The existing ground in the shipyard is generally level and at an
elevation of about 5 0 to 5 5 feet above MSL except in the depressed
movable track area which is about 2 5 to 3 0 feet lower
The site is partially paved and covered with loose black sand which
appears to have been used for sand blasting operations
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions of the project site were explored by
drilling four 4 borings to depths of 46 5 to 105 0 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of these borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The field exploration indicates that in general the site is mantled
by a fill crust about 5 to 10 feet thick consisting of medium to dense
sandy and gravelly subsoils except at the concrete pier which has a
6 foot thick concrete deck
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2237 00 Page 5
The upper fill crust is underlain by about 13 to 25 feet of medium
dense to dense sandy and gravelly reef detritus over a relatively thin
layer of loose to medium dense lagoonal sand and gravel deposits Below
the lagoonal deposits the borings encountered coral detritus with erratic
consistency and occasional thin coral ledges of a medium to hard strength
at variable depths
A continuous coral formation appears to exist at a depth of about 92
to 97 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was measured at relatively shallow depths of about 4 5 to
6 0 feet below the existing ground or at about 0 to 0 6 foot elevation
above Mean Sea Level MSL in the drilled borings during the field
exploration
Due to proximity to Kewalo Basin groundwater level in the project
site is anticipated to fluctuate in response to tidal influences in the
basin
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration encountered groundwater level at a shallow
depth of about 5 feet and medium dense to dense fills and reef detritus in
the upper 20 to 30 feet in the project site Therefore thick mat
foundations may be used to support the proposed buildings and resist the
uplifting buoyant forces on the submerged basement levels of the
structures
Alternately pile foundations may be considered We recommend that
short piles penetrating about 40 feet down below the proposed building
basement elevations or end bearing on the upper coral ledges be used
Longer high capacity piles are not recommended due to construction
difficulties anticipated in predrilling and pile driving through
occasional upper coral ledges
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2237 00 Page 6
We recommend that additional boring exploration be conducted to
further delineate the occasional upper coral ledges within the proposed
building areas if pile foundations are to be used to support the
buildings
The proposed building basement excavation should be retained with
adequately designed steel sheetpiles driven below the basements to
sufficient depths for stability and dewatering requirements A suitable
efficient dewatering system should be developed by the contractor to
maintain the stability of the excavations below the anticipated high
groundwater level
Mat Foundation
Based on the groundwater and subsoil conditions encountered in the
borings we believe that continuous mat foundations may be used to support
the proposed aquarium building quarantine building and wumderground
parking structure Due to the anticipated medium dense to loose subgrade
and wet soil conditions a working platform consisting of a minimm of
one foot of 3B Fine gravel may be needed at the bottoms of the mat
foundations
We recommend that the mat foundations be designed sufficiently to
resist the uplifting buoyant forces imposed on the submerged basement
structures The critical condition would be under high groundwater level
when the filter tanks fish tanks and parking spaces inside the buildings
are empty during maintenance or recess periods
It is estimated that the net bearing pressures generated from the
proposed buildings on the underlying subsoils will be relatively small due
to the large quantity of earth removal by basement excavations and the
uplifting buoyant forces under the anticipated hydraulic head Therefore
we recommend that an allowable bearing capacity of 2500 p s f be used for
GEOLABS HAWAII

preliminary mat foundation design on minimum one foot of 3B Fine gravel
layer The allowable bearing value is for total dead plus live loads only
and may be increased by one third for short duration uplift wind or
seismic loads
Mat foundation settlements under the design loads are anticipated to
be quick and should be minimal less than one half of an inch due to low
net bearing pressures
lateral loads acting on the proposed buildings may be resisted by
passive soil pressures developed against the basement structures and
frictional resistance developed at the bottoms of the mat foundations We
recommend that equivalent fluid pressures of 300 p s f above water
table and 115 p s f below water table per foot of depth be used to
calculate the passive soil pressure developed against the basement walls
and a frictional coefficient of 0 4 be used to calculate the frictional
resistance between the bottoms of the mat foundations and the gravel
cushion layer
Pile Foundation
Since medium dense to dense fills reef detritus and occasional medium
to hard thin coral ledges are present in the upper subsoils at the
project site short pile foundations may also be used as an alternate
foundation system to support the building colum loads However
high capacity piles of longer lengths are not recommended due to
construction difficulties anticipated in predrilling and pile driving
through the occasional upper coral ledges
Additional borings are recommended to further delineate the
occasional upper coral ledges within the proposed building areas
prior to design of the pile foundations

Twelve inch 12 square precast prestressed concrete piles are
recommended for use
Due to the proposed deep basement excavations the piles would
probably be driven first from the existing grades or above the high
water level prior to basement excavation It is our opinion that the
piles should be carefully aligned during driving to minimize offset
from their design locations at the basement bottoms
A minimum one foot thick gravel cushion of 3B Fine gravel would also
be needed at the excavated basement bottoms to stabilize the
anticipated loose and wet subgrade conditions prior to constructing
the pile caps and concrete placement in the dewatered excavation
area
We recommend that the piles be driven at least 40 feet below the
proposed building basements or about 50 to 60 feet below the existing
grades depending on the finished invert elevations at the bottom of
basement slabs The piles would derive their vertical support from
adhesion between the pile shafts and the medium dense to dense sands
and also partially from end bearing on the underlying subsoils
Some of the piles may encounter refusal and could bear on the
generally medium to hard upper coral ledges which may be present
within the 40 foot depth below the building basements and therefore
may derive their vertical pile support primarily from end bearing on
the ledges
Vertical Pile Capacities
Based on the existing boring data it is estimated that a total
allowable vertical pile capacity of forty 40 tons per pile may be
developed for both types of piles either penetrating at least 40 feet
below the basement bottoms or bearing on the upper ledges The

Pile Uplift Capacities
The vertical uplift capacity of the piles would be developed
primarily from adhesion between the piles and the medium dense to
dense sands It is estimated that the piles may develop a total
allowable vertical uplift capacity of 0 4 ton per foot of the
embedded pile length below the basement bottoms A ground water
level up to 3 feet MSL may be used to compute the buoyant uplifting
forces on the basement structures and to determine the total pile
uplift resistance requirement
Pile Lateral Resistance
If pile foundations are used to resist lateral loads from the
proposed buildings the passive earth pressure around the pile caps
and tie beams may be used in addition to the individual pile lateral
resistance developed for individual 40 foot piles which is estimated
to provide 5 kip resistance To utilize the passive soil resistance
the space around the pile caps or tie beams should be properly
backfilled with select granular material compacted to at least 90
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 An
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth of the
caps beams may be used to calculate the passive soil resistance

Pile Driving
We recommend that a pile driving hammer which can deliver a minimm
of 24 000 foot pounds of energy be used for the pile driving The
40 ton piles should be driven to a minimm pile length of 40 feet
below the proposed building basement bottoms or to a resistance of
about 30 blows per foot for the last foot of penetration
The driving process should be continuous without interruption for
each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be
modified if the driving process is interrupted for more than four 4
hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is within loose soils the
hammer stroke or energy should be carefully controlled so that
excessive tensile stresses are not developed in the pile
The pile driving should be observed by a qualified geotechnical
engineer to see that the piles are driven to the sufficient depth or
into competent bearing materials
In addition to the above recommended additional field exploration a
test pile program is also recommended to aid in the development of a
safe and economical pile foundation design for the proposed
buildings
The test program should be undertaken to fulfill the following
objectives

criteria

Basement Excavation
Based on the preliminary project data available basement excavations
about 15 to 25 feet deep are anticipated for the construction of the
proposed buildings
Excavation Method
The method of excavation and the equipment required should be the
choice of the contractor within practical limits and safety
considerations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered in this preliminary
geotechnical exploration relatively easy excavation is anticipated
for the proposed building basements
Excavation Support
Considering the anticipated depths of basement excavations we
recomend that free standing or cantilevered steel sheetpile walls be
used to support the excavations The steel sheeting should be
installed prior to the basement excavations and maintained tight
against the excavation faces by proper interlocking between
individual sheet pile during construction Properly installed
sheetpile walls are essential to reduce lateral movements of the
adjacent ground behind the sheeting which may cause settlements and
other damages to the existing adjacent structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O
2237 00 Page 12
Due to the anticipated high groundwater level and generally loose
lagoonal soil conditions underlying the basement excavations it is
also essential that the steel sheeting be extended sufficiently deep
below the excavation bottoms to increase the stability of the
excavations and reduce the possibility of a quick condition at the
bottoms
The adjacent ground near the basement excavations should be
continuously monitored for cracks dips or other indications of
movements and possible sloughing of the excavation faces Such
monitoring is particularly critical in areas where existing
structures and utilities are adjacent to the excavations
The design and safety of the sheetpile walls should be the
responsibility of the contractor However it is advised that the
project geotechnical engineer be consulted for the design of the
sheetpile support systems
Due to the anticipated high groundwater table and granular nature of
the subsoils at the project site it is our opinion that dewatering
of the basement excavations will be a critical concern during
construction Therefore a proper and efficient dewatering system is
required to maintain the stability and safety of the excavations
during construction
The method of dewatering should be left open to the contractor
provided adequate stability of the excavations is maintained The
subsoils encountered in the preliminary borings consist basically of
reef detritus and lagoonal deposits within the anticipated depths of
the excavations These granular subsoils are relatively permeable
and where encountered at the excavation bottom will be susceptible
to piping and quick conditions

Site Grading
Based on the relatively level existing grades at the site it is
anticipated that minimal or no site grading will be required for the
proposed site improvements except for the underground basement
excavations Significant demolition work is anticipated to remove the
existing structures
At the onset of earthwork the area within the contract grading
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Any unsuitable
materials such as rubbish vegetation old foundations and other
deleterious materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
The existing ground surface area to receive fill slab or pavement
should be scarified to a minimm depth of six 6 inches below the cleared
grade level moisture conditioned and prevented from drying and
recompacted to a minimum of 95 maximum dry density per ASIM Test Method
D 1557 78
Any loose soft or yielding areas spots encountered at the scarified
subgrade level should be over excavated to in situ firm ground and
replaced with on site sandy gravelly soils or imported non expansive
granular material compacted in maximm 10 inch loose lifts to a minimm of
95 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
We believe that the on site sandy and gravelly subsoils including
the fill crust and coral detritus materials may be used as a source of
fill or backfill material if needed
Fills or backfills should be placed in 10 inch loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 95 maximm dry density per ASIM Test Method
D 1557 78

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from borings Variations of conditions
between the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until construction If variations then
appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations
given in this report
The locations of the borings in the project site were determined by
taping from the existing structures as indicated on the topographic survey
of the project site prepared by Towill Shigeoka Associates Inc dated
Elevations of the borings were determined by
interpolation from elevation points on the map The physical locations
and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the
degree implied by the methods used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
borings depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such
may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on any profiles
or cross sections are based upon interpolation between borings and may not
represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the borings were collected at the times given
on the graphic representations and or in the text of this report These
data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of
this report However it must be noted that fluctuation in water level
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
