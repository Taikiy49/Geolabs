SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Ewa Beach Sewers Section 4 D in the District of Ewa on the Island
of Oahu Hawaii The project site is within an existing residential subdivision
North Road separates the site into two portions The northern portion is bounded by
Kehue Street Kaunolu Street Kauiki Street and Haiamu Street The southern portion is
bounded by Apole Place and Kilaha Street The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of
the excavation sewer pipe installation fill placement and road pavements only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is on the eastern and northern sides of Fort Weaver Road within
an existing residential subdivision in the Ewa Beach area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
Currently the existing subdivision is serviced by cesspool systems It is proposed to
replace the cesspool system with a new sewer system Several alternatives such as
using individual grinders and pumps with small diameter 1 5 inch pressurized lines
conventional gravity sewers and combinations of the two systems are being
considered
In order to obtain general subsurface information to evaluate the soil condition in
this area twenty borings were drilled within the subdivision streets Four borings were
drilled at the southern portion of the site and sixteen borings were drilled at northern
portion Several sewer alignments were being considered for the project at the time this
report was prepared

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating
sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and further south
these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef
formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from 0 feet in the area of
H 1 freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and
serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of
southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time
through the present

Earth Pressure Loads on Sewer Lines
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the friction between the backfill
material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried sewer
pipes we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot
pcf for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 45 Earth forces upon
the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the width
of the trench should be kept to a minimum In addition traffic loads on the buried
pipes should also be considered for the portions of pipes within roadway areas
Pipe Bedding
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the sewer lines The
granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a minimum width of
24 inches
Backfill
In general free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 6 inches above the pipe to provide adequate support around the pipe and to
reduce the need for compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of pipe
damage Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not be allowed
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of general fill material The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in maximum 8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable
future ground subsidence

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest considering the following three basic criteria when selecting a suitable
method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing buildings roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also
provide safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible in allowing
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The contractor must carefully evaluate the potential impact of the dewatering
system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table prior to dewatering
The contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and
evaluate the dewatering system used
The contractor should be solely responsible for the impact and safety of the
dewatering operations His her qualified representative who should be continuously
present on site during dewatering activities will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but
not limited to slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations

condition or softening at the excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering

3 6 Horizontal Directional Drilling Methods
Since the site is an existing subdivision with in place utilities under the roads and
numerous improvements at each private lot installation of the low pressure system
pipes by Horizontal Directional Drillling HDD was considered to reduce inconvenience
to the residents damage to existing improvements and hard excavation through the
coral formation
Horizontal directional drilling HDD is a trenchless steerable installation method
using a multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface
The pilot hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical
cutting or a combination of the two methods Depending on the subsurface conditions
water only or water mixed with bentonite and or polymer can be used as a drilling fluid
in a directional drilling application The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to
accommodate the pipe to be installed The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by
the drilling machine Most HDD systems use slurry to stabilize the walls of the borehole
and to reduce the frictional drag on the pipeline being installed Steering corrections are
made in the subsurface materials by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired
orientation and then advancing the shoe and string without rotation
In general the HDD pilot hole is drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit
angles between 8 and 15 degrees measured from horizontal However steeper entry
angles about 20 degrees may be required to avoid underground utilities and or
obstructions Shallow working pits may be required at each end of the bore to allow
placement of the drill pipe and to allow transition to the conventional pipe trench
installation The product pipe should be installed in one continuous operation preferably
without pause and should protrude a sufficient distance beyond the entry and exit
points to allow proper termination or connection at a later time

available for HDD it may not be practical to install the laterals by HDD and
conventional trenching is probably required Since conventional trenching is required for
the laterals installation of the main lines by HDD would probably not be economically
viable Also the shallow depth of the line would favor installation by conventional
trenching
Due to the existing improvements present i e retaining fence walls trenching for
the laterals would require significant demolition and restoration of these improvements
A possible alternative to trenching under these improvements is to use a displacement
hammer installation driven by pneumatics such as the Grundomat system These
systems use a driving head that is pushed horizontally into the ground by air pressure
The driving head displaces the soil to create a cavity for the pipe installation These
systems are efficient when the surrounding soil is loose to medium dense by may be
difficult in very stiff or hard soil We anticipate that this system would have difficulty
penetrating the hard coral but may be successful in the overburden soils above the
coral

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
20 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 20 to depths of about 15 5 to 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger and
coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 20
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of coral materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 4 0 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 80 It should be noted that RQD does not apply to coral material
However the RQD values of the coral material are included in the boring logs for rough
characterization of the coral material

were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Seven Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on
Plates B 1 and B 2
