Project Considerations 
 We understand that the contaminated soils encountered on the project are to be 
buried in an excavated pit with approximate dimensions of 145 feet wide, 95 feet long, and 
34 feet deep. The excavation side walls of the pit are proposed to be sloped at one 
horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) inclination during the temporary excavation and 
backfilling operations. The excavation pit will be within the perimeter wall footings of the 
new building structure. Based on the borings, it is likely that basalt rock formation will be 
encountered at various depths within the lower portion of the excavation.  
We understand that approximately 5,000 cubic yards of contaminated material is 
expected to be excavated from the first 3 feet of soil beneath the existing grades and 
backfilled and buried within the lower portion of the excavation pit. It is estimated that the

Excavation 
Based on the borings, we anticipate that the excavation pit will likely encounter 
the underlying fill materials consisting of clayey silt/silty clay, sands and gravels. It is 
anticipated that the surface fill materials may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment. Cobbles and boulders may be encountered in the fill material.  In 
the lower portion of the excavation, medium hard to very hard basalt rock formation is 
anticipated.  Chipping and/or the use of hoe-rams to remove the cobbles and boulders, 
and the basalt rock formation to achieve the finished cut subgrade elevation should be 
anticipated.    
The excavated materials should be properly stored to reduce the potential for 
contaminating other fill materials at the project site.  
Backfilling of the Excavation 
As indicated above, we anticipate that basalt rock formation may be encountered 
at the finished cut subgrade level.  Cavities and voids may be encountered within the 
basalt rock formation.  Therefore, we recommend proof-rolling the finished cut subgrade 
with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of six passes to help detect 
and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids at the bottom of the excavation.  The 
finished cut surface should be leveled prior to the proof-rolling. 
The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative.  Yielding areas, loose area, or cavities disclosed during the proof-rolling 
operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials.  
Contaminated Boulder Fill 
For the purpose of discussion in this report, boulders are defined as rock 
fragments between 12 to 36 inches in largest dimension.  We understand that it is desired 
to dispose of the boulders that are encountered within the contaminated soil layer in the 
excavation pit.  The following guidelines should be used for these contaminated boulders. 
1. The contaminated boulders should be placed at the bottom of the excavation 
pit. 
2. Prior to placement of the boulders, the finished cut subgrade should be 
prepared as previously recommended. 
3. Some amount of crushing with a hoe-ram or heavy equipment may be 
necessary to reduce the boulders to acceptable sizes.  Boulders greater than 
36 inches should be reduced in size.

6. Boulder fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding 60 inches.  Spreading and

Contaminated Soil Fill 
In general, the backfill of the contaminated soils should follow the 
recommendations previously provided in the referenced geotechnical report and this 
addendum. The contaminated soil fills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 
percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Mechanical 
compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials. Compaction 
efforts by water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed. 
Backfills placed within the excavation against the excavation sidewalls not 
consisting of basalt rock formation should be keyed and benched into the temporary 
excavated sidewalls. The backfilling operations should start at the lowest point and 
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill 
placement recommendations.

Anticipated Ground Settlement 
As indicated previously, the excavation and backfill depth will be about 34 feet 
thick from the proposed bottom of the excavation to the finish floor of the building and 
within the perimeter wall footings of the new structure. Therefore, we believe that due to 
the varying ground conditions between the interior shallow footings and the exterior wall 
footings, the differential settlement may occur. In order to reduce the effects of the 
differential settlement, we recommend capping the deep excavation with a minimum of 
36-inches of non-expansive select granular fill measured from the bottom of building 
footings. The select granular fill should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should have a California 
Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The material also should contain between 10 
and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. Imported fill materials should be 
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for 
the intended use. The capping should extend beneath the entire new building structure 
and to two feet beyond the exterior footings. 
We believe that significant ground settlements likely will occur due to the 
placement of backfilled clayey contaminated soils, the compacted general fills, the select 
capping fill, and the construction of the new structures over the deep fills. 
In general, we anticipate ground settlements of about 3 to 4 inches would occur in 
areas with fills of up to about 34 feet thick. Based on our analyses, we believe that about 
80 to 90 percent of the estimated total settlements should occur in a period of about 3 to 4 
weeks after the placement of the capping fill. Due to the anticipated ground settlements, 
we recommend implementing a settlement monitoring program to evaluate the magnitude 
and rates of settlements.  
Based on the above discussions, we recommend conducting a settlement 
monitoring program for at least 4 to 8 weeks to monitor the magnitude and rate of ground 
settlements prior to construction of improvements (such as underground utility lines, 
structures, and pavements) in and on the fills and backfills. To reduce the impact of the 
appreciable ground settlements caused by consolidation of the contaminated soil placed 
under the new building structure, construction of the on-grade building structures, utility 
lines, and pavements should be delayed as long as possible until after the majority of the 
settlements have occurred. 
It should be noted that residual settlements (post-construction settlements) on the 
order of about 1 inch may still occur over a long period after completion of construction. 
Therefore, the new fills should be placed as early as possible and the fills should be 
allowed to settle for as long as the construction schedule allows to reduce the amount of 
residual settlements after completion of construction.

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our subsurface exploration, the project site is generally underlain by a 
surface fill layer about 2 to 13 feet thick followed by medium dense to dense sands and 
gravels with clay to about 13 to 29 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the sands 
and gravels with clay, basalt rock formation was encountered extending to 30.5 feet, the 
maximum depth explored. The surface fill material consisted of stiff to very stiff gravelly clay 
and clayey silts.  The basalt rock formation was hard and was interbedded with a clinker 
layer.  We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of the field 
exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads 
anticipated, we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous 
footings may be used to support the proposed building structure.  Due to the variability of 
the underlying fill soils and to provide uniform bearing support for the new structure, the 
subgrade material below the bottoms of the footings and building slabs-on-grade should be 
over-excavated a minimum of 36 inches and replaced with fill materials compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  The over-excavation should extend at least 
3 feet beyond the edge of the new building footprint.  Prior to backfill placement, the finished 
subgrade of the over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, moisture-
conditioned to near optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
The excavated on-site materials may be used as backfill material for the over-
excavation provided the on-site materials do not contain cobbles and boulders, and 
expansive clay soils.  In addition, select granular fill material may be used as backfill 
material. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 psf may be utilized for the design of 
shallow foundations bearing on the minimum 36-inch re-compacted on-site fill material or 
compacted select granular fill material. In general, the bottom of footings should be 
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), 
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No. 3 Fine 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. Where the slabs will be subjected to 
machinery vibration, such as the mechanical room, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer 
of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer.

performed for the proposed Life Sciences Building project, located at the University of 
Hawaii at Manoa on Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity

recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this

Project Considerations

a new classroom and laboratory building at the current Henke Hall location in the central 
area of the Manoa campus on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The new building planned 
consists of a three-story concrete and steel framed structure designed for interdisciplinary 
collaboration to support teaching and research laboratories, laboratory support services 
and office spaces.  The new facility is required to replace the existing obsolete and 
unsuitable Snyder Hall and will be located at the site of the existing Henke Hall Building 
at the east end gateway to McCarthy Mall.

which is bordered by Moore Hall to the north, Hamilton Library to the west, Keller Hall and 
Kennedy Theater to the south, and East-West Road on the east as shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2.  Currently, the site is relatively flat with grass and landscaping around the 
existing building structures.

on the information provided and current site conditions, we envision that some site 
grading of up to 5 feet will be required for the new building.  Based on the existing site

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano reached 
the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the northwest 
to southeast trending rift zones. These lavas consist of interbedded pahoehoe and 
a’a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form. “A’a” is the Hawaiian term for 
basaltic lava flow typified by a rough, jagged, spinose, clinkery surface. “Pahoehoe” is the 
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth, billowy or ropy surfaces. After 
the cessation of the main shield-building phase of Koolau Volcano, there was a long 
period of erosion. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Along the crest of the ridge along the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley, there are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. 
When these vents were active, one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down the 
westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor. The lava flows ponded and 
filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley. The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it 
cooled forming dense massive rock. The new lava flows from these vents blocked Manoa 
Stream and forced the stream to the east between the East-West Center and Waahila 
Rise. These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili District, where the basalt rocks were 
quarried in the past. Reef limestone related to the Waimanalo stand of the sea underlies 
the basalt at the former Moiliili Quarry. Man-made fills were placed in the area and brought 
the project site to its current conditions.

2.4.1 Earthquake and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island 
of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small 
that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the 
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are

The Diamond Head Fault feature is believed to extend northeasterly away from the

masonry building walls, and a broken underground water main. In addition, some 
areas on the Island of Oahu, including the Tantalus, Iwilei, and Tripler areas, reported 
more intense ground shaking, severe enough to have cracked reinforced concrete.

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the 2006 International Building Code, the project site may be subject to 
seismic activity and should be evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore 
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In 
this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibrations (such 
as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing 
an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to 
dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) 
of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up 
could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to 
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires 
mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not 
confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands 
and loose silts with little cohesion. It generally is acknowledged that liquefaction will 
not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the ground surface. In 
deeper deposits, the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent 
liquefaction from occurring. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be constructed for the new 
classroom building. Surface fills consisting of gravelly clays and silts are present at the 
ground surface at the project site. In addition, we anticipate that the consistency of the 
underlying fill materials may vary.  The near-surface clayey fills encountered may exhibit 
a slight to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To 
reduce the potential of structural distress to the lightly loaded slabs, we recommend over-
excavating the subgrade material below the slabs a minimum of 36 inches and replacing 
with fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The over-
excavation should extend a minimum of 3 feet laterally beyond the edge of the slab. 
Prior to placing fill material, the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  The excavated on-site 
materials may be used as backfill material for the slab over-excavation provided the 
on-site materials do not contain cobbles and boulders, and expansive clay soils.  In 
addition, select granular fill material may be used as backfill material. 
 Slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend 
thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks. Saturation and subsequent yielding of 
the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-
excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill.

over-excavated to expose firm materials, and the resulting excavation should be 
backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should 
be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.

After clearing, demolition, and over excavation, the excavated subgrades should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-
compacted fill. The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions 
should be evaluated by our field representative.

3.4.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness,

materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in 
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Mechanical compaction equipment should be used 
to compact the backfill materials. Compaction efforts by water tamping, jetting, or 
ponding should not be allowed.

3.5 
Utility Trenches 
We envision that new underground utility connections will be required for the 
project development. It is anticipated that most of the trenches for the utility lines will be 
excavated in the surface fills and/or dense gravel fills anticipated at the project site. 
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and 
backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities. The contractor should determine the 
method and equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and 
safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable 
federal, state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for the 
trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. 
Free-draining granular materials such as No. 3B Fine gravel also should be used for the 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support 
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the 
potential for pipe damage. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the 
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material and placement could result 
in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of approved on-site soils 
(maximum particle size of 3 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be 
placed in maximum 8-inch level loose thickness, and mechanically compacted to no less 
than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence. Where trenches are located in paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench 
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent 
relative compaction. Where the utility line crosses beneath the footings, the utility pipes 
should be concrete jacketed.

Drainage 
Finished grades outside the new structure should be sloped to shed water away 
from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the 
structure. It also is advised to install gutter systems around the new structure and to divert 
discharge away from the slab and foundation areas. Excessive landscape watering near

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
eight exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101, 101A, 102, 103, and 201 through 
204, extending to depths of about 5 to 30.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In 
addition, Boring No. 103A was drilled to a depth of about 10 feet below the existing ground 
surface to perform infiltration testing only.  Boring Nos. 201 through 204 were performed 
subsequent to the demolition of the existing building structures. The borings were drilled 
with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid-stem continuous flight augers 
and coring tools. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-8. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  Rock cores were

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the total core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
The fines content of the samples tested (percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve) are 
indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of 
the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plates B-3 thru B-5. 
Two one-inch ring swell tests were performed on a remolded sample and a 
relatively undisturbed sample to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. 
The test results are summarized on Plate B-6. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the silty and 
clayey soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Six Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock 
formation encountered.  The test results are presented on Plate B-9.
