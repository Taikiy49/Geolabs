2 1 Regional Geology
In general two major volcanoes form the base of the Island of Maui The two major
volcanoes include the older West Maui Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui
also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently
sloping plain located between these two volcanoes The project site is located at the
northern half of this gently sloping plain as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on West
Maui It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui
Haleakala The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and rock
masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials are transported downslope toward
the Isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the stream in
the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments
are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this the
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists
of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the
erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the materials
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In

Underlying the alluvial deposits are overlapping lavas from the West Maui and East
Maui Volcanoes The bulk of the Haleakala Shield Volcano was built during the late
Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the
Honomanu Volcanic Series During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the lavas
changed to very hard thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition These lavas have
been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series Typically the basalt consists of thinly to thickly
bedded a a and pahoehoe type flows

Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by very stiff to hard silty
and sandy soils overlying extremely to highly weathered basalt rock formation extending to
the maximum depth explored of approximately 13 feet below the existing ground surface
The thickness of the silty and sandy soils ranged from about 1 to 8 feet in the borings
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on our field exploration and the laboratory tests we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to support
the structures for the proposed Central Maui Landfill facilities and infrastructure In
addition laboratory tests performed on the near surface soils indicate that the on site silty
soils exhibit a slight potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture
fluctuations In order to reduce the potential for structural distress to future slabs on grade
resulting from the shrinking swelling of the on site silty soils we recommend that concrete
slabs on grade be supported on at least 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill
materials In addition the subgrade soils below the non expansive select granular fill
materials should be properly prepared and kept moist until covered by the concrete slab
Based on the percolation test conducted on the near surface soils the percolation
rate in the near surface silty and sandy materials is about 48 minutes per inch According
to the Manual of Septic Tank Practice prepared by the U S Department of Health
Education and Welfare the on site silty and sandy materials would be suitable for the
siting of the absorption system based on the percolation test conducted
Based on the grading plan provided we anticipate that site grading for this project
will involve cuts on the order of approximately 16 feet and fills on the order of about 6 feet
to achieve the design finished grades It should be noted that the site is underlain by
extremely to highly weathered basalt rock formation at shallow depths based on our field

3 1 Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site
we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
new structures planned for the project An allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of foundations bearing on the
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
The near surface soils at the site appear to be dry friable and susceptible to
erosion Therefore we recommend that the footings for the structures be embedded a
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade If foundations are located next to
utility trenches or easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench As an alternative the
footings may be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
In general the bottom of footing excavations should be compacted to a firm and
unyielding condition prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft and or
loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations the soft and or
loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation to remove the soft and or loose materials should be backfilled with select
granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
footing may be extended deeper to bear directly on the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements of the footings to be less than 1 inch

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the proposed new
structures As mentioned previously the laboratory tests performed selected soil samples
indicate that the silty soils encountered in our borings exhibit a slight potential for
shrink swell when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural
distress resulting from the swelling of the slightly expansive soils we recommend that a
layer of non expansive select granular fill materials capping fill be provided for a
minimum depth of 12 inches below the building slab The capping fill should also extend
beyond the perimeter of the building slab a minimum of 2 feet or to the edge of the
perimeter sidewalk surrounding the building
The non expansive select granular fill material required under the slab areas should
consist of imported granular materials such as select borrow crushed coral or basalt
gravel The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater
than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills including the non expansive select granular fill below
the slabs for the project should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement for the upper 8 inches of fills below the pavements should be
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557
Where fills are composed of cobbles conventional compaction testing may not be
practicable Therefore a testing program to evaluate the number of passes of a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted
at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a
large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons traveling at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at
least six times in order to achieve 90 percent relative compaction

also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fills to be placed on existing slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against
sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face
In general slope benches may be omitted on this project for cut and fill slopes up to
25 feet in vertical height Appropriate measures for cut and fill slopes with a vertical
height of 25 feet or greater should be considered to reduce the velocities of the
surface water runoff and potential erosion of the slope face In general water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and appropriate slope planting or

other erosion control measures should be provided as soon as possible to reduce
the potential for significant erosion of the finished slopes
3 5 Septic Tank
We understand that a septic tank with an associated leach field is planned for
on site sewage treatment and disposal within the proposed new facilities and infrastructure
site Therefore a field percolation test was conducted to obtain data pertaining to the
preliminary design of the sewage disposal system One field percolation test was
performed near the location of Boring No 3 at the project site as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The field percolation test was performed in general accordance with the State of
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State s Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The test was performed by drilling a 3 inch
diameter borehole to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Approximately 2 inches of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole to protect
the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments During testing the hole was carefully
filled with water to a minimum depth of 12 inches above the gravel The time intervals and
water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of the
percolation test was based Based on the percolation test conducted the percolation rate
is on the order of approximately 48 minutes per inch at the test location The results of the
percolation test are presented on Plate 3
Based on the Manual of Septic Tank Practice prepared by the U S Department of
Health Education and Welfare the on site silty and sandy soils with the percolation rate
obtained from the test would be suitable for the siting of the absorption system at the site
3 6 Pavement Design
We understand that asphaltic concrete pavements will be required for the access
and exit roadways and the parking facilities In general we anticipate that the vehicle
loading for the project will consist primarily of passenger vehicles pick up trucks and
some heavy trucks Based on our laboratory test the near surface silty and sandy soils

appears to have a CBR value of about 19 with a swell value of about 1 2 percent Based
on the available CBR value and the anticipated traffic loading we recommend the
following flexible pavement section be used for the preliminary pavement design
3 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
15 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The subgrade soils below the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction CBR tests and or field observations should be
performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above
design section is adequate The aggregate base course should consist of crushed
basaltic aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavements Surface water ponding should not
be allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs
extend a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil to reduce the potential for
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section
3 7 Utility Trenches
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended
under the pipes for support Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the
pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
W O INC Page 18
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction
3 8 Drainage
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water
infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength In addition the foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

borings extending to depths of about 5 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface The

solid stem augers

accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
