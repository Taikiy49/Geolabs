SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The project site is underlain by medium dense to dense granular fill material to a 
depth of approximately 2.5 to 4.5 feet below the existing ground elevation.  Below this 
layer, a layer of gravelly silt extends to a depth of about 4.5 to 8.0 feet.  Highly weathered 
coral limestone formation was then encountered to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Standard details for the design of traffic signal light foundations are provided by the 
Guam Department of Public works.  In this document, the recommended sizes of 
cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts are given for traffic signal pole arm lengths of 25, 35, 
45, and 55 feet.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and the 
structural load demands on the building foundation provided by Parsons Brinckerhoff Inc., 
we concur with the foundations recommended by the standard details.  Based on our 
analysis, we recommend the following drilled shaft foundations. 
25 foot pole arm: Diameter of 36-inch and embedment depth of 8.5 feet 
35 foot pole arm: Diameter of 36-inch and embedment depth of 10 feet 
45 foot pole arm: Diameter of 36-inch and embedment depth of 11.5 feet 
55 foot pole arm: Diameter of 36-inch and embedment depth of 13.5 feet 
These recommended drilled shaft dimensions will result in less than 0.6 inches of 
lateral deflection.  The foundations were also found to be adequate to support the axial 
loads imposed by the traffic signal poles. The text of this report should be referred to for 
detailed discussion and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Route 8, Canada-Toto Traffic Signal project in the Barrigada area on 
the Island of Guam of the Mariana Islands. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project is located on existing public right-of-way and involves the design of 
improvements at the Route 8/Canada-Toto Loop Road intersection. Based on the 
information provided, the project consists of constructing a new signalized intersection 
of these two thoroughfares and requires the installation of three traffic signal poles.   
We understand the poles will be supported by cast-in-place drilled shafts or 
spread footings.  The type of foundation is dependent on the existence of underground 
or overhead utilities at the planned traffic signal pole locations. We understand that the 
loading will generally be light, with lateral wind loads governing the foundation design. 
1.2

recommendations for the design of the proposed mast arm traffic poles. The work was 
performed in general accordance with the scope of services outlined in our fee proposal 
dated December 6, 2012. Generally, the scope of work for this exploration included the 
following tasks and work efforts:

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island 
archipelago, which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming 
the Mariana Trench. The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches, which extend from 
Japan to the Palau Islands and include the Japan Trench, Mariana Trench, Yap Trench, 
and Palau Trench. 
The Island of Guam can be divided broadly into three geologic provinces: 
the southern mountains, the central mountains, and the northern plateau. The island 
formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas. 
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic 
rocks and conglomerates, tuffs, and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene Epochs. 
Lava flows and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority 
of the southern one-third of the island. The plateaus of the northern portion of the island 
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level. During the 
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs, a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an 
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits. Tectonic activity has lifted these 
coral-algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau. 
The central portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic 
materials of the southern mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau. 
The project site at Barrigada is situated in the central portion of the island. Geology 
maps from the Water and Environmental Research Institute of the Western Pacific 
(WERI) indicate that the project site is underlain by the Mariana Limestone formation 
and is situated within the transition zones of geologic members Detrital Facies and 
Hagatna Argillaceous Member. As defined by WERI, Detrital Facies consists of friable 
to well-cemented coarse to fine grained, generally porous and cavernous, white detrital 
limestone, mostly of lagoonal origin. The Hagatna Argillaceous Member consists of 
coarse to fine-grained pale-yellow, tan, or brown fossiliferous detrital limestone

2.2 
Site Description 
The project is located on existing public right-of-way and involves the design of 
improvements at the Route 8/Canada-Toto Loop Road intersection on the Island of 
Guam of the Marianas Islands. The proposed traffic signal mast arm poles will be 
located at the northwest, northeast, and south corners of the intersection. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, each extending to a depth of about 
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a layer of fill material consisting of medium 
dense to very dense silty gravel underlain by medium stiff to stiff gravelly silt with some 
sand.  Highly weathered coral limestone formation was encountered in Boring Nos. 1 
and 2 at approximately 4.5 feet and 8 feet, respectively, and extended to the maximum 
depth explored of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The coral 
limestone formation was observed to be medium hard and broke down to medium 
dense silty coral gravel with some sand when sampled using a 3-inch outer diameter 
Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. 
Groundwater was not encountered in either of the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that the water levels at the project site may be influenced 
by seasonal precipitation, run-off, storm surge conditions, and other factors.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a layer of medium dense granular fill 
material underlain by a medium stiff to stiff layer of gravelly silt.  Medium hard limestone 
formation was encountered and observed to extend to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  We did not encounter 
groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
The diameter and length of the drilled shaft foundations depend primarily on the 
length of the traffic signal pole arm.  Four standard sizes are given in the standard 
details provided by the Guam Department of Public Works: 25, 35, 45, and 55-foot long 
arms.  The recommended drilled shafts were analyzed using the subsurface soil profiles 
observed during our field exploration. The soil samples were obtained with 3-inch O.D. 
modified California samplers with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow 
counts to drive the sampler were converted to “N” values using a conversion factor of 
0.55. This conversion factor is based on our experience and review of several 
references (Burmister, 1948 and LaCroix & Horn, 1973). Based on the converted “N” 
values from the two borings, the subsurface soil conditions should be considered “Soft 
Soil” conditions. Using the loading conditions provided by Parsons Brinckerhoff Inc., we 
confirmed the adequacy of the standard drilled shaft sizes.  A summary of the 
recommended cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft sizes are presented below.

3.1.3 Foundation Settlements 
Total settlements of the drilled shafts embedded in the limestone formation as 
recommended herein should be less than about 0.5 inches. We believe the 
foundation settlements are elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
3.1.4 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
In general, the performance of drilled shafts depends upon the contractor's method 
of installation and construction procedures. The following conditions would have an 
effect on the quality of the drilled shaft foundations. 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on the 
friction between the shaft and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper construction 
techniques especially during the drilling operations are important. The contractor 
should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the 
drilled holes. Therefore, observation of the drilled shaft installation operations by a 
Geolabs field representative is recommended to confirm the assumed subsurface 
conditions. 
As mentioned previously, highly weathered limestone formation was encountered 
at the project site, generally at a depth of about 4.5 to 8 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Therefore, some hard drilling conditions into the limestone 
formation likely will be encountered at the project site and should be expected. The 
drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill 
through the hard limestone formation, where encountered in the subsurface.

Spread Footing Foundations 
Due to overhead constraints, shallow spread footing foundations may be required 
at selected locations. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square 
foot (psf) may be used for the design of footings bearing on compacted structural fill or 
stiff in-situ materials. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by 50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the 
lowest adjacent exterior grade. In addition, foundations next to other foundations, utility 
trenches, easements or other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility 
trench, or the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility 
lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement. 
The dead weight of the structure and the foundation may be used to provide the 
required uplift load capacity. Additionally, the weight of the backfill material placed 
above the foundations may be used to provide resistance to uplift loads, if applicable. A 
unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) may be used for the on-site compacted 
materials. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated until dense materials are exposed in the footing excavation. 
The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may 
extend down to bear directly on the underlying competent material. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than 
1 inch.

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.4 may be used for footings bearing on structural fill materials or stiff in-situ 
soil. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent 
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) assuming that the 
soils around the footings are well compacted. The passive pressure resistance in the 
upper 12 inches of the soil should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the Site Plan transmitted by AECOM 
on July 16, 2013. The field boring locations and elevations should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil and/or rock types and, as such, may 
denote a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the 
times shown on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. 
The data has been reviewed and interpretations have been made to formulate this 
report. However, it must be noted that water level  fluctuation may occur due to variation 
in tides, rainfall, perched groundwater conditions, groundwater withdrawal, and other 
factors.

Signal project in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, each extending to a depth of about 
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill rigs equipped 
with continuous flight augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. The blow counts were not 
corrected for overburden pressures.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & 136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1.
