PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The projéct involves repair of the existing Waiohinu Transfer Station Facility in
the Waiohinu/Naalehu area in the District of Kau on the Island of Hawaii. The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
Accentuated distress of a portion of the refuse collection structure (timber wall retaining
structure) was reported after the strong October 2006 earthquakes.
We understand that it is desired to evaluate the existing timber wall distress from
a geotechnical engineering perspective for immediate safety concerns and potential
future failure. Because the Waiohinu transfer station facility will be completely
re-constructed at a later date, it is desired to evaluate various interim repair alternatives
to maintain the safe operation of the facility until reconstruction.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The Waiohinu Transfer Station (TMK: 9-5-05: 01) is on Kaulia Road in the
Waiohinu area of the District of Kau on the Island of Hawaii. The project site is in a
broad gently sloping topographic basin likely underlain by silty volcanic ash soils
overlying basaltic rock formation. We understand that some earth fills have been placed
at various locations around the vicinity of the transfer station.
The transfer station generally consists of an elevated refuse collection concrete
pad adjacent to a drop chute and a lower elevation refuse receiving area (as shown on
Photograph No. 1). The elevated pad is bounded by a gently sloped embankment on
the southern perimeter and southwesterly-facing retaining structures. The concrete pad
is cantilevered over the existing retaining timber wall.
The retaining structure consists of a northern section of large drystack boulders
(as shown on Photograph No. 2), a central section of timber retaining wall, and a
southern section of timber (as shown on Photograph No. 3) and other large drystacked
boulders with rip-rap facing near the interface of timber wall and larger drystacked
boulders (as shown on Photograph No. 4). The retaining system provides grade
separation of about 14 feet between the upper pad and the lower driveway.
Based on our observations, the timber retaining wall consists of about eleven to
twelve horizontally stacked timbers (typically 12 inches in diameter) that are bolted to
about seven vertical timber columns for support. The vertical timber columns (reportedly
embedded about 5 feet) are tied-back at the upper portion to buried dead man anchors
consisting of timber poles (as shown on Photograph No. 5).

The central section of timber retaining wall below the refuse collection concrete
pad has been affected by a previous fire and may be compromised to some degree.
The cantilever concrete structure was constructed after the fire incident. We understand
that the design of the cantilever concrete structure was to relieve the timber retaining
wall off the vehicle loadings.
The southern section of timber retaining wall has experienced approximately
6 inches of lateral deflection at the top of the wall (as shown on Photograph No. 6). One
of the vertical timber columns was rotted and hollow at least about 3 feet down from the
exposed top of the column (as shown on Photograph No. 7). The top two rows of the
horizontal timbers were observed to be separated from the vertical timber columns.
Ground settlement on the order of about 6 inches was observed at top of the
southern section of timber retaining wall after the strong earthquakes in October 2006
(as shown on Photograph No. 8). The settlement areas were about 5 by 6 feet in plan
dimension (as shown on Photograph No. 9). Some backfill materials were observed to
be eroded out from the gaps between the horizontal timbers (as shown on
Photograph No. 10).
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field observation described above, we believe that backfill
materials behind the southern section of timber retaining walls have experienced
densification during the strong earthquakes in October 2006. The densification resulted
in ground settlement observed on top of the timber retaining wall. The densification may
also cause or accelerate lateral deformation of the southern section of timber retaining
walls.
As a short-term remediation, the rotted and hollow timber columns may be
replaced by a steel soldier pile. A wire tieback anchor may be installed to support the
soldier pile. The gap between the horizontal timbers may be filled with shotcrete
associated with weep holes to minimize additional soil erosion.
In lieu of wire tieback anchor, two steel soldier piles may be installed to provide
sufficient lateral support to the timber retaining wall.
For long-term remediation, backfill materials behind the timber retaining wall may
be removed and backfilled with Control Low Strength Material (CLSM) to relief lateral
earth pressure off of the timber retaining structure.
It should be noted that the above-mentioned remediation is based on the existing
conditions observed and information provided. The as-built information of the existing
timber retaining wall and cantilever concrete structure were not available at the time of
this report preparation. Details of remediation methods and design parameters are
pending further review of as-built drawings and subsurface investigation.

LIMITATIONS
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based upon visual
observations made during our site visit. Variations of subsurface conditions may occur,
and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until actual
borings are conducted.
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Engineering Partners, Inc.
and the County of Hawaii Department of Environmental Management, and _ their
sub-consultants for specific application to the Waiohinu Transfer Station Repair project
in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and
practices. No warranty is expressed or implied.
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design
engineers in the planning and conceptual design of this project. Therefore, this report
does not contain sufficient data, or the proper information, to serve as the basis for
actual design, preparation of construction cost estimates or contract bidding. A design
engineer or contractor wishing to design or bid on this project should retain a competent
geotechnical engineer to assist in the interpretation of this report and/or in the
performance of site-specific exploration for design/bid estimating purposes.
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated subsurface conditions are
commonly encountered. Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as soft deposits, hard
layers, loose fills, or perched groundwater, may occur in localized areas and may
require additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction
delays) to attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund
is recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs.
This geotechnical engineering site reconnaissance conducted at the project site
was not intended to investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials existing
at the project site. It should be noted that the equipment, techniques, and personnel
used to conduct a geo-environmental exploration differ substantially from those applied
in geotechnical engineering.
