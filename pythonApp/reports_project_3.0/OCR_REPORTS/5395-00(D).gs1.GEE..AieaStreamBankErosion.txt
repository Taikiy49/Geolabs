SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our exploratory borings drilled at the proposed soil nailed slope site at Aiea Stream
generally encountered a surface fill layer consisting of medium dense gravel and sand
and medium stiff to very stiff clayey silt with sand and gravel The fill layer was about
1 foot thick The fill layer was underlain by an alluvial deposit to the maximum depth
explored of 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface The alluvial deposit generally
consisted of stiff to very hard clay and silt with sand gravel cobbles and boulders We
encountered groundwater in the borings at depths about 43 and 49 feet below the existing
ground surface at the time of our field exploration The groundwater levels encountered
correspond to elevations from about 19 3 and 13 feet Mean Sea Level
Several remediation alternatives were developed for the stream bank erosion
consisting of the installation of soil nails and shotcrete facing on the slope installation of a
U shaped concrete channel and extending the existing concrete box culvert structure
We understand that the soil nails and shotcrete facing method of slope remediation is
desired
The soil nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the slope A shotcrete facing is applied to the face of
the reinforced slope The soil nailed slope will have a finished slope of about 1H 1V to
inclination with slope heights of about 18 feet
Based on our soil nail analysis we recommend dividing the soil nailed slope into
five sections due to the loading conditions and slope geometry The soil nails should be
installed by drilling an 8 inch diameter hole with an inclination of approximately 15 degrees
from horizontal The soil nail bars should consist of ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar
with minimum bar diameter of 1 25 inches The recommended nail lengths range from
15 to 35 feet Because of the corrosive subsoil conditions we recommend using epoxy
coated bars surrounded by cement grout or sand cement mixture with minimum 28 day
compressive strength of 4 000 psi
It should be noted that potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered
during the installation of the soil nails due to the presence of cobbles and boulders within
the alluvial soils and hard basalt rock encountered in the borings drilled at the slope site
The contractor should anticipate using a temporary casing to maintain an open hole for the
soil nail installation In addition the contractor should anticipate the presence of tree roots
since several trees were observed along and near the top of the slope at the site

Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Interstate Route H 1 Aiea Stream Bank Erosion project in
Aiea on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of the soil
nailed slope only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The proposed erosion control project is along Aiea Stream in Aiea on the Island
of Oahu Hawaii The project site is adjacent to the Aiea Shopping Center and at the
end of Laka Place The project involves remediation measures for the continued
erosion problem along the stream bank adjacent to the shopping center
An initial study was performed to develop remedial measures for the project The
remedial measures considered included the use of soil nailing with shotcrete facing
installation of a U shaped concrete channel and installation of a box culvert structure
The remedial measure utilizing soil nailing with shotcrete facing was selected as the
preferred remedial measure for the project

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of
lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When
the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits

When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also causes induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material that is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
The project site is on the southwestern flank of the Koolau Range The Koolau
Range is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava from the Koolau
Volcanic Series At the project site the basaltic rock has been overlain with alluvial
deposits transported along Aiea Stream

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 The borings extended to depths ranging from
about 46 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings generally encountered a surface fill layer with thickness of about
1 foot The fill layer consisted of medium dense gravel and sand and medium stiff to
very stiff clayey silt with sand and gravel The fill layer was underlain by an alluvial
deposit to the maximum depth explored of 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The alluvial deposit generally consisted of stiff to very hard clay and silt with sand
gravel cobbles and boulders In one of the borings medium hard to very hard basalt
with clinker layer was encountered below the alluvium at about 33 feet below the
existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of about 43 and
49 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration The
groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to elevations from about
19 3 and 13 feet MSL The groundwater levels will likely vary in response to the
water level in the stream In addition groundwater levels may also be influenced by
seasonal precipitation and other factors

Soil Nailed Slope
The soil nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the slope A shotcrete facing is generally applied to
the face of the reinforced slope The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and
shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the slope The reinforced soil
mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing We
understand that the face of the soil nailed slope system will have a finished slope of
about 1H 1V to inclination with a slope height of about 18 feet A typical cross
section of the soil nailed slope system is presented on Plate 3
We understand that the soil nails installed may be capped with sculpture
shotcrete to simulate the natural rock appearance
Construction of the permanent soil nailed slope system should be performed by a
specialty contractor experienced in the construction of soil nailed slope and or soil nail
retaining walls Due to the specialized nature of the soil nailed slope construction a
Geolabs representative should be present to observe the geotechnical aspects of the
soil nailed slope and testing of the soil nails

Soil Nail Testing
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nailed slope we
recommend performing a minimum of two pre production verification tests on
sacrificial soil nails In addition proof tests should be performed on at least
10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond
stresses used in the design

The verification tests should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent
of the design load In addition the load at every loading increment should be held
for at least 10 minutes and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes The
proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the
design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes until stable The
test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nailed slope provided the nail
satisfy the test criteria Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are integral
parts of the design of the soil nailed slope system Therefore a Geolabs
representative should observe the pullout tests and creep tests
Shotcrete Facing
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman Prior to
production shotcreting it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete
be constructed for inspection We understand that the bottom key for the shotcrete
facing will be constructed with shotcrete The bottom key should extend beyond the
anticipated design scour level
Drainage
The soil nailed slope should be well drained to reduce the potential for the build up
of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system for the soil nailed slope would
consist of 2 foot wide strips of a prefabricated drainage product such as Miradrain
or Enkadrain placed on the face of the slope between the soil nails The
prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top to the base of the slope
and should be hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall as
shown on Plate 3

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths
ranging from about 46 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled using a track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring
tools The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend
Plate A 0 1 Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the borings are
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 1
Six direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 2
through B 7
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 7012 Method C were performed
on rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the underlying
basalt rock formation The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depth
Two sets of and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test results
are presented on Plate B 8
