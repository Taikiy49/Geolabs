INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Takemoto residence at 4423 Kahala Avenue in the Kahala
area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and recommendations resulting from our field
exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These recommendations are
intended for the design of foundation site grading and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
The proposed project site is located at 4423 Kahala Avenue in the Kahala area of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The subject lot was previously occupied by a
two story residence which was demolished prior to our field exploration
It is proposed to construct a new two story residence at the subject site Details of
the proposed project were not available at the time this report was prepared However
we anticipate that the proposed new residence will be a slab on grade structure Based
on the preliminary grading plan we understand that the existing grade will be raised
approximately 1 to 2 feet at the proposed house location Therefore site grading for the
proposed construction will involve minimal cuts and fills on the order of about 2 feet In
addition we understand that new access driveways swimming pool tennis court utility
lines and other site improvements are also planned for the project

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 5
at correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials

Based on our field exploration the proposed project site is underlain by medium
dense to dense sand over loose to very dense silty sand extending to the maximum
depths drilled of about 16 5 and 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface Based on
our field exploration and laboratory testing we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils
and or compacted engineered fills may be used to support the proposed new residence
We anticipate that site grading will involve minimal cuts and fills on the order of about

Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we recommend that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils and or compacted engineered fills be used for support of the
proposed new residence An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per
square foot p s f may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the compacted
subgrade This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one
third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces The bottom of
footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent ground
level
We understand that new fills less than 2 feet thick will be required to attain the
design grade at the site Due to the granular nature of the subsoils we believe that
ground settlements would be small and would occur rapidly Where new structures and
pavements are located on fills greater than 2 feet above the existing ground surface
Geolabs Hawaii should be consulted to reevaluate the site conditions and to provide
additional design recommendations as appropriate
Footing subgrades should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction If loose and or soft soils
are observed at the footing subgrade level they should be over excavated to expose firm
ground and the resulting over excavation should be backfilled with open graded material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or lean concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 8
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
If the foundation is designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total footing settlements under the anticipated design loads are estimated to be on
the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements of approximately inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the
soil around the footings is well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for
foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of friction of
0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted subgrade soils
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed new
residence Based on the preliminary grading plan provided we anticipate that fills of up
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 9
to about 2 feet relative to the existing ground surface will be required to attain the design
grade for the proposed structure
Prior to the filling operation the subgrade to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction In general the fill material
needed to raise the grades to the finish subgrade may consist of the on site sandy soils
and or imported non expansive granular material The fill material should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
For more uniform support of interior slabs we recommend that the concrete slab
on grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch layer of cushion fill The cushion fill layer
should consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or basalt termite barrier BTB which will also serve as a capillary
moisture break The slab cushion fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
scarification and recompaction of the existing subgrade soils as recommended above
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration of
moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform curing of the
slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane we recommend that a
2 inch layer of moist fine sand or BTB be placed above the membrane prior to placing the
slab concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to placing concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 10
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slab we recommend that the slab be supported on a 6 inch layer of base course
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in lieu of the 4 inch cushion
fill and 2 inch sand layers The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
The subgrade should also be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction For design of structural slabs a modulus of
subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted
base course The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
If exterior concrete walkways are planned for the proposed project we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel be provided below the
concrete walkway slab for more uniform support The subgrade below the concrete
walkway should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Expansion joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with
construction joints at right angle intersections
Swimming Pool
Based on the information provided we anticipate that a swimming pool is also
planned for the proposed residence The walls of the swimming pool should be designed
as retaining walls to resist lateral earth pressures General guidelines for the design of
the retaining walls are as follows
1 Foundations for retaining walls should be designed in general accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this
report The footing embedment for retaining walls should be a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 11
Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or
utilities the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary
45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade
structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential of foundation settlement
2 All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth
pressures due to adjacent soils hydrostatic pressure and surcharge
effects An active lateral earth pressure of 35 pounds per cubic foot p c f
expressed in equivalent fluid pressure is recommended for design For an
at rest earth pressure an equivalent fluid pressure value of 50 p c f may be
used
The value provided above is for on site sandy soils or imported granular
soils Backfills behind retaining walls should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to between 90 and
95 percent relative compaction Over compaction of the wall backfill should
be avoided
3 The surcharge effect due to traffic areal surcharges line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the height of the wall should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the
loaded side of the wali a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure
equal to 35 percent of the surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of
the wall which is free to deflect cantilever may be used in the design
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 12
4 For lateral load resistance a passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot
of depth may be used In addition a base friction factor of 0 35 may also
be used to evaluate the resistance against sliding of the walls
5 Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide
zone of permeable material No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe
perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to a discharge facility
or weep holes A layer of filter fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent
should be placed between the drainage material and the compacted backfill
material The drainage material should extend from the base of the wall to
a height within 2 feet below finish grade The remaining backfill should
consist of an impervious capping of select non expansive soil to reduce
water infiltration behind the walls
Site Gradin
Based on the preliminary grading plan provided we understand that site grading
will involve minor cuts and fills of up to about 2 feet relative to the existing ground surface
for the proposed construction Site preparation should be observed by a representative
of Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe whether
the exposed soil conditions during excavation are similar to those encountered in our
exploration The following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the
design of site preparation work
At on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials generated from demolition should be removed and disposed of off site After
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 13
clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of
8 inches properly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For subgrades of
improvements subjected to vehicular traffic such as garage slab and pavements the
compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
An open pit as a result of swimming pool removal was observed at a central
portion of the site Based on the preliminary grading plan a portion of the open pit is
located within the future building area It is important that the open pit be properly
backfilled to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The backfill material
should consist of approved non expansive material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In addition all existing
utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressures
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Prior to
placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed of off site

W O 3688 00 Page 14
The on site granular soils generated from the excavation may be reused as a
source of fill and backfill material Where used as fill and backfill material the on site soils
should be properly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirements for the upper 2 feet of fill
and backfill below the pavement and swimming pool deck subgrade should be increased
to a minimum of 95 percent Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to
one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed
and benched
We envision that imported fill materials will be required for the non expansive
select granular fill material In general the imported material should consist of non
expansive material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material should be
tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery
to the project site for its intended use

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections may be needed for the proposed project
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site sandy soils or imported
granular material The backfill should be placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the residence should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the structure and that the discharge be diverted away from
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3688 00 Page 17
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil

from the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings to depths of approximately 16 5 and 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with solid augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the
laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials encountered is
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive or the middle 12 inches of a 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM Test Method D 1883 was
performed on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the soil strength for
preliminary pavement design Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 2
