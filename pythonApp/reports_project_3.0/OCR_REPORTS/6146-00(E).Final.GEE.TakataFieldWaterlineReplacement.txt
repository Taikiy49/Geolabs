SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
engineering analyses performed for the new 12-inch waterline alignment project from 
Wisser Road near its intersection with Funston Road to the south end of Takata Field at 
Fort Shafter, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based 
on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses performed. The 
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of trench excavation 
and backfilling. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
We understand that the project involves re-routing and replacing approximately 
450 lineal feet of existing 6-inch waterline near Takata Field and Funston Road at 
Fort Shafter in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The waterline will be replaced 
with 12-inch ductile iron water pipe with polyethylene encasement from the existing 
12-inch waterline extending down from Chapplear Road to the existing 12-inch waterline 
at Takata Field.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The project site is on the 
southern flank of the Koolau Volcano.  The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of 
basaltic lavas from the northwest to southeast trending rift zones.  These lavas consist 
of interbedded pahoehoe and a’a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant 
form.  “A’a” is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flow typified by a rough, jagged, 
spinous, clinkery surface.  “Pahoehoe” is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows 
typified by smooth, billowy or ropy surfaces.  After the cessation of the main 
shield-building phase of Koolau Volcano, there was a long period of erosion. 
The Fort Shafter area is geologically situated on the southwest flank of the 
Koolau Volcanics.  These volcanics are composed of basaltic lava flows of early 
Pleistocene Age.  The basalt is overlain by a complex interlayered system of terrestrial 
deposits.  In more recent time, the efforts of man have shaped the land to its present 
form by placement of fill in low areas.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration indicates that the lower part of the project site 
inside the Takata Baseball Field is underlain by fill materials of stiff clayey silt, and silty 
gravelly sand over stiff colluvium deposits of clay and cobbles/boulders while the upper 
portion above the slope is generally underlain by a fill layer of stiff clayey silt over stiff to 
very stiff residual/saprolite soils of silt and clay.  
Based on preliminary information provided for the waterline relocation, the 
proposed 12-inch diameter water pipe will have inverts depths ranging from about 
4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings generally indicate medium 
stiff to very stiff silt and clay soils within this zone. Based on the relatively competent 
subsurface soil conditions encountered within the proposed drainage alignment, we 
believe that the in-situ soils encountered at the site may be used to support the new 
water line.  
An allowable lateral bearing capacity of 1,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for the design of the reaction blocks for the new pipeline bearing against the 
compacted on-site clayey silts and clays.  
The steepness of the slope adjacent to the baseball field (estimated at about 
27% or about 15 degrees) from the toe to approximately 140 feet uphill and the 
presence of CRM buttress and grade separation walls present some construction 
challenges particularly in the compaction of the trench backfill along the steeper 
sections. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of waterline installation, 
and other geotechnical aspects are presented in the following sections.

mains will involve excavation into the underlying fills, colluvium, residual/saprolitc soils, 
and basalt formation.  It is anticipated that the fills, colluvium, and residual/saprolitic

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the ground 
surface will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Deeper excavations 
may be required where the water mains traverse existing underground utilities.  Where 
deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or 
sloping and benching should be used.  Based on our site observations, we believe that 
temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations is not practical in the existing roadway 
areas. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut vertically necessitating the use of 
shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all 
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe 
changing or unforeseen conditions, such as; groundwater seepage, inappropriate 
construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential 
for excessive ground movement.

It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such 
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously 
monitored for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with instruments until 
the subsurface drainage pipes and manholes are finally backfilled. 
3.3 
Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe.  Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration.  For pipes bearing on the very stiff clay with boulders and cobbles and 
medium hard to hard basalt rock, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding 
material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided 
below the pipe.  A typical section of the trench is presented on Plate 3. 
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the 
pipe.  Prior to the placement of bedding material, a Geolabs representative should 
observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials are exposed at the 
bottom of the trench or whether the bedding should be extended deeper.

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches 
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free draining granular material 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting 
stresses on the pipe.  In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce 
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in 
backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
Based on our field exploration, we believe there may be a significant amount of 
boulders and cobbles generated from the trench excavation. We believe that a majority 
of the excavated on-site soils will be suitable for use as trench backfill.  The upper 
portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to the pavement 
subgrade should consist of on-site or imported fill material less than 3 inches in 
maximum size without deleterious material. 
3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfills should be moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade 
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
It should be noted that some of the on-site soils generally exist in a moist 
condition. Therefore, moisture reduction may be required to achieve the minimum 
90 percent compaction criteria, especially for materials primarily consisting of silts and 
clays. Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort to achieve the 
specified compaction would generally reduce the rate of fill placement for this project.

3.6 
Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes.  The thrust force acting on 
the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block.  We anticipate that the planned inverts would be about 4 to 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
Based on our field exploration, very stiff clayey soils and medium hard to hard 
basalt rock were encountered at shallow depths.  We recommend using lateral bearing 
pressures of 1,500 psf for the design of thrust blocks bearing on stiff clay with boulders 
and cobbles.

3.7.3 Drainage 
In general, retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, 
No. 67 gradation), directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at 
the base of the walls discharging to an appropriate outlet or weep hole. Weep holes 
should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart. As an alternative, a prefabricated 
drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the 
drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically 
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
As previously indicated, the backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may 
consist of on-site soils or select granular fill material. Unless covered by concrete 
slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious 
material, such as the on-site clayey soils, to reduce the potential for significant 
water infiltration behind the retaining walls.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, which were drilled to depths of 
approximately 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig (CME-75) equipped with continuous flight augers. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our field engineer/geologist, who monitored the drilling 
operations on a continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the 
Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 
through A-4. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch OD split-barrel sampler with a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and 
third 6 inches (a total of 12 inches) of an 18-inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
