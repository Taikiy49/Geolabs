SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Pauwela Bridge Replacement 
project. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate that the majority of the pavement 
subgrade soils will likely consist of near-surface fill material with an R-value of about 72. 
New HDOT pavements (or County pavements that receive federal funding) typically 
include a drainage layer to facilitate the removal of stormwater from the pavement section 
and increase the pavement life. However, the existing pavement section along Haiku Road 
does not include a drainage layer; therefore, only pavement structural section without a 
permeable drainage layer was considered in our pavement design. 
Based on the traffic volume assumptions described herein, the R-value obtained for 
the subgrade soil and our economic analyses, we recommend using a flexible pavement 
structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC over 4 inches of ACB over 6 inches of 
aggregate base course for the new pavements along Haiku Road at the project site. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement 
surface should be adequately sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.  
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed 
discussion and specific pavement design recommendations for the new roundabout 
project.

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located on Haiku Road on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. Project 
improvements include bridge structure replacement, in-stream scour protection at the 
abutments, roadway paving, and signing and striping. We understand that the existing 
abutments will remain in-place, and the construction of a temporary bypass bridge is not

Concrete Rubble Masonry wingwalls are located along both ends of the abutments. 
We understand that the proposed project will be federally funded. Therefore, a 
pavement justification report conforming to the requirements and guidelines of the State 
of Hawaii - Department of Transportation (HDOT) will be required. The new pavements 
will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002, prepared by the HDOT, Highways Division, Materials Testing and

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes: the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these 
two volcanoes. The project site is located on the north-central portion of the island. 
The East Maui Volcano is represented by the older rocks of the Honomanu Volcanic 
Series, buried beneath younger volcanic deposits. The Honomanu Series is overlain by the 
Kula Volcanic Series, which generally consists of thick lava flows with appreciable 
embedded pyroclastic (ash and cinders) materials. Rocks of the Kula Series cover much of 
the present-day surface of the East Maui Volcano. 
A long period of deep erosion and sediment deposition occurred between the end of 
the Kula Volcanic Series and the beginning of the Hana Volcanic Series. The Hana Series 
generally consists of the youngest lava flows and cinder cones presently encountered at the 
East Maui ground surface. Although the Hana lavas were generally more fluid and the lava 
flows tended to be thinner and less massive than the preceding Kula lavas, thick 
accumulation of lava did occur in the valleys and valley heads. 
The erosional process of volcanic islands is dominated by the detachment of 
weathered soil and rock materials from the interior steep mountain slopes and the 
movement of the eroded materials downslope by gravity toward the lowland mountain 
perimeter areas. Once these eroded slope materials (colluvium) reach a valley stream, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are further transported by flowing 
water and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, mountain stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
stream flows may transmit large volumes of water for a very short duration. Because of this, 
the transport of sediment is intermittent, and the bulk of a stream's hydraulic load may 
consist of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fine soil particles. 
When the erosional base level of a land mass changes through long-term changes in sea 
level or the sinking/raising of the island mass, sedimentary deposits may either by

When unconsolidated sedimentary deposits are buried in-place for long periods of

transport, these newer sediments may be generally transient in terms of geologic time.  
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located at the Pauwela No. 39 Bridge on Haiku Road on the 
Island of Maui. The project location is presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The existing Pauwela No. 39 Bridge, within the project limits, is a one-span 
reinforced concrete tee beam bridge. The bridge was constructed in 1911 and spans over 
the Pauwela stream. It is 17 feet by 9 inches wide and 26 feet by 2 inches long, supported 
on CRM abutment structures. The bridge consists of concrete tee beams overlaid with an 
asphaltic concrete (AC) surface. A concrete bridge railing runs along both sides of the 
bridge, with heights of 2 feet by 6 inches upstream and 2 feet by 9 inches downstream. 
There are no approach guardrails, but CRM wingwalls are located at both ends of the 
abutments. Based on visual observations, the existing AC pavements appear to be in fair 
to good condition, although transverse cracks were observed.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed for the bridge replacement project. Typically, 
new HDOT pavements (or County pavements that receive federal funding) include a 
drainage layer to facilitate the removal of stormwater from the pavement section and 
increase the pavement life. However, the existing pavement section along Haiku Road 
does not include a drainage layer; therefore, only a pavement structural section without 
a permeable drainage layer was considered in our pavement design. 
Based on the traffic volume assumptions described herein, the R-value obtained 
for the subgrade soil, and our economic analyses, we recommend using a flexible 
pavement structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC over 4 inches of asphaltic 
concrete base (ACB) over 6 inches of aggregate base course for the new pavements 
along Haiku Road at the project site. 
In general, the following sections of this report provide a discussion of the areas 
necessary for pavement analyses and design. 
3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The flexible pavement sections presented in this report were generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design 
Manual dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the HDOT, 
Highways Division, Material Testing and Research Branch. The pavement design 
methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the 
California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections are generally based on the 
design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for 
Concrete Highway and Street Pavements.”

3.6 
Compaction Requirements 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate subbase and 
base course materials required for the new pavements should conform to the 
requirements stipulated in Subsections 703.17 and 703.06 of the Hawaii State Standards, 
respectively. The ASB and base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to no less 
than 95 percent of the maximum density according to AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). 
Asphaltic concrete base material should consist of asphalt-treated basaltic 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined 
in accordance with AASHTO T209 (ASTM D2041). 
Asphaltic concrete material should be constructed in general accordance with 
Section 401 – Hot Mix Asphalt Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications 
for Road and Bridge Construction (2005) and subsequent amendments.

3.7 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
As previously mentioned, a permeable drainage layer was not incorporated into 
our pavement design. Therefore, it is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately 
and to maintain positive drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface 
water may be carried off the pavement quickly for proper discharge. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of about 50 and 50.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample, designated as Bulk-1, was 
obtained near the ground surface to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight auger and coring tools. The boring and bulk sample locations are 
presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses,” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, 
mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the samples tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) are indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were 
performed on selected relatively undisturbed soil samples in accordance with 
ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” samples. The test results and the
