INTRODUCTION
This report represents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the design of the two 2 proposed Kapaa Water
Tanks Hawaii
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our study was to explore the subsurface soil
conditions at the proposed reservoir site in order to provide
recommendations for use in the design of the site grading and foundations
of the proposed facility

The work was done in general accordance with our proposal of January
13 1989
The scope of our work included the drilling and sampling of five 5
borings to a depth of 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface Also
included were laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the
field exploration engineering evaluation of the field and laboratory
data the development of design recommendations and report preparation
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and laboratory testing
are presented in the appendices of this report
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the southeastern corner of the
intersection of Kawaihau Road and Kaapuni Road in Kapaa Homesteads area on
the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location of the site and its
vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Topographically the site is nearly level with a very gentle slope
down towards the east An existing square shaped water reservoir
measuring 61 by 61 feet in plan dimensions is located in the westerm
portion of the site There is an abandoned field to the east
The area surrounding the existing water tank is covered with well
trimmed grass enclosed by a chain link fence Several manhole structures
exist on the northern side of the tank
The eastern portion of the site is generally covered with grass and
shrubs A shallow drainageway trending to the east is located near the
eastern boundary of the site

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct two million gallon capacity
reinforced concrete reservoir structures on the site The reservoirs will
be approximately 70 feet in diameter and 15 feet in height
The exact details of the proposed site grading were not available at
the time of our exploration However it is anticipated that some cuts
are planned to create a level pad for the reservoirs It is also planned
to demolish the existing reservoir
A new 12 foot wide service road is required around the new
reservoirs
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano
built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series
during the late Pliocene more than 2 3 million years before present
Following the cessation of this main shield building phase there was
renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of the basaltic lavas of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series Interbedded with the Koloa lavas
are the sedimentary conglomerates and breccias alluvium of the Palikea
Formation Preliminary reconnaissance of the site and its vicinity
indicates that the area is underlain by thick residual and saprolite soils
derived from the in situ weathering of Koloa lavas
Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by
drilling and sampling five 5 borings to a depth of 26 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown of the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the borings encountered a surface layer composed of stiff
reddish brown clayey silt with traces of sand and gravel residual soil
approximately 8 0 feet in thickness A 3 to 4 foot surface fill layer

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Two MG reservoir tanks are proposed for the site The
existing square shaped tank is planned for demolition If the
proposed location of one of the tanks is to be situated over the
existing tank site we recommended it be placed to match or to be
slightly higher than the existing tank bottom elevation The other
planned reservoir should be located 50 feet away from the existing tank
site
The subsurface exploration at the site generally encountered
underlying subsurface soils having a moderately to relatively high
potential for compressibility and having high in situ moisture contents
For tanks located away from the existing tank site we recommend the
lowering of the finish grade of the new tanks to reduce the anticipated
settlement of the soft compressible soil stratum encountered at about
12 5 foot depth To lower the finished grade of the tanks either the
grade of the site should be lowered or the tanks should be depressed or
buried underground

Preloading or surcharging of the site to reduce ground settlement
prior to reservoir construction was considered Because of the 12 5 foot
surface crust and the estimated length of the surcharge period on the
order of six 6 months or more for a 10 foot surcharge we feel that
this method of reducing the anticipated foundations ground settlements
would not be as effective as the other alternatives discussed above
Availability of surcharge material could also pose a problem
New Tank Over Existing Tank Site
The existing 61 by 61 foot square tank is buried about eight 8
feet below the existing ground surface Some disturbance probably from
over excavation may have occurred outside of the tank structure based on
Boring Nos B 1 and B 5
If one of the new tanks is to be placed over the existing underground
tank site we recommend that the new tank footing or bottom elevation be
slightly raised to match the other new tank footing elevations Some
amount of structural granular backfill would be required to raise the new
tank s finish grade from the existing 8 foot depth We believe footing
settlements would be tolerable if the new tank bottom elevation is raised
up to about four 4 feet below the existing grade
All structural backfill should consist of import select granular fill
materials placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to obtain a
minimum of 95 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
New Tanks Over Existing Ground
The subsurface soils encountered at the site have a moderately to
relatively high potential for compressibility and have high in situ
moisture contents It is our opinion that soils of this nature would
experience excessive settlements under the anticipated tank loads at
maximum water storage conditions

Based on an assumed tank floor finish grade near the existing ground
surface the calculated total floor settlement at the center of the tanks
is on the order of 1 5 to 5 5 inches under the maximm water loads
Estimated footing settlement under an assumed column load of 120 kips is
on the order of one 1 inch Therefore it is our opinion that in order
to reduce total and differential settlements to tolerable limits removal
of overburden loads by lowering the finished grade of the tanks should
be considered to balance the weight of the reservoir
Lowering of Finish Grade
To reduce excessive total arid differential ground settlements due to
the reservoir loads the finished grade of the proposed reservoir slab
should be considered in order to remove sufficient overburden soil to
balance most of the reservoir loads It is our opinion that by lowering
the finished grade six 6 feet below the currently proposed finished
floor elevation most of the reservoir loads would be compensated by the
removal of the existing overburden Therefore the ground settlements
would be reduced to negligible amount Estimated total settlements would
generally be on the order of less than one 1 inch
By lowering the finish grade four 4 feet below the proposed
finished floor elevation estimated total settlements would generally be
To lower the finished grade of the reservoir the overall grade of
the site may be reduced by site excavation However lowering the
surrounding grade down four 4 to six 6 feet would require a large
excavation quantity and cut slopes down to the finished grade from the
existing Upper ground surface The excavated reservoir site would provide
a larger area which may possibly expand the property lines
Another method to lower the finished grade of the reservoir would be
to depress or bury the tank underground The embedded portion of the
reservoir walls would then function as earth retaining structures as well

as tank walls The excavation around the reservoir should be properly
backfilled with select granular materials such as 3B Fine gravel to
minimize compaction effort required and reduce lateral earth pressure
against the underground portion of the depressed tank structure
The site grading of the ground surface around the reservoir should be
designed to divert water away from the reservoir
Reservoir Foundations
The borings disclosed that the site is underlain by a wet soft
clayey silt stratum at an approximate depth of 12 5 feet below the
existing ground surface If the finished floor is lowered the subgrade
would be underlain by the wet soil stratum Therefore in order to
provide adequate bearing support for the proposed reservoir it is our
opinion that a gravel blanket of 1 foot thickness should be placed below
the bottom of spread and wall footings Also we recommend that a 1 foot
thick 3B Fine gravel below the reservoir slab be provided
Minimal compactive effort will be required in the 3B Fine gravel
cushion fill placed above the over excavation and subgrade soils
We believe that an allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f may be
used for foundations bearing on the above prepared gravel layer
The allowable bearing value may be increased by one third for
Exterior wall footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade Footings located next to
utility treriches should extend down to the bottom of the utility trench or
the trench backfill should be well compacted to minimize footing

Site Grading
The details of the proposed grading of the reservoir site were not
available at the time of our exploration However major site grading
consisting of excavations will be required if the finished grade of the
reservoir and surrounding grade of the site is lowered
Due to the high annual rainfall the natural moisture content of the
on site soils are generally high 40 to 70 Therefore significant
drying out of the excavated material would be required prior to attaining
the recommended compaction criteria Alternatively drier off site
material may be used for the project fill if required
