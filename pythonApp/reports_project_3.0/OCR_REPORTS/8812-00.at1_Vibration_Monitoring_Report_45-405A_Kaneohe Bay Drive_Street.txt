This technical memorandum presents a summary of the findings from the 
vibration monitoring performed at the 45-405A Kaneohe Bay Drive property in Kaneohe 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand the homeowner noticed increased noise 
pollution and vibrations at his residence, especially when large trucks traversed over the 
traffic calming speed hump installed on Kaneohe Bay Drive. Vibration monitoring was 
performed at the 45-405A Kaneohe Bay Drive property between December 20, 2023 
and January 4, 2024. 
VIBRATION MONITORING SETUP 
We understand it is desired to monitor vibrations experienced at the 
45-405A Kaneohe Bay Drive property in Kaneohe. In order to differentiate the vibrations 
induced by the speed hump and by regular traffic, the vibrations were monitored at two 
locations within the property, as described below. 
One near-distance location from the speed hump designated as VM-1 as 
shown on the Site Plan (Plate 1). The VM-1 location is situated 
approximately 18 feet from the speed hump. 
One far-distance location from the speed hump designated as VM-2 as 
shown on the Site Plan (Plate 1). The VM-2 location is situated 
approximately 42 feet from the speed hump. 
Both monitoring points are located within the 45-405A Kaneohe Bay Drive 
property near the southern property line of the residence. The approximate vibration 
monitoring locations are shown on the attached Site Plan, Plate 1. The site plan is an 
aerial view captured from 2024 Google Earth ProTM ©2024 Google Inc. Image dated 
August 6, 2022. The vibration monitoring setup is presented on the photographs on 
Plate 2. 
VIBRATION MONITORING RESULTS 
Vibration monitoring was performed using two Instantel Micromate monitoring 
units equipped with geophone sensors and telemetry to monitor vibrations on a near-
continuous basis during the monitoring period.

The Instantel monitoring units consist of a 3-component geophone and a signal 
conditioner/recorder unit. The geophone senses ground motion along three mutually 
perpendicular (orthogonal) axes; therefore, it provides detection of ground motion in the 
longitudinal, transverse, and vertical directions. The longitudinal direction of the 
monitoring units runs approximately perpendicular to the longitudinal direction of 
Kaneohe Bay Drive. The signal conditioner/recorder unit receives and processes these 
signals to provide a graphic record of the vibrations. Resultant peak particle velocities - 
peak vector sum (PPV PVS) in units of inches per second are automatically computed 
by vector summation of the motions along the orthogonal axes. The data is stored on 
the conditioner/recorder unit’s onboard memory and downloaded from the unit at the 
end of the monitoring period. 
The Instantel monitoring units are set at a scanning interval of 15 minutes and 
continuously monitor vibrations 24 hours per day. During the 15-minute scanning 
interval, the monitoring unit samples the vibration data at a rate of about 1,000 data 
points per second and stores the resultant peak level of vibration, which occurs during 
that 15-minute interval. At the end of every scanning interval, a record is produced 
representing the highest vibration level observed. The magnitude of the highest 
vibration level observed and its corresponding time are plotted, and a histogram is 
generated. The vibration monitoring histogram data recorded during the monitoring 
period for each vibration monitoring location, including PPV PVS and frequency, are 
presented on Plates 3 through 6.

DISCUSSION AND EVALUATION 
Based on the AASHTO R 8-96 (2004) document, “Standard Recommended 
Practice for Evaluation of Transportation-Related Earthborne Vibrations,” AASHTO 
recommends a vibration limit of 0.12 inches per second to prevent (cosmetic) damage 
to historic sites and other critical locations. For residential structures with plastered 
walls, a vibration threshold of 0.2 to 0.3 inches per second is normally adopted. For the 
subject residence at 45-405A Kaneohe Bay Drive, we recommend adopting a damage 
threshold limit of 0.2 inches per second for the residential structure in accordance with 
AASHTO Guidelines (Note: AASHTO stands for the American Association of State 
Highway and Transportation Officials). It should be noted that these adopted threshold 
limits may be considered conservative as these limits are for steady-state (continuous) 
vibration sources, which are lower than limits for transient (single-event) vibration 
sources. 
The vibration threshold limit of 0.2 inches per second along each of the three 
individual axis was not reached nor exceeded at the monitoring locations during the 
monitoring period presented in this summary report. From the vibrations measured, the 
two highest Peak Vector Sum (PVS) values recorded consisted of the following two 
isolated instances.  
 VM-1: December 21, 2023 at 15:18 PM, PVS = 0.17 inches per second 
 VM-2: January 1, 2024 at 12:30 AM, PVS = 0.049 inches per second 
To compare levels of vibration and vibration frequency at the near-distance 
(VM-1) and the far-distance (VM-2) from the speed hump, the histogram data recorded 
at the two locations are plotted on the same plate, differentiated by color-blue and 
color-orange, respectively. Based on a review of the histogram data plots, except for the 
two instances mentioned above, the levels of the vibration PPV PVS recorded at the 
two locations appear to be within the same ranges of approximately 0.005 and 
0.04 inches per second, as shown on Plate 7. Similarly, the vibration frequencies for all 
three components recorded at the two locations appear to be primarily between about 
10 and 40 Hertz, as shown on Plates 8 through 10. 
The recorded histogram data suggests that the induced vibrations at the 
near-distance monitoring point (VM-1) are generally at about the same level as those 
recorded at the far-distance monitoring point (VM-2) away from the speed hump. Even 
the higher vibration levels monitored at both monitoring locations are within the 
0.2 inches per second threshold adopted for evaluation at this site. Therefore, we
