SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by surficial fills 
and cultivated soils about 1 to 2 feet thick overlying stiff to hard clayey and silty alluvial 
soils extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The exception was along the existing berm on the southern portion of the 
project site where uncontrolled fill material up to about 6 feet thick was encountered.  
Field observations during our exploration indicated the surface soils at the project 
site are dry and friable. In addition, we observed shrinkage cracks at the existing ground 
surface throughout the project site at the time of our field exploration. Additional test pits 
and water filtration testing indicated that the shrinkage cracks might extend no deeper than 
3 feet below ground surface. 
Based on our laboratory test results, the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate 
to very high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture 
contents. Considering the lightly loaded structures planned for the development, we 
believe that structural distresses could occur to the new buildings and pavements due to 
the moderate to very high shrink/swell potential of the on-site soils. We recommend 
special subgrade preparations be implemented during mass grading construction, to 
reduce the risk of structural distresses resulted from the moderately to very highly 
expansive soil conditions. 
We recommend the site grading preparation consist of the following procedures for 
creating a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near-surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These 
soils should be disposed of properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, 
if appropriate. 
2. 
Over-excavate and remove an additional 12 inches of soil and 
moisture-condition to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum 
moisture-content for replacement, or replaced with general fill compacted 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
3. 
Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as fill, the bottom of the 
over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative 
compaction.

The intent of the above site preparation is to create a 2-foot thick reworked soil 
layer within the entire grading limits. The reworked soil serves to close-up the shrinkage 
cracks observed throughout the site. It would also reduce the future soil swelling/heaving 
potential by preparing the clayey soils with elevated moisture contents and reduced 
compaction requirements. It should be noted that the 2-foot thick reworked soil layer 
should also be constructed below the finished subgrade and pad grade in cut areas.  
To moisture-condition the soils effectively, the hard clayey clumps should be 
thoroughly broken down and mixed by the compaction equipment, such as large 
sheepsfoot compactors and/or large dozers, until achieving a relatively uniform 
moisture-condition. Contractors bidding on this project should anticipate additional 
processing of the on-site soils to break down the hard clayey clumps and achieve a 
relatively uniform moisture content. 
Based on our field exploration and analyses, we believe the lightly loaded 
residential building structures may be supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation 
system bearing on the 2-foot reworked soil layer or general fills placed below the finished 
pad grade. The Wafflemat is a post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. We 
recommend using 12-inch high Waffleboxes to provide a stiffer foundation system in order 
to reduce differential movement and resulting angular distortion. We also recommend 
installing a vapor barrier underneath the Waffleboxes to a minimum of 2 feet beyond the 
slab perimeter to reduce water infiltration beneath the slab foundations. 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: general fill and select granular fill. General fill may consist of the on-site soils 
with a maximum particle size of 6 inches and should be free of deleterious material. Select 
granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline 
and/or basaltic materials. In general, the excavated on-site materials (not including the 
12 inches of soil materials removed after clearing and grubbing) may be re-used as a 
source of general fill material, provided they are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material and other unsuitable materials. 
The on-site clayey soils should not be allowed to dry out and should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capping it with new fill. If the on-site 
clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks develop, the soils should be re-scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and recompacted again.

geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southwestern 
flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time 
through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is a vacant lot designated as East Kapolei II LDA Parcel 2 
(TMK: 9-1-017: 109) in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This rectangular-shaped 
parcel is bounded by Keahumoa Parkway to the north, Ewa Villages Golf Course to the 
south, Ho`opili Development to the east, and DHHL’s detention basin and vacant land to 
the west. 
At the time of our field exploration, the site was generally covered with light to 
heavy vegetation consisting of haole koa, keawe, and weed grasses. An asphalt-paved 
access road was observed along the southern portion of the site. We understand this 
access road is an easement for drainage purposes and is designated as Mango Tree 
Road. 
Shrinkage cracks were observed at the existing ground surface throughout the 
project site at the time of our field exploration. These shrinkage cracks were measured 
to be about 2 to 3 inches wide at the ground surface and extend to depths of about 
1.5 to 2 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our experience in the project 
vicinity, we anticipate shrinkage cracks at the project site may extend up to about 
2 to 3 feet deep. Photographs of various shrinkage cracks observed throughout the 
project site are presented on Plates 3.1 and 3.2. 
Based on our site observations and topographic survey map provided, the project 
site generally slopes gently down from north to south at an average slope gradient of 
about 1 percent. The exceptions were a ditch and berm located parallel with each other 
along the southern portion of the site adjacent to the paved access road. The existing 
ditch generally varies from about 1 to 7 feet deep and about 10 to 30 feet wide at the 
top of the ditch. The existing berm generally varies from about 1 to 4 feet high and 
about 10 to 30 feet wide at the base of the berm. Based on the information provided, 
ground surface elevations range from about +72 to +58 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at 
the northeastern and southwestern portions of the project site, respectively.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 22 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 22, extending to depths ranging from about 20.5 to 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, eight bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-8, were obtained to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and 
bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the project site is underlain by surficial fills and cultivated soils 
approximately 1 to 2 feet thick over the majority of the project site. The exception was 
Boring Nos. 18 through 21 where the fill layer extended down to a depth of about 6 feet 
below the existing ground surface. It should be noted that these borings were located 
along the existing berm on the southern portion of the site. The surficial fills and 
cultivated soils were generally underlain by clayey/silty alluvial soils extending down to 
the maximum depth explored of approximately 21.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
The surficial fills generally consisted of stiff to hard silty clay and dense silty 
coralline sand and gravel. The cultivated soils generally consisted of clayey soils with 
organics and agricultural debris that varied from stiff to hard. It should be noted that the 
organic portion was generally limited to the upper 1 to 2 feet of the cultivated soils.  
The encountered alluvial soils underlying the surficial fills and cultivated soils 
generally consisted of stiff to hard clays and silts. The clayey alluvial soils were 
generally encountered to depths ranging from about 10.5 to 16 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The silty alluvial soils were generally encountered underlying the 
alluvial clays and extended down to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. A thin layer of coralline detritus was encountered in 
Boring Nos. 1, 4, 10, and 12 at depths ranging from about 10.5 to 20 feet below the 
existing ground surface. This coralline detritus generally consisted of dense silty sand 
and ranged in thickness from about 0.5 to 1.5 feet. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change

Highly expansive clayey soils beneath concrete slabs-on-grade

We recommend installing Wafflemat foundation systems directly on the reworked

Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the 
Ewa/Kapolei area during prolonged dry weather conditions and were observed at the 
existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. Shrinkage cracks adjacent to 
the building foundation are potentially detrimental because water could readily infiltrate 
into the subsurface through the crack openings and soften the subsurface soils around 
the building foundations, thus affecting the foundation performance. Therefore, it is

3.1.3 Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: general fill and select granular fill. General fill may consist of the on-site

3.1.7 Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
As indicated previously, we observed substantial shrinkage cracks at the existing

content as the soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions. The 
development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the 
prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Kapolei/Ewa area resulting in large 
fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to

over-watering of the graded surfaces should be avoided to reduce the potential for 
saturation and softening of the surface soils.

3.2 
Building Foundations 
Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked 
soil layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a 
post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. This foundation system utilizes a 
series of recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side 
down on the soil. The Waffleboxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings 
throughout the floor system, creating a very stiff foundation support. A post-tensioned 
concrete slab is monolithically poured over and between the boxes. The empty space 
beneath the boxes allows for soil expansion, reducing uplift of the slab. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
may be used for the design of the Wafflemat foundations bearing on compacted 
reworked soil layer or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations 
presented herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased 
by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Generally, minimum subgrade preparation is required for the Wafflemat 
foundation system. Considering moderate to very high expansive soil conditions, we 
recommend preparing the Wafflemat subgrade in accordance with the Site Preparation 
section herein. Prior to placing the Waffleboxes, the subgrade soil should be 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction, and covered with a vapor barrier 
to maintain the soil moisture. If the on-site clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks 
develop, the soils should be re-scarified, moisture-conditioned, and recompacted again. 
We recommend using 12-inch high Waffleboxes to provide a stiffer foundation 
system to reduce potential differential movement and resulting angular distortion. We 
also recommend installing a vapor barrier underneath the Waffleboxes. The vapor 
barrier should be extended a minimum of 2 feet beyond the slab edge to reduce the 
potential for water infiltration against the slab foundations, as shown on Plate 8.

If the tops of the structures are not free to deflect beyond this degree, or are 
restrained such as box drain walls, the retaining structures should be designed for 
the at-rest condition. These lateral earth pressures do not include hydrostatic 
pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the structures. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structure should be considered in the 
design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the

3.5.3 Drainage 
The retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up 
of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at 
the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an 
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may 
be used instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also 
should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or 
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential 
for significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches 
of the retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to 
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure 
unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface.

and/or biofiltration. We also understand that the adjacent DHHL detention basin, located 
west of the project site, was constructed during the initial site development, to 
accommodate the stormwater runoff from the master DHHL development. 
In general, we anticipate that the in-situ clayey soil would have very low to no 
infiltration capacity. Based on our experience in the project vicinity, we believe infiltration

3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
Six sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300.0), were performed by our laboratory and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on 
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration, to evaluate the corrosivity of 
the on-site soils. Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in Appendix B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at 
the project site have resistivity values ranging from about 500 to 1,000 ohm-cm (the 
soluble soil sample prepared in accordance with Standard Methods for the Examination 
of Water and Wastewater SM 2510B) and pH values of 5 to 7. 
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a 
corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed project, in 
accordance with the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of 
Water Supply. Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other 
metallic piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the 
substructure.

3.8 
Pavement Design 
It is anticipated that access driveways and parking areas will be constructed for 
the proposed affordable rental housing development. In general, we anticipate vehicle 
loading will consist primarily of passenger vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and occasional 
heavy vehicular traffic, such as delivery, container and garbage collection trucks.  
Based on the moderately to very highly expansive soil conditions, we 
recommend placing the pavement sections on a minimum 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive, select granular fill. Based on the above assumptions, we recommend 
using the following flexible and rigid pavement sections for preliminary design purposes: 
Light Vehicular Traffic and Parking Areas – Flexible Pavements 
  2.5-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
  8.5-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
Light Vehicular Traffic and Driveways – Rigid Pavements 
  5.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
11.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
As mentioned previously, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry 
and friable and exhibit moderate to very high shrink/swell characteristics when 
subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 
non-expansive, select granular fill material, we recommend that the subgrade be 
properly scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to

90 percent relative compaction, as specified in the Site Preparation section herein. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to re-compacting. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material 
requirements for Aggregate Base Course and Select Borrow for Subbase Course as 
specified in Sections 31 and 30, respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public 
Works Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to 
the project site for the intended use. 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement for 
the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course and should be compacted to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.

It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed project will consist primarily of 
water, sewer, and drain lines. In general, good construction practices should be utilized 
for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities. The 
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for excavation, 
subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The excavation should comply with

responsible for trench shoring design and installation. Trench shoring and bracing 
should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of 
free-draining backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging

material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of 
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site 
clayey soils as backfill directly around the utility pipes is not recommended and should 
not be allowed. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill 
material. The backfill material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence. The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the 
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill material 
considering the clayey nature of the on-site soils. 
3.10 Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
As indicated previously, we observed shrinkage cracks at the existing ground 
surface of the project site. In general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to 
the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under 
the sun and dry weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground 
was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Kapolei area 
resulting in large fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to 
reduce the potential for significant soil erosion soon after completion of the site grading.

3.11 Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed 
water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It 
is highly recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert 
discharge away from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near 
the foundations and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. 
Planters next to foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and 
drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface. 
In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be 
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
22 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 22, extending to depths ranging from 
about 20.5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, eight bulk samples 
of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-8, were obtained to evaluate 
the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring 
and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-22. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Thirteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) including three hydrometer tests 
[ASTM D422-63(2007)e2] were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-3. 
Thirteen one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-4. 
Two Expansion Index of Soil tests were performed on selected samples in 
general accordance with ASTM D4829. The test is a standardized test used to 
determine the expansive characteristics of the soil. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-5. 
Twelve one-dimensional swell or settlement potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is 
remolded to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to 
simulate the anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle 
until it has stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil 
sample to evaluate its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test 
results are presented on Plates B-6 through B-8. 
Five Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-9 through B-13 
Two Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples in accordance with ASTM D2850 to evaluate 
the undrained shear strength of the soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curve is presented on Plates B-14 and B-15.
