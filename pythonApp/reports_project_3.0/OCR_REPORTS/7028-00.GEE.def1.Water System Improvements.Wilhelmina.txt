SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
engineering analyses performed for the Wilhelmina Rise Water System Improvements, 
Part V project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based 
on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The 
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of trench excavation 
and backfilling, replacement pavement structure, and thrust block/vault soil design 
parameters only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves rehabilitation/improvements to the existing water supply 
system along several streets in the Wilhelmina Rise area in Honolulu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The planned improvements will involve installation of water mains, fire 
hydrants, service laterals, pipe locator devices along waterlines, thrust blocks, concrete 
valve vault and other appurtenances.  It is anticipated that the underground 
improvements will be installed by the cut-and-cover method. 
1.3 
Purpose and Scope 
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration is to obtain an overview 
of the surface and subsurface conditions along the proposed waterline alignment to 
develop a generalized soil/rock data set to formulate geotechnical engineering 
recommendations for the design of the new water system. In order to accomplish this, 
we conducted an exploration program, in accordance with our revised fee proposal 
dated November 18, 2013, generally consisting of the following tasks and work efforts:

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is situated near the upper portion of a planeze on the 
southwestern facing flank of Koolau.  The geologic conditions underlying the site are the 
result of in-situ chemical weathering of basaltic lava flows.  The shallow soils are 
generally residual soils consisting of reddish to brown silty clays which grade with depth 
to silty sand saprolitic soils which, in turn, grade with depth to highly weathered basaltic 
rock. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located at the top of Wilhelmina Rise residential area above 
the Kaimuki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The water system 
improvements will be along the following municipal streets: 
Maunalani Circle 
Sierra Drive 
Halehoola Place

on the order of about +855 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) in the southwestern portion of 
the site at Sierra Drive and Halehoola Place to about +1,045 feet MSL at the higher end 
of the improvement area in the north on Maunalani Circle.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8, extending to depths of about 10.5 and 11.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.8. The conditions encountered in the borings may be 
generally summarized as follows: 
The borings generally encountered a surface pavement section of 2 to 5 inches 
of asphaltic concrete (AC) over 5 inches of Portland Cement Concrete (PCC).  This 
appears to be an AC overlay over an older PCC pavement.  Below the pavement 
section, the borings generally penetrated a soil layer of brown silty clay (residual soil 
derived from the in-situ weathering of basaltic rock).  This residual soil was in turn 
underlain by saprolite (i.e., residual soil that retains the structure of the parent rock). 
This saprolite reduced to a silty sand with decomposing gravel.  We did not encounter 
groundwater within the borings at the time of our field exploration.  
Some of the borings encountered thin ledges or zones of highly weathered 
basaltic rock in their intermediate depths.  Based on these ledges or zones, it is 
possible that trenching work at the site may encounter isolated remnant boulders or 
ledges of rock material.   
We did not observe groundwater in the borings drilled and sampled for this field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels may occur or fluctuate depending on rainfall, 
temperature, surface water runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. Also, 
seepage zones are commonly encountered in the area. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of 
Borings are presented on Plates A-1 through A-8 in Appendix A. Results of the 
laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples retrieved from our field exploration 
are presented in Appendix B.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by stiff 
clayey residual soils overlying silty saprolitic soils.  Weathered basaltic rock formation 
was encountered in the deeper portion of some borings. 
It is anticipated that the new waterline would have invert depths on the order of 
about 5 feet to 8 or more feet below the existing ground surface. The borings generally 
indicate stiff clay soils within this zone. Based on the relatively competent subsurface 
soil conditions encountered, we believe that conventional excavation methods may be 
used for installation of the waterline.  However, there is a potential that remnant 
boulders or isolated ledges of rock may be encountered at shallow depths at the site. 
An allowable lateral bearing capacity of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for the design of the reaction blocks for the new pipelines bearing on 
compacted on-site clays.  
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of waterline installation, 
and other geotechnical aspects are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the new water main inverts would be on the order of about 
5 feet to 8 or more feet below the existing ground surface.  Installation of the proposed 
water mains will involve excavation into the underlying stiff clayey residual soils and stiff 
silty saprolites.  It is anticipated that these soils may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment.  However, based on previous experience with similar sites, there 
is a potential that boulders and cobbles may be encountered within the residual and 
saprolite soils. 
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is 
based solely on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site and 
previous experience in the project vicinity.

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 5 feet to 8 or more feet 
below the ground surface will be required for the installation of new waterlines. Deeper 
excavations may be required where the water mains traverse existing underground 
utilities.  Where excavations greater than 5 feet in depth are planned, temporary shoring 
or sloping and benching should be used.  Based on the existing site conditions (i.e., 
active roadways in a residential area), we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut 
excavations are not practical.  On this basis, trench walls would have to be cut vertically 
requiring the use of shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative (i.e., competent person 
as defined in OSHA regulations) should be on-site at all times during excavation and 
construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe changing or unforeseen 
conditions, such as, high groundwater, inappropriate construction sequence or 
techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential 
for excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such

3.3 
Pipe Bedding 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe.  Therefore, the pipe bedding is an important design 
consideration.  For this project, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding 
material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), should be provided 
below the pipe.  
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the 
pipe.  Prior to the placement of bedding material, a Geolabs representative should 
observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials are exposed at the 
bottom of the trench or whether the bedding should be extended deeper. 
3.4 
Trench Backfill 
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches 
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free draining granular material 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting 
stresses on the pipe.  In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce 
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in 
backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
Based on our field exploration, the existing subsurface soils at the site are of 
poor quality for use as trench backfill, within 18 inches under pavement areas. The 
trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 18 inches below pavement 
subgrade may consist of excavated on-site material less than 3 inches in size. Backfill

Hawaii Department of Transportation 2005 Standard Specifications, Section 314, may 
be used to backfill the trenches.  The use of CLSM would also reduce the potential for 
future settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic. 
3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfills should be moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  The upper 2 feet below the pavement grade 
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.  Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the 
specified compaction.

Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide

the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block.  We anticipate that the planned inverts would be about 5 feet to 8 or more feet 
below the existing ground surface.

3.8 
Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosivity tests (i.e., pH and Minimum Resistivity) were performed by 
TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected samples of the on-site soils at the project 
site. The laboratory test results are presented in Appendix C. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, lower soil resistivity values indicate a greater corrosion potential for corrosion 
on metallic objects in contact with the soil. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less 
likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity survey results, the subsurface soils at 
the project site have resistivity values ranging from about 5,500 to 59,000 ohm-cm, 
corresponding to corrosion ratings of 4 to 5, respectively.  
Based on the results of the laboratory resistivity tests, we believe that an average 
corrosion rating of 4 may be used for the proposed project. Design of metallic 
substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider the 
effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
However, pH testing indicated values ranging from 4.78 to 8.05.  In general, the 
soils in the vicinity of Boring No. 1 should be considered to have a corrosion rating of 1. 
3.9 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes.  The net increase in loading may cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert.  Based on our calculations, settlement on 
the order of less than 0.5-inch is anticipated for the project.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and bulk samples. Variations of conditions 
between and beyond the field borings and bulk samples may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If 
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the topographic plans transmitted by 
Belt Collins Hawaii LLC on September 4, 2014. Elevations of the borings were 
interpolated based on their relative position to spot elevations and contours shown on 
the same plans. The field boring locations and elevations should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8, which were drilled to depths of 
approximately 10.5 and 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.8. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in 
the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 
through A-8.  
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch OD split-barrel sampler with a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and 
third 6 inches (a total of 12 inches) of an 18-inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. Rock cores were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
