INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Lahainaluna Switching Substation project located near
Kelawea in the Lahaina area on the island of Maui Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recom
mendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation site
grading and pavement only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located to the south of Lahainaluna Road near
Kelawea in the Lahaina area on the island of Maui Hawaii It is proposed to develop
the site into a switching substation for the Maui Electric Company
Based on the existing topography and proposed finish grades for the project
approximately 10 feet of cuts on the east side and 16 feet of fills on the west side will
be required to achieve the design finish grades of the substation pad A single story
control building and asphalt concrete pavements are also planned to serve the
switching substation site

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Maui is composed of two shield volcanoes West Maui and
Haleakala built up by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the Pleistocene Epoch
East Maui or Haleakala Volcano which is much larger was formed later and banked
against the West Maui mountains A flat isthmus connects the two volcanoes The
proposed project site is located on the West Maui Mountains
The very permeable and primitive Wailuku Volcanic Series of West Maui were
formed during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene time from the extrusion of basaltic
lavas along two main rifts and many fissures The area is underlain by highly
weathered materials derived from in situ weathering of the Wailuku lavas

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located to the south of Lahainaluna Road near Kelawea in the
Lahaina area on the island of Maui Hawaii The site is approximately bounded by an
existing drainage ditch to the north and sugar cane fields to the other three sides It
is accessible through existing cane haul roads that traversed throughout the project
site
The subject site is presently used for the cultivation of sugar cane The surface
materials across the project site generally consisted of reddish brown clayey silt soils
with shrinkage cracks at some isolated locations The existing topography is gently
sloping towards the east at a gradient of approximately 13 percent Based on the
grading plan provided the existing ground surface elevations range from about 430
feet Mean Sea Level MSL in the east to about 470 feet MSL in the west

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the site is generally underlain by residual soils
and saprolites overlying clinkers and basalt rock formation at greater depths Based
on the existing topography and proposed finish grades for the project we anticipate that
approximately 10 feet of cuts on the east side and 16 feet of fills on the west side will
be required to achieve the design finish grades of the substation pad The on site soils
are highly silty in nature and are susceptible to loss of strength upon saturation
Based on the current siting of the proposed control building we anticipate that
the structure will straddle cut and fill transition In order to provide more uniform
support for the building foundation and to reduce the potential for appreciable
differential settlements resulting from differing subgrade conditions we recommend that
the footings be supported by a minimum of 12 inches of compacted granular fill
materials This recommendation would require that the footings located in cut areas
be over excavated by a minimum of 12 inches below the bottom of footing and be
replaced with compacted granular fill materials

Based on our laboratory swell test results presented on Plate B 1 the near
surface soils exhibit a moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations Therefore we recommend that the building slab be supported on a
minimum of 12 inches of non expansive granular fill material to reduce the potential for
future distress to the lightly loaded slab on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling
of the moderately expansive soils

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the relatively light structural
loads anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow foundations
consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on compacted granular fill
material may be used for support of the proposed control building Based on the
existing grades of the site and the finish floor elevation of the control building we
anticipate that the facility will straddle across the cut and fill transition In order to
provide more uniform support for the foundation and to reduce the potential for
appreciable differential settlement resulting from differing subgrade conditions we
recommend that the footings be supported on at least 12 inches of compacted granular
fill materials
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the compacted granular fill
materials This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3469 00 Page 8
third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces The footings
should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the compacted granular fill materials as recommended herein to be
on the order of 1 inch total with differential settlements of about inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted
granular fill materials Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f This value assumes that the concrete for footings is cast neat against
the in situ stiff soils or compacted fill materials Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be
neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by near
surface clayey soils with moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations Unless floor slabs constructed above the moderately expansive soils are
properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slab on
grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the moderately expansive clayey soils
due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress
resulting from the swelling of the expansive subgrade soils we recommend that the
concrete slab be supported on at least 12 inches of non expansive granular fill material
The non expansive granular fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the
perimeter of the proposed building structure The 12 inch non expansive granular
capping fill layer also serves to reduce the potential for slab cracking due to differential
movements at the cut and fill transition
In addition to the 12 inches of non expansive granular fill we recommend that
a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
cushion be placed below the slab to provide more uniform support of the slab and to
serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce future moisture infiltration an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on
top of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand should be placed above
the plastic membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the
slab concrete Thickened edges of slabs where provided adjacent to any unpaved
area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 6 inch thick layer of compacted select borrow material be
provided below the concrete walkway slab Construction joints should be provided at
GEOLABS HAWAII

intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle
intersections

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft
and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be disposed of
off site

Boulders or over sized rocks encountered during the site grading operations may
be disposed in deep fill areas as recommended in the Boulder Disposal
subsection of this report The near surface silty and clayey soils encountered
in cut areas may be stockpiled and reused as a source of fill material provided
that the recommendations presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection are
properly followed
After clearing and grubbing the pavement and the area within the proposed
control building limits and the area extending a minimum of 2 feet laterally from

the minimum 12 inch thick capping layer of non expansive granular fill material

Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill should consist of well graded
materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension The on site cut materials
generated from excavations into the underlying clinkers and basalt formation
may be used as general fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened
of the over sized materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension and
processed to meet the above gradation requirements This requirement is to
facilitate future construction of building foundations and utility trench excavations
Over sized rock fragments may be used in deep fills or broken down to smaller
sized materials and incorporated into the fill material If the excavated materials
do not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material borrow
materials should be considered to provide the required gradation and particle
sizes to develop a well graded material Alternately the near surface soils may
be blended into the excavated basaltic rock materials and disposed in deep fill
areas
Imported material where needed and the non expansive capping layer recom
mended below foundations slabs and pavements should be non expansive
granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 12 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or
less Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for
its intended use

Boulder Disposal
Over sized materials such as cobbles greater than 6 inches in dimension and
boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size are
anticipated from cuts into the basalt formation and may be used in general and
rock fills provided that the following recommendations are followed
1 Boulders should not be placed in general fills within 4 feet of the finish
subgrades
2 Boulders up to 4 feet in largest dimension may be placed at the bottom
of deep fills greater than 8 feet in depth and must not be nested
together They should be placed such that the compaction equipment will
be able to compact between and around them
3 Boulders up to 4 feet in maximum dimension should not encroach within
4 feet of the finish subgrades or within 2 feet horizontally or within 2 feet
vertically of utility inverts
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the proposed
utility alignment and within the depths of the proposed utility lines to
reduce the potential for encountering the boulders during excavation for
the utility trenches
5 Boulders larger than 4 feet in maximum dimension should not be used as
boulder fill unless reduced in size

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
boulders The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water
trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water
continuously during placement Compaction equipment such as a 20 ton
compactor or other similar compaction equipment providing suitable energy to
achieve the required compaction should be utilized for rock fills

Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed switching substation site is
underlain by clinkers and volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard
at shallow depths It is anticipated that the volcanic rock may be excavated with

where the rock is near the existing ground surface However excavations into
the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the drilled borings it appears
that most of the cut slopes would expose the stiff clayey soils and relatively
dense clinkers and basalt rock with frequent fractures In general we believe
that the cut slopes planned at the site may be designed with a slope inclination
of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Where the cut slopes expose
dense basalt formation at the slope face the cut slope may be steepened to a
slope inclination of 1H 1V Cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face
should be backfilled and or grouted if necessary
Permanent fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter For cut and fill slopes steeper than 4H 1V with vertical heights greater
than 20 feet we recommend that a minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at
the mid point of the slope to reduce the potential for erosion due to surface run
off water We also recommend that V ditches or earth berms be provided at
the top and bottom of slopes In addition we also recommend placement of
geotextile fabrics or rip rap on slopes that expose silty soils at the surface to
reduce the potential for erosion
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3469 00 Page 16
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design
slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face
In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching require
ments should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade
with sliver fills Alternatively cut slopes may be buttressed with fill slopes
constructed of non expansive fills Water should be diverted away from the top
of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce
potential erosion of the finished slopes

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill
up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes
and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select fill
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90
percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the control building should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the
subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations
Corrosion Control
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil charac
teristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil In general the lower the resistivity
the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure Conversely the
higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion
Four sets of soil electrical resistivity measurements using the Wenner 4 Pin
method were conducted at the proposed switching substation site The approximate
locations of the soil resistivity survey are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Results of
GEOLABS HAWAII

these tests are presented on Plate B 4 of this report Based on the results of the field
resistivity measurements the on site soils range from slightly to moderately corrosive

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to a depth of about 30 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock
material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Four sieve analyses were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils The test results are also summarized on
Plate B 3
