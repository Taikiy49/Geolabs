performed for the Makena Resort Wastewater Reclamation System located within the
property limits of the Makena Resort east and west of Makena Alanui Road in the

project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures site grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene
Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3763 00 Page 7
volcanoes The project site is located near the southern end of the island or on the lower
southwest slope of the East Maui volcano Haleakala
Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows The
lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene early Pleistocene Epoch
consists of thinly bedded tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a a These lavas tend to be
very permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit is the Kula Volcanic Series
early Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker alkalic a a flows which may
contain many inter stratified thin ash soil layers A long quiescent period followed during
which time canyons were carved in the volcano although a few eruptions may have
occurred during this erosion interval in addition to erosion due to eustatic changes higher
and lower stands of sea level At the end of the rest period copious flows erupted along
the southwest and east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit Known as the Hana
Volcanic Series Pleistocene Epoch The lavas range in composition from ultra basic
olivine porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts
The project site is located on the lower southwest slope of Haleakala just north of
the Puu Olai cinder cone The subsurface soils consist of volcanic ash material overlying
cinder cone and a a basalt formations of the Makena and Oanapuka Series In general
a a basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the
porous rock matrix along joints through fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
through clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features The presence of soil seams on the other
hand tends to reduce the overall permeability of the rock formation

PERMEABILITY TESTING
A falling head permeability test was performed in Boring No 5 after soaking of
the open borehole subgrade material to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface
materials at the proposed bottom of the infiltration basin elevation The proposed
bottom of the infiltration basin elevation is at 317 feet MSL corresponding to about
GEOLABS HAWAII

7 to 8 feet below existing ground level in Boring No 5 In general the falling head test
measures the rate of drop of a given column of water with respect to time without the
addition of additional driving forces or head Based on the test the permeability of the
silty cobble materials was found to be about 6x10 feet per minute or 3x10 cm s
The results of the test performed are provided in Appendix C
If a higher permeability is desired the bottom elevation of the infiltration basin should
be lowered to the underlying basalt formation Due to the highly vesicular and fractured
nature of the basalt formation and the presence of clinker zones we anticipate higher
permeability values as evident by the water consumption rate during the drilling
process Based on observations during our field exploration a permeability value of
6 x10 feet per minute or 3x10 cm s may be used for the underlying basalt formation
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that the proposed wastewater
reclamation facility structures and the pump stations may be supported on a shallow
foundation system bearing on the on site soils the underlying basalt and cinder rock
formations or properly compacted fill material Due to the presence of cavities voids
and loose clinker zones in the basaltic rock as encountered in the borings we
recommend that a foundation probing and grouting program be implemented during
construction for foundations bearing on basalt rock or on less than 5 feet of compacted
fill material Details of the probing and grouting program is further discussed in the
Foundation Probing section of this report
Based on the anticipated depths of cuts for the proposed structures including the
lagoons excavation into the underlying basalt and cinder rock formations will likely be
required It should be noted that excavations into and through the upper soil horizon with
cobbles and boulders may also present some excavation difficulties Heavy excavation
equipment and or hoe ramming may be necessary for removal of the hard zones

The base of the wetwell and drywell at Pump Station ML is set near elevation
8 feet MSL Based on the groundwater measured at about 1 7 feet MSL during our
field exploration the base of the wetwell and drywell will extend below the groundwater
level Therefore the wetwell and drywell structure should be designed to resist
hydrostatic uplift forces due to the groundwater conditions In addition we believe that

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that a shallow foundation system bearing on the medium stiff silts dense silty cobbles or
basalt rock formation encountered would provide suitable foundation support for the
proposed structures To provide more uniform support for the shallow foundation system
bearing on cut materials we recommend that the materials below the bottom of the
foundations be over excavated and be replaced with at least 6 inches of select borrow
subbase materials The 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
For design of foundations bearing on compacted fill or on the 6 inch select borrow
subbase over on site soils medium stiff silts dense silty cobbles or basalt rock an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot p s f may be used If
needed the allowable bearing pressure may be increased to 5 000 p s f where the basalt
rock surface is within 3 feet of the bottom of foundation elevation These bearing values
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3763 00 Page 13
are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces For design of the mat or structural slab
bearing on the select borrow subbase a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per
square inch per inch of deflection p c i may be used
Due to the potential for presence of cavities and or voids as encountered in our
borings we recommend that a program of probing and grouting the foundation locations
be implemented during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe
the probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered
Probing and grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing

In general foundations should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade For foundations constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground the foundations should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum
horizontal set back distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of foundations to
the slope face If foundations are located next to utility trenches easements or adjacent
below grade structures they should be embedded below a 45 degree or 1H 1V imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench below grade structure
or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement

less than one inch total Total settlements for foundations bearing on the select borrow

than 0 5 inches

The bottom of foundations should be relatively level This may be difficult to
achieve due to the presence of over sized materials such as the cobbles and boulders
and the required rock excavation Depressions may be filled with properly compacted
on site soils or lean concrete For foundation excavations into the coarser type material
such as the cobbles and boulders the subgrade will need to be proof rolled to a dense
and an unyielding condition prior to filling with the select borrow subbase
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of foundation

Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration cavities and or loose zones appear to be present in
the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below foundations and
structures we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted
during construction As a minimum the probing and grouting program should include
structures such as the headworks facility ultraviolet contact tank effluent filter and
secondary clarifier
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each
isolated spread foundation location up to 50 square feet in area and at 10 foot on
centers for the continuous footings For mat foundations we recommend that a probe
hole be provided for every 50 square feet of foundation footprint area The probe holes
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected
during the probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to
help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes
and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to
9 inches
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule

The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
20 borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 108 at the proposed pump stations
and along the proposed force main and gravity line alignment and Boring Nos 1
through 12 at the proposed Wastewater Reclamation Facility extending to depths of
about 10 to 35 feet below the existing ground elevation at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 through 7 The borings were drilled using a truck
mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 20
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of
an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality of
rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
_ Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification
The results of this test are presented on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits Test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
this test are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 2
One 1 inch ring swell test was performed on a remolded sample to evaluate the
swelling potential of the soil under a surcharge load The test was performed on an
air dried sample Results of the swell test are presented on Plate B 3

APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Permeability Testing
Permeability testing was performed in accordance with the Auger Hole method
a falling head permeability test This method is a variation of the borehole method in
which water is added to fill an auger hole at a given level and then the fall of the water
level is observed with time The permeability is based on the assumption that a unit
hydraulic head gradient is assumed for the flow through the bottom and side of the hole
This method is expected to give only a very approximate indication of the actual
permeability of the soil
